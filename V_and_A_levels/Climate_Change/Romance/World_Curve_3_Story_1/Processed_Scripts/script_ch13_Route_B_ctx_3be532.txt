You are waist-deep in mud before you fully realize you meant to keep your boots clean. The salt clings to your neck, the compass at your collar a hot coin against your skin. Rain slices the air in thin, stinging sheets; the prototype struts shudder where they bite the silt. Volunteers move like a single, breathing organism—hands and voices that know how to make do. You smell diesel, wet rope, and the faint sweetness of cordgrass dragged in from the Flats.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq's voice snaps in your ear, fierce and small under the wind: "Mara—now. Wedges and a jack. We can counter-tilt it, hold the seam."

You answer without thinking—the language of work first, soothing later.
Mara Lin: "Kai, wedge the base. Rosa, lift the seaward brace like you're lifting a stubborn door." 
Your chest is tight, every muscle wired to a single, ridiculous optimism: we can keep this; the town will see proof that a community can hold itself.

A volunteer's hand slips; mud claims a knee. Someone curses and laughs at the same time. The strut quivers, a low, angry sound—like a throat clearing before a shout.

Eloise Varela stands a little apart, AR rings flashing across her lenses. Her voice is calm, surgical.
Eloise Varela: "Bring it down to safe levels. No improvisation that risks people."
There's steel under the words. She's right—her words are right in the voice of municipal duty—but rightness tastes like paper when the people underfoot are the ones who will go hungry if the pilot fails.

Your compass bites into your sternum as if to remind you of the choice you already made when you walked back to Asterport: to be here, in the mud, before other people's plans decided them away.

You try to move and your legs do not obey the speed your heart needs. Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq is in front of you—his hand finds your arm and holds, not to stop you but to anchor himself. His jaw is tight; his braid flutters with salt spray.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "Mara—"
he says, voice raw with something like apology and terror tied together.

Eloise Varela steps forward, voice amplified by a megaphone because logic must be heard in noise.
Eloise Varela: "Clear the site. Now."
Her calm is a blade that cuts the frantic clutching of volunteers into order. She is saying the municipal line—safety first—and you see cameras chew the moment into frames that will be played on loop.

But it is not only policy: her eyes flick, just for a breath, toward the rising digital boards across the water—the ones with investor tickers and civic slogans. For a second the image there is a cold calculus. You see the choice she will make, the one that will cost people but promise a number the city will call "security."

You pull at a dislodged brace, hands clumsy with mud and adrenaline, and the pilot collapses in a final, shuddering surrender. Steel slides, the noise is huge and inorganic, and everything that had seemed to align—your planning, the volunteers' faith, the prototype's promise—comes apart in a wet, undeniable coda.

Eloise Varela's voice is already being shaped into policy by the municipal feed.
Eloise Varela: "We will accelerate measures. Emergency approvals will begin immediately."
Eloise Varela: "Relocation plans will be enacted for zones at immediate risk. Temporary housing and compensation protocols are being mobilized."
Her words are air that becomes law.

Samira stands behind her, hands trembling. You see the bureaucratic machinery click into place—the necessary, terrifying thing: fast safety, fast displacement. It is both a promise and a severing.

You want to scream that the Flats belong to the people who know the mud by name. You want to tell Eloise Varela that real safety is messy, slow, anchored to soil and trust, not polished PR. Instead you feel something else: an ember of fury that is not only rage but calculation. You press your palm to the compass and feel your mother's initials in the brass like a benediction.

Rosa's voice comes from your left, loud and steady as a bell.
Rosa: "We rebuild where we can. We gather the stories that will not be bought away."
Her hands are already sorting sandbags, the old rituals of saving soil and memory.

Kai rushes up, rain painting his hair pale where it clings. He looks at you with a softness that terrifies and comforts at once.
Kai: "They'll need food, ropes, lumber. And they'll need someone there who knows the Flats—someone like you."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq catches your gaze; the world narrows to the two of you for an instant in the aftermath: mud, rain, the metallic tang of floodwater. He steps closer, face open in a way he rarely allows.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "They'll give money and walls," he says. "They'll give zones. But walls are not always shelter."

Your reply is a small, stubborn thing.
Mara Lin: "We will make a plan. We will keep people here if we can."

He breathes out, a sound that could be a laugh or a sob.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I know how to build things that stay. I can take a lead on structures—if that's what you want."
He looks down at his hands, salt-caked and callused. "But...there's an offer. Upstate. A steady contract. Safer work for a family I can start. They need someone to run the retrofit teams full time."

There is a place under your ribs that forgets how to move. You see, for a beat, every possible future unfurling: one where you two scrape by together in rust and mud building with hands you both trust; another where he leaves with steady money and a quieter life, and your nights are full of the echo of his absence.

Mara Lin: "You can't—"
you begin, and stop. The cliff of blame is easy to fall from.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq interrupts you, gently, like setting down something fragile.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "Mara, I can't ask you to risk what we built—what you built—on my choice. I can't promise I won't leave you with one more loss. If I stay, I'll make decisions from that constant fear."
His voice breaks invisibly. "I can't keep doing that to people I care about."

Your mouth moves, searching.
Mara Lin: "So what? You leave because you're scared of losing me?"

He laughs, bitter.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I leave because I'm terrified of failing you all again. Because I know how fast a town can die if the foundations fail and I can't—"
He shuts his mouth. The rain hides the rest.

You want to argue, bargain, promise. Instead you find your hands curled around the compass until it hurts.
Mara Lin: "If you go, promise me you'll keep building. Promise me you'll help others. Promise me it's not because you think you don't belong here."

He meets your eyes, and for a moment there is no pretense: just a man who has carried towns on his shoulders and is now setting one down because it might crush him.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I promise," he says. "But promise me you'll keep them with you. Keep the Flats being people—not zones."

Your answer is not elegant.
Mara Lin: "I promise."

He leans forward and presses his forehead to yours, a gesture so ordinary it ruptures the noise for you. Rain leaks between you. His breath smells like solder and sea.
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "Take care of them," he whispers. "Take care of yourself, Mara Lin."

You want to tell him to stay. You want to beg him to trade a long life of fear for a shorter life of meaning. But the tide has a voice that does not listen to pleas: priorities, offers, the weight of ruined prototypes, the calculus of municipal promises. Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq steps back. His bag is a small thing with a welding torch clipped to the side. He moves in long, economical strides toward the quay.

Mara Lin: "Ibe—"
you call after him—everything you can't give him in a single line.

He doesn't turn. You see the outline of his shoulders against the gray. A small, simple gesture: he raises a hand and lets it fall, almost like a salute. The ferry light across the water blinks as if in answer. He gets on the boat.

The market that afternoon is a circle of work and rumor. People crowd around stalls with a new carefulness, their voices lower, stitches of community knitting around the raw places. You move among them like a stitch-finder, pulling stray threads together.

Rosa lays out a map on a soaked crate and marks the houses the city has designated "at immediate risk." Each red mark is a tiny funeral; your stomach flips. Kai passes you a cup of bitter tea and a towel. He laughs too loudly to keep from crying.
Kai: "They'll rebuild—different, maybe. But they'll rebuild."

Eloise Varela's press conference is the river's other bank: composed, unsparing. She speaks of "necessary timelines" and "the difficult but unavoidable work of saving the city." Her words are carefully polished; they land in different mouths like coins. Some people will trade for them. Others will count the price.

You listen, and something colder than grief slides under your ribs: the recognition that systems trade culture for safety and call it progress. You think of the little alley your mother taught you to garden in; of a rope swing you promised you'd hang for the kids who would never know it if the neighborhood was cut into a "resilience zone."

Night comes down like a demand. On the rooftop greenhouse the misting lights make tiny halos around the plants you and the volunteers have coaxed from seed. You sit at a table with a mug cooling in your hands. The compass necklace keeps you rooted to the old, to memory.

Rosa sits opposite you, hands still smelling of soil.
Rosa: "We did what we could," she says simply.
Her eyes are a map of long survival. "We keep doing it. People will move, some will stay. The Flats will always be in our stories. That's how land keeps its shape."

You want a tidy ending because you are tired of loose things. But endings in Asterport are like tides: an attempt to measure change by reference to an unmeasured sea.

Kai appears at the greenhouse door with a small stack of salvaged boards.
Kai: "We salvaged the mural from Miguel's front wall. Rosa says to find a place to stitch it into the new terraces. She wants the story to be visible."

You trace your finger along the rim of the mug and feel for the city's pulse. It is not the municipal schedules or the engineer's blueprints; it is this—people choosing, again and again, to show up.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq's absence is a hard, bright thing. The bed in the corner of your shared place is still folded the way military folks fold their lives—compact, without much room for spontaneous mess. You walk past it and press both palms to the mattress as if it could tell you where he is. The thought of him on some far, safer shore building walls that might never hold the same life stings like salt in a wound.

There is work to do: the Flats need hands, proof, care. There will be hearings, experts, and money with strings. Eloise Varela's plan will move forward with speed; so will the city's relocation orders. There will be legal fights, and there will be quiet afternoons where you lay planks and plant cordgrass until your fingers are raw and you can read the tide in the shape of your knuckles.

You stand on the greenhouse roof at dusk, the horizon smudged with oil-slick clouds and a strip of neon where the city refuses to go dark. Below, a group of volunteers sings something off-key but true as they set up night tarps. The sound is small, human; it cuts through the heaviness.

You put the compass between your palms and close your eyes. It is warm, like a memory. You think of Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq's last promise—not a vow to stay, but a vow to build, to help others even if he's gone. You think of Rosa's steady hands, Kai's ridiculous optimism, the volunteers with mud under their nails who will not be turned into an empty statistic by form and policy.

Hope is not a triumphant shout tonight. It is the stubborn, stubborn decision to keep planting, to keep defending a community's right to exist where it has always been, even as the city appoints itself the arbiter of safety in ways that fracture lives. It is loss wrapped in work, which is its own kind of tender.

You are tired. You are angry. You are alive.

You sit alone for a long time. The city keeps moving even while you grieve. The municipal edict will arrive tomorrow in a stack of forms and polite language that will not know the names of the children who will be uprooted.

You close your notebook and, with deliberate care, begin a new page. On the top line you write: "Keep the Flats alive—community-led rebuilding, legal watchdog team, emergency fund, documentation of possessions and stories." Under it you pencil names: Kai Moreno—market liaison; Rosa—marshkeeper counsel; Samira—contact for municipal leaks. You add your own name at the bottom, under a note: "Mara—stay."

You tuck the compass back under your shirt and stand to look at the city you love. The wind murmurs across the rooftop, carrying the salt of a changed tide. You let yourself feel the whole of it—loss threaded with endurance. You will fight Eloise Varela's plan in the halls and on the streets, and sometimes you will win. Sometimes you will not. You will build terraces and anchor cordgrass. You will keep a list of who was moved and where. You will teach other people how to read the tide.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq is gone. The pilot failed. Eloise Varela's plan will become law with accelerated momentum. Families will be offered relocation that will feel, for some, like the only safe option. And yet, in the quiet of the greenhouse, you feel a small, stubborn ember of something like optimism—because the people who remain will not stop making this place theirs.

You press the compass to your heart and let the air taste of wet soil and future storms. There is no neat happy ending to hand you. There is only the work, the people, and the promise—fragile, stubborn—to keep trying.
