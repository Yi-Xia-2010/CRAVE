You taste copper whenever you touch the metal model Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq is welding into shape. The workshop smells like salt, hot metal, and the lemon oil from Rosa's reclaimed tool box. Your fingers remember every fold of the maps you carried; they ache with the memory of work and worry.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "If we stack these units like this, they flex with the tide instead of pushing it back. The joints sit lower—less cost, less wall. People can stay."

You lean in, hand hovering over the miniature joint. The idea is both salvation and compromise: a way to temper Eloise Varela's hard line with something that won't uproot everyone. You feel hope, immediate and fragile, like glass tempered but not yet annealed.

Mara Lin: "We need fail-safes—channels for sediment, places where marsh plants can reclaim after a high tide. If we only harden edges, we rebuild the same problem."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [half-smile, focused]: "We can put in breakaway segments where the marsh needs room. Modular, replaceable, community-maintainable. You draw the steward nodes; I’ll torque the anchors."

You both speak fast; plans tumble out in overlapping motion. The rhythm of making soothes you and tightens you at once—something practical to do with the gnawing anxiety under everything. Tools clink. The map on your knee bears smudges of both ink and rain.

Rosa arrives with a bundle of cordgrass and a thermos of thick coffee. She sets it down like an offering.

Rosa: "You two look like the tide did a duet on you. Speak clear. The people will want names and nights—don't give them excuses."

Mara Lin: "We won't. We're proposing a hybrid. Something that grants safety goods promised by the Hall but keeps neighborhoods—keeps people—intact."

Rosa [soft, steady]: "Good. Keep your eyes honest, child. Let them see the labor and risk with the plan."

A knock, light but determined: Kai slips in, mud on his boots, the Harbor Market's latest bulletin in hand.

Kai: "Samira said she could spare an hour. She wants to hear phrasing—what to tell council. Also," (he drops his voice) "there's murmurs. Eloise's team is already drafting talking points."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq's shoulders tighten at the name. You feel the room shift a degree colder.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "We need Samira to believe the hybrid is credible. She can stall if she trusts the data."

Mara Lin: "Then we give her the data and the story."

[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Late Afternoon]
[Background:"ch7_453e40_2"]
[Sound: Bees in the trellises, the far rumble of road graders on a distant levee, intermittent wind through the greenhouse vents.]
[Music: A slightly higher, hopeful thread overlays the undercurrent—still wary, still tight]

Samira arrives, clipboard held like a talisman. Her municipal vest sticks damp against her back; the city smells of wet pavement and printer ink where she came from the Hall.

Samira [quiet, constrained]: "I can only ask for a delay if I present a credible alternative. They want a single headline: shield. They don't do nuance well in a one-hour vote."

Mara Lin: "We give them a headline they can read without misplacing the footnotes."

Samira [rubbing her temple]: "You know what I risk. My superiors—" (she stops, searching) "—they have deadlines and a board that loves numbers over people unless the people are already on a payroll."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [leaning forward]: "We give her numbers. Cost curves, maintenance estimates, a rollout schedule divided by neighborhood. We show how community labor cuts operating costs by—"

Samira [holding up a hand]: "And show how it reduces displacement metrics. I'll try."

You hand Samira a section of the model plan, your palms slightly muddy from the cordgrass. Her fingers tremble when she takes it; it's a small thing, but you register a human being squeezed between duty and conscience.

Rosa: "Remember—don't let them peddle fear for votes. Fear is a fast sale."

Mara Lin: "We frame it so fear can't monopolize the narrative. We include contingency funds, training, local hiring schedules. We make it undeniable that this keeps people in their places."

Samira [a breath]: "If I can stall for one more day—if I can get the advisory board meeting deferred—I can force a committee review. That buys you time to mobilize the proofs."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [soft lilt]: "Time is what we've been asking for."

You meet Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq's eyes over the prototype. There's a warmth there—an exhaustion shared—and something that feels like an unspoken promise to see this through. But you also feel the shear of politics: a schedule can be rigid; public sentiment can be fragile. The municipal machine has teeth and timers.

Rosa: "Good. Make them sit with the humans behind the numbers."

Kai [light but loaded]: "And bring the market folks. If voices come from the people who live it, politicians start to feel called-out."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I'll translate the tech into training modules. We can show how workshops will empower crews to repair and adapt."

You think of the volunteers who came last month—callused hands, kids learning to tie knots, neighbors swapping food for hours of labor. The image steadies the wobble in your stomach.

[Scene: Municipal Council | Evening]
[Background:"ch7_453e40_3"]
[Sound: The murmur of councilors arranging papers, the soft chirp of a notification echoing from Eloise's AR glasses.]
[Music: Tension returns, low strings matching the chamber's chill]

Eloise Varela [measured, public]: "We have a choice between decisive action and gradualism. The shield is decisive. It offers immediate reduction in risk."

Mara Lin [to Samira, under breath]: "We don't have to call it gradualism. We call it sustainable."

Eloise Varela [turning slightly, her smile controlled]: "And I welcome proposals from our citizens. The council will hear them. Time is the variable we cannot ignore."

Her words are smooth; you cannot tell whether the smile carries a blade. Samira looks at you briefly—complex, resigned, grateful; the look settles and shifts into the professional mask she must wear here.

You step forward to present. Your voice carries better than you expect—steady, slightly raw.

Mara Lin: "Thank you. Our proposal centers on modular, community-maintainable defenses integrated with marsh restoration. It reduces capital cost and preserves neighborhoods, and it builds local capacity for long-term upkeep."

Councilor [skeptical]: "How do you account for storm surge events that require immediate, large-scale response?"

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [quick, practical]: "The modules are designed to be tied into temporary, deployable reinforcement. Think of them as a distributed system—some elements are flexible, others are sacrificial, designed to let marshes do the work post-storm."

You watch faces across the chamber shift. Some tilt toward curiosity; others tighten with the strain of political accounting. Eloise Varela's expression remains unreadable—a practiced neutrality. You remind yourself of Rosa's advice: don't expect a type of purity from the city. Expect work.

Samira [presenting data]: "We've run cost projections and displacement risk models. The hybrid shows a 40% reduction in the number of households flagged for relocation under the existing shield plan."

A murmur. Someone on the council scrawls a figure. Eloise Varela inclines her head, almost imperceptibly.

Eloise Varela [mild]: "Those are promising numbers. Implementation logistics remain a concern, as well as investor assurances."

You can feel, like an undercurrent, how quickly the city's need for a headline can drown out the small print.

Councilor Two [probing]: "Who's responsible for maintenance? Who pays if modules fail?"

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [leaning into the mic]: "Local co-ops trained during rollout, municipal oversight funded by reallocated capital—community contracts for maintenance. We estimate initial investment is lower and community employment higher than the current shield model."

The back-and-forth stretches. You answer, then listen, then answer again. The exhaustion in your throat grows but the words keep coming—because words, here, can buy a day, a committee, a Plan B instead of bulldozers.

You feel both lighter—because the council listened longer than you dared hope—and heavier, because the listening hasn't yet become action.

You think of the Flats: the mud, the reed fences, Rosa stooped planting cordgrass into a future you hope will still be there. You think of Samira squeezed into this room, doing the best she could with teeth far sharper than her own.

Mara Lin: "Tomorrow we start the field tests. If the modules hold, we get data; if they don't, we learn fast. Either way—"

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "—we're there with the people who will have to live with it."

Your compass thumps warm at your collar. The rain hisses as it meets greenhouse glass. You stand on a rooftop between a council's long room and the Flats, between words that might buy time and a storm that will not wait for committees to finish.
