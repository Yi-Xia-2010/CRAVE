Kai Moreno: "Morning, captain." (He grins; paint flecks on his jacket look like constellations.) "Rosa sent this list and said, 'Don't make her do it alone.'"

[Scene: Rooftop Garden & Communal Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: A mosaic of planter boxes and solar panels under a softer sky; greenhouse glass beaded with condensation]
[Sound: Misting valves hissing, footsteps on recycled pallet boards, the soft shuffle of volunteers arranging soil]

You climb the faded stairwell with Ibe at your shoulder. He carries a battered toolbox; the patchwork elbow on his jacket catches the light. Up here, the city compresses: the Flats' low marshes are an indistinct smear; the Planning Hall's glass is a pale rectangle on the horizon. Up here, you can still pretend decisions are something you can influence with hands and elbow grease.

Rosa greets you at the greenhouse threshold, palms still smelling of peat and salt. Her braid swings like an old flag.

Rosa Alvarez: "Mara. I brought the willow stakes." (She nods at you, eyes soft but clear.) "The cordgrass wants to be gentle at first. You don't force it into shapes it won't hold."
Mara Lin: "We plant the edge; we hold the line." (You say it like a promise, though your chest makes a small, careful hollow inside.)

Volunteers drift in—some steady, others nervous—carrying trays of seedlings, tarps, hand tools. You move among them giving instructions, tying down trellises, and smoothing soil with slow, exacting motions. Each small directive is a tether: "Place the marsh grass three fingers apart. Watch this trench for run-off." Your voice calms others and, in a quieter way, steadies you.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq finds a corner and unfolds a simple diagram: a series of modular anchors and lightweight uplift frames meant to gently raise walkways and planters during high tides. He traces the lines with a fingertip.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "If we section the frames—modular lifts—people can keep their own plots. No mass uprooting, but something durable enough to handle the next season. We can pre-fabricate panels at the workshop, recruit more hands."
You look at the sketch, feeling the geometry of it—clean, efficient, designed to solve a problem with cleverness rather than long labor.

Mara Lin: "It sounds like it solves something technical, Ibe. But I'm thinking about the way people sleep above the flats, where Rosa's brother keeps his tools, where the market carts are chained. Uplift alone doesn't protect the stories." (You test the words; they are the small, slow things you mean to protect.)
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I know. The anchor points are designed to be installed without tearing through belongings. We make access ramps, storage. We don't make people leave." (He pauses, searching your face.) "We can't wait for permission that never comes."

Your exchange is calm but edged—the sort of conversation that layers worry under practicalities. Raised voices would be easy and showy; instead your friction wears the soft cloth of intimacy: careful, real, private.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "She didn't say they were sending anyone to the Flats, not yet. Just... that things are accelerated." (He avoids the attempt to define what accelerated looks like.)
You think of the Planning Hall's glass façade: bright presentations and final slides that cut neighborhoods down to color swatches. The Schrödinger rules of the day claw at the edges of your worry—so many possible pasts and futures, and you have to act without knowing which is real.

Mara Lin: "Acceleration usually benefits whoever moves money quickly. Is Samira… sympathetic?" (You ask it with a careful balance—need for an ally and the memory that institutions bend.)
Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "She is. She hates having to pass messages she can't fix. But fear drives decisions fast; fear plus investors runs like a current." (He steels his chin.) "We have to be ready before they come with a finished map."

Mara Lin: "We start the Flats tomorrow. That was the agreement. But the Hall moving faster means we might have to show them a plan sooner than we thought." (You push a corner of a map toward the center, where the Flats are shaded in charcoal.)

Samira arrives as if collected by the map's gravity: municipal vest damp with rain, clipboard still clutched. She stands a respectful step back before she speaks, which is her way of staying human inside a system that often isn't.

Samira Chen: "I don't have a directive. I can pass concerns upward, stall approvals if I can but—" (She tries and the sentence collapses into a small, exhausted smile.) "They've been told to compress timelines. There's pressure to present something that looks decisive."
