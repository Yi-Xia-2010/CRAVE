[Scene: Nursery Plots | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Rows of newly planted marram grass and sedge, thin stakes marking test plots; damp sand dark between clumps]
[Sound: Wind tearing at canvas tarps; distant gull calls; the soft scrape of a rake]
[Music: Fast, insistent strings with a steady driving pulse — bright under pressure]

You walk the lab-lined paths between the nursery plots, palms dusty with sand and peat. The living-dune tests hold. Where the grasses were thin, they have threaded together a spine; where the burlap fences were placed, sand has begun to build. Volunteers joke with each other over awkward shovelfuls, and their laughter makes the air taste like hope and wet rope.

Your field notebook is clipped to your parka — pages smudged with tide charts and Nyla’s hastily drawn schematics. Your thumb finds the tide-watch in your pocket; the brass is warm from your skin. You feel less like a strategist this evening and more like someone who has sewn stitches into a living thing and is watching it take.

Nyla [oily boots, algae in her hairline like confetti]: "The sensors are saying what we wanted. Last three runs show sediment accretion up ten percent in the southern swale."

Maya Serrin: "Ten percent." You let the number sit. It feels larger when everyone in town can see it on a screen, larger when a tide-watch warms in your pocket. "Good. That'll help the grant narrative — tangible stabilization, not just promise."

Nyla [a quick look]: "Mayor Hale already called. He wants a demonstration he can point to in two weeks for the funding panel."

You inhale; the dune-smell is green and iron-rich. Two weeks. The timeline tightens like a winch.

Volunteer (off): "We're bringing more coir tonight. Aiden's crew said they'd double the shift."

Aiden Koa arrives then, sleeves rolled, his jacket smelling of tar and diesel. He moves with the calm of someone who has spent years balancing on decks in heaving seas. He doesn't say much at first — just watches the new ridges, palms open as if wanting to feel whether the plant roots have actually taken.

Aiden Koa [flat]: "Looks good."

Aiden Koa [softening]: "You're stubborn as a cliff, Maya." (He nods at the dune.) "That's not a small thing."

Maya Serrin: "Neither are you." Your answer is softer than you planned; the dunes hum between you like something fragile and ferocious.

[Scene: Tide Research Station | Early Evening]
[Visual: Low glass building with instruments blinking; inside, amber screens map rising curves]
[Sound: The soft beep of gauges, a printer spooler, low hum of server fans]
[Music: Electronic percussive motif layered over the strings — urgent and bright]

Inside the station, the lights throw a warm glow that makes the ocean outside look almost serene. You and Nyla lay out the recent model runs on the glass table: color-coded, annotated in your own shorthand. The forecast horizon shows a tightening storm window — modeled probability of a severe swell has shifted up by almost a third.

Mayor Hale is already there when you step in, hands clasping a folder with his usual habitual pressure. His face is a map of small lines and practical fatigue: budget spreadsheets, volunteer rosters, re-election calendars. He is the human hinge between the town's patience and the grant's ticking clock.

Mayor Hale [practical, quick]: "Maya, good work on the dunes. But the funders want to see risk mitigation now. They want measurables. We need a plan that reads clean on a proposal."

Maya Serrin: "They'll get measurables. The test plots are working. But the storm window — these models shift quickly. We need enough buffer to hold the neighborhoods if the worst hits."

Mayor Hale [lowering his voice]: "That's why Elena's proposed a conditional partnership. Fast money, fast equipment. It gives us a headline: engineered corridors plus the sensors you want. But it's a trade."

Your stomach tightens despite the warm light. Trade-offs have teeth.

Nyla [pointing at a heat-map]: "If the revetment goes at those points, it will change current flows. The co-op's passages to the north could be cut—"

You glance at the map and then at the mayor. The mayor watches you for a beat too long, the way a man trained in compromise will.

Mayor Hale: "She can lock down a protected access corridor. Your sensors run; her firm installs automated barriers. We look like action."

Maya Serrin: "At the cost of what?" You hear the edge in your voice before you name it. "At the cost of fishermen losing lanes they’ve used for generations? At the cost of our community's agency?"

Mayor Hale [sighing]: "You know how politics is, Maya. If the town looks indecisive, we lose the entire grant."

The station's fan hums. Outside, the ocean compresses to a single low line on the horizon, a patient power.

[Scene: Community Hall | Evening — Town Meeting]
[Visual: Long wooden tables, strings of bulbs; townsfolk gathered shoulder to shoulder]
[Sound: Murmurs, the clink of cups, papers shuffling]
[Music: A rising choir of strings and sparse piano, tension threaded with warmth]

The hall smells of coffee and damp wool. People lean in, faces lit by amber bulbs. You stand at the head of the room, tide-watch in your palm, the weight of the town stretching behind your ribs. Dr. Elena Park steps forward, immaculate in a graphite coat that eats the soft light and returns it in a clinical glint.

Dr. Elena Park [measured, persuasive]: "Our firm will fund a protected access corridor and automated sensor barriers. We deploy quickly. We save lives. We can begin construction immediately on engineered revetment at the pinch points."

Maya Serrin [steady]: "Which pinch points, Elena? And what guarantees for passage to the co-op?"

Dr. Elena Park [tilting her head]: "We can model the current and include gated passages at specific locations. But the revetment will alter flow at certain inlets. There will be trade-offs. We offer funding and technology, but we need certain clear construction zones to be effective."

Aiden Koa stands now, jaw tight, hands balled into a fist. He does not speak at first. You can feel the room lean toward him, waiting for the wave.

Aiden Koa [low]: "You mean at the north inlets. Those are the lanes my crew uses. You fence those off and half of Saltwood's catches move somewhere else."

A woman from the co-op — an older fisher with a voice like wind through rigging — interjects: "You tell me where the nets go, Doctor. You tell me where my son will fish. This town's not a lab."

Dr. Elena Park [soft but firm]: "This isn't about erasing tradition. It's about preventing entire neighborhoods from washing away. We can design with care."

Mayor Hale [pressing]: "We don't have the luxury of slow wins. The funders want to see demonstrable infrastructure. If we don't show progress, we risk all funding."

The argument spirals in the hall — not ugly, but urgent. Voices overlap: Nyla urging for more data, a volunteer pleading for the aged residents in low blocks, an alderman worrying about re-election headlines. You feel the music swell inside you, an adrenal current that makes your hands tremble.

Maya Serrin [to the room, voice rising]: "We designed these dunes because we believed people should steward their own shores. We didn't plan to hand our presence to outside contractors who decide for us."

Dr. Elena Park [interrupting, gently]: "Maya, I'm offering a partnership that keeps you in governance. We can write binding oversight into the contract. We can create a community supervisory board."

Maya Serrin: "Can you?" The question is a knife. You allow yourself to imagine the contract, the legalese swallowing small voices. "Will the board have veto? Will the co-op's rights be in there as clauses, or as afterthoughts?"

Dr. Elena Park [eyes steady]: "We can negotiate specific oversight protocols. We have legal teams that will put terms in."

Aiden Koa's silence breaks like a snapped rope. He stands and moves toward you as if closing distance will change the physics of the offer.

Aiden Koa [close, fierce]: "You don't get to sell peace of mind by giving away the sea." He looks at you, and the room flattens to the beat of his words. "If you sign off, Maya—if you let them carve those lanes—how do you tell the fishers you kept their future?"

Your throat tightens. You want to answer with policy and data and the soft comfort of Nyla's models. Instead, you feel the small heat under your parka and the brass of the tide-watch against your palm. Your mother's house — you see, suddenly, the soaked floorboards and the lavender curtains turned to water — a raw, personal ledger of what loss looks like.

Nyla reaches for your arm, grounding. "We have three options on the table," she says, quiet but precise. "Refuse and double down. Accept a tightly governed hybrid. Or launch a public campaign and push for the grant without ceding control. Each has risk."

You scan the faces — the co-op members, volunteers with callused hands, an anxious mayor whose job is to keep people housed. The town is small and brittle and stubbornly full of life. The models thrum in your head like a warning and a promise.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Night — After the Meeting]
[Visual: Half-submerged boardwalk under a low, silver sky; the town lights like warm teeth behind you]
[Sound: The ocean's steady, closer breathing; a single dog barking far off; distant construction lights at the corporate site]
[Music: Crescendoing strings and brass — urgent, luminous]

You and Aiden walk the boardwalk in the spill of night. Your boots click on warping planks. The sea is a black mirror with white teeth; the lights at the corporate site are clinical pinpricks in the distance. Between you, the silence is not empty — it's full of questions.

Aiden Koa [quiet]: "They want to take away the lanes and call it progress. They call it safety. Who decides that safety? People who fish in low coves or people who sit in boardrooms with models?"

Maya Serrin: "Both have to be heard." The answer tastes like sand. "That's the point of community oversight."

Aiden Koa [bitter laugh]: "And when the oversight gets buried in ten pages of clauses? When engineers call a passage 'inefficient' and close it for 'optimization'?"

You stop at the railing and look out. The boardwalk groans beneath you. Your hands curl around the tide-watch until the brass is cold. You think of your mother on the porch, the smell of stew and old wood; you think of Old Man Cala's stories of channels that were once open and then closed by someone else's hand.

Aiden Koa [reaching, not touching]: "Whatever you do, Maya — don't let them make your signature into a cession."

Maya Serrin: "I won't." The words come faster than you planned, fuelled by an urgency that tastes almost like fear but is steadier — responsibility. "But I also won't let people die waiting for the perfect solution."

He looks at you then, and the anger softens into something wary and raw. "So what'll you do?"

Your chest thrums. The arousal in the night is high, a living drum in your veins: the models demand quick action, the people's lives demand respect for tradition, the mayor demands optics, Elena demands space to act, and you — you carry your mother's loss like ballast.

You know the choices lined before you like paths cut into wet sand. Each will shape who keeps the town, who loses it, and who, if anyone, pays the cost.
