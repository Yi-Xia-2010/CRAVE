You walk away from the Beacon's glow. Your compass thunks against your thigh with each step, a metronome counting the distance. The promenade underfoot is the same wood you grew up on and yet it rings
with newness — it carries the memory of what used to be and does not promise return.
You do not look back when Noah calls your name once more. You do not turn. Your shoulders are heavy, and your breath makes small clouds in the humid air. Around you the town rearranges itself
into a new geometry where safety has a price and some people pay it with houses, others with memories.
You move toward a different shore, toward vans you'll outfit with living-shore kits, toward places where the law still bites and the tide still speaks. You will be itinerant, teaching people to stitch cordgrass, to raise
platforms, to read the slow language of marsh and water. It is not everything. It may be too late for some. It will not be the Beacon's model, but it will be honest and salted and
human.
Your heart is raw, but it keeps moving. Love is not annihilated here; it is reformed into a ache you will carry like coral: beautiful, sharp, useful as a lesson.
You stop once at the rail and take the compass out. The brass is scratched, warm with the friction of your grip. You press your thumb to it as if to remember an old map. When you tuck it back in, it swings once and settles.
You take the first step toward the ferry landing. Behind you, the Beacon's light bleaches the pier. Ahead, a different dark waits, the promise of work that will be small and immediate and stubborn.
You do not know if the town will keep its heart. You know that you will not be there when the new plaques go up. You know that some people will be able to live in
their houses and some won't. You know also — indelibly — that the cost of safety has been paid in names and kitchen tables and neighborly breakfasts that will not resume.
You walk into the night, and the sound of the sea rises to meet you: indifferent, relentless, honest.
