Marta folds herself into a doorway and thumbs through a list of names on her tablet, every line a life. Mayor Rosa moves with that ledgered calm you've seen a thousand times — she negotiates storms like debts. Noah Reyes: "Asha." (He stops close enough that you can smell the coffee that's become his armor.)

Asha Moreno: "They called a special session. The investors want a 'stabilization package' — board seats, access to data, covenants on waterfront leases."

Asha Moreno feels your hands clench where the compass rests. Your knuckles go white through cotton.

Asha Moreno: "Board seats? Covenants? They want to fence in the sea and put a tollbooth on memory."

Noah Reyes (frowning): "They think in different terms. Risk metrics, binding covenants. They say it's the only way to secure the grants that'll pay for the sensors and the wall patches."

Asha Moreno: "You hear the words and you make them sound like salvation. Those covenants don't buy safety for the elderly who can't keep the rising rates. They buy predictability for investors."

Noah Reyes: "You know I don't want people priced out." (He rubs his forehead.) "I negotiated harder than anyone. But Rosa—Rosa's balancing an impossible budget. The legal fees, the emergency shoring we couldn't do because the firm pulled funding—"

Asha Moreno: "Lila Park pulled funding because we exposed the clauses. Because we called out that the Beacon's terms were meant to privatize access. You told me you wanted rights and transparency."

Noah Reyes (voice drops, quick and sharp): "I still do. But right now we have 72 hours to sign something or the town's insurance ratings tank and every mortgage-holder gets a notice. If we don't accept a package, the private equity offers more — and they'll do the shoring overnight."

Asha Moreno's chest tightens as if someone is winding a winch around your ribs. Panic is a map without ink; you don't know the routes it will choose.

Asha Moreno catches yourself laughing, hollow and fast, because you know what the word 'displace' becomes when numbers fall across names.

Asha Moreno stands and speaks with a voice that wants to be steady but is raw.

Asha Moreno: "We asked for sovereignty over our sensors, for community governance. We asked so the next storm would not be somebody else's balance sheet. You asked for transparency and we gave you the contract. Transparency got us a withdrawal—then the market took the void."

Marta: "They told us a managed model could keep the markets safe." (She grips a cup of something gone cold.) "They said there would be subsidies."

Asha Moreno: "Subsidies that stop when investors want higher yields." (You swallow.) "They'll preserve the houses' exteriors, but if you're below the income threshold, you won't be in the house when the next wave comes."

Marta goes quiet. Her hands find the seeds in her pocket as if to reassure herself that some things are still tangible.

Noah Reyes finds Asha Moreno again, hands wet with the same rain. His coat is heavier, as if threaded with decisions.

Noah Reyes: "They offered me the technical director role, Asha." (He waits for the hit of your reaction before continuing.) "It keeps me here. I can steer how the sensors operate. I can push for better access tiers, for community allocation."

Asha Moreno: "Steer from inside the machine." (Your voice is brittle.) "Or choose the ship's deck and steer with the people. You can be inside and still become a cog."

Noah Reyes: "If I don't take it, someone who doesn't care about people will. I can make sure data isn't used against neighborhoods. I can—"

Asha Moreno cuts him off, not because you want to be cruel but because silence has become its own betrayal.

Asha Moreno: "You can make it less terrible. That's different from making it right."

Noah Reyes (anguish tightening his jaw): "Right doesn't keep houses standing when the insurance refuses to underwrite us."

Asha Moreno: "Right keeps people there. Right doesn't let a vacancy index become an acceptable cost."

Noah Reyes: "I love you, Asha. Part of the reason I'm doing this is because I believe we can keep people here and keep them safe."

Asha Moreno: "What you're doing now is keeping people safe — if those people can afford the price. I need to stop pretending 'keep people safe' and 'keep the town whole' are the same hands."

Noah Reyes (voice breaks): "So what, then? Walk away? When everything is falling apart?"

Asha Moreno (your words a blade): "I stay out there." (You gesture to the market, to the garden beds, to the empty boat hulls.) I'll run training at the edges, teach living-shore techniques, coordinate relocations—whatever it takes. But I can't be comfortable with the compromise you are choosing."

Noah Reyes: "You mean you won't take the job with us?" (He says 'us' with a tremor that forces you to look at the breadth of the gulf.)

Asha Moreno: "I won't be part of building fences that shut our neighbors out."

Noah Reyes: "If I take it, I can do good from the inside. I can also negotiate mitigation funds, bring in community oversight—"

Asha Moreno: "You can also accept the terms that require managed access to the very thing we fought for."

Noah Reyes: "Asha—"

Asha Moreno: "Noah." (One name. A lifetime of halves.)

Asha Moreno: "Then I won't be there to watch you make the compromises I can't live with."

Noah Reyes reaches for you, hands shaking.

Noah Reyes: "Don't go like this. Not with all we've done."

The ache of what remains — the love unsentenced to ruin but altered by the price of safety — settles in your chest. You hold it like a map that no longer fits the shoreline.
