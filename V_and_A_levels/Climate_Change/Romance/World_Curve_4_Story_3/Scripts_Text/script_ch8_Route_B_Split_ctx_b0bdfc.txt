You step into the Beacon's plaza carrying the aftertaste of the lab's feed — salt and circuitry, the small electric thrill that meant the trial worked. That success is already a lever in someone else's hand.

Lila Park: "Our deployment has proven the network's responsiveness. Scale this, integrate engineered seawalls, floating platforms, and designated resilience districts, and Marabel becomes a model — safe, fundable, repeatable."

Her voice is flat, efficient—that same voice that convinced councils as easily as it convinced investors. Around her, slides click through: flood-lines, cost-benefit curves, rendering after rendering of a smooth, defended coastline. People lean forward like gulls leaning into a wind.

Narration:

You frown at the phrase—"preferential occupancy" feels like a promise wrapped in a legal glove. Your chest tightens because you can imagine how promises can be translated, contorted, auctioned.

Noah Reyes [quietly, into your ear]: "We can negotiate terms — more community seats, veto powers, audit rights. It won't be perfect, but it's leverage."

You feel the warmth of his breath like a small tide at your collarbone. He places the rolled map he carried on the table between you and Lila, an old gesture of solidarity that suddenly feels charged.

Noah Reyes: "We need clear legal guarantees. Resident veto on land disposition. Binding timelines. Transparency clauses."

Lila Park [tilting her head]: "We can draft operational charters. The consortium is amenable to community representation, provided implementation is not compromised."

Narration:
Her words are precise and practiced; her eyes hold the steel-gray calm of someone who believes speed is salvation. The plaza hums with pressure. You feel urgency like a physical current, tugging fingers and loyalties.

Lila Park: "Mayor, this is not the erasure of a community. It's preservation at scale. We are offering stewardship, not dispossession."

Narration:
Her words slide over bone. Preservation at scale. Stewardship. Language that sounds protective becomes a lid, and you can hear in it the possibility of being boxed into safety that isn't yours. Your father's boat flickers behind your eyes — a memory of salt-smelled wood and hands that understood the sea. You imagine the boat against rocks that might be fixed, and the dock bulldozed for a new pier with a clean logo.

Noah Reyes [softer]: "Rosa, can we draft binding resident vetoes? Real seats, equal voice? If we accept, it must be under ironclad terms."

Mayor Rosa [rubbing the bridge of her nose]: "Every day we wait is another household at risk. If we stall and a storm comes—"

You cut him off silently. The room feels like a trap with three open sides. Each word you say will shift who keeps what.

Narration:

You sit across from Mayor Rosa with Noah to your right and Lila directly opposite, the trust draft between you like an un-opened mollusk. The city's files smell faintly of wet paper and exhaustion.

Mayor Rosa: "I want to keep Marabel whole. I want safety. Lila, your offer is the fastest way we've seen."

Lila Park [leaning in]: "Mayor, timelines matter. Flood lines are rising faster than projections from five years ago. We can secure the inlet and protect the most vulnerable neighborhoods within implementation months."

Noah Reyes: "And the trust?"

Lila Park: "The trust pools management for efficiency. It keeps maintenance standardized and funding intact."

Narration:
Lila's hands move as if handling a model: precise, clean. Mayor Rosa's pen hesitates above the page. In the corner, a legal brief lists the words that fracture communities: "management," "transferability," "in perpetuity."

Noah Reyes reaches out, thumb finding the back of your hand under the table—brief contact, but enough to anchor you. His jaw tightens; you can see the thought-process in his eyes, a nervous cartographer trying to redraw lines of power.

Noah Reyes: "You said community representation. Can we make that binding? Two-thirds resident board approval for disposition, independent audit clauses, sunset review periods?"

Lila Park [cool]: "We can consider stronger governance language. But delays cost lives. Every modification adds time to the implementation schedule and introduces legal complexity that could invite litigation."

Mayor Rosa [voice breaking, tired and human]: "If legal complexity prevents deployment and a storm hits before the defenses go up, I'll be responsible for choosing inaction."

Narration:
Her words crack like thin ice. Inaction as culpability. You taste metal and cold: guilt as a daily bread. Your mind loops to your father — to the boat he could not save, and to the neighbors who slept in attics during storms. The moral calculus quickens: immediate safety vs long-term control.

Lila leans forward, voice softening into strategist mode.

Lila Park: "Asha, you understand systems. You can shape the forensic access to sensors, the operational transparency. Join us at the drafting table. Hold us accountable from the inside."

Narration:
Her outstretched hand is a trap wrapped in a partnership. You imagine signing the paper and then having to stitch community oversight into a corporate engine. You imagine standing in a courtroom as terms unravel. The room compresses; the rain hammers its impatience.

Noah Reyes [exasperated, but trying to steady]: "We can demand enforceable audit triggers, a majority resident veto on land sale, and a legally binding maintenance schedule. But you have to agree to timelines and clausal clarity."

Lila Park [measured]: "Propose them in writing. We'll review. But remember — legal wrangling pushes timelines. The consortium cannot be hostage to endless negotiation."

Narration:
Every sentence sharpens the choice. Your pulse is a drum; your vision narrows to the legal brief before you. The romantic warmth you'd shared with Noah minutes ago cools into a pragmatic partnership: two people trying to shepherd a town through a storm.

Noah Reyes [whisper]: "Asha—however you advise, we'll carry the consequences. I don't want us to fracture over this."

You see the strain: his fear of confrontation warring with the need for decisive action. Your own tendency to shoulder burdens makes the choice feel like a tidal gate you alone must lift.

You picture the promenade at high tide, the skiff of your father's boat lurching. You picture the old boatyard, the smell of sawdust and tar, replaced by a polished promenade and a security gate that glows with a consortium logo. The image twists.

Narration:
The urgency spikes. Lawyers draft clauses in the hall outside; footsteps are fast and heavy. The Beacon's sensor ping accelerates like a failing heartbeat. Outside, the ocean is a low roar reminding you that choices here are not hypothetical.

Your shoulder presses against Noah's under the table; he squeezes back, a silent plea for steadiness.

Noah Reyes: "We need your voice at the meeting. How do you want us to frame this? Harden our terms and accept the project with those guarantees? Or push for a referendum to make it a town decision? Or mount a public campaign and risk losing the funds but preserving local control?"

Narration:
His questions splinter into a dozen possible futures, each sharper than the last. Your chest is a drum and the strike is coming.
