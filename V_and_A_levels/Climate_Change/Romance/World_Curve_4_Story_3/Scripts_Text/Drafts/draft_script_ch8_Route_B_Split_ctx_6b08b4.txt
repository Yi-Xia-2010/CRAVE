### DRAFT
# Route: Additional / Ending (Sad)


[Scene: The Beacon — Glass Conference Room | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Rain-smeared glass wall; Lila Park stands by a holographic tide model that pulses azure and red]
[Music: Tense, minimal strings; a steady electronic thrum underneath]
[Sound: Low murmur of lawyers, the distant hiss of automated shutters]

You sit near the window, fingers hooked under the brass compass hanging at your throat. The metal is slightly warm from the sweat of your palm; its edge is a tiny, private anchor in a room designed to make anchors obsolete. Outside, the Promenade’s lights smear into halos through the mist. Inside, the Beacon minimizes weather to neat data points.

Lila's voice is precise, practiced—the kind of cadence that erases friction. Her presentation slides flip like tide charts: engineered seawalls, modular floating platforms, resilience districts with layered redundancies. Each concept is rendered as gleaming infrastructure that promises a clean line between danger and safety.

Lila Park: "Our consortium can deliver measurable reduction in inundation within two years. We model this at a ninety-percent mitigation for the waterfront sector alone."

You taste the numbers like salt and metal. They are comforting in the mouth. They are also a ledger line with teeth.

Mayor Rosa sits to your left, her fingers folded around a warm paper cup. The tired beneath her eyes is visible even under the Beacon’s flattering light; she breathes like someone carrying a town in her lungs. The lawyers speak in placid, careful clauses—'managed trust,' 'fiduciary oversight,' 'retained for resident use (subject to governance protocols).' Their sentences curl like fishing line, strong and hard to see until you pull.

Lawyer (firm): "The trust structure allows for consolidated capital deployment and ensures maintenance. We can include community representation seats and revisitation clauses—within defined intervals."

Your internal counterpoints form like tide lines: who defines 'community representation'? How many seats matter when votes are coded in bylaws and upkeep is contracted? The trial's sensors performed perfectly—today's calm is proof of that. Lila's offer is the natural next paragraph. But the paragraph includes the town's waterfront as an asset to be managed.

Lila notices you watching, and for a second her steel-gray eyes split the soft light and become hard glass. Her gaze is not unkind; it is certain.

Lila Park: (leaning in) "Asha, with your background, you'll see the technical robustness here. What I'm asking is: will Marabel let us turn data into action before the next major season?"

You want to answer with every local memory you carry—the smell of wet planks on your father's dock, the small ritual of his lines looped and waiting—but that answer feels impossible in this room. Instead you find language that translates to contracts.

You: "Action that preserves people and place has to include local governance, meaningful oversight on asset use, and binding clauses that prevent unilateral sale."

Lila's jaw tightens, the first break in her smoothness.

Lila Park: "We can write governance in. But speed dilutes negotiation. The more conditions you ask for, the more time—and funding—slips."

Mayor Rosa: (softly) "We have families still sleeping with one eye open. We can't stall forever. If this buys safety—"

The room exhales into a new tension. Outside, a gull bristles in the wet dim. The pitch becomes less about engineering and more about who keeps the keys.

[Scene: Municipal Offices — Mayor Rosa's Small Meeting Room | Early Evening]
[Visual: A round table scarred by previous town meetings; a single lamp pools light; rain tick-taps on the window]
[Music: Low timpani under strings, heartbeat tempo]
[Sound: Papers shuffling, a distant speaker announcing a delayed ferry]

The lawyers move in pairs now, conspiratorial and courteous. They speak in hypotheticals as if hypotheticals were harmless: "assume a Phase One," "imagine fiduciary responsibilities." Rosa rubs her forehead between two fingers. You see the ledger of votes, of obligations, of state deadlines that don't care for sentiment.

Noah stands across from you, leaning on the table with that slow, familiar steadiness that has always steadied something inside you. He hasn't yet said anything dramatic; instead his eyes are mapping the room, asking questions with half a smile.

Noah: (low) "They're leaning into leverage. That trial—great optics. It'll look like safety to the council. We can get more seats on the trust, stronger reversion language if we push—"

You: "But will that be enough? A seat at a table doesn't stop a clause that allows sale under 'extreme fiscal pressure.'"

Noah: "We negotiate hard on triggers then—clear thresholds for any property disposition, resident vetoes on certain classes of decisions. It will be legal-heavy, but it's possible."

Mayor Rosa: "The state's inspection deadline is three months. If we don't show a viable plan, grant funds evaporate. I am not immune to pressure. I want to protect people."

Your chest tightens into a knot. The map of your father's boat flashes—smaller things composing a life—and then the possible image of his dock replaced by engineered plazas that exclude the small, the artisanal, the messy—your town turned efficient but unfamiliar.

Eli's voice interrupts the calm. He stands in the doorway, wood-smudged palms still, face weathered and raw.

Eli Duarte: "Rosa, if we hand over the waterfront, they might fix the flood line but they also fix who gets to be here. My workshop's pegged to the water. Do I move my family to make room for a maintenance corridor?"

The room shifts. The lawyers shift terms into subtler shapes. Lila watches, intent and cool, like someone measuring the weight of small things against an outcome she already calculates as net-positive.

Lila Park: "We intend to retain residential access. We are not in the business of displacing communities. Engineering without habitation is pointless."

Her words are answers prepped for the question you don't want to ask: who decides what 'retention' looks like in practice?

[Scene: Floating Market & Raised Gardens — Early Night; Lanterns Glimmer | Dusk]
[Visual: A blur of awnings; raised plots breathing lightly in the mist; people huddle with thermoses]
[Music: Urgent acoustic guitar plucked faster, voices layered like tide calls]
[Sound: Laughter cracked by argument, a vendor slapping wet change into a palm]

You move through the market like someone walking a memory, each stall a small citadel of routine. Here, the Beacon's models are a rumor. Here, the seawall could be salvation or a wall where your grandfather used to fish.

Marta meets you by the garden plots, hands stained with potting mix. Her face is a map of lines for patience and endurance; tonight her patience is threadbare.

Marta Chen: (bitter) "They heard one alarm and suddenly investors are kissing our seawalls. People want safety, but they don't want a different town."

You: "How much of the waterfront do you think they mean to consolidate?"

Marta: "Enough to run maintenance corridors and pay back bonds. Enough that the renters who live above Eli's shop will get priced out."

Eli, who has been repairing a boat hull nearby, wipes his hands and leans against a piling, salt and sawdust in the grooves of his palms.

Eli Duarte: "Does the Beacon know how to fix a net? No. Does it know how to listen to the sound of a rope? Maybe not. I'm not against engineering—hell, I want roofs over heads—but not if it erases how we live here."

Your throat is a narrow channel. Every face in the market carries the same double-signed emotion: relief at the idea of fewer alarms, and a sour taste of loss.

You: "We need terms that lock in more than optics. Resident vetoes. Reversion clauses. Community stewardship on the board."

A young vendor at the corner, someone you remember from childhood, cuts in with a sharper fear.

Vendor: "If the trust brings 'redevelopment,' where will my stall go? Where will my fishmonger be? I can't pay for permissions that aren't even decided yet."

Your options feel heavy as buoys.

[Scene: Promenade — Night; The Boardwalk Is Slick | Night]
[Visual: Floodlights pool faintly over puddled wood; the town behind you murmurs]
[Music: A single cello bows into a rising tremor; heartbeat quickens]
[Sound: Waves rubbing at pilings, the distant beep of a Beacon alert testing itself]

You step away from the market and walk the boards where you used to learn rope knots. The past is tactile here: splinters, tar aroma, the cadence of steps across planks. The Beacon's lights glow on the horizon like a lighthouse that doesn't need you. Your phone buzzes in your pocket.

Noah: (on the phone, quietly) "I don't want to pressure you. I know what this asks of you. But listen—those trustees? They can be negotiated. It's not all-or-nothing. Get stronger ownership clauses, resident veto powers. We get them seats, we force transparency."

His voice is low; it is a hand in the dark. You can hear the rain in the line, or perhaps it's the Promenade's current in his background. He says the thing you worried he would say: thread the needle, accept what you can keep.

You: "If we accept in principle, we make it harder later to unpick power. They can keep rewriting the margins until the core is gone."

Noah: "Then negotiate the core. Demand reversion triggers, clear definitions of 'public use,' legal escrow for funds, and punitive clauses if the trust attempts forced sale. We can make it so binding that no board—no matter how corporate—can corrode it without the town's consent."

You imagine clauses written like seawalls but in law: thick concrete phrases that stop erosion of right and place. You also imagine corners where the legal concrete cracks and the sea seeps in.

Noah: (after a pause) "I can sit with you in the meetings. I can push for language that doesn't just sound pretty in a slide. We can make transparency mandatory—mandatory audits, public dashboards, resident recall rights."

You: "And if they walk? If funding evaporates?"

Noah: "Then we take the fight outside the room. Then we rally. But right now—if we're honest—some of this is our foothold."

He waits for your decision like a man holding a rope that might save you.

You think of your father's boat again, of Eli's hands, of Marta's garden plots, of Rosa's tired face. The pressure pulls in several directions at once. Each option feels like a lever that moves someone else's life.

Your chest is tight; anger and fear and pragmatism rise as a single swell. The Beacon pulses over the horizon, an unblinking eye that recorded the trial's success and now watches the negotiation.

You are an engineer of systems who is also the bearer of tiny domestic memories. The question is not just which legal tool will hold water, but which compromise you can live with in the long wash.

The town waits in different rooms—some for safety, some for a promise they fear is a disguise. You can smell ozone, hot coffee, damp wool; you can feel the compass weight like a ledger against your sternum. Momentum is building; this choice will define how Marabel answers the tide.

[Music: Strings reach a higher pitch; tempo quickens]
[Sound: The Beacon emits a soft alert—an echo of the trial that gave Lila leverage]

You look down at your hands, at the compass, at the ring of places you belong to. The urgency of the hour sharpens: act now and set terms, or push the town to decide collectively and risk losing the moment.
