You step back inside from the salt and dusk, the greenhouse air warm and heavy on your face. Your clothes smell faintly of tide: kelp and old rope, the memory of the flats clinging to you. In your pocket the plan is folded into something thinner than courage but steadier than hope. For now, the work is granular — spores, trays, long-lines — and that steadiness is all you have.

Mika is at the bench, leaning over a mason jar of spore slurry, fingers stained a soft, sea-silt brown. She hums under her breath, more focused than usual, sorting labels with a practised economy of movement. Small stacks of sample tubes catch the light like a city's tiny lamplight. Kaori "Kai" Matsuda is a coral-bright slash in the corner, braid tucked over one shoulder, boots planted on a puddle of condensation. She gestures at a rough blueprint pinned to the panel: a string of barges, seeded early-season, drifting on controlled lines.

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda: "If we don't drop barge-lines this season, the window closes. Temperature spikes will shift growth windows by weeks, not months. We seed now, we get the yield before the heat spike. It's simple."

You inhale the humid, chlorophyll warmth and set the satchel on the counter. The brass spectrometer at your belt clicks softly when you move. You open the case and pull out the printouts — small, neat graphs, annotated in your cramped handwriting. They are not just numbers to you; they're a map of how the flats might knit themselves back together.

Marina Reyes: "Look. The growth curves show a better chance with staggered long-lines closer to shore. Barge seeding has risks — drift, predation, regulatory blowback. These are the projections for a cooperative-managed array."

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda's eyes flash — not unkindly, but sharp. She comes closer, braid catching a stray beam and flaring like neon.

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda: "You're talking projections while the tide keeps changing the rules. You came back to fix things from the ground. Why are you bringing the town's calendar into the greenhouse? Why are you courting… paperwork?"

There is an edge in her question that circles something older than this argument: the time when the town's trust split into camps and when you and Elias drifted apart. You feel the impulse to reply with data, to point at the graphs and let the lines talk; you feel the other impulse — to say why you came back, to name the private, stubborn reasons that led you to the glasshouse table in the first place.

Mika watches you both, quiet. She finally lifts her eyes and tilts her head.

Mika: "Whatever we do, it has to be testable. Kai's barge idea could work if we pair it with sentinel sensors. We can log everything — temp, salinity, growth rates. If the town sees hard numbers, they'll listen."

You nod. The idea of sensors makes your chest loosen; it gives the cooperative a language other than rhetoric. Still — beneath that practical relief is the worry that numbers can be turned both ways, used to heal or to justify hard choices someone else will make.

Marina Reyes: "That's part of what I want. I want data the town can read for itself. Not so the council can bulldoze us, but so neighbors know what we're building and why."

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda folds her arms, metal bracelets clinking. Her jaw sets.

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda: "You keep saying 'the town,' but half the town's votes are already being courted by a promise of walls and payouts. They're scared, Marina. Scared people want the quick concrete fix. If you're going to lean on 'data,' don't let it become someone else's justification for closing doors on the people you want to keep."

Her words are not accusation exactly — more a dare that presses on a bruise. You let silence hold for a moment, listening to the pumps and to your own breath.

Marina Reyes: "I didn't come back to hand my work away to plans that move people off the flats. I came back to keep us here. But I also don't want to pretend there isn't a place for structures that buy time for the cooperative's living systems."

Kaori "Kai" Matsuda exhales. For a heartbeat she looks unreadable — that complex expression you learned to recognize after returns and reconciliations. It's neither a yes nor a no; it's a gauntlet.

An old radio in the corner clicks; static and then a low municipal announcement you can barely make out. You lean in, catching a few words: "council... vote... floodwall..." Instantly the greenhouse seems to tighten, the humidity like a held breath.

Old Tom's shadow falls across the doorway before you see him. He walks with that slow, patient gait of someone who has watched tides swallow and return things. His cap is damp at the rim. Up close, his face reads like a chart of weather: creased, mapped, precise.

Old Tom: "You two, keep it down so you don't scare the seedlings. And Marina — have a moment?"

Marina Reyes: "Of course, Tom." Your voice mirrors the care you feel for him.

He comes in, eyes sliding over the rows of trays and landing on the long-line sketches. He inhales deeply — there is the scent of authorial salt that comes from being outside for long enough that the sea reads you.

Old Tom: "They've got that vote set for next week. Floodwall's got momentum. Council's thinking that's the only way to keep the school and the docks upfront. Folks are talkin' about buyouts, too."

You feel the greenhouse tilt, not physically but in the sense of shifting priorities. The world outside is making a decision that could carve the map of the town for years.

Marina Reyes: "Is this new? Or is it just louder?"

Old Tom lets out a low, dry laugh that sounds like stones rubbing.

Old Tom: "It's louder. The storm last winter reminded folks who holds the memory of loss. But you were a child when the tide took your father's rowboat, weren't you, Marina? You remember the sound?"

You don't have to answer to know he remembers. His look is not pity; it's a recognition of shared weather-worn weight.

Old Tom: "If you can show them a way to make the shore keep them — not just hold it with concrete — they'll listen. But you'll have to tie the language of the living shore to the language of the council. Find the bridge."

His advice is straightforward and patient, like someone naming tide lines: practical, necessary, gentle. He pats your shoulder with fingers that smell of rope and sea oil before stepping back out into the early evening.

You watch his silhouette recede. The greenhouse hums on. Inside, the models and the barge sketches sit side by side like two different maps of the same country.

You stay, arranging trays for the first test runs. Your hands move on habit — tying small knots, labeling tubes — while your mind arranges political timelines and neighborly conversations. There's a rhythm to preparation that both calms and sharpens you. It is in this quiet that Elias Kato arrives.

He steps in without flourish. The trench coat dampens at the collar; his amber eyes find you immediately, steady and contained as ever. He moves as if he's measured his step twice over — precise, intentional.

Elias Kato: "You wanted the seeds. I brought copies of the council briefing packet too, in case you wanted to see the floodwall models."

Marina Reyes: "Thanks." Your voice is small against the hum. You feel the old urge to close — to fold around the cooperative and keep others out while you build it — but something steadier pushes you toward the packet.

Elias Kato sets the papers on the bench with care, then leans against the counter. He watches you as if he's cataloguing the room and the person in equal measure.

Elias Kato: "They're meeting Wednesday evening. Briefing's open. I thought you might want to hear the presentation before the vote — to ask questions, to offer alternatives if you have them."

There's a guarded softness in the offer. He doesn't say 'join me' or 'support me'; he offers presence, a procedural foothold. The old friction between you — where idealism met municipal pragmatism — is there folded into his phrasing.

You search his face for unreadable things and find instead the same steadiness you remember from before. He is practiced at containing emotion in a clear envelope: his warmth is practical, like a hand on a steering wheel.

Marina Reyes: "I haven't decided how to approach them yet. I don't want the cooperative swallowed by someone else's timetable."

Elias Kato: "I don't want that either." He pauses, and whatever edge his words might have had softens. "Listen to the presentation. Ask the questions you need to ask. If there's a way to integrate living infrastructure into the plan, I can push for it in the meeting."

There it is: the opening door. Not a promise, not an agreement, but a willingness to include you in the process. It feels like a thread tossed across a small gulf.

You look down at the briefing packet and then to the trays at your hands. The tug you feel is not just between methods; it's between two ways of being in the world: one that moves with neighbors, knuckles raw and hands wet, and one that moves through meetings and deliverables, trying to keep as many people as possible from being erased.

Marina Reyes: "I'll listen."

The words are simple. You say them more than once in your head—listen, rather than protest. The promise is small, private; it settles like a pebble in a pond, making concentric questions ripple outward.

Elias Kato watches you, and for a moment he lets something unguarded through — not a confession, but a weight shared.

Elias Kato: "That's all I'm asking. Come with questions. Come with your people. If we can show the council how this could be part of a mixed approach, maybe we avoid the worst outcomes."

You consider pushing for more — a guarantee, a joint statement, a shared plan. But you don't. The cooperative still feels too fragile for grand gestures. Instead, you tuck that possibility into the pocket where you keep your buoy pendant: close, quiet, warming.
