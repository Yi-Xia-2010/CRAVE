He steadies you as a colleague steadies a colleague in a crisis. The gesture is practical, effective. Elias Kato: "We can negotiate the terms. We can make safeguards."

Marina Reyes: "We can also lose people's trust if they think we're setting them up to be counted."

The night tilts. Municipal Hall's promise of conditional funding becomes a lever that pries at loyalties. In return for the pilots, the town will get money, but it will be distributed in stages and strung to the metrics that make an engineer sleep. Where you had wanted kinship anchored in shared labor and mutual aid, this plan places a clock and a scoreboard.

Your cooperative survives the immediate political moment. That should feel like victory. Instead, it tastes like ash. The funding is granted — not a gush, but a measured release contingent on compliance. Rina shakes your hand for the cameras; her grip is efficient and not unkind. The pilot funding will mean equipment, training, and a roof over part of your seedbank. It will also mean codified metrics that can be weaponized by future administrations.

You listen and your heart lurches with the two truths you must hold: that his competence can deliver resources, and that competence is not the same as justice. You remember the warmth you once shared and realize now that the institutional scaffolding has crept into the space between you. Your partnership is being sanitized into a governance model, your tenderness reformatted as professional alignment.

A car door slams. The lights of the hall blink. You want to ask him if any of this will ever feel like love again. Instead, you say nothing. Your silence is loud in the car. It is not rupture yet, just bureaucratized intimacy — a steady, efficient closeness that can sign contracts but cannot always take apart the calculus of grief.
