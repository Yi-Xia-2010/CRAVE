# Route: Additional / Ending


[Scene: Glasshouse Lab & Seedbank | Dawn — Storm-scarred, urgent light]
[Visual: Broken condensation beads on the translucent panels. Shelves sag with damp; a single lamp burns over a boxed stack of labeled spores. A tarp flutters like a wounded flag.]
[Sound: Distant generator coughs; gulls raucous; the town's bell, irregular, stutters in the background.]
[Music: Jagged strings, insistent percussion — a pulse that won't let you settle.]

You wake to a thin, metallic taste in your mouth and a library of small disasters arranged in the half-light: a torn crate, a shard of glass cutting a sheet of paper into a map of small losses, a pump with a hose held on by a strip of duct tape. Your hands are already moving before your thoughts assemble — elbows deep in a crate to test the seals, thumb tracing labels you know by heart.

Mika sleeps in a chair with boots still on, face streaked with salt and dirt; Amalia is already ticking off inventories on a clipboard, lips pressed so hard the paper trembles. Kai stands at the hydroponics bay with her braid thrown forward like a defiant flag, eyes scanning damage and people at once. Old Tom leans on a cane by the door, his mouth a thin line. Elias is there too, coat rimed with grit, watching, measuring you with the same steady amber gaze that's comfort and calculus at once.

You think of what the town looked like from the flats last night — roofs stripped clean like peeled fruit, one pilings line gone, a family van half-submerged in mud. You don't know exactly which houses stood and which sank; you do know the sound of a world changing its mind on you. The list in your satchel is heavier than it should be.

Elias steps forward, his hands empty but steady. "We did what we could," he says, and his voice has the bluntness of someone used to plans failing but not to seeing the bodies of those failures.

You want to say: don't frame it as 'we' unless you mean it. You want to say: you always meant to make this tidy with lines on a map. Instead you hold a sealed packet of spores and let the word you most need — apology, blame, consolation — thrash somewhere behind your teeth.

Kai's laugh is half-bark. "Tidy? That's a generous grammar for yesterday. You think plans save people. People save people." She doesn't say it to Elias only; she says it into the room.

Old Tom's voice cuts through. "Save your arguments for when the pumps run. Right now, we patch, we pull, we count breath." His hands shake when he points to a waterlogged field notebook.

The lab smells of wet burlap and ozone and a fruit-sour of spoiled cultures. Your buoy pendant rubs against your palm like it knows the weight now.

Amalia looks at you. Her eyes are open and furious and tired. "Marina," she says, but it's both an accusation and a plea. "We couldn't get to the eastern line. People—"

"We'll rebuild," you hear yourself say, and the sentence feels brittle. You want to mean it with the whole weight of the cooperative you'd imagined into being. You have seeds; you have plans; you have stubborn, uncompromising hands. But the tremor in your own voice reveals a fracture you hadn't allowed yourself to catalogue.

[Scene: Tidal Flats | Midday — Mud and grief]
[Visual: The flats stretch like an open wound. Shorn pilings, overturned boats, a scattering of household objects half-buried in silt. Rows where seaweed pens once breathed are shredded into thin, useless ropes.]
[Sound: Cries and shouted names; the slap of boots in thick mud; the distant rumble of the municipal loader. Wind gnaws at tarps.]
[Music: Low brass and frenzied percussion; a relentless, hurried tempo amplifies the chaos.]

You walk the flats with Old Tom and Mika at your shoulder, each step a soft explosion in the mud. People you know — neighbors, kids who used to sell shellfish at the market — sift through the wreck like ghosts inhaling their own pasts. Someone calls a name and the shout bounces back, empty and terrible.

Elias is there too, clipboard replaced by hands and a shovel. He doesn't make speeches. He hands you a length of braided cord and nods, as if handing over wood were the same as handing over apology. His presence is a complicated pressure: comfort and accusation braided together.

"How many homes?" you ask, and the number lands like a stone in a cold pool — less precise than the models, but more alive. You can't be sure whether the families of the lost houses will be offered municipal relocation, or whether they'll be left to cobble lives from other people's leftovers. You think of the Schrödinger list of choices the town has lived in; tonight the list is no longer theoretical.

Mika finds a child's toy, half-buried: a plastic whale, barnacled and sleeping in mud. She presses it into your hand. "We can salvage the lines if we get to the posts," she says, quick and practical, eyes raw. "But we need anchors. And people. And rope."

Kai stands apart, hands on hips, watching the bulldozer as if it were a leviathan swallowing a village. When you approach, she doesn't soften. "You think planting a line will change tide charts? It's not about charts. It's about trust. We lost trust last night." She spits the words like salt, and the taste in your mouth goes metallic.

The argument is not new; it's the same old arc in different timbres. You could point to the models, to grant applications, to evidence. Or you could point to the bruised faces and the boats and the salt-stiff clothes. Both feel true and impossible.

Old Tom grips your shoulder; the skin of his palm is papery and hot. "Plant something, girl. Even if it's for yourself," he tells you. "Do something that isn't just numbers."

You think of the long-line you once imagined — a cord of kelp and rope and people tied together — and realize the plan feels both fragile and obscene against this tableau. But there is a thing you do with your hands when you cannot answer with words: you tie knots.

You and Mika knot line after line, carving labor out of grief. Each loop is a tiny insistence that life continues. Around you, the town moves in a kind of furious choreography: people arguing with contractors, helping a neighbor wrench a door, carrying a mattress out of the mud. Elias moves between groups, offering a hand, looking like a man balancing a ledger between two flames.

[Scene: Cliffside Lighthouse & Storm Watch | Dusk — Electric, bruised sky]
[Visual: The cliff is a knife-edge, wind-torn grasses whipping at your knees. The lighthouse stands squat and stern behind you. Far below, the flats are a patchwork of dark water and mud; points of light — lanterns, flashlights — look like a scattering of frightened stars.]
[Sound: Wind slicing; a distant siren; the slap of waves hitting remnants of the shore. Occasional, muffled voices downstream.]
[Music: Full orchestra in a savage, soaring cry — piercing violins, drums driving like heartbeats.]

You climb the path alone at first, the rope of the long-line over your shoulder, the buoy pendant warm in your palm. The wind wants to steal sound; it strips the corners off sentences. Each step is a small act of defiance against the pull of the sea and whatever it has taken.

At the cliff's lip, you stop. Below, the cooperative's patched anchors look like stitches in a wound. You can feel the night pressing in from all directions: the sea's appetite, the town's anger and grief, the bureaucratic coldness that aligns itself with preserving infrastructure over memory. All those things press a finger into your sternum.

Elias emerges from the shadow of the lighthouse, breath white and ragged. He doesn't approach like someone crossing to make a speech. He approaches like someone who has come to stand on your side of the map and cannot be sure whether it's safe.

"Marina," he says. It's half-name, half-supplication. "We can—"

"Don't offer plans as consolation," you cut in. Your voice is a whip. "We have plans. We have seeds. We have hands. We lost houses."

He inhales, as if your words are oxygen and also salt. "I know. I can't fix all of it. But I can help keep what's here from going next."

"You speak of 'keeping' as if people are furniture." Your laugh is a dry, short sound. "Some left. Some are gone. Some won't forgive."

He closes his eyes for the barest second. When he opens them again, they are steady and, for a moment, unreadable. "Maybe they won't," he admits. "Maybe I don't deserve their trust. But I keep showing up."

The wind takes the rest of his sentence and throws it over the cliff like a discarded kite.

Kai appears then, sudden as a flare, face flushed with exertion and something harder — grief turned molten. For a beat you see every version of her: the organizer, the inventor, the friend and the adversary. She doesn't look at Elias. She looks at you and the long-line, and something like a shutter falls over her expression.

"This isn't enough," she says bluntly. "It won't be enough unless you make it be. And I don't know if I can stand next to anything you do that smells like compromise." Her hands ball into fists. "We bled for tides and you let the machines do the talking."

The words land like heavy pebbles. Behind them, Old Tom's footsteps on the path, Amalia's voice somewhere lower, Mika's breath like a bright, stubborn thing. The community is a chorus of broken harmonies around you.

You open your mouth. The language that comes isn't for closure. It's a small, clean thing: "I can't bring back roofs. I can't put back a father's hands. But I can plant a line. Right here. Right now." Your fingers twist the rope until it sings.

Elias moves closer. "Let me—" he starts, and then stops. His hands hover near the long-line, wanting to help, to plan anchors, to make everything less raw with procedural things.

You look at him. The buoy pendant knocks against your palm like a small, steady bell.

There is a moment — an electric, thinned-out second — where every possible outcome sits like a storm cloud. You could take his hand and let planning and partnership become a slow warmth. You could turn away and do it alone, save yourself from compromises that cost lives. You could let Kai's rage become a wedge that drives her away for good.

But decisions are sharp, and the cliff wind feels like a blade.

You plant the first stake with trembling hands, drive it home, lash the line, and let the rope slip over the edge toward the flats, a rough, live artery connecting cliff to shore. The line is not enough. It is everything.

Elias helps. Kai does not step forward; she stands a few paces back, watching the knot you tie like someone watching a wound close. Old Tom hums an old working song under his breath; Mika cries once, quick and fierce, then sets her jaw and keeps working.

The tide steals and returns in its slow, indifferent gesture. The town below moves like an animal stitched back together while bleeding. Some lights are already dark. You count the losses like beads on a string, each one a weight, each one a story you now keep as a map burned into your body.

You drive the final knot through salt and wind and call it a promise — not a guarantee, and not a declaration of triumph. A promise is less arrogant than the rest. It is honest, small, and stubborn.

Elias brushes mud from your sleeve with a thumb that trembles. "We will try," he says, and the words are both an offering and a confession.

You look at Kai. Her face is unreadable, the lines around her mouth hard as a shut door. She turns and walks away without a goodbye. You do not chase her. For some things, acknowledge the fracture is the only kindness left.

Old Tom pitches his voice across the night. "We plant, we remember, we register what we can. Then we go back and do the work." He laughs — a short, sharp sound of a man who has crossed fewer days than he deserves.

You hold the buoy pendant in your palm, the same small weight that has steadied you all these years. You breathe in the salt and the smoke of someone burning the last of their furniture, and you let the rope pull taut under your hands.

The sky cracks open with a distant, furious thunder, a reminder that the weather will test every stitch and plan you make. You are tired beyond measure. Your chest is a hollow drum of grief and stubbornness.

You pull the line, secure the buoy to the rope, and throw it out to the flats. The buoy bobs like a heartbeat. Below, the lanterns of the town blink, one by one, in a slow, grieving cadence.

You plant another stake. Then another. Your hands blister; you don't feel it. You count anchors as if counting breaths.

Elias does not ask you to stop. Kai does not return. Old Tom sings. Mika works like a machine of mercy. Amalia organizes the survivors into crews with a voice that refuses to break.

The cooperative is mangled and waterlogged, but there are seeds intact. The town is wounded, and the map of it is redrawn in losses and in the new lines you lash. Your relationship with Elias is not healed — it is a complicated ledger of small mercies and unspoken liabilities. Some of your connections are broken, some are braided into a fragile, pragmatic alliance. The grief is a physical thing you carry beside you now, and you do not pretend it isn't massive.
