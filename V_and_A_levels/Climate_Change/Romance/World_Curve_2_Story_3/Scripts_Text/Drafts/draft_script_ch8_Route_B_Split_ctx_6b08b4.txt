### DRAFT
# Route: Additional / Ending


[Scene: Municipal Hall Lobby | Late Afternoon — Storm Raking the Windows]
[Visual: Fluorescent lights buzz; projection glow from the conference room bleeds through the glass doors.]
[Sound: Heavy rain patters against the concrete façade; distant rumble of construction equipment; a phone vibration, sharp in the hush.]
[Music: Tense, clipped strings — heartbeat tempo; low brass undercurrent.]

You are still seated when your phone vibrates in your pocket. The projection behind the glass is a pale rectangle of future-maps and colored risk bands; rain streaks the window like a smear of urgency.

Your chest is a drum you cannot silence. You can feel every choice you've ever deferred collecting at the base of your throat: the long-line, the Glasshouse trays, Mika's texts, Old Tom's weathered maps. The projection in the meeting is supposed to be a neutral thing — numbers, not people — and yet it hangs between you like a verdict.

Rina's voice carries from the conference room: "We need a motion. We need to decide now." It sounds small and brittle under the projection light.

You slide the phone free. The message preview is a line from the community list: Construction crews mobilizing at dawn. Volunteers needed. Another notification: an anonymous upload — a copy of private flood projections, stamped internal and unreleased.

Your fingers go cold. The document’s margins are full of red flags; the modeled inundation is worse than the public maps — worse for certain neighborhoods you thought were safe. The leak could force the council into emergency action, fast and uncompromising. Or it could ruin you for breaking confidence. Or both.

Rina approaches, shoulders squared like a dam against the crowd. Her brow is a map of sleepless nights. "Marina," she says, not unkind — only efficacious — "if that's the data, you know what this means. We can't ignore it."

Elias lingers by the doorway, wet coat clinging at the shoulders, eyes fixed on the projection with the same quiet focus that has brought him close to you and then kept him measuredly distant. He does not reach for the phone. He is waiting for you to speak.

You stand because standing is an action people can see. Speech is harder. You keep your hands in your pockets like an anchor.

You: "How confident are we that the leak is genuine?" Your voice is steadier than you feel.

Rina: (a quick, bitter laugh) "It's a printout from the modeling team. Internal. Someone didn't mean for it to go public. Whoever took it — you can't assume their motives are pure."  
Elias: "But if it's real, the council's public maps are underrepresented. It compresses the timeline on retreat decisions."  
Rina: "Compresses the timeline and exposes us to panic, litigation, and property claims. It could collapse trust overnight."

The words pile up. Trust. Panic. Litigation. Each one a shard, ready to cut.

Old Tom's cane scrapes across the tile as he arrives, shoulders hunched beneath a dripping coat. He smells of salt and smoke and the kind of memory you can't Google. He leans on the banister and looks at you as if he can see the map of your insides.

Old Tom: "Back when the seawall failed in '86, we didn't have models. We had a man with a radio and folks who listened. This is different. Maybe better. Maybe worse." His voice is a rasp that carries more authority than the town's bylaws.

Elias moves closer until he is near enough that the warm line of his arm brushes yours. It should calm you. It doesn't. Not now. Not with the leak humming like a live wire between options.

Elias: (quiet) "If you recommend release, it will be you who pushes the town into a scramble. If you keep it, you will be sitting on leverage — maybe protecting people quietly, maybe making decisions that never see light. Either way, someone loses something."

You imagine both losses. You imagine a line you cannot untie.

[Scene: Glasshouse Lab | Early Evening — Green Light Through Curved Panels]
[Visual: Humid air, trays humming, the faint phosphorescence of algae under grow-lights; the lab is a sanctuary, but tonight the warmth from the tanks feels small compared to the cold outside.]
[Sound: Pumps whir; a kettle somewhere hisses; the rain thumps harder, as if impatience takes physical form.]
[Music: Low, pulsing synthesizer; a discordant piano motif snags on a single note.]

You and Elias move away from the municipal hall together. The walk is a corridor of wet umbrella silhouettes and flashing turn signals. In the Glasshouse, the green light softens your edges, but it does nothing to slow the fever building behind your ribs.

Kai is already there, sleeves rolled, fingers stained with nutrient solution. She gestures for the files and takes the leaked projections like a dare.

Kai: (sharp, immediate) "We show the people. Data or not, we do a livestream, a demo on the flats at dawn. Visuals beat numbers — people need to see a proof of life, not another chart."  
You: (internal) Proof of life. Proof that kelp and long-lines can hold when concrete fails. Kai's voice is adrenaline — and you are rapidly running out of oxygen.

Elias: "A controlled demonstration is not the same as a full-scale solution," he says, voice tight. "If the demonstration fails, politically, that's it. Kelp could look like a placebo."

Kai snaps the file shut with a bang. "If we don't show them something that moves them, they'll sign the retreat as the simplest line on a map." Her eyes are near feral now — all motion and consequence.

The lab feels too small for the weight in your hands. Mika passes by with a drone controller, eyes wide, offering a taut grin that reads as prayer. "We can map the channels by morning," he says. "We can make footage. The town will watch." He does not know about the leak's provenance. He does not know how many lines will be crossed tonight.

Rina's call comes through, clipped and managerial. "The council schedules an emergency session at midnight. A vote could be staged at dawn. We need a recommendation." The words slam into you, time snapping inward.

Elias pins his thumbs together, an old planner's habit. He looks at you as if at a hinge.

Elias: "Marina, I want to protect as many as we can. If the projections are accurate and we release them, the council might be forced to push immediate retreat orders. That's abandonment for some. If we keep them, we can focus relocation resources on the most vulnerable. But I know how that looks — secret deals, insider bargains. You understand both worlds."

You can taste metal at the back of your tongue. The lab smells of sea-sap and warm algae and something like betrayal.

Old Tom's text pings on the lab whiteboard: "Come to the flats. People are anxious. Some say we'll be torched if we don't act." He has that thing he does where he speaks in the language of story rather than charts; it lands harder than any model.

Kai: "Either way, if we do nothing, people will be forced by the sea. If we release the projection, people panic and some will be left behind in the scramble. If we hide it, the families in the red zones could be quietly moved and compensated — but what about the rest? The rest will have their hope tricked."

Her voice has an edge now, and you can feel the lab's temperature tighten to match it.

[Scene: Town Streets & Laurel Bay Flats | Night — Lanterns Wavering]
[Visual: Lanterns along the market square bob in the wind; puddles mirror the frayed neon of a clinic sign; a few neighbors huddle under canopies, faces lit by phone screens.]
[Sound: Shouts in the distance; the steady slap of rain; the creak of a tidal piling somewhere down the lane.]
[Music: Tempo increases — strings rise; percussion like anxious footsteps.]

You step back into the town with the file burning a small, private heat in your satchel. People cross your path; a child in a yellow raincoat tugs at a parent's hand without seeing the map that might take their home. The thought drops into your stomach and lodges there.

Rina corners you by the market stall, turning the question into a scalpel. "If the projections go public and the council acts — fast — we could save infrastructure. Schools, clinics, the power nodes. But we can't save every house. The optics are brutal. If you support release, you're asking some to leave now to save many later."

You remember your father's hands tying knots; you remember his laugh when the tide came in faster than expected. You remember promising to keep this place intact. The word "compromise" tastes like a half-remembered apology.

Elias finds your side again. "You have influence," he says, not pleading but offering. "You could mean the difference between a chaotic exodus and a managed transition."

You think of Old Tom's stories of the '86 wall shuddering and falling slowly like an animal in its sleep. You think of Kai's urgency, of Mika's eager hands on the drone controller, of Rina's ledger of obligations. The room of your mind narrows to three doors.

Your heart accelerates. Sounds around you blur into a single, urgent drum: the meeting at midnight, the construction at dawn, the leak's hungry glow.

You realize then — with a clarity that feels like a blade — that whichever door you open will cut someone. You will be the one whose voice tips the balance. The rain increases to a clap as if the sky itself demands an answer.

Elias reaches for your hand as if to steady you, but he stops himself and lets it hover there, ambivalent heat and space. His face is unreadable in that pause: complex and watchful, as if he wants to be the person who stands with you no matter what, but is terrified by what standing might mean.

Elias: (softly) "I'm going to stand behind whatever you recommend. But I need to know — what do you want us to be? The people who shove the town into panic to force action? The people who quietly triage? The people who try to make the world see us by showing them what's possible?"

You could choose all three and break. You could choose none and fracture slowly.

[Music: Builds to a sharp, staccato crescendo; all instruments at once — a high-arousal peak.]

You glance at the projections again, feeling the pull of each path like tides tugging at different edges of your life. Your mouth is dry. The sky outside opens with a thunder that makes the town hold its breath.

You realize this is not just a policy choice. It is moral arbitration on a scale that will leave scars.

You steady your shoulders. The moment for talk has contracted to a single, volcanic point.
