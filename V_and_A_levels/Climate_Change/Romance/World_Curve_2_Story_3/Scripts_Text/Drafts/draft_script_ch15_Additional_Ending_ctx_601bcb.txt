### DRAFT
# Route: Additional / Ending


[Scene: Cliffside Lighthouse & Storm Watch | Dawn — The morning after]
[Visual: The cliff path is littered with sea-spray and ash; the lighthouse stands squat and salt-streaked against a bruised sky]
[Music: Dissonant strings — high, urgent; a heavy percussion undercurrent]
[Sound: Broken gulls, distant hammers, and the low thrum of generators. A wind that feels like a hand pushing at your ribs.]

You stand at the lip of the cliff with the buoy pendant cupped in your palm. The metal is warmer than it should be, rubbed smooth by habit — a small, steady relic in a morning that has none.

Below, the town is a cut-paper collage of what’s left and what’s gone: roofs like missing teeth, a crooked line of pilings that used to hold nets, the tidal flats a black glass strewn with flotsam. Smoke hangs in the channels like a rumor. Voices carry up from the shore — sharp, urgent, raw — and every one of them presses against you as if the sound itself could alter what happened.

Your breath comes ragged. You can feel the night’s adrenaline still inside you, electric and guilty. The memory of people shouting your name at the pile-driver, of bodies forming a braided rope against steel, of the hydraulic teeth that chewed at the edge of what the town once called safe — it’s not an image you can smooth into a lesson. It is a wound.

You had hoped bargaining would buy time. You had hoped a preserved seedbank might be a talisman against loss. You had hoped bodies on the line would be enough. The town answered all those hopes with ruin and small survivals.

Far below, a long-line you started yesterday lies partly tangled, half-buried in an unexpected eddy. Its rope is salt-stiff, the kelp thinned and frayed at the surface. It is both emblem and accusation.

An unfamiliar, brittle sound breaks out — a child laughing, then sobbing. The cliff seems to tilt.

[Visual: Cut to close-up on your hands — the buoy pendant’s frayed cord; your thumb traces the indented groove polished by your father’s fingers]
[Sound: The pendant clinks once against itself, a tiny, impossible bell amid the chaos]

You close your fingers around it until it hurts. There is no map in the pendant, no engineer’s guarantee. There is only the memory of a hand steady as a tiller, and the knowledge that steady hands are needed now — whether or not that steadiness will be enough.

You hear a voice below — Elias — and you cannot tell if it is an offer or a reprimand. The wind steals syllables. His figure is a vertical line in the ruins, hesitant, pocketed in a trench coat that looks like it belongs to someone who still expects plans to fit the world.

Elias Kato: "Marina. I—"

You: "Don't make me have to choose a name for this, Elias. Names don't fix tide lines."

Elias Kato: (He steps closer, boots scuffing the wet gravel) "No. They don't. But they help people remember why they stood. Help me remember why—" (He stops, a private thread cut short.) "Why we tried."

His hands find the strap of his wrist-map like a talisman. He is practical even in grief. For a beat, you watch him gather the kind of composure that used to make you feel safe and instead now makes you furious for the compromises it cost.

Elias Kato: "I didn't think we'd lose the flats like this. I didn't—" (He exhales, voice cracking) "I didn't know the storm would take that line in the channel. I thought—"

You: "You thought you could protect everyone."

Elias Kato: (No accusation; just fact) "I thought I could make covers where there were holes. I thought we could buy time. I still do. But time—" (He looks at the town) "—isn't neutral. It chooses."

You both stand in the wind like two objects that once fit together. The town's breaths rise as a chorus beneath you: anger and grief braided and howling in equal parts.

[Scene: Glasshouse Lab & Seedbank | Midday — After the initial chaos]
[Visual: The reclaimed warehouse is streaked with soot at the edges; inside, humid light picks out draped tarps and rake-worn benches]
[Music: Sparse piano, then a rising scrim of violins; tempo accelerating]
[Sound: The hum of pumps — irregular, sometimes stuttering — and the hurried voices of volunteers trying to catalog, salvage, count]

You move through the Glasshouse like a ghost in a place you once considered a citadel. The trays are a testimony: some trays are upended, a few are intact, many ambered with salt. Mika is there, hands black with pump oil, eyes ringed with exhaustion but moving with a precise, youthful fury.

Mika: "We lost half of the grow-beds in the back, but the sensors picked up pockets that survived. We can triage. We can—" (He meets your face) "—we can rebuild the pumps if we get a new controller board. There's a list."

You: "Read it to me."

Mika: (He hands you a soaked clipboard; his voice is too steady) "Priority one: seeds we can save. Priority two: repair pumps. Priority three: re-tie the long-lines before the next high tide. We'll need volunteers, and—" (He swallows) "—we'll need time, and we don't have as much as we thought."

You breathe the humid, algae-sweet air and taste copper. The glass feels smaller. It is both a sanctuary and a witness.

Old Tom is there, shoulders bent but still making the rounds, touching jars and labels the way a priest blesses relics. He looks at you with an expression that is not exactly pity. It is older than pity; it is a ledger.

Thomas "Old Tom" Bellamy: "We had houses here when I was a boy that sat where this greenhouse does now. We thought we were letting go then. We thought we knew the sea. The sea knows more ways to change than we know to fix gates."

You: "What do we do with that knowledge, Tom? How do we carry it without it becoming an excuse?"

Old Tom: (He puffs a breath like a small storm) "You carry it open-eyed. You tell the people what it looks like to lose a harbor. You don't let the stories get smoothed into speeches. You make small, sensible things that keep mouths fed and hearts near."

Kai's presence — when you see her — is a sharp, discordant note. She moves like someone who could not be made comfortable with compromise. Her smile has a hard edge; her jaw tightens when she looks at the municipal paperwork stacked under Rina Corbett's neat file folders. Whether she was at the line or not last night doesn't untangle the way you read her: raw, disappointed, furious in the masculine and feminine shapes of the word.

Kai: "I tried to stop the machines before they got to here. I tried to get folks to see what would happen when the tide cut loose. Some listened, some didn't." (Her hands are stained with portable panel sealant; they shake a little.) "We tied what we could. But the wall—" (She spits the word out) "—the wall turned into a promise that eats people who can't move."

You: "We did what we could."

Kai: (She laughs then, a short hard thing) "We did what you agreed to. That's not always the same thing. But complaining changes the weather less than doing something does."

The old argument surfaces, the one that dates back before you and Elias and Kai: the trade between policy and protest, between broad protection and rootedness. The lab hums with it. The pumps flicker like hesitant hearts.

Elias Kato: (He speaks to the group more than to you) "We pushed for triage routes, temporary levees, prioritized rescues. We got the seals to move faster. We couldn't convince the region to fund emergency relocations. We did what the agency allowed." (His voice is even, but the words have lost their soft edges.) "I own mistakes in the modeling. I own decisions that put resources where I thought they'd do the most good."

You: "Do you own what it did to Kai's people? To the families who had to leave with a single bag?"

Elias Kato: (He flinches, then answers) "I own that they had to make impossible choices. I own that statistics look like numbers until you put a face to them. I—" (He searches and fails for words that don't sound like a defense) "—I want to help fix it. But fixing takes time and materials. It takes the cooperation of people who are tired of compromise."

There is a silence that is almost physical. Everyone is searching for the grammar of apology and the verb of repair. None of the fit.

[Scene: Tidal Flats | Late Afternoon — The planting]
[Visual: Low sun cuts through clouds in a thin, violent blade; the flats are a black mirror with scattered wreckage]
[Music: Percussion rises into a pounding cadence; a violin line soars and then fractures]
[Sound: Shouts of coordination, the slap of wet rope, a generator coughing into life; the sea makes a low, impatient exhale]

You are on the flats again, waist-deep in cold tracking water, the rope slack in your hands as if it might snap the moment you look away. The long-line you plant now is not the untested arrangement of bright hope you imagined months ago. It is knotted from spare rope, from old nets, from the stubbornness of people who refuse to let a place vanish quietly.

Mika works beside you, teeth clenched, fingers raw. Old Tom holds the other end, leaning into the work with a steadier rhythm than his body should permit. Amalia moves up and down the line, coordinating what volunteers remain, her clipboard smeared but functional.

Amalia Reyes: "We don't have the buoy chains like we wanted. We have patched drums, but they'll ride. We have enough to hold the line through this tide if we weight it right."

You: "Then we weight it right."

Your hands move in the knot you learned from your father. Muscle and memory conspire: a loop, a tension, a tuck. For a second — a sharp, impossible second — you are not thinking policy or loss or who left; you are thinking only about the shape of rope passing over rope and the certainty of a loop that will hold if tied well.

Elias Kato is not beside you; he is half a bay over, coordinating municipal salvage, speaking into his wrist-map with a steady voice. He turns and looks at you across the flats, his silhouette a small flame of intention and exhaustion. His eyes find yours and carry the weight of unsaid things: apologies that aren't finished, plans that still depend on numbers, a love that lives now in the margins of politics.

He calls your name — it sounds like a plea and a permission both.

Elias Kato: "Marina. We can bring in temp barriers. I can get the pumps running—"

You: (You cut him off because there are no more clean continuations left) "We are tying this now. This line is for the people who sleep in a house that still has a porch. It is for those who won't leave. It is for the ones who do. It's for the ones who mistrust walls and the ones who need them. This is for them."

Elias Kato: (He nods slowly; the motion is like a small surrender) "Then we tie."

The storm doesn't return, but the sea answers like a patient animal: it swells and tests, it pulls and murmurs. The line goes out, heavy with makeshift floats, and the community's collective breath becomes a metronome for the labor. You loop, you cinch, you heave. The rope bites into your palms; your skin is a constellation of new abrasions.

Kai watches from a distance, arms folded, jaw set. She does not come forward. Whether she is there to judge or to give space is a question that hangs unresolved between the salt and the fog. Everything important in Laurel Bay is now a question.

At the far end, Old Tom ties the terminal knot with hands that have known thousands of tides. When he finishes he places his thumb on the rope and looks at you the way elders do — as if passing along a ledger that must be paid in years and memory.

Thomas "Old Tom" Bellamy: "Whatever this does, remember that we tried. Remember that trying is a kind of truth."

You: "I remember."

You drive the final stake into the sand with a hammer someone found in a wrecked shed. The sound of metal on metal is bright and finite. The line settles; the tide takes a test and returns. The kelp shivers in the water and the floats ride.

For an instant the world seems to inhale — a fragile pause where the line exists between the disaster and the work of making whole.

Then the sirens start again, a long, sorrowful keening. Someone on the edge of town cries your name like an accusation and a blessing. The volunteers start to disperse toward houses that still stand but have lost roofs, toward people needing blankets and food, toward the slow, painful triage of lives and livelihoods.

Elias crosses the flats toward you, water slapping his boots. His face is wet, not all from the sea. He meets you at the rope, and for a moment you are both so tired that nothing sharp can be said.

Elias Kato: "I can't fix all of it."

You: "No one can."

Elias Kato: (He looks at the line, at your hands) "But I will help keep what's left. I will help rebuild where we can. If you'll let me."

You feel the old longing rise like a tide in your chest — soft, dangerous. There is forgiveness there, curled tight and wary. There is also a list of losses that will never be paid in apologies or plans.

You: "I don't know what letting you in looks like anymore. I know what letting the town down looks like, and I can't have that again."

Elias Kato: "Then we'll do it in ways that don't ask you to trade your roots. We'll do it together. I—" (He hesitates, and the hesitation is a small, human cliff) "—I don't want to be the person who makes you choose between your work and what's left of you."

You: "Promises are cheap now."

Elias Kato: (He smiles, a small ragged thing) "Then I'll do the work to make them not cheap. If you'll let me show you."

You look at his hands. They still know how to hold things steady. They have also done harm, even if unintentional. You think of Kai watching from a distance, of the families who left with one bag, of the greenhouse half-salvaged and half-ruined. The ledger inside you balances in uneven columns.

You: "We will do the work. We'll keep the cooperative going, even if it looks different. We'll mend where we can. But I won't let this become a story you get to tell without the people who lived it."

Elias Kato: "I don't want to tell it without them."

You let him mean that, and you let him mean it imperfectly. There will be meetings, compromises, people who feel betrayed and people who feel saved. The town will bear the marks. Your relationship with Elias will be honest but taxed; sometimes he will be near, sometimes distant. Kai may not forgive you. Old Tom will keep reminding you of tides. Mika will keep fixing pumps with hands too young for so much work. Amalia will organize recovery markets and do it with a smile that gathers tears when no one is looking.

You close your fingers around your buoy pendant and thread it back over your head. The cord smells faintly of sea and smoke.

You look out at the long-line you planted. It is small against the horizon, a thin attempt at architecture in a world that reshapes itself without asking. It is both defiance and confession.

You: (softly, to the wind) "We will keep tending it."

Elias Kato: (He leans in, voice low) "I'll be here."

You watch him walk back toward the town. His silhouette shrinks and grows with every step between the wreckage and the makeshift shelters. Kai does not approach. She turns away, shoulders set toward a path that might not run the same as yours.

A child runs past, clutching a patched stuffed fish. The child trips and scrambles, then laughs. The sound hits you like a small, impossible bell.

Everything is broken and so many things are not. The town smells of salt and smoke and something like bravery. The cooperative is a scarred promise and the seedbank sits somewhere between survival and ruin; whether it's whole or a handful of possibilities you cannot name exactly, but you carry its purpose like a stitch in your ribs.

You will stay. You will push and tie and argue and cry. You will try to make a living sea where the shore is still willing to be lived on. The ending is not tidy. It is a ledger of losses and the stubborn accounting of people who will not let the place go without carving memory into action.

[Visual: Wide pullback — the long-line gleams faintly as the sun cracks the clouds; volunteers move like small, devoted currents across the flats]
[Music: Single, sustained cello note that trembles and then relaxes into a quieter, resolute chord]
[Sound: The sea, steady; distant hammers; someone singing softly as they mend a net]

You step away from the rope and toward the town, buoy warm against your chest. The cliff behind you holds the outline of all that is lost and all that must be rebuilt. You cannot undo what happened. You can only keep returning to the work of keeping a place alive.
