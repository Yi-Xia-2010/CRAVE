The air is warm and sifted with iodine; the smell of wet rope and compost bites the back of your throat. Every instrument is a small promise; every cable, a line to a future you are trying to braid together.

Mika is crouched by a laptop, shoulders tight with concentration. She doesn't look up when you come in; the screen throws maps across her face in blue-green shadows.

Mika [her voice is small but steady]: "Syncing the tide gauges now. Live feed's stable. Drone battery's at eighty-six percent and the stream's clear if you want to show the kelp pens."

You swallow. You can picture the festival lights already — the bulbs, the stalls, the hush that falls when a crowd leans in. You can picture the municipal hall's committee table, Rina's even hands folded above a stack of papers. You can imagine Elias standing there, the careful tilt of his head when he weighs a number.

You try to name how you arrived here — whether you marched in with the packet in your satchel, or you slipped from a closed meeting, the air between you and Rina still sticky with compromises. The past choices blur; what matters is the present weight of the decision: open instruments, open truth, open consequences. The moral geometry is sharp enough to cut.

Internal Voice: "This will change things. People will understand the physics of floodlines, or they'll read panic into it and run. Either way someone will lose something tonight."

You meet her eyes and the line between intention and outcome frays. You are certain of the ethics but not of the effect. Your cooperative is a fragile thing; transparency could invite support — or it could precipitate the exodus that would hollow the town.

[Scene: Reclaimed Wetlands Festival / Night Market | Evening — Wind Picking Up]
[Visual: Bulbs swinging above packed stalls; projections smear as rain begins]
[Music: Staccato strings clashing with the murmur of a crowd]
[Sound: A single, loud ripple of rain; the screen whines as pixels shift]

When you step into the festival, it feels like stepping into a held breath. Lanterns halo faces; salt and smoked fish and the faint copper of blood mingle in the air. Old Tom stands near the front, a silhouette in a battered coat, his beard like knotted rope. Amalia moves through the crowd with a clipboard and a conductor's patience, trying to smooth under the tension. Rina stands near the municipal tent, composure like a shield. And Elias — he is there, trench coat collar turned up, eyes narrowed against the wind, watching you with an expression you can't read.

Your heart is a fist. The public theater is unforgiving. You hook the satellite link on your shoulder and feel the weight of live things humming at your back.

You take the stage because someone has to. The crowd hushes as if the harbor itself is listening.

Marina Reyes: "We set up real-time instruments because guesses aren't enough anymore. You deserve the data — and the chance to respond."

A ripple moves through the crowd: some faces open with dawning comprehension; others close into fear. A child at the edge of the crowd wails. Somewhere, someone begins to murmur the word "leave" like a mantra.

The screen blossoms with graphs: tide projections, recent sensor spikes, drone footage tracking frayed seawalls and exposed footings. The numbers are sterile and monstrous at once.

Old Tom: "That's just a forecast. Forecasts have been wrong before."

Marina Reyes: "Forecasts are not fate. They are information. We can plant, buffer, and adapt — but we need decisive, honest support."

Elias Kato moves forward, the crowd sensing a bridge, or a wedge.

Elias Kato: "We can work with these numbers. We can phase in seawalls where they're needed and fund kelp pilots in others."

Marina Reyes: "And who decides which houses get seawalls and which get told to evacuate? Who decides which families are sacrificed for the grid to stay up?"

Elias Kato: "We decide together, Marina. We can't save everything — but we can save much more with a measured plan."

Marina Reyes: "Measured plan is code sometimes for leaving the people who can't move. Measured is a polite way of saying 'acceptable losses'."

Elias Kato: "No. Measured is a way to avoid losing everyone at once. Being honest about trade-offs saves—" (he stops because his certainty cracks under the pain of what that sentence implies)

His silence sits between two halves of the town like a low tide.

A woman near you, voice thin as tideweed: "If this is the truth, I'm not staying." Her shoulders start to shake; someone grabs her hand and whispers a plan.

Meanwhile, Kaori "Kai" Matsuda is shouting into a small handheld: "Show them the pens! Show them the kelp growth numbers!" She pushes forward, eyes blazing. The drone footage swings over the long-lines; young blades of kelp shimmer like dark flags in a current that could swallow them.

The crowd is no longer a unit. It is a hundred different small storms.

Rina: "We heard the data. The council will convene emergency sessions. We will allocate emergency funds and—" (she's interrupted by a chorus of shouts)

Crowd Member: "So we wait for meetings while the tide eats our foundations? How is that justice?"

Rina: "We cannot flip a politician's ledger overnight, but we—"

Crowd Member: "You can if you put the money where—"

The argument is a door slamming open to a hallway of old grievances. Old Tom catches your eye, and for a moment his face is a map of all the seasons he's seen — anger, grief, resignation layered like sediment.

Old Tom (voice breaking): "You show us these numbers like they're a verdict. But numbers don't comfort. They don't replace a ruined house. They don't bring back the boats."

You want to tell him that you know that; you want to tell him that your father would have known how to translate this into a net of care. But your words fall down into the wet between you and him.

Elias Kato squeezes your shoulder in a gesture that is quick and weirdly personal in the public chaos. You flinch and then feel the warmth of it anyway.

Elias Kato [soft, nearly to you]: "We have to corral this into something actionable. Let me talk to Rina. Let me—"

Marina Reyes: "No more quiet fixes. No more deciding behind closed doors."

He looks at you, the expression unreadable and complex. There's loyalty there, and a calculation. You know he's trying to hold both things at once — love and the imperative to salvage as much as possible — and that makes him seem both painfully near and horribly distant.

Mika's voice cuts across the din: "Live tide gauge — now!" Her finger hits a key. The screen refreshes with a new feed.

For a second, the world seems to condense. The live gauge has always been a line that climbs like a promise; tonight it climbs like an accusation.

The number ticks faster than it should.

Phones are out across the crowd, faces white light reflected in wet eyes. A woman drops to her knees near the stage, hands buried in her hair. Two men begin to argue and then start shoving. The lanterns swing like pendulums.

[Scene: Municipal Hall — After the Festival | Night — Sirens in the Distance]
[Visual: Pale concrete facades, a line of headlights; construction lights strobing]
[Music: A single dissonant chord stretched thin]
[Sound: A distant siren, shouting reduced to haloed fragments; a wall of rain]

You ride the momentum to municipal hall because decisions will be made there tonight, in the fluorescents and the hard angles where numbers are translated into directives. The hall smells of damp wool and coffee gone cold; a map of the town is spread across a table where hands hover like birds.

Rina is already negotiating with a line of council members. Her posture is precise aggression — a machine calibrated to leverage.

Rina: "We can reallocate contingency funds for immediate kelp pilots and an emergency buffer in Sector Three, but only if the cooperative can guarantee rapid deployment. We'll need matching municipal oversight."

Marina Reyes: "We'll deploy. We'll take volunteers. We'll plant lines tonight if you can confirm equipment access."

Rina's gaze flicks to Elias Kato. He nods, the kind of bureaucratic assent that reads: I will stand in the doorway between empathy and policy.

A councilman inhales as if to grant a victory, then freezes.

Councilman: "Relocation funds are limited. We cannot commit to full buyouts. Some families will need targeted support. We can prioritize seniors and those with mobility issues."

Amalia cries out — a raw, animal sound — and slams a palm on the table.

Amalia: "You said we'd be protected. We trusted the plan. We trusted you."

The room fractures between arithmetic and accusation. Each proposed resource is a bandage that tugs at some open wound elsewhere. The math is merciless: the funds can't cover all roofs and all boats. Someone offers a loan program; someone else suggests temporary housing vouchers. Each idea is a compromise that tastes like betrayal to the people who will be told to go.

Your chest tightens with the each-and-every calculation. You feel the cooperative's small pilot — the kelp lines you have tied, the volunteers you've rustled — slip into being one among many pieces in a game that will decide who keeps place and who loses it.

Elias Kato turns to you across the table, voice low but edged.

Elias Kato: "If we can get a pilot designation and frame it as part of a mixed approach, we get buy-in from enough people to pass the appropriation. The kelp pilot will be seed funding, not full coverage."

Marina Reyes: "Seed funding won't save houses. It might help a few. It might buy time."

Elias Kato: "Buying time is not nothing."

Marina Reyes: "Buying time is being polite about postponing devastation."

He huffs, a brief sound that could be anger or despair.

Elias Kato (pleading): "What do you want me to do? Burn the plans? Walk out in front of the council and tell them to fund the impossible? We need votes. We need strategy."

Marina Reyes: "I want honesty with teeth. I want commitments that don't leave the most vulnerable behind."

The siren crescendos into the room like a knife. Outside, a new sensor report pings on Mika's tablet — a different segment of the flats registering an unexpected influx. The feed flickers: a drainage channel backing up faster than predicted. Rina's pen pauses mid-scratch.

Rina: "We need an evacuation advisory for low-lying sectors immediately."

Amalia: "Who will help them move? Where will they go?"

Councilman: "Temporary shelters can be readied at the school gym."

Old Tom's voice — older than the building's concrete — cuts through: "Gyms don't hold a lifetime."

Your blood pounds. The math on the table is a ledger of people and their rooms and their boat loans and their children's schools. Every line is a life.

You feel something inside you split — not cleanly, but like an oyster shell. You have argued for openness because hiding information felt like theft. But raw truth is now ripping at the seams of the town's social fabric: protesters carving out camps at the edge of the wetlands; friends turning into administrators of scarcity; lovers like you and Elias pressing against the same brittle map and finding different shapes.

Elias looks at you. The look is wordless, complex, threaded with things he doesn't say aloud: fear, loyalty, the calculation of how to get the most lives saved with the least political fallout. You stand inches apart in a fluorescent room that smells of damp paper and failing promises, and the gap between pragmatic rescue and communal reckoning yawns.

Mika's tablet begins to buzz again, frantic. You glance down. The live gauges — the same ones you unveiled to the festival — are spiking in a sector you had thought safe. A soundless, impossible number tears across the screen.

Your mouth is dry. For a moment, all the noise condenses to that one pixelated line: up, up, a slope that refuses to flatten.

The messaging board in the lobby flickers. Someone yells through the window: "Advisory now! Evacuate sectors one and two!"

People in the hall begin to move as if pulled by a tide: volunteers grabbing tarps, neighbors dragging armfuls of bedding. Others stand frozen, small and stunned as fish in a lamp.

Your hands tremble. Your buoy pendant — habit — rests warm against your sternum, a small, private weight.

You could give a speech. You could run. You could stand and try to translate the numbers into practical steps. You could blame the council, the models, yourself.

A child's voice wails in the next room. Old Tom's knees buckle and someone steadies him. Amalia's face is ruined with grief and fury and fierce, terrible resolve.

You breathe in. The air tastes of salt and wet cardboard and adrenaline. The storm outside mirrors the storm within: a collapse in miniature.

For a heartbeat, the world narrows to one urgent fact — what you show, and when — and the crowd's reaction that will follow.

Everything you believed about openness, about the necessity of truth, presses on you like a physical force. You taste iron.
