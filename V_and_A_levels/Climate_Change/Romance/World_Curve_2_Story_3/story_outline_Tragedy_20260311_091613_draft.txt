## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Ash and Kelp

---

## World View
Laurel Bay is a near-future coastal town where centuries-old fishing terraces meet newly forming tidal wetlands. The town’s economy and identity are threaded with salt, seaweed, and small wooden docks; clapboard homes perch on stilts while experimental seaweed pens ripple in sheltered channels. Technology exists but is patchy: DIY solar microgrids and drone surveys sit beside rust-streaked pilings and a market of mended nets. Politically, responsibility for adaptation is split between a cautious municipal council that promotes large-scale, top-down resilience projects (seawalls, elevation, managed retreat) and a network of grassroots actors who favor regenerative, place-based solutions (seaweed cooperatives, microgrids, floating farms). Religion and formal ritual are sparse; reverence is expressed through memory — Old Tom’s tales, the buoy pendant at Marina’s throat, the seedbank ledger in the glasshouse. Class tensions are quiet but real: multigenerational fisher families feel their claims to land in danger; newcomers bring capital and models. Geography is a crescent temperate coast: wide tidal flats, a reclaimed glasshouse lab, a municipal hall with a looming construction site, a windswept cliff lighthouse, and an evening market that keeps the town’s social weathered heartbeat alive. Atmosphere is painterly-real: sea-glass greens, rust, low suns, and the tension between beauty and loss.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Marina Reyes, a marine ecologist returned to Laurel Bay to found a living-seaweed cooperative and restore tidal flats after your father’s death. The municipal council, guided by Elias Kato, advocates strategic infrastructure and managed retreat; Kai Matsuda pushes decentralized, fast experiments and direct action. Tension escalates at a town hall where decisions about a proposed floodwall and relocation plan are framed as the only practical solutions. You choose a course — siding with grassroots resistance, collaborating with municipal planning, or trying to broker a hybrid strategy — and each path leads to increasingly fraught choices: organizing risky direct actions, negotiating political compromises, or running a risky pilot during storm season. Split decisions appear again and again: whether to escalate protests or protect fragile experiments, whether to share damaging data publicly or keep it confidential to sway voters, whether to let a few families relocate to save many or hold the line to preserve place. The emotional arc is a steady fall: optimism and community hope gives way to misjudgment, political failure, and climate-driven disaster. The endings vary in tone — loss, sacrifice, fractured love, and hard-minded purpose — but all close on a nadir: evidence that good intentions cannot fully avert certain losses.

### Chapter 1: Homecoming Light [Fall] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I arrive at Laurel Bay, stepping onto the tidal flats at low light, buoy pendant heavy at my throat. I take in the wet wood, the sea-glass palette, and meet Mika at the Glasshouse Lab to check the seedbank. Quiet optimism and ties to my father’s memory drive me. I notice Elias in the distance, trench coat neat against the salt air; seeing him stirs complicated memory. I reacquaint myself with the tidal nursery and the shelf labels in the Glasshouse, feeling both competence and a private ache. The community festival is weeks away; the municipal resilience plan rumor is everywhere. I resolve to start a cooperative and plant the first long-line of kelp; technical small wins feel possible.
* **Visuals:** Golden low sun on wet sand; chestnut hair with turquoise tips pulled half-knot, buoy pendant visible; Glasshouse Lab’s warm green interior; Mika’s blue-tipped hair and tool belt; Elias in a navy trench, brass compass at his throat glinting.
* **Valence:** Positive
* **Arousal:** VeryLow
* **Branch:** Common
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I’m standing barefoot on the flats while the town looks like a watercolor. The water pulls away slow and certain; glassy channels reflect a sky the color of bruised sea-glass. My fingers smell of kelp; the cord of my buoy pendant is warm against my throat. Mika is waiting at the Glasshouse entrance, fugitive and efficient — blue tips flashing as she waves a drone controller. Inside, the reclaimed warehouse breathes humid and hopeful: trays of spores, the seedbank ledger with my father’s notes stitched into the cover. I run my brass pocket spectrometer along a tray, proving salinity is where we expect it to be. The data feels like a promise I can keep.

Then I see Elias across the yard, trench collar turned up against the spray, the silver strand above his temple catching a slant of sun. He nods — measured, steady — and in that gesture I feel thirty-something versions of us: college debates, the resilience thesis he once loved, the quiet breakup that left my optimism bruised. He’s here to "help the town," he’ll tell everyone; he does it with tidy plans and the kind of loyalty that trades heat for structure. For a moment I imagine collaboration. For a moment I let the optimistic muscle flex. It’s the first evening since I returned; my resolve pulses: start the cooperative, plant kelp, keep people here. The flats answer with a gull calling and the far low hum of the town generators. It feels possible.

I fight the urge to close off and instead promise myself to listen. I feel the tug between roots and policy: cooperative work requires community trust; top-down plans require sacrifices I worry about.

* **Valence:** Neutral

The Glasshouse smells of wet wood and cheap insulation. Kai moves like trouble you can’t be angry at — her long braid brushes hydroponic tubing as she insists on a floating-raft prototype. "We can bolt seedlines to old pilings and power them from modular solar," she says — the solar wristband at her wrist chiming as she jots a schematic. She says words like "autonomy" and "anchor" and my chest tightens. Mika hacks a drone feed to show a gull’s-eye of the flats, and underneath the beauty I can see erosion scars like fingernail marks.

Old Tom arrives with a thermos and a story about a tide that came three months late, how his father saved a child with nothing left but a rope. He presses his weathered hand to my cheek the way elders do in small towns — an act that makes me both small and fierce. The municipal memo on the board is curt: "Council briefing, public comments accepted." Elias’s note is formal, an invite to the briefing: "I want your input." His amber eyes hold more than professional concern; they hold the calculus of saving a town. I feel the real constraints — funding cycles, voter fears, houses on stilts that will never be the same. I want to root and repair and hold, but the map of policy is waiting. I tell myself I’ll go — to listen, to bring science and the story of the flats. I tell myself I’ll not be naive. But old guilt rolls up when I think of my father. I promised him we would do right by this place.

Kai stands on a folding chair with a solar strip across her shoulder and the kind of spark that turns screens into bonfires. She points at the seawall model and calls it placemaking by demolition. Old Tom’s testimony about a tide that took a pier thirty years ago should settle something — but the council is terrified of immediate expense and political fallout.

When it’s my turn I lay out kelp long-lines, living buffers, a cooperative ledger. I show data and my father’s handwritten maps. I feel everything in the room lean: trust, suspicion, fear. The vote is deferred. Outside, the construction site hisses under a gray sky. The town’s separation is visible with a clarity that makes my chest ache.

I step back and breathe. I still have choices. I can escalate with Kai and anchor the town from the ground up; I can walk into Elias’ apparatus and try to shape policy; or I can risk our fragile experiments in a public demonstration — show the town a future it might choose. Each path will test the edges of love, community, and what I can hold. I press my palm over the buoy at my throat and decide.

We move like a tide that doesn’t wait for permission. Kai’s voice is a drum, urgent and contagious; she straps a modular strip to my shoulder like armor. I think of my father and whether stubbornness becomes something like grieving. I think about who pays when the tide takes more than a line of kelp.

I feel the weight of my father’s gaslit map in my satchel: his handwriting looping around a channel he swore would hold. I promise myself I will not let fear determine everything. I also promise I won’t let reckless valor cost a neighbor their house. I taste salt and fear both.

### Chapter 6: Floodline (Ending A1 — Route A Outcome) [Fall] (Branch: Route A / Ending)

* **Entry Condition:** From Chapter 5, Option A
* **Summary:** The mass action escalates. The blockade temporarily halts heavy machinery but provokes a violent confrontation when a storm hits earlier than predicted. Security attempts to force lines; a piling collapses and tide surges faster than we can respond. Our long-lines strain and snap; the seedbank loses critical samples to salt intrusion when the Glasshouse’s pumps fail. Several families’ low-lying homes are devastated by an unexpected tidal surge. I survive, but the cooperative’s pilot is destroyed. Relationships fracture; Kai accuses me of poor judgment for agreeing to the action. Elias is devastated and blames the escalation for political backlash that ends the municipal compromise. This ending is a hard nadir: community losses are immediate, the town’s optimism is drowned, and your bond with Elias is severely strained.
* **Visuals:** Night of pouring rain, electric-blue welding lights, kelp lines snapping like ropes in a gale, my hair plastered to my face, the buoy pendant half-buried in mud, Old Tom’s greatcoat soaked.
* **Valence:** VeryNegative
* **Arousal:** VeryHigh
* **Branch:** Route A / Ending

We held the line until the sea held us instead. The blockade drew a circle of hands and lanterns and hope, but the storm had other plans. It arrived as a bruise in the sky, and in the dark the construction crew pushed forward with equipment lit like small suns. Men in hard hats shoved at us across the plank-bridges; someone yelled and then the world found the sound of a piling giving way. Water came like an answer to a question long asked.

My hands were in the mud trying to tie a frayed line when the surge pulled the kelp pens like wind pulls a sail. We fought in the dark with nothing but rope and fury. The Glasshouse’s backup pumps failed — a simple relay lost to water — and seed trays sloshed and spilled spores into already-salty drains. Families who had put their trust in our promise lost lower rooms, boats, and storerooms. I held someone’s soaked photograph and felt the grit at my teeth of a loss that cannot be mended by plans or petitions.

Kai stood across from me in a coat plastered with rain, her braid like a neon stripe of hurt. She said, "We told you not to do this," and in her voice was exhaustion and betrayal. Elias came after the tide with mud on his cuffs; his amber eyes were hollow with a calculation undone. Rina’s phone lit up with angry callers and a council that recoiled.

When the water stilled in the dawn, it left a map of wreckage. People were cold and quiet, and my buoy pendant was a smear of mud at my chest. I had wanted to save a place; instead, in the worst line of weather, the cooperative that could have been a seedbed for something brave was a ruin. I had hoped to protect people — and instead I had put more on the line than I could bear to hold. The town’s songs had been drowned before the chorus even began.

I stepped into the hall with a satchel full of data and my father’s ledger under my arm, feeling the claustrophobic certainty of bureaucracy. Rina greeted me with a folder and a look that suggests diplomacy is a muscle she’s built to iron. Elias sets me a place at the table between lawyers and engineers; his amber gaze finds mine and holds — not an offer of romance exactly, more the steadiness of someone who will anchor you to a plan.

We translate kelp long-lines into pilot metrics: erosion reduction estimates, job creation numbers, measurable carbon sequestration. We propose "cooperative labor credits" as a political language to make community labor count as part of infrastructure delivery. Elias insists on phasing and compliance; I insist on people’s stories. Kai calls us technocrats who water down life to spreadsheets. She comes by with a ring of volunteers who laugh in the face of permits; she glances at me and says, "You’re softer than you look, Mari. Don’t let him paper over the sea." The words land warm and stinging.

Outside, construction continues. People come to the hall with photos and a steady ache in their voices. The plan needs public buy-in, votes, and timing. I know what I want to do: get kelp in the water and keep people here. But the machines in the lot and the deadlines at the grant office test my patience. For all my hope, the project relies on the same system that can displace us.

### Chapter 9: Political Winter (Ending B1 — Route B Outcome) [Fall] (Branch: Route B / Ending)

* **Entry Condition:** From Chapter 8, Option A
* **Summary:** You release the leaked projections. Panic and anger spread; the council is forced into emergency action. Political maneuvering accelerates but without careful social supports. Eviction notices for a handful of low-lying households go out in weeks. The municipal plan proceeds under pressure; Elias is praised for decisive action but you see the human cost immediately. Kai calls the move a betrayal; community trust fractures. The cooperative’s momentum stalls amid accusations that you weaponized data. The town endures an immediate stabilization of infrastructure but at the cost of displacement and fractured relationships. Personal guilt and community rupture form a bleak winter.
* **Visuals:** Emergency notices pasted to doorframes, municipal floodwall cranes silhouetted against a slate sky, my sea-glass eyes rimmed with sleeplessness, Kai’s neon braid pulled into a tight knot, Old Tom’s gaunt, furious face.
* **Valence:** VeryNegative
* **Arousal:** VeryHigh
* **Branch:** Route B / Ending

The projections burned more than the paper that printed them. They sparked action: cranes came, permits were fast-tracked, and the council voted emergency funds. On paper, it was efficient. In practice, it meant people got notices that their parcels were "unsustainable" and that relocation offers came with time limits. The town’s sense of shared fate cracked along those lines.

Elias stood at a podium with a tidy speech about saving "as many homes and services as possible." He was hailed in grateful op-eds. I stood behind him, feeling applause that sounded like breaking glass. My data did what data does: it clarified trade-offs and made choice possible. But the choice made by others did not weigh the small human stories I’d held in my satchel.

Kai confronted me at the market, voice shaking: "You handed them a scalpel and told them to cut." She said it in a way that left no room for my explanation; I had already made the choice. The cooperative’s volunteers felt abandoned to a system that prioritized numbers. Old Tom spat into the mud and called me "clever, but soft."

That winter was cold in a way the sea hadn’t been before. Roads and drains were reinforced, and the cranes huffed like giant beasts. People who could move did. People who could not stayed and built higher walls inside their houses. I had wanted to save our place; instead I had made the calculus that let power move faster than compassion. The town had been stabilized by engineering, but at the cost of kinship that might never return.
