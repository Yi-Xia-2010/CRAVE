[Scene: Marrow Bay Boardwalk | Late Afternoon → Dusk]
[Background:"ch5_4001e7_1"]

You stand where the boards slope toward the sea, your boots planted on wood warmed by a stubborn sun and still slick from last night's tide. Around you the town has gathered like a creature circling a wound and deciding—together—how to stitch it closed. Faces you know lean over the rope barrier: Jun with a stack of leaflets, Noah hauling a crate of hot cups from the vendor, Aunt Lila steady in the crowd like a small lighthouse herself.

Your fingers curl around the coral scarf at your throat. It is a small, human anchor: your mother's hands, Aunt Lila's stories, the list of tasks in your Moleskine all braided into something you can tuck in close. The Saltworks prototype breathes beside you—a patchwork of micro-turbines, solar sails, a shallow tide-garden that looks half-lab, half-altar. It answers the wind with a mechanical, hopeful whisper.

Elias Park is at the control panel, sleeves rolled, face lit by indicator LEDs. He glances up and that soft, attentive look in his eyes colors everything as if someone has tuned the world to a warmer frequency. For a heartbeat the fear inside you eases—proof is here.

Elias Park: "All right. Lights on in three. Coffee, please, if anyone's got it." (a half-smile)
Mira Santos: "Make them count." (your voice tight; you force a smile that doesn't touch your eyes)

Elias Park's hand rests on a lever. He hesitates, checking connections. The crowd hushes as if waiting for a held breath to settle. You feel the hush like pressure in your chest; it wants release.

Noah (from the side): "Make this thing sing, Mira. Make them see we can do this without anyone bulldozing our homes for glossy renderings."

Mira Santos: "We will—no staged flash. Show the work, show who does it." (you step forward, then stop, palms pressed into the railing)

Elias Park: "Ready?" (he looks at you, asking for permission that is also a small offering)

You nod. The lever clicks. Turbines rise a fraction, pumps shudder, and then the little array takes the day into its hands. Solar sails angle and a line of string-lights flares—there's a ripple of applause, then a cheer that sounds fragile and fierce in the same breath. The warm amber of the strings spills over patchworked faces; the sound of the prototype is not a roar but a steady, practical heartbeat: power, proof, possibility.

You move through the crowd afterward, answering questions, handing out quick explanations—touching hands, offering the kind of facts that feel like stitches: how floodwater is channeled, how tide charges batteries, how beds of cordgrass cushion waves. Your voice stays calm; inside, something like a tremor pushes up and threatens to unthread everything. The celebration around you feels like glass: brilliant, fragile, dangerously bright.

Aunt Lila squeezes your shoulder, voice small with pride. "You did good, niña. You made them listen."

Mira Santos: "We made them see a different way." (You want to believe the words as if speaking them into being)

Jun catches your eye and waves you over. He hands you a flyer scribbled with community commitments—garden rosters, volunteer shifts, a plan to build more modules. The paper is already smudged with damp sea-spray.

The sun drops. The salt scent grows sharper as wind teases up and the sky bruises into indigo. People begin to fold up chairs and trade plans for numbers; somebody starts laughing too loud, someone else cries. You linger near Elias Park as the crowd eases away, hands busy gathering tools and crates. The pilot glows like a small hearth against the darkening bay.

Elias Park: "They clapped for you as much as me." (he teases, but there's a hitch to it)
Mira Santos: "They clapped for the idea that we can do something. Claps don't pay for permits." (you shut your mouth against the bitterness)

Elias Park's face tightens; he worries about scale and about promises cast into concrete. He remembers, you guess, other projects that began with applause and ended with bulldozers. He reaches for your hand. For a moment the world narrows to that touch—warmth, grease, the tiny scrape of a callus on his thumb.

Elias Park: "We'll make it work. One step, one neighbor at a time. I'm going to log the data tonight—get it on record." (soft, practical)
Mira Santos: "Get it on record." (you repeat, as if setting a hook)

The mood should have been buoyant, an optimism threaded through practical steps, but the air elsewhere snaps: a single, crisp ring of phone notification—too many to be ordinary. Jun's face changes first; he steps back and unfurls his phone with a hand that shakes.

Jun: "You need to see this." (he says it like someone offering a hand at the lip of a cliff)

You reach for the device; a civic notice has been posted to the town's public feed and—worse—there's a clip attached: Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock, in a sleek office with a glass wall that reflects a horizon unlike yours. Her hair is impeccable; her suit eats the light. She smiles in that way that sorts things into boxes: efficient, final.

[Background:"ch5_4001e7_2"]
[Sound: Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock's recorded voice, calm and precise; a low wind like the world exhaling]

Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock (on screen): "Marrow Bay's safety is non-negotiable. Given the escalating risk and the need for coordinated resilience, my office has petitioned—effective today—for an expedited redevelopment permit covering the coastal commercial zone. This will allow immediate stabilization work and interface with state resources. We will consider community proposals, provided a funded alternative is presented within thirty days."

Mira Santos: "Thirty days." The words land like a stone in your gut. It is not a suggestion. It is a deadline. It is a procedural bolt driven with a hand that looks like care and feels like a guillotine.

Neighbors gather closer. Some shout—"We won't be moved!"—and others press palms to foreheads in disbelief. Noah's jaw works; he runs a hand through salt-caked hair and laughs a single sound that is equal parts fury and something rawer.

Noah: "She can't—she can't just—" (he stumbles for the legal language and flails; the crowd fills the gaps with curse words and accusations)

Aunt Lila steps forward to touch your arm, steady as tidewood. Her face is a map of all the storms she's weathered.

Aunt Lila: "They'll call it speed. They'll call it safety. They'll call us temporary. But they'll take the shells with them. They'll take names from porches and call it necessary." (her voice cracks like a rope)

The celebration curdles into a different language: urgency, plans rewritten into laundry lists, the specific, abrasive details of procedure crowding out the warm blur of communal joy. Your chest contracts so hard it is difficult to breathe. The scarf at your neck feels like a tourniquet.

Mira Santos: "Who filed it? Who in town signed off?" (your voice is a knife)

Jun: "Municipal feed says it's under Cassandra \"Cass\" Whitlock's emergency petition. It's been filed with the state. She invoked a coastal-stability clause—" (he stops, his voice small)

You can't think in hierarchies and clauses. You think in houses, in Aunt Lila's stories, in the murals on the pier that will disappear beneath some glossy master plan. Your mind runs through a checklist of what to save, who to call; it skips over strategy to animal things—grab, protect, run. Panic blooms like a bruise speeding through tissue.

Elias Park's hand tightens on yours. He looks at you with that reticence that has always been his shelter—calm on the outside, a storm sheltered within. He wants to do something measured; you can feel him counting steps like beads.

Elias Park: "We can log tonight's data, push a petition by morning, get Jun on school outreach, and—" (he lists, breath by practical breath)
Mira Santos: "Thirty days isn't enough to build the legal scaffolding we need. It's enough to smooth over a lot of paperwork and hand over our shoreline." (the words are a spit of bile)

A woman from the crowd calls out—she's the owner of the bait-and-bookshop, her face streaked with rain and salt, hands raw from mending nets.

Bait-Shop Owner: "What are we supposed to do? Move? When? Who pays for a move? Who pays for what they're calling 'stability'?" (her question hangs, threaded with fury and dread)

Mira Santos answers because speech is the only way to slow the rising panic: you lay out a small, brutal schematic—data collection tonight, witnesses to document the pilot, a public record of community alternatives presented to the council, a chain of custody for materials that anchor your claim. The words are sharp tools you hand to strangers.

But paperwork and community will meet steel and money; you know the scale of what Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock can pull: lawyers, timelines, influence. Her "safety" is a tidy word wrapped around a bulldozer. The clip on the phone ends with her smile; the feed brightens then dies. The screen goes black and the wind pushes salt across the boards like a smear.

[Music: Music swells—strings and percussion crack like thunder]
[Sound: The boardwalk thrums with a new, violent energy: neighbors shouting, phones calling, someone pounding the prototype's casing in panic]

You find yourself pulled to the Saltworks platform; you need the small, improvised sanctum—those raised reeds and patched solar sails that smell of mud and hope. There, in the fragile lab-light, neighbors form a ring of hands and voices. Fingers brush with yours; others grasp them and speak like a chorus: protect, resist, document, litigate. The breath you share tastes of salt and iron.

Noah: "We start tonight. We get witnesses, we log micro-output, we get the town papers to run this. We don't give them thirty days of silence." (his voice is a drumbeat of action)
Mira Santos: "We need a clear ask. Not just protest. A plan that looks like an implementable alternative." (you force the words into an outline)

You think of all the trade-offs that pushed you here: the net of community decisions, the labor of small neighbors, the ethics you can't abandon. You think of Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock's smoothness and how quickly a time limit compresses everything into a panic. The feeling isn't only urgency; it's a tide moving in that will not be stopped by goodwill alone.

Aunt Lila's hand finds yours in the dim. Her nails are ink-stained from lists. She speaks like someone who remembers when the town's timeline was slower and kinder.

Aunt Lila: "They will make a calendar of our grief if we let them. Count the days not as doom, niña, but as a map. We will put every day to work." (she says it as if she stitches a plan into you)

You nod, but inside a new, corrosive thought gnaws: thirty days is not a challenge to be met; it's a trap that will hollow you out while you work. The very thing that gave you light today—the demo, the proof—has become the reason you must sprint and maybe fail.

The night closes in. The prototype's lights are a dim constellation against the dark water. Someone starts a chant low enough to be a prayer. You mouth the words as if saying them will make them true.

Your phone buzzes again. Another municipal update. The little red notification is an ember of dread.

Later—because decisions require movement—you and Elias Park walk up to the Old Lighthouse where the wind breaks and thoughts can find edges. The lamp room is small and smells of oil and wet wool, and for a moment you let the sound of the horn fill the silence between you. You both are caught between the loudness outside and the intimate terror here.

Elias Park: "We can't let them co-opt the narrative. If Cassandra \"Cass\" Whitlock frames this as 'emergency,' she'll fast-track more than plans—she'll fast-track consent." (he says it low; the words tremble)

Mira Santos: "Then we make our consent loud. We show every week of progress; we gather signatures; we get media." (you try to order chaos into strategy)

Elias Park looks at you, searching. His hand finds yours again, fierce and brief.

Elias Park: "Whatever you ask, I'm with you." (there is an ache in his voice you haven't heard before)
Mira Santos: (your reply is swallowed by the horn; you breathe and say) "I need you to be here in the open. Not just in the wires." (you force honesty into the small space between you)

He hesitates, unreadable in the way of someone who has been burned by promises. You remember the Schrödinger of his past: did he once step away when a project collapsed? You don't know for sure. His face, in the lamp's amber, is complex. You tell yourself that for now, his hand means something real.

Between the two of you, plans churn like weather. But even as you make lists, the echo of Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock's thirty-day ultimatum grows teeth, gnawing at the edges of every strategy until your chest hurts. The lighthouse lamp swings; its rhythm becomes the beat of a deadline.

You realize, with a slow, cold clarity, that tonight's victory shifted something fundamental. The town saw what could be. Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock has turned that sight into a calendar that counts down the space in which you can act. Hope has become a stopwatch.

You do not yet know which sacrifices will be demanded. You do not yet know how many doors will be closed as others open. The thirty days is a blade; it can shape or it can cut. The wind seems to take the world and sharpen it.

Aunt Lila's hand squeezes yours, steady as if to say: measure, do not break. Noah's voice from below the lighthouse is a rising chant. Jun paces like an electrical current, lighting up possibilities and then crossing them out. Elias Park says nothing for a long time; when he speaks, it is in small, steady words.

Elias Park: "We get our evidence in order. We go to the council tomorrow. We get the cameras. We call in anyone who will stand with us." (he breathes each plan like a prayer)

You nod. The plans form like a net, but nets have to be held taut. Your chest feels raw and exposed as you look into the dark water. The town's future pulses like something fragile and enormous.

Outside, the sea keeps working. Inside, you and your neighbors set to work under a ticking sky.
