You open the Moleskine. The tide charts inside are smudged with old pencil marks. You begin a new list—contacts, legal language to refine, people to meet in other towns, dates for hearings you will push to create. The list is a map for a different kind of returning.
You think of Elias, of Aunt Lila, of Noah and the bait shop and the potted geranium at the municipal intake table. Each memory is a small argument for stubbornness. Each is also a wound.
You do not know whether this separation will be the life-raft that keeps something alive or the wedge that splits your future. You only know that the choice was made—by circumstance, by law, by the shallow arithmetic of 'safety'—and you have chosen a response.
You close the notebook and slide it back into the waterproof sleeve. The shuttle pulls away. Through the window, the lighthouse becomes a pale wash of light and then a small, persistent dot. The rain is loud enough now to blur faces into watercolor.
