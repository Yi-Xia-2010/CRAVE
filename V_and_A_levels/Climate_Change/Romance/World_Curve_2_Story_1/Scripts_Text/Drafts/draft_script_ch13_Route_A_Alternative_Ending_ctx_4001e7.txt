### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Alternative Ending


[Scene: Boardwalk Demo Site | Late Afternoon — Storm-Breathing Sky]
[Visual: Rows of folding chairs, a scuffed demo platform with ragged solar sails, posters taped to pylons. Cass' glossy projection van is parked at the plaza edge, reflection screens running renderings of a "Secure Future."] 
[Music: Low, urgent electronic pulse under a rising string ostinato]
[Sound: Wind-driven salt spray, murmurs of a gathered crowd, the distant thunk of a photographer's shutter]

You stand behind the salvaged lectern, Moleskine and contract folded on top. The concrete between you and Cass' projection van tastes like metal—like the promise of engineered certainty and the weight of what it costs. Around you neighbors clutch thermoses, children press against parents' knees, and a line of press keeps a respectful, hungry distance. Elias is there, shoulders squared, the copper cuff at his wrist catching the light; he looks like a man waiting for a bridge to hold.

Your palms are damp. The paper under your hand is thinner than you expected. It is, by every measure, a weapon and a confession.

[Visual: Close-up on the contract's header—Cass Whitlock Industries—stamped with a signature line]
[Sound: A single, sharp cry from the back as a gull banks low]

You inhale. You name the feeling like a prayer: this is what truth does to a small town—it rips the scab clean and leaves the wound exposed. You do not want to do this. You do not want to hurt people. But not doing it is a different violence.

The crowd leans forward, waiting for what you do next." || Choice B ("Lay the contract flat on the lectern; read every line aloud") -> Reaction: "You flatten the paper under your palm so the breeze cannot steal it. The paper trembles in the wind—every clause visible, every omission now theatrical. Elias' jaw tightens; he meets your eyes, a question without sound."]

[Music: A violin-scratch quickens]
[Visual: Camera pushes in on your face; salt streaks at your temple]

You choose. The choice is no longer about action—it's about who you are willing to be in front of everyone.

You clear your throat. Your voice is small at first, swallowed by wind and the rustle of paper. Then you let it widen because silence would be consent.

You: "This contract—so-called 'easement protections'—was written to appear protective. The language is legal camouflage. Clause twenty-one allows for 'accelerated acquisition' in the event of 'systemic risk,' and clause thirty-four defines 'community heritage' in ways that exclude elders' oral claims. Clause fifty-one grants indemnity to contractors for 'mitigation-driven relocation.' That's not protection—it's permission to displace."

[Sound: A hushed gasp ripples through the crowd; a camera's zoom whines louder]
[Visual: Cass steps forward from the shade of her van; her suit is immaculate, wet salt beading on its lapel like a dark promise]

Cassandra Whitlock: (calm) "Mira—if I may—language like that is standard. Emergency protocols require clarity. We are not here to evict anyone. We're here to keep Marrow Bay standing."

You can feel Elias' hand move at the small of your back. Not an admonition—an encouragement. He knows you. He knows that when you say a thing you have weighed its bones.

You: "Standard language has consequences. 'Mitigation-driven relocation' is a euphemism—you know that. And 'accelerated acquisition' means there's a legal path to buy out neighborhoods under the guise of protection."

Cassandra Whitlock: (a fraction of ice in her smile) "You are dramatizing. We can discuss specific clauses after the pilot. This community's survival is not a negotiation with the press."

You smell the sharp antiseptic of Cass' perfume under the salt air—an attempt to sanitize urgency.

Elias: (quiet, threaded with urgency) "Cass, there's another clause—non-disclosure conditions tied to performance metrics. If these pilot funds are used, the town agrees to confidentiality on—"

Cassandra Whitlock: (interrupting) "We cannot have public speculation undermining the pilot's viability. Contracts require trust. Trust requires expertise."

Elias: "Trust shouldn't mean blindsiding the people who live here."

Cassandra Whitlock: (smile tightening) "And neither should presuming harm without evidence."

Your heartbeat is a drum that answers every accusation in the air. You're halfway through the paragraph when someone from the back—someone you didn't expect—shouts a name like a flare.

Harper Kline: "Read the annex! Read the annex now!"

[Sound: A sudden pitched intake from the crowd; a microphone squeal]
[Visual: A small woman—Harper—steps forward, face white and set; she had been near the scaffolding, hood up, hands wrapped around a battered legal pad. The press converges before a single word can leave her mouth]

Harper Kline: (voice shaking but undeniable) "I helped draft parts of this. I signed off while they rewrote 'heritage' to mean 'redevelopable.' I—I'm not safe to stay with this out."

Elias' face goes still—an arrested ship. For a breath you see the map of him thinking: exposure, consequences, the possibility of rescue or ruin.

You: (to Harper) "Harper—"

Harper Kline: "My lawyers—I've already—" (she looks like she is pulling herself inside a shell) "I can't stay. They'll make an example."

[Visual: Harper's silhouette against Cass' projection van; she turns away before the press can push closer]
[Sound: The scramble of footsteps; someone shouting "Wait!"]

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Step forward to stop Harper from leaving") -> Reaction: "You move, lungs burning, to take Harper's sleeve. Your fingers close on fabric that smells of printer ink and fear. She looks at you like someone who's already decided the world will burn to keep her alive; you see a flicker of both gratitude and apology." || Choice B ("Let Harper go; you cannot put her in more danger") -> Reaction: "You do not reach. Letting her walk away feels like letting the tide take a child, but you keep your hands where the paper won't fly. Harper looks back once, and there's an accusation you cannot bear—then she's gone."]

[Music: Stings into a harsher register; the wind seems to lean in]
[Visual: The crowd fractures—some murmur assent, others shout that this is a circus. The projection above Cass' van switches to a market-stability slide]

Cassandra Whitlock: (voice raised, sharply civil) "This is reckless. Whomever 'Harper' is, anonymous claims cannot supplant due process. If legal counsel believes there are grounds, they will pursue them through channels—"

You: "Due process takes time. Time is a form of displacement when contracts are allowed to be enforced before a community can respond. This 'pilot'—" (your voice is a wire pulled taut) "—is structured to produce outcomes that favor acquisition."

[Sound: A microphone bangs; the shout of a neighbor—Noah—rises above the hubbub]
Noah Rivera: "She's right! We saw your maps, Cass! You tried to change property lines at the midnight meeting!"

Cassandra Whitlock: (cool contempt) "Those meetings were procedural. Your concerns are heard."

Elias steps forward now, the film camera hanging heavy against his chest like a heart.

Elias: "Cass, look—look at the clause that forces arbitration under an external jurisdiction. That strips town oversight. Who benefits from that?"

Cassandra Whitlock: "It benefits efficiency, Elias. It keeps the pilot viable in the eyes of investors."

Elias: (brandishing, not unkindly) "Investors are not the people who have to breathe the air, live with the tide, or carry the dead from their houses."

[Visual: A heavy beat; the sea beyond the boardwalk throws a white shoulder against the rocks]
[Sound: The press moves—cameras flashing—capturing every taut second]

You watch Cass' composure crack like varnish. Something mechanical in her posture shifts; the smooth surface of certainty flutters.

Cassandra Whitlock: (flat) "Mira, you could have had oversight. We offered conditional governance—"

You: (laugh, half-broken) "Conditional oversight that requires you to hold the gavel, Cass? That's not governance. That's guardianship by foreclosure."

Cassandra Whitlock: (a slow gathering) "You are doing real harm. This jeopardizes lives. People will panic. Developers will pull back from funding—"

You: "Or they will be forced to examine what they already wrote in secret."

[Sound: Loud, insistent footsteps—security edges in near Cass' van. The press forms a tightening ring]
[Visual: Elias' hand finds yours; his fingers are warm, trembling with the same rawness you feel]

Elias: (close, urgent) "Mira—if you read the whole annex—there's personal legal risk. They will come after you. Cass will sue. The company will leverage that to move faster."

You feel the sentence. The threat hangs like a buoyline: legal retaliation, precinct notices, relocations accelerated under emergency statutes. Everything you feared begins to organize itself into procedure.

You: "Then let them. We are not illusions. We are names. I'm not afraid of them putting our names down."

Elias: (soft, a plea and an ultimatum) "I'm afraid of losing you to the fight. I'm afraid of what that does to both of us. If they strike legally—if they come at you—will you let it take you?"

You: (a cold, honest answer) "If the choice is me or exposing a structure that will displace whole families—"

[Sound: A microphone falls; a child cries somewhere in the crowd. Harper's absence is a new hollow in the air]
[Visual: The crowd leans in; someone livestreams. You set your jaw, and open the annex.]

You: (reading) "Annex C—'Mitigation-Driven Mobilization.' In event of 'predetermined elevation thresholds' the company may, at its discretion, petition for emergency eminent acquisition 'to expedite safety.' 'Expedite safety' defined as 'reconstruction under corporate-led frameworks with defined compensation schedules, negotiable at company's discretion.'"

[Music: Orchestral percussion crashes in; a wind-lashed cymbal]
[Visual: The faces in the crowd change—some blanch, some nod, some begin to whisper about lawyers and packing lists]

Cassandra Whitlock: (white-knuckled composure) "You are taking a paragraph out of context—"

You: "Context is written here in fonts and margins, Cass. It's not just about wording. It's about the authority this contract gives you to do precisely the thing you said would be necessary if we accepted scaled funding."

[Sound: A shout from someone who knows they'll be next on a list—"What does this mean for my street?"]
[Visual: Noah's knuckles are bone-white as he grips the rail; Aunt Lila's voice breaks through the crowd, a ragged chant of memory and loss]

Aunt Lila Santos: "They can't take our stories like this. You can't put a price on a procession!"

Cassandra Whitlock: (eyes hard) "Names and stories are not infrastructure. You can't rebuild a town around nostalgia."

You: "You don't get to define our worth in spreadsheets."

[Visual: A law firm's SUV squeals into the plaza; two men in suits step out like vultures checking the tide]
[Sound: Phones ring, lawyers beginning their choreography]

Elias: (fierce) "Cass—do you realize what giving this up will do? You will quicken the clock on relocation. You will tie our hands."

Cassandra Whitlock: (slowly) "We will do what must be done. Tough choices save lives. That may be an ugly thing to admit, but it's an honest one."

You feel your throat constrict so hard your words are a wire. This is the moment that will etch the town into before and after.

You: (to the crowd, rasping) "Will we be the people who let experts decide who counts as a town? Or the people who remember what counts and fight to keep it?"

[Sound: A collective inhale; the sort that sounds like the town holding its breath against an incoming swell]
[Visual: The camera finds Elias' face, then sweeps to yours; a thousand small frames capturing the strain of two people standing on opposite shores of a single decision]

Cassandra Whitlock: (leaning in, quietly coercive) "Mira—if you continue this, we will file for injunction. We will seek damages. The pilot will be canceled. The town will be exposed to risk while litigation runs its course."

You: (a raw laugh) "Litigation is a language of power. We don't have to speak it to mean what we say."

[Sound: The first hard raindrop slams the demo platform; umbrellas bloom like dark mollusks]
[Visual: The boardwalk edges are slick; placards flutter, a banner half-peeled by wind]

Elias: (under his breath) "They'll come for the Saltworks—regulatory probes, safety shutdowns. They will tie our hands with red tape and call it 'public safety.'"

You watch the slow tightening of legal and financial machinery—an inevitability that feels like prearranged tide.

Harper Kline's absence hangs like a bell. The knowledge that she left to save herself—maybe a necessary selfishness, maybe a betrayal—echoes in your ribs. You think of the people who will have to pack boxes, who will be told their houses no longer fit the 'new resilience model.'

You: "We expose it now. We make the town witness it. Let it be clear who filed what, and when. If they retaliate, we'll be ready to answer. We cannot let our future be written behind closed doors."

[Music: Crescendo—strings tearing into a high, metallic pitch]
[Visual: Cass' jaw sets; she steps back, calculating]

Cassandra Whitlock: (with finality) "So be it. My attorneys will be in touch. Consider your town warned."

[Sound: Camera shutters staccato; someone cries "They're coming for us!"]
[Visual: Elias squeezes your hand; you can feel the tremor in him as if it's yours]

Elias: (quiet, broken) "Mira—this will change everything. If they take legal action, if they press the clause—"

You: (a hush; the wind seems to go out of the world) "If they do, I'll stand in the way. I will make the cost of that action greater than their certainty."

Elias: (a shattered whisper) "At what cost to us?"

You: (there are no easy answers) "At the cost that keeps the names of the town off a spreadsheet."

[Visual: A messenger from Municipal Hall pushes through, breathless; someone has already started reading an official notice on their phone]
[Sound: Notification pings multiply; a legal fax tone somewhere like a knell]

You feel the pressure pivot from a public revelation toward procedural retribution. Eyes on you, the town now knows the terms. The machinery has a target, and it is focused on you and the Saltworks and anyone who stood on this platform.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse | Night — Wind Raking the Stones]
[Visual: Moonless sky, the lantern room a small, trembling island of lamplight. The sea throws itself against the rocks in white flares. Your coral scarf hangs limp at your neck.]
[Music: Sparse piano, brittle as glass; distant thunder like an audience's slow clap]

You climb the lighthouse steps with Elias behind you. Each stair is a sentence of the day, worn smooth by other people's hands. You smell damp wool and the ash of a too-quick cigarette—Elias' attempt at a steadiness that fails.

Elias: (trying for light, failing) "You could have run a calmer strategy."

You: (the laugh tastes like salt and iron) "Calmer strategies didn't stop the clauses from being written. They just made the truth slower."

Elias: (his voice breaks) "I am not ready to lose you to this."

You: "You're not going to lose me—" (you want to mean it; it fractures) "—unless we give up the right to be a town on our own terms."

Elias: (closer now, hands hovering, the space between you charged) "And if the town comes at you? If legal injunctions and supervised closures come for the Saltworks and your name—if they make you a symbol to be discredited—what then?"

You feel the question probe a place you keep for quiet things. You think of Aunt Lila's stories, of the coral scarf around your neck, of Harper's frightened shadow leaving the plaza. There is an ache that is not only for the town but for the private softness you have with Elias—that small harbor you have not fully anchored in public.

You: "Then I will answer. I will tell the truth louder. I will not step aside so others can be written out."

Elias: (a fragile fight) "And us?"

You: (the lighthouse lantern throwing your face into high, honest contrast) "We will be tested. Maybe we break. Maybe we go on. But right now the choice is whether I'm willing to sacrifice the town's memory for a quieter life with you. I can't."

[Visual: Rain begins; salt and water make the world a smear of silver]
[Sound: The sea seems to speak a verdict]

Elias reaches for your hand—fingers brushing callus and salt—and you take it. It is a small clasp against an enormous falling sky.

Elias: (voice lost to the wind) "Then stand with me. Let me stand with you in the open, not just in secret."

You: (honest, and fierce) "I stand. I am standing. But I will not be quiet."

[Music: A single struck chord, the highest point of the day's crescendo—shocking, unresolving]
[Visual: Close-up on your coral scarf, whipped by the wind; the lighthouse lamp swivels, painting the sea and the two of you in staccato intervals]

You both look out over the black water. The town below is a scatter of small lights and louder claims. The exposure you forced today has changed the calculus: the municipal attorneys will meet, Cass' legal team will mobilize, relocations will be drafted faster under the new scrutiny, and the whistleblower—Harper—remains absent, sacrificed to safety.

You let yourself feel the very negative shape of it: moral clarity that cuts like glass, rippling into lives and homes. The arousal—the panic, the urgent legal and social machinery—rushes toward you like an incoming tide.

You breathe. You taste iron and salt and the raw edge of what you have chosen.
