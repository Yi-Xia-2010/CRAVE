### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Ending


[Scene: Café Back Room | Just After Midnight — Rain Against the Roof]

[Visual: A cramped room above the café. Lamplight skims stacks of paper; a string of damp coats hangs on hooks. Napkins with frantic sketches lie beside typed affidavits. A window beads with rain; the harbor beyond is a dark smear.]

[Sound: Rain hammers the roof in a steady, urgent rhythm. Voices whisper, then rise. A phone vibrates against wood. The kettle on a small stove hisses and dies.]

[Music: Urgent strings; a driving loop that pushes the tempo higher with each cut of dialogue.]

You step over a spill of coffee and stop at the table. The files are heavier than you remember; weight measured not in paper but in consequence. Prof. Noor's hands are in the center of the map — soil under her nails, ink on her fingertips like battle scars. Rosa's jacket, a flash of yellow, leans against a chair; her protest buttons catch the lamp and throw back tiny sparks.

Noor: "We cross-referenced receipts, transfers, and the consulting agreements. Look here." (she slides a printout toward you; the paper trembles in the lamplight) "Short-term bonds, shell companies, commitments tied to construction milestones — the pattern is consistent."

You lean in. The smell of damp wool and strong coffee mixes with the faint marine tang that has followed you since the boardwalk. The documents make a map of promises: dates, lines, wires that stitch Elias's firm to the towns that ended up with seawalls and empty harbors.

Rosa: (voice quick, knife-bright) "They told Ben's cousin — and half the co-op — that this is a done deal. 'This time will be different,' they said. Then bids closed overnight and jobs vanished." She slaps a napkin with a scrawl of names and times onto the table. "People gave up boats; they took loans."

Ben Harper: (low, gravelly) "They had a man from the firm put fancy charts on my table once. Told us the sea could be tamed with stone and steel. My brother laughed at the idea. 'You can't fix an ocean with a ledger,' he said. He was right." (Ben's eyes go to you and hold.)

Aiden: (standing near the doorway, cap pressed low) "We're not just dealing with numbers. We're dealing with families. If we expose this and the town fractures—" (he doesn't finish, but the sentence is a rope taut between you two)

Your chest tightens. The guilt that has sat like ballast under your ribs — the house your brother couldn't save — shifts into something sharper. Not vengeance. Not joy. A fierce clarity, a kind of clean light that shows the edges of a choice.

Noor: (soft but unyielding) "They've already filed for injunctions. Lawyers are asking judges to freeze our meetings. That's how they buy time — and public fatigue. We can go to an independent council with everything: chain of custody, corroborated testimony, forensic accounting. It will be slower. It will be cleaner."

Rosa: (pounding a fist into her palm) "Slower means Elias gets to charm another press conference. People forget. People go back to work and the momentum dies. If we dump to a national outlet, the cameras come, the story spreads — and we force a reckoning now."

Ben: (interjecting, voice thick) "Or you burn it. You make sure no one gets hurt by the fallout. Sometimes the town has to be kept whole even if it's at your expense. God knows we've kept secrets before for a reason."

Aiden steps forward; his hand finds the back of an empty chair, knuckles white beneath the knit. His amber eyes are rumpled with something close to fear and something warmer that you used to see in sunlight.

Aiden: (quiet, the line between anger and care) "Maya, I'm with you. But if this splits people who trusted him — folks I grew up with — we risk losing more than policy. We risk being the reason someone's livelihood collapses."

You look at him. For a moment everything else recedes: the rain, the lamplight, the weight of paper. His protectiveness is familiar, like a harbor that holds against wind. But there is steel there too; he isn't asking you to step back. He's asking you to carry consequences with him.

You open your mouth, then close it. Words feel inadequate. So you tap a finger against the file in front of you, feeling the edge of the paper like a coastline.

Rosa: (half-smile, dangerous) "That's the one that ties them to those quick-build contracts in Larkspur and Harrow's Quay. If that sees daylight—"

Noor: (interrupting, brisk) "We need to document everything. Chain of custody. Witness statements signed. Forensic timestamps." (she taps her stained fingers on the table) "If lawyers move fast and claim defamation, we will need airtight records. The council route buys us legal posture. The outlet route buys us reach."

A man in the doorway — one of the fishers you brought in, salt-streaked and raw from the docks — swallows. He lays out a handwritten ledger and points to a date.

Fisherman: "They told us they'd cover loss. We sold traps, paid fees. The check from 'Voss Coastal' cleared Quincy Bank three days after the meeting. Then the promises changed."

Ben leans in, his voice a gravelly metronome of warning and memory.

Ben: "We did this before. A fancy plan, a 'rescue' that left us with docks no one could use. You remember that; you remember how long it took to fix even a bit of that damage. But you also remember how we got through it — or how we didn't. This town is stubborn, Maya. Use that stubbornness."

Your pulse is a drum now. The kettle's last hiss seems like an echo of your breath. Outside, rain shifts into a harder percussion, knocking at the window as if urging you to hurry. The legal letters — crisp, stamped, threatening — sit like coals in a shallow pan.

Aiden moves to the table and rests both palms on the wood. His voice drops low, and it's as if he is talking to the five-year-old who still believes every storm can be read and weathered.

Aiden: "If you go public, some of my family will call you a liar before breakfast. They'll say we betrayed the town. If you bring it to the council, Elias will smooth everything over in the media and accuse us of obstruction. If you destroy them..." (he looks away) "If you destroy them, will you forgive yourself?"

The room holds its breath. Noor closes her eyes for a fraction of a second, then opens them like someone who has decided something.

Noor: (firm) "Whatever you choose, do it with the knowledge that the aim is to stop harm. The law is a tool; the press is a tool. If you can keep people from being hurt while exposing the pattern, do it. If you can't, sometimes exposure is the medicine."

You think of your brother's hands, the way he held a wet rope when the sea took the house. You think of the compass on Aiden's neck — a small carved thing that never left his side. You think of Ben's steady presence and Rosa's flash of righteous fury. Courage is not absence of fear; it is the willingness to break something in order to build something else.

Noor: (exhaling) "We can make a plan for both speed and legality. Simultaneous tracks. But someone will have to take the first leap."

Rosa: (a grin that is all teeth) "Then let it be you, Maya. You've been holding this like a live coal for months. Throw it where it will start a fire that cleans the rot."

Ben: (soft, a worn edge) "Or bury it, if you think the town will not survive the quake. You choose the kind of wound you're willing to carry."

Aiden: (a whisper, raw) "Don't make a choice to spare yourself. Make one to spare those who can't bear to be broken."

Your hands tremble. The rain is a machine now, attacking the glass. Somewhere below, someone slams a car door and curses. The music swells; the strings are a blade.

You catalog the options like tides on a chart: the urgent tide of national press that will batter lies into splinters; the slow, precise swell of an independent council that will dissect and build a legal scaffold; and the last, low tide — destruction — that would protect the town at your cost.

You taste salt and iron on your tongue. Your voice comes out thin but steady.

You: "We decide how the story starts. We decide what we risk and what we save. I won't let this be about me."

Rosa reaches for the folder you pushed earlier and flips it open with a fierce, careful hand. Noor pulls a pen from behind her ear and begins to draft witness affidavits. Ben leans forward, voice a low rumble, adding dates you almost forgot.

Aiden watches you, every line of him taut. He doesn't tell you what to do; he only offers himself as ballast.

A sudden buzz from your pocket: an anonymous text thread, a legal clerk's number, the first of many threats. The intensity ratchets tighter — injunctions, lawsuits, townspeople who will choose sides in hours, not days.

The decision sits like a lighthouse beam, revolving and impossible to ignore. You can feel the arousal of the night — not merely loud panic but the heightened, razor-sharp energy of people pushed to the brink, ready to act. It is dangerous. It is electric. It is, in its own way, hopeful.

Noor lifts her head, the light catching the soil-dark line under her nails.

Noor: "We have to choose. Tonight. The injunctions make public gatherings illegal in effect. If we don't move, the moment will pass. If we move badly, we risk scattering truth like broken glass. Decide how we release it."

Your throat is dry. Papers rustle, pen caps click. You sense the swell of everything you've worked toward — the saltmarsh plans, the living breakwaters, the thousand patient months of data — compressed into a handful of paper that will either save the town's future or tear it open.

You breathe. You remember your brother's laugh in a kitchen that no longer exists. You remember the smell of wet rope. You remember why you came back to Elara: not to win small fights, but to change the rules that let people lose their homes to neat charts.

The room leans on you like the sea leans on the shore.
