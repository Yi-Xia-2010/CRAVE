# Route: Route A — Ending: Happy

[Scene: Community Rooftop Garden & Meeting Hall | Dawn]
[Visual: Golden-hour light spills over raised beds; strings of fairy lights still twinkle. A low mist threads between solar panels.]
[Sound: Gulls wheel; distant engines of a news van settling into the lot below; the muffled clink of shovels and laughter from the boardwalk.]
[Music: Urgent strings under a bright, hopeful brass motif — tempo quickening]

You wake to gulls and the small electric hum of a phone that won't stop buzzing. Your shoulders complain from the pilot planting — a corporeal memory of salt and dirt — and your field notebook rests like a warm stone against your ribs. Outside the rooftop, Lina is already converting panic into schedules: she moves like someone with a map burned into her palms, clipboard in hand, voice cutting through the garden like a bell.

Lina: "Two-hour shifts, tops. Rotate the kids through weeding after noon; Priya, can you vet the waiver drafts for the minors? Mateo wants a volunteer list he can hand to the co-op."

Priya Anand steps closer, binoculars still swinging at her throat, a small stack of legal templates tucked under one arm. The paper slides against your thumb when she hands you one: neat clauses, funding contingencies, a box for matching funds. Her smile is quick, ally-ready.

Priya Anand: "We can fast-track the permit language if we scope the pilot areas clearly. The NGO can front the application fees, but they'll need a matching commitment from municipal coffers or private donors within thirty days."

You scan the template — clauses that smell faintly of bureaucracy and hope. The rooftop tastes of wet soil and coffee, sunlight collecting on your knuckles where the mud dried. The crowd below thickens; you can feel the town's momentum as a weight and an upward push in your chest. This is not a whisper anymore; it's a chorus.

Aiden Reyes arrives with that wind-ruffled hair and a thermos that looks like it's seen three winters. He drops into an empty crate, boots thumping, eyes scanning the rows of seedlings as if he can read their future by the tilt of their leaves.

Aiden Reyes: "You look like you slept in a pile of Spartina and won." (He grins, then his expression sharpens, careful.) "How's the volunteer roster?"

You trade a look — long enough that something private warms the space between you and the world. It's a look you both can hold and let go of; outside, everyone else needs different kinds of holding.

Maya Kwon: "Packed. Lina's split-shift genius is in effect. Priya's ready with legal drafts, but the NGO wants matching funds soon. Vernon & Crow are feeling the heat—news vans are already circling."

Aiden Reyes: "Good. Momentum's our currency." (He hesitates, then lowers his voice.) "Maya — about planting lines near the north channel. The older fishers are nervous. If we close that strip off, nearshore nets get crowded and fathers like Mateo will—" He stops himself. "We have to keep them working."

You feel the urgency in his words like a quick current. He cares, and that care curls under your ribs like a second tide. You know — in the exact geometry of memory and anger and love — that what you do with the marsh will touch his hands as much as the town's ledger.

[Scene: Marsh Planting Sites | Mid-morning]
[Visual: Mud-streaked volunteers, mismatched rubber boots, sun hats, and flash of orange vests. Newly dug channels reflect the sky in quick, trembling lines.]
[Sound: The slap of water against pilings; low conversation; a boom of a camera shutter from a news van.]
[Music: Percussive rhythm builds; high strings, forward motion]

On the marsh the day detonates into organized chaos: kids with hands too small for the spade laugh when mud sprays them; Old Man Reyes moves through the crowd like a slow tide, each step a story. He pauses by a small cluster where shells rest like tiny altars.

Mateo Reyes: "Don't rush the old beds, little ones." (He taps a scarred palm against a wooden edge.) "There are shrines here. There are lines that have to be walked with a light foot. You remember that, do you?"

You kneel and feel the soft give of the mud, the cold sucking at your fingers. His voice — weathered and sure — threads patience into the work.

Maya Kwon: "I remember. We'll mark the beds. We'll walk the lines. I promise we'll protect them."

Mateo's eyes don't soften so much as they steady. He doesn't look at you like a lover or an opponent — he looks at you like someone who could be trusted to learn the map of a place by listening. That trust matters; it is a kind of currency you have been earning with every dawn.

Priya Anand intercepts you with a terse breath, a tablet screen casting graphs on her face.

Priya Anand: "Granters want numbers. Sediment accretion, Spartina survival — they want the metrics. But to get matching funds we either show municipal support or a contract with an engineering firm. Vernon & Crow would push for the second; it looks good on paper, fast, scalable."

You scan the mud and the faces: the kids with splotchy cheeks, Lina's clenched jaw as she redirects an overenthusiastic volunteer, the way Aiden lingers by a net repaired in the morning light. Your chest tightens — the kind of ache that edges hope with risk.

A rumble of truck brakes, a crisp set of footsteps. Dr. Elara Voss steps down from a raised dune with a clipboard so tidy it looks offensive in this mud-splattered theater.

Dr. Elara Voss: "Good work. Rapid mobilization looks promising." (She snaps a tablet closed.) "I've had conversations with Vernon & Crow. We can deploy mechanized restoration rigs — build living terraces in weeks. It would create immediate jobs and measurable shoreline reduction in risk."

Your first instinct is to say yes. The town, mouths open to weather and bills, needs something that promises speed. But Elara's suit cuts across the marsh like a line on a map, and when she sketches the proposed footprint in the mud with a stick, your stomach drops: a low marsh tongue — the one Mateo just circled — is within the contractor's sweep.

Maya Kwon: "If your rigs carve into that tongue, you sever eelgrass beds and several shellfish patches. That's not just habitat — that's heritage. Mateo just pointed out shrines along that line."

Dr. Elara Voss: "I know the cultural concerns." (She meets your gaze. For a moment her face is unreadable.) "But we can redesign the berm to avoid the most sacred points. The footprint is negotiable if we secure funding—Vernon & Crow want to underwrite a pilot if we can tie in job training."

Aiden Reyes appears at your elbow, voice taut.

Aiden Reyes: "Training's good. Jobs are good. But not if it means fishing grounds get boxed out. Men will lose income before the plants even root."

Dr. Elara Voss: "Then we include access corridors. We also reduce the angle of the terraces. Compromise exists."

Your mind races—every permutation a stack of lives, lost tides, and future songs. The team below cheers as another planting bed takes root; your body thrums with possibility and the wordless insistence that this town deserves better than a fait accompli.

You stand between the stick-sketched future and the real, breathing body of the marsh. Your hands ache from planting, but they are not empty: you can draw lines in the soil, and those lines will be read by others.

[Scene: Vernon & Crow Lobby | Afternoon]
[Visual: Cool fluorescent lights, a polished reception desk, translucent screens showing shoreline models. The lobby smells faintly of coffee and disinfectant.]
[Sound: The low hum of air conditioning, reporters murmuring through the glass doors, the distant slap of a camera lens.]
[Music: Tensioned strings with a bright brass undertow — urgency peaks]

The lobby is clinical and loud with expectation. Reporters hover near the glass, recordings on, pens poised. The firm’s models are sleek and convincing — smart lines and slope angles that promise certainty in a world that has offered little.

Vernon & Crow Representative (Mr. Lydell): "With engineered terraces, insurance rates drop, and investors show confidence. We can scale coast defenses across the county. The pilot here would be a flagship."

You meet Dr. Voss in front of a translucent panel showing before-and-after renderings that make your stomach lurch and your heart swell at once — the 'after' is a softer coastline, but one that eats a curve of marsh you hold in your memory.

Dr. Elara Voss: "We can secure municipal backing quickly if we show our proposal as a jobs and resilience package. The NGO money Priya mentioned uses your community labor as a match as well. We have a path to both speed and scale."

Aiden Reyes stands near the doorway, arms folded, jaw working. His voice carries the same cadence as the sea: patient but inexorable.

Aiden Reyes: "Speed won't mean anything if it takes away the things people fish for. We need corridors for nets. We need to keep access."

Maya Kwon: "And we need the marsh tongue kept intact. That area is a living classroom for the kids and a refuge for shellfish. If we lose it, we lose a part of who we are."

Mr. Lydell: "We can relocate certain functions. Engineering always trades off. But compromise is a tool."

The air hums. Everyone is polite but taut like a wire; the energy crackles. The town's eyes — volunteers turned to pressure groups, news vans turning dossiers — find you. For a breathless moment you feel the current you helped start come back as force. It lifts you like a wave and slaps you with responsibility.

Aiden Reyes steps closer; his expression is complex in a way that makes something in your chest jump — pride, fear, possibility; you cannot parse it into just one name.

Aiden Reyes: "Maya, I trust you to hold the line. But promise me we won't trade access for optics."

Maya Kwon: "I promise I won't sell a line of people's livelihoods for a ribbon-cutting ceremony."

Dr. Elara Voss watches you both, an analyst taking inventory.

Dr. Elara Voss: "Then let's present a blended path to the council: community labor plus targeted contractor support where mechanized help is essential. It gives the town jobs and keeps you in the lead, Maya."

Your heart accelerates — very high, very alive. The rooftop's warm sun memory, the marsh's cold mud, Lina's tireless scheduling, Priya's legal tablets, Mateo's quiet insistence — they compose into a single electric question: how to scale without losing what you love.

You think of Aiden's hand on a repaired net, of Mateo's slow map of shrines, of the kids who stomped through nets with the insouciant joy of the young. You think of the NGO deadlines and the slick promise of Vernon & Crow.

Everything is moving faster now. The news van cameras tilt as if sniffing the future. Volunteers text updates from the marsh. The base of the decision hums like a drum in your bones: urgency, possibility, the very real chance that this moment will shape the town for decades.

You close your eyes for half a second — a breathing pause in a current — and when you open them the decision is not theoretical anymore. It is a door opening on three paths, each with a cost and a promise. Your pulse is a drumroll.
