You sit on the cracked vinyl seat with the recorder cupped in both hands. Its red light is a small, accusing heartbeat in the dim. Your thumb shakes when you press play.

Elias Hart (on recording): "Delay isn't safety. Delay is liability. People can't wait for idealism to move at the speed of tides, Alea. If we don't act this quarter, you'll be looking at bodies of water where houses used to be."

You flinch as if he'd spoken in the room. The diesel-sour air smells heavier now, as if the trailer absorbed his words and exhaled them.

Your chest tightens. The offer sits in the tape like a hot coal: he will elevate you — technical signatory, the face the funders will trust; he will free capital to pilot the living components you keep sketching in margins; he'll let you scale the dune gardens you've only dared imagine. He frames it with the language of care: "humane," "decisive," "we won't wait."

The very possibility of scale opens a corridor in your mind so wide it's dizzying. You see cedar posts planted across the marsh, oyster reefs knitting shorelines, a data set that turns skeptics into converts. You see not just Harborstone saved but ten towns copied after you. The image tastes like the sea-salt you used to lick off your lips when you were six and believed you could hold the horizon.

And then, immediate as a sting, you remember why you pressed record: a tactical lick of fear that wanted leverage. The moral shape of the action is jagged. Recording someone without their consent is a small betrayal; recording Elias Hart — the man who could tip the scale — feels like lopping a branch to block an incoming wave. You justified it in the parking lot: "If he lies, we need proof." You told yourself the town might need that proof someday. Now his softer voice and the way he says "humane" make your justification smell like rust.

Your hand moves, nearly of its own accord, to rewind. Your fingers hover.

Your notebook, taped corner visible from where it leans against the trailer wall, is full of living-shoreline schematics and species lists that smell faintly of field mud and dried algae. You started this work to fix one reckless past decision. Recording him felt like an insurance policy against your own failure. But insurance, you think, is often the slow work of eroding trust.

They turn when you approach, and you cannot read their face cleanly. The expression is layered — relief to see you, fear, and something you know you have the power to scar.

Rin Sato: "You're late. I thought you'd stayed in the trailer arguing the ethics all night."

Alea Maren: "I was...listening."

Rin Sato folds into that word as if it's a small, dangerous animal. "Listening to what?"

You tell them because silence feels like a lie: you tell them about the recording, the softening of Elias Hart's voice, the offer stitched in the tape like a promise and a trap. You speak fast, like pouring out water before the vessel tips.

Rin Sato listens. Their hands flex; you watch the way they measure your face like they're taking inventory of shoes for mending. When you finish, there is a long beat where only the promenade's damp breath answers.

Rin Sato [quiet]: "You recorded him."

You taste the simple sentence — it is not accusation only; it's consternation, hurt, and a complex knot you recognize but cannot untie.

Alea Maren: "I thought—if he goes public and lies, if the board moves without oversight, we might not get any leverage to make the pilot ethical. Evidence can force conditions, Rin. I thought I could secure the funding and the oversight clauses and—"

Your voice cracks on the last syllable.

Rin Sato interrupts, not unkindly. "And you thought you could be the judge and the jury." They step closer, the scent of wet wool and lemony soap. "You think you can tape people for their better good and keep your hands clean."

The accusation lands like a stone in shallow water: it sends concentric rings through your sense of self. You haven't told Rin Sato everything: not the exact clauses, not the part where Elias Hart promised to make you the technical signatory with the authority you crave. You want to. You want to show them the recording and hold its frankness like a proof that the ends justify the means. You also want to protect the town from a rupture that could fracture the very social fabric that makes community mitigation possible.

Rin Sato: "If this gets out — if you use it — people will say you weaponized trust. They'll say you compromised the very things we say we stand for."

You almost say: "But if I don't, families will lose roofs and livelihoods." The sentence evaporates. You remember the child lost in the old flood; the image is a fissure through your ribs.

Rin Sato's hand is suddenly on your forearm, gentle but firm. "Don't make them choose between my trust and the town's future, Alea Maren."

There it is: the intimate tie that turns choices into betrayals, a friction you both avoid and orbit. Their touch is not an offering of rapprochement; it is an anchor. You feel both steadied and suffocated.
