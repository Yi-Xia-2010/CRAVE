# Route: Route B (From Chapter 8 Option C)


[Scene: Old Pier | Night — Storm Surge]
[Visual: The pier under a low, bruised sky; modular panels and scaffolding slick with rain]
[Music: High, urgent percussion; wind-driven strings]
[Sound: Waves slamming, ropes creaking, distant shouts]

You are soaked to the skin. Rain threads down the curve of your glasses until the world smears into streaked halos of white and sodium light. Sea-glass teal hair clings to your temple; the single silver streak flashes like a sudden alarm. Your jacket is heavier than it should be, salt and rain pulling at the fabric. Every breath tastes of iron and brine.

The prototype’s panels scream against the wind—metal complaining, seams flexing—and then, with a sound like something giving way inside a giant chest, a seam buckles. A thumbnail of floodwater rips through, tearing at the platform's edge. The modular panels, the “temporary” armor that was supposed to keep this night from being a catastrophe, shudder and fold like paper.

You shout until your voice is raw—half instruction, half accusation—at the technicians strung along the scaffolding. Their faces are pale under headlamps. Tools flash; someone curses about a grounding fault. You hear a sensor chirp, then a sequence of tones that is wrong: frequency spikes, an alert that Dr. Lian’s portable recorder will later call “scouring events near the fishing channels.” The word sits in your chest like a stone.

Dr. Lian: "Marin—(yelling over wind)—the sediment displacement is off every model we ran. Look—look at this readout!"  
You press closer to the tablet she holds, water beading on its glass. Numbers strobe red. The taste of vertigo presses at the back of your throat.

You: "What does that mean, Lian? Say it plain."  
Dr. Lian: (voice clipped) "It means the flow found a weakness—channeling where we never anticipated. If it keeps cutting, it'll undercut the launch lanes. Small skiffs won't find grip."

Kaito is at your shoulder, breath coming fast, hazel eyes flashing with panic wrapped tight around fury. One of his crew—thin-shouldered, young, with a rope burn along his wrist—has just staggered up the pier, face as white as the foam. The skiff they’d tried to run took a wall of water sideways and bobbed like a toy. You can still see the arc of salt in the air where it nearly flipped.

Kaito: "They nearly lost Toma's boy out there—nearly."  
You: "We stopped that from turning into… what? A body count?" (Your voice is raw with anger you can’t afford.)  
Kaito: "You think I don't see what happened? Those panels—Aria's setup—it's not built for the channels we run. It keeps the big surge out of the main bay but it redirects here. We—" He chokes on the word. "We lose mornings if they close it."

Aria stands near the prototype hub, coat plastered to her frame, hair slick against the precise line of her jaw. Her face is a pale plane of composure; she moves with the kind of controlled energy that used to calm you. Tonight it feels like an accusation. She kneels, fingers hovering over a control slate, then looks up at you.

Aria: (cool, loud) "We followed every specified tolerance. The seam failure is isolated—"  
You: "Isolated? Isolated? People almost got swept—"  
Aria: "—and we will deploy countermeasures. The array will be reinforced. We have redundancy."  
You: (cutting) "And the scouring—Lian's sensors recorded channeling at the exact points the co-op uses every dawn. What is your redundancy for livelihoods, Aria?"

She meets your gaze, unflinching. For a long beat the rain seems to hush around the two of you, as if the air itself is waiting for her answer.

Aria: "You know the math, Marin. We can scale defenses across the region. This system reduces risk by orders of magnitude for thousands."  
You: "And at what cost? Orders of magnitude of what? Access? Tradition? The simple fact that your 'reduction' turned a fisherman's launch into a hazard?"

Jun Park appears at your elbow then, cheeks slick, breath catching. He is younger—nervous energy like a thrown stone—and his jaw is tight in a way that makes his voice small.

Jun: "Marin—" (hands you a damp dossier, corners softened by rain) "I—this clause is buried deep. It's in the maintenance and safety protocols." He swallows. "It lets TideLine crews restrict launches under 'safety protocols'—morning closures, access limitations. It's legal cover."

Your fingers find the paper as if of their own accord. The font swims. The language is precise, clinical, written to be unremarkable until you read the implication out loud.

You: (reading, voice thin) "'TideLine maintenance personnel shall have the authority to suspend public launch operations when deemed necessary for system integrity and public safety.'"

You feel the sentence like a hand closing around your throat. The idea of morning launches—the ritual sunrise that keeps families fed and schedules intact—seized by corporate "safety" is an edible, crushing betrayal.

Old Tomas, leaning on his cane on the near end of the pier, laughs—his laugh sharp and broken by more than years. He spits into the wind.

Old Tomas: "They'll tell us it's for safety. They'll tell us it's for the greater good. But whose good? Whose tide?"

The crowd on the boardwalk has thickened; torchlamps and phone lights bob like a flotilla of warning beacons. Voices rise in a rough chorus: blame is a quick currency. "Traitors." "Sellouts." "We trusted you." The sound is a physical force, hitting the platform in waves that nearly match the ocean's own.

Mayor Ana is at the railing of the boardwalk, coat thrown over the rain like a thin shield. Her face is set, the public official's mask stretched tight. She lifts her hands, trying to pacify; the motion is small and human and not quite enough.

Mayor Ana: "Please—please, everyone—let us hear Dr. Lian and the TideLine technicians. We need facts, not accusations—"  
Crowd Member: "Facts? Facts got us a broken pier and a near-death!"

You press the dossier flat against your chest, each breath a pressure that makes the paper whisper. The fabric of community trust is flayed, and you can feel the exposed fibers in your own hands.

You keep watching the prototype with a professional's eye even as your stomach folds tight. The panels don't just fail; they reveal a blueprint of priorities. Scale, efficiency, measurable reductions in risk—the language of safety that erases small patterns of living. And the sea keeps chewing at the boardwalk. You are halfway between wanting to weep and wanting to break something solid.

Dr. Lian: (urgent) "Marin—these readings are clear. The baseline models didn't account for this coupled feedback. If the panels redirect flow into the channels repeatedly, we will see progressive scouring."  
You: "Which means—"  
Dr. Lian: "—more closures, more restrictions, and the co-op loses its launch windows. The system's safeguards will be the mechanism by which access is controlled."

Kaito's hand finds yours, grip tight and hot through fabric. He is both anchor and grenade. His face is wet, grief and rage crosscut and raw.

Kaito: "We can rebuild this pier with the co-op. We know the channels. We know the tides… We can—" (he bites his lip) "—we can hold back if we need to, but not if they put locks on our mornings."  
You: "You mean work with me, Kaito? To prove a different way?"  
Kaito: "I mean—Marin—do you want to be the person who negotiates with them or the person who screams at them until they leave?"

Your throat constricts. This is not just a technical choice; it is the axis upon which your identity as someone who bridged science and community spins. You remember nights in the greenhouse, algae tanks pulsing teal, the quiet vow you wrote: Bridge. Listen. Protect. You feel that vow go brittle in the wind.

Aria, watching you orchestrate—sometimes leaning into collaboration, sometimes standing at a remove—finally approaches, rainwater carving dark lines down her suit.

Aria: (low, not conciliatory) "Marin. You asked for oversight. You have it. We offered joint monitoring. You took it."  
You: "Joint monitoring doesn't mean joint outcomes when your clauses let you lock the door and declare it 'safety'."  
Aria: (a flinch) "Our legal teams framed that clause as contingency. They expected provisional maintenance windows—"  
You: "Contingency becomes permanence when it's written into who gets to use the shore."

Jun lingers at her shoulder, eyes darting to the assembled crowd. He swallows hard.

Jun: "There are ways to amend it—through the mayor's office. Quiet fixes can avoid a public panic—"  
You: (bitter) "A quiet fix erases the town's voice while pretending to preserve it."

A woman from the crowd pushes forward, rain-sleek hair sticking to her cheek. Her son's face is puffy with salt and cold and exhaustion.

Crowd Woman: "My son gets up at four. He can't fish at noon. What are we supposed to do if they tell him he can't go out at dawn?"  
Mayor Ana: (hands lifting) "I will negotiate. We can—"  
Crowd Member: "Negotiate while our boats rot?"

The pier thrums with a collective animal sound—cry, argument, the metallic heartbeat of machinery. Every person is a small bell of accusation and fear. You feel the chain of responsibility running down your arms as if you could physically brace a broken world.

A technician wrenches at a rail; a bolt snaps and a piece of equipment clangs into the water. Someone screams. You lurch to the edge, stomach lurching with it. The sea has already taken more than it should.

Aria's voice rises—calm like a blade—cutting through the chaos.

Aria: "We can issue a statement. We will repair the seam. We have mobile teams—"  
You: "A statement won't bring back the morning the boy nearly drowned. A statement won't change the clause that gives your crews the right to halt launches."

Jun steps closer, hands up like a man trying to steady a sliding scale.

Jun: "Marin—if you go public with this now, it will be legal war. TideLine will push back. They will leverage every contract clause. We will be in court; money talks."  
You: "And if we don't? If we let their clause become precedent so 'safety protocols' become control—what then?"

Your chest is a tight knot. The sky is a black fist. The town's future—its rituals, livelihoods, and small mercies—hangs off a legal phrase made elegant by lawyers and cold by design. The very thing you promised to protect trembles.

Old Tomas speaks again, voice like gravel.

Old Tomas: "Marin, you know what the sea can do. You also know what people do when they are scared. Choose so we can move. Don't let us hang on the edge like fish hooks waiting to be pulled."

Everything accelerates—waves, shouts, heartbeats. The prototype's lights strobe as backup systems kick in; generators cough. The mood has shifted from panic to a sharpened decision: fight loud, fight through the halls of power, or try to prove a different path by rebuilding where their tech failed.

You can feel the moment tightening around your shoulders like a harness. This is no longer just about engineering models or a grant. This is about sovereignty: who gets to decide who uses the shore when the sea is angry.

Your notebook, soaked and folded in your jacket, presses against your ribs. Your pen is a small, stubborn thing. You can taste metal and rain and the bitter tang of knowing any choice will wound someone you love.

You look out at the surf, the crowd, at Kaito—hands still callused and wanting—and at Aria's stoic face, where conviction and ambition are braided into something dangerous. Jun's damp dossier rests against the wet wood at your feet like a loaded question.

You inhale. The storm answers.
