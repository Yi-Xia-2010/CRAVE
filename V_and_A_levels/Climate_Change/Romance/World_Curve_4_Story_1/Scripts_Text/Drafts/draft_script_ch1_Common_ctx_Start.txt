# Route: Route C — Alternative / Catastrophic Ending


[Scene: Lira Bay Harbor | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the crescent harbor at low tide; long reflections on rippled water]
[Music: Low, coastal ambient—distant gulls, soft synth pads]
[Sound: Wooden planks creak, a buoy knocking rhythmically, distant engine hum]

You step down from the bus with your bag slung across one shoulder and your Moleskine damp and warm under your hand. The boardwalk smells like old rope and seaweed—salt and a faint diesel tang that folds into the memory of countless summers. Your jacket is still stiff with mud where you kneel in sample plots; the elbows bear the stitches you made on the ferry two days ago. The air holds that peculiar heaviness that comes before rain, but the light is golden, forgiving.

You look out across the flats. The marsh channels are different than in the photos you kept on your phone—narrowed in places, opened in others, silt drawn into odd barbs like a script you almost understand. At low tide the channels map the bones of the bay; they tell stories of where water ran and where it didn't. You press your thumb to the leather cover of your Moleskine and feel the pulse of a familiar urgency—that same quiet tug that made you leave grad lectures for tidal mud and late-night lab runs.

Narration: You inventory the obvious: tide is low enough for wrack sampling at first light, the south spur has silted toward the western reeds, and the old marker buoy by Dock 4 lists at a shallower angle. Less obvious: the lane of boats is tighter than you remember; some pylons are newly reinforced. People adjust before thinking they have to — you see that even here.

[Scene: Pier | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Close-up of splintered planks underfoot; Rita stands with a floral scarf tied tight]
[Sound: Rita humming under her breath, a gull calling near the lantern house]
[Music: Soft acoustic strum]

Rita Soler: "You finally came back, niña."

You let your shoulders drop in a way you didn't realize you were holding them. Rita's voice is a small knot of warmth and reproach. She hasn't changed—small frame, hands like dried sea fans—but the way she stands against the wind makes it clear she has been holding ground for everyone.

You: "I had to. The grant paperwork took forever, but it wasn't the papers I worried about." (You rub the mud from your sleeve.) "I wanted to see the flats before the next tide."

Rita Soler: "Good. They need your eyes. And so do I." (She studies your jacket, then your face.) "How's the city treating you? Too many suits talking about numbers and forgetting what the bay sounds like?"

You consider the rightness of that answer. Evelyn Hart had been mentioned in emails—polished proposals, feasibility studies—but Rita's question threads to something older than policy talk: practical memory. You remember the house on the hill that used to fold around family dinners when storms came in; you remember how it felt to be small and helpless on the porch.

You: "The city has good maps. They don't have the tides that hit midnight kids. They don't have Rita's memory." (You try to make it light; it lands as a soft confession.) "I missed the sound of these pilings."

Rita Soler: "You missed it, eh? Then you'll know what to do." (Her smile is brief, knowing.) "But don't be soft with them. They like to dress urgency as inevitability."

You: "And you? How's home—your garden, the roof—"

Rita Soler: "I've got the gutter patched. And I've been keeping an ear on Arin. He was down near the lantern house this morning. He couldn't stop fussing over his nets."

Rita's look toward the pier carries a weight you both understand. People here move in patterns neither of you invented but both must respect.

Rita lets out a small chuckle that dissolves any pretence. She reaches inside her cardigan and pulls out a folded paper map, edges softened by years of hands.

Rita Soler: "You should see their new markers. Mayor's boys say it's progress. But progress sometimes means bulldozers you don't need."

You take the map. Your fingers flatten against the paper and trace the new lines—outsized concrete zones where your restoration plots should be. Your tongue presses the inside of your cheek as you file the sight into the ledger in your head: where to prioritize coring, who to call, who to ask for a favor.

You: "I'll look at those margins first thing. If we stagger plantings along the north spit we might slow that silt." (You close your Moleskine reflexively, as if sealing the plan.) "But I'll need to get the logger out and sync it. The tide rhythms may have shifted."

Rita Soler: "You always talk like that. Promise me you won't forget warm hands when you talk to cold maps." (She studies you, soft and fierce.) "Promise me you'll eat before you go to your trailer."

You promise. You mean it.

[Scene: Fisherman's Pier & Lantern House | Dusk]
[Visual: The lantern house silhouettes; Arin stands with his back to the bay, cap pulled low]
[Sound: Waves whispering at low tide, the distant thump of a winch]
[Music: Low, rhythmic—heartbeat tempo]

Arin Kai: (without turning fully) "Maya."

His voice is even; somehow calm enough to be an anchor. He pulls his cap off as you approach, scuffed boots planted on the wet wood. Up close the brass compass at his throat catches the last light. He looks older around the eyes—days spent in wind can do that.

You: "Arin." (You nod, letting the name be a bridge.) "How's the boat?"

Arin Kai: "It's holding. Took a new rib for a month, but it's back in the slip." (He shifts, the faintly defensive motion of someone used to shouldering risk.) "How have you been—field work, big city life? Or both?"

You: "Both. Mostly trying to put them in the same sentence without making them lie." (You force a small smile.) "I need to walk the flats tonight if the water allows."

Arin's jaw tightens, a small thing that makes him honest.

Arin Kai: "Be careful. The south channel's been sneaky. Took a crab pot yesterday I wasn't expecting." (He points, then retracts the hand—an apology for being blunt.) "If you're thinking of planting near the spit, know there are boats that need passage. My folks—"

He stops, the rest of the sentence suspended between practical defense and something more personal. You hear in it the rhythm of livelihoods, the old ways entwined with the new science you bring.

You: "I know. I don't want to cut anyone off. Restoration doesn't mean closing the bay. It means making room—for fish, for nets, for the town." (You try to find a way to say that that doesn't sound like a lecture. You fail a little, then recover.) "I want us to keep working the water."

Arin studies you, amber eyes narrowed by sun and thought.

Arin Kai: "That's what you said before. It sounded good then. It sounds good now." (He looks away at the horizon where the sky bruises.) "But you coming back—are you bringing papers or promises? People are tired of promises."

You: "I'm bringing data. And plans. And—" (You hesitate, thinking of your stubborn compassion.) "—I'm bringing myself. For now, that's what I have."

Arin Kai: (he lets out a breath that might be a laugh or a sigh) "Good. That's one thing this bay can't run out of." (Then, quieter) "Don't forget—if you need a skiff to check the outer markers, tell me. I owe you for that sandwich you smuggled me at grad school."

You both smile then—an easy, small thing that grounds the conversation in years you don't need to catalog. The air between you is complex: comfortable, understated, the quiet of two people who know the same geography of fear and hope.

Arin Kai: "Walk with me to the trailer. Rita says you've got a logger to sync. If it's drinking batteries like last time, I'm going to swear and then help."

You: "Deal." (You head toward the lab trailer with him, the two of you falling into a companionable silence that holds as much as your words.)

[Scene: Lab Trailer | Dusk]
[Visual: Interior of a cramped lab trailer—snap hooks, taped charts, a battered centrifuge in the corner]
[Sound: The soft click of a refrigerator, a kettle faintly boiling in the distance]
[Music: Sparse piano tones]

You set your bag down on the folding table. The trailer smells like ethanol and boiled coffee—work smells, honest and unromantic. You strap your wet boots off, setting them to the side to dry. The wrist logger sits like a small, blinking true north on your forearm; its screen flickers with nocturnal statistics.

Narration: You run a quick diagnostic. Battery: good. Last sync: three months ago in the deep lab. Timestamp: oddly offset—local time is a hair out. Data packets appear segmented—your absence left gaps, not in the bay but in your record of it.

You: "The logger's fine. Just needs a time update and a field note." (You tap the device; a soft chime answers.) "I'll sync with the tide station tonight. Get a baseline before the rain pushes through."

Arin Kai: "You always make the instruments sound like people." (He leans across the table and points to a smear of silt on your sleeve.) "Hey, patch that jacket. The tear on the elbow's going to catch more than mud if a winter blow comes."

You: (You pull out a needle and some strong thread from your kit, habitually practical.) "I can fix it. It won't take long."

Arin Kai: "I'll hold the light." (He reaches for a flashlight and then hesitates.) "Tell me something—do you still think the marsh can come back the way you teach in your papers? Or has being away made you change the question?"

The question sits between you, respectful and direct. It's not an accusation—it's an invitation.

You: (You look up at him, needle poised.) "Being away sharpened the limits: funding is narrow, storms are wider, and people's patience has shrunk. But it also gave me perspective. Restoration isn't about bringing back a past marsh exactly. It's about designing a future one that people can live with." (You make the first stitch.)

Arin Kai: "Designing and living with. That's a hard mix." (He watches your hands, the deliberate motion of mending.) "Do you think we can do both without breaking either?"

You: (You tap the logger, eyes on the small glowing screen.) "I think we can—if we listen to the tides and to each other. If we map the human geography the same way we map the water. Names of skippers, times they fish, where kids learn to swim. Those things matter as much as salinity curves."

Arin's mouth twitches, almost a smile that doesn't quite arrive.

Arin Kai: "You say it like a plan. I like plans that have room for the weather." (He picks up a spare patch kit and hands it to you.) "I'll source you some better thread. My mum's got a sewing box that survived three moves."

You: "Thanks. And bring her a jar of the pickled lemons you were bragging about." (The exchange is small and domestic; it makes the trailer feel more like home than the municipal hall ever will.)

Narration: You stitch. The motion is steady, almost meditative. The clouds outside thicken, a wash of pewter sliding across the western sky. The trailer's heat hums. Outside, the harbor breathes slow and low.

You finish the patch. Your jacket smells faintly of thread and coffee; it's better for the repair. The logger blinks a steady light; your notes accumulate like small islands of intent.

Narration: Standing in the trailer, you let the day's inventory settle: the silted channels, Rita's map, Arin's guarded optimism, and your own stitched jacket. There's work to do—mapping points, re-taking transects, calling a meeting with Lina and the Mayor. None of that feels urgent in the way a siren is urgent; it's a steady, persistent pull. Low arousal—methodical, deliberate.

Arin Kai: "You want to take a look at the north spit before dark? The tide's generous for now." (He folds his arms, waiting.) "Or are you going to hole up and make lists?"

You look at your Moleskine, then at the bay where the channel narrows, then at the sky—clouds thickening, but still some light on the horizon.

You: "One last walk. I need to see how the new silt fingers behave up close. Then I'll start the lists."

Arin Kai: (he nods) "I'll come. Old habits die hard—might as well form new ones together."

Narration: The two of you gather gear—meter, corer, a coil of rope. Outside, the light softens further; the harbor takes on a cool wash. Somewhere a dog barks, but mostly there's the slow rhythm of things that endure.

You breathe in the sea and feel the traction of return: familiar, complicated, necessary.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand closing the Moleskine; camera lingers on the cover as rain tastes the air]
[Music: A single piano note holds and fades]

You walk out of the trailer with Arin at your side and Rita's map folded in the pocket of your patched jacket. The moment hums with small resolve—the kind that builds quietly.
