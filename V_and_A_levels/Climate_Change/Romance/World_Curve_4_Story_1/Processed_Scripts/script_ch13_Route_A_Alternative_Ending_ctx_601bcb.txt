The packet you asked Lina to file — every redline, every obscure clause Calder's draft hid two pages deep — is open on the podium like a wound. You thought transparency would breathe light where there was darkness. Now the light is hot and unforgiving.

Maya Soler: "These clauses hand over access to marsh margins, not for restoration, but for control—easements that prioritize redevelopment footprints over habitat buffers. Lina Park: "You said bring the receipts, Maya. You said we'd make them answer."

You watch the chamber fracture into angles of accusation. Reporters press forward. Phones light faces into halos. Someone in the back screams for a comment about jobs. Someone else whispers "lawsuit" like an invocation. Panic is contagious; it spreads through the crowd faster than any measured argument.

Evelyn Hart [cold, precise]: "You knew the political risk. Transparency is noble until it destabilizes a community mid-crisis."
Her eyes are steel-blue, unblinking. "Do you want any more families out of work?"

You hear the edge in Evelyn Hart's question and the weight of it lands like a punch to the ribs — not because it's new, but because the loss she hints at is immediate now. The grant backers freeze their transfers overnight. Legal notices start to arrive with the hunger of wolves.

You open your mouth to answer, to articulate that the cost of secrecy was already being paid in small, slow ways — the clogged channels, the juvenile counts cut, the sinking bank behind Rita's porch — but every word you might give is swallowed by the room's rising panic.

Names you would have assumed unwavering — young fathers, neighbors who once held Rita's fence — are loading gear into vans with careful, distant hands.

Noah Reyes: "It's the offers. Inland work, construction upstate, anything that pays steady."
He tries to smile; it doesn't reach his freckled eyes. "They won't risk the lawsuit. Not with kids to feed."

You step into the mud and the silence hits like cold water. Without Calder's bridge funds, the stipends that kept volunteers turning up are gone. Promises of payroll are written in a ledger now frozen by lawyers. Momentum decays faster than you can marshal it.

Maya Soler: "We can re-prioritize. Smaller crews, citizen science shifts — we can stretch what we have."
You try to offer a plan. Your voice is rational, but you feel the tremor under it.

Lina Park: "We can, but the people on those paychecks don't have the luxury of hope."
She looks away, the practical brutality in her face. "We can ask for donations, for pro bono legal counsel, but that won't pay for fuel or rent."

Arin Kai: "They're offering incentives. My cousin got a job inland today. Said it's steady, pays better than the fishing season lately."
He approaches from the boardwalk, salt on his collar. He speaks mostly to you; his jaw is tight. "You knew this would cause a stir. Did you think how many would be pushed to leave immediately?"

Every word Arin Kai says lands like a pebble in your chest, widening a ring of guilt. You thought honesty would forge a durable future. Instead it has started an immediate, brutal migration — people choosing a guaranteed paycheck over the slow, uncertain promise of ecological repair.

[Background:"ch13_601bcb_4"]

Arin waits with his back to the water, hands shoved into his coat. When he turns, his eyes are a quiet thing that overloads into anger.

Arin Kai: "Did you think of my father's nets? Of the quota rules they'll tie to every 'environmental offset' now? You dragged this out in public without a plan for people who need to eat."
He doesn't shout; the calm makes it worse.

You search for the argument that will ease this, some syllable that will braid your ideals and his livelihood back together.

Maya Soler: "I thought about them every hour. I thought about Rita's house sliding another foot down the bank. I thought about the kids who play in the marsh grasses and the fish that won't come back if we sell the margins off. I thought about the future."
You stop. The words are not defense; they are confession.

Arin Kai: "That's easy to say when your future is your work. My future is my family. My brother's been offered steady work inland. He told me he won't risk being the one who stays here and watches the rest of us flounder because someone read a clause out loud."
He steps closer, lowering his voice until it is sharp with hurt. "Do you think I wanted this?"

Your throat tightens. You had imagined standing together in storms, elbows scraped and hands working in sync. Instead you have a gulf that smells of diesel and salt and unshed tears.

Maya Soler: "I didn't want this either. But hiding it would have made the costs worse later. We would have paid them in a single, larger catastrophe."
Your words are brittle.

Arin Kai [bitter humor]: "A single catastrophe, or a thousand smaller ones? You can't trade slow pain for sudden pain and call it justice."

The pier creaks under your weight. Behind Arin the horizon starts to darken — cloud eating cloud, a bruise crawling toward the bay. You can feel the weather answering the town's unraveling as if the sky itself is weighing accountability.

Maya Soler: "What do you want me to say? That I made the wrong call? That secrecy was better? Because I cannot live with myself for that."
You look at him, empty of rhetorical armor.

Arin Kai: "I don't want an apology I can't use. I want us to survive together."
He swallows. "But survival looks different now. Maybe it doesn't include both of us staying the same way."

Silence spreads like oil between you. The lanterns swing in a sudden gust and throw angled shadows across your faces. You try to picture a compromise — smaller restorations married to temporary job programs — but the backers have retreated and the town's trust, like a torn net, is full of holes.

You want to say the kind of thing that patches more than it wounds — that truth is a seed that can root in hard places — but the sky is uncooperative tonight. The rain is a hand that tries to pull everything away.

Lina Park [breathless]: "Funding's frozen. We've got legal notices. The volunteers who stayed are exhausted. The hearings haven't stopped the panic; if anything they've amplified fear. Some shelters are filling."

Mayor Tomas Greer [on your radio, voice thin]: "We've dispatched crews, but the tide rose faster than forecast. There are people trapped on the second floors of the seafood market."

The terrace gives way to stairwell to pier to street and—the town is a chorus of alarms and shouted names. Your hands move on habit, but your mind is fractured: every action you take now feels like a balance between saving a person and speeding the economic exodus you triggered.

Arin Kai: "We will rebuild what we can. I don't know if I'll stay. I don't know if you will either."
He breathes, the admission like letting water out of a slowly filling jar. "But tonight—tonight we did what mattered."

You let the admission settle like ash. The cost of exposure is visible: lives rerouted, labor thinned, a neighborhood awash. You can catalog the moral victory until your throat is hoarse, but the sight of boarded-up porches and the rows of empty toolsheds is a counterweight no moral ledger can fully reconcile.
