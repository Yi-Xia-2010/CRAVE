[Scene: Seawall Construction Site | Afternoon]

You stand on a strip of packed earth that smells of wet concrete and diesel. Wind slices the air in sharp gusts; it tugs at your braid and whistles through the skeleton of the seawall. The crowd is a living tide — fishermen in oil-streaked coats, elderly faces shaded by scarves, a smattering of reporters, a dozen municipal workers in high-visibility vests. Camera shutters click like a nervous insect hive.

Evelyn Hart stands to your right — immaculate even at a construction site, coat pinned against the wind. She holds the mic with the practiced poise of someone who has made a life out of steering decisions. She does not look at you to steady herself; she looks at the town.

Evelyn Hart: "This is the beginning. Phase One secures the harbor, protects Main Street, and brings immediate jobs. We will follow with ecological offsets, adaptive monitoring, and community investment."

Her words land like promissory cheques. The crowd swells; someone whistles. Mayor Tomas Greer steps forward, voice roughened but buoyant.

Mayor Tomas Greer: "We asked for protection. Today, we get it. Funding is locked. Work starts now."

You feel that collective relief—the inhale of a town that has been waiting for rescue. It pulses through you and sets your jaw. Somewhere in the back of your mind a file opens: sediment transport models, marsh edge retreat rates, the brittle list of species that won't tolerate sudden scour. Your training unfolds in a cascade, precise and unignorable.

Maya Soler: "If I may—"

(you step to the mic; the crowd hushes)

Your voice is steadier than you feel. The scientist in you wants the ledger to be visible: the tradeoffs, the unknowns, the ways a seawall moves problems downshore and severs the living shore. But the political current here is strong and raw. Evelyn's eyes flick to you — not cold, not warm, just measuring.

Evelyn Hart [soft]: "Maya has been instrumental in modeling the hybrid approach we discussed. She'll advise on ecological offsets as we phase the construction."

The crowd reacts with a new wave of approval. Someone calls your name like a benediction. In the space between Evelyn's syllables and the applause, your internal map redraws itself — you as legitimator, the scientist lending authority to a policy you know will hurt some habitats. The thought lands like a pebble in your chest and makes the surface ripple.

Your internal monologue runs, crisp and involuntary: The seawall will reduce tidal inundation immediately along the pier and lower Main Street's flood frequency by the modeled thirty to forty percent. But the marshes: diminished sediment supply, altered current velocities; the north spit will narrow and the silt fingers will be starved. Species that rely on gradual inundation will lose nursery grounds. There's mitigation on paper—transplants, constructed marsh cells—but the success probabilities are noisy and long-term.

You take the mic again, trading a raw critique for a tempered line you can live with publicly.

Maya Soler: "We need to measure, monitor, and adapt. If Phase One proceeds, I want a guaranteed monitoring fund written into the contract, a science advisory seat at the decision table, and a timeline for adaptive management. We can't stabilize the town at the cost of the thing that lets us harvest and heal."

Evelyn's posture doesn't change, but there's a small shift: the angle of her chin, the minuscule tuck of a shoulder. She sees the leverage and the optics. This is negotiation.

Evelyn Hart [affable, quick]: "That's reasonable. We can set aside oversight funding and establish a community advisory board. Maya, we will ensure your team has access to site data and a formal role in adjustments."

Mayor Tomas Greer: "Agreed. Let's get these agreements on the table."

Applause roars. Flashbulbs pop. You feel—immediately—a thin, bright thread of relief. Jobs, immediate protection, money. People around you relax for the first time in months. You should be carried on that relief. Instead, a cold countercurrent pulls at your feet: the cost, and the faces who will say you sold them short.

You choose to temper your public insistence into a formal request rather than an immediate challenge. The cameras catch the gesture: you and Evelyn in apparent alignment. A reporter grabs your elbow and asks for a sound bite; you give a concise line about collaboration and "evidence-based adaptation." It is a tidy phrase that feels both honest and insufficient.

The press briefing melts into a small, ramshackle celebration—folding chairs, coffee, sandwiches wrapped in greaseproof paper. Men and women clap backs on the shoulder; a foreman hands out hard hats. Conversations bloom like algae along the edges of the site: contracts, subcontractors, timelines. Every mention of money makes the ground shore up beneath you.

Lina Park finds you near a stack of plan rolls, her beanie pulled low, the megaphone slung at her side like a wound. Her face is a landscape of compressed lines.

Lina Park [brittle]: "You actually stood there and let her do that."

Maya Soler: "I didn't 'let'—I negotiated oversight. We have to get protections funded if anything will happen."

Lina Park: "You lent her credibility. You gave the town an excuse to trade away long-term resilience for a quick fix and a construction contract."

Her words come out sharp and fast, each syllable a small stone. People glance over. Lina's hands tremble not with cold but with the violence of righteous fury.

Maya Soler: "Lina, I'm trying to make this work. Without funding and infrastructure, people lose homes. This gives us a seat at the table."

Lina Park [bitter laugh]: "A seat on a table that's already been set. How many times do we watch 'adaptive management' become 'we'll see what happens'? You know what we found last year at the Flats—"

Her voice drops; it becomes the soft, terrible accounting of small losses. You could talk samples and success probabilities until the tide turns, but Lina wants to be heard, not reasoned with.

> Choice A: "Apologize and promise to push harder for community-led measures"
> Choice B: "Defend your decision: point to immediate jobs and the fact that money is secured"
[Outcome A: "You reach for her hand, voice low. Lina pulls back an inch but doesn't step away entirely; there's the ghost of a truce there, fragile as glass."]
[Outcome B: "You cross your arms and plant your feet. Lina's face hardens; she folds her arms too. Her eyes say you won't bend. The distance between you takes shape in the cold air."]
[MERGE: The narrative continues.]

You try to bridge the gap. Your words are earnest and exact: "I'll make the monitoring and mitigation legally binding. I'll bring volunteers, our data sets—I'll work with you, Lina." She looks at you as if searching for something behind your words: consistency, courage, allegiance. Her reply is a slow inhale.

Lina Park: "We need more than promises, Maya. We need trust. Don't ask us to trade that away."

The confrontation leaves a residue on your skin. Your chest tightens; adrenaline hums through veins like saltwater. You scan for Arin.

He is at the edge of the crowd, hands shoved in his pockets, jaw clenched. When he sees you, something in his posture—guarded, taut—becomes an accusation. He walks up to you, boots slapping the compacted gravel.

Arin Kai [low]: "You okay? You were—" (he searches for the right word) "—fronting for them."

Maya Soler: "I'm trying to get protections that will keep people from losing their homes. This is real funding, Arin."

Arin Kai [bitter]: "At what cost? You didn't tell me you were going to be the face of it."

You look at him. Wind tangles the ends of his hair. The sunlight hits the brass of his compass pendant, and for a moment the memory of the two of you walking the spit before dark flares: quiet, practical, intimate.

Maya Soler: "I told you I would engage. We talked about pragmatic steps—"

Arin Kai (interrupting): "Talked? Maya, you stepped up there and signed us to a message we didn't craft. People are already saying it's the end of the marsh. Lina thinks you sold out."

You feel the word "sold" like a blade. It slices into the place where your stubborn compassion lives. Anger rises first—not at Arin but at the impossible geometry of the situation: every solution pushes something else off a cliff.

Maya Soler: "I didn't sell out. I traded caveats for leverage. If I push too hard now, the funding pulls. If I don't, there's nothing to fund the monitoring."

Arin Kai [soft]: "And if the monitoring fails? If the wall does what walls do—shift the problem? Do you realize what that would mean for my family's boat? For everyone who can't just move inland?"

A sudden gust throws sand into your face. Salt sticks to your lips. The crowd's chatter becomes a distant roar. The sound of a hammer on steel punctures the air like a metronome counting down the hours before the tide.

Your internal monologue accelerates — not just models now but images: the hatch on Arin's boat buckled in a surge, Rita's hands folding as she remembers her childhood shoreline, children wading where the marsh used to soften the current. Each image amplifies the ache. Your decision feels less like strategy and more like choosing whom to protect and whom to leave behind.

Evelyn appears at your side again, smooth as tide-worn stone.

Evelyn Hart: "Maya's guidance will ensure the project is both effective and responsible. We need to move. Every delay costs lives and livelihoods."

Her words are clinical, but there's a tremor in the set of them — urgency braided with calculation. The construction foreman signals that the first concrete pour will start by tomorrow. A contract template is already moving through a municipal office that hasn't yet seen your edits.

You know what comes next: legal drafts, clauses, funding milestones. These are the levers that will fix or fracture everything you've fought for. The arousal swells to a high, tight knot: the prospect of immediate relief and the knowledge of deferred ecological cost. The public record now lists your name beside the plan. You are both ally and compromiser; both roles will be used against you.

Mayor Tomas Greer [raising his voice to be heard]: "We have a council meeting scheduled to finalize the agreements. I recommend we convene with Evelyn and Maya to iron out the oversight language."

You catch Arin's eye. He looks at you as if waiting to see whether you'll stand with him in the coming fight or step onto the scaffolding Eve has offered. The wind feels colder. Lina's beanie bobs as she walks away, the megaphone silent but heavy in her hand.

You take a breath that tastes of salt and iron.

You look at the contract on the clipboard. The next step is unavoidable: meetings in municipal offices, late-night reviews, signatures that will turn promises into binding obligations.

You let the sound of the construction — distant, relentless — fill the space between each heartbeat.
