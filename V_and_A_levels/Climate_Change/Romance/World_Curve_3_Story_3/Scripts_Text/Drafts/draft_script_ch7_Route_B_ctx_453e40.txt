### DRAFT
# Route: Route B (Ending)


[Scene: Tideward Promenade | Late Afternoon → Dawn]

[Visual: Wide shot of the Promenade; tarpaulins billow, platforms float on gentle swells]
[Sound: Hammers rhythmically punctuate the air; ropes creak; distant gull calls]
[Music: Propulsive, quiet percussion—heartbeat tempo]

You chose the makers’ route. The choice leaves a geography: a line of people working with their hands instead of waiting for a council room to decide for them. The promenade answers you in noise and motion—hands knotting rope, a chorus of instruction rising and falling like the tide.

You step between platforms, the boards underfoot slick with sea-spray and sawdust. Salt tangs the air; the smell of wet canvas and hot solder mixes with coffee from a makeshift thermos. Your jacket rubs against the brass pendant at your throat; its tide line seems to nod. You feel both steadiness and a tightening—an energy like a wound pulled taut, ready to release.

Emil is everywhere at once: elbow-deep under a pontoon frame, then up on a ladder measuring a keel, then back to retying a tarpaulin line. His hands move like someone who’s read the water and learned how to make something hold. When he looks up, his sea-glass eyes find you. He doesn't wait for drama; he hands you a roll of laminated posters without pausing.

Emil: "We made the tide-alert stickers smaller so people can put them on their satchels. Diego hacked the API so it flashes red on low batteries."

You: "Good. Make the map simplified—neighborhood names, safe assembly points, a QR for the petition. No jargon. No charts."

Emil: (wiping his palms on his pants) "You sound like you came from the lab and then decided to be a carpenter."

You: "Someone has to translate the lab into something people can afford to follow." (You set the posters on a crate, smoothing a corner with a work-rough hand.) "If we can't make the data touch skin, it stays a lecture."

Emil: "Then let's make it touch skin." (He says it like a plan and then, softer) "And if someone trips over a rope tonight, I'm taking them home."

The banter keeps the edge from sharpening into panic. But Diego cuts across with a megaphone and a list.

Diego: "Block A will take pontoons; Block B will handle gardens; Block C does outreach. Volunteers at 18:00 for assembly. We hold the slated staging site at first light—no one moves the equipment while we're there."

You: "And the human chain?"

Diego: "Dawn. We get a line from the eastern pilings to the staging site. Social ops pushes the story tonight."

Diego’s voice is firm, the kind that organizes people into a thing that can make noise and resist being invisible. You watch him hand out gloves, label them with neighborhood names, and one of the younger volunteers stumbles, cheeks bright with exertion and a laugh. The scene is messy and honest—exactly what you wanted.

[Visual: Cut to a cluster of posters pinned to a board; your model of the living shoreline sits in a crate beside them]
[Sound: Camera shutters, the low chatter of livestream viewers]
[Music: Low synth underscoring urgency]

You set up a livestream with Emil kneeling beside you, the model balancing on a milk crate. Your voice steadies as soon as words find shape.

You (to camera): "Neighbors—this is what a living shoreline looks like in practice. These beds don't just bloom—they slow surge, they give people time. They keep stalls standing. They're made with what we can build together."

A comment scrolls by: They need this. Another: Don't let them take the staging site. The feed is small-scale democracy made visible, but it hums. In the taped corner of your tablet, a notification blinks from the Council—Noah Ortega's office—an official request to explain the occupation plan publicly tomorrow. The needle of time shifts; the bandwidth for mistakes narrows.

[Asha approaches, carrying a damp bundle of marsh reeds; her coat smells of mud and rain]
[Visual: Tight on Asha's hand touch—damp on your shoulder]
[Sound: Her shoes slap softly on wet wood]
[Music: A single bowed note, grounding]

Asha: "You look wind-worn." (She presses a damp hand to your shoulder.) "Don't carry every risk alone."

You: "I know. I'm trying to turn risk into plans."

Asha: (half-smile) "Plans are good. Telling your people why they matter is better. Make them feel the tether—not just the threat."

You: "Emil is talking in knots; Diego is talking in routes; I have the maps. I need them to feel the same urgency without hearing panic."

Asha: "Then tell them how to be useful. People can handle urgency if it's handed as a tool."

Emil, overhearing, glances over and says, "Or hand them a hammer." He grins, but his eyes are sharper when he says, "We can do the staging site tonight. The pontoons are almost ready. If we can keep the Consortium machines off the barge line, we keep the site visible."

Your chest tightens at "machines." The word conjures cranes, contracts signed behind polished doors, and crews who will clear a line in the night if no one watches.

You: "Noah will call us idealists."

Diego: "Let him. Labels are cheap. People are not."

That line draws a laugh—easy, defiant. The laughter is a pressure valve you all need. But then your tablet chimes again. A short video: Mayor Lila Chen walking the waterfront with a consultant, her expression folded into concern. Someone sends it into your group chat with the caption: The Mayor worries about timelines.

You watch the clip three times. Lila's mouth is steady. Her voice in the background—muted on the video—says something about "coordination" and "avoiding chaos." You imagine the Council chamber, fluorescent and hungry for order. Your mind clicks through likely scenarios: the Consortium accelerates permit filings; security is contracted; a press release frames you as obstructionists.

Your internal calculus sharpens into tasks. Translate the lab data into stories. Keep the staging site occupied. Build a narrative people care to share.

The choices you offer are small, flavor-tinted—what kind of momentum you want to build in the next sixty minutes. The action doesn't change the arc, but it shapes your breath.

Time compresses. Evening becomes a damp, luminous blur. You pass out wet gloves, pin laminated maps to posts, and a volunteer hands you a steaming paper cup that tastes of bitter coffee and diesel. The posters read simply: Communities Build. Hands Protect. Names of neighborhoods ring the margins.

At dusk a drone hovers low—Noah's office sends an emissary instead of a man. A projection appears against a tarp, the face of an advisor advising you to "consider the big picture." The voice is clipped, familiar but distant.

Advisor (projected): "You risk the timeline for a patchwork of localized installations. The Consortium can speed protections; we need uniform coherence."

You: (to the projection) "Uniform protection that ignores people is a map without a heart."

Advisor: "We will not tolerate illegal occupation."

The projection cuts off. A rustle runs through the crowd—some fierce, some worried. The human body is a lighter, more chaotic political instrument than a Board line-item. Diego's jaw tightens.

Diego: "They can call it illegal. We call it life."

You feel the arousal rise—no longer a low steady pressure but a sharpened, electric urgency. The Promenade hums like a collective engine, all pistons firing. You are no longer prepping a demonstration; you are equipping a living resistance that will have to hold space against bureaucratic timetables and machine calendars.

You choose (or don't choose—this is a moment for feeling rather than plotting). Either way, the night is broken into watches. People trade shifts, trade stories about flooded childhoods and stubborn vendors. Emil ties his bandana around a volunteer's wrist, his fingers sure and warm against your palm when you hand him a knot to check.

Emil: "Promise me something."

You: "What?"

Emil: (his thumb brushes yours in the dark) "If it goes sideways, come find me. Not the other way around."

You feel that little—anchor-to-anchor—pressure in your chest. It is not a vow; it's a coordinate.

The night wears thin. You map routes by headlamp, mark tide-lines with chalk, rehearse holding a human chain against a soft, insistent swell. Every crackle on the radio snaps you to attention; every shout tightens the knot in your neck. The arousal builds toward a point: the dawn human chain, the occupation at first light, the inevitable meeting of Mayor's staff and Council timetables.

A final briefing in the gray before sun: Diego stands on a crate, voice cracked but steady.

Diego: "We form at 05:30. Line one holds the eastern pilings. Line two holds the staging area. Media meets at the boardwalk. If security shows up, we don't push back with force—our people are tethered and visible. We anchor the story: neighbors first."

You scan faces—smudged with grease, salt, mascara-combed tears, steady eyes. The communal rhythm is carved into the small gestures: shared cigarettes, a sandwich split three ways, a child instructed which rope to hold. The city is an organism of small habits assembled into a defense.

Asha sidles closer. Her voice is low, not a reprimand but a steadying.

Asha: "You translated the data well. Now translate the risk—not as a horror, but as a thing people can help fix."

You: "That's the plan. The models are good. We've got demonstrations. We have neighbors."

Asha: (looking out toward the dark water) "Then trust them. And trust yourself enough to step back when you need to."

You want to tell her how the guilt sits in your ribs—the memory of streets emptied after a storm, the vendor who left a stall and never came back. But words would make the night heavier, and the night already hangs with enough weight. So you nod instead.

Emil: (quiet, almost to himself) "If they criminalize us, they'll criminalize the idea that people can keep their own neighborhoods. That's bigger than this equipment."

You: "Then we'll make sure everyone sees what we're protecting."

He takes your hand—brief, practical. The touch is not a flourish but a hinge.

[Visual: Pale strip of pre-dawn on the horizon; the city is a silhouette]
[Sound: The tide murmurs; radios hiss; someone hums off-key]
[Music: Tension builds—pulsing strings and a steady snare roll]

You stand at the edge of the Promenade and feel the sea breathing against the pilings. This is the moment the action coalesces into consequence. The plan is set. You have translated models into posters, data into narrative, and a cityside resistance into an organized occupation. The machines that could erase the site are a day away from deployment if the Council buys expedience.

Your arousal has been rising—now it tightens into a single wire of readiness. You can taste salt on your teeth and metal in your mouth; you can hear every small sound as if the world tuned to your frequency. Dawn will be the test: the human chain, the occupation, the tweets and livestreams that will make the narrative legible to those not standing in the damp.

Page-turn thought: The city will decide if hands or machines shape the shoreline. You and Emil have chosen hands. The tide will either lend you time or carve out a lesson.

[Visual: Slow pull-back from your joined hands to the long line of lanterns on the promenade]
[Music: Tension holds; a single, unresolved chord]
