You fold the tablet again because your hands need the motion. The device feels heavier than when you closed it in the council chamber; the weight is not silicon and glass but the shape of decisions that haven't been made yet. On the screen a leaked PDF scrolls in your memory: a consortium amendment, language sharpened like a blade—accelerated timelines, “asset reset” phrasing folded into legalese, clauses that could justify relocating entire low-lying blocks under the guise of efficiency.

Your brass tide-line pendant presses warm against your sternum as if trying to point you away from the shore. It doesn't. It spins, not with the ease of a compass but with a frantic insistence that makes your chest tighten.

You read the sentences again in the dark. Each line feels like a measurement you already failed to take: "expedited procurement," "foreseeable asset consolidation," "contingent eminent domain." The words are bureaucratic varnish over a blunt mechanism: speed at human cost.

Memory comes like cold spray. You are back on a gravel street with sandbags in your arms, the night a sheaf of rain, the generator's breath rasping in the alley. Neighbors—names you have not said aloud in months—calling for help you couldn't give fast enough. A child's small hand under a soaked blanket. You had mapped the storm then and failed to turn the curve. The guilt is a tide you wear.

Emil Kwon is there before you fully cross the main boardwalk. He stands with his hands thrust into the pockets of his patched canvas, shoulders squared as if bracing for a wind that isn't only meteorological. Sea-glass eyes find you and hold—wet, rimmed in the same gray as the sky, but steady.

Emil Kwon: "You saw it."
He doesn't wait for a reply; his voice is a low current.

You nod. You don't have to say the words; the amendment lives under the skin of the morning.

Maya Reyes: "The clauses are worse than the rumor. They don't just speed things up—they re-title assets under contingency. It's a legal shutter."

Emil Kwon's jaw tightens. He pulls a flyer from his satchel and hands it to a woman nailing a patch on a tarpaulin: bold letters—"NO DISPLACEMENT." His face is an open map of contained anger.

Emil Kwon: "They'll call it triage. They'll call it efficient. They'll call the people 'collateral.' I won't let them call our neighbors that."
He exhales, a small, fierce sound.

Diego Ramos materializes from beneath a canopy, armfuls of flyers, rain beading on his shoulders. He breathes in and out like a man used to shouting over crowds.

Diego Ramos: "We got the leak out on the boards an hour ago. People are pissed. People are scared. If the vote goes like that, my cousin's block—gone. Told them to meet at the community hall tonight."
He spreads a map with trembling fingers.

You study the map. Asteria's coastal lab, the Promenade, Old Beacon: pins like bruises on a city you love.

Maya Reyes: "Mayor Chen called an emergency session. Noah Ortega's face is already in the headlines with that pin."
You make a small gesture, half laugh, half curse. "He's framing it as necessary protection."

Emil Kwon: "Of course he is. It's what he does—sells the idea of safety while clearing the decks."
He looks down at his hands, then up at you. "Maya, what do you want to do?"

Your throat tightens. The options collect like stormclouds you can map but can't disperse: negotiate amendments at the council table, stand visibly with Emil Kwon and the makers in street resistance, or dig for the consortium's strings and expose them. Each is a different kind of exposure—public bargaining, public defiance, or surgical public unmasking.

Diego Ramos leans in, voice low and urgent.

Diego Ramos: "We can stand at the Hall tonight. Make it loud. Make them see the faces. If people show up—if we break their veneer—maybe they stall."
He slams a damp palm on the map. "We have to make it political, not just technical."

Your mind runs through protocol, calibrations, projections. Negotiations can be precise; they can also make compromises that look like protections and feel like betrayals. Resistance can hold people together, but can it stop bulldozers and bonds? Investigation can root out rot, but it takes time—time the council says they don't have.

A nagging, corroding thought blooms: he can deny motives, but the presence of his network in the press images, the speed with which bond proposals circulated—someone moved a plan through channels designed to avoid the slow bleed of community consultation. The leak wasn't a mistake; it was a pressure applied.

Your internal monologue is a tight wire: you feel the old failure—sandbagging night—reconstituting into a present threat. You can hear the voices of people you failed, feel the small, terrible obligation to not let it happen again. The VeryNegative weight presses, not into panic but into a cold resolve.

Asha Karim is there, coat pulled tight, spectacles catching the last of the light. She waits at the top step as if she'd been standing for hours with the weight of another era behind her.

Asha Karim: "Good. You're not alone."
No preamble—only a steadying fact.

Maya Reyes: "The leak's everywhere. Noah Ortega's denying it. Mayor's called an emergency session. People are scared."

Asha Karim nods, then lets out a long breath that sounds like a concession to reality.

Asha Karim: "I read the amendment this morning. There are contingency phrases that could be weaponized. This isn't only an engineering problem; it's a legal and social one. If the council votes this through raw, neighborhoods will be reclassified and sold. This is displacement dressed as protection."

You hear each word like a plank settling into place. The very-negativity of the situation—displacement, the weight of past failures—thickens the air. Your chest tightens in a way that's almost physical pain. Asha watches you and speaks with the firmness of someone who has long tended both ecosystems and people.

Asha Karim: "You can try to bargain for a hybrid—get amendments, protections, binding community oversight. That would make you a negotiator in the room. You can organize resistance with the makers—public, visible. Or you can dig. Find the paper trail tying the Consortium to certain voting blocs, expose conflicts of interest. Each choice brings risk."

Maya Reyes: "Negotiating buys time and might save neighborhoods if I'm convincing enough. Resistance could force the Mayor to pause or at least stall a vote with optics. Investigation could peel back the forces pushing this, but it may not stop the immediate vote."

Asha Karim leans forward; the lighthouse throws concentric pools of light against the fog. Her voice becomes quieter, rougher.

Asha Karim: "And Maya—no matter what you choose, you will feel like you left someone behind. That's the cost of this work. You know your history. You know the way guilt will eat at you if you step away. But you also know that paralysis is a luxury people can't afford."
