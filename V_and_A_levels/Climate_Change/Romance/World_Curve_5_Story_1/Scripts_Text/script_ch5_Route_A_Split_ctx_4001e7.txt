[Scene: Marisol Bay Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — Storm Approaching]
[Music: Urgent strings with clipped electronic pulses]
[Visual: Wide shot — a crowd on the salt-darkened boardwalk; protest banners flap; Voss posters bleached by rain; a drone hovers above like an insect]
[Sound: Persistent drone hum; gulls call; distant thunder; rain beginning to spit]

You are already wet where the jacket isn't waterproof. Salt and rain braid together on your scarf; each droplet is a small, bright accusation. The pilings at the water's edge bite into the low tide like broken teeth. You can hear the bay sucking at the shore—the tide answering the day's decisions in slow, bone-deep rhythm.

Hands are moving around you: tying chains, chalking a thin white line across a Voss poster that reads as both map and manifesto. The chalk smells of dust and wet lime; mud comes up over boots. The boardwalk is crowded and precise—people shoulder-to-shoulder, each with a role. You memorize faces the way you learn tides: where to watch, who will keel if the current finds them.

Luka Maren is beside you, sleeves pushed up to his elbows, the controller a cold block in his palm. His knuckles whiten, then loosen. He breathes like someone holding a refrain—measured so as not to break the rhythm of what's happening. You can see the amber of his eyes when he looks up at you, then back to the drone, and in that tiny half-second you file a dozen small judgments about what to trust and what to shield.

[Visual: Cut to drone feed POV — Corinne Voss stands on the raised quay in a pristine trench coat; her image on the drone's monitor is immaculate against the rain-smudged sky]
[Sound: Faint, tinny amplified voice coming from the quay speakers — filtered, corporate calm]

Corinne Voss (over PA): "This demonstration will not change the facts. Disruption endangers your livelihoods. Step aside and we can negotiate in good faith."

Her voice is glass and architecture; it does not seek to comfort. The drone tilts, framing her like a photograph: immaculate hair, wet suit clinging in impossible neatness, the Voss crest glinting. Her camera is wide. Her eyes, as seen in that square, are successively cool and assessing.

Someone near the front laughs—a nervous, sharp sound that the rain swallows. A security van idles at the quay; a line of municipal officers steps off with wet coats folding around them. They do not shout; they take positions. Voss's security moves with precise mechanical care, cataloguing attendees with small cameras, faces dipping into their memory banks.

[Sound: The distinct click of multiple shutters; the hum of two dozen phones going live]
[Music: Percussion spikes — rapid snare, heartbeat mimic]

You feel the crowd's energy bundle into harder angles: anxious, defiant, taut. Mateo's voice threads through the chatter—low, practical.

Mateo: "Amaya—if they ticket us for obstruction, we lose momentum. The council will clamp down fast."

You look at him. His hands, cut from rope and nets, don't tremble but they carry a weight that presses at the base of your neck—this isn't theoretical for him; it's his nets, his seasons. You want to answer with a plan you can carry for both of you.

Amaya Reyes: "We knew the risks. We planned this to be visible, not violent. Keep it tight—no sudden moves. Luka, keep the feed steady but muted if it goes hot."

Luka Maren: "Muted?" (He glances at the feed; his finger hovers.) "If it's muted, they call it censorship. If it's live, they twist a shove."

You say it quickly, practical-to-practical, more instruction than comfort. You sense Luka's hesitation—he is an engineer who measures outcomes, and outcomes are suddenly slippery.

A Voss security guard steps forward—not aggressive, but precise. He photographs faces like inventory. His camera's light is a small, predatory eye. A volunteer presses a thin chain loop through a piling and staples a laminated leaflet: "HOMES NOT WALLS." You clip yourself in. The metal is cold, as if the ocean itself had been forged into it and left its temper.

[Sound: A shout—sharp. A body collides with another. A scuffle uncoils behind you]
[Music: Violins screech; a single low brass thunders]

Someone from the other side of the line—one of the hired demonstrators, tall, scored with corporate plastic—tries to pull a poster free. Fingers twist. A fist meets a forearm. The video cameras pan. You do what you've always done: move toward harm. The body in front of you stumbles into a volunteer; a hand reaches for balance. In the same breath you push—not hard, just an instinctive shove to steady someone falling toward the rails.

Time fractures into frames. The drone's camera hovers; a phone catches the shove. Corinne's feed snaps tighter; a lawyer's face appears at the edge of the quay like a legal shark smelling blood.

[Visual: Slow-motion style close-up of your hand on another's shoulder, then a sudden burst as the crowd's tempo changes]
[Sound: A collective intake of breath, the boardwalk's wooden planks creak]

The shove looks worse in pixels than it did in the moment. On one screen the shove is defensive; on another, edited, it becomes violent. Comments explode. The hashtag mutates. People who meant to protest calmly now speak of assault. Voss attorneys begin to mobilize like clockwork servants—text messages sent, calls placed. Within minutes the legal language arrives: "Assault," "trespass," "injunction pending."

Tamsin moves up beside you, tablet clutched as if it were a talisman. Her expression is careful and unreadable—borderline of ally and official. There is something in her shoulders that tells you she has been weighing probabilities all afternoon, and the balance just tipped away from you.

Tamsin: "Amaya—this is—" (Her voice is cut by the rain, then steadier.) "We can mediate, but the council's hearing this as liability now. They want assurances—money, evacuation plans, legal cover. They are not seeing nuance."

You look at her. You want to parse her face for betrayal or fear. The Schrödinger between ally and gatekeeper is thick; you call it "complex" in your head and keep your mouth to sharper purposes.

Amaya Reyes: "They've been asking for assurances since their flyers arrived. 'Liability' is a word for what they think they can buy. We know what we're protecting."

Tamsin: "If we don't de-escalate, they'll fine organizers, issue injunctions, and the donor meeting tonight will collapse into risk mitigation. Voss will say it's too volatile to partner."

Abuela Rosa's hand finds the inside of your wrist. Her fingers are small, knotted, older than the town but still warm. She leans close so that only you can hear.

Abuela Rosa [soft, crystalline]: "You can make storms, niña. You must also know when to steer."

Her voice is not a rebuke; it is a guidepost. You feel steady in the sudden off-balance. She is both anchor and season—she remembers other displacements, other bargains.

Mateo: "If they push permits and we get fined, we lose seed money for the marsh beds. If they get an injunction, we can't plant without risking arrest."

His statement is arithmetic. Every choice is an accounting of loss and leverage.

[Visual: Cut to a small cluster where a live stream amps up — a commentator with a megaphone misreads the shove as aggression]
[Sound: The megaphone amplifies misinterpretation; cameras zoom; the hum becomes a howl]

The scuffle unspools into legal theater. Voss sends a representative down with a clipboard and a smile that is all edges. The representative's phone is already sending footage to a corporate PR inbox. You watch him watch your face as if cataloguing a specimen.

Luka Maren's shoulders tighten. He steps forward, then catches himself.

Luka Maren: "Amaya—we can use our recordings. If we timestamp everything, show the sequence—" (He speaks fast, words tripping into each other.) "We can do a clean release. Show intent, show reactions. Use the metadata. Make the narrative match the moment."

Amaya Reyes: "You think the legal system moves fast enough? You think the council will wait for metadata while Voss fills the air with 'danger'?"

Luka Maren: "No. But we can slow them one step. We can force review. We can make their pictures look like what they are—selective angles."

Your anger/hope axis clinks—neither you nor Luka linger there; you are working in practical terms. The boardwalk is a machine now, every gear grinding louder: PR, law, security, social media, town politics. Your stubbornness that used to be something to rally behind now risks becoming a fulcrum you can't control.

Abuela Rosa squeezes your wrist once, hard.

Abuela Rosa: "Remember the people, not the pictures."

You hold the image of the town like a ledger: names, houses, livelihoods. You also hold the knowledge that the next move will be assessed on screens, papers, and polished shoes in rooms that smell of engineered citrus.

A lawyer from Voss appears at the edge of the crowd, mobile hotspot glowing in his palm. He palms a folded document—an injunction drafted in anticipating hands. He speaks into his earpiece, calm and administrative.

Voss Lawyer [offhand]: "We'll file within the hour. Injunction for public nuisance and unlawful obstruction. We'll involve municipal authorities if needed."

The words are small arrows. The donor meeting scheduled for tonight on the quay has shifted like tectonic plates; what was a pitch for partnership is now a list of risks. The council, sensitive to that ledger, will not be moved by speeches soaked in mud.

Tamsin's hesitation closes like a door. She looks at you with something that asks, without asking, whether you've considered the cost. Her tablet's glow maps fines and timelines. The room where the council weighs in is not made of boards and ropes; it is made of bylaws and quorum counts.

Tamsin: "Amaya, you started this to protect homes. I won't lie—the law could protect you if we show coercion. But proving it takes discipline. Or you can force the publicity route—go big, make Voss's reputation costly. Both paths cost lives in different currencies."

She doesn't frame it as betrayal; she frames it as arithmetic. That in itself is neutral, clinical, devastating.

You close your eyes for half a breath. The rain finds the seam of your face and sweeps away salt. You feel the town lean on you like an old hull on the tide, and every plank is giving you a place to stand—and a place that can be pried loose.

Mateo's jaw sets. Abuela Rosa's mouth is thin and patient. Luka Maren's hands go cold again on the controller. Corinne Voss's drone watches like an indifferent eye.

Everything accelerates. Headlines will be written, laws will be drafted, and donors will recalculate. The momentum of the crowd is a wave that can lift the town or push it under. You can taste metal—anticipation, fear, the salt of possibility.

This is the furious, clean center: a choice that will direct resources and shape people's roofs and lives. You have seconds, maybe minutes, to decide the mode of resistance—whether to amplify the confrontation, pivot toward legal documentation, or step back into negotiation.
