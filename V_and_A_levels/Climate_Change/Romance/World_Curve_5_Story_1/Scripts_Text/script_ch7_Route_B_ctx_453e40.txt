[Visual: The glass pavilion reflects a slate sky and the churning bay; inside, a single glass-walled conference room overlooks the quay. A long table, clinical, too bright. [Music: Tense BGM — fast, insistent strings under a steady electronic pulse]

You already know the shape of this room before you step in: too clean, every surface calibrated to reflect control. It makes your boots feel heavier on the polished floor. You smudge the salt on your jacket cuff without thinking, an instinctive mark of territory — a human stain on their stainless shine.

Amaya Reyes: "Thank you for hosting us. We appreciate the time."

Corinne Voss [smiling, precise]: "We appreciate the willingness to talk, Ms. Reyes. Voss only moves when it meets measurable protection and regional benefit. Today's proposal is a pilot model—raised quay segments, modular aquaculture units, and co-funded marsh restoration led by TideLab."

The rendering blooms in the air: a model coastline with a polished quay, neat blocks of aquaculture pens, then — unexpectedly — a ribbon of green kicking against the edge: marshland stitched into corrals of engineered oyster beds. For a heartbeat something like hope flares.

You feel Luka's hand brush yours under the table. His fingers are warm; he doesn't pull away. The contact steadies you, and also sharpens everything else — the stakes, the temptation.

Tamsin [low, balancing]: "We set constraints. Municipally, we need assurances on displacement, on environmental review, and a clear timeframe for the pilot's reassessment. Voss has agreed, in principle, to co-fund TideLab sensors—your small-tech network, Mr. Maren."

Luka Maren [quick, almost a laugh]: "In principle is a start. Those sensors would give us real-time data across the marsh edge. We could prove efficacy."

Corinne Voss [tilting her head, the AR hovering like a halo]: "We want to scale what works. We don't build forever; pilots inform broader deployment. The sooner we protect vital infrastructure—the ferry hub, the secondary road—the fewer people suffer long-term."

You watch the presentation and force your voice to stay clear, the way you learned to in public meetings: precise, not pleading.

Amaya Reyes: "We need binding oversight. A community board with veto on relocations, line-item funding for TideLab's equipment and training, and legally enforceable timelines for reassessment before any permanent construction."

Corinne Voss [smile narrows in a way that doesn't reach her eyes]: "Binding is feasible when it's proportionate. But you must understand municipal timelines and funding cycles. Indefinite community vetoes can delay protection and increase risk. We propose mediation language: dispute resolution, escalator clauses, and relocation carve-outs for 'at-risk zones.'"

The phrase "relocation carve-outs" lands like a stone. Your chest hardens.

Abuela Rosa [measured] : "I have pulled nets from water with hands that knew the bay when it was kinder. We are not numbers to be moved for the sake of a map. If the plan means neighbors become stories, then I will not bless it. But I also know when water comes and takes more than pride."

Tamsin [sharper now]: "Pilot language is the crux. If the municipal code doesn't limit the pilot's duration and scope, a private actor can argue that successful elements justify permanence. The 'pilot' could become the precedent. We need sunset clauses, adjudicated reassessment, and escrow funding for community relocation assistance — should relocation be unavoidable."

Corinne Voss [leaning forward]: "Legally, that slows the process. Escrow is an admission of displacement risk. We can structure conditional payments tied to performance thresholds that, frankly, reduce moral hazard."

The phrase sits heavy; you picture people in town.

Luka Maren [quiet, to you]: "This could let us prove the model to other towns. With Voss backing—"

Amaya Reyes: "With Voss backing comes control. 'Prove the model' has become code for 'let us do it at scale, on our terms.'"

Luka Maren [defensive]: "That's cynical, Amaya. Data powers policy. If the sensors show marsh restoration reduces storm surges, we win arguments later."

Amaya Reyes: "But winning arguments 'later' means people get moved now. We can't treat names on my ledger as variables in an experiment."

The exchange is small, but it draws lines. Luka's jaw tightens; his hands flex on the controller. His temptation to accept the scale of Corinne's resources is a raw, visible thing — hope mingled with fear of failing without it.

Corinne Voss [softly]: "No one wants relocation. That's why we invest in marsh and quay together. We can stage construction to minimize displacement. Our engineering team has phased plans."

Tamsin: "Phases don't protect neighborhoods from legal exposure. If 'minimize' isn't defined, you'll be left with subjective judgments."

A heated silence. The AR rendering spins to a close-up of a street in Marisol, and the label "Phase 2 — At-Risk Zones" pulses red. Your stomach drops.

Abuela Rosa [measured]: "I have pulled nets from water with hands that knew the bay when it was kinder. We are not numbers to be moved for the sake of a map. If the plan means neighbors become stories, then I will not bless it. But I also know when water comes and takes more than pride."

Her words are not a simple yes or no. They are a memory and a warning. You feel the gravity of living on a shoreline that has always demanded trade-offs — shelter for someone, exposure for another.

The room thrums with pressure. You can feel your pulse in your temples. Each suggested clause you make is countered, reshaped, reframed — a negotiation that eats away at certainty. High arousal reverberates through your body: quick breath, damp palms, the tightness that means making a decision that will weigh on you in the years to come.

Corinne Voss [steps back to the model and swipes a gesture]: "Here is a firm timeline we can agree on today: Phase One — sensors and marsh pilot within six months; Phase Two — quay segments within eighteen months contingent on marsh metrics. If TideLab indicators meet thresholds, we proceed. If not, we halt. All parties sign an MOU today; the detailed contract follows."

Tamsin [warning]: "An MOU isn't a binding contract. The municipal code requires a permit and a judicially enforceable agreement for any relocation. An MOU that punts core decisions to later litigation is a liability."

Corinne Voss [cool]: "Legal teams can draft enforceable terms. We are offering to underwrite TideLab equipment now. We are offering community jobs, technical training, and a cooperative wetland model in our public materials."

You hear your own name in the sentence about 'underwriting' as if TideLab were a line item instead of a living thing. Your chest tightens until it hurts. The room presses inward — glass walls and a sky that looks like a bruise.

Luka Maren: "Amaya. If we get the sensors, the data will be undeniable. We can build a case that marsh works and stop further destructive walls. This is leverage."

Amaya Reyes [breathless]: "Leverage only works if we are able to wield it. If they control the timeline and the legal language, leverage is a story we tell later — while people get moved now."

Tamsin [to Corinne]: "Can Voss agree to escrow funds managed by an independent trustee appointed by the community? Can we have third-party auditors on the metrics?"

Corinne Voss [pauses, then smiles, white and sharp]: "Escrow isn't the problem. Trustee selection and audit criteria are. We can agree to auditors with joint nomination, provided their remit is scientifically validated and not motivated by local politics."

Your internal monologue snaps like a brittle reed. "Not motivated by local politics" is a polite way of saying the elders' testimony could be discounted as parochial. You can feel the town's pulse in your mouth — angry, frightened, trusting you to keep them whole.

You swallow responsibility, the old reflex. The weight of every face in your ledger presses into your jaw. You can feel how close you are to nodding. It would be a rational, pragmatic nod: accept limited risk for massive resources. But the rationality tastes of salt and erosion.

Abuela Rosa watches you still, the shawl folded like a harbor. Her hands rest in her lap — patient, waiting. Her look is neither approval nor condemnation; it is a tide waiting to break. You must decide how to hold the town against that pressure.

[Music: Strings accelerate, electronic pulse quickens — the room seems to pulse with urgency]

Your voice tight, you try to find the right words to keep both promise and people intact.

Amaya Reyes: "We can do this. But only with clear, enforceable, independent oversight frameworks spelled out today. No ambiguous 'pilot' language that allows permanent creep. Escrow with community trustees. Sunset clauses. Binding audit protocols. And a clause that any relocation must pass a judicial standard and community consent."

Corinne Voss [studies you like a specimen presented under glass]: "Those terms are... exacting, Ms. Reyes. They will complicate deployment timelines."

Tamsin [softly]: "They also protect the town."

You feel all the air leave the room when Corinne lifts her chin and sits down at the table. Her fingers steeple in a gesture that underlines the moment: business resolve meeting community demand.

Corinne Voss [slow]: "We will take your proposed language to legal. We can begin drafting with those as a basis. Voss wishes to be a partner, not an antagonist. But understand—the longer negotiations drag, the longer high-risk infrastructure is exposed. We will not be patient indefinitely."

The phrase "not be patient indefinitely" is a deadline wrapped in threat. Your heart spikes. The pressure is a physical thing now, like tidewater pressing against a seawall. You feel the decision perched at the edge of something sharp.
