# Context: f69160

[Scene: Marisol Bay Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Wet planks gleam under overcast light; strings of solar lanterns sway. Chalk tide lines curve over damp posters bearing Voss’s glossy logo. A low quay looms; beyond it, a glass pavilion glints like a foreign moon.]
[Music: Rapid, syncopated percussion building into a driving pulse]
[Sound: Drone rotors thrumming, camera shutters, rain hitting wood in staccato bursts]

You move along the boardwalk like a current follows a channel—purposeful, forced into the narrow way other people have cleared. Your boots slap wet wood. Salt and tar and the metallic tang of rain sit in the back of your throat. The air tastes of magnets: wet iron and electricity, of something about to be drawn together.

On the pilings, volunteers take their places. Rope and reclaimed chain, a ring of bodies, a symbol in hardware: you lean forward and loop the link through the timber, fingers numb and precise. Chalk squeaks as someone draws a high-tide line across a Voss poster; the white arc looks absurd and sacred against corporate cyan.

You hear Luka before you see him—his breath caught in the cold, the soft click of the controller in his hand. He is two steps to your left, close enough that his sleeve brushes your arm. The controller is heavier than usual. His cheeks are wind-raw; his smile is a quick, brittle thing that does not reach the eyes. He looks at the drone overhead, then at you.

Luka Maren: "What if we angle the feed—give people the marsh first, not the suits? If we show the beds we saved, the fences we built—"

Amaya Reyes: "We already have marsh footage. They need to see the faces this will push out, Luka. And not just faces—stories. Tell Jules to cut to the elders next."

You keep your voice low.

Luka Maren [hesitates]: "Right. Elders. Jules is on it. But—" (his fingers tighten) "If security tags us—if they photograph everyone before we start—"

Amaya Reyes: "Then they photograph what they see. They photograph stubbornness."

You say the word flatly. It tastes like chipped glass.

A low hum grows: the drone hangs like an eye. On the quay a bright HUD overlay blooms—Voss maps, animated tide forecasts—then a thin rectangle of live video, Corinne Voss's face smudged by distance and rain, scales on the feed like a dam across the world. Her suit is immaculate even at a pixel's distance, a smear of steel against the sky. Her gaze is a line you can feel.

[Visual: Close-in on drone feed—Corinne Voss's AR projections flicker; an attendant types rapidly; security cameras swivel]
[Sound: Electronic blips, a microphone calibration noise, muted footsteps on concrete]

A security officer steps forward from the quay and raises a camera. He is methodical: framing, shutter, metadata capture. He photographs faces as if cataloguing species.

Mateo appears at your elbow, the fishermen's sweater heavy with rain. He runs a hand through hair that is close-cut; his jaw is set like an anchor. He glances between you and the crowd, then at the line of pilings you are binding.

Mateo: "Abuela says don't do anything stupid." (He tries for a joke and misses.) "She also says if you get taken away, she knows where to find me."

Abuela Rosa comes to your side in a slow, deliberate shuffle. Her shawl is bright even under the gray. Her hand—small, knotted—is warm and grips your wrist with a calm pressure that steadies like a tideblocker.

Abuela Rosa: "Listen." (She speaks low, the voice that taught you to read currents.) "Make the line you mean to keep. Not for fury—so we know why we stand."

You feel the sound of the crowd tighten like a chord and then snap: someone at the rail starts a chant, a single voice that folds into others. A live stream icon in the corner of your field of vision blinks alive: viewers climbing—hundreds, then thousands.

The police arrive in clusters, practiced and quiet. Their boots are measured. An officer reads a script into a megaphone; the words are spaced by procedure and caution.

Police Officer: "This is an unlawful assembly. You are ordered to disperse or be subject to citation."

Tamsin stands near the municipal liaison tent, tablet in hand—her face is a ledger of calculations. You see the newness of her hesitance fold into something harder: the pictured compromise between town and firm has become a ledger with red ink in both columns.

Tamsin [voice tight]: "Amaya—if this escalates, the council will move to fines. We could lose the permit hearing. The donor summit—if they pull—"

Amaya Reyes: "Or we keep the hearing honest."

You cut her off.

Amaya Reyes: "Tamsin, they hired photographers before they set plans on the table. They came ready. Which side are you on?"

Tamsin: (looks away, then back) "I'm on the side that keeps people in their homes, Amaya. But I am also on the side of not getting everyone arrested."

Your conversation with Tamsin blooms into an uneasy duet—words that circled and missed meaning.

Tamsin: "We can open mediated talks today. We can ask for a cooling-off period."

Amaya Reyes: "Cooling-off is what they call a delay when they're already drawing lines on a map."

Tamsin [a whisper]: "We have to be practical."

Abuela Rosa's fingers tighten on your wrist like an anchor line.

Abuela Rosa: "Do not trade our names for tomorrow's promises."

You can feel the temperature around you rising—not in heat but in pressure—blood humming, footsteps shifting. The drone dips, and a figure in a Voss raincoat appears on the livestream, walking along the quay, phones held out. Comments tumble across the screen: "move them" // "they're blocking progress" // "protect the city".

Jules signals you with a camera—the shot is close, shaking in their hands. Luka Maren keeps filming the drone feed and the crowd alternately; the controller is cold and slick in his palm.

Luka Maren [under his breath]: "We can mute the feed if it goes sideways." (He looks at you.) "We should probably mute the feed if it goes—"

Amaya Reyes: "Don't mute it."

You hear your own voice, too loud.

Amaya Reyes: "If they edit it, people will know. If they cut sound, their frame will speak for them. Keep it raw."

A man in a suit pushes through the milling security and reaches for a poster, fingers snapping it off the wall. Someone near him shoves back reflexively—a lockjaw move, more indignation than violence. The camera tilts; a woman loses footing on the wet planks.

Time splinters. The live feed jerks, screen going jagged as viewers flood the comments. A hand catches at a sleeve—then the push becomes a shove in the frame. In the stream, in accelerated motion and over-encoding, the shove looks worse. A body falls. The audible gasp is magnified into a howl across the network.

[Sound: A single chair scraping—then a loud thud; the drone camera whips; static edges the audio]
[Visual: Live-stream overlay—floating comments cascade. A red alert pops: "INCIDENT REPORTED."]

Luka Maren's hands go white around the controller. His jaw works. He leans forward, voice raw.

Luka Maren: "I didn't—"

He starts, but there are ten more seconds of footage. Ten seconds of interpretation.

Amaya Reyes: "Get Jules on the other angle."

You bark.

"Get Abuela up—someone get Abuela in frame."

Jules [shaky, yelling]: "I'm on it—I'm on it—"

The scuffle becomes choreography: security tightening ranks, a police officer grabbing a wrist, someone shouting legal rights, another person crying out. Voss's security begins systematic face-capturing again—photograph, catalog, withdraw. A lawyer's terse message flashes across Tamsin's tablet; she looks at it, color draining as if the screen evacuated warmth.

Tamsin [voice small]: "They're filing for an injunction on grounds of trespass and obstruction. The Voss counsel says they'll seek emergency orders—"

Mateo [barks]: "We chained ourselves to pilings, not to profit! This isn't obstruction—this is defense."

The camera on the quay pans up; the figure of Corinne Voss—immaculate, removed—tilts her head like an adjudicator. Her feed shows a neat message overlaid: "Voss Marine Solutions: Committed to community safety. We cannot have unlawful interference with critical infrastructure." The phrasing is clinical; the effect is immediate.

A courthouse clerk's ringtone blares from somewhere—an official line. You watch as papers and permits, once abstract, coldly translate into legal threats. Within an hour, a terse statement arrives: Voss has filed for an injunction; the council will convene an emergency session to discuss fines and permits. The donor meeting that had been scheduled to pep up investment is now a conference about risk assessment, and in a private message that leaks across channels, a prospective donor writes: "We can't be associated with unrest."

Your stubbornness—your insistence on being the first to lock hands and name the sea—feels keyed up into something with cost. Each decision you made to be visible ripples outward and becomes an argument in someone else's ledger. The calculus is no longer ecological alone; it is reputation and capital.

Abuela Rosa draws you closer. Her breath smells faintly of citrus and dried herbs; she leans so close you feel the patchwork of her shawl against your cheek.

Abuela Rosa: "Stand. Speak. Do not be bitter. Be clear."

You want to be clear. You want to name the threatened houses, the fishing coves, the children who learned to wade here. But clarity now is a blade that cuts both ways.

[MERGE: Either choice amplifies the incident; metrics spike and legal pressure increases.]

The livestream surges; metrics spike. Anonymous accounts launch hashtags. The town's private channels vibrate with messages—some rally, some warn. Voss issues a short, formal release via their PR account, sympathetic in syntax and firm in intent. The council's hotline rings off the hook. You watch Tamsin, and she watches the tablet, her face folding into a decision none of you want her to choose.

Then the legal machinery plants its flag: an emailed PDF appears on Tamsin's screen, stamped and urgent—an emergency injunction demanding dispersal and threatening arrest for noncompliance. The council's emergency line texts code: "CONSIDER FINES / PERMIT SUSPENSION." A private donor thread scrolls with polite withdrawals.

For a moment you stand in a strange, compressed stillness—drowned in noise but distant in the part of you that counts consequences. The storm's first real gust throws spray across the boardwalk; someone curses, someone laughs nervously, someone weeps quietly. Your chest drums the rhythm of pressure.

Mateo finds your other hand and squeezes. His grip says what words won't: homes, nets, wharves, the slow geometry of people's days.

Mateo: "What do you want us to do, Amaya?"

You taste the salt of rain and choice and weigh the ledger that sits inside you—community, data, strategy, pride. The range of possible moves expands and narrows all at once: each is a path forward and a cutting back. Each has different lengths of time and types of pain.

Your mind runs through the three frames you've held since TideLab began—grassroots refusal; the slow, precise pull of paperwork; the compromise table with its cold coffee and warm promises. Each feels necessary. Each feels like a door whose hinges you cannot yet lubricate.

The boardwalk hums around you. The drone's red lights flash like a heartbeat. The feed is still live. The injunction notification pings again. Tamsin's shoulders slump and then harden, a new calculation manifesting across her face.

Abuela Rosa [firm]: "Decide."

You inhale. The decision must be made now, with wet hands and raw voices and the knowledge that whatever you choose radiates outward into lives that will change in consequence.
