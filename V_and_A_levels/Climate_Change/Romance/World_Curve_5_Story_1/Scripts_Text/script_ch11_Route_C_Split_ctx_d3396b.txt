[Scene: TideLab | Late Morning — Rain Still Falling]
[Visual: Inside the repurposed fish-shed. Amber shop lights hum. Tide maps pinned in brittle curls. A bank of mismatched solar batteries blinks a nervous green.]
[Sound: Rain on corrugated metal, a distant gull calling once, like a punctuation.]
[Music: Tense, driving percussion under a steady synthetic swell]

You taste metal on your tongue again — the residue of too many meetings, too many deadlines. The ledger you signed is folded under your elbow; the words you wrote are still warm with ink: "Concurrent strategy — legal route continues. Community education & measured public actions to be scheduled. No unilateral protests. Secure evidence."

The room reacts to that sentence the way tidewater reacts to a storm: small, insistent movements building into an inevitable swell. Jules is sorting footage into labeled folders, fingers moving faster than her speech. Mateo is on the phone behind you, voice hushed but taut; you can feel the vibration of his knuckles against the counter when he hangs up. Luka Maren stands by the bench with his drone controller clasped like a compass — eyes flicking between the court filing printout and the live stream of a Corinne Voss PR drop on his tablet.

Jules: "We got raw footage from the west pier—storm surge models, and… footage of their excavation prep. Good angles. If we cut it with the tide-maps, it's visceral."

You look at the footage. The screen shows a crane silhouette against a bruised sky, bulldozers chewing at a shoreline. The image is curiously clinical; Corinne Voss's PR caption is the varnish.

Amaya Reyes: "We need context, not outrage. The filing needs evidence chain, timestamps, metadata. Jules, seal those originals and hand me a copy."

Jules: (nods, sharp) "On it. I can scrub the watermarks if we need it for broadcast—"

Luka Maren: (interrupting) "No. Don't scrub. The chain of custody matters more than cinematic shots. If the video looks edited, Marina's affidavit will be shredded."

A small silence forms. It's not empty; it's loaded.

Amaya Reyes: "We are threading a needle. Visibility helps — regulators notice loud things — but visibility also gives Corinne Voss an opening to discredit us."

Luka Maren: (quiet, measured) "And there's another opening. Corinne Voss has been floating an invite to a shared research network. If I join, we could access their environmental monitoring feeds. Influence from the inside."

The air tightens. You can smell the wet fabric of Luka Maren's smock, salt and solder; a human counterpoint to the technical proposal.

Amaya Reyes: "Influence is not neutral. It's optics. If your presence gets spun as 'insider support,' our credibility in court and with the town is compromised."

Luka Maren: "I know. I also don't like feeling locked out of data that matters for the injunction. I could act as a data conduit—no public-facing deals, just access, and strict logs."

Mateo, who has been leaning against the sample shelf, finally folds his arms and steps forward.

Mateo: "And what about the small stuff? Lines of credit, Amaya. We can't feed the boats if everything freezes. A moratorium that drags could sink families."

Abuela Rosa, steady as tide-rock, shifts her shawl and speaks in a voice that carries more history than any filing.

Abuela Rosa: "Courage must be measured. The law is a net; it catches those with the right weight. But nets tear. We need to know where to mend."

You feel the conversation pull you in all directions at once. There are legal levers, PR levers, human levers. Each one snaps against the others like rigging.

Jules moves to hand you a sealed folder. The fresh paper smells like hope and printer toner. Your thumb finds the edge where Marina's notarization will be stamped later today.

Amaya Reyes: "We have three practical tracks. One, go loud — coordinated public campaign aligned with filings. Two, keep it quiet and exacting — pride of precedent. Three, negotiate a conditional moratorium with Tamsin, using legal pressure as leverage."

The words hit the room like a bell. The rain on the roof grows louder, as if the sky itself is listening.

Luka Maren: (leans in, voice low) "If we go loud, we force regulators' hands faster. But Corinne Voss has the PR machine. They soak up heat and redirect it. They'll claim we're obstructionists."

Amaya Reyes: "If we go quiet, we risk approvals cutting across our filings. The legal system is slow. Approval could be granted while our motion ticks through."

Mateo: "And if you sit in the middle and negotiate, the moratorium can give people time to breathe. But 'moratorium' can mean any number of things. If it's loose, it's a paper shield."

Abuela Rosa: (soft, decisive) "Paper shields do not keep children dry. But steady hands—steady pressure—can move a tide."

The swell of sound in the lab builds. Your heart is a snare drum—faster, until it's a roll. Your palms are damp, the cuticle of your thumb catching on the ledger's leather.

Amaya Reyes: "We can't just trade moral clarity for breathing space. We also can't let families run out of credit because ideology is perfect."

Jules: "We have one more tool: viral narrative. Not showy clips, but a structured campaign timed with the filing. We stitch Tara's affidavit to cold metadata, release it to local networks, and push for regulator attention."

Luka Maren: "If you do that, they weaponize 'anarchist smear' lines, and we spend the next month defending ourselves in press cycles."

Amaya Reyes: "And if we don't, regulators might ignore passive filings. Precedent is persuasive, but momentum is persuasive too."

The lab buzzes. Batteries tick, the rain bangs its own impatient rhythm.

You run your thumb along a faded tide map — the lines are crowded in the bays that have always been vulnerable. Each inked contour is a person, a mortgage, a memory. The choice is technical but it is human; the legal instrument bends toward lives.

[Scene: Council Chambers | Early Afternoon — The room smells of varnish and damp coats]
[Visual: Long oak table, a projector humming. Tamsin waits near the municipal display, tablet in hand; she looks practiced but tired. A municipal clerk adjusts the microphone.]
[Sound: Murmured greetings. The rain's cadence follows you in, like a metronome.]

Tamsin meets your eyes first. Her planner's face is composed, professional; an undercurrent of fatigue softens it.

Tamsin: (careful) "Amaya. Luka. I appreciate you coming on such short notice. The council's schedule is tight today. Corinne Voss's permit is on the consent docket."

Amaya Reyes: "We'd like to talk before that happens. Conditionally, if possible. We have a filing that we are preparing to seal this afternoon. It will be formal, not performative."

Tamsin: (tilts her head) "Formal motions often lose urgency in courts. If you want a moratorium, we can draft terms, but I'll need a defensible legal posture to bring to the council and to Voss."

Luka Maren: "Defensible means specificity. Define scope, duration, enforcement. We propose an immediate six-week moratorium or—"

Mateo cuts in, voice tight: "—or we start reef restoration now with municipal funding pledges. People need to eat, Tamsin."

Tamsin: "Funding is controlled by budget cycles. Emergency allocations are possible with council support, but council needs a political out—proof of imminent harm."

You feel the thread of it: the how of getting council to care. You also feel the clock in the margin of Tamsin's tablet — a permit deadline creeping toward a vote.

Abuela Rosa steps forward, voice steady in the chamber's hush.

Abuela Rosa: "You speak of proof. We have lives as proof. We have the tide lines etched into our bones. Give us time to plant roots that hold."

Tamsin pauses, her expression flickering with the weight of the job: liaison, gatekeeper, the person who must translate competing moral claims into procedural language.

Tamsin: (quiet) "I can take a conditional moratorium to the council as a referral if you can guarantee legal teeth. Marina's motion could be that tooth — if it's grounded in admissible evidence and timelines."

Amaya Reyes: "We can guarantee an emergency motion. Marina will file within the hour. But we need the breathing room for tags and to activate community work crews."

Luka Maren: "And if you publicly tie us to Corinne Voss data, even as an advisory exchange, you create a narrative of co-option. That could undercut legal standing."

Tamsin: (looks at you) "Your motion's optics matter. I can lobby for a moratorium if it is narrowly defined: geographic boundaries, a clear start and end date, and enforcement clauses. But council will demand a path to compliance, or Corinne Voss will sue."

You feel your jaw lock. The pragmatic part of you is already drafting the moratorium's clauses mentally: start date, scope, penalties, who enforces. The activist part measures the human cost if those clauses are watered down.

Amaya Reyes: "We need to know what 'narrow' looks like. We need to know stoppage enforcement. And we need guarantees for immediate relief — food security, small-business credit extensions. Not promises. Legal guarantees."

Tamsin: "I can draft it, but I need your legal team's input. Marina will have to bless the language."

Luka Maren: (suddenly) "And there's another risk: a moratorium can be framed as negotiation if it's too generous. Corinne Voss could accept it publicly while using the time to entrench equipment on adjacent parcels."

Mateo: "Then make the moratorium strict and enforceable. Make the cost of violation too high."

Abuela Rosa: "Make it such that they would shiver to break it."

The council clerk clears his throat and announces that Council will convene in twenty minutes. The temporal pressure is no longer theoretical.

You find yourself at the center of a tightening coil. The lab's possible campaigns, the court's timelines, the council's mechanisms — all of them fold into one axis of decision.

You imagine the faces you would be making this choice for: Mateo standing by a dock in a dawn storm, Jules with a camera strap biting into her shoulder, Abuela Rosa at her kitchen table stitching another list of names into a ledger. The image hardens into resolve and something more complicated beneath it: a private ache that tightens at the ribs when Luka Maren looks at you as if trying to measure how much of him you're willing to put in the breach.

Luka Maren: (voice softer, almost offhand) "Whatever you choose, I will support it. But I will also resist anything that sells the town out."

Amaya Reyes: (searching) "Support isn't the same as surrender. If I ask you to do something that risks your career, I need you to tell me before I make the move."

Luka Maren: (holds your gaze, unreadable for a beat—complex) "Then tell me what you plan to ask. I won't make decisions based on what might be romanticized later. This isn't about us. It's about Marisol."

There's tenderness in his words, but it's edged with a professional fuse. The friction is not absence of feeling; it's the pressure of two people balancing affection and duty under very hot conditions.
