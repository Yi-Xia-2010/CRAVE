### DRAFT
# Route: Route A / Option C


[Scene: Polished Quay | Midday]
[Visual: Voss Marine pavilion catching sunlight; the quay is polished concrete and clinical glass, but the ocean behind it is a bruise of storm-slick gray.]
[Music: Warm, steady strings; medium tempo]
[Sound: Low murmur of gathered townspeople, distant gull calls, the soft clack of reporters’ gear]

You stand with the salt still in the seams of your hair, the scarf tight at your throat. The boardwalk’s rain from the other day is a memory that sits in the ribcage—raw but steady now. Around you, microphones align like a row of curious gulls. Cameras tilt. Tamsin’s tablet glows in the coolness of her hands; Corinne’s reflection is a clean edge in the pavilion glass.

Tamsin: "Thank you for coming, Amaya. We wanted this public—transparency matters."

You feel the crowd as a single organism: ruffled planning documents, folded arms, a child with a damp shoelace. Your voice comes out controlled, practiced.

You: "We asked for a voice at the table. Today, we get the details."

Corinne Voss steps forward—platinum hair, precise smile. There’s no triumph in it, only the kind of calm that makes people accept numbers as fate.

Corinne Voss: "Marisol Bay is a priority for Voss Marine. We’re scaling back the full port plan, funding immediate marsh restoration, and committing to a ten-year community oversight board funded by our foundation."

You nod even as your eyes find the clause Tamsin hesitates over—language tucked into legalese: an operational reservation that cedes certain low-lying blocks for port expansion. The words feel like a tide line drawn across a neighborhood.

You: "The restoration funds—will they include long-term relocation assistance for households in the ceded zones? Not after-the-fact, but proactive transition planning."

Tamsin: (quick, precise) "Yes. There are allotments for housing, job transition programs, and a community-managed trust. The oversight board will have veto power over operational expansions for five years."

Corinne: (calm) "We will protect main infrastructure—school, clinic, the quay’s access routes. We will also invest in modular wetland barriers designed with TideLab’s input and fund retraining programs for fishery workers."

Your stomach twists—practical protections closing against the real cost. This is the bargain: structure for safety, acreage for commerce. You hear Mateo’s breath behind you, a low steady note.

Mateo: "And the houses? My friends—"

Corinne: "We have a relocation fund and purchase offers. No one will be left without options."

The crowd exhales as if a storm has moved on. Relief rolls like a warm current. Still, the ceded blocks are on a map now, labeled in small, clinical font.

Luka’s presence is a steadying weight beside you—his lab smock folded beneath a clean jacket, fingers stained with solder and salt. He looks at the map, then at you. There is a question on his face he is not speaking out loud.

Luka: (soft) "Amaya—there’s another part. They want me to consult on the modular systems. I can be inside the project, push for community design."

You: (a beat) "Inside? For them?"

Luka: "If I refuse, they’ll hire someone else. If I accept, I can keep TideLab's voice at the drafting table. I can make sure the wetlands tech is distributed, open-source specs, training programs—things we’ve always wanted."

The wind takes the corner of a printable plan and sends it toward the harbor. Corinne watches, expression unreadable.

Corinne Voss: "Luka is an asset. We want to bridge corporate scale with community resilience. His role will be part-time, advisory. We’ll fund TideLab’s pilot and give priority contracting to local teams."

You breathe. The offer is tidy and pragmatic; it smells faintly of engineered citrus and polished promises. Relief hums under your ribs—roofs fortified, clinic supplied, the school safe in a worst tide. The immediate pressure that had been tightening for weeks loosens now, enough that you can hear the gulls again.

You: "So you scale back the disruptive parts. You fund restoration and oversight. You guarantee resources for people forced to move."

Corinne: "Exactly. It’s compromise with real protections."

Mateo folds his hands into his sweater, hard and quick with anger that tears itself into a pragmatic, worn care.

Mateo: "We keep what matters. We don’t throw people away."

Abuela Rosa: (quiet, vivid) "You save the hull, niña. But some planks will be taken. We mend what we can. We plant new roots."

Her voice is soft and stubborn and older than municipal maps.

Corinne extends a hand—an offer that has been ironed flat into policy. Cameras surge forward, flashes like sun on chop. Around you, people murmur; at the edges you hear grief, but louder is the sense that severe harm was stopped before it struck.

Corinne Voss: (firm) "We can start community projects next month. TideLab will receive a grant for wetland sensors and community training. Luka’s involvement secures design integrity."

You look at Luka—at the way he tries to make the calculus of compromise sound like strategy rather than surrender.

You: "If you do this—if you take that position—you don’t disappear into their cycles. Promise me you’ll keep us first."

Luka: (after a breath) "I promise. It’s not perfect, but it keeps a lever. It keeps us in the room."

There’s a beat. The crowd seems to inhale, waiting for the seal—the handshake, the signatures. It’s the climax you’ve been building toward: not a dramatic arrest or a shouted eulogy, but the steady, legalized clasp of two parties making a pact that will change lives.

You sign because the signatures will mean immediate shelters rebuilt, marsh saplings planted where they can help, and funding for TideLab that buys time for your experiments. You also sign because you cannot let the town be hollowed out in the name of efficiency. The signature burns your thumb with ink that smells faintly metallic.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The boardwalk’s planks are warm with the lowering sun; the crowd disperses in small clusters, animated and exhausted.]
[Music: Acoustic guitar with a hopeful minor turn]
[Sound: Children playing in the distance, occasional clack of cups from a nearby café]

Back on the boardwalk, people approach you with questionnaires folded into pocketed hands—requests for help, offers of labor, cooking pots for volunteers. Relief threads through the conversations, but grief weaves through it too: families comparing paperwork, elders naming plots that will be claimed.

Mateo finds you and takes your hand with a fisherman’s practicality—no sermon, only plans.

Mateo: "We’ll organize the moving crews. We’ll do the shoreline plantings. Don’t let them sell us charity—make it work for us."

You: "We will. TideLab will run the restoration pilots. We’ll train crews and sign community contracts. I’ll design the monitoring plan so we can prove what’s working."

Mateo: (a small, fierce smile) "You always loved maps more than speeches."

Abuela Rosa ambles near, her shawl a bright island. She looks at you as if weighing weather and harvest in the same breath.

Abuela Rosa: "You kept a larger number safe, niña. That is not nothing. Plant seeds where you can. Teach them to stay."

You: (soft, steady) "I will. We’ll make these funds mean more than the port’s margins."

Jules Park appears with a camera and an exhausted grin.

Jules: "The crowd’s split, but a lot of people said they’d work the plantings. We can get the first marsh line started in two weeks with Luka’s specs and your maps."

You inhale salt and diesel and possibility. The choice you made has unpacked into a thousand tiny tasks: tenancy arrangements, training rosters, sapling orders. The arousal is mid—an engine of busy, purposeful movement rather than panic. The day is busy, human, moving forward the work that matters.

[Scene: TideLab (Community Research Hub) | Dusk]
[Visual: Amber worklights hum over cluttered benches; jars of brine sit in a row; a whiteboard lists tasks in looping handwriting.]
[Music: Low piano, warm and reflective]
[Sound: The distant hush of the quay, the close scrape of a chair being pulled]

Night folds in like a blanket and the lab smells of solder and wet soil. TideLab’s solar array hums in a slow afterglow. You set the field notebook on the bench—maps and marginal notes, a smudge of watercolor tide-lines—and feel the weight of the day settle where you usually keep it: on the left side of your chest.

Luka comes in with a small package of tools and a thermos. He moves like someone who’s learned to carry two lives at once. He sets the thermos down, takes off his jacket, and for a moment the lab is just the two of you and the quiet machinery.

Luka: "They want me in a formal position. Part-time consulting. I told them I’d only accept if TideLab’s practices are open-source and if local contractors are prioritized."

You: "And you’ll be able to refuse technical specs that hurt people?"

Luka: "Yes. I’ve pushed for veto-overrides in project design—community vetoes. I’ll have a seat."

You: (quietly) "That seat will be lonely sometimes."

Luka: (a small, rueful laugh) "Maybe. But maybe having one of us there keeps the rest of you from being shouted out."

The two of you speak in the slow, layered return of people who have learned the contours of each other’s reasoning. Your questions aren't accusations; they are careful, because you want him inside the plan and you also want to protect the core of what TideLab is.

You: "Promise me you’ll still—call me when it’s small. When a sensor fails. When someone asks a question you can’t answer."

Luka: "I promise. And you—promise me you’ll let people help. You don’t have to carry it alone."

You: (a long breath) "I know. I’ll try."

There’s a coolness in the space between you now—not sharp, just realigned priorities, a new rhythm that will take time to sync. It hurts like a bruise that will fade into familiar muscle. Still, Luka’s decision means something crucial: you have leverage where there could have been only concession. That feels like hope.

You turn back to the bench, to the maps, to the list of plants to order. There is work to do—measurements, community meetings, training schematics. Each task is a stitch.

Abuela Rosa: (from the doorway, a smile like a lighthouse) "You will mend things, niña. You always do."

You: "We’ll mend, Abuela. We’ll teach them to mend."

You pick up the braided bracelet of wildflower seeds from the hook on your bag. Your fingers rub the tiny beads—reminders that restoration is not only policy but practice. You take a seed, hold it between thumb and forefinger, and then plant it in a salvaged cup with damp soil on the windowsill. The gesture is small, an insistence that life continues in the exacting work of hands.

[Visual: Close-up on the tiny seed sinking into dark soil; outside the window, the last strip of sun slides into the water.]
[Music: The piano resolves into a warm major chord]
[Sound: Soft wind against corrugated metal, the lab’s hum]

You feel the fatigue in your shoulders, but under it is a steady thread of something very close to hope. The town kept its heart—if not every plank—and you have resources now to make restoration more than an idea. Luka will be in rooms he once feared; Corinne will be a presence on plans you can now influence. The moral cost sits with you like a bruise and a lesson. It will not go away. But the immediate danger has receded, and that, for now, means people have roofs, children have schools, and hands have work.

You close the notebook and write one line where you usually list tomorrow’s tide times: Keep people in the center of every plan.

[Visual: Fade to a simple shot of the window—salt-slick glass, a small potted sprout in the windowsill—then to black]
[Music: Strings fade to a single sustained note]

You have accepted a compromise that costs some things to save others. It feels heavy and hopeful all at once. You will carry the weight of that choice like a tool—used, scarred, necessary.
