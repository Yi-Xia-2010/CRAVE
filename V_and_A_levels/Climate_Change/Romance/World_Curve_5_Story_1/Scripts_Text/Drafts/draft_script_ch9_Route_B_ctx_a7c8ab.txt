### DRAFT
# Route: Route B


[Scene: Marisol Bay Boardwalk | Late Morning]

[Visual: A tight shot of the wet boardwalk planks, salt crystals catching light. Camera rigs and press tents line the curve; microphones like black flowers reach toward the water.]
[Sound: Distant murmurs of reporters, the steady clam of a generator, gulls calling over the hubbub]
[Music: Warm, steady strings—hopeful, not triumphant]

The leak you pushed has become a weather system of its own. You stand at the edge of the crowd and feel it in the small, practical muscles of your face: not surprise so much as recognition. You expected noise. You did not expect how many voices would answer when the town's story left your mouth and entered the country.

Your notebook is in your jacket, pages stiff with tide maps and margin notes. You thumb it without looking—because the ritual steadies you more than the contents ever do. Around you, Marisol wakes to the fact of being watched. A drone hums overhead like an insect you can't swat.

Jules squeezes your shoulder, eyes bright and a little wild. "They want statements. Channel twelve wants an on-camera." He hands you a paper cup that smells faintly of lemon and burnt sugar—too much coffee by the dozen. "Mateo's on the quay. Abuela's holding court by the entrance. Luka's… weaving through cables like a nervous crab."

You inhale. The air tastes of salt, wet wood, and a trace of something metallic—camera rigs, hot lights. You are not surprised that the reporters came; you are surprised at how quickly they felt righteous. The leak did what you hoped and feared it might: it made the invisible rules visible.

[Visual: Wide shot — reporters clustered, people from the town standing in small, taut groups; in the middle, a raised platform with a makeshift podium and a Voss banner folded back like a reluctant flag]
[Sound: Microphone feedback, a chorus of small, anxious questions]

Reporter: "Amaya Reyes! Can you comment on the documents that show relocation maps were prioritized in Voss's internal plan?"

You step forward because that is what you are: someone who answers when the question meets the people who will be affected. Your voice is measured, the kind of clarity built from long nights of translating data into lives.

You: "Those documents show the risks we all live with—and also show how decisions get made without our input. We asked for partnership. We are asking for representation, not permission."

Reporter: "Voss says they already offered a pilot with community oversight. Why go public now?"

You: "Because oversight on paper is not the same as enforceable, legally binding community control. We pushed because promises drift with budgets and board members. Legal teeth matter."

A camera tilts closer, hungry. The question thins into itself and you hear other voices—the chorus of people who will have to carry these changes. You fold your statement back into your chest and let your eyes find Luka across the crowd.

[Visual: Medium shot of Luka by a tangle of cables, hands stained with salt and grease; he looks up and catches your gaze]
[Sound: The soft mechanical chirp of his controller]

Luka steps away from a microphone stand and crosses toward you, his stride a quiet apology. He smells of solder and the ocean, tethered to both. When he reaches you, he doesn't put on the engineer's shield; he looks knotted, honest.

Luka: "I—" (he searches for the right word) "—I didn't think the board would scramble like this. When you leaked the memos, the story landed harder than I expected."

You: "You told me once that data that sits in drawers is useless. I believed you."

Luka: "I still believe that. But… the headlines changed everything. Corinne's been on three networks already. Her spin team is good. They almost got away with framing this as 'necessary relocation for safety' until nonprofits and local papers dug the rest up."

You: "We wanted oversight, not spin."

Luka: "The board called an emergency session. Voss is fragile right now—the public blowback, investor nerves. Corinne—" (he hesitates, then keeps going) "—Corinne called me yesterday. And they offered a position for me to coordinate community sensors—under Voss's oversight. She made it sound like a bridge."

You feel your shoulders tighten. Bridges are built by both sides, or they are excuses to keep traffic flowing in one direction.

You: "Did you—what did you tell her?"

Luka: (a small, private laugh) "Honestly? I told her I needed to think about how to keep the tech from being co-opted. I told her I wanted guarantee language—community vetoes on data use, public repositories, a clause for open-source protocols."

You: "Hard guarantees. Enforceable."

Luka: "Yes. And—" (he looks at you in a way that makes the noise around you shrink) "—and I told her I won't take anything that pulls me out of Marisol. If I go, it's to keep this here."

You: The relief is a cool, complicated thing. It settles in your chest like saltwater—cleansing and sharp. "Promise me you'll read every clause."

Luka: "I will. You know me. I read the fine print like a love letter."

He smiles, a small, private thing that feels like a joint investment in a fragile future. It softens the edges of the panic that had been sharpened by the morning.

[Scene: Boardwalk — Near the Quay | Early Afternoon]

[Visual: Corinne Voss standing beneath a low overcast, her platinum bob immaculate despite the weather; a bank of cameras faces her like a metallic tide]
[Sound: A murmured, escalating crowd; the click of a live feed switching networks]
[Music: Low, steady cello—pressure building but controlled]

Corinne takes the podium with the composure of someone who has rehearsed this moment in a dozen variations. She does not look like a villain to you—she looks like someone who has practiced benevolence until it is indistinguishable from certainty.

Corinne Voss: (into the microphone; measured) "Voss Marine believes in protecting coastal communities. We will not let Marisol Bay fall into chaos. We proposed a pilot to safeguard infrastructure and livelihoods."

Reporter: "Internal memos show you prioritized port development and moved relocation planning to a 'low-visibility' channel. How do you justify that?"

Corinne: (a thin smile) "The memos in question were early-stage logistics. They reflect internal risk assessments—not final policy. But the board hears your concerns. We have convened emergency oversight to revisit those allocations."

You watch her because she is the pivot. For a long moment, the boardroom she inhabits and the dock where you stand seem to breathe the same air. Her composure cracks—not in malice, but in something colder and more human: calculation giving way to pressure.

Tamsin steps forward beside her, tablet in hand. Her expression is tight; you can see the strain of being the town's intermediary in her shoulders.

Tamsin: "We have negotiated a framework that includes legally binding oversight. A community board will have a veto power on relocation decisions and on how data is used. The pilot will include independent audits and a sunset clause we can revisit with joint stakeholders."

You: The phrase "legally binding" lands like a small bell. It is what you asked for in the dark of the lab, in the margins of reports, in the tired hours of advocacy. Still—there is a trade-off in Tamsin's voice that you cannot ignore.

You: "Who defines the 'marginal blocks' listed for relocation? And what protections are in place for families who will be moved?"

Corinne: (a softer tone than the cameras caught before) "We agree to fund relocation assistance plans—housing, compensation, and prioritized rebuilding elsewhere in town. We'll also commit to expand marsh restoration funds and to phase port development around an agreed cooperative plan."

There it is: a compromise. A ladder built on some people's roofs and the cleared lots of others. It is not perfect. It is not everything you imagined in your stubborn dreams. But in the face of national attention and legal scrutiny, the firm has stepped back from a few worst-case edges.

A reporter nearby shoves a microphone toward you. "Is this enough?"

You: You think of Mateo's hands mending nets, Abuela Rosa's stories of the old shoreline, the children who chase the tide pools like a fragile religion. You think of the families in the low blocks—your mental map aches. "It's a start," you say. "It's legally enforceable. We will hold them to it."

Corinne watches you with a gaze that is difficult to parse. There is defeat in it, but there is also something like respect—brief and brittle, then gone.

Corinne Voss: (quiet, almost private) "We will put community representatives on the oversight board. We will accept independent audits. The pilot will be legally enforceable."

You: You nod because the words are what you demanded and because there is work ahead that will scrape the grain of people into a new pattern. Your chest lifts with the ease of a small victory, and you feel the country in the crowd take a breath you didn't know it had been holding.

[Scene: Boardwalk — Late Afternoon]

[Visual: The sun shifts low; puddles hold the sky like glass. Volunteers move through neighborhoods, passing out leaflets and schedules for relocation assistance. A few moving trucks are parked near the marginal blocks, families loading with quiet, practical movements.]
[Sound: Soft, steady footsteps, distant conversation, the muffled hum of television vans]
[Music: Piano with a rising, hopeful motif tempered by minor chords]

You walk the line between triumph and loss. The enforceable pilot exists because you insisted; because the leak made the public ledger visible; because the nation watched and weighed in. But the cost is written in the small gestures around you—boxes taped, a grandmother's shawl folded carefully against the cold.

Mateo finds you near a cluster of trucks, hands greasy from repairs, a strain at the corners of his mouth that looks like both pride and grief.

Mateo: "You did it, sis. You made them listen."

You: "We made them listen. But listen is different from undoing what was planned. Some people are leaving—temporarily, they say. Some will stay. We saved most of the town."

Mateo: (he picks up a small carved fish that someone is carrying out) "Most is better than none. I—" (he swallows) "—I can live with that. We'll make the nets better."

You: The curve of your mouth is wet with something. It isn't only triumph. It is the complicated, very human tug of having won something that still leaves scars.

Luka approaches, hands empty now, his coat smeared with salt. He moves like a man whose work will no longer be purely his—because coordinating with Voss puts him inside an institution that once planned against the town. His face holds a kind of determined softness.

Luka: "They offered me the coordinator role in writing. Community sensors, public data repositories, regular audits. They agreed to the clauses about open-source protocols."

You: "You accepted?"

Luka: (a small, careful nod) "I did. I told them I'd only take it if the community board could appoint half the members, if data stayed public, and if I had a direct line to the independent auditors. They wrote that in."

You: Multi-turn exchange continues.

You: "Does it feel like a compromise?"

Luka: (close now, voice low) "It feels like trying to keep the flame where it is needed. The board might try to steer things—spin them—but being inside gives me leverage. I can't promise I won't be tempted by bigger offers, but I can promise I won't leave Marisol in the lurch."

You: "Leverage and vows. Those are small things to carry."

Luka: "They're what we have. And we can build on them."

You: You find your fingers brushing his—habit, solace, a mutual pact. The touch is brief and steady, a small enough anchor in everything else that is shifting.

Abuela Rosa approaches, shawl wrapped tight. Her eyes catch yours like a lighthouse beam.

Abuela Rosa: (soft, certain) "You did more than make a noise. You made a law. That is work. The families who go will carry roots into other places; those who remain will tend the marshes you saved. The sea takes and gives. But you—" (she looks at Luka, then at you) "—you keep giving it reason to take less."

You: Her blessing tastes of rosemary and rain. You lean into it because you have done the work and because you know the debt you carry to the people who did not ask to be experiments in policy.

[Visual: Montage — volunteers planting marsh grass with new funding, community meetings signing oversight charters, a small group of families saluting as they are assisted onto buses. Luka shows a child how a sensor works. Corinne steps into a closed room with board members, documents spread; the Voss crest looks smaller from the dock.]
[Sound: The soft scrape of shovels; distant laughter; an indistinct round of applause from a small crowd]
[Music: Strings and piano in a gentle, resolving progression—warmth suffused with a grain of sorrow]

You stand at the edge of the boardwalk as dusk leans in, looking over a town that will be different tomorrow than it was yesterday because people chose to make it so. The pilot is enforceable. There is funding for marsh restoration. Port development will proceed on a slower, negotiated track—but proceed it will in prioritized zones. Some families leave; others stay. The town, in aggregate, survives—changed, bandaged, breathing.

Luka: (quiet) "Are you angry with me?"

You: There are many questions you might ask—about compromises, about whether pragmatism will wear down ideals—but the question you hear is smaller and truer: whether what you share can survive the new arrangements.

You: "No." (your voice is careful) "Not angry. Cautious. Proud. Maybe all the adjectives that mean 'we'll have to work for it.'"

Luka: He lets out a breath that is almost a laugh. "That's the most Amaya answer ever."

You: You both watch a group of kids running along the boardwalk, chasing a tide pool's reflection like a bright, impossible coin. The evening wind tugs loose a thread in your scarf. You tuck it in.

You think of TideLab—its rickety bench, its jars and seed packets, its stubborn lights. You think of the work to come: constant, mundane, necessary. You feel relief—clean, quiet—and alongside it, a steady grief for the neighborhoods that will leave, for the things that cannot be put back the way they were. The grief does not erase the victory. It seasons it.

You: "We'll stay," you say, finally. "I'll stay. You'll stay. We'll make the oversight work."

Luka: "We will. We'll write the sensors' code open-source. We'll make sure every displaced family gets priority in rebuilding plans. We'll show them what community-run tech can do."

You imagine that future—slow, stubborn, full of meetings and muddy boots and late nights with bread and maps. It will not be easy. It will be honest.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands—one holding Luka's, one on your battered notebook. The tide rolls in, obedient and endless. Lights along the boardwalk blink on, small, deliberate suns.]
[Music: The strings resolve into a warm cadence; a single piano note lingers]

You let the tide tell you what endings look like here: not a final vanish but a rearrangement. You breathe in the salt air and feel the town's pulse sync with your own. Relief and grief move together like two tides meeting—a complicated, inexorable rhythm.

Abuela Rosa: (softly) "Tomorrow we plant. Tonight we remember who we are."

You nod. You do not speak the convenient slogans. You speak in plans and in acts: meetings scheduled, clauses read, a network of sensors waiting to be unrolled by hands you know. You have won something important. You have lost something too. You stand with the people who will do the making and the mending.

[Visual: Wide shot of Marisol Bay from the boardwalk—lanterns and working lights woven into the shoreline; the Voss pavilion is a distant, cooler glow; the community's light is warmer, patchwork and alive]
[Sound: Waves, closer now; the evening chorus of the town settling]
[Music: Quiet, resolved piano and strings]

You hold Luka's hand as the last reporters pack their cameras. The relationship between you has a new shape: steadier in some ways, hollowed and honest in others. It will take effort to rebuild trust where institutions touch it. But it will also be the scaffold upon which TideLab and the community build their shared future.

You look out at the bay and let the sensation of being both survivor and steward settle in your bones. The victory tastes like salt and new wood. It tastes like the promise of work and the ache for the cost.

[Music: Gentle, final chord]
