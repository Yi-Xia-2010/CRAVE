### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C
# Context: c4ca42

[Scene: Community Hall | Late afternoon — storm approaching]

[Visual: Cold yellow light from a lone projector washes the wooden walls; hand-painted maps pinned to the grain show faded neighborhood names. The room smells of wet wool and old varnish. A thin ring of damp fog clings to the doorframe.]
[Music: Low, sustained cello — restrained, minor key]
[Sound: Rain pattering on the tin roof; occasional murmur of voices]

You step into the hall with your notebook under your arm and the salt smell stuck to your collar. The projector throws a rectangle of pale warmth onto a sheet; TideLab’s tide maps bloom there in watercolor strokes — the same pages you pressed your thumbs against in the shed an hour ago. Your hands are steady, but your chest holds that familiar weight: responsibility folded into a kind of tired armor.

The room is fuller than you expected. Mateo stands by the back bench, shoulders broad beneath his fisherman sweater, jaw working behind a face that never wears more than what the day carved into it. Abuela Rosa sits close to the front, her shawl bright against the plain wood, tapping the knit rhythm that keeps her listening. Tamsin is near the door, tablet clutched like a lifeline — neutral, attentive, worry threaded into the set of her mouth. Luka leans against the sideboard, fingers trailing a controller’s edge; he watches the projection but his expression reads small, unreadable shifts.

You breathe in: damp wool, coffee from a thermos someone left near the projector, the metallic, clinical clean of Corinne Voss’s arrival before you even see her — a scent almost manufactured, like citrus-laced antiseptic. The room tilts. Conversation thins to a hush.

[Visual: Corinne Voss enters; her trenchcoat immaculate, holo-projection unfurling at her sleeve in clinical cyan]
[Sound: Quiet authoritative footsteps as she crosses the floor]

You start. You cannot afford to stumble through this: every line on your maps is an argument, every jar of sediment a witness. You step forward and place the leather notebook on the table, the spine whispering against the wood.

Amaya Reyes: "Thank you for coming. I know this weather scares some folks into silence, but the maps we’ve been tracking are not opinions — they’re patterns. The salt wedge is pushing farther into the estuary each season. Our oyster beds are showing increased mortality in the zones mapped here, and drifting debris is changing where young marsh shoots can settle."

You let the projection linger on the smear of indigo that marks the salt wedge. Fingers in the room — Tamsin’s tapping, Mateo’s clenched jaw, Abuela’s slow, almost imperceptible nod — answer back.

Tamsin: "Amaya, this is thorough. (She flicks the tablet, bringing up a municipal overlay.) If what TideLab is showing is correct, it has budgetary and zoning implications. There are vectors here that would require permits to rework tidal channels..."

Amaya Reyes: "Permits are the paperwork of consequences. We can’t wait for permits while the salt chokes our nurseries. Low-tech solutions — marsh planting, oyster socks, distributed berms — are within reach if we mobilize now. They keep people here. They keep livelihoods."

Tamsin’s eyes flick to the doorway where Corinne Voss stands, then back to you. She speaks in that careful planner’s voice, the one that weighs language as if it were a set of keys.

Tamsin: "I can push for quicker temporary permits, but I need political cover. The council will ask for guarantees."

Before you can answer, Corinne Voss steps forward, the holo around her casting an even, cold light. Her voice is clear, practiced, a blade honed on spreadsheets.

Corinne Voss: "Guarantees come from scale. Voss Marine is proposing a phased protection system that will secure key infrastructure and the port. Consolidation is an unfortunate reality, but it's necessary to protect regional economic function and prevent greater loss downstream. Our model minimizes long-term costs; short-term relocation of vulnerable blocks reduces systemic risk."

Her words fall into the warm projector light and slice it. The room cools as murmurs rise.

Amaya Reyes: "Consolidation — displacing families so a port stays profitable — that is not protection. It’s a market decision. You can’t pretend displacement is the only humane option."

Corinne Voss (tilting her head, composed): "I don’t pretend anything. I present outcomes. The models optimize for lives saved over decades, for ROI that will fund maintenance and future protections across the coast. Community sentiment is important — but sentimentalism cannot undercut viability at scale."

Mateo’s hand curls into a fist on the bench beside him. He stands as if pushed by something in his chest.

Mateo: "So whose lives count less? My dock? My nets? You’ll move my family inland and call it efficient."

Corinne Voss (brief, dismissive): "We aren’t deciding which two houses are valuable. We’re choosing the configuration that keeps most people secure."

Abuela Rosa’s shawl makes a soft sound as she stands, her voice rough with years and salt.

Abuela Rosa: "We have kept this shoreline longer than your models knew how to count years, señorita. The sea remembers different things than your calculations. Do you intend to listen?"

Corinne Voss: "I listen to data and to risk."

Abuela Rosa: "We are data."

A pause. You feel the room tilt between two orders of proof: the maps you brought and the glossy, vaulted plans Corinne Voss lays across the air.

Amaya Reyes: "You can talk about risks and ROI, but you haven’t walked the marshes at dawn when the air tastes of kelp and salt and the little things — the seedlings, the tubes of forming oyster — matter. These are living defenses."

Corinne Voss (interlocking her fingers, unruffled): "Living defenses have timelines. We are accountable to immediate threats and to investors who fund maintenance. If the bay’s infrastructure fails, the cost is national. We propose a managed consolidation paired with engineered restoration where feasible."

Tamsin: "What does 'managed' mean in practice? Who decides which neighborhoods qualify for protection and which are moved?"

Corinne Voss (a small, precise smile): "We model for maximal regional resilience. That’s what the data demands."

You can feel the collective exhale of the hall — a thin, anxious fog. People shift in their seats. The projector hums like a second heartbeat.

Luka Maren steps forward then, voice low, measured, an attempt at bridge-building.

Luka Maren: "Amaya’s maps show weak points that are fixable now. Small, distributed tech can monitor salinity, seed oysters, seed marsh. We don’t have to choose between nature and infrastructure if we scale local solutions smartly."

Corinne Voss (with a quick, appraising glance at Luka Maren): "Local solutions are valuable as supplements, but they cannot substitute for systems-level protection. Local devices have maintenance footprints and can be brittle under sustained stress."

Luka Maren (defensive): "They’re resilient if we design them to be repairable by the community. They’re affordable and keep knowledge local. People can run them, not just corporations."

Corinne Voss (cool): "That’s an optimistic narrative. The reality is complexity and funding. You have talent — useful talent — but ambition on its own is not policy."

You watch Luka Maren’s fingers close around his multi-tool. There’s a small tremor in his jaw. His expression is complex—part concern, part resolve, part a worry that he’s being measured for usefulness and traded. You don’t know whether he sees Corinne Voss’s offer as a dangerous compromise or an uneasy opportunity. The uncertainty stings.

Tamsin exhales and taps her tablet again, the light reflecting off the screen like a second set of eyes.

Tamsin: "There are three routes here that I can present to the council with community backing. One is a public mobilization opposing Voss’s plan in full — grassroots marsh restoration and civil resistance. Second is mediated talks — negotiation that folds community needs into a hybrid development. Third is a prolonged legal and data-driven campaign aimed at injunctions and regulatory leverage."

You feel the words settle like tidewater. Each option is a lever. Each lever moves people.

Amaya Reyes: "And if we choose the resistance route, we may face corporate security, political pushback, and immediate hardship in the short term. If we choose negotiation, we risk ceding land and control. If we choose legal routes, we expose municipal weaknesses and the town’s private data, and it could take too long."

Corinne Voss (matter-of-fact): "Time and resources are finite. Pick a path and we will respond accordingly."

Mateo: "Pick the one that keeps my nets and my house, not the one that reads best on a ledger."

Abuela Rosa: "Pick the one that keeps our stories where the water can hear them."

Luka Maren’s hand finds the notebook you left on the table earlier and opens it, fingers tracing tide-mapped margins as if reading the same prayer you wrote there. He looks up.

Luka Maren: "Whatever we pick matters for the tech I can build. If we choose to confront, I can keep things decentralized and community-owned. If we talk, I might be pulled into partnerships that compromise that. If we litigate, we’ll need ironclad chain-of-custody on our data. I don’t want to lose what makes the work humane."

Amaya Reyes: "I don’t either. And I don’t want to lose you."

His look is a quiet thing — complicated and private. You avoid casting it in certainty. You cannot know the shape of his future choices; you can only count the weight of this moment.

[MERGE: The room slows. Rain gathers on the windows, making the world outside feel like a soft, distant thing. Inside, every sentence feels like a small decision made on the edge of something larger.]

You gather your notes. The projector ticks. Tamsin’s tablet pings up comparative timelines. Corinne Voss folds her hands with the calm of someone used to being decisive. Mateo’s jaw is set. Abuela Rosa’s fingers stroke her shawl.

Your voice is quieter than you expected when you say it.

Amaya Reyes: "We have three choices. Each risks part of the town, part of ourselves. I can call the collective to action and organize public opposition — marsh restoration and civil resistance. I can open mediated talks with Tamsin and Voss to negotiate a hybrid plan. Or we can build a data-driven legal strategy: share TideLab findings, seek injunctions and regulatory leverage."

The words hang. The room tastes of rain and iron. Your heart, steady until now, nudges the edge of something tighter. This is where the tide turns: not in a storm, but in the choosing.

[Sound: A single long note from the cello, held and unresolved]

You wait for the hall to answer, but the decision—your decision—must be deliberate. It will shape who stands beside you and what Luka Maren’s work will become.
