You left the Community Hall with the taste of iron still on your tongue—what you said replaying in the hollows of your skull. From Chapter 3, Option A. Organize. Resist. Plant.

Now the words are work. TideLab is all elbows and elbow grease: volunteers in borrowed boots wrestle with bundles of mangrove seedlings, clipboards bloom with plotted coordinates, and the battery bank in the corner hums like a small, patient animal. The shed smells of wet soil, seaweed, and the faint, sweet chemical tang of salvaged fertilizer. Your field notebook is open on the workbench, its tide maps annotated in your cramped handwriting; you set your palm flat on a margin and feel the paper's salt-stiff edge under your skin.

You give orders you did not know you could give. Your voice is shorter, more precise than it was in the meeting—less rhetorical, more tactical.

Amaya Reyes: "Stagger the sponge bundles along the low flats, not in a straight line—curves trap sediment better. Marta, you and Jules take reef placement two; stagger the oysters in a checker pattern to encourage juvenile habitat. Mateo, you and I will mark the channels; Luka will fly a grid run and check for subsurface flow before anyone digs."

Jules Park: "Got it. Checker pattern, reef two. If the tide's high at noon, should we push the grid earlier?"

Amaya Reyes: "Earlier. We don't risk washing the seed bundles. Time the digging to the falling tide—remember what Abuela Rosa taught us about the moonlines."

[Visual: Luka stands at the doorway with his controller; salt-bleached strands of hair cling to his temple; drone lifts]
Luka Maren [softly]: "I'll map the subsurface channels twice. If there's any unexpected culvert or scar, you'll see it before hands go into the mud."

Amaya Reyes: "Good. Call it out. If the data and the tide disagree, trust the water."

His laugh is quiet, almost a groan. He knows you—knows how you make absolutes of cautions—and he knows when to push you to let others act.

Mateo approaches, salt and sun baked into the lines at the corners of his mouth. He wipes his hands on his sweater and studies the seedlings like a captain poring over a chart.

Mateo: "Never thought I'd be planting instead of pulling nets. Feels…right in a way, yeah?"

Amaya Reyes [trying for lightness]: "It keeps the bay fed and your nets from emptier days."

Mateo [half-smile]: "And keeps you from pacing the entire coastline at night."

Amaya Reyes [deflecting]: "I've got a list. Keep your boots on and your hands busy."

The flurry of preparation leaves no space for long sentiment. Volunteers tape grafts on sun-faded scarves, tie labels, pass buckets that smell of peat and brine. The mud is cold and stubborn between fingers; every handful yields a small, apologetic squelch as roots settle.

[Scene: Marsh access path outside TideLab | Midday]
[Visual: A ribbon of compressed mud tracked by boot prints; clouds have moved lower]
[Sound: Drone overhead; the scrape of boots; a child’s distant laughter turned to a cry and then quiet]
[Music: Percussive pulses, heartbeat-like]

The drone feed on Luka’s tablet paints the water in bands—pale for sandbars, darker where currents run. He calls coordinates:

Luka Maren: "Two meters north of stake C, there's a seam—looks like an old culvert. Adjust reef placement by three meters."

You mark it. The collective moves around your directions like a school of birds shifting at your cue. Muscle memory becomes choreography: dig, press the bundle into the mud, tamp, make a shallow dike with spare mesh. Every action seems urgent, as if the tide itself is hurried because it knows the town is fighting for time.

Suddenly, a small voice cuts through the rhythm. A volunteer—Marisol, who ran the bakery before the storms—hurries up the path with her daughter on her hip. The girl's knee is scraped to rawness, slurry of mud around the wound. Marisol's face is pinched with apology and worry.

Marisol: "I—I'm so sorry. She slipped when we were hauling the mesh. I didn't see—"

You move faster than you think. Your hands are background tools: a quick clean with salty water, a strip of gauze from the first-aid kit, a hand on the girl's shoulder so she knows it's steady. The wound is small but jagged and the sight of it pulls a hot thread of guilt tight in you—old, familiar: you always imagine the worst when small harm touches the people you protect.

Mateo [softly]: "You okay?"

Amaya Reyes [forcing calm]: "I'll check the kid's bandage twice today. Make sure there's no infection. Marisol, sit—take five. I'll take over reef placement for you."

Marisol: "I should've been more careful. I'm sorry, Amaya."

You want to tell her there's nothing to apologize for, that the work itself makes people messy and brave. Instead you say what you can act on: protocols, bandage checks, reassignment schedules. The practical response is your refuge.

[Visual: The boardwalk is a line of patched storefronts and hand-painted signs; a Voss banner snaps in a brewing wind]

By noon a small delegation from Voss appears on the boardwalk. They do not march—everything about them is surgical. A woman in a neutral blazer—steely, efficient—steps forward with a binder of glossy, folded renders and a polished smile that does not reach her eyes. In her wake, a Voss representative with a tablet scans faces and measures posture like a surveyor. Corinne Voss's brochures make promises in emulsified cyan: jobs, infrastructure, a grander plan to 'secure' the bay. The language is clinical benevolence.

Voss Rep [smooth]: "We appreciate the community's efforts. Restoration is important. Voss can accelerate protections with a comprehensive shoreline program—jobs, coastal defense, and a reinstated harbor economy. There will be community consultations."

Tamsin stands slightly back, tablet glowing. Her expression is unreadable—neutral, but the small tightening at her jaw says she's already balancing conflicting pressures.

Amaya Reyes [voice low]: "Consultations don't make up for bulldozers. Jobs tied to a single corporate plan leave families vulnerable if it fails."

Voss Rep [tilting her head]: "We're offering guarantees. Relocation is never our first choice. We want to collaborate. Scaling up is the only way to protect the broader coast."

You can hear the gallop in your chest—adrenaline sharpening anger into urgency. Corinne's glossy offer is a threat disguised as a solution. The permit—someone's tablet coughs a notification; your pulse drags it into focus: fast-tracked. You haven't seen the paperwork yet, but the machine behind the smiling face is already running.

Mateo [quiet]: "They're selling safety like a product."

Amaya Reyes [brittle]: "They're selling certainty. And someone will pay for it with displacement."

Tamsin [careful]: "We need to avoid an escalation. If we block every conversation, we risk control of the narrative—and permits can move while negotiations stall. I can ask for a delay if you commit to limited talks."

You look at Tamsin. Her eyes flick to the Voss Rep and back to you—this is the hinge of municipal compromise. Your instinct is to refuse anything that buys time for Voss. Your instinct is to build pressure on the ground. The volunteer workloads, the scraped knee, the drone maps: these are not abstract tests. They are hands in mud and children with bruises. You feel the old guilt—the one that makes you vow to carry danger yourself so others do not.

Corinne Voss doesn't speak herself; her presence is in the brochures and the poised representative. The rep's smile doesn't change, but there's a sharpness in her measuring gaze—she notes who is visibly tired, who flinches, who is immovable.

Voss Rep [flattening optimism]: "We also have community outreach programs—training, placement, infrastructure studies. Think of long-term resilience."

Amaya Reyes [bitter]: "Long-term for whom? The family that loses their house is not a line item."

The conversation spirals; each word is a small stone thrown into a lake. Around you, neighbors tighten their coats against a wind that suddenly tastes of rain and salt and corporate varnish. The drone feed on Luka’s tablet shows new shadows: an unfamiliar patrol boat circling farther out. A campus of corporate security would be a bright, moving thing all its own.

[Scene: TideLab | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Mud-spattered boots clustered under the shelter; the light is merciless and gray]

By late afternoon, the air tightens. Luka's brow is damp with condensation; the drone blip shows a notification: security advisory—an anonymous tip of "unpermitted demonstration" filed at the docks. Tamsin's tablet pings again with the permit status—fast-tracked, approved for preliminary works in the harbor zone. The words feel like a slap.

Jules Park: "Is that legal? That fast?"

Tamsin [wearily]: "The administration can expedite under emergency provisions. Corinne's lawyers are efficient."

You feel the room narrow. Volunteers look to you, waiting for instruction; your refusal to compromise has become a stance. It's no longer abstract. The town's activities are visible and, now, targeted.

You close your notebook as if to shut out the weather. A memory slides up uninvited—Abuela Rosa's hands pressing tiny seeds into a reed mat; the way she looked at the horizon and named storms like relatives. The memory is warm and aching, and the guilt returns a million-fold—because for all your planning, for all the maps and reproducible protocols, a child still came home with a scraped knee, and your chest fills with a cold, practical horror at the thought of worse things to come if the standoff intensifies.

Mateo [voice low]: "We can put up night watches."

Amaya Reyes [clenching]: "We will. But not to provoke. To protect."

Luka Maren moves to your side, his presence a small, steady force. He doesn't have a solution for corporate permits or fast legal rollers, but he has maps and machines and the way he looks at you when your jaw tightens—like he's cataloging which parts of you to hold.

Luka Maren [quiet]: "We make the work visible. We document everything. Live-stream the restoration; timestamp the placements. If they try to claim prior works, the records will say otherwise."

Amaya Reyes: "And if they send a cease-and-desist? If security doubles down?"

Luka Maren: "Then we have evidence. And we won't be alone."

A Voss-branded security lancha cruises nearer, its outline a blunt, black silhouette against the horizon. On the boardwalk, a man with a camera from a regional outlet asks pointed questions about safety and legality. Your volunteer count tightens at the edges: some faces young and resolute, others older and worried.

You feel your refusal harden into a line drawn in wet sand. The thought hums through you—this is a standoff now. They have permits and polished brochures; you have people and muddy hands. Neither of you will fold without cost.

Rain picks up. It strikes the corrugated metal with a small, insistent percussion that sounds, absurdly, like an approaching drum. Your chest tightens until every breath feels carefully rationed.

You look at your hands—salted skin, calluses along the knuckles; they look smaller than your confidence. The guilt is a familiar companion: you imagine each scraped knee multiplied, each tired volunteer knocked down by legal force or corporate machinery. You imagine Abuela Rosa's face folded into worry. You think of Luka's amber eyes, the way he keeps showing up with ideas and drones and a softness you don't earn.

The tide is rising in more ways than one. You stand at the center of the work, and someone will have to decide how far to push, and how much of the town to risk. You feel the responsibility like a physical weight on your shoulders—the kind that makes your spine ache.

The moment stretches: the drone's rotor buzz, the rain's drum, the murmurs on the boardwalk. Visibility is no longer safety; it is exposure. You are a bright target.
