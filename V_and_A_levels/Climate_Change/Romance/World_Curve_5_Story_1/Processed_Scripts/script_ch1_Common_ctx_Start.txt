[Scene: TideLab | Dawn]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a slow, steady drip from a leaky gutter]
[Music: Low, somber piano—sparse, held notes]

You wake to the smell of wet soil and diesel, the kind of smell that lives in TideLab like a memory. Your overalls are patchwork and damp at the cuffs; salt has crusted along the soles of your rubber boots. The merino scarf at your collar is damp where you fell asleep over tide maps, and when you move you feel the stiffness of a night spent hunched over a table full of sketches.

On the bench, your leather-bound field notebook lies open. Watercolor tide lines blur at the edges where salt met pigment; tiny, desperate handwriting annotates each smear—dates, heights, the names of places you can't stop visiting in your head. You count losses the way other people count breaths: an absent front door, a shifted pier, a house whose porch now angles toward the bay.

You run a thumb over a small painting of the boardwalk undercut—inked notations you can follow in your sleep. The name of your lost friend is written in the margin, a vertical stroke that refuses to be tidy. The guilt presses under your ribs like salt in a wound: the precise, patient ache that shaped the rest of your life.

[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: A quiet scrape as someone knocks on a metal table]

The shed door creaks; Luka Maren steps in, drone controller cradled like a guilty phone. He moves as if sound might shatter something fragile—hair tied back, a shy grin that doesn't reach the tired lines beside his eyes.

Luka Maren: "Morning. Brought coffee—well, reheated—and the drone's back online. The gimbal's sticky but—"
He holds up the scuffed controller like an offering.

You study his face. He looks like someone who has been holding his breath and forgot to exhale.

Amaya Reyes: "You shouldn't have. But thanks." Your voice is blunt, as always—practicality worn like armor. "Check the battery cycles. And the firmware; I want a clean log before we fly over the northern planks."

Luka Maren [softly]: "Right. Numbers. We'll get them."

Luka Maren's smile thins in a way you read as apology and as hope both.

Luka Maren: "Yeah. I ran the diagnostics. I'll patch the logs. Also—"
He hesitates, thumb worrying a sticker on the controller. "There's a job inquiry I've been turning down. I—"

You stop him with a look that isn't unkind, just focused.

Amaya Reyes: "Later. Today is panel prep. The hall wants numbers, not hypotheticals. We need the boardwalk undercuts quantified, the probability ranges laid out. No spin."

Luka Maren [softly]: "Right. Numbers. We'll get them."

Luka Maren moves to a bench and begins to tinker at the drone. His hands are careful; he treats metal like muscle. You breathe the lab's air—oil, salt, a faint sweetness of seedling soil from the greenhouse extension—and for a moment the world contracts to tools and lists and the steady rhythm of work.

The shed door opens again. Mateo steps in carrying a crate; his hands are sun-leathered and trembling as though the sea itself runs in his blood and remembers roughness. The crate is full of brittle kelp samples, paper-thin and fragile. A briny tang follows him in.

Mateo: "Found more along the reef strip. Didn't look right."
He sets the crate down with a soft curse. "Whole beds looked… glassy. Like someone froze them mid-sway."

You pull on gloves, fingers moving on reflex. You lift a frond and the kelp flakes, fine salt shedding like old paint.

Amaya Reyes: "How many beds?" (You keep your voice steady; the steadiness is deliberate—a tool.)

Mateo: "Enough to worry. Could be the bloom cycle. Could be the runoff from the new tanks up the channel. I don't know." He rubs his thumb along a callus. "I thought of you."

Amaya Reyes [tight]: "Good. We sample everything. pH, nitrates, temp profiles. Don't let it sit in the crate more than an hour. Bring me the logs from last month and the new bait permits."

Mateo nods, jaw working. He looks smaller under the lab lights, and the crate seems to take more of his weight than it should.

[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]
[Sound: Paper rustling; the low hum of a mismatched battery bank]

Abuela Rosa arrives as the first gray light thickens. She moves with the practiced economy of someone who has walked this shore every season of her life; her shawl is bright against the neutral palette, a small bloom of color. She sets a faded map on the bench—hand-drawn markers, ink faded, a coastline remembered before it was on any official chart.

Abuela Rosa [soft, precise]: "This marker. My cousin called it Las Olas. She said when the fifth post falls, the sand changes like a fever. It is now."

You kneel to look at her map. Her handwriting curves like tide lines.

Amaya Reyes: "People marked spots by where they planted the roses." (You don't say it aloud—how personal this is. It's a concession to history.)

Abuela Rosa: "We are stories and oaths and rope knots between us. They come offering concrete and charts; we offer where the ground sings. Neither is enough alone."

Amaya Reyes [quiet]: "We need both. And we need to be listened to."

Abuela Rosa studies you, eyes bright as glass.

Abuela Rosa: "Listen is earned. Keep your ledger, child. But do not let it be the only voice."

The day stretches out with hushed purpose. You and your small crew map the boardwalk undercuts in ink and numbers, measuring the distance from the planks to the new low-tide marks. Each measured gap is a small resignation: wood eaten away, pilings exposed, the shadow of loss lengthening.

[Scene: Marisol Bay Boardwalk | Morning]
[Background:"ch1_Start_4"]
[Sound: Footsteps, distant water slapping against pilings; the creak of boards as you walk]
[Music: Single sustained cello note—quiet, resonant]

You step onto the boardwalk with a notebook in one hand, a rangefinder in the other. Mud squeezes at the edges of old nails; algae makes the planks shine in absent light. You mark undercuts with a practiced hand, the notation small and efficient. People pass—faces weighed with different brands of worry. The collective breath of the town moves in small waves, and you are the measuring instrument that tries to make sense of the pull.

Back at TideLab, as you catalog the boardwalk measurements, Tamsin calls. Her voice is even, practiced.

Tamsin: "Amaya. I need TideLab's raw logs. Council is asking for all available data before the briefing. Voss will submit their render today; we need your measurements to juxtapose."

You feel the ledger under your palm like a small, heavy thing.

Amaya Reyes: "You'll have everything I can compile. Full transparency, but—"
You deliberate, choosing words. "—we need to preserve the integrity of data. If you pass raw files to Voss, I want to be in the room when they interpret them."

Tamsin [a pause, then practical]: "That's reasonable. I can't promise Voss won't present their own analysis, but I'll ensure TideLab is credited. We need numbers that stand up. There will be reporters."

Amaya Reyes: "Reporters are noise. Make sure the panel gets the variance ranges, not just a best-case curve. We can't afford a sugar-coated promise."

Tamsin: "Understood. Send the files by noon?"

Amaya Reyes: "By noon." You hear the click at the end of the line—absence where a human voice was, and you taste it like metal.

You sit in the low light of TideLab, watching the progress bars and the slow mechanical breathing of the place. The sun moves higher but feels as if it's climbing through molasses; everything is slightly delayed, as if the world is holding its breath.

Then the holo projection blooms at the edge of the room—a glossy render that seems too clean for this salt-ruined town. Corinne Voss [Holo]: "Good morning, TideLab. I'm Corinne Voss. Voss Marine proposes a comprehensive seawall and raised quay system that would secure port infrastructure and regional logistics for decades. We understand the pain of change. Our plan is a responsible compromise."

[Sound: Subtle synthetic chime as hologram initializes; Corinne's voice perfectly modulated]
[Music: A faint, clinical synth wash under her words]

Tamsin: "Corinne, this is Amaya Reyes of TideLab. She'll be presenting community data this afternoon."

Corinne Voss [smile practiced and efficient]: "Ah—Amaya. The town's own scientist. Your work is, well, admirable. We hope to demonstrate how engineering can spare the larger community from catastrophic loss."

You look at the render. The seawall is beautiful in its geometry—clean lines, efficient grades—but what it does not show are the houses behind those lines, the fishing nets, the places with names. The proposal mentions relocations almost as an afterthought; the render frames it as an editorial adjustment, a necessary pruning.

Amaya Reyes [slow]: "Your render doesn't show the informal docks, the low-income blocks along South Pier, or the community gardens in the marshland. How many homes would need to be relocated under your proposal?"

Corinne Voss [gliding]: "We anticipate a small number in the immediate footprint—measured relocations—balanced by long-term security. Displacement is difficult; it is regrettable in the short term, but the alternative is far worse. With our model, we protect critical infrastructure that underpins the entire bay's economy."

A low, resigned fury stirs in you, tightly coiled and controlled.

Amaya Reyes: "Measured relocations for ports and 'critical infrastructure' is different language for moving people you decided were expendable. Who decides which neighborhoods are critical?"

Corinne Voss: "Urban planners and risk analysts—professionals with the necessary scope to weigh individual hardship against collective survival. These are hard choices."

Abuela Rosa watches the floating image with a quiet gravity.

Abuela Rosa: "Hard choices are not always wise. There are other rhythms. You do not listen to the shoreline in models."

Corinne Voss [calm, unruffled]: "We respect local knowledge. We also respect the need to act at scale. Engineering can be decisive. We would like to meet with community representatives—TideLab included—to refine the model."

You feel the old guilt sharpen: the friend whose porch was taken in a single night, whose name is the vertical stroke in your notebook. The guilt loops into a promise—an unspoken covenant to do better, to prevent the last.

Amaya Reyes [quiet]: "We will attend. But our attendance does not imply agreement."

Corinne Voss's smile does not falter. The holo shimmers and then dematerializes, leaving a little chill in the air, like the echo of a door closing.

You sit for a long moment with your hands folded around the field notebook. The tide maps stare back like patient witnesses. You rehearse evidence aloud—probability ranges, mapped undercuts, socioeconomic overlays—phrases that feel like armor against a smooth render and a practiced smile.

You think of people you cannot afford to lose: Mateo and his boat, Abuela Rosa and the oral histories she holds, the volunteers who patch nets and plug holes with their own hands. You think of how easily a glossy plan reduces those names to coordinates.

Your chest is tight—an old tension you have learned to carry like a tool. The arousal inside you is cool and slow, not a flare but a steady burn: practical, unyielding. The day ahead is a low-lit corridor of meetings and small confrontations; none of them will be loud, but all will be decisive.

You tuck the notebook back into your bag, the leather soft and salt-stained. Outside, the sky gathers iron-gray clouds. The boardwalk will fill with people; the hall will fill with voices. You can hear the rain beginning somewhere beyond the windows—the prelude to something larger, or perhaps simply weather. Either way, you stand and pull the scarf tighter around your neck.

You are blunt. You are protective. You are loyal. You rehearse the names of who you cannot afford to lose like an inventory, and you step out of the shed.

A single thought lingers as you move toward the Community Hall: the ledger in your hand and the ledger of the town are not the same thing, but they must now be read together.
