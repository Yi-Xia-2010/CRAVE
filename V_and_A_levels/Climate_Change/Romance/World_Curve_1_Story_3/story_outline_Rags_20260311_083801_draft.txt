## Visual Novel Title
Tides of San Brisa

---

## World View
San Brisa is a mid-sized coastal town where daily life is scored by the rise and fall of the tide. Once a fishing hub with mid-century storefronts and a handful of family-run businesses, the town now sits in uneasy tension between memory and development. Aging wooden piers, salt-streaked neon signs, and low, brick storefronts rub shoulders with speculative construction sites and gated lots funded by outside investors. Politics are local and transactional: a small town council balances community petitions, corporate promises, and the urgent mathematics of insurance and emergency planning.

The culture is centered on interdependence — neighborhood cafes, rooftop markets, and church basements where oral histories are kept and passed along. Religion is diffuse; ritual sits in memorial plaques, fishermen’s knots, and the annual tide-watch vigil. Class lines are visible: long-time residents and fishers with weathered hands and small businesses face buyout offers and relocation pressure; developers and investors arrive in tailored coats and glossy presentations. Environmental conflict crystallizes around competing adaptation philosophies: Aurelia Holdings and Councilor Marco push engineered, rapid-protection measures while grassroots groups led by locals like Maya and Eli advocate for nature-based, community-driven resilience.

Geography is immediate: a narrow harbor hugging the main street, a glass-domed Tidewatch Lab overlooking the bay, a crumbling lighthouse on tidal stones, and an Aurelia construction site where cranes and sheet piles rise like teeth. The atmosphere is humid and lived-in — fog in the mornings, warm-dusk light, rust and salt on surfaces — visualized with sea-glass greens, storm-grays, and a soft film grain.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maya Reyes) return home as San Brisa lives its slow, rising crisis. The story charts my effort to implement a community-centered resilience plan while navigating a complicated professional and romantic collaboration with Eli Navarro, a sandy-haired sustainable architect. June Park and Aurelia Holdings press a competing, engineered seawall. Across a branching set of choices, I balance science and intimacy, community trust and political negotiation, and the ethics of compromise under pressure. The emotional arc is Rise: we begin weighed down by guilt, risk, and fracturing community ties; through sustained effort, coalition-building, and acts of vulnerability the town and my relationship with Eli gain momentum. Multiple routes emphasize different strategies — grassroots pilot projects, public exposure and legal pressure, and negotiated hybrid solutions — each culminating in outcomes that reflect progress and the town’s adaptability. Every path remains grounded in sensory, local detail: salt-bleached hair, patched cargo pants, a brass compass, Polaroids of seedlings, fogged greenhouse glass, and the clang of construction.
