### DRAFT
# Route: Route C (Ending)


[Scene: Council Chamber | Early Evening]
[Visual: Fluorescent panels hum above a long oak table; projection light pools on schematic slides. June Park’s high-tech vest glints against her tailored coat; Eli’s sketchbook lies open beside your elbow. Tomas stands near the back, hands still smelling of oil. Rosa occupies the doorway, shawl folded tight. Asha’s posture is steady, like a rooted reed.]
[Music: Driving, urgent BGM — a rapid low pulse undercut with bright string runs]
[Sound: Low murmur of the room, the soft clicking of a pen, an intermittent buzz from someone’s phone set to silent]

The air in the chamber tastes like boiled kettle metal and sea-spray residue. You can feel salt under your tongue from the walk over — the town breathes the bay into its lungs, and tonight that breath is fast.

You take the chair at the negotiation table because it’s the only practical place to sit. Your field notebook is closed, but the brass compass on its cord swings once as you set your hands flat on the wood. The slide behind June reads: "Aurelia Coastal Defense — Immediate Protection Strategy." Below her title, line after line of timelines and modeled flood maps march like soldiers.

June Park: "Thank you all. I appreciate the town making time. We don’t have much runway—the winter storms are compressing decision windows. Our model shows a potential breach on Pier 4 in under five years without intervention."

You taste the figure as if it’s a physical thing — five years, sharp and cold. You push back the immediate flare of panic with a practiced inhale. There is too much to say, too many names to protect.

You: "Models are useful, June. But they are abstractions. They don’t carry Rosa’s recipes, Tomas’s tools, or Asha’s species lists. If we’re going to build, it must preserve function, not just shape."

June Park (tilting her head, the silver streak at her temple catching the light): "Shape can preserve function if engineered correctly. Veneer-detritus planting is fast, affordable, and —" (she taps the tablet on the briefcase) "— monitored with sensors. We can guarantee immediate protection."

Eli leans forward, fingers stained with clay and paint. He spreads a fold of paper between you: a quick, fluid sketch of a concrete buttress that steps into living terraces — sea grasses planted in coir baskets, rock pockets for juvenile fish, and access points for fishermen.

Eli Navarro: "Make it permeable where it matters. Not just walls, Maya — modules. Stairs that let tidal exchange happen. We can bolt up a top course for commerce and let the lower face breathe."

You feel the heat of his optimism; it’s infectious and reckless in the best way. You want to stand and pull those modular lines into the projection, to show how a hybrid face can be both immediate armor and habitat scaffold.

June Park (cautious, sharp): "Modules are time-consuming and expensive. Scale favors engineered bulkheads. Our investors need predictability."

Asha intercepts with the weight of someone who has measured a coastline’s decline over decades.

Asha: "Predictability doesn’t mean permanence. If the lower intertidal is sterilized, you’ve created a false security—a dead zone behind a shiny wall. There must be governance: ecological benchmarks, enforcement, adaptive triggers. Without them, the veneer becomes theater."

Your pulse climbs a notch. The room seems to constrict and expand with each speaker. This is the high-stakes choreography you feared and hoped for — words like ballast and benchmarks, like promises and legal guarantees, stacked against the roar of models and funding.

June’s eyes narrow with that precise, assessing stare you’ve seen in council reports and at early site visits.

June Park: "If Aurelia binds itself to monitoring, are you asking us to tie our hands to community boards and open-source design reviews? That’s a governance risk."

You: "It’s a trust instrument. If you want acceptance, you accept accountability. We don’t ask for performative greenscaping. We ask for clauses that make ecological function a deliverable: percent habitat connectivity, tidal exchange metrics, and legally enforceable community stewardship."

There is a small, charged silence — the kind that holds votes and futures. Tomas clears his throat from the back. The smell of oil is strong when he speaks; it grounds the idea in lived economy.

Tomas: "Maya—if you take this fight, will Aurelia force anyone out? Will they buy us out at pennies? I can’t rebuild the pier if my shop’s gone."

Your chest tightens at the rawness in his voice. You remember the magnet on his workshop board: a list of regulars, names in a looping, impatient script. Each one is a decimal in the ledger of the town.

You: "We make guarantees that protect tenure and access. Part of the agreement will be buyback and relocation protections for small businesses, a schedule for community employment on the build, and a review board that includes residents."

Rosa watches from the doorway, mouth pressed into a wary smile. She steps forward, coffee-scented shawl and all, and says in the tone of someone who’s seen promises made and melted like spring salt.

Rosa Alvarez: "Words mean very little if my café ends up two blocks inland and feels like a stranger in its own skin. This town is memory. Any design that moves memory without consent is violence."

June’s jaw softens — just a hair. Something in the room makes the steel of her posture flex.

June Park: (a long exhale) "I didn’t come here to erase anyone. I came here to keep the town standing. If we must accept oversight—real oversight—then we need parameters that won’t kill the project before it starts. Investors won’t fund endless committees."

Eli presses his palm to the sketch as if holding a heartbeat.

Eli Navarro: "Then we tie funding tranches to deliverables. Investors get a phased design with go/no-go points, and the community gets seats on the governance board with veto power at key thresholds. You get protection now; we get habitat function measured and meaningfully enforced."

Your breath is a staccato thread as you match phrases in your head to legal templates Asha taught you. Very high arousal — the room crackles like an exposed wire. Every concession is a risk; every clause is a lifeline.

Asha: "And adaptive management. Not a one-off report, but a covenant: monitoring, independent audits, and a community fund that can pause construction if indicators drop. Make it contractual. Make it binding."

June Park: (after a beat, voice steady, decisive) "We can write adaptive covenants into the contract. Aurelia accepts independent audits and a community oversight panel for the pilot. But—" (she looks directly at you) "—we need compromises on materials and schedule. We will proceed as a pilot on the east harbor if the town approves design principles and oversight."

You feel something break like thin ice and settle into a new shape. The concession is real. The pilot window is narrow — a single, shining gap in a wall of possible futures.

June nods, making notes; Eli grins, picturing Tomas elbow-deep in the work." || Choice B ("Demand immediate ecological performance thresholds be written into the pilot agreement.") -> Reaction: "You speak for the mussel beds and eelgrass — Asha’s face eases as the words fall; June rubs the bridge of her nose, but the legal team at her side begins drafting language."]

The room accelerates: legal aides whisper, June’s tablet lights throw quick diagrams on the ceiling, Councilor Marco’s pen scratches a new line in the margin. The pulse in your ears is loud and jubilant — a drumline for a fragile victory.

June Park: "We’ll deliver a draft. It will include monitoring schedules, independent auditors, community governance, a funding tranche linked to milestones, and labor provisions. If the community signs off, we start the pilot next quarter."

You taste relief like salt and citrus. Your shoulders, which have been locked and upward for months, drop a fraction. There’s an awkward warmth in the table’s center — not romantic, not soft, but the kind of ember that forms when opposing hands strike a match by coincidence.

Eli leans over and murmurs something you can scarcely hear over the thrum of your own blood.

Eli Navarro: (softly) "You did it."

You want to say you didn’t. You want to list every caveat and conditional and hidden clause you can think of. Instead, you close your fingers on the edge of the sketch and let yourself register the import: a living compromise that keeps people in place and insists the shoreline remain alive.

Asha places a hand on your arm — a benediction and a warning. Her eyes are bright.

Asha: "You’ve bought us a runway. Don’t waste it."

Tomas nods, relief and fear tangled. Rosa lets out a breath that sounds suspiciously like a laugh.

Rosa Alvarez: "We’ll hold them to it — together."

You stand to leave with the draft hovering between your palms — digital and paper promises waiting to be signed and hammered into reality. The fluorescent lights feel suddenly less clinical and more like an operating theater where a fragile repair has just been announced.

[Visual: Close on your hand folding the draft into your notebook; the brass compass rests on top, steady]
[Music: Climactic swell — bright strings, driving percussion, then a held chord that vibrates with possibility]
[Sound: The room buzzes with resolved energy — pens stop, chairs scrape, a single cheer murmurs at the back]

You feel a strange mix of relief and distrust, like warm rain over a sun-scorched deck: life returning, but cautious, testing.

Outside the chamber window, fog is thinning. You catch, for a second, the reflection of the lighthouse in the bay—an old shape and a new light. It pricks something at the base of your throat, an urgency and a promise braided together.

You fold the draft into your notebook. The pages rustle like surf. You step toward the door, the negotiation still buzzing in your ears, the town outside waiting for the pilot to be explained, for the clauses to be read aloud, for the signatures — or for skepticism to gather and test the new accord.

You breathe in salt air that smells of possibility and diesel and coffee, and you let the moment stretch: a narrow window has opened. You can already see the work that will follow—the community meetings, the audits, the plantings at low tide, the nights spent drafting enforceable language with a trembling hand.

[Visual: Wide on the chamber as figures rise and move; light slides across the table, leaving the drafted agreement in the center like a promise]
[Music: Fade to a sustained, hopeful note — urgent but full of light]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the quick cadence of footsteps on stairs]

You walk out into the dusk with a draft in your pocket and a town at your back, the pulse in your chest answering to a new tempo: quicker, expectant, alive.

[Page-Turn Moment]
Your thumb rubs the corner of the draft until the paper softens. You think of the pier you loved as a child, the one the sea took; you think of Tomas's hands and Rosa’s laugh, Asha’s steady stare and Eli’s grin. The pilot is imperfect, the window is narrow, but the agreement is a real thing now—something to be defended, improved, and lived. For an instant you allow yourself to imagine the first stretch of planted coir baskets at low tide catching juvenile fish. The image blooms like a sudden gull call above surf, sharp and true.
