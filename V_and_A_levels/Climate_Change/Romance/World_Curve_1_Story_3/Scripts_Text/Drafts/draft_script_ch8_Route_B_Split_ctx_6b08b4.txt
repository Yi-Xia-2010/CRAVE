### DRAFT
# Route: Route B (Alternative Ending)


[Scene: San Brisa Harbor | Late Afternoon — Rain Threatening]
[Visual: Wide on the harbor — slick cobbles reflecting battered neon; the Aurelia construction site looms at the pier, sheet piles like teeth. June Park stands framed by concrete pylons, her trench coat sharp against the gray. A small cluster of suited investors huddles under a plastic canopy.]
[Music: Rapid, driving strings with a bright undertone — momentum and urgency]
[Sound: Camera clicks, the murmur of a growing crowd, distant ship horns, the slap of rain starting to fall]

You stand where the cobbles slope toward the sea, rain-slick hair clinging to the back of your neck. The injunction bought you breath, but the town has already turned that breath into movement. Picket signs bob like small flags of stubborn life; a local broadcaster's windscreen flaps as someone shouts into a microphone. Diesel and wet wood fuse into one smell — clean salt undercut with the oil of heavy machines. Your palm is damp around the brass compass; it feels too heavy to be only metal.

Near the sheet piles you can see June — not a caricature, not a villain from a headline, but precise and composed as the manilla folder at her elbow. She speaks in quiet cadences with the investors; you catch fragments: "timelines," "mitigation," "protecting assets." Her face is perfectly unreadable until she turns and spots you — for a beat, a flicker of something passes: control tested, memory pricked. She inclines her head like a practiced gesture and then returns to the numbers.

Eli is not at your side, but his presence is audible: the staccato rhythm of his camera clicking as he builds a visual timeline. He moves along the waterfront, documenting seedling trays tucked under tarps, salt-etched boards, Tomas's shop window where a faded repair list peers from beneath a magnet. When he catches your eye he lifts a finger to the viewfinder as if to say, keep going — document, show them what matters.

You taste the river-wet air, iron on your tongue from the rain. Each small sound — a chant being taught at the fringe of the crowd, the urgent whisper of a producer coordinating livestreams on a phone, a metal panel being hammered down on the construction site — threads together into a fierce, hopeful orchestration. This is how movements swell: not in a single roar, but in thousands of matched breaths.

[Visual: Close on your compass in your palm, the needle trembling]
[Sound: A sudden swell in percussion — the town's heartbeat accelerating]
[Music: Percussive strings rise; a bright brass hook hints at triumph]

You walk closer to the construction boundary. A security guard in a yellow vest blocks the nearest access point; his badge reads Aurelia Security. They are efficient, practiced, the tide-slick machinery of a firm used to moving the sea of public opinion. Still, the crowd is not cowed. Rosa stands among them near a banner, her shawl bright against the damp, calling names and stories into the microphone between sips of scalding coffee. Her voice cracks, but the crack is human and warm; people respond with shouts and the rattling applause of saucepans and lids from a nearby kitchen volunteer station.

Asha appears at your shoulder as if she has grown from the mist. Her hands are alive with printed graphs and field notes, the paper edges curled from too many palms. She does not take your hand; she places a small stack of pages in your other palm — baseline salinity results, species counts, the chart that shows when eelgrass beds failed. Her eyes, behind thick glasses, are steady and soft. "We have what we need," she says. "We can make the science sing so people hear it."

You swallow. Your throat is raw with argument rehearsed a thousand times: evidence braided with memory. This is not only about stopping a wall — it's about giving the town a language for what it loses and what it can still keep. Your mind runs quickly now, matching options to consequence with the brutal efficiency of a tideboard: exposé, council coalition, demonstration. Each pulls taut some part of the town: allies who prefer quiet negotiation, neighbors who want to be seen and heard now, the risk of legal entanglements and broken trust.

Eli arrives with a Polaroid set tucked into his chest pocket and a wet smile. He hands you one that shows Tomas's shop a week ago — the same window now shadowed by scaffold. "Chronology," he says, his voice a small grin. "People respond to pictures. History in an instant."

You feel the warmth of that grin like a small torch. He steps closer and his tone drops, earnest and fast. "We can do all three in sequence," he suggests. "Drop the exposé, but also push the council — force the meeting dates. And we bring people to the site if June doesn't play fair. We keep options open."

You speak before you think much: You: "All three at once risks burning those we need." Your voice is low but reaches the cluster of volunteers nearby; a hush follows. "If the town fractures—if Tomas and Rosa get caught in the crossfire—what good is a halted wall?"

Eli: "Then we make the risk meaningful. We protect the small risks." He gestures toward the volunteers, toward Rosa, toward the workshop where people patch signs and sip tea. "We have lawyers, we have reporters, and we have Asha's testimony. We can stagger the hits — expose the truth while we lock down council votes."

You: "Staggered hits are good in principle. People are not machinery." The rain learns to fall in harder lines, and the harbor's scent sharpens with it. "They get scared. They move. They sell when they shouldn't. Tomas has been up all night worrying about customers. He asked me not to put him in front of a camera."

Tomas, who has been standing a respectful half-step behind you, now cracks. He steps forward, shoulders hunched from long hours bent over bent metal and motor oil. Tomas: "I didn't ask for a spotlight. I asked for roofs over my head and customers who can find my door after a storm. If this goes on too long, I'm sunk." His voice is raw, sudden. "I know what people do when panic starts. They take the safe money. They leave."

Rosa, sharpening a point with a spoon, calls across the crowd. Rosa: "No more hiding the stories, Tomas. If those boys who built that wall think money buys memory, let them see ours." Her laugh is fierce, then soft. "But I hear you. We can't throw our people under headlines."

Asha folds her arms, thinking aloud: "An exposé can create a legal emergency that buys us time — injunctive relief, public attention. But we must pair that with policy muscle: tighten approvals, make conditionalities that force Aurelia to redesign. Demonstration is the show of popular will, but it has to be strategic."

You taste the equation on your tongue: momentum equals risk plus visibility. The arousal inside you climbs like wind up a cliff — a hard, bright energy that could snap or could carry something through.

June's PR machine hums across the site. A film crew in clean jackets sets up a branded backdrop reading "Aurelia: Protecting San Brisa." A spokesperson approaches the investors with a confident cadence, deflecting a shouted question from a reporter with practiced phrases: "We are committed to community safety"; "independent reviews will follow"; "we welcome dialogue." The words are polished; the effect is to slow the panic and funnel it into institutional places — hearings, white papers, time.

You think of the legal breathing room you already have. The injunction is a pause, not a victory. You can use this to file, to gather, to pressure. Or you can make the pause erupt into a spectacle that the internet can't ignore.

Eli comes back smiling, breathless. He drops a Polaroid onto your palm — Tomas's shop with a hand-lettered sign that reads "We Fix What the Sea Breaks." He meets your eyes. "People will follow faces," he says. "They'll follow faces they know."

You turn and catch the sight of the town gathered: young people with raincoats, elders with umbrellas. Someone hands out hot soup; someone else sets a string of lights that will turn the harbor into a beacon when dusk falls. The crowd hums with a resolve that feels, impossibly, like celebration. This movement has cadence. It also has teeth.

Every conversation you have multiplies the stakes. The PR team will spin; Aurelia's lawyers will file counters; investors will blink at projected returns, not at eelgrass counts. But the town is also learning how to speak in one voice. You taste the electric possibility of it, bright and saline.

June walks toward the edge of the construction lot and, for the first time since you arrived, makes a small gesture of acknowledgment in your direction. Her lips do not say much; the expression across her face is complex — not cruel, not kind, like a tide that doesn't announce itself. She stops within earshot and then, with the same unreadability, returns to her investors.

You feel something in your chest that is both terror and exhilaration; the two are not opposites here — they are the same current. The crowd thickens; the rain grows. Microphones are handed out, and volunteers form a chain near the site's barrier. The arousal that had been a simmer now surges — chants begin to shape into structured phrases that camera crews can repeat. Momentum is catching.

You look at Asha, at Eli, at Tomas, at Rosa. You see the town in each of them: people who will lend their names, their faces, their memories. You imagine headlines and court summonses, viral videos and hushed negotiations. You imagine a policy calendar shifting because people looked, listened, and insisted.

All that remains is the next move.
