[Scene: Conference Room | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Overhead projector casting a pale rectangle on a peeling plaster wall; a slide of the Aurelia site lighted at dusk]
[Music: Low, steady ambient hum — municipal tones]
[Sound: The soft chirr of a fluorescent light, distant gulls through a cracked window]

You stand beneath the projector's wash, the light thinning the lines around your eyes into a map of fatigue. The hum presses gently at the base of your skull like an approaching tide. Outside, on the slide, Aurelia's cranes are already staged — bright floodlights stabbed into the dusk — and for a moment the construction looks less like a plan and more like a promise of permanence.

The air tastes faintly of metal and coffee. Someone's umbrella drip puddles by the door; Rosa's shawl smells of cardamom and old wood. You can feel salt at the back of your throat even though you're inside — a memory of the harbor, of boards that warp and sigh when storms come. Your brass compass, warm from your palm, sits on the table between you and the council like a small, stubborn star.

[Visual: Close on the compass; its needle trembles slightly]

You set the compass on the table with careful fingers. It is ridiculous and necessary, both. Ridiculous because it's only brass and glass; necessary because it is a thing that points when everything else argues. The room leans in. You clear your throat.

Councilor Marco: "Maya. The floor is yours."

June Park watches you with an even patience that doesn't need to hurry. Her gray eyes are polished and precise; the rest of her is tied up in a neat trench coat and a briefing packet. She has the kind of composure that reads as competence in every press photograph you've seen. Now, in the same room as your neighbors, it reads as certainty.

June Park: "Securing immediate loss is— (she chooses the words like placing stones) —our priority. Aurelia's seawall will buy the town time. It will protect Main Street, the commercial strip. Data supports it."

Eli leans forward beside you, one elbow on the table. His sea-glass eyes are softer than June's, searching for the seam where your resolve might fray so he can hold it.

Eli Navarro [quiet]: "We can't pretend this isn't urgent. But urgency doesn't have to mean one fixed tool. Living edges can be deployed in tandem; we can prototype and protect."

You feel Asha's presence as a small, steady pressure at your back — the weight of someone who has mapped tides with a pencil and walked spits of land while sea levels rose a fingertip each year. Rosa's hands fold on the table like an anchor; her mouth turns at the corners with worry that tastes like memory.

Tomas [voice clipped]: "Buyouts are in the memo. I need to know if I sell the shop, sis. If council votes for Aurelia, I don't get a choice. I—" He stops, jaw tightening. His sunburnt hands curl on the laminated agenda, knuckles white.

Your pulse is a quiet metronome. Look at the room. See how people are not just a list of stakeholders but a mesh of lives — a neighbor whose porch will flood first, a young couple with a baby in the next block, an old man who remembers the pier that used to sing its boards in summer. You are not arguing numbers; you are arguing futures.

You push the projector remote; the image switches to a diagram: cross-sections of a seawall, a living shoreline, cost estimates, and a smear of Aurelia's logo. The title at the top reads: "Protecting San Brisa: Immediate Solutions."

You choose — and the room shifts under that choice, just slightly. If you led with data, you feel validation ripple through the planners; if you led with memory, eyes soften and the numbers have faces.

You take both: the memory to anchor attention, the numbers to give it shape. Your voice finds an even cadence.

Maya Reyes: "Engineered protection can secure commerce quickly, but it also severs ecological connections that communities rely on. A seawall shifts wave energy. It changes where fish spawn, where marsh can migrate. Those are not abstract losses — they are losses that feed our tables and our stories."

June Park [tilting her head]: "And if we wait, Maya — if we bet on slowly implemented pilots — people will lose houses this season. Aurelia moves at scale. We can put steel where water is taking wood, and buy time for residents."

June's tone is not belligerent; it is exact, like a calculation. You respect the clarity. But the town is not a calculation only.

Eli touches the back of your hand — a light, private contact meant to steady and to say We are here together. His fingers smell faintly of clay and the salt from his pockets.

> Choice A: "Let Eli's touch steady you"
> Choice B: "Pull your hand back and keep professional distance"
> Choice C: "Squeeze his hand briefly, then let go"
[Outcome A: "The contact is a small, warm anchor and you draw breath. Your voice even now, you can feel his patience pulse against your skin."]

[Outcome C: "You give his fingers a brief, private squeeze — acknowledgment and a promise — then return your attention to the council. His eyes lift; there is a question there without words."]
[MERGE: You let small human things thread through the meeting because this is not a courtroom of abstractions — it's your town.]
[END INTERACTION]

You let small human things thread through the meeting because this is not a courtroom of abstractions — it's your town.

Asha Malik adjusts her glasses and slides a packet across the table to Councilor Marco. Her voice is calm, precise.

Asha Malik: "We can model phased living-shoreline deployments. They reduce erosion, support biodiversity, and can be staged incrementally. The upfront risk is lower than people fear when we coordinate evacuation plans and emergency funding."

Rosa leans in, voice husky with years and coffee: "When they put up the last seawall in my cousin's town, the fish simply moved out. The kids had no place to learn the bay. We cannot put a fence between us and the water and call that protection."

June Park [countering]: "Aurelia's proposal includes adaptive elements. The wall is engineered with sensors. It will be maintained. If you work with us, we can include ecological offsets."

Maya Reyes: "Offsets are not the same as habitat. There's also the legal and political power differential; Aurelia can outspend us on monitoring and lobbyists. The community's knowledge of place — Rosa's histories, Tomas's storefront life — that's irreplaceable."

Tomas [voice wavering]: "So, what then? People wait for prototypes and the buyouts keep happening in the meantime? I have rent, bills. Compromise means my family doesn't get left behind."

There is a tightness now under the ribs of the room. The hum of the projector feels louder by contrast, time compressed. You take another breath, tasting salt and metal and roasted coffee; your mouth goes dry.

Councilor Marco [leaning back, careful]: "We've heard data, lived testimony, and corporate plans. The motion before us is to decide a town path. Maya, you've been at the center of the community plan — we need your recommendation."

Your compass sits between you and the council like a miniature star. You know where it points; you know what you fear. You also know that every choice trades one set of risks for another.

You step forward. The light on your face makes the hollows beneath your cheekbones shallow; you can see your reflection in the polished surface of the projector like a faint ghost.

Maya Reyes: "An engineered seawall as the only option risks ecological collapse of the shoreline and, in the mid- to long-term, greater costs and displacement. A living-shoreline pilot buys us adaptive learning and community ownership. A legal/media campaign can expose Aurelia's risks and pressure better terms. A negotiated hybrid could harness immediate protection while reserving rights for ecological interventions."

June Park [quiet, precise]: "Those are three very different paths. Each has trade-offs. Which do you advise we choose?"

You feel the room hold its breath. The moment is not cinematic; it is practical and pointed. This is the hinge point: not a dramatic showdown, but a municipal decision with real people on either side.

Your thoughts momentarily fragment into the small faces of those affected: Tomas's freckled hands, Rosa's coffee-stained apron, Asha's worn field jacket, Eli's hopeful smile. You consider the image of Aurelia's floodlights cutting the harbor into a foreign geography and the living plans that might knit back what is fraying.

[Music: The hum becomes a low, steady pulse — the meeting's tempo tightening without flare]

You could outline the community pilot in full, call for legal counsel and a press strategy, or step into negotiation with June with carefully carved conditions. Each path will demand a different version of you: organizer, litigator, or broker. Each will shift alliances, test trust, and ask neighbors to make very personal bets.

The air tastes metallic. Your mouth is cotton-dry. The compass seems to tick, not with time, but with possibility.

Councilor Marco [solemn]: "Maya Reyes — we ask you to recommend a path forward for San Brisa."

You inhale. You think of measured timelines and immediate storms, of livelihoods and histories. You glance at Eli; his eyes are asking not for a vote but for faith. You glance at June; her face is unreadable, composed. Rosa's hands are folded in an old rhythm of prayer and paperwork. Tomas's jaw is set; fear and pride in close quarters.

[Visual: Wide on the room — everyone waiting; projector light haloing the decision like a small sun]
[Sound: The soft click of a pen as someone writes the moment down]

You are at the fulcrum.
