You take the podium after Livia finishes, the microphone catching your breath and making it too loud. Your notes are there—neat handwriting, tide charts and schematic sketches pressed flat by a thumb smudged with potting soil. You can feel the room holding its breath with you. The fluorescent light casts a halo over your page, translating everything into hard edges.

Mara Kestrel: "Our simulations don't promise a total fix. They reduce wave energy at the neighborhoods most at risk. They keep livelihoods—fishermen, small shops—intact. They can be built with local labor, repaired by the same hands that will rely on them."

A murmur ripples. Grandma Hira's hand clasps the edge of her shawl; her face is taut with a plea you don't need to parse. Rafi stands at the back like an old mast—steady, solid—nodding once.

Industry Rep: "With respect, those are pilot projects. Pilots in the face of escalating storms are compromises you can't afford."

You hear the accusation in his tone—an implied moral failing. You counter with statistics, with sensitivity analyses that show how distributed measures change flood line projections by measurable margins in plausible scenarios. You lay out the maintenance plans, the training programs, the phased cost curves.

But the chamber remembers other rules—speed, scale, the siren call of capital. Livia rises, the slide behind her switching to a glossy rendering of a thriving promenade and an numbers board that reads like an investor pitch.

Livia Chen: "Scale saves people quickly. The shore needs a decisive, unified barrier. Fragmented solutions cost more in the long run; they delay action."

You sense the tightness under her words now—a microtremor—like a wave under an iron plate. For the first time in public, her control shows a hairline crack: a brief hard set at the corner of her mouth, a finger that lingers on the slide advancer longer than necessary. One of Tess's notes flutters in your memory: covenant strings. You can't tell if that string will hold the promise or pull the whole town apart.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan rises after you. He walks to the microphone with the casual certainty of someone who has built things from scrap and made them sing. His voice is bright—open, but with an edge that carries the weight of scars.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan: "This isn't either-or. You can put a seawall where it makes sense and—" (he glances at Livia) "—but you also have to keep the neighborhoods functioning, the crews employed, the knowledge passed on. If we outsource everything to a wall, where do the people go when the wall fails?"

There is a shift in the room. A few heads tilt; some councilors exchange glances. Eli's words land like a small hammer on a brittle surface and it cracks enough for sound to move through.

Livia Chen [cool]: "Mr. Rowan, what you propose sounds—idealistic. The council has to think in population protection. Logistics. One coordinated barrier is administratively simpler."

Elias 'Eli' Rowan: "Simpler for whom?" (he looks at the audience, then at you) "For the contractors, perhaps. For the people who live here, simpler can mean abandonment."

The exchange grows teeth. A woman with a campaign lapel — a backer of Livia's plan — coughs loudly; someone shouts for "jobs." Grandma Hira stands now, and when she speaks her voice is small but sharpened by history.

Grandma Hira: "I have seen the boardwalk swallowed once. We sang while we mended it. We buried the ones the water took. You tell me to move every time. When do we keep what we love?"

Her question lands like a bell. The room tightens. The air tastes faintly of salt and fear.

You find yourself parsing motives like sea charts. Livia genuinely believes in order through scale; Tess's notes suggest legal levers that can bind the promise; Rafi's posture says he will show up to build; Eli's earnestness is tempered by past mistakes he barely mentions. You feel, underneath everything, the fragile thing that lives between you and Eli—trust that might hold or fray depending on the choice you make.

Council Chair [gravely]: "Ms. Kestrel, the council looks to you for a recommendation. Your models and experience are central. The town needs a path forward. Speak plainly."

You can feel the gallery pressing inward—rows of faces that have stories folded into their skin. The room is a physical weight: the brass reflecting the light like cold scales, the ocean beyond the windows a flat, indifferent witness.

A flurry of rapid exchanges erupts as councilors seek clarification—line items, maintenance responsibilities, insurance clauses. Voices overlap; the projector hums louder. The tempo rises; your pulse keeps perfect tempo with it. You count the breaths without meaning to. Each second stretches and compresses.

Livia watches you with that calibrated gaze. For the first time her smile is not quite gentle; it is an assessment. Her next words are lower, a steel wire beneath velvet.

Livia Chen: "Ms. Kestrel, your models are compelling in controlled conditions. The reality is messier. The council needs a plan that politics and markets will fund without years of piloting. We can begin construction this season. Delay risks lives."

You bite a thought back. The word "delay" becomes a blade that slices through every softer alternative. You feel the room pivot between fear of inaction and fear of wrong action. The air is charged—electric, too tight to hold without snapping.

Rafi [loud, steady]: "We've weathered storms together. We can learn to fix our own seams. But we need the tools and the rights to work. Don't hand us a promise that locks us out."
