You nod, tasting salt and metal. The rooftop smells of wet soil and the green-sweet rot of seedlings; the scent is a small mercy. Rafi leans on a rafter, the edges of his voice frayed but steady.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan: "I know." (he exhales sharply) "We have to make it work."

The pilot glows like a small miracle for a while. Rooftop gardens yield lettuce and hard herbs that smell of lemon and wind. Modular harvesting devices hum at low tide, their alarms quiet and sensible. The community garden at the lighthouse is a buzz of hands and laughter—early morning lessons on pruning, evenings where Rafi tells the old storm stories and everyone eats the first batch of emergency greens straight from the beds.

You keep records. You log yields, failure rates, maintenance cycles. Naomi's annotated charts go into your battered field notebook. Tess drops by with administrative snippets that make you laugh in disbelief—bureaucracy translated into human-scale wins.

Livia Chen comes by twice: once to cut a ribbon that means cameras and cameras mean headlines, once to place a careful hand on a planter and make a quiet, public promise. Her smile is efficient; the cameras eat it. In private, she is small and awkward in the dirt—her heels abandoned at the gate like a sacrificial reminder of the world she navigates.

Livia Chen [measured]: "The pilot is a good test-bed. If it proves resilience, we can argue for more funding on evidence."

You: "Evidence won't stop the ones who want a single answer, Livia. They will always want the big wall."

Livia Chen: "We buy time with the pilot. Time is something that can be used well."

The turning point is not grand. It arrives in a thin, bureaucratic voice over a bad connection. A supplier misses a milestone—a delayed shipment of stabilizing composites, a paperwork mix-up with a subcontractor—and an investor, who had been patient through months of small wins, invokes a contract clause. "Risk mitigation," the manager says. Funds are rerouted: away from the pilot to the main seawall project. The decision is legal and plausible, antiseptic and final.

Your throat closes with something like fury and grief at once. The wind leaps; the rooftop hums. The string of months tightens and snaps: half of what you built is enough to patch some neighborhoods; the rest—those alleyways and small storefronts you mapped in your head as lifelines—are rezoned. Sentiment becomes a zoning code.

You run. You go to Council Hall. You argue. You wave data and point to maintenance logs and plead for what is left of the pilot to be honored. You listen as Livia speaks to an audience that is half relieved and half furious. The optics have shifted—stability has a face now, and it is a concrete hum, a vacuum of displaced neighborhoods filled by promise.

You: "We can maintain the pilot. It needs the staff we trained. It needs the modest funding. If you redirect now, you sacrifice the lessons we've built, not just the pilots." (voice cracking)

Livia Chen [tight smile]: "We asked for a covenant that preserves the pilot's oversight. We're still holding to that."

Rafi's hands shake when he slaps a map down on the table. He doesn't swear; he scarcely needs to. His anger is a slow-burning coal that touches every thin place in you.

Rafi [old, bitter]: "They'll put up a wall and call it safety. Then they'll cut up the rest and say it was inevitable."

You: "We tried—God, we tried. We signed and we asked them to keep the oversight." (the admission tastes like iron)

The investors' decision carves city blocks. A handful of small enterprises are rezoned—favored plots reassigned, sentimental alleys reassessed as 'non-viable' or 'relocation required.' Familiar murals are painted over in planning renderings; a fishmonger who taught your neighbor's kid to gut mackerel is told the lease can't be renewed. You watch doors close that would have been doors you knocked on for help.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan goes quiet when the zoning notice arrives. His laugh, which had been a buoy, is smaller now—more infrequent and brittle. He starts taking odd jobs: a repair in a distant town one week, an installation in a suburban retrofit the next. He comes back smelling of diesel and antiseptic and is, at first, shrugging it off as practical. The grin blinks out gradually; his hands still know how to coax a valve into place, but there's a distance in his eyes you haven't bridged.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan: "I'll be back soon."

You: "How soon?"

Elias 'Eli' Rowan: "I don't know." (he rubs his jaw) "We keep the modules running here—someone has to. It's not over. It's just...smaller."

The town endures in pieces. The pilot persists—but as a skinnier thing. The rooftop gardens keep a few families fed when the storms press in. A couple of streets have stronger foundations. But sentimental alleyways wink out: a seamstress's shop rezoned into mixed-use retail, a corner of the boardwalk shifted into leveled frontage. Those losses are small on a ledger yet enormous in daily life.

Grandma Hira comes to the community garden with a slow, deliberate gait. She sits on a low crate and pulls a sprig of rosemary from her shawl, fingers steady in that way old hands become. She looks at you, and the expression is bone-deep: grief arranged like a prayer.

Grandma Hira [small and huge all at once]: "We kept what we could, child. We gave what we had."

You: "It feels like a bargain I didn't mean to make."

The final night is wind and glass and the slow erosion of everything you mapped emotionally as home. You climb the lighthouse stairs because that is where you have always come to think. The lens room is warm from the rigged heaters. You press your palm to the cool glass and look out across a town that has been saved and pared and rearranged.

Elias 'Eli' Rowan's last hug feels like an apology you both accepted. He kissed you then—brief, bringing the salt of his mouth and the diesel under his nails—and you held him like someone fastening a fragile module to a larger frame. You both acted with the tools you had: he with wrenches and brigades, you with covenants and midnight pleadings. That doesn't erase the ache.

You: "Did we do the right thing?"

You stand on the Tidewatch Lighthouse in a winter that feels like a verdict. The tide pulls away more than water; it pulls away storefronts, alleys, a barstool where someone told a joke you loved. The town endures—its bones held together with tape and argument and stubborn networks of neighbors. But there is a hollowed-out quiet now, a taste like metal behind your teeth.

Livia Chen, in a council address that is broadcast and measured, speaks of stability and jobs and a future that will be safer for most. She does not say the word 'compromise' in public. She does not need to. The cameras and the investors do the translating.

Livia Chen [public, polished]: "We chose the option that minimizes risk. We will continue to support community-driven pilots where feasible."

You watch her on an old tablet in the lighthouse lens room. Her face is made of careful light and shadow. You feel, at a physical level, both relief at fewer immediate losses and a deep, corroding sorrow for the small things that will not be returned.
