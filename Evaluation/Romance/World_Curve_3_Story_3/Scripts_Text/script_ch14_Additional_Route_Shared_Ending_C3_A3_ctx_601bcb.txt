[Scene: Loading Bay, Marisora Outskirts | Dawn]

You breathe in and the morning tastes of diesel and tomato leaves — a strange, grounding mix. The seed-bank crates sit open on the back of the truck, burlap and hand-lettered labels, the smell of peat and pepper seedlings curling up into the cold air. Your fingers know the weight of each packet: decades of saved varieties, the slow, stubborn history of people who refused to let the sea erase the whole of them.

You run your thumb along the brass rim of the tide-watch. It is warmer where your skin presses; the second hand is a patient small thing, marking steps you did not imagine you'd have to take. Leaving had a political logic when you argued it at the meeting. Now it is a choreography of hands and vehicles and promises that must be kept until they are true.

Elias crouches by a crate, his hair flecked with soil and salt, jaw working as he ties rope. The light catches the sawdust on his sleeves like a constellation.

Maya Reyes: "There — label facing out. Arlo, keep the dried-mango packs with the medicinal seeds. Rita, the saplings go last; they're the most delicate in transit."

Elias Jun [breathing out, looking up]: "You always make it sound like a military operation." (He smiles, but it doesn't touch his eyes yet.) "Good. Whoever planned this didn't expect the kids to get sentimental about compost."

Maya Reyes [half a laugh]: "Sentimental about compost is exactly the profile we need for resilience."

Elias Jun: "Right. Hard to mobilize people without a little biodegradability." (He stands, shoulder brushing yours by accident — a small, ordinary thing that steadies you both.)

Rita [from the truck, cheerfully efficient]: "We've got the manifests. Truck one goes upriver to the LaGuardia docks. Truck two is for the rooftop kits — pots, soil mix, the telescopes — because you insisted telescopes are non-negotiable."

Maya Reyes (there in the sound of Rita's voice is the town's continuity — a practical warmth): "Thank you. Make sure the cold boxes are checked again. Nothing damp at all."

Rita: "Checked twice. And Maya?" (Her tone softens.) "You look like you're carrying the harbor on your back. Let someone else carry a corner, yeah?"

You pause, the sentence settling like a pebble in your chest. The ledger of responsibilities you kept to yourself is not, in practice, a private object. It is rostered across the shoulders of everyone here, and the weight shifts when you let it.

Maya Reyes: "I'll carry the tide-watch."

Rita [half grin]: "As long as you don't try to navigate by it. We have a map for that."

Elias Jun: "That sounds like a promise and a plan, which is better than either alone." (He taps the crate as if it were a heart.) "Write to me when the tide-line changes."

Keep Rita's number close — she'll be the one making sure everyone's on the manifest."

The vehicles roll, cargo shifting with the small, impatient sounds of life moving. You climb into the passenger seat of the lead truck, and for a long moment the town dissolves into frames in the window: the bakery's board empty now, the breakwater a ragged stitch, the harbor's shape in your mind like a carved bowl.

[Scene: Moving Highway, Salt Flats and Reedbeds | Midday]
[Visual: Wheels cutting the salt-swept road; horizon low and wide; mirrored pools]
[Sound: Tires on wet asphalt, a radio murmuring municipal updates every so often]
[Music: Low cello underpinning, strings lifting; the rhythm of travel]

You sleep in half-hour stitches, the kind of micro-sleep that comes in the hollow of exhaustion and adrenaline. When you wake, Elias is sketching plans on a scrap of waterproof paper — a modular aquaculture barge, simplified, a sketchalike promise. He makes little notes in the margins, hands moving with the authority of someone who builds with their palms.

Elias Jun: "If we anchor the grow-frames with cross-tension, the upriver current will bring nutrients and pull away excess bloom. Less maintenance. More yield."

Maya Reyes: (you read the lines; your scientist's ribs rearrange to match his builder's intuition) "We can model the nutrient flux, but we'll need a sensor array to monitor salinity daily. I can set up a simple loop — cheap sondes that ping to a hotspot."

Elias Jun: "You talk expensive-sounding, and I'm into it."

Maya Reyes: "It's not expensive if we prioritize — and if we tag it as community infrastructure, we can tap the regional grant Ayla mentioned."

Elias Jun [a pause; then, quietly]: "Ayla helped pick the town, didn't she?"

Maya Reyes: "She did. She negotiated the slots, smoothed the permits. We argued about timelines, but she got us the corridor."

Elias Jun [a little friction in his voice]: "She still makes it sound like an equation."

Maya Reyes: "She is an equation. You and I are the variables that make other people care about the numbers."

He looks at you, and there is a softness there, a gratitude braided with grief: gratitude for the plan, grief for what is left behind.

Maya Reyes: "Thank you. We'll need power runs for the sensor array and space for the seed bank. Can you tell me about local fishing communities? Their seasons?"

Municipal Liaison: "They're mixed. Some run small riverside nets; others are experimenting with oyster lines. You're not alone here."

Maya Reyes (a swallow of relief): "Then we can trade techniques — mangrove slips for oyster-line know-how."

Elias Jun unloads a crate and the movement of his shoulders after a day on the road is the most beautiful practical thing you see. He sets the crate down and opens it with a care that is almost ritual. Inside: jars of seeds, labeled in Rita's hand, a small knitted bag of your grandmother's scarves, a battered tide-watch in its pocket.

Elias Jun [whispers]: "This is home-work, I can tell."

Maya Reyes: "It's our work. It'll keep us up some nights. It'll keep us steady on others."

Elias Jun [leaning against the table]: "You were right about the sonde array. The salinity reads are steady, and the oysters already like the lines we put in. Looks like we have a viable cycle."

Maya Reyes: "Good. Once the grant clears we can automate the sensors and start cross-checking yields with nutrient loads. If we open the data publicly, other towns upriver can adopt the same model."

Elias Jun: "You always want the world to be a little more generous and a little less mysterious by the numbers."

The months fold into each other: satellite calls with Ayla arranging permits and monitoring corridors; Rita organizing barter markets so Marisora craftspeople could sell their wares in the new town; Arlo taking a management course and then falling in love with a local oyster farmer. You send boxes back upriver — not just seeds, but memory-objects: a festival banner, a battered sign from the bakery, jars of preserved lemons.

Elias Jun: "I picture that too. I picture us old and grayer and still fixing something every week."

Maya Reyes [a laugh, then quieter]: "I sometimes picture sitting on the same rooftop, a hundred small roots tangled in the pots between us."

Elias Jun: "Promise me something stupid and kind."

Maya Reyes: "What?"

Elias Jun [smiling]: "That we'll keep sending ridiculous postcards to Marisora. That we'll keep the festival banner in the truck and never throw it out. Promise me that when the tide-watch finally stops ticking in a hundred years, someone will use it to anchor a childhood fort."

Maya Reyes (you press the tide-watch to your palm on the table): "I promise. We'll keep the artifacts and the seeds and the songs. We'll build in the new place until it's full of the same stubborn love."

There is a fullness in your chest that is not absence but an accumulation of purpose. Marisora will always be a bruise and a balm in the same place; leaving did not sever it. Instead, you learned to carry it more flexibly, to share its pieces where they could grow again.

Maya Reyes (it takes you half a second to answer, the honest one): "Sometimes, for a moment. But then I see this — the seedlings, the kids learning to tie oyster-lines, Elias' ridiculous sketches becoming barges that actually float — and regret becomes a tool rather than a chain."

Maya Reyes: "To making roots."

You set the tide-watch on the crate between you and Elias Jun and, for once, you both let your guard down. You are tired in a way that means you have done enough for tonight. The future waits with a busy, quiet patience.

You tuck a small mangrove slip into Elias Jun's coat pocket without ceremony. He slips it back to you later for safekeeping. The gesture is practical, affectionate, the way hands teach love.
