# Route: Additional Route (Ending C3 / B2 — Sad)


[Scene: Town Hall | Early Evening]
[Visual: Long shot of the dais; rows of mismatched chairs half-filled with worried faces]
[Music: Low, tense strings; a distant metronome of a clock]

You return to the Town Hall with the smell of hot coffee and lemon cleaner clinging to the air. The fluorescent lights buzz like indecision. The choice you made at the threshold—open resistance, legal pressure, a blockade—sits in your chest like a stone. You promised yourself action; now the legal forms, the volunteer lists, and the names of witnesses press against your palms.

Rina stands under the bulletin board, sleeves rolled, a laptop balanced on her knees. Her face is taut but ready—efficient, precise, and braced like a person before a long haul. Tomas leans on his cane nearby, the lines at the corners of his eyes carved deeper by worry. Nia is moving through the rows, handing out clipboards like paper talismans. You hear murmurs that have the texture of a town breaking up into camps.

Rina: "We filed for the preliminary injunction this afternoon. It'll be in the county docket by morning, but—" (she taps the screen, eyes sharp) "—the judge needs cause, and the developer's counsel has already prepared a pile of affidavits. They'll call us obstructionists. They will call you radical."

You feel the air go thin. The word radical hangs there, more accusation than description.

You: "We knew they'd try to frame it that way. Evidence is evidence—shoreline surveys, eelgrass transects, Tomas’s maps of historical high-water lines. We have standing."

Rina: "Standing—yes. But standing alone won't buy time if the graders are already cutting into the headland tomorrow at first light." (She exhales, fingers worrying the laptop edge.) "We need eyewitnesses, documented disturbance, people physically present when they try to start."

Tomas: "I can bring in old logs, tide markers from my grandfather's day. They'll remember the sound of the marsh before the dredgers came. That memory has weight." (He taps the cane twice for emphasis.) "And they know me. They know when I'm telling truth."

Nia arrives, cheeks flushed from the cold, a thick stack of signed sheets in her hand like a small treasure. She presses them toward you; the signatures are uneven, earnest: fisherfolk, teachers, shopkeepers.

Nia: "We hit every stall on the wharf. People are scared, but they signed. They want the town, not a sea wall that steals the flats." (She looks at you, fierce.) "We need more of that. We need bodies on the flats."

A low hum from beyond the doors feels like the tide preparing to turn.

You: (thinking) The legal scaffolding is only as strong as the people behind it. An injunction buys time, but time will mean nothing if they push heavy equipment into the mud and make a fait accompli. We have to both file and stand between the machines and the marsh.

[Scene: Town Square | Dusk]
[Visual: The square glows under strings of battered lights; posters rustle in the wind]
[Sound: The distant slap of a generator; gulls calling like distant alarm]
[Music: Low drums with a single piano note repeating]

The square feels smaller now, crowded with bodies and purpose. You walk among them, passing clipboards and legal updates, smelling salt, fryer oil, and the faint, sweet rot of seaweed. Someone presses a mobile into your hand with a message: a grader was seen at Azure Crescent an hour ago. The headland is being surveyed ahead of schedule.

You find Eli by the fish stall, fingers stained with varnish. He looks like he hasn't slept—shadowed, careful around words. The sight of him detaches a hollow-lucky thing in your ribs. You chose resistance; he chose his hands and the tradition that keeps people afloat. Neither of you can pretend the other hasn't chosen differently.

Eli: "They started measuring early. Security vans. Cass had a crew there by ten." (His voice is low; the amber in his eyes is all heat and fatigue.) "My uncle's asking if we'll be the ones to chase them off. He says he saw it coming. He says—" (He stops, the rest unsaid.)

You: "Then we show up. We document. We stand. We don't let them pretend the marsh signed off." (You try for steady.) "We filed the injunction, Eli. Rina's on it. The court might—"

Eli: (cutting in) "Courts move slow, Mara. Have you seen my uncle’s ledger? He needs nets mended, boats paid. The job Cass is promising—people'd eat for months on that." (He pats your arm, a practical, guilty touch.) "I'm not saying we roll over. I'm saying the town will not forgive if people go hungry because we were right on paper."

You feel your throat tighten. The fight you chose is legal and physical; it will make mouths around tables mention you with gratitude or with curses.

You: "If we don't stop them now, there won't be marsh left for future nets. If we stop them, there's a chance—" (You search for a phrase that isn't a cliché.) "—a chance to force a conversation about jobs that don't destroy the flats."

Eli: (after a long pause) "You ask for bodies. You're going to get mine." (He tests the words like a piece of driftwood.) "But know this—I will not let my family's livelihood break because the only plan in town is to replace the flats with condos."

You both stand in the square, a compromise forming between stubbornness and necessity. His expression afterward is unreadable—enough to be both worry and solidarity, and that uncertainty feels heavier than a clear refusal.

[Scene: Headland / Azure Crescent | Night]
[Visual: Heavy machinery silhouettes against a half-moon; corporate floodlights stain the sky
]
[Sound: The metallic groan of winches, radios chattering, the occasional barked instruction]
[Music: Sparse, dissonant cello tones]

You climb the ridge with a cluster of volunteers—flashlights bobbing, breath fogging. The headland smells of cold earth and diesel. In the beam of a spotlight, stakes and flags mark property lines; the ground is raw where surveyors have driven metal bars into the stubborn soil.

Cass Rey stands with arms folded, coat immaculate against the raw wind, a small satellite rig blinking at her shoulder. She looks like a map come to life—sharp lines, certain edges. Behind her, a few security contractors drift like black birds. A camera crew from a local outlet angles for a soundbite.

Cassandra Rey: (measured smile) "Good evening. I appreciate the passion, but the town needs jobs. Azure Crescent is a responsible development, designed to protect the harbor with a sea wall and to provide employment. Opposition like this—" (she gestures, politeness folding into steel) "—risks people’s livelihoods."

You: "Your surveys are rushed, Cassandra. You cleared lines before working with those who know the marsh's breath. You call it protection; many of us call it theft."

Cassandra Rey: (smiles, close to predatory) "Names are loaded things, Mara. Protection is what I offer. We've engaged experts. This is progress."

Tomas steps forward, cane tapping the earth like punctuation.

Tomas: "Your 'experts' do not know the old tide. They do not know the smell when the eelgrass first come back—how the harbor used to sing. Progress without memory is a highway to nowhere." (He spits the last word like a small, ancient salt curse.)

Cass's smile tightens.

Cassandra Rey: "Respect for place is not incompatible with development. We can create both." (She looks past the crowd at you, assessing.) "You could have sat with us and helped shape this. Instead you chose confrontation."

The reporters angle questions—words flung like pebbles at the line of volunteers. A security man moves closer to your group, asking for identification. Someone records it on their phone; Rina sets up a live feed.

Rina: "We are documenting encroachment. If they attempt to grade in, this will be evidence." (Her fingers move, always moving.) "We will keep the cameras rolling."

You: (internal) The cameras and the legal filings are scaffolding, but the physical line in the mud is where it matters. If they push machines into living sediment, the marsh's structure will be redefined in an instant. A judge's order can undo machines on paper, but not drowned eelgrass.

The tension coils higher. Voices rise; a contractor barks that they're within their legal easement. A volunteer shouts that the land is already a habitat. The contractor pushes a sleeve, something mechanical in his manner suggesting orders and indemnity.

You step between the contractor and a young volunteer, palms raised to stop a shove that never comes.

Eli arrives at your shoulder, breathless from sprinting the paths. He looks at the machines, then at Cass, then at you. For a moment, the world narrows to a flicker of a tide buoy somewhere beyond the headland.

Eli: "I'm not letting them start." (He says it more to you than anyone, a line of fire and protection.)

Cassandra Rey: (loud enough for the camera) "We don't want violence. We want a lawful permitting process. But we won't be held hostage to a group that refuses compromise." (She lets the word "hostage" hang like accusation; the cameras sharpen.)

A man in a suit starts reading a notice—an affidavit from the developer declaring that any delay will cost jobs. The crowd responds with a low rumble. The first heavy vehicles idle, engines ticking like mechanical beasts.

Nia: (to you, small and fierce) "If they start grading, it's all over. We can't let that be the story."

You feel the weight of the evening like a physical load. The injunction may arrive in the morning. The presence of machines tonight proves intent. Each side is building evidence for or against the other. Each side is a story told in the mud.

[Scene: Dock / Aster | Late Night]
[Visual: The skiff bobs quietly, LED lamps warm against the salt-dark water]
[Sound: Water lapping against hull, a gull's faint call, the distant mechanical ticking from the headland]
[Music: A single low piano note, held]

Later, on the dock, you and Eli sit on the Aster's gunwales, knees nearly touching, the town's lights a scattered constellational smear. Your fingers trail the cold rope and taste diesel on your tongue. The exhaustion in your bones is wide and honest.

Eli: "I saw Cass on the ridge. She played the cameras like a violin." (He lets out a small laugh that is mostly tension.) "She calls this 'protective infrastructure.' My uncle calls it theft. I don't want to lose him to a jobless winter."

You: "I don't want to lose the marsh. If they flatten the flats, the net returns drop. It's not just sentiment—it's fisheries biology. The flats are nursery habitat. We lose them, the whole system shifts."

Eli: (listening, then) "Promise me you won't drag the town into something that leaves us without a roof. Promise me you won't gamble people's bills on a judge who might not care about eelgrass." (There is fear in the ask, practical and raw.)

You feel the plea under the words: fear that your conscience could become other people's hunger.

You: (thinking) This is how hard a moral landscape gets—when science and survival sit on opposite sides of the same table. You can see the marsh in equations, but you can't eat equations. You can file injunctions and livestream testimony, but the boats that bring dinner home require nets mended and mouths fed now.

Eli reaches for your hand, then doesn't. His wrist rests against yours in a quiet declaration that tries to be both warm and restrained.

Eli: "Whatever happens, I'm here tonight. Tomorrow—" (He doesn't finish; his incompletion is a map of possible futures.)

You: "Tomorrow we go to court. Tomorrow we stand on the flats if we need to. Tonight we rest our bodies, keep the feeds live, and hope the judge sees what we already know." (You try to make the hope sound like a plan.)

A broadcast chirps from Rina's phone: the county clerk's office received the injunction paperwork—marked for review first thing. The small message is a brittle shard of comfort. You let yourself hold it for a moment.

[Music: A tense chord slides under the scene, unresolved]

You think of the marsh as a thing with lungs—slow, tidal breaths measured in seedlings and sediment. Tonight, it feels like a patient holding its breath while strangers consider surgery.

You look at Eli and the town around you and hear the night—distant machinery, gulls, the hush of water over wood—and you understand that the fight will not be neat. Winning may cost. Losing will cost more.

Page-turn thought: If the court does not move, the graders will. If the graders move, the marsh will not forgive. If the marsh is lost, what stories will the town tell its children?
