# Route: Route A (True Ending)
# Context: 4001e7

[Scene: Boathouse | Early Morning]
[Visual: Warm dappled light through salt-smeared panes. Seedlings bathed in gold. A map tube leans against a workbench. Volunteers move quietly in the background, folding lists and sorting plant trays.]
[Sound: Low hum of drip-irrigation and a radio segment speaking in a friendly local voice; gulls in the distance]
[Music: Gentle, rising strings — a hopeful undertone]

The radio on the counter is still playing the feel-good piece from the morning — a bright, practiced story about partnerships and progress. You can hear Elena's measured laugh on the segment, and Maren Voss's voice offering assurance like polished glass. The town sounds small and like it could fit inside the boathouse: the clatter of seed trays, Mika's light footsteps, a volunteer whispering a poem of encouragement.

You run your thumb along the brass clip of your notebook. The metal is cool and familiar; it anchors you. For a moment the victory from yesterday is warm in your chest — the council's applause, the way people looked at the marsh as if it mattered. That warmth is not gone. It's a steady ember under everything else.

[Visual: Close on your hands — one holding the notebook, the other brushing soil off a seed packet.]

Then your tablet pings: a single, small sound that slices the glow. The subject line is blank. The sender is anonymous. The file attachment is labeled simply: "ATL_INTERNAL_MEMOS_2028_v2.pdf."

[Sound: Soft electronic ping; a volunteer mutters something under their breath]
[Music: A quiet, curious chord — not hostile, just expectant]

You open it because you do what you always do: you read the data. Your fingers feel clumsy for a second, as if they don't belong to someone who is supposed to hold other people's faith.

[Visual: Screen light on your face; amber-green eyes catching the glare]
The memos are clinical and cold in the way only internal documents can be — numbers, timelines, phrases with weight: "downplay residual habitat impacts," "compress mitigation timelines," "prioritize procurement flexibility." There are footnotes about predicted marsh loss framed as "acceptable trade-offs" and a timeline that runs like a quiet clock: mitigation begins late, monitoring windows shortened, contingency actions outsourced.

You taste salt and metal on the back of your tongue. You feel the room tilt — not into despair, but into that particular sharp clarity you get when a possibility becomes a duty.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Scan the memos for the source headers"
> Choice B: "Call Dr. Kaito quietly"
[Outcome A: You run the headers and metadata, looking for anonymous markers. Your hands steady as you annotate the dates and internal codes in the notebook's margins.]
[Outcome B: You tap his number with a thumb that trembles. The call goes to voicemail; a soft, familiar voice asks you to leave a message, and you don't.]

[Scene: Boathouse Interior — Center Table | Early Morning]
[Visual: Lucas across the table, AR goggles folded above his brow; the brass lighthouse key in his palm as if balancing a thought. Mika stands at the doorway, volunteers paused on the threshold like a choir inhaling.]
[Sound: The radio fades into the background, replaced by the sharper sounds of breath and paper; the hum of the town presses at the glass]

Lucas Herrera: "What is it?"

He leans forward; his voice is measured but there is an edge. He watches your face more than the screen.

You close the space between you with a kind of professional attention — the same way he studies a plan, looking for misalignments. You see his jaw work; his fingers press the lighthouse key until the brass fleck darkens. For a second his blue-gray eyes are distant, like a horizon under cloud.

You could accuse him here. You could flip the tablet toward him and let the file speak for itself, let the room fracture. You want truth. You also know how fragile a bridge can be when someone discovers the hidden rot.

You keep your voice even.

Amaya Saito: "These came in an anonymous drop. Internal memos — impact evaluations and mitigation schedules. They contradict several parts of the draft we just applauded."

Lucas Herrera: (He inhales slowly.) "Anonymous drops get messy. We shouldn't jump. Do you know the source? Is it verified?"

You: "Not yet. But the language is specific. The timelines are… shortened. They call some losses 'acceptable.'"

Lucas Herrera: (He raises a hand, almost apologetic.) "I — I didn't see these. If Atlantech edited anything without telling us, I would have expected to be informed. I was part of the negotiation team for the engineering specs, Amaya." He avoids saying "I promise." He presses the key into his palm as if it steadies him. "If there's anything wrong, I want to know."

You watch him for signs: shadow in the eyes, tremor in the hands. His denial arrives with a professional cadence — plausible, practiced. But the community is already half-sanguine with victory and half-on-edge with suspicion. Every plausible answer is a thin reed.

Mika: "If this is true, we need to act fast. We can't be softened into surrender by nice words."

She folds her arms; there's a fierce optimism in her face — a readiness to roll up sleeves and plant roots.

Lucas Herrera: "I don't want us to break the agreement before we've had a chance to fix it. Public panic gives Maren Voss the leverage to bulldoze through clauses and call it 'risk management.'"

He gives a half-smile that doesn't reach his eyes. "We negotiated transparency; we can demand the transparency we were promised."

Amaya Saito: (You feel the ember of yesterday burn brighter.) "Demanding transparency is different from letting a known manipulation slide. We need to choose how to pressure without giving up what we've won."

[Music: Strings swell gently, hopeful — the sense that action, not paralysis, is the next step]

Lucas Herrera's denial isn't a slamming door. It's a complex thing between you — equal parts defense and confusion, like a cracked shell trying to hold a living thing. You want to believe him. Believing him keeps the negotiation alive; exposing him publicly could snap everything.

At the same time, you know the memos are leverage. They are a hinge. What you choose to do now could force accountability or force the town into concessions that won't hold.

Dr. Kaito: "Give me a look," he says. "If the data's there, it will stand up to scrutiny. The town deserves that."

Dr. Kaito reaches for the tablet with that same careful touch he once used to lift fragile specimens — reverent, methodical. The old scientist's presence steadies the room. It's a reminder that facts can be forged into protection if they're shaped correctly.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Ask Dr. Kaito to run a rapid validation on-site"
> Choice B: "Demand Lucas explain publicly at the council briefing tonight"
[Outcome A: You nod and hand over the tablet. Dr. Kaito's fingers move like slow instruments; he begins to cross-check dates and citations. The volunteers lean in, drawn by the prospect of verifiable truth.]
[Outcome B: You picture the council chamber: Elena's scarf, the reporters, the way Maren Voss's smile sets a tone. You feel the thrill of accountability — but also the weight of what would be lost if the deal collapses.]

[Scene: Boathouse | Peripheral — Volunteers and Quiet Engines | Early Morning]
[Visual: Volunteers gather around windows, their faces soft with worry and resolve; seed trays glint in the morning light]
[Sound: Soft murmur of plans forming; someone hums an old work-song; the radio host brushes the topic in a lighter tone, unaware of the file on your screen]
[Music: Low, insistent rhythm — like boots finding a path]

The community in the room holds its breath as if it could buoy the truth. You can feel the town's hope as a physical presence: a weight on the benches, a hum in the air, a patchwork of intentions knitted into a fragile quilt. There is betrayal in the memos, yes, but there is also the possibility they will become the lever the town needs to pry open Atlantech's closed doors.

You think of your father's notes in the brass-bound journal — of small adjustments that prevented collapse; of patience. You think of the volunteers' hands, of Dr. Kaito's patience, of Elena's political strain, of Lucas Herrera's key, warm in his palm.

This is a hinge. Not between ruin and rescue — between a superficial, possibly doomed fix and a reparation that could set a precedent: demanding real transparency, binding mitigation on an enforceable schedule, insisting on third-party oversight.

Your eyes sting; not purely from salt or the sea fog on your lashes, but from the strain of choosing a path where truth and care must both be preserved.

[Visual: The sunlight shifts; the seedlings throw long, resolute shadows across the table.]

You outline the options in your head, each one a different way to press for accountability while trying to keep the town's fragile victory intact. None of them is clean. All of them are possible. The choice is the moment the town moves from a hopeful plan to an enforceable future.

[Music: A single, uplifting chord — the sound of a plan forming]

You look from the memos to Lucas Herrera to the faces gathered here. The tide of events is not a force you must only resist; it is something you can learn to steer. The memos are not just an exposure; they are an opportunity to insist that protection means honesty, and that protection without truth is no protection at all.

[Scene: Boathouse — Close on your face | Early Morning]
[Visual: Amber-green eyes, steady; a breath drawn that tastes like salt and new paper]
[Sound: The radio cadence softens; outside, a gull cries once, emphatic]

You take a breath. This is the hinge. You have the memos. You have allies. You have, beneath the brittle disbelief, a civic muscle that has practiced resilience.

You do not decide yet.
