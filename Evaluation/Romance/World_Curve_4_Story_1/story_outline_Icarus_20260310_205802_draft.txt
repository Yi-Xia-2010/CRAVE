## Visual Novel Title
Tideline Promise

---

## World View
Solán Bay is a harbor neighborhood in 2035, a near-future coastal city where rising seas and sharper storms reshape daily life. The public face is paradoxical: resilient grassroots projects—community gardens, sand-sculpted berms, improvised flood gates—stand shoulder-to-shoulder with construction cranes and glossy campaign posters promising rapid redevelopment. Municipal offices hum with tempered optimism and budgetary dread; the mayor’s meetings alternate between measured planning and the pressure of investors. Social strata are less about wealth than proximity to the water: lifelong harbor families, volunteer organizers, municipal staff, and outside developers jockey for the shoreline’s future.

Culture here is tactile and oral: elders like Rosa Marin pass stories and Indigenous restoration knowledge; volunteer crews swap practical lore under salt-streaked skies. Religion and ritual are modest—sunrise vigils at the Lantern Lighthouse, seed-blessing ceremonies in rooftop beds—but civic belief runs deep, embodied in shared labor. Geography folds from the cracked Harbor Promenade and the storm-battered Stormfront Beach to the humid warmth of Rosa’s Greenhouse Conservatory and the sterile Municipal Planning Office. The atmosphere is painterly realism—sea-glass greens, storm grays, rusted copper—with cinematic lighting that makes every puddle and patched tarp feel like a moral decision. At personal scale, conflicts center on choices between pragmatic short-term fixes and patient, communal restoration—the decision that will test love, leadership, and what I’m willing to sacrifice.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mara Voss) lead a campaign for community-led coastal restoration while my relationship with Tomas Herrera deepens. Early choices—alliances, tactics, and compromises—propel us quickly to apparent victory: pilot projects win funding, public opinion shifts, and political doors open. This rapid ascent culminates in a high-profile win that promises to secure living shorelines, protect neighborhoods, and fuse municipal resources with grassroots stewardship. But the Icarus curve turns: a catastrophic storm and a cascade of political maneuvers expose fragile compromises, unraveling the victory and forcing wrenching consequences. Across branching routes, the nature of our rise varies—technical success versus rhetorical triumph, rooted restoration versus symbolic concessions—and so does the crash’s shape. Some paths end in guarded hope and heavy costs; others culminate in loss and personal estrangement; one route reframes the fall as a catalyst for systemic movement-building. The romance between Tomas and me stays central, tested by urgency, trust, and the choice between staying beside each other or following divergent calls to duty.
