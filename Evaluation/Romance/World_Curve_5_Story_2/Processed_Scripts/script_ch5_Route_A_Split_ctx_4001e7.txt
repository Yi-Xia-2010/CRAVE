You made a choice and the world pivoted. Whether you stood at the window or stared at the message in the lamplight, the letter's tenor didn't change: the firm would fund an immediate scale-up of the pilot, provide expedited permitting, and — crucially — require a seat on the governing board and a clause for coordinated public messaging. A "strategic partnership," the letter called it, in a voice that promised stability and deadlines.

You feel the words like weight pressing against your sternum. "Expedited permits" could cut through bureaucratic dead ends—good. The board seat could mean influence—good. But the other phrases hang like a cold shadow: "coordinated public messaging," "non-disparagement," "project pacing to align with firm objectives."

You put the phone down and the room folded tighter. Jonah, who had stayed, reaches for a mug and lifts his eyes to you. His grin is small and careful; he has a way of making you feel seen and also dangerously hopeful.

And—" you pick the letter at the right place "—a clause about coordinated messaging and operational timelines that—and I quote—'ensure project continuity aligned with stakeholder deliverables.'"

Jonah Reyes: (letting the words land) "That's…big. Faster than we could ask for on our own." (He exhales slowly.) "And it solves the funding cliff."

Elena "Lena" Maris: "It also asks for control. Not control outright—two words that mean a lot: oversight and alignment."

Jonah leans forward. He runs a thumb along the rim of his mug as if tracing a map that only he can see.

Jonah Reyes: "Are they offering anything we can't get from grants and the council, in time? I mean—expedited permits could buy us seasons. The living shorelines need that momentum."

Elena "Lena" Maris: (thinking) Momentum. You can feel its pull. You think of Rosie’s volunteers, the seedlings Dr. Asha germinated in the office window, the galvanizing proof-in-the-month that will bring neighbors off the sidelines. You also think of the smell of cedar in the Abandoned Dunehouse and the hollow of houses already emptied of memories.

Elena "Lena" Maris: "It could buy us seasons. Or it could buy us a timeline someone else sets."

Jonah Reyes: "What if the board seat keeps us honest? We'd be in the room." (He meets your eyes hard.) "You'd be in the room."

You want to believe that being in the room is the same as keeping the room honest. Your chest tightens with the memory of your mother’s house—how decisions made behind polished doors had arrived like storms on your street. You did not want that for Brinehaven.

Marco's voice from the doorway cuts across the smaller acoustic of your doubt—he's been hovering, cap in hand, the new scab on his knuckle caught in the twilight.

Marco: "So? A fat check and a shiny name on the plaque. How long before they decide what counts as 'community'?"

Marco steps in, breath warm with the town's salt and oil. He doesn't hide how money looks to him: possibility and trap in the same breath.

Elena "Lena" Maris: "I don't know." (A useless phrase. You know, and you also don't. You feel both precisely at once.)

Rosie Alvarez: (hardening) "Reporting isn't the problem. The problem is strings that let them dictate what 'progress' looks like. If that becomes their metric, our work gets reworded into marketing."

You listen to the room leaning against each other. The music outside your chest has shifted—no longer an upward nod but a low, insistent minor that tugs at the edges of your optimism.

The sentence lands and makes the room colder. You can feel it: the falling mood, the shape of stakes. The grant is a ladder with a missing rung; take it and you climb faster but on someone else's supports, refuse it and you make your own footing slower and more brittle.

Your phone lights up again—an incoming call. The name on screen: Valence Urbanworks. The call could be Iris. A small, irrational part of you hopes it's a misdial.

Do you refuse and risk budgets and seasons slipping away? Or do you negotiate for watchful power and accept that stipulations will slow what you can build?
