### SCRIPT
# Route: Ending
# Context: e67f19

[Scene: Rooftop Community Garden | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of raised beds slick with overnight dew; string lights looped between panels, a printed header—"Marisol Mosaic — Charter (Draft)"—still pinned to the notice board]
[Music: Soft piano with a slow, ascending motif]
[Sound: Faint gulls; the distant clack of a dock gate; gentle breeze through windbreak panels]

You sit on the edge of a crate, the morning air sharp with salt and wet earth. Your palms remember the nail's cold bite where you pinned the Charter; the ink has dried into the tiny ridges of the paper. Around you, volunteers fold tarps and tuck seedlings into crates. The rooftop is small, but the work makes it feel like a ship's deck: everyone aligned on one horizon.

Your notebook lies open on your knees—columns of percentages and deadlines, smudged sketches of pilot sites, and, tucked into a margin, a list of names: children, elders, fishers, builders. You trace the list with a fingertip and feel momentum under your finger like a current. The ache you carry from other storms is present, but today it is movement rather than weight.

Dr. Kenji lifts a thermos and offers you a paper cup. He smells of field soil and old paper; his eyes crinkle when he smiles, offering the same steadiness he brings to graphs.

Dr. Kenji: "The oyster larvae are healthy. Settlement rates are promising in the pilot tanks."

Amina Reyes: "Good. They need to survive the first flush. If those cages establish, the filters buy us time—biological leverage rather than concrete."

Dr. Kenji [nodding]: "Exactly. Measurable outcomes could be turbidity, biomass, and settlement percentage. Six months gives us signal-to-noise."

You close your notebook and feel each number as a face: a child's laugh at school, Niko's hands stained with tar, Marina's hopeful, tired eyes. Data will heal and also decide. The rooftop smells like wet wood and coffee and the faint oil-slick tang from the town below.

[Visual: Cut to montage — children carrying small PVC cages; elders unrolling weathered nets at the school; volunteers ferrying juvenile oysters]
[Music: Bright strings weave under the images]
[Sound: Children chattering; the slap of small boots on the boardwalk]

At the elementary school's tiny amphitheater, a cluster of children lean over a low trough. Marina stands beside them, hand steady on a clipboard, cheeks flushed with purpose.

Marina: "Alright—count with me. One… two… three."

You kneel so your face is level with a boy whose cheeks are still freckled from winter sun. He hands you a cage, the plastic cool and slightly tacky with algae.

You feel the weight of it—light, fragile, full of possibility. You could take the cage and place it in the shallow bed yourself, say a small blessing and feel the job done. Or you could hand it back and watch a dozen small hands steady the future.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Place the cage yourself"
> Choice B: "Let the children do it"
[Outcome A: "You set the cage gently into the planted grid, your fingers used to small, exact movements. Children gasp a little; the boy's chest puffs with pride at your quiet competence."]
[Outcome B: "You guide the child's hands instead—palms warm against yours. He takes charge, and his grin is as wide as the bay. Around you, other small faces go bright with ownership."]
[MERGE: The scene refocuses on Marina watching you with that familiar look—part teacher, part conspirator.]

Marina [softly]: "They'll remember this. Not just the oysters—this feeling. That we did it."

Amina Reyes: "They should. This is how knowledge spreads—hands first, then stories."

Elders nearby begin to speak in low, looping sentences—tales of boats that found new channels after storms, of a lone net mended with a promise. The wind carries a hundred years of accents. Their stories are not just nostalgia; they are scaffolding for policy, a mapping of cultural resilience.

[Visual: Close-up on a weathered palm, the elder's skin like barnacled wood]
[Sound: Elder's voice, low and clear against the gulls]

Elder: "We always worked with the water. Not against it. You learn the tide like you learn a child's face."

You breathe in the salt and the faint sugar of frying dough from a nearby stall; the demonstration is warm and messy. Cameras from a regional outlet bob in the crowd, lenses catching the earnestness. For a moment you see the town reflected back at you: not as data points, but as people who will turn their hands to anything to keep the place they love.

[Scene: Rooftop Community Garden | Late Morning — Return to Planning]
[Visual: Volunteers clearing up the amphitheater, the rooftop again quiet but for a few lingering conversations]
[Music: Piano returns, a hopeful triad]
[Sound: Folding chairs, the rustle of paper, a distant traffic hum]

Elias Hart arrives briskly, his slate coat unbuttoned against the sea-salt breeze. He carries a tablet in one hand and a compact dossier in the other—professional, contained, carrying the gravity of regional bureaucracy.

Elias Hart: "Morning. Sorry—traffic. I have the preliminary briefing from the authority. They've reviewed the living-shore pilot concept. There's funding, conditional on demonstrable metrics within six months."

You feel the word "conditional" like a hinge clicking; possibilities open and also lock. Elias Hart sets the tablet down and turns it so the screen glosses with a chart—projected funding curves, milestone checkpoints, a timeline that compresses.

Elias Hart: "They can move funds within the quarter if we show turbidity reduction, habitat establishment, and community engagement numbers. It's not everything, but it gets us significant matched funds."

Niko Kaur, who has been leaning on a post with a bandana at their neck, flattens his hands on a crate and lets out a sound that is half laughter, half irritation.

Niko Kaur [sharp]: "Six months? To produce nature on a spreadsheet? You want me to make the marsh behave like a quarterly report?"

Elias Hart [calm]: "We're not asking the marsh to behave. We're asking the science to show progress in metrics funders care about. That unlocks larger phases—retrofitting, jobs."

Marina [worried]: "What if the milestones force us to cut corners? What if contractors come in because the clock says they should?"

You look at your notebook—percentages, deadlines, a column of 'risks' scrawled in the margin. The rooftop hums with a dozen vested alignments: Niko Kaur's craft, Marina's organizing ethic, Elias Hart's institutional leverage, Dr. Kenji's models. You are the seam.

Dr. Kenji folds his hands, sunlight catching the rim of his glasses.

Dr. Kenji: "From a scientific perspective, shorter, measurable pilot periods are not impossible. We can design metrics that reflect ecological process, not just cosmetic change. But the design must avoid perverse incentives—no planting for the camera."

Elias Hart [leaning forward]: "We can write the milestones to require both community participation and ecological thresholds—shared accountability."

Niko Kaur [bitter]: "Shared accountability doesn't pay for a crane when the storm season hits. My boatwrights can't wait for paperwork while their livelihoods float away."

The conversation loops—good faith attempts to translate values into checkboxes. It frays and mends as each person pushes and pulls.

You close the tablet gently, careful not to seem abrupt.

Amina Reyes: "Let me be blunt. We want funding that accelerates action without erasing agency. Any grant that asks us to show outcomes within six months must respect how the work actually happens: hands, boatyards, schools—not just contractors' reports."

Elias Hart [measured]: "Agreed. The regional authority is open to co-design. If we bring a strong monitoring plan and community-verified indicators, we can amend milestones to include craft-led deliverables."

You feel a small lightness at his words—an opening. The rooftop seems to lean in. The music lifts subtly, a sense of progress threading through the air.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Ask Elias to commit to community-verified indicators now"
> Choice B: "Push for a draft timeline before signatures"
[Outcome A: "You insist on naming at least three community-verified indicators—engagement hours, restored shell density, and workshops held. Elias's jaw tightens, then eases; he types notes into his tablet with an uncharacteristic quickness."]
[Outcome B: "You press for a detailed timeline before any signatures are put forward. Elias frowns—he's used to moving fast—but he nods. 'We can do an annex,' he says, already calculating contingencies."]
[MERGE: The group considers trade-offs; Marina and Niko speak up about risks and labor.]

Marina reaches across and taps a line in your notebook where you've scribbled 'education + jobs' in the same breath.

Marina: "The schools—if we get funding, we can guarantee curriculum and labor hours for students on the pilots. That turns money into steady work."

Niko Kaur [softening]: "Work that keeps people on boats and in yards—yes. I want our apprentices to be paid, not tokenized."

Elias Hart: "And the authority will be more likely to extend funds if the engagement is structurally verifiable. Community co-management is an asset, politically."

You sense the room tilting toward a decision that will be lived every day. Hope rises—it's a practical kind, a stubborn optimism spliced with realism.

You stand and feel the deck under your boots, feel the tide's metaphor in your chest. This is the hinge moment—not just for funding, but for the shape of the town's future. You can almost hear the marsh answering: patient, resistant, willing to be tended.

[Music: Swell into a warm, ascending chord]
[Visual: Pull back to wide shot of rooftop, the town spread like a map below; the marsh glints at the horizon]

You look at each person: Marina's hands inked with charcoal, Niko Kaur's calloused palms, Elias Hart's steady eyes, Kenji's quiet curiosity. You fold your hands over the notebook and make space for what comes next.

[Scene: Town Hall Meeting Room | Noon]
[Visual: Long table with maps unfurled, a half-eaten sandwich, printed milestones stacked in neat piles]
[Sound: Muffled traffic outside; a clock ticking in the corner]

The group reconvenes. Stakeholders from the town and a representative from the regional authority join via video. The air carries a new tension—a hopeful pressure as opposed to panic. You present the draft monitoring plan, the community indicators, and the blended option that would allow a small pilot tranche followed by parallel grassroots fundraising.

Elias Hart: "With these indicators, I'm confident the authority will accept a pilot tranche with co-managed milestones."

Niko Kaur: "And if they don't? If their lawyers shape our language into something that favors corporations?"

Marina: "We keep doing what we do. We keep the campaign visible. The grassroots won't vanish if we accept a small secure grant."

Dr. Kenji: "From a risk analysis, a dual-track hedges systemic failure. But it strains administrative capacity."

You close your eyes briefly, imagining the future—children planting oysters, boats patched together by hands that taught them, a town that keeps its stories and its jobs. The feeling is a tide lifting the rooftops.

You open your eyes and the decision sits before you like a door. The room leans forward, expectant and trusting.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Call for an immediate vote to accept the phased regional grant"
> Choice B: "Propose a contingency: accept only a small secure grant and continue independent campaigns"
[Outcome A: "You press for a vote now, arguing that secured funding keeps momentum steady. Some nod; others look at you with the weight of hope. Elias's expression is steady—encouraging."]
[Outcome B: "You propose the split path: a small grant now, parallel grassroots work. The room considers the added administrative load, and Marina exhales as if relieved at the word 'parallel.'"]
[MERGE: The final conversation tightens around trade-offs; papers are signed as tokens of seriousness.]

The moment is alive with possibility. Hope is not naïve here; it's procedural and demanding. Your palms are damp as you sign a few printouts, not yet a commitment, but a promise of seriousness.

You think of the marsh again—of the long work and of the small victories that make the long work bearable. The coalition has built momentum from living rooms to rooftops to the town hall. The choice now will decide how that momentum is steered.

You set your pen down. Voices settle into a hush, as if waiting for you to translate thought into action.
