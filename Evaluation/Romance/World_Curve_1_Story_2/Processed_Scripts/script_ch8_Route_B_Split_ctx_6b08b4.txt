[Background:"ch8_6b08b4_3"]

The site tastes of cement dust and engine oil; the sodium lights hanging on temporary towers have a sterile, clinical quality in the bright noon. Here the future looks like slabs and angles. The contrast is deliberate: where the Tidehouse smells of salt and ink, this place smells like things that will stand.

Liora Kade is here—precise and composed, the edge of her black bob catching sunlight like a blade. She stands beneath the crane's shadow, hands folded, the brooch at her lapel glinting in the hard light. She has the look of someone who rehearses empathy and then applies it with a deadline.

Liora Kade [Smiling, Even Voice]: "Mira. Samir. Thank you for coming. I wanted you to see the scale—we can protect the economic core and buy time for adaptation across the city. The models show this will reduce catastrophic exposure by forty percent."

Mira Solace [Measured]: "Fewer catastrophes is good. But we need to know who gets left behind. A wall that protects 'the core' while moving neighborhoods elsewhere is a victory for budgets and a loss for people."

Liora Kade [Tilts Head]: "That's why the relocation program runs concurrently—compensation, voucher systems, retraining funds. The firm has proposals."

You feel the familiar friction: her language is efficient, her compassion structured. You do not dismiss it—you note the mechanics that keep the compassion from swamping the scale of a plan.

Samir [Interjecting]: "The legal wording could bind relocation protections to specific thresholds. But it requires enforceable triggers. Otherwise, money becomes a carrot and not a right."

Liora Kade [Softly]: "Then make them triggers. I want the project to succeed without leaving people behind."

There is a moment—thin and electric—where the words align and you can almost see policy turning into practice. Hope feels like an alignment, measurable and plausible.

Etta arrives with a small posse of neighbors. She wears a crown of braided silver hair and a scarf rusted by years of wind. Up close, her face maps every storm the neighborhood survived. Her eyes scan the concrete skeleton with a mixture of sorrow and appraisal.

Etta: "You can build walls all you want," she says, to no one and yet to everyone, "but what about the fish that came when the old docks still held water? What about the songs we lost when the docks were paved over? If this is to be our future, we need the people who remember on the board that decides."

Noah, standing beside you, folds his arms and taps the rolled shop plan.

Noah: "Homes and shops are tied. If they move one without the other, the neighborhood dissolves. If the city's going to sign checks, I want jobs for the crew who know how to keep a boat from rotting."

The conversation coils into technicalities and values: triggers, funding windows, oversight, recall clauses, capacity money. Each proposal forms a different shape of justice.

You find yourself listening for leverage—not to wield power but to distribute it. The muzzled mechanical noises around you form a backdrop; the human voices overlay like a chorus finding a tune.
