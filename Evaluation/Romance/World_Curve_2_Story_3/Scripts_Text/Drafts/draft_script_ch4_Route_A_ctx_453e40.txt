### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Option A


[Scene: Harbor Pier | Early Evening]
[Visual: Wide shot of the pier, banners flapping; wet wood, salt glistening]
[Music: Low, restless strings; distant gulls]
[Sound: Drum of footsteps on planks, muffled chants swelling]

You stand where the pier narrows, the crowd pressing behind you like a slow-moving tide. Rain threads the air—fine, persistent—flattening hair and banner fabric, making the painted letters run at the edges. The compass in your pocket presses against your hip as if sensing the pull of what you've chosen.

Your voice is hoarse already from calling people into place, but you keep speaking. Instructions are small, practical things: "Anchor the banner here," "Keep the generator clear," "Two people on the scaffold with the reef modules." Each directive is a stitch toward something you cannot simply model on a map. People answer with a dozen small, patient noises—grunts, laughs, the way a hand slaps another shoulder in agreement. The pier smells of wet rope, diesel from a nearby skiff, and the iron-tinged tang of the sea.

Luca is easy to find: orange safety harness bright against the muted town colors, perched on the scaffolding as if the scaffolding were an island and he its lighthouse. He looks down at you, and for a moment nothing else exists but that look—amber eyes narrowed, fierce and warm. He flashes a grin that is all mischief and conviction.

Luca Moreno: "You're louder than I expected, Isla. That's a good thing." (He readjusts a strap.) "You ready to get your hands covered in reef goo?"

You laugh—it's thin, but real—and step up onto the scaffolding. The wood gives a complaint under your boots. Up close, the demonstration reef modules look clumsy and beautiful: bundles of reclaimed oyster shells, woven with mesh and seeded with native eelgrass plugs. People pass them hand-to-hand like offerings. Your fingers find the cold center of a module and grip.

You work with Luca. The tasks are physical; the intimacy of labor is more urgent than words. Salt dries in the crook of your arms as you fasten another loop, your palms and forearms registering the texture of rope, the sting of sea spray. He forgets to let go of something; his hand bumps yours. Neither of you say anything, but the small shrug between you is a private sentence.

Luca: "Good call—both of them," he says after a beat. "Your timing's precise. Don't vanish on me after this; I need someone to tell me I didn't dream it."

You: "I won't disappear." (Your voice is steadier than your pulse.) "But I won't pretend this won't hurt people either."

He looks at you, and the grin softens into something like worry. He has that tendency—wants to fix the world with braces and bolts and a smile. You appreciate it and fear it. The reef goes into the surf; the first modules sink with a collective, slurping release, and a cheer ripples through the crowd.

Around you, chants build and unwind—practical, urgent lines: "Our town, our tide!" "Save the flats, not corporate walls!" The chants are not poetic; they are instructions and insistence. A megaphone rasps as Ravi barks cadence. The crowd's energy is a physical pressure against you; it lifts and threatens to pull apart at once.

Then a voice cuts through, controlled and exact: Aria Chen's public address—amplified and elegant. Her arrival is announced by the metallic click of her council badge, by the rustle of a briefcase, by a small pocket of polished authority that never quite blends into the damp weather around her.

Aria Chen: "This demonstration is disruptive and ill-timed," she says, voice measured so every syllable lands like a coin on wood. "We have a process. We have timelines. This—" (She gestures at the reef modules and the people) "—is not the way to build consensus."

You step forward before you decide to. You sense the crowd lean into the space between you. People are watching both of you the way a tide watches two rocks.

You: "Consensus isn't built when decisions are already sold," you say. (You keep your tone even; irritation is a dangerous thing on a wet pier.) "We're showing an alternative that won't hollow out the flats to pay for short-term jobs."

Aria: (A half-smile that doesn't reach her eyes.) "Isla, you always make arguments into absolutes. There are jobs on the line. There are families—"

You: "There are also long-term losses, and we're not willing to hand the town's future to promises that wash away." (You pause, because the crowd exhales and you can feel the weight of the argument.)

Aria: "And you're willing to risk immediate livelihoods for theoretical long-term gains?"

The interruption is immediate. A woman from the fish market—hands stained with old bait—shoves into the conversation, voice raw.

Market Woman: "You want us to wait for some future that may never pay our bills? Some of us can't afford that patience, Miss Chen."

Aria: (Faltering, then finding a practiced cadence.) "I'm proposing a plan that brings investment. Jobs provide stability—"

Luca: "Stability bought at the cost of our shoreline isn't stability at all," he snaps, louder than you expect. (He looks at you, and there is apology in the set of his jaw, like a note passed across a crowded room.) "We can create jobs with the reef—maintenance, monitoring—real, local work."

The exchange spirals—voices overlap, fingers point, and the rain grows heavier, as if the sky is trying to wash the argument clean. The tension is the kind that makes your teeth hurt. You're trying to hold the center of a conversation that refuses to be contained.

Then—sudden, physical—the crowd stumbles into panic. An elder fisherman collapses near the rail: his knees give out and his head drops forward. He is not a name you can fetch from memory; he's one of the faces that have always been in the background of town life, a presence like a weathered wall. Panic is a short, sharp smell—metallic and wet.

Jonah Merrick is at his side immediately. Old hands find the man's shoulders and lift, voice low and full of old calm.

Jonah Merrick: "Easy, now—breathe, boy. Breathe." (He steadies the fisherman like he'd steady a boat in a chop.) "Someone fetch a blanket. Give him space."

You are halfway across the pier before your mind can name why your chest has tightened so sharply. Guilt blooms—a small, hot thing—because you feel, with awkward clarity, that your decision to force the issue has pressed heat onto the town's already-tender skin.

You kneel as Jonah instructs, and the crowd folds around you like a tide pulling back. The chants stutter. For the first time in hours, your hands are not making policy or reef modules but pressing into the ocean of someone else's crisis.

Ravi: (kneeling opposite you, breath rapid) "He said his chest—no, he's breathing—keep him warm." (His hands are steady even though his voice shakes.)

You: "Someone call the clinic. Now," you say. Your voice is businesslike because panic does not help charts or heartbeats. Your mind is logging details: pallor, sweating, the way the man's fingers curl. You are a scientist and healer by instinct and training, but you are also the instigator of the demonstration, and that fact presses at the back of your neck like a tide marking the line you crossed.

Jonah: (folding his jacket under the man's head) "This is why things get hard. People get afraid, and fear orders them to pick the nearest lifeline." (He looks at you, not unkind, but heavy with history.) "You meant to help, Isla. Meaning doesn't stop the hurt that follows."

You: "I know." (Your reply is a taut string.) "I know I can't make everyone choose what I think is right."

Jonah: "But remember—people judge by what stays." (He squeezes the fisherman's shoulder once, with the one hand that remembers rope.)

The confrontation with Aria resumes in fits and starts—arguments folded into care for the man who collapsed, into whispered consultations and the flash of someone dialing a phone. Aria's expression shifts; the polished veneer fractures into visible stress. She changes tactics, suddenly quickening her words.

Aria: "Because of tonight's disruption, the council will accelerate the timetable. We cannot have more demonstrations before the vote."

Luca: (bitter laugh) "Accelerate into what? A secrecy vote? A rushed contract with developers?" (He looks at you like the world is a set of levers he wants to pry.)

Aria: "We are balancing needs." (Her tone says you are naive and he is reckless.) "I want to work with people who want solutions."

You: "Solutions—real ones—need community involvement, not backroom deals." (You don't soften the words. The pier is small and your voice reaches farther than you intend.)

The crowd splinters into accusations. Some people shout support—others shout that you've jeopardized wages. The market woman returns, face hard.

Market Woman: "You two are tearing the town apart. People who depend on the docks are frightened—you're making them more so."

You: (Your internal monologue sharp as flint.) You had hoped passion could be bridged with data, and that urgency would translate into care. Instead the choice has become binary in people's mouths: jobs or preservation. The truth is messier, and the guilt of having pushed the needle sharpens into something like responsibility.

Night falls like a blanket, and the promenade lights sputter into isolated islands. Police presence arrives in a few marked vehicles—shadows moving with intent. Conversations mutate into strategy. Ravi and a handful of volunteers begin a slow, organized attempt to block access to the council plaza: a human line, bodies linked. It's the kind of nonviolent direct action that asks the town to watch and to choose a side.

The officers move in with commands, their voices flat and procedural. People are detained with an economy of motion you've only watched in videos and training modules. There is shock because it is real; there is anger because it is political; there is a coldness in the air as flashlights pick out faces.

Ravi: (to you, as officers close in) "We trained for this—document, stay calm." (He slides a camera into someone’s hands.) "I'll be fine—"

You: "Don't be reckless." (Your mouth says it, but your hands want to stop the approaching figures.) You are not ready to lose people to cages, paperwork, the ripple of criminal records. Yet the arrest is swift: Ravi is handcuffed, murmuring apologies, eyes like small burning coins before they are taken away. Others follow.

You: (Narration—internal) The guilt settles into you like silt. It is heavy and quiet and refuses to be flung off. You led the push; your hands built the reef they came to see. People cheered because they glimpsed a future; they feared because futures require sacrifices many cannot bear. You can trace a line from your compass to the point where the line meets another life being put behind bracelets of metal.

Jonah: (quiet, close) "We did something honest tonight," he says, but there is no triumph in it—only exhaustion. "The town will judge by consequences."

You look at the band of officers, at the small group of detained neighbors, at the way faces around you have shifted into private calculations. You think of the elder fisherman, the way his lips moved as Jonah steadied him; you think of the market woman's hands. You feel the canvass of your conviction pulled taut and slightly torn.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand closing around the compass]
[Music: A single, low cello note that lingers]
[Sound: Distant doors slamming, murmurs]

You straighten the maps in your mind, but they no longer fit a single clean line. The rain makes the ink run; the crowd disperses into smaller, grinding currents. You are left with a sense that something necessary has happened and something terrible has been set into motion.

You breathe salt air into your lungs and realize the town will be judging not your intent but the way the night landed.

[Visual: The promenade at night, police lights painting the wet stone]
[Music: Cello trails off into a thin, unresolved chord]

You hold the compass and imagine your father's hands—steady, weathered, unyielding. The image steadies you and also carries the weight of what happens when people you love are endangered by the choices you make.

There is no applause. Only the soft, urgent sound of people counting losses and making quiet decisions. You know, with the same clean clarity that you used to outline adaptation models, that tonight will become a reference point in ways you cannot fully predict.

[Page-Turn Moment]
You fold your jacket tighter and step off the promenade. The lamps smear like distant beacons; the town rearranges itself into stories it will tell at kitchen tables and in the council chamber. Your guilt is no longer a private thing—it is braided into communal memory. For the first time since you sharpened those maps, responsibility tastes like a salt-heavy ache in your chest.
