# Route: Additional


[Scene: Tidewatch Lab | Early Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the converted boathouse. Dawn through north-facing windows, pale light catching salt crystals on the sill.]
[Music: Low, brittle strings]
[Sound: Distant gulls; the soft mechanical hum of the pilot instruments]

You step into the lab and the air closes around you—cool, briny, the smell of wet timber and old oil. Instruments click and the pilot on the flats sends a tidy pulse to the screen: data, stillness, and the promise of a thing that can be argued into meaning. Luca stands by the map table, his hands stained with tar from the previous site visit. He hasn't tied his red bandana to his wrist; instead it hangs loose, drying.

You feel the gravity of the joint review like a weather system; it presses at your ribs with a cold steadiness. Here, in the hush of the boathouse, the trial begins to feel less like controlled science and more like a conversation with a city that will not be swayed by graphs alone.

Luca: "They shifted two of the monitoring buoys further inshore. Contractors said it was a depth issue—easier to anchor. Seems small until you model the fetch."

You: "Small moves ripple. Buoys measure edge conditions. Move them in, and the data will understate erosion on the flats."

Luca leans over your shoulder, eyes scanning your tablet where your model lines are thin and honest. He taps a jagged section of the curve.

Luca: "Aria wants a steady story. Investors want a steady story. We gave them a joint trial to be honest. Now 'steady' is taking the shape of small edits."

You pause, tasting metallic salt and the bitter tang of compromise. Your thumb finds the rim of your father's compass in your pocket; it is warm with early-morning warmth and stubborn as memory.

[Scene: Salt Flats — Living Breakwater Site | Midday]
[Visual: Wide, low-angle across the flats. Gray sky, reed shadows bending. Clusters of engineered reefs like dark teeth in the shallow water.]
[Music: Dissonant piano, distant thunder]
[Sound: The thunk of pile drivers, shouted instructions muffled by wind; the smell of diesel and disturbed mud]

You drive out with Luca and Ravi, boots digging into mud that remembers older tides. Contractor crews move in patterns both purposeful and rushed; men and women in neon vests nod to the supervisor and then to the excavator. You watch as prefabricated reef modules are nudged into place—some where you indicated, some not. Where the modules sit now, thin lines of runoff catch in new eddies. The flats respond with a soft, guttural sound, like a place waking.

Luca crouches beside a new reef, fingers tracing the barnacle-stippled concrete. He flips open his smartwatch and scrolls through the privately supplied developer modeling: glossy renderings of a placid promenade and an embankment that dwarfs the salt flats.

You: "Their model slices the wave differently. It's smoother. They hide the worst-case we modeled."

Luca's face narrows. He gestures toward a crew member adjusting reef placement.

Luca: "They've been rewriting the deployment as they go. If the data comes back biased because of where those buoys are, the story changes in their favor."

You want to step forward and tell the crew to stop—argue the science in the mud by pointing at lines of tide—but the foreman is already watching you. His expression is not hostile so much as practiced. He gives you a curt nod that holds no apology.

You breathe in diesel and brine. The sky tightens; a bruise of cloud rolls in from the south and the first small stitches of rain appear.

[Scene: Town Hall — Council Anteroom | Afternoon]
[Visual: Oiled wood and brass, a neat table with stacked folders. The room smells faintly of lemon polish and damp coats.]
[Music: Taut strings; a low cello undercurrent]
[Sound: The patter of rain on windows, muffled conversation beyond the door]

You carry your annotated models and a stack of printed notes into the anteroom. Council aides move like small tides, arranging and rearranging documents. Aria arrives in a coat buttoned to the throat, hair a precise line. Her briefcase is a dark thing on the table between you; when she closes it, the sound is final.

Aria: (measured) "Isla. The trial shows promising direction. The interim report can unlock the next tranche of funds. That money keeps protection going."

You: "Promising—if the monitoring locations are what we agreed. If their buoys were shifted, the data is compromised."

Aria's eyes shift, not warming. She reaches for a folder and slides a glossy developer summary toward you.

Aria: (even) "The adjustments were engineering compromises. We consulted. Investors need confidence. We—"

You: "You didn't disclose the contingency plans for a hard promenade in the packet we were given."

Her expression folds in a way you can't read—tight as the lock on a box. For a beat, the room holds its breath. The rain is a small drum against the glass.

Aria: (cool) "We are managing multiple interests, Isla. The promenade is just that: one contingency. Hard infrastructure has a different role."

You: "A contingency that would erase the salt flats—that's not a small footnote. That's a rewrite of the ecosystem."

Aria's mouth thins; she leans forward, voice quiet but not softened.

Aria: "You are an excellent scientist, Isla. You speak in certainties where I must speak in probabilities. If we delay funding for the next phase for a fight—"

You feel the sentence like a ledger closing on the town's chest. Outside, the wind has picked up; the promenade lights flicker through the rain.

Luca steps in, words blunt and quick.

Luca: "Isla found a ledger. It's not in the deliverables. It lists promenade contingencies and alternate development schedules."

Aria's hand goes, almost unconsciously, to her briefcase. Her face is unreadable—steeled and small.

Aria: (softly) "Disclosure is a political tool. We chose a staged release to avoid panic."

You: "Avoid panic for whom? Investors? Developers? Not for the people who fish here."

Aria: (flat) "You know as well as I do that pushing everyone at once risks losing everything. Sometimes we have to shepherd outcomes."

You can feel the room narrowing, the air charged with policies and stakes. Every syllable bends toward consequence.

You decide to look inside the briefcase while Aria is distracted arranging the council packets—the briefcase left open, papers shifted. Your fingers find a worn leather-bound ledger, the edge dark with handling. When you flip it open the world tilts: precise dates, marginal notes, a penciled map overlay where the salt flats are shaded out and a promenade is inked in. There are budget line items, allocations that appear to redirect money from living breakwater maintenance to paving and lighting.

Your stomach drops; the ledger is a quiet thing, the weight of a plan moving in the dark. It is not a theoretical danger now. It is a paper trail.

Aria turns, voice folded into the same room, carrying neither apology nor surprise—only a steady, careful cadence.

Aria: (quiet) "You shouldn't be looking in other people's files."

You: "You didn't disclose it to the technical review. You didn't tell the pilots."

Aria: (humane, clipped) "Isla, none of us like the choices here. I am trying to keep the town solvent."

You: "Keeping the town solvent by erasing what makes it a town."

Aria's face is unreadable, and you remind yourself to call that what it is: complex. Not malice, not innocence—strategy. The Schrödinger rule crawls at the edge of your mind; you do not know every conversation she had, every pressure put on her by investors you have never met. You only hold the ledger, and it is heavy.

Luca: "If you go public with this, you risk the funds. If you don't, you become complicit."

You: "There are more options than binary guilt."

Luca's jaw tightens. He looks at you, pleading with an urgency that is almost boyish and dangerous.

Luca: "What will you do, Isla? If we place this on the table now, it becomes a council fight. If you sit on it, they'll call you obstructive when they find out."

You close your eyes. The compass in your pocket feels like a pulse. Rain drums harder on the window; lighting in the promenade sputters like old breath. Somewhere outside, a barge's horn sounds—a low, impossible sound that seems to predict a storm.

You think of Jonah's hands folded over rope, of your father's face wet with rain and unafraid. You think of Maya painting a rooftop bed with careful strokes. The ledger is a vector. Each reaction pushes the town toward a different shore.

Your mind catalogs consequences the way you once cataloged sediment cores: layers, pressures, potential collapse.

Confront publicly and force accountability, risk funding withdrawal—and political backlash.
Refuse to sign and delay funding—buy time but invite the 'obstruction' frame.
Collect more evidence quietly—build a more airtight case while the tide might change the town irreversibly.

Your chest tightens. The salt air feels colder in the throat. Outside the anteroom, muffled voices grow louder—someone is arranging a council session for the coming week. The trial will report; the town will watch.
