You wake to the taste of salt in your mouth and your tablet buzzing like a trapped insect. The embargo lifted faster than you'd planned. Your data slid into the public current and the town responded with a roar—headlines, hot takes, frantic hands on steering wheels. You fold the blanket from your shoulders and let the first cold slip of morning reach your skin. Your compass rests in your palm, a quiet weight that used to mean sure direction. Now it feels like ballast.

People stand in small, angry groups: old friends arguing over facts, dockworkers whispering about lost wages, a schoolteacher holding a stack of petition signatures like a shield. The camera crews prowl the edges, faces bright and professional, voices flat with practiced outrage.

Luca Moreno finds you in the tide of bodies before you see his face; his expression is all forward motion—fast breath, hands working at a strap on his vest—then, when he sees you, the breath catches and his shoulders slump in a way that unravels something in your chest.

Luca Moreno: "We lost a few donors overnight. Ravi's been fielding calls—people scared of lawsuits. Did you—did you think through the legal? The promoters already sent a cease-and-desist to the paper."

(You want to answer with the models, the error bars, the weight of peer review; instead you swallow the catalogue of trade-offs, the things you told yourself at three in the morning.)
Isla Morgan: "I thought the embargo would force a pause long enough for peers to look. I didn't expect them to move that fast. I didn't want to—"
Your voice breaks on the last word and you force it steady.
Isla Morgan: "—for this."

Luca Moreno [He runs a hand through his damp hair, the red bandana trailing like a wound.]: "Pause is one thing. This is a grenade. Investors smelled weakness and they closed. Contractors are pulling their crews. People will be out of work by the end of the week if we don't—"

Your eyes hunt the promenade. A man in an expensive coat walks past with a sleek tablet; his jaw is set like someone who measures everything in outcomes. Aria Chen appears a few paces behind him, immaculate even in drizzle, the jade pendant at her throat a calm note of older money. Her face is composed to the point of hardness. She crosses to stand between you and the cameras; her presence cuts the noise like the blade of a boat through fog.

Aria Chen [The same careful cadence as always.]: "Isla. You knew the legal implications. You went public before peer corroboration. You swung a hammer at a building that hasn't even been inspected. Do you understand what you have done?"

(You taste salt and a memory of your father's hands; you feel instead the pressure of a moral balance your work always tried to keep.)
Isla Morgan: "I understood the risk of delay. I understood the need for scrutiny. The models show—"

Aria Chen [She lifts a gloved hand, stops you with the tiny, practiced motion of someone who has run meetings and campaigns for years.]: "You show what your models allow you to show. The town wakes up to the story of litigation, not to cautious science. Investors talk about contracts, not climate. You intended to force scrutiny; what you actually forced was panic."

The investor's tablet lights up and he leans in, voice like a blade dipped in oil.

Investor Rep: "You must understand legal exposure, Dr. Morgan. The company will pursue damages for lost revenue unless the injunction is removed. We're not interested in taking your town's future as a chess piece."

(You feel the compass in your pocket—brass cold against skin—and an old, animal part of you bristles.)
Isla Morgan: "The injunction is a pause to avoid irreversible damage. That is exactly the sort of window we needed."

Investor Rep: "A pause is unacceptable. The developers have contracts. Claims will be made. Towns—" His eyes flick over the clustered faces, "—need certainty."

Aria Chen [To you, quieter, but sharper for that.]: "You made a choice. I'm asking you to admit what you hoped would happen and what you didn't expect to break."

(You remember the file name on your tablet: tidal_flux_2035_final. You remember late nights, Jonah's stories, your father's compass. The calculus that led you here is exact and cruel.)
Isla Morgan: "I hoped to protect people. I'm still trying to protect people."

Aria Chen [Her answer is not mercy.]: "People are already being hurt."

[Music: Sparse, low percussion; heartbeat-like]

The injunction lands like a new tide. Contractors are called back, trucks leave in long, quiet lines. The men and women who built the pilot defenses stand in clumps, jackets folded around hope and worry. You knew the legal system would slow the project, but you had imagined civic eyes opening to science, not headlines turning into legal scythes.

A foreman—young, sunburned—walks up with a stack of schedules and the kind of tired politeness that has learned to ask for nothing.

Foreman: "Look, Dr. Morgan—people pay rent here with wages from builds. The company says they can't guarantee coverage with the injunction. They're pulling crews. If you can't lift it, we've been told to stand down."

(You hear Jonah's voice in the room behind your ribs—"They remember what stays"—and you think of nets left to rot and of empty plateaus of unemployment.)
Isla Morgan: "Is there a chance they'll come back if we—if we find alternative funding? If we can show a new dispersal plan?"

Foreman: "Maybe. But not in two days. Not without money. People have families, bills. They don't want to be martyrs for a delay that might become a lawsuit."

Luca Moreno [He steps in, trying to be practical.]: "We can crowdsource emergency stipends. We can pivot to quick wins—temporary revetments, community labor. Ravi's already talking to donors, but until things stop smelling like courtrooms, donors are skittish."

(You feel the idea of community labor like a warm, fragile thing—possible and yet not sufficient. The numbers in your head, the wave heights, the failure thresholds—these are not solved by elbow grease alone.)
Isla Morgan: "We need a plan that keeps people working and keeps protections in place. I can—"
Your sentence dies with the wind. You don't have the legal muscle. You have models and goodwill. Goodwill doesn't pay payroll.

The foreman shrugs, heavy with the knowledge of the sea and its demands. "We can shore a few houses tonight. That's all. After that—" He lets the sentence hang like a rope frayed at the end.

[Scene: Salt Flats | Late Afternoon — Low Cloud, Wind Rising]
[Visual: The engineered reefs and reed beds stretching toward the horizon; sky bruised with hard, gray clouds]
[Music: Wind-driven strings, a slow rising tremor]
[Sound: The scream of wind across exposed flatlands, the snap of tarpaulin straps]

You go to the flats because you need to touch what you are trying to protect. The grasses are bent flat by wind and the engineered reefs look small and tentative against the swell of an impatient sea. The makeshift defenses—sandbags, reclaimed pallets, rope—sit like bandages on a wound. Volunteers move like slow ghosts, faces set, fingers raw.

Isla Morgan: (You press your palm to a pallet. The wood is salt-rough and warm from labor, and for a second that is all that anchors you.) "How secure are the reef anchors?"

Volunteer: "They'll shift if the surge comes in strong. We got them tied to old pilings, but the bolts weren't meant for constant battering. We did what we could."

The sky splits in a way that makes the light go thin; someone calls out toward the horizon and the word 'gale' seems to spread faster than wind. You feel the models tighten into a single, cold answer: the storm is stronger than the last forecasts you used.

Luca Moreno [He is there before you feel the first hard drops; his hands smell of diesel and his eyes are raw with a different kind of weather—anger honed to a point.]: "Why didn't you wait for peer corroboration? If we'd had that, we could have used it to hold the injunction and keep the work funded."

(You open your mouth. The words hang like fishing line cast and trembling.)
Isla Morgan: "I thought forcing a pause would prevent construction that would lock us into harm. I thought the pause would be a shield while experts—"

Luca Moreno [He cuts you off with a hand that trembles.]: "A shield that left us without supplies and without people. The crews left. The donors left. We don't have the materials we need. Houses will flood because we couldn't keep the team paid."

(You feel the accusation like wind through salt-stiff fabric: cold and cutting. You think of the nights you spent writing the embargo note, of the reasons that made the risk feel like the only honest thing to do.)
Isla Morgan: "I couldn't let them—to sign contracts that would make it impossible to reverse structural harm. Once built it would've been catastrophic."

Luca Moreno [His voice is simpler now, not political—raw and personal.]: "And now people are losing nets and roofs in the storm that we might've kept them safe from if we hadn't pulled their support. Do you understand the scale of that, Isla? This isn't policy. That's my—"
His voice breaks. He means the lives, the people he slept near, the hands that built the defenses.

(You can feel how fragile the line is between intention and outcome. You wanted to stop long-term harm. Instead the town faces immediate loss.)
Isla Morgan: "I know. I know."

Luca Moreno [He laughs, a sound that is all pain.]: "Knowing isn't fixing. Not this time."

The engineered reefs, designed in calmer analysis, take a battering they weren't rated for. A section of a living breakwater collapses with a wet, sick sound. Sandbags split. The volunteers shout, trying to redirect the flow, but the current is a blunt instrument and it doesn't bend for human pleading.

(You run toward a low-set house where the family who helped knit the reed beds sits in the doorway, hands on knees, faces gone white.)
Isla Morgan: "Take what you can."

Neighbor: "It came from the north so fast—like the sea had straight teeth."

(You think of nets, of pylons ripped free and spinning like pale fish. You think of Jonah's story about a season when the town rebuilt everything twice over. This feels like that, but smaller and meaner.)

You find a high ridge where the path looks over the flats. The water catches the last light and slips away like a secret. For a long time you just stand, and the compass in your pocket is heavier than it has any right to be.

Luca Moreno comes up behind you, boots sinking into the wet ground a half-step off. He doesn't touch you. Words are blunt here; everything else is. He looks at the flats in the same way observers look at a burned photograph—something they once owned, now altered forever.

Luca Moreno: "Do you feel it? The town's breath—short, tight. People will point fingers. They're already looking for someone to blame."

(You look at him—at the way his jaw has set, at the exhaustion that lines his face like rope marks. Once, his quick optimism felt like a promise of repair. Now that promise has been hardened and turned into anger.)
Isla Morgan: "I wanted the truth to matter more than the short-term calculations."

Luca Moreno: "And look where that got us." (His voice is small, like an animal trying to get noticed in a storm.) "Look at the flat. Nets gone. The boys who used to fish here are out of work for months, maybe more."

(You see it all at once—the models that would have warned you and the humans who won't live by error bars. The injunction slowed the machines but not the sea; your action made space for scrutiny and litigation, and the space you made was filled by lawyers and fear.)
Isla Morgan: "I thought I was saving people."

Luca Moreno [He looks at you now with something that is not just anger but a raw, precise grief.]: "You weren't saving them today, Isla."

Silence settles between you like a weight. The tide slows; the flats glisten. In the distance someone starts singing, an old fisher's chant that belongs to Jonah's generation. It is not comfort so much as ritual. You press the compass into your hand again, feeling the brass cold and real.

(Your thoughts untangle into one clear, terrible shape: intentions can create consequences as measurable and cruel as any storm. You leaked the findings because you believed truth could avert future catastrophe; instead you delivered immediate harm in a town already fragile. The calculus you trusted has teeth.)
Isla Morgan: "What do we do now?"

Luca Moreno [He looks away from the flats and back at you as if looking for a person you were before the broadcast.]: "We pick up what we can. We help who we can. But understand this—people will remember who pulled the cord."

(You feel the sunlight die on the water and with it, something in you settles into a hard, inevitable knowledge.) The only thing left is to keep being present in the wake of what you made. Presence now tastes like ash.

Isla Morgan: (In the wind, you make a promise you don't know how to keep.) "I'll be here."

The promise feels like the only honest thing left.
