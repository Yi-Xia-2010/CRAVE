[Scene: Town Hall Hearing Room | Morning]
[Visual: A long, narrow room under a resolute strip light. Papers are stacked in neat and messy piles at the lectern. The regional council banner hangs behind a row of impassive chairs; the windows show a gray spill of sea beyond.]
[Music: Warm strings begin, rising into a gentle, steady motif.]
[Sound: Murmurs settle into a hush; a single dry cough, the rustle of printed pages.]

You stand before the lectern with a paper that smells faintly of copier toner and sea breeze — the forensic review you and Asha pulled together nights ago, printed from a laptop that refused to sleep. Your hands tremble once, not from fear of the room but from the distance between what you've known in the lab and the real consequence of putting it into the light.

This was the path you walked into from Chapter Five: the path that refused quiet compromise, that chose public pressure with Cassian at your back. You remember the march’s salt-stung banners, the scrape of paint on plywood, the way the town felt awake and dangerous with hope. You remember promising yourself you would not hide the truth for the sake of comfort.

Your throat tastes of vinegar; the tidewatcher pendant rests heavy at your throat like a hinge.

[Visual: Close-up on the first page of the report, the header stamped with Tidelab's seal. A schematic with annotated frequency curves blinks in contrast against the margins of council typology.]
[Narration]
The forensic review is simple and stubborn in its language: resonance amplification at certain wave periods; a coupling with the seabed rebound Jonah’s design ignored. The math is blunt. The models that promised civic security concealed a feedback loop that would, in a worst-case storm, concentrate energy against the very neighborhoods the barrier intends to guard.

You breathe in the room — coffee and disinfectant and the wet wool of a thousand coats. People are here: volunteers from the planting brigades, the tired municipal clerk you argued with last spring, parents clutching damp children who will inherit the decisions you make now. You hear Cassian's breath behind you, quick and concentrated the way it is when he's about to start one of his street songs.

A councilmember clears her throat. Jonah sits three rows forward, hands folded like a practiced ordinance, face composed. You have seen him bargain and build across tables. Tonight, his steel-blue eyes look a little human at the edges.

You slide the pages forward. You will speak by the numbers — you always have trusted numbers; you also trust the slow, honest ache of a community's memory. This is both.

You step up.

You: "The review shows a modal amplification in the proposed barrier's response to recurring storm periods. It's not a minor uncertainty —it's a systematic resonance with the bay's tidal signatures that will magnify force at the seaward base. If constructed as proposed, the barrier will change where the energy concentrates during a storm." (You pause to let the technical words fold into the room.)

Councilmember Park: "Can you explain what that would mean for neighborhoods already identified as at-risk?"

You: "Yes. It would likely transfer stress to the east-facing inlets —areas with older pilings and fragile soils. Those places are the heart of Lowry Bay: the Drowned Orchard, Ruben's docks, families who have nowhere else to go. The consortium's models assume homogeneity the bay doesn't have."

[Visual: The camera lingers on Ruben in the audience; his hands are his own map of years on the water: callused, ink-stained with old rope burns.]
[Sound: A low hum of recognition passes through the room.]

Jonah: (standing, voice even) "Mira… you know the modeling team, the advanced simulations—"

You: (looking straight at him) "I audited the modeling assumptions, Jonah. The simulations used damping parameters that do not match the bay's measured substrate heterogeneity. I ran field-matched tests with Asha's sensors; they show a consistent bias. I am not delivering opinion — I'm presenting a forensic assessment."

Jonah: (a fraction of impatience) "You could have brought this privately. We could have adapted."

You: "We tried private channels. We asked for transparency and were redirected by contracts and deadlines. Those deadlines were the very mechanism that normalized assumptions. This is about preventing a catastrophic misread—preventing lives lost because a model made a promise it couldn't keep."

[Dialogue continues — multiple turns]
Jonah: (softens) "Mira. My intention has always been to buy time. To protect."

You: "And I want time, too. I want security. But not if that security is built on a lie of understanding. The math says it will redirect harm, Jonah. The harm will be concentrated where people can least absorb it."

Jonah: (the guard slips for a moment — fatigue, maybe surprise) "If this sinks the project, how do you propose we keep the town from sinking into uncertainty and unemployment?"

You: (the practical ache surfaces) "By redirecting funding into adaptive, nature-based reinforcements and accountable, phased construction —projects that hire locals for longer-term maintenance. By insisting on independent audits and public oversight so that work doesn't privilege a single outcome. I know it means different money flows. I know it means a harder path. But it avoids creating a hazard we can't undo."

[Visual: Faces in the room shift — some skeptical, some visibly relieved. The elder woman near the back dabs her eyes with a blue handkerchief.]

Asha: (standing, voice warm and firm) "We can scale ecological work faster if funding is reallocated and if contracts are renegotiated for local labor. Tidelab will volunteer technical oversight. We'll train more technicians. We can make jobs without building what would hurt the bay."

[Music: The strings swell, then resolve into a hopeful, breathing rhythm.]

[Scene: The Boardwalk Outside City Hall | Midday]
[Visual: The boardwalk is a smear of salt and paint: protest banners drying over railings, people clustered in muted conversation. The sea beyond is a pale, restless mirror; gulls wheel reluctant circles.]
[Sound: The distant clack of workers' tools, a harmonica played softly, the slap of wet sneakers.]

You step out into sunlight, the printouts folded under your arm. The town's reaction waves through you like weather: anger, relief, fear, gratitude — each face a microclimate.

Cassian finds you where the paint-splattered mural is mid-swirl. He doesn't approach quietly; his steps are punctuation.

Cassian: (the first words cut) "You blew it open."

You: (you brace for the criticism you knew might come) "I did what I had to. The report needed to be public."

Cassian: (voice tight) "People were counting on the construction boom. The market said there'd be jobs. The café owners we met last week put rent on those prospects. Some folks are livid, Mira. They think you picked principles over survival."

You: (you hear the accusation and fold it into what you feel: guilt, yes — but a steadier current of conviction) "I can't sell a temporary paycheck at the cost of long-term hazard. I know it hurts. I know the timing is brutal."

Cassian: (the stubbornness that fights hopelessness shows itself) "Maybe. Maybe we find a way to make those jobs, just different ones. But you could have… you could have let us plan that before you dropped an atomic truth bomb on the funding."

You: (a small, rueful smile) "And who do you think taught me to drop truth bombs? You. The street theater, the murals, the impossible banners — you taught me that silence is a form of consent."

Cassian: (breath catches — anger folding into something softer) "I am angry because I thought I saw us holding something together. Because I worried you’d shoulder the blame alone. But—" (he gestures to a small cluster over by the Drowned Orchard where families are hugging) "—look."

The fallout is immediate and complicated. Investors freeze funds. Jonah's consortium announces a review; public outrage splinters into rallies and quiet acts of neighborly repair. Some call you reckless. Others call you brave.

You move through the town like a tool passed between hands, being used where needed. You sit with merchants who fear rent hikes without promised construction contracts and help them draft petitions for emergency small-business grants. You stand with volunteers as they file for training; Asha runs a crash course on sensor maintenance and living-berm construction that fills the greenhouse with excited, practical noise.

Jonah seeks you out again, this time without a table of banners between you — just a single bench at the edge of the square.

Jonah: (close, lowered voice) "You cost a lot of jobs tonight, Mira."

You: "You cost them the illusion of safety. There's a difference."

Jonah: (looks at the map pinned to the wall) "And now the regional council will open hearings on accountable infrastructure. The consortium isn't the only institution that'll be scrutinized. This will change more than a project timeline."

You: (feeling the shape of what you've started) "It should change that. Contracts shouldn't be a way to bypass civic oversight. People should know what they are signing away."

Jonah: (a long beat) "I misjudged the substrate data. I signed easier models to keep the schedule. I thought—" (he stops, too much to fit in the spaces between people) "—I thought certainty was what you needed."

You: "Certainty built on shortcuts is dangerous." (You soften) "You didn't intend this. But we both have to live with the results of how we tried to solve fear."

Jonah: (voice small, unexpectedly vulnerable) "I may not be the villain some will make me. I will—" (he hesitates, then offers) "—I will support the independent audits. I will advocate for a redeployment of funds into hybrid solutions. If there's a way to help restore trust, I will try."

[Scene: Old Northpoint Lighthouse | Dusk]

[Music: A single cello finds a warm, conclusive line. A choir hums under it like a distant crowd.]

You climb the wet steps alone. The stones are cool and hold the smell of algal dusk. The interior is cramped; maps are tacked to the wall, lines of ink that used to be your private promise and are now the town's coiled blueprint. You fit into the narrow top like something made to be there.

You stand with your back to the window and look at Lowry Bay spread in small lights — a stitched map of lives, gardens, docks, and the Drowned Orchard's thin silhouette. The report you published has altered trajectories. Funding evaporated; promises broke; some faces hardened in anger. But homes did not move overnight. The orchard still bears fruit on its stilts for now. Neighbors argued and stayed. The council hearings will be long and noisy, but they will be public. That is a change you wanted.

Your chest is a quiet bell. The sound is not a shout of triumph; it is a steady toll that acknowledges loss and chooses direction.

Cassian comes up the spiral steps, boots soft on stone. He pauses by the doorway, looking less like the restless fire of the street and more like someone who has learned how to hold its heat.

Cassian: (without theatrics) "You look like a lighthouse yourself."

You: (smiles, the salt wind catching the corner of your mouth) "I've dreamed of being small and useful."

Cassian: (moves closer, lowers his voice) "I was angry. I was scared. My mouth said things I didn't mean. But then I saw Mrs. Ibarra's storefront stay, felt Ruben's hand… I thought of what would've happened if your call had been quieter."

You: "I know what you lost to this, Cass. I know the weight."

Cassian: (takes your hand; his fingers are paint-stained and real) "We will make new work. We'll teach, we'll hire, we'll fix. I don't know what shape our life takes from here, but I know this: you did what was true. And that — that's more than I can say for a lot of promises."

Cassian: "And we'll show them how to hold it."

You stay until the beam swings past and the sky deepens. The lighthouse stands as a promise: to keep watch, to warn, to guide repair. In the dusk you feel a careful contentment, the kind that comes after a difficult choice when the rightness of it is not a comfort but an obligation satisfied.

You think, not of the money lost or the sleepless nights, but of small, exact scenes — the orchard lanterns swinging, children learning sensor maintenance, Asha teaching recruits how to read the tide. You think of policy hearings that will be tedious and important, of councils that will have to face more public eyes. You think of Jonah working to rebuild trust instead of moving forward with certainty; of Cassian turning his anger into long-term organization; of Ruben's quiet blessing.

You step out into the dusk. The air tastes like brine and possibility. You have no comfortable title waiting, no secure salary from the consortium, no easy path. But below you, the town clings, argues, and begins the slow work of making resilience that listens to the people it protects.

Ruben's hand squeezes yours one last time when you descend; his voice is a dry, sure thing. "Good work, child."

You let the lighthouse's lamp fade behind you and walk toward the boardwalk. Somewhere, someone strings a lantern. The town is an imperfect, living ledger of choices. Tonight, one of those choices will ripple farther than you imagined — into hearings, into policy, into towns downstream who will demand the same transparency you insisted on.

You are tired. You are certain. You did as much as one person could hold and gave the rest back to the people who will keep doing the work.
