You walk into a room that smells faintly of printer toner and lemon disinfectant. The Mayor's office, which had felt like a hinge — a place where differing futures could turn together — now feels like an overburdened seam. The fluorescent light hums, and every incoming call sets the edge of your nerves to ring.

Mayor Lin Park looks smaller than you'd pictured amid the paper and pleas. Her hands hover over a wall of post-it maps as if trying to hold the town up by sheer pressure. Jonah Hale stands near the window, shoulders tailing into the rain outside; his slate is clutched like a shield. Cassian is a storm of paint and breath, his satchel at his feet, face raw.

Cassian's voice breaks the air as if he had been waiting for the exact sound to release. "They printed 'acceptable displacement,'" he says, and the words hit like a stone thrown against glass. He slams his satchel onto the council table; flyers and a harmonica clatter across the wood.

Cassian: "An engineer writes acceptable displacement and now half of Lowry thinks you planned to move them like furniture. Who leaked this, Jonah? Who thought this was okay?"

Jonah: (defensive, hands up) "It was an internal draft. Truncated. Taken out of context. We never—"

Cassian: (cuts in) "Taken out of context? Or shaped? The wording sounded like a table of trade-offs, not an apology."

You feel the words ricochet inside you, both accusation and plea. Cassian's eyes flash with hurt that has sharpened into rhetoric. Jonah's jaw tightens; the engineer's precision has calcified into public contempt. Mayor Lin's face is flat with exhaustion, the map pins like tiny witnesses.

Mayor Lin Park: "People are calling the office. We have neighbors showing up at the docks, and—" (she exhales) "—Ruben just left a message saying he'll close his shop if this is the path. We need a message. We need a plan."

You want to pull everyone back into the careful cadence of the pilot: data, transparency, community deliberation. Instead, you are listening to an argument that has already become a public wound.

Mayor Lin Park: (sudden, weary) "Answers take time. But right now, the phone bank is melting down. The volunteers are splitting—some at the berm sites, some at the rallies. Unity is unraveling in real time."

You sense the texture of the split: the practical volunteers, hands raw with rope and reed, and the ones who want spectacle and urgency to force a faster political hand. Both are right. Both fear.

[Scene: Tidelab Greenhouse | Early Evening — Lamps Low]
[Visual: Amber horticultural lights, damp floorboards reflecting tiny blurs of lamplight; a laptop screen shows truncated model snippets and a red-circled phrase: "acceptable displacement".]
[Sound: The distant clanging of protest drums down the boardwalk; inside, the slow drip of condensation.]
[Music: A muted piano theme with dissonant high strings.]

You return to Tidelab because it is the place you know how to be most yourself — hands in soil, numbers on a grid, people arriving to plant something that will outlast a headline. Asha is sitting at the potting bench, shoulders small, hands trembling around a mug gone lukewarm. Her screen is open to the model files; her face is a ledger of guilt and calculation.

Asha Mehta: (voice thin) "The leak… they're using the sensitivity outputs as policy, Mira. The press pulled an internal sim and a line from Jonas' technical notes. Without the full run, it looks like the plan endorses displacing neighborhoods as an efficiency trade-off. It doesn't. But the words are out."

You reach for her hand before you consider the formality of it. Her fingers are damp from watering trays; they close around yours as if to anchor a buoy against a slow tide.

You: "Tell me how it looks from your end. What in the model is being misread?"

Asha: (takes a breath) "The truncated model showed scenarios where engineered barriers are one variable among many. In the doc someone excerpted, thresholds were highlighted for analysis: at what point emergency relocation becomes statistically less costly than repeated rebuilds. The memo was about stress-testing, not prescriptions. But the headline only needed a phrase."

You listen to the way her words fold into the warm greenhouse air—technical specificity turning into public harm. You smell wet soil and the metallic edge of your own pulse.

Asha: (voice breaking) "And they found the 'acceptable displacement' phrase in a separate note — a horrible phrase, I agree — someone debating metrics wrote it without thinking of the language's weight. It's a terminology failure as much as a data one. But headlines don't parse that."

You press your thumb along the seam of Asha's wrist where the sensor strap has rubbed the skin. Her hand trembles like a lamp about to go out.

You: "We hold the context. We publish the whole model. We walk the town through every step. We do the forensic review. We show everyone the caveats."

Asha: (shakes her head) "If we publish everything, funding could collapse. The pilot could be halted. The consortium will pull resources. Without the pilot, the margin for maneuver narrows. People will look for certainty in the wrong places."

You feel the old pulse of fragility — the line you underlined in your notebook three nights ago — drawn across the room. It isn't academic anymore. It is the difference between scaffolding and shattering.

You step back into the town that taught you how to read tides. The boardwalk smells of salt and fresh paint — both attempts at preservation, both attempts at covering something with a new coat. Others move in a different rhythm, marching toward City Hall with placards kissed by rain.

Ruben's storefront is shuttered. You can see him through the slats — his silhouette hunched, the carved cane resting like a small harbor. He looks up when you pass and gives a nod that is both apology and resignation.

Cassian catches your sleeve. His hands are paint-smeared and raw. "They think a wall will fix this," he says. "They want a single thing to point to so they can stop thinking about who loses. I'm not sure I'm done yelling about that."

You: "Jonah says it was an internal sim. Asha says publication could collapse funding. The Mayor is drowning in calls. People are scared."

Cassian: (voice fraying) "People were already scared. The leak just gave them a direction—toward top-down promises. I don't know if they'll listen to nuance tonight."

You can feel how the town is dividing into a geometry of loss: who will leave; who will stay and fight; who will trade their histories for a chance at dry foundations. Every alley seems to ask you which side you're on.

Mayor Lin Park: (approaching, voice tight) "Your volunteers are split, Mira. The night smells like wet paint and salt and fear. Sirens thread through the air like a measured, persistent tide.

You realize the town no longer has the luxury of slow deliberation. The leak pushed the conversation into a politics of emergency. You feel responsibility pressing like a hand against your sternum.

You stand at the center of the storm of people, numbers, and language. Your options stretch before you like tidal inlets—each route carving a different shoreline. None are clean. All are consequential.

Your throat is dry. You look at each face around you: Jonah's steely carefulness, Cassian's brimstone fidelity, Asha's trembling competence, Mayor Lin's pinched decisiveness. You can feel the cost of every choice in your bones.
