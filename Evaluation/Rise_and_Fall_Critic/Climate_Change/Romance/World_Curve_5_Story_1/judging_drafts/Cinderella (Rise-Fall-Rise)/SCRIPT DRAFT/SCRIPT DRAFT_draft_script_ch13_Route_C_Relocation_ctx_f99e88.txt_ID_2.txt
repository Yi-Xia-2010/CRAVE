You stand at the edge of the plaza with Mayor Lin at the podium and Jonah to her left, his slate tucked under his arm like a treaty. Nadia's small hand finds yours and squeezes it once, serious as a bell.

Mayor Lin clears her throat. The microphone takes her voice and sets it against the water.

Mayor Lin: "After weeks of deliberation, public testimony, and revised safeguards, the council has approved the Relocation Accord. It is voluntary. It includes guaranteed employment in the ecosystem projects, a civic fund for community land trusts, and oversight provisions run by a mixed council of residents."

A thin cheer rises, then subsides into a ragged, complicated applause. You watch faces—relief, suspicion, something like gratitude folded into grief. The accord is teeth and balm both; it will protect many, but it will take others from the place their lives learned to inhabit.

Jonah steps forward. He speaks with the measured cadence of someone who engineered contracts into life.

Jonah: "My team will provide seed funding and some fast-tracked infrastructure support where it's needed. Oversight will be community-led. I know trust is earned, not delivered. I am here to help build that trust."

There's a rustle from the crowd—some scowl, some nods. Jonah meets your eyes for a long second. His expression is cool but not unkind; there is a quiet promise in the tilt of his chin. You imagine all the ways help can come wrapped with strings. You imagine all the ways it can be untied.

[Scene: Schoolyard / Temporary Stage | Afternoon]
[Visual: A makeshift stage of pallets and tarps; children in hand-sewn costumes; knitted lanterns hang like small moons above.]

Nadia stands center stage in a costume stitched with painted apples. Her voice is steady; beneath it, you hear the town's layered history.

Nadia: (projecting) "We plant and we carry, we remember what was and build what will be. We do not erase the orchard. We place its stories where people can reach them."

The play is earnest and small and perfect. Parents weep, not just for loss but for continuity. Ruben reads a line about seed types and tides, and someone in the crowd laughs through tears when he drops in an old fisher's curse in the middle—a ritual laughter that softens the edge of grief.

After the play, neighbors gather beneath the hung lanterns. Cassian is on a ladder, paint bleeding from his fingers as he marks the first broad stroke of a new mural along the boardwalk—an arc of roots and a rising tide, two hands meeting in the middle.

You brush paint from his sleeve without meaning to; the motion is automatic and intimate.

Cassian: (soft) "They're putting your block on the relocation list, you know."

You: "I know."

Cassian climbs down and stands near you, his hazel eyes wet with something like grief and something like defiance. He looks at the mural, then at the line of boats waiting, then back at you.

Cassian: "I wanted to do something different. To make everyone stay. But this—this feels right, too. We can save people. We can teach them to live with the bay instead of getting swallowed by it."

You: "You always think first of story."

Cassian: "And you think first of being useful." (He smiles, small and half-broken.) "We both do what we can."

You talk. The conversation spirals—plans for murals in the new neighborhood strip, how to make the job training not feel like charity, whether to put words on the mural or leave it pure symbol. You interrupt each other with ideas, with tenderness, with the practicalities of who will paint which panel.

Cassian: "When you get anxious, you look at the tidewatcher."

You: "When you get stubborn, you start a mural the size of a wall and finish at dawn."

He laughs, and for a second the weight between you lifts like a tide. You let your forehead rest a moment against his shoulder, smelling paint and salt and the clean, raw ache of commitment.

[Scene: Higher Ground — Temporary Settlement | Dusk]
[Visual: Rows of raised platforms and small, sturdy dwellings; lanterns swing like constellations; a line of newly planted marsh grass fans into the dim.]

[Music: A warm choir-like swell, violin harmonics suggesting healing.]

You move along higher ground with Ruben, watching him catalog each heirloom sapling—a small, knotted list on a clip board—his hands sure despite everything. He names each variety aloud as if saying a prayer.

Ruben: "This one's a 'Lowry Gold'—grew from my wife's aunt's graft. Keep it close to the community orchard plot. People will come back to its fruit and they'll remember."

You: "Will they come back?"

Ruben: (shrugs) "Some will. Some won't. The important thing is there is a place to come back to. We'll hold it."

His answer is a lighthouse—steady, workmanlike; it steadies you more than you expect. You think of the houses that will remain and the ones that will not. Memory, like salt, etches itself into every corner.

Jonah appears at the edge of the settlement with a crate of supplies and a quiet nod. He stays in the background, hands folded, offering tools rather than speeches. You accept a roll of waterproof sheeting and the briefest word of thanks.

Jonah: "I've redirected some crews to reinforce the access ramps. Oversight board will get real-time feeds. If there's anything you need, tell me before sunset."

You: "We will. Thank you."

He does not search your face for more than a heartbeat. The bluntness of his practicality sometimes feels like a balm. You want to tell him how dearly you guard community stories, but the moment would be a small transgression in the face of this coordinated move. So you nod.

[Scene: Higher Ground — Lantern Vigil | Night]
[Visual: Knotted lanterns suspended above a communal table; the mural's painted tide is visible down on the boardwalk, a bright crescent in the dark; the sky is clear for once, the moon a careful witness.]

People gather to light the lanterns. One by one, they place them on poles that line the perimeter—a procession of small suns filling the settlement with a patient glow. The air is damp and smells of wood smoke and damp earth; hands are callused and warm.

You walk the line and touch the paper of a lantern that once hung from your childhood porch. Its faint smell is of apple pies and wet wood. You have kept the smell tucked in your throat for days.

Cassian finds you in the lamplight. He wraps his coat around both your shoulders and draws you close. His touch is familiar and decisive.

Cassian: "We made something out of a hard thing. It's not what either of us wanted at first, but it's honest."

You: "Honest can be kind."

Cassian: "And brave."

You lean into him, and the hush is steady. Around you, people share food, tell easy stories about work crews and curious children, about a neighbor who will teach grafting and another who will run the tool shed. There is a practical joy in the exchange—skills traded like currency and affection braided through labor.

Ruben joins you, leaning on his cane but bright-eyed.

Ruben: "We left nothing to chance. Not everyone leaves. Those who go know we carry them here—soil, stories, names."

You: "We will keep them in the town's story."

Ruben: "Good. Keep it well."

Mayor Lin walks past, stopping to accept a mug and an offered slice of bread. She meets your gaze and for an instant the public office melts into a private human exchange.

Mayor Lin: "We did what we could. We'll keep pushing for oversight and funding for the trusts. This—this work will keep the story honest."

You: "Thank you."

She smiles, and the smile is small, relieved, exhausted, and resolute—every flavor of the settlement's new future.

You feel proud and hollow at once—pride in the coordinated care, hollow because comfort cannot erase the empty porches, the absence of the bay's old map. But that hollow is not a void of defeat; it is a room being refurnished by memory and intent. You vow—softly, as a word to the dark—that you'll tell the stories of those who left; you'll teach the children the names of the trees; you'll plant a new 'Lowry Gold' with Ruben's hands guiding yours.

Cassian: "We'll build a place where people can come back to, not because they have to, but because it belongs to them."

You: "We'll build it together."

He kisses you then, quick and sure, not a sealing of endings but a tether for ongoing work. The kiss tastes like sea salt and paint and the promise of morning.
