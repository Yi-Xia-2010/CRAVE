[Scene: Embankment | Dawn]
[Visual: Pale, thin light slants across newly poured concrete. The seawall's face is raw, wet, and stubbornly vertical against the tide. Machinery lines cast long, angular shadows. A ribboned contractor's banner flutters—white letters on corporate blue—half-covered by salt spray.]
[Music: Low, slow minor strings — a funeral for what was not yet dead]
[Sound: Distant diesel thrum, a seabird's croak, the tide breathes in and out like a patient holding its breath]

You arrive before the town wakes properly, because if anything about this morning is true it is that the work began without permission and with applause. The seawall sits like a scar with neat seams and stamped concrete logos; it smells of wet cement and diesel, and that smell tastes of compromise in the back of your throat.

You stand with the surf at your boots. The sky is the slate of a bruise; light finds the edges of a gull's wing and does what it can. Around you, half a dozen contractors in high-visibility vests move like a choreography practiced elsewhere, efficient and clean-faced. Behind them, a line of municipal trucks—Celine's banner on the cab—glitters with a newness that makes the harbor's old rust look poor and ordinary.

You want to catalog everything—fault lines in the plan, clauses you haven't seen, who will be allowed to keep a slip, who will be asked to leave. Instead you breathe and let the cold air fill you, because counting is a kind of armor and you have to keep your hands steady.

[Visual: Close-up on your face; salt crystals coat your lashes]
[Sound: Your breath; a faint radio chatter about "completion timeline"]

You don't expect Celine to be open to argument at daybreak. You know her—polished certainty, the kind that makes you want to bristle and also breathe. But there she is near the middle of the site, speaking into a tablet as if recording the morning's perfection. When she sees you, she allows a smile that has been practiced to the shape of this town.

Celine Harrow: "Alys (you): good morning. Early day for someone who likes the greenhouse's warmth." (She tucks hair behind an ear; wind flirts with the scarf at her collar.)

You: "Good morning." (Your voice is steadier than you feel.) "You've moved fast."

Celine Harrow: "We had to. The storm season timetable hasn't been waiting for council debates." (She tilts her head, eyes bright with the gleam of a decision that has been sold as necessary.) "This keeps families dry. It protects the wharves; it sends a signal to investors that Saltwick is serious."

You study her, searching for the moment when conviction became concession. There's a ledger in her expression; you've seen it in meetings—numbers meant to be incantations. You hear the contractors laughing quietly as they splice rebar. The harbor answers with a dull metallic heartbeat.

You: "Protection that locks the harbor against the sea is something else than protection that locks people away from their boats. Who owns the slips? Who can maintain the shoreline plantings when they're not contractors' responsibilities?"

Celine Harrow: "We negotiated municipal stewardship. There are clauses for access. And the funding—" (her voice softens with the flavor of victory) "—this money means school repairs, emergency supplies. The town couldn't wait for piecemeal grants. Ivo, for instance, recommended rapid measures."

[Sound: His boots scuff; distant, a volunteer's radio clicks.]

You look toward him. The lines at the corner of his mouth could be a conceding breath or a sealing of lips; his expression is complex—there's warmth, a grinding practicality, and something you can't parse into a single emotion. He has always been someone who does things to keep people safe. Today his hands are marked with cement, and you are not sure if that makes him closer to saving Saltwick or complicit in its erasure.

Ivo: "Morning." (He folds his hands against the cold. His tone sits somewhere between apologetic and resigned.) "It had to go up now. There were families with leaks, roofs flapping. This—this buys time."

You: "Time for what, Ivo? Time for us to do the rest? Or time for whoever financed this to decide what happens next?"

Ivo: "Alys—" (he steps a fraction closer; his voice drops) "You know me. I fix what's in front of me. People couldn't wait for a slow program. We needed something that doesn't give every storm a victory."

You hold back the list you have been rehearsing at the greenhouse table: clauses about privatized access, indemnities that limit municipal oversight, maintenance contracts that favor the contractor's affiliates. You also hold back a different list: the faces of neighbors who slept under tarpaulins last winter, kids whose school was closed after the last flood. You are the sum of both lists — an inconvenient contradiction.

Celine Harrow: "Alys, you are a scientist; you know the limits of evidence. I consulted engineers, auditors. Sometimes—" (she glances at Ivo) "—we make the pragmatic call. We cannot let perfection be the enemy of safety."

You: "Pragmatism that turns civic space into private profit is not safety. It's leverage."

Celine Harrow: (soft, almost pleading) "We can negotiate protections around community access. We can establish a fund for relocation assistance where necessary. The shore will be safer for the next decade."

Your hands curl into fists. Your thumb scrapes a faint seam in your palm as if to anchor you. You have prepared public statements, community oversight proposals, red-lined contracts. You have written the words "community enforcement" until they had the grain of scripture. But writing isn't the same as stopping a poured wall.

[Scene Transition: Harbor | Midday]
[Visual: The harbor crowd has thickened—residents, volunteers, a scattering of cameras, and the bright, angular banners of contractors. A plywood placard has been nailed up near the ferry ramp: "PRIVATIZED BOATYARD — NO PUBLIC MOORING." It is weathered already by salt and urgency.]

Maya finds you near the placard. Her apron is smeared with paint, and her hands are nail-marked from long days of fixing community spaces. She looks small in the face of the municipal trucks. The harbor's light catches her eyes and makes them fierce.

Maya: "Sis." (She reaches for your sleeve without quite touching.) "Some of the older folks—Oba—he says they were promised community wharves. They'd talk to Celine, we—" (her words tumble) "—we're losing more than docks. We're losing memory. Who will tell the tide songs now?"

You: "We tell them." (You answer before you can make it a promise you aren't sure you'll keep.) "We will tell them, and we will fight what's in those clauses."

Maya: "How? The seawall's almost finished. Ronan was here earlier; he tried to chain himself to a piling and they cut him loose with a rope. He's furious, but—" (she swallows) "—they took the footage down and sent out their own, with smiling families in hard hats. People believe the pictures."

You feel the ache of being outmaneuvered in the public eye. Celine has lawyers; contractors have public relations. You have data and stubbornness. Data breathes slowly; PR floods. The harbor smells of hot tar and of the future being monetized.

Ronan Pike approaches in a rush, palms raw with rope burn, cheeks flushed with indignation.

Ronan: "We tried to stop them. They said 'safety' and put a kid carrying a hard-hat into a staged shot. We called it out. They called it 'community participation.' It's—" (he laughs, a brittle sound) "—it's theater."

You: "Ronan, pull yourself together. We need signatures, public records requests, a press release. Leyna can run an analysis of the long-term erosional impacts—"

Ronan: "And who will look out for the families tonight when the tide creeps higher against the new wall's base? The contractors say it's fixed, but what about the marshlands they bulldozed to pour the footing? That's where the water is going to funnel. You can't just—"

You: "I know." (You know; it is a hollow echo that makes your teeth ache.) "I know, and I'm filing for an injunction as we speak."

Ronan: "Will that stop them?"

You: "It buys us time." (You try to measure the value of what you can do. Time is tangible in some ways now—court dates, hearing notices—but time will be measured in how many houses stand when the next storm comes.)

Ivo crosses the quay and stops between you and the board that now reads "PRIVATIZED BOATYARD." He doesn't touch the sign. His jaw is set as if bracing against a wave.

Ivo: "You can't make them reopen contracts, Alys." (His voice doesn't try to soften the blow.) "Celine signed what she signed. There are clauses that make municipal reversal—" (he searches, his eyes not meeting yours fully) "—complicated."

You: "Then we make it politically costly." (You want to believe the steadiness of righteous pressure can chew through legal hedges.) "We call the town meetings, we organize the fishermen who've already lost space, we show the press the environmental cost."

Ivo: "And Celine knows how to survive a headline. She survives because she makes deals. If we make it personal—" (he nods toward vendor stalls across the quay, where small uprisings would mean lost livelihoods) "—we put people out."

You: "Not if we fight smart. We can demand oversight. We can rally Oba, the mariners, the galleries—"

Ivo: "You rally while people need roofs, Alys. The money built that wall. Houses get saved tonight because of it. The rest you say you'd fight for, but the rest doesn't keep a kid dry in the rain this week."

There's a hardness in his words that hits like cold wind. It's not accusation as much as a brutal tally. He has always been a man of immediate actions; now those actions have become the instruments of a change he didn't fully anticipate.

Your throat tightens with the shape of impossible choices. You had hoped, perhaps unreasonably, to stitch Ivo's urgency to your vision and make something that could do both. The town has been forced to choose speed over deliberation, and you sit on the edge as if watching a river split—you can see both currents, and you can feel the friction.

[Scene Transition: Privatized Boatyard | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A tall gate with a corporate emblem locks off an alley. A sign is being installed that reads "AUTHORIZED ACCESS ONLY." Beyond the gate, familiar boats sit behind new fencing, gleaming in the wrong way under a cold light. A handful of longshoremen stare as if they have been turned into scenery.]
[Music: A single, descending piano line; the sound of a door closing]
[Sound: The soft rattle of chains; the scrape of a sign being bolted in place]

You stand by the gate with a small group—Maya, Ronan, two old fishermen from the quay—and across from you a security guard holds a tablet and a policy manual. The official voice of 'property rights' administers itself with a blandness that makes anger taste metallic.

Security Guard: "Access is restricted per municipal contract. Authorized users will be notified. Any questions should be directed to the town office."

Maya: "Are you telling me Oba can't bring in his net there? The boat's been there since before these trucks drove in."

Security Guard: "Oba's status will be determined by the access registry." (A polite shrug. He turns away.)

You: "You're looking at the wrong list." (You step forward. The words are narrower than what you feel—an edge you are willing to use.) "Ask who drafted the registry. Ask who benefits when the slips are leased for ten years. Ask why the maintenance clause exempts contractor affiliates from ecological restoration."

The guard blinks. For a moment you feel what it is to name a system that doesn't want to be named. People gather; someone records the exchange on a phone. The footage will be small; it will be eaten by the algorithms that prefer a smiling hard hat to a complex grievance.

Celine arrives later in the afternoon, her scarf damp with sea-spray, and stands with a delegation. She is camera-ready; her smile is the mechanism that keeps administrations functional.

Celine Harrow: "We need order. We built something that will keep homes dry. In a storm, you will all thank us."

You: "Some of us will, yes. Others—" (you swallow) "—others will lose access to their livelihoods because profit dictated parceling of the shore."

Celine Harrow: "We made provisions for 'community transition programs'—we'll work with you on job placement and compensation." (She says it like an anesthetic, as if softening will blunt the pain.)

You: "Compensation doesn't give you back spaces of belonging. Money is pragmatic but it doesn't stitch memory to places. You can't put a price on the tide-chant Oba sings when he lands a catch."

Celine Harrow: "Memory is important. But the town's survival is, too. Hard choices are on my desk, Alys. They don't come with guarantees, only possibilities."

You look at the people assembled: old hands who remember when salt marshes were broad and public; young volunteers whose hands still smell of seedling soil; families who will sleep under a roof because of the wall. The town is physically safer in one count, socially fractured in another. You have both the knowledge and the ache of that arithmetic.

Ivo stands close enough to hear, close enough that your shoulder almost brushes his. He watches the sign as if memorizing its letters. He doesn't speak for a long moment. When he finally does, there is no condemnation—only a tired, pragmatic cadence.

Ivo: "We keep people's roofs over their heads now. I can help with that. I'll mobilize the rescue squads to reinforce the weakest points along the inlet next week. We'll stop flooding where we can."

You: "And when the contractors' maintenance schedule leaves the marsh uncovered? When access is limited and the nursery beds you planted are paved over, will the rescue squads be enough?"

Ivo: "No. It won't be enough. But it keeps people alive in the short run. I can't pretend otherwise."

His honesty lands like a stone. You hate its truth, but you also understand its necessity. There is no villain here who is purely bad; there is only a town pushed into a binary that wasn't meant to hold its complexity.

You think of Oba's cane tapping out the rhythm of tides, of Ronan's rope burns, of Maya's paint-stained apron. The seawall stands; children will sleep in dry beds tonight. But the harbor has been partitioned, clauses signed, and an economic logic has found new footholds along the shore.

You gather what you can: testimonies, a list of potential legal points, the names of sympathetic journalists. Leyna promises to run models; Ronan and volunteers promise to collect evidence; Maya promises to rally the artisans. You organize, you resist, you file. You try to turn grief into labor.

But it is not victory. The wall is finished. The contract is in force. Some slips are leased to vendors from outside, and the "authorized access" gate is closed at dusk.

You stand at the edge of the privatized boatyard as the sun dies behind a cloud bank. Light bled out like something honest that couldn't be saved. The town will not be destroyed—there are roofs, and dry floors, and a future that is safer in measure—but something else has been sold in the process: a sense of common ground. Saltwick's shoreline is less a shared story than a series of fenced plots.

Celine's press releases circulate, framed with curated images of smiling families beside the seawall. The contractors speak of 'efficiency' and 'resilience.' You watch and feel your words becoming longer and more complicated; so are the concessions.

You had hoped that science and civic will could be sewn together into something that protected both the people and the place that made them a people. Instead, speed came dressed as safety. It bought roofs now and a different set of vulnerabilities later.

Maya sits beside you on a low step, her shoulders small but present. Ronan passes out printed lists of affected families. Ivo lingers, a complex figure in the lamplight, neither wholly opposed nor wholly allied. Celine's convoy leaves toward the council house, its lights like a promise and a threat.

Maya: "Do we keep fighting?"

You: (You look at the seawall, at the sign that now reads 'AUTHORIZED ACCESS ONLY.') "We do. We have to. Losing battles doesn't mean giving up the war. It means changing tactics."

Maya: "But we lost something."

You: "We did." (You let the admission be its own small, clean truth.) "We lost access to some places. We lost the simplicity of being able to say 'this belongs to all of us.' We gained roofs, and we gained a harder battle."

You think of the choices you didn't get to make: a slower scaffolded program, a hybrid of ecological labor and immediate shore protection, a municipal contract that enshrined community oversight instead of circumventing it. Those were the plans you lived on in the greenhouse — seedlings of policy and plantings and patient restoration. Today those seedlings are bruised; some have been uprooted.

Yet even bruised seedlings show green.

You gather your notebook—pages are filled with technical notes, a list of witnesses, a scrawl that reads "transparency, oversight, stewardship." The words are a tenuous spine. You will keep writing them. You will keep assembling the tools — legal, social, and scientific — that may yet push back against the privatization of place.

You look at the candles, at the faces around you, and understand the honest cruelty of civic work: you save some things and, in doing so, lose others. The town will not vanish, but it will be different. You cannot unmake the wall; you can, however, file, protest, teach, and keep watch.

You file the grievances, draft a public transparency petition, and plan a memorial for the marshlands that were paved. It is not enough to restore what was lost, but it is a way to keep naming it.
