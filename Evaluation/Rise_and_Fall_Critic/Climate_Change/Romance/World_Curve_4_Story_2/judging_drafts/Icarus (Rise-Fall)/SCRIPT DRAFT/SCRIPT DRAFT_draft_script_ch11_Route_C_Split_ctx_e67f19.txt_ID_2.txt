You stand at the water's edge, boots sinking once in soft mud, and watch the same footage that folded into your mind last night: a juvenile fish threading through the new blades of seagrass, sunlight caught like coin on its flank. The marsh smells of wet peat and salt, clean in a way that feels like a promise rather than a memory.

Your notebook is open in one hand; the dented brass compass presses cool against your palm where it peeks from your jacket. You breathe in rhythm with the marsh. Small things are doing what small things do: holding the edge, trapping sediment, inviting life back.

Oba Kofi is already ahead, cane tapping a patient rhythm against the boardwalk. He nods without flourish when he sees you, the corners of his weathered face softening. Maya is behind him with a thermos and a loaf wrapped in cloth; flour dusts one wrist like a smudge of blessing.

Oba Kofi: "Look how it gathers. Took the current a while, but it found its place."

You: "It did. The mats caught enough to slow the flow. The filaments are knitting soil."

Dr. Leyna Ortiz arrives with her tablet tucked under her arm, breathless from the climb. She doesn't waste a greeting—she swipes, and a waveform blooms: clearer oxygenation numbers, a rise in detritivore counts, a tightening confidence in the model.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "We have three more points above the baseline from last week. Juvenile survival is trending up in the sheltered patches. If we scale this, the feedback loops get faster."

You feel a warm pressure behind the sternum, a small bloom of optimism that is practical and earned, not naïve. Leyna's data is not poetry, but it's the kind of truth that rearranges plans.

Maya: (tipping the loaf) "Neighbors already asking where to sign up. Ivo sent a message this morning, said the volunteers are lining up like tide markers."

You: "Bread and bodies. Good sign."

Oba Kofi: (with a single gruff laugh) "And if someone brings tea, I will preach the gospel of patience."

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: Sunlight slanting through salt-flecked panes; rows of seedlings glow like a miniature forest]
The greenhouse is warm enough to fog your breath away for a moment. Humidity clings to your jacket sleeves; the scent of damp soil and basil rises in a small, domestic rush. Seed trays huddle in racks; hydroponic rigs hum quietly with patient machinery. From the window, you can see the cliff and the angry sea, but inside is a different weather—a cultivated hope.

Ivo is already there, sleeves rolled, oil smudged on his forearms, a battered notebook open beside a coil of rope. He looks at you with that half-smile that softens the world and stiffens the resolve in the same breath.

Ivo: "They brought cedar planking to the north ridge. If we lay it right, it could divert sheet flow away from the lowest rowhouses next month."

You: "That's faster than a full living breakwater, but it keeps people dry now."

Ivo: (shrugs) "People sleeping through the storms are worth a few compromises."

Before you can answer, footsteps come from the greenhouse door—measured, sharp. Celine Harrow steps into the warm air like she was always meant to be here, her coat buttoned despite the heat. Her presence changes the light; her composed manner folds into everything like an argument wrapped in ribbon.

Celine Harrow: (smiling, hands loose at her sides) "You're making beautiful things here, Doctor Maren. News travels faster than I expected."

You feel the word "beautiful" pin you between the seedlings and the view. Her eyes scan the trays with a politician's appraisal—what can be scaled, what can be leveraged.

You: "The pilots are working. Leyna's numbers say we're moving in the right direction."

Celine Harrow: "Which is precisely why I called. Saltwick needs both shelter and recovery. The municipal coffers can be freed up if we accept outside capital—public-private partnerships, co-managed projects. I propose a hybrid municipal plan: immediate protective work to secure homes, coupled with scaled restoration, supervised by a shared oversight board. Ecological covenants would be written into contracts."

Leyna's fingers tighten on her tablet. I see the calculation in her jaw: covenants could protect habitats, but they could also become instruments if oversight is absent.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "Covenants could ensure ecological priorities are locked in. But covenants also require rigid monitoring and independent enforcement. Who watches the watchers?"

Celine Harrow: (tilting her head) "We can set up auditing, independent scientists on the board. Transparency clauses. Funds released only when agreed biophysical milestones are reached."

Ivo: (leaning forward, blunt) "And who signs the deal? Who gets to decide what 'agreed milestones' actually mean when people are standing in water and facing losing everything?"

Celine Harrow: (softly) "The council, with community representation. We can design the governance together."

You feel a familiar knot: the urge to protect those you love now, and the knowledge that rushed contracts can bind futures in ways you can't later unpick. Yet the greenhouse is sun-warmed and full of possibility; the pilot mats are proving a thing can grow when helped.

Maya, hovering by a seed table, watches you both with the blunt clarity of kin who will live whatever comes. Oba Kofi's cane rests against a bench; his eyes watch Celine without surprise, cataloguing.

Oba Kofi: "Money moves like the tide, Alys. It helps, but it can also carry away what you thought fixed."

You: "What I want is simple: that whatever help we accept, the community's voice isn't a footnote."

Celine Harrow: (smiles, practiced) "Of course. My team will draft covenants that require public review, local hiring quotas, and ecological triggers for funds. We avoid the old mistakes—no land grabs, no opaque clauses."

Ivo: (skeptical) "And if a company tries to bend the language? If a future council wants a quick port, are we stuck with our hands tied?"

Celine Harrow: "That's why the covenants are enforceable. There's legal recourse. There's precedent elsewhere."

You listen to the exchange like a tide chart: high points mapped, undercurrents hidden. Leyna steps forward, tapping an image on her tablet—a model overlay showing temporal trajectories if funding is applied versus if it's not.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "With hybrid funds and strict covenants, we can scale restoration sites three times faster. Species return sooner. But the governance complexity doubles the administrative cost and introduces political risk. Without community-anchored oversight, the risk of mission drift is real."

You: (quiet) "So the choice is speed and scale now with strings, or slower, community-first scaling that keeps control local."

Celine Harrow: (gently) "And there are ways to bind the community into the oversight. Training, seats on the board, legal stipulations. We don't have to be adversaries."

Ivo: (under his breath, but clear) "Words matter. Actions matter more."

You press your thumb to the braid of salvaged fishing line at your wrist, feeling its roughness. Your mind catalogs: volunteers lining up, Leyna's numbers, Oba's memory, Maya's bread, the pilot mats glinting like small flags in the marsh. Everything feels like a strand in a net you are trying to cast.

Celine studies you as if weighing your moral ledger.

Celine Harrow: "A hybrid plan is not a takeover. It's an acceleration. Think about the families on North Beach Ridge. Those cedar boards Ivo mentioned—they help now, but if we can scale both simultaneously, we buy time for the living systems to take over."

Ivo: (measured) "If we do both, coordination has to be ironclad. No unilateral sign-offs. No closed-door renegotiations. We need teeth in oversight."

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "We can write thresholds into the contracts—if ecological indicators slip below X, emergency funds reallocate to restoration. If community labor increases by Y, funding unlocks. It can be a hybrid that demands community buy-in."

Maya: (softly) "And if we train neighbors to monitor? If the town learns the jobs of oversight, then it's not just outsiders checking the boxes."

Oba Kofi: "Teach them truth and give them tools. That's how it lasts."

You let their words fold into your chest. There is still risk—political strings can strangle—but there is an energy here you have not felt in years: people, talk, money, science, and a hum of accountability. The combination could be a lever.

Celine's smile is careful and steady; she reaches into her folder and lays out a preliminary timeline, inked with possible funding tranches and proposed governance seats.

Celine Harrow: "If you lead the scientific oversight, Doctor Maren, and Ivo helps design the emergency measures, we present a united front to the council. The town sees action and stewardship together."

You: (feeling the lift of possibility) "A shared plan, with real enforcement for the covenants, public review, and community seats. If the words mean something, it could change the timeline."

Ivo: (looking at you) "You would be co-lead on scientific oversight?"

You: (heart thumping — equal parts responsibility and a strange, warm courage) "I would if the structure guarantees community control and independent auditing. No secret clauses. No bypassing the oversight board."

Celine Harrow: (nods) "Agreed. Transparency clause, independent auditors, community seats with veto power on development actions that affect ecological thresholds."

Leyna watches you with an expression that's half-hopeful scientist and half-protector of fragile truth.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "There's a lot to draft. There will be legal language that matters. There will be cost analyses. But it is possible."

The greenhouse light seems clearer, as if the glass has been wiped and everything is visible. The plants lean toward that light like small congregants.

You think of the pilot mats knitting the marsh and the volunteers getting their hands muddy. You think of people who will sleep easier if their house is not swept out by the next storm. You think of what you cannot afford to lose—your integrity, the town's ownership of its fate.

A sense of momentum gathers — not triumphal, but real. The possibility of a plan that stitches shelter to restoration, speed to ethics, feels within reach. Hope expands in practical increments.

Celine steps back, folding her hands as if closing a small prayer.

Celine Harrow: "So—shall we draft the elements together? Public session next week, then council vote. Saltwick needs to see both compassion and action."

You take a breath. The warm greenhouse air fills your lungs. Seeds wink in trays around you; the ocean a distant drum beyond the panes. The choice ahead is not about whether to save lives or ecosystems. It's about tempo, trust, and the shape of the covenant you are willing to accept.

You know that whatever you choose will set the town's pace. You also know that the moment is alive with possibility — not a trap but a hinge.
