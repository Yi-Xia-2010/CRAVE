[Scene: Embankment | Morning]
[Visual: A low, sodden stretch of reinforced earth; a ragged seam where sand met clay has split, thin water running through it like a vein]
[Music: Sparse, low strings — a single descending motif]
[Sound: Wind cutting across plastic tarps; distant horns from the harbor; the quiet click of camera shutters]

You come up on the embankment with sand under your boots and salt on your breath. Auditors' vans crouch like flat-backed birds against the pale sky. Men and women in hard hats consult tablets; their voices are measured, the tone of people trained to name things and keep their names apart. The official report is in their hands. The word deviations sits in the air and tastes metallic.

You remember sitting on that bale of compressed sand the day before—the compass at your wrist, the thin line in your notebook: "Hold to the shore, hold to one another." It was a promise and a risk. Now the risk has a shape: concrete poured unevenly, aggregate mixed wrong, an engineered seam that split when the tide pressed.

You smell diesel and damp earth; beneath it, the faint sweet rot of seaweed torn from its bed. Your jacket is damp where you leaned against a post. You can feel the salt in the fibers against your skin like an accusation.

Ivo is already here, sleeves rolled, hands raw where rope burned them the night crews stayed late. He doesn't look at you at first—only at the seam, measuring angles with the same farmer's economy you do: count, guess, act. When he finally meets your eyes, there is a hardness there that rides a familiar warmth; you cannot tell whether it steadies you or nicks you.

Ivo: "They sent two auditors and a photographer. The contractors are saying it was a 'localized settlement.' The auditors called it 'systemic deviation.'"
You: "Meaning?"
Ivo: (barks out a short laugh that has no humor) "Meaning they didn't build it to spec. Meaning—" (he presses his palm to a broken sandbag) "—this won't hold like it was supposed to."

You open your mouth to ask if the contractors deliberately economized, but the question lands as a grenade between you and what you want to be true. You do not know if it was malice, negligence, or a cascade of small compromises that nobody wanted to speak about until tide and time forced the language out.

Ronan shuffles up, carrying a coil of wet rope. He looks younger than the morning, as if the water has taken more than the night. He meets you with a kind of blunt loyalty that has always been his: simple, immediate help.

Ronan: "We got people ready to move. Boats at the slip. Oba's got a list of the elders who need priority. Maya's running a relay from the warehouse."
You: "How many?"
Ronan: (hesitates) "Enough that it matters."

Oba appears a step behind Ronan. He walks with his cane, but his shoulders are square. He smells of tobacco and the sea. He puts his hand on the soil like he's gauging the memory of the tide itself.

Oba Kofi: "The sea remembers where we put our trust," he says quietly. "Sometimes it gives it back. Sometimes it shows you where the stitches were tied too loose."
You: (you want to say you're sorry; you want to say you're responsible; instead you say) "We will get people out. We will save who we can."

The auditors are methodical. One of them, a woman with a voice like a bell, reads into her recorder: "Deviation noted in Section 3b: material substitution without approved variance; compaction below specified tolerance; joint reinforcement omitted." The recorder eats the words and leaves nothing for warmth.

Auditor: "We recommend immediate evacuation of adjacent properties pending stabilization. We can prepare a technical summary for Harrow Hall—"
You: "Do it." Your voice is small and exact. "And the town needs to know now."

You can feel the way the ground has changed—how decisions once theoretical have become weight. You walk the length of the breach and see tiny things that feel like verdicts: a child's plastic shovel buried half in wet sand, a fishing net snagged on rebar, the corner of a ledger soaked through with salt. Each object speaks of a life that nearly could be washed away.

[Scene: Harrow Hall | Midday]
[Visual: Sunlight through high windows slants across the council table; faces in the audience are half in light, half in shadow]
[Music: Dissonant piano chords; a slow, descending cello]
[Sound: Murmurs; the crisp rustle of paper; occasional coughing]

Harrow Hall smells like lemon oil and old wood; it smells like civic obligation. The room fills. Cameras line the walls like small, impartial eyes. People you know—people you grew up alongside—sit with the strain of betrayal made public. Some wear the hard lines of anger; some press their palms to their mouths with the shock of the newly bereaved.

Celine sits at the dais with a dossier fanned open in front of her. Her face is composed, practiced at this; she keeps her hands loose on the table, a posture of control. Her eyes flick to you and then away, unreadable—neither accusation nor absolution, only the thin, exacting look of someone who understands what this moment can be used for.

Celine Harrow: "We will cooperate fully with the auditors' findings, and we will secure temporary housing for affected families immediately," she says, measured. "I propose emergency funds to be released for evacuation and stabilization."
You: (you want to let that be enough; you want to believe in the motion of action) "Those funds should be conditional, with oversight. The town cannot rebuild trust without transparency."

A council member—an old friend with a mouth that runs ahead of his brain—leans forward, voice sharp.

Council Member: "So you blame the contractors, you blame the oversight, and you blame the administration? Who is held accountable, Dr. Maren?"
You: "Accountability must be clear," you say. "But answers alone won't make people's houses stand. We need shelter plans, evacuation routes, and independent review."

The room fractures into side conversations. Someone shouts that the embankment was their only defense; someone else says it was a makeshift and better than nothing. You hear your name called—some accuse, some plead—and a thread of something like grief unspools through the chamber.

Ivo rises later, not with the blunt rhetoric of a politician but with the weary, practical speech of a man who has pulled neighbors from water.

Ivo: "We saved lives tonight," he says, and his voice catches. "We did. That matters. But it matters even more that when we put our faith in a wall, we can trust the people who made it. We won't stop building. But we will insist on who gets to build."

His words are a bridge and a blade; some in the hall nod, others look at him as if he's a traitor for saying both. He looks at you when he finishes, and the look that passes between you is complicated: gratitude, accusation, a mirror of shared fatigue.

Maya stands near the back, apron clumped with sand and salt. When she speaks it is less polished and more raw.

Maya: "My kids slept in the school gym. They woke and thought the city had moved. We packed until dawn. You promised we'd be listened to." She points—no breaking of gaze—at the dais. "Listen to us."

The hearing will be a ledger of loss: the auditors' deviations, the contractors' defenses, the council's motions. It will end with a vote to release emergency funds and to commission a full criminal inquiry. It will not end the line of blame.

[Scene: Harbor | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The harbor is a tableau of urgency and small, stubborn heroism — boats bobbing, people carrying crates; ribbons of sun flare off wet metal]
[Music: Slow, resigned strings under a single bright note of solo violin]
[Sound: The slap of rope on cleats; a child's distant cry; the stomp of boots; a gull wheeling and complaining]

The harbor tastes of brackish water and hot engines. You move through it like someone walking through a wound. Rows of small boats are being readied to ferry people to temporary shelters. You help where you can—handing a bag to a neighbor, tying a knot, checking manifests—actions you have rehearsed a thousand times in training but never with this particular ache threaded into them.

A woman clutches a plastic tote with a knitted blanket poking out. Her face is hollowed by shock. You pass her to a volunteer and see, for a moment, the human cost in scales: a blanket, a photograph, a certificate floating on tide-level panic.

Ronan improvises a system at the slip, barking orders in short bursts. He works like someone who cannot sleep until everyone is accounted for. You find Maya near the roped-off loading zone, her face streaked with salt and soot—no more theater in her expression than the rest of Saltwick's resilience.

Maya: (voice breaking) "Some of the older buildings are gone. Mrs. Dorran won't leave her house until someone forces her. Oba says we should move people to the church first."
You: "Do it. Prioritize the elders. Take a manifest. I’ll coordinate with the transport boats."

Oba watches from the head of the pier, leaning on his cane. He lifts his chin and for once words exceed the silence.

Oba Kofi: "You came back for this," he says softly. "You came back to stitch what had unraveled. But stitches can tear. We thread them anew. That is all anyone can do."
You: (you want to tell him the stitches were yours; you want to tell him the compass at your wrist meant more than a sentimental heirloom; instead you nod and let the sea sound fill the space between you.)

Ivo finds you at the boat ramp. For a long time neither of you speaks; the harbor makes the small things bigger: the way his jaw tightens, the way his knuckles are raw. When he finally talks, it's in fragments.

Ivo: "We did what we could. We couldn't—" (he swallows) "—we couldn't stop the seam. We pulled people out of water. We—"
You: "Saved lives." The words are both balm and blade.
Ivo: (looks away) "That doesn't make it right."

You both watch as a child's plastic shovel, the same one you saw at the embankment, floats near the harbor mouth, bobbing like a lonely buoy. Maya steps forward and retrieves it, rinses it off, and while she gives it back a small boy clasps it like a totem. For a moment, a small comic of normalcy: the boy laughs.

The saving of lives sits beside the fracture of trust like two rocks in the same river. They do not cancel each other. They exist. You feel them both settle into you.

The day bleeds into a bruised dusk. Lights ripple off wet surfaces. People transfer from improvised cots to buses bound for neighboring towns. Some leave quietly; others walk with faces set, lips a hard line. There are vows made in the corridorways of Harrow Hall—vows of inquiry, oversight, reparation—but also vows like bitter seeds: accusations whispered, names paired with blame.

At the edge of the pier you stand with Ivo, Oba, and Maya. The town is smaller tonight in one sense and larger in another: smaller because some families are gone; larger because everyone knows exactly what the cost of failure looks like.

You reach into your jacket for the brass compass, feel its ridged ring under your thumb. It is warm from your skin. It doesn't point you to a simple rightness. It points to north, to practice, and to stubbornness. You turn it over in your palm and let the memory of your father sit beside the heavy present.

You think of Celine's composed face at Harrow Hall, unreadable in the way that power often becomes—smoothed and distant. You think of the auditors with their technical language, of contractors with reasons and liabilities. None of these will be the only story told. You know this: the town will have to battle in courtrooms and in opinion, in small kitchens and in cramped shelters. People will demand mouths for their grief.

When the last evacuation boat pushes off, you let out a breath you didn't realize you'd been holding. The harbor swallows sound. You can still see the sheen of seagrass beds near the shallow—thin ribbons of green struggling in the bruised water, their blades bent but not gone.

You have saved people. You have not saved everything.

You think of the word "hold"—how it can mean to steady, to keep, to delay. You think of the notebook line that began all this: "Hold to the shore, hold to one another." Tonight it feels like a command, and a failure, and a plan.

Ivo looks at you once more, an unreadable question in his hazel eyes. Maya touches your arm and squeezes, a small, human pressure. Oba taps the cane twice on the pier and then the sound is gone into the water.

You will face hearings. You will be blamed and praised in the same breath. Houses will be lost; some lives will be saved. The town will fracture, and in the split pieces you will either see ways to mend or reasons to leave.

You fold your hands around the compass and let the harbor night set over you. The wind moves through the rigging, a hollow sound like a last counsel.
