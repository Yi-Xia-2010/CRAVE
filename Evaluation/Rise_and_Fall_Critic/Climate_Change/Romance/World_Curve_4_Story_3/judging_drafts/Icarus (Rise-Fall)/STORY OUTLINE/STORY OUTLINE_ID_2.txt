## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Havenpoint

---

## World View
Havenpoint is a near-future coastal town (2035–2040) cushioning itself against rising seas with a patchwork of civic ingenuity and contested investments. Once a fishing and shipbuilding hub, the town now balances hand-built living shorelines, community gardens, stepped solar terraces, and contentious proposals for engineered seawalls. Politics are a steady negotiation between townsfolk, small-business livelihoods, and outside capital; Councilor Ben Rhee mediates permits and funding, and Lydia Voss brings polished, investor-friendly infrastructure plans. Culture prizes maritime heritage—boatwrights, tide lore, dockside markets—and spiritual continuity embodied by Elder Mae Hargrove. Geography alternates between weathered boardwalks and ruined shipyards, terraces that hum with inverters, and a clinical glass pavilion perched on a bluff where Lydia lays out hard projections. Atmosphere is painterly and tactile: salt haze, filmic lighting, and a muted coastal palette with neon accents at dusk. Religion is local ritual and reverence for the sea rather than formal creed; class tensions emerge where investor money promises quick protection at the cost of displacement. The town’s moral heart is contested: do you preserve place and livelihoods or accept top-down fixes that diminish the town’s soul? Romance threads through adaptation politics, becoming both solace and leverage.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
Icarus in miniature: I rise—Marin Solace, a pragmatic environmental engineer-activist returned to Havenpoint—builds momentum for community-led adaptation. My cooperative’s prototypes, alliances (even with Lydia’s polished machine), and public wins create rapid success: funding, media attention, and a deepening intimacy with Jonah Reyes. The early chapters are a burst of agency and hope—Greenridge pilots thrive; community votes swing our way; Jonah and I begin to imagine a future with the sea intact. But the ascent attracts investors and political pressure; compromises and tactical missteps let Lydia’s engineering-first approach gain leverage. The peak of visibility becomes the precipice: investor timelines, legal manoeuvres, and misrepresented impact studies fracture trust. In the fall, visible success splinters—seawalls approved, tidal ecologies damaged, moorings lost, homes displaced, and my leadership and relationships tested or broken. Across branching routes, the emotional arc is the same: a swift rise to a peak of perceived victory, then a catastrophic crash whose scale and personal consequences vary—bitter compromise, public backlash, resignation into decentralized activism, or the worst-case displacement and cultural erosion. Every path is first-person, intimate, and colored by the salt, grit, and debts of hope.

### Chapter 1: Returning Tides [Rise] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I return to Havenpoint after years away, carrying maps and a satchel of prototypes. The town smells of kelp and diesel; my patched field jacket and sea-glass brown hair are still damp from the ferry wind. I meet Asha at The Saltworks, sense the fragile optimism threaded through the community, and begin cataloguing where living shorelines could buy us time.
* **Visuals:** Wet boardwalk reflections, my steel-blue eyes scanning hydrographs on a rolled map; my bracelet of recycled beads clinks as I set down the satchel; the Saltworks’ seafoam-green corrugated walls glow through mist.
* **Valence:** Positive
* **Arousal:** Medium
* **Branch:** Common
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I smell the ocean before I see it: brine tangled with engine oil, the old dock’s gull-cry threaded through the market’s canvas canopies. My hair—sea-glass brown, the silver strand at my temple catching the light—sticks damp to my neck; my jacket, patched at the elbow, is flecked with salt. I shoulder the rolled site map and feel the satchel’s familiar weight, notebooks rubbing against the enamel lighthouse pin. Asha greets me in a paint-splattered apron, and we walk the Saltworks’ north-facing windows where mist halos hanging planters. The lab is a theater of small interventions: soil trays, marsh-forms, a soldering iron on a bench. I listen to fishermen trade tidal lore outside; Jonah’s voice floats in from the boardwalk—warm, impatient, a laugh edged with worry. Returning feels like putting my hand into a tidepool full of both creatures and sharp shells. I say nothing at first; I map. The town needs solutions that aren’t spectacle. I think of the cooperative’s prototypes and the nights I left to study; I think of the promise I made at my mother’s kitchen table to protect what’s left here. The work begins.

The rain comes like a verdict—hard, immediate, and indifferent. Jonah calls from the dock: his voice breaks when he says the mooring’s gone. I run to the Old Shipyard Ruins and find hulks shifted, barnacled ropes snapping. The press turns from praise to fury; social feeds harrow my face on opinion pieces. Elder Mae’s shawl is wrapped tighter; she speaks in a voice like gravel—said things I had not wanted to hear, about what it means to lose a place. My rise becomes a fall; I taste ash, and the future we’d pictured fractures into sharp, wet shards.

* **Summary:** The peak implodes. A rapid construction timeline, regulatory loopholes, and a surprise storm create catastrophic ecological impacts. Investor-driven seawalls and misapplied mitigation destroy tidal flats; longstanding livelihoods unravel. Jonah sells the repair shop and leaves for seasonal work elsewhere. I am left with public scorn, lawsuits, and the bitter paradox that our visibility hastened the very solutions that harmed the town. It is a hard, public fall.
* **Visuals:** The Old Shipyard half-submerged under a sullen sky, Lydia’s pavilion lit but distant, my satchel empty save for a resignation note, Jonah’s braided rope bracelet left on a bench—my steel-blue eyes red and salt-streaked.
* **Valence:** Negative
* **Arousal:** Very High
* **Branch:** Route C (Alternate Ending — Fall)

The morning after the surge, the town looks like a photograph left in the rain: streaked, blurred, parts missing. The investors’ seawall—erected too quickly, with mitigation promised but poorly enforced—changes the bay’s currents in ways the models had not fully caught. Marshes I walked through a year ago are now flat stretches of black mud; eelgrass beds die back in a single warm week. Jonah sells the repair shop; his voice when he tells me is a distant thing. People I grew up with pack trucks; some boards are nailed shut with words like “For Lease” and “Closed.” The press that once framed me as a visionary now runs op-eds asking whether visibility and compromise are a recipe for the very harm we swore to prevent. Lawsuits arrive; the cooperative’s bank accounts are frozen pending investigation. Lydia’s pavilion is lit like a lighthouse for a harbor we no longer own. I stand on a wet cobble and watch a lone skiff bob, untethered, and feel the ground drop out from under me. The fall is not theatrical so much as comprehensive: reputation, livelihood, love—everything I had climbed for crashes into the water. I am left with a bitter geography of regrets and a map for how not to repeat this elsewhere. The town survives, but the aftershore is quiet and full of ghosts.
