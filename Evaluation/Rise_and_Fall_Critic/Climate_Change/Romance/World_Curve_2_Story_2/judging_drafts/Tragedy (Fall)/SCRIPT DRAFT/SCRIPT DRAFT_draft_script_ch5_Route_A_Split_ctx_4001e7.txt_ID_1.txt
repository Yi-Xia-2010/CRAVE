[Scene: Hana's Diner | Late Evening]
[Visual: Warm steam fogs the window; neon sign outside pulses a tired blue. The diner's interior is a patchwork of mismatched stools, a counter polished by decades of elbows, and a single ceiling fan that moves the humid air in a slow, tired circle.]
[Sound: Low radio playing an old seafaring ballad, the hiss of the espresso machine, occasional clink of cutlery. Outside, distant gulls cry and the sea taps a steady rhythm against the seawall.]
[Music: Sparse piano under a low cello; a persistent minor chord that never quite resolves]

You push the door open and the little bell above it makes a sound that feels like permission. The draft wraps around your jacket and the tide-chart scarf tight against your throat. The draft contract is folded flat against your palm; the top page bears a dull ring of coffee where Hana's hand must have slipped as she made your cup. Ink and corporate language blur into one—precise verbs riding on a hunger you can taste as salt on the air.

Hana looks up from behind the counter, flour on her apron, and gives you one of those smiles that tries to smooth the edges of worry.

Hana: "You're late, stubborn, and you look like you swallowed a gale. Sit. You need soup, not legalese."

You set the paper down on the counter between you as if it were an actual thing that might bleed. The contract's weight is smaller than the feeling behind it, and yet—every clause is a little lever. Every lever will pivot someone's life.

You: (inside) You can still feel Lux's immaculate smile. "Save people," she said. The phrase has no edges. It is a bright thing that refuses to say whose lives will be remade to make room for others.

Hana reaches for the contract with fingers dusted in flour, hesitates, and then pushes it back toward you with the same protective gesture she uses for bowls of stew.

Hana: "You read your own handwriting better than any lawyer will. But tell me—are you taking it? That's the thing that keeps the whole town awake."

You can hear the friction in her voice—friend, diner-owner, heartbeat of Harborfall. You think of Tomas on the pier, his hands like maps of old storms; you think of the line of small coves that have names no investor map bothers to include.

Hana waits on the counter with two steaming mugs and a look that says she'd break Lux's knees if it would keep Harborfall from losing its stories.

The bell over the door jingles again. Kaito arrives with the rain still in his hair and salt on the cuffs where circuitry and brine have rubbed together—vials and notes peeking from a satchel, a sensor pendant blinking faintly beneath his collar. He leans on the counter as if the world were a machine he was momentarily repairing.

Kaito: "I saw Lux's name on the message header. Thought I'd find you here. Hana, you have the usual? And Maya—"

He drops into the stool beside you, sea-green eyes skimming the contract, then returning to you as if searching for the place where policy meets people.

Kaito: (softly) "Tell me what you're thinking, not the legalese. Tell me who stays."

You: "Who stays is exactly the question."

Your voice cracks in a way you don't show them, that small fissure formed by years of keeping people safe by being the one to plan. Kaito's hand hovers near the contract without touching it, as if proximity might warm or scar it.

Kaito: "Lux's timeline is efficient. She can begin construction fast if you sign. That buys lives—literal, measurable lives saved this decade. But the footprint…"

He pauses, searching for the right words.

Kaito: "The footprint will change how people live. Some coves might be cut off, access rerouted. There's also the quiet stuff—easements, shared ownership clauses that slowly reassign stewardship. Legally subtle things that end up being whole neighborhoods priced out."

You picture Tomas' hands—gnarled fingers, callused palms threading nets like knots in the town's memory. You feel the brother's watch heavy around your wrist, its steady ticking like a small, relentless count.

Tomas appears at the far corner, as if called by the smell of soup or the scent of consequence. He doesn't come in like a man ready to argue. He comes in like the tide: inevitable and patient. He settles into a booth, folds his hands over his knees, and watches. His look is steady—years flattening emotion into attention. You cannot unread the lines on his face, but you cannot read them either; his silence is a kind of verdict that refuses simplification.

Tomas: (gravelly) "Any plan that remembers the nets is a start. Any plan that forgets them will be remembered for its omission."

Kaito leans forward, earnest and a little pleading.

Kaito: "Maya, there's another path. Slow, but durable. Sensors, pilot berms, nature-based buffers—give us time to prove a model that can be scaled without swallowing the coves. Grant us funding and we can protect more than sandbags. We can protect livelihoods."

You look at him. He trusts data; he trusts you more. That trust feels like a fragile instrument—accurate but easily bent.

You: (inside) The town needs something it can trust. You need to believe you can hold both people's lives and their ways of life. But you also remember storms that rubbed the town raw. You remember a brother who never came back.

Kaito's jaw tightens. He knows the calculus is cruel: either you buy speed at the cost of place, or you buy place at the cost of time.

Kaito: "If you force Lux to accept legally-binding governance clauses—local oversight, binding impact assessments, compensation guidelines—she may stall. Investors might pull. She might walk. Or—if you leak the contract now, show the clauses to everyone, public scrutiny could shame the worst of the language into change. But that also risks scaring off capital we need."

Hana slaps the counter once, a quick punctuation.

Hana: "You keep talking the roads they'll take, but what about the people walking them? We can't let 'what-ifs' become excuses for doing nothing. Whatever you do—remember that it's the town that's stuck with the mess or the miracle."

The diner's radio fades into the background; the piano and cello swell and sigh. You feel tired in a way the sea knows: eroded at the edges, patient in the center.

Kaito: (after a beat) "I can rework some of this—if you want me to push back. But we should be honest: Lux's team is good at contracts. Their language is leverage. You need conditions that are enforceable, not just promises."

You feel the weight of being the person who turns promises into law or scars. You imagine the forum tomorrow: faces lit by phone screens, hands that have known both bread and loss, a mayor who will balance political survival against moral survival. You think of the way Lux's smile had been both kind and clinical—an example of care that prefers the sweep of the map to the bend of a shoreline.

Hana leans close, voice lower, all diner-owner and confidante.

Hana: "If you sign tonight, it's quick, it's tidy for some. If you insist on governance, you tie them with your own knots and pray those knots hold. If you leak it—you make them public and force a hand. You pick poison with care."

You: (inside) You are tired. Every breath tastes of salt and negotiation; every thought is a ledger. You have carried too many maps of other people's futures. The town wants you to decide. You know that choice will create wounds, not erase them.

The light above the counter throws a thin halo across the contract. The page edges are sharp, like the decision itself.

You fold your hands around your coffee as if a warmth could rearrange the legal verbs. The watch on your wrist ticks. The diner hums around you—the small, ordinary persistence of the town. Outside, Harborfall waits with every house a tiny demand.

You open your mouth to answer and find that words fracture into fewer, harder things. There are three ways forward, each a kind of sacrament:
