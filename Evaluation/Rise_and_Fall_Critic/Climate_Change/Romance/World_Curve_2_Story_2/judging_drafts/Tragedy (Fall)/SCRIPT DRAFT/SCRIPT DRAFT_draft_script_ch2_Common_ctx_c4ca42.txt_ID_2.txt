[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Afternoon]

[Visual: Salt-smeared glass panes streaked with rain, sunbeams cutting through mist into a patchwork of green. Close on a clipboard edge, watercolor stains on a tide-chart scarf.]

[Music: Low, piano undercurrent with distant, uneasy cello]

You push the greenhouse door open and the smell hits you first — a briny, loamy warmth that feels almost indecent when the town below smells of diesel and fog. Steam rises from soil warmed by the sun; a fly hums and then vanishes between a line of seedling tags. Your fingers close around the clipboard until the stamped metal edge bites into your palm. The dive watch at your wrist ticks in a cadence you have learned to obey.

Kaito looks up before you reach the center bench. His sleeves are rolled; the pendant with its blinking sensor hangs against his chest like a quiet heart. For a moment his sea-green eyes meet yours and you feel, absurdly, like the greenhouse's warmth is aimed at you both.

Kaito: "You're on time." (half-smile, but his hands are stained, and he lets them show)

You: "Always." (your voice is steadier than you feel; you lower the clipboard to reveal the tide-chart scarf wound around your neck)

Dr. Noor wheels a stack of datapads forward, their screens blinking in diminished light. Tomas's notes are laid out in careful columns, a coffee ring bleeding into the margin. Hana's cookies sit under a jar lid; the sugar smells like home and something you can trust.

Tomas: "You look tired, Maya." (rough, not unkind; his eyes fix on your hands) "We don't have forever."

You: "I know." The words are small and true. Your thumb drums the edge of the clipboard. "We need to be clear about trade-offs before the mayor walks in."

[Sound: The greenhouse vents sigh; a gull cries far below]

The door clicks again and Mayor Elena steps in, blazer sharp against the greenhouse's unruly green. There is that particular tiredness in her face — a ledger of votes and budgets — and she exhales like someone trying to keep heat from freezing.

Mayor Elena: "Thank you for gathering on such short notice." (formal, then softer) "Walk me through it, Maya. Briefly."

You nod and scan the room. Each pair of eyes carries a different demand: Tomas's weight, Kaito's careful hope, Noor's clinical neutrality, Hana's steady faith. You clear your throat.

You: "Berms and mangrove transplants are slow work. They require house plots to be set back, coordinated volunteer labor, years of care. They preserve fishing coves, eel-grass beds, the things that make Harborfall Harborfall. The cost is time and, in the short term, exposure."

You feel the choice in your chest but you make both — because this meeting needs both stories to be heard. You move from names into graphs, and the greenhouse becomes an amphitheater of trade-offs: a map of berms scrawled next to a simulated cross-section LuxCorp projected last week.

Dr. Noor: "Ecologically, the transplants increase shoreline resilience over decades. The model improves with local stewardship input. But if a major surge hits in five years, the berms alone won't be enough."

Kaito: "Which is why I proposed sensor pilots." (he taps at his pendant, it blinks) "Small, distributed tide and structural sensors plus early-warning systems can buy time. They inform managed retreat of vulnerable plots and let us phase habitat work where it matters."

Mayor Elena: "Sensors don't stop a wall from being built." Her voice is a knife softened with regret. "Investors consider a visible structure a political win. LuxCorp's plan is fast, it snaps the optics into place."

You: "And it snaps the landscape too." The words are sharper than you meant. You feel the image form in the room's air: a chrome wall cutting off cove access, boats shadowed by concrete. Tomas's jaw tightens.

Tomas: "You can't cut the coves off and call it protection. The sea is our life." His voice trembles, not with age, but with anger.

Hana moves a plate of cookies closer to you as if grounding you with sugar. She says something you can't find the taste for answering yet.

Kaito leans forward. "There might be a middle path. Limited engineered structures only where critical infrastructure is at risk, paired with boosted habitat corridors and community maintenance programs. Fund the pilots, prove within a season that sensors reduce false alarms and targeted interventions, then scale organically."

Dr. Noor: "That's the hybrid model I've been simulating. Statistically plausible. Requires phased funding and—"

Mayor Elena interrupts. "Plausible doesn't get me the emergency funds LuxCorp is offering tomorrow. Their terms include matching federal relief — immediate construction. The council wants a solution they can point to."

You feel the room constrict. Every breath seems to catch on the glass and salt in your lungs.

You: "If we take LuxCorp's money, we fast-track protection but at a cost I won't pretend we can ignore. We lose access to traditional fishing zones, small plots will be consolidated, cultural sites could be buried under armor. Our town changes."

Mayor Elena: "We might lose people if we don't act fast, Maya."

You: "I understand that. I don't want anyone out on the flats at the next storm." Your jaw tightens. "But a wall that erases what we are isn't protection; it's relocation by another name."

Kaito reaches out a hand, an attempt to bridge the sound between urgency and preservation. "Let me pilot three sensor nodes at the cove and one at the eastern berm. Give us ninety days and discretionary funds. We run the data in real time to the emergency dashboard. If the data shows imminent risk to neighborhoods, we prioritize targeted structural reinforcement, not wholesale sealing."

Mayor Elena folds her hands. "Where does the money come from for the pilots?"

You glance at Tomas, at Hana's cookie-crumbed fingers, at Dr. Noor's neutral face. You hear your brother's ghost in the tick of your watch, an old storm that taught you how useless promises can be.

You: "Crowdfund, municipal allocation, emergency reserve, and—" You stop because the amber blink in your pocket pulses against your thigh like a heartbeat.

You let the amber light stay where it is, dim and urgent, a private metronome. The greenhouse seems suddenly smaller; the sun slants as if the day has tilted on an axis you hadn't noticed.

Mayor Elena: "We are a week from a council vote. LuxCorp's lobbyists are already here. They'll frame inaction as negligence."

Tomas: "And acting without the town will be the same."

Noor: "The models show the hybrid reduces expected losses by a margin, but only if community labor and maintenance are guaranteed. Without that, the efficiency drops."

Kaito presses his palms together, the motion a bridge between data and plea. "We need the mayor to stall the contract by ninety days, long enough for pilots to run and for community meetings to secure commitment. If the data supports it, we can present concrete numbers that make sense to the council and to LuxCorp's funders."

Mayor Elena: "I'm accountable to voters and to the emergency fund board. Sixty days might be on the table, but ninety is steep. LuxCorp won't wait if they sense hesitation."

You feel the hinge of the room shift toward disaster. The fear in your chest tastes like the salt on your scarf. You find your voice and use it anyway.

You: "Give us sixty days. If the pilots show even marginal improvement in warning accuracy and targeted mitigation, I'll convene a public session and present the data. If not, we accept the rapid deployment and I'll help the transition to preserve as much as we can."

Your promise lands in the greenhouse like an offering and a threat. It is honest — you mean to keep it — and it stings because promises have weight.

Tomas studies you for a long moment, then nods once. Kaito exhales, a thin sound you take to be relief. Dr. Noor taps her pad, recording the condition as protocol. Hana touches your sleeve and squeezes, her eyes full of steady faith.

Mayor Elena looks at you, and for a blink her politeness cracks into something fatigued and raw.

Mayor Elena: "Sixty days. You understand the political risk, Maya. If this fails, people will blame whoever promised them hope instead of armor."

You: "I know." The watch at your wrist ticks loud as accusation. In your head your brother's voice slips under the noise: Promise less than you can give. Keep the rest.

[Sound: A distant rumble — a ferry's engine or the first low roll of storm — the greenhouse windows tremble faintly]

For a moment everything is a set of balances — time, faith, data, lives. Lux's shadow hangs in the projections on the glass and also in the tilt of the mayor's jaw. You feel the clock tighten around your ribs.

You breathe, tasting earth and salt and a long, cold fear. You hear your own heartbeat over the watch. The weather alert in your pocket blinks amber again, in time with your pulse, with the town's small, fragile drum.

You promise sixty days, and the words are both a lifeline and a leash.

[Visual: Close on your watch, second hand catching sunlight, then on the tide-chart scarf against your chest]

[Music: Single cello note held, unresolved]

You look at the faces around you — at the brittle hope, the measured optimism, the worry lines — and know that whatever comes next will be measured in storms and apologies, in hands folded in hunger and in small victories that taste like salt.

You step back from the bench, the clipboard heavier than it was when you came in. The greenhouse hums with plants exhaling. Outside, Harborfall keeps breathing, gulls circling the gray edge.

A siren somewhere indistinct chirps; your pocket blinks faster.

You want to say more, to make the promise softer, truer, less likely to break. But the sun is moving; the council clock is counting down. The decision you've set into motion begins to fold outward without you.

You rest a hand on the doorframe as if trying to keep the world from slipping.

[Music: Piano trailing into silence]

You feel the page of the day turn under your thumb. You can still hear the watch and your brother's ghost and the faint, impossible hope that sixty days will be enough.
