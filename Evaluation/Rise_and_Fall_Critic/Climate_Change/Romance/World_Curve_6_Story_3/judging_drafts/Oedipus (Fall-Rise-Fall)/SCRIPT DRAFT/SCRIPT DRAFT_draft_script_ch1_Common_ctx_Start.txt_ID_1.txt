[Visual: Wide shot of the harborfront in misty dawn light — peeling seafoam paint, puddled cobblestones, shuttered windows]
[Sound: Distant gulls, low thrum of a harbor engine, soft clink of mooring lines]
[Music: Sparse piano with a low cello undercurrent]

You step off the bus and the air takes you like a hand — cold, salt-bright, and stubbornly familiar. Your trench is still damp from the spray. The yellow hood curls at your shoulder like a promise you haven’t yet kept. You close your fingers around the leather of your field notebook until the ridge of the cover leaves a crescent in your palm. The hydrophone hangs against your ribs in its canvas sling, a small weight that remembers tide maps and late-night recordings.

Something is missing where everything should begin. The storefront you grew up under is a hollowed frame now — the awning gone, its lopsided sign a memory eaten by mildew. Puddles collect in the lip of the curb and hold, for a beat, the quartered sky: a broken reflection of your face and the empty glass where display mannequins used to stand.

You run your thumb across the tarnished compass pendant at your throat until the metal grows warm. It doesn’t tell you a direction anymore; it tells you which way you’ll have to look.

The phone in your pocket vibrates with the kind of bluntness you have learned not to ignore. You slide it out. A flood advisory banner folds the screen: SURGE WATCH — 24 HRS. Beneath it, a single message from Noah threads the alert: "We can make sensors sing. Talk tonight?" No emojis. No qualifiers. Practical, as always. Your breath catches and turns into determination — then pinches into doubt.

You should be glad to be back. Instead, the harbor hums like a held breath.

[Visual: Mid-shot of the narrow slope toward the Kelp Gardens; stalls line the way, tarps and ropes and morning steam]
[Sound: Shuffling footsteps, a metallic scrape from a stall being readied, someone humming under their breath]
[Smell: Diesel, wet rope, kelp slim and damp; the faint sweetness of frying empanadas from a vendor]

Elena waves from the next stall over — paint-splattered overalls fluorescent against the grey. She always goes loud when she’s trying to be gentle. You haven’t heard her call your name in three years. Her grin is the same: stubborn and small and so close to weary it hurts.

Elena: "Mara Evans — of course you'd pick the dawn to return and make everyone feel guilty."

You consider replying with a joke. Instead you rub your sleeve and let silence outline what guilt can’t say.

You: "I thought I'd try the quiet hours first."

Elena: (leaning on a crate) "Quiet? This place doesn't do quiet. Not with that billboard staring down every morning." She tilts her chin toward a crane farther along the waterfront, where a glossy billboard blazes with Arman Kade’s smiling blue eyes and the slogan—MODERN SHORELINE. SAFE FUTURE. The image is too clean for these weathered streets.

Elena: "He's taking a city-wide ad budget now? Cute." (Her mouth tightens.) "You hear from anyone at the Lab yet? Or are you going to rebrand us all from the ground up before lunch?"

You: (half-smile) "The Resilience Lab sent a letter. Funding footnotes and a polite stapled uncertainty. They want models and a pitch deck."

Elena: (scoffs) "Footnotes. Of course. Footnotes write eviction notices when no one’s looking."

Your chest constricts. You remember the night the storefront folded in on itself: wind like a fist, water climbing the staircase. You remember hands — yours and Tomas’s and neighbors’ — hauling salvaged boxes beneath a sky that ripped open. You remember promising yourself you would not let it happen again. Every memory is an eddy that tightens into the same knot of responsibility.

You move down the slope, the market narrowing into the floating patchwork that is the Kelp Gardens. The platforms bruise the surface of the water into darker shapes; someone has strung up lanterns that still hold last night's bioluminescent glow. Tomas stands at the edge of a modest terrace, a battered cap pulled down and white stubble catching the morning light like salt on rope. He squints at the horizon as if the sea might have grown a face and be speaking to him.

Tomas: (gravel-rough) "You're up early, child. Thought you’d sleep the sea-sickness out of you."

You: "Old habits. And the advisory." You say surge advisory and it sounds like a reprimand.

Tomas: (turning to you slowly) "Storms take what they like. People forget that sometimes — the sea doesn't bargain." He rests a hand on a kelp line and fingers it with the care of someone pausing an old story. "We plant and weave so the water might give back. The big walls, the ones they sell on billboards... they keep something safe, but they also keep something out."

You pause — that is the sentence your plans revolve around; your living seawall needs to keep people in, not push them away.

Tomas: (with a small, sideways smile) "You look like you carried the map here. Don't let it be all maps and no listening, Mara. The reef remembers what the council forgets."

You feel the weight of his look: not doubt about your competence, but a caution about the cost of certainty.

Elena: (butting in) "And while you're listening, can you listen to the businesses on this block? Those shipping containers by the promenade? Tomas, we need more kelp lines, but we also need a real conversation with Cass before she signs anything."

You: (trying to fold plans into words) "I've been sketching a phased approach. Living modules anchored to the old pier, terraced access for vendors, kelp nursery expansion that doubles as a soft break—"

Elena: "Phased is good. Phased sounds reasonable. The city likes reasonable, then turns it into a checkbox."

Tomas: "Reasonable doesn't feed folks the night after a storm."

Your notebook opens on its own while you talk — habit more than intent — and you trace module shapes in the margins: floating walls of entwined kelp and timber, sensor pockets for Noah's devices, terraces that step down to a public edge. Funding footnotes hover like a line of salt on your tongue.

Noah's text buzzes against your thigh again before you can decide. You glance at the screen: "We can make sensors sing. Talk tonight?" It is a promise and a plea in one short line. The word sing makes you think of keening tides and delicate frequencies mapped into something useful, something humane. It should lighten you. Instead, it only sharpens the sense of marching time.

You fold the phone back into your jacket and the harbor takes over your senses: gull screams, the slap of water against a floating barrel, a distant siren that might be a drill or might be an alarm. The billboard with Arman's face catches a stray beam of sun; the image is too perfect, a photographic smile that doesn't know how to be tired.

You remember Arman as a rumor before he was a man on a sign: a man who tells people efficiency is compassion. You feel a ripple of anger — rational, hot — and beneath it, another, slo wer current: fear. What if the city's resources go to the quick-fix wall that seals people out? What if your living wall is labeled idealistic and left to rot in a council footnote?

Elena: (quiet now) "You should get to the Lab, Mara. They want numbers. They want models. They want to know whether our idea can be sold as 'safe.'"

You: "And if they don't like what we design?"

Elena: "Then you make them like it. Or you make people like it. There's a difference."

You want to tell her that sometimes making people like something feels like coercion. That sometimes making the city like something means trading a few families' backyards for a clean line on a map. But you don't. Instead you press your forehead to the cool metal railing and listen to the water breathe.

Tomas: (softly) "Don't hold the shore hostage to your guilt, Mara. Use it."

His words land not as accusation but as permission: permission to be strategic with grief.

You outline the plan in your head again, faster now — living seawall modules knitted with kelp, staged nursery expansion to seed the terraces, sensor collars that feed Noah's models so tides read like language. You imagine public terraces where children wobble on the edge of salt and laughter instead of boarded windows. You imagine conservation turned into livelihoods, not displacement.

But the Resilience Lab's letter, the bureaucratic edges of funding, and Arman’s glossy hand promise collisions you aren't sure you can steer around. The harbor morning folds into the harried rhythm of people starting their day; a crate slides and someone curses under their breath. You tuck the hydrophone closer, a talisman and a tool, and you walk the line toward where the Kelp Gardens gather into a community of hands and rope and stubborn hope.

You take one last look at the billboard as you step beneath the creak of an old pier. The eyes on the advertisement are unreadable — the expression a polished public face that conceals arrangements you haven't yet seen.

You let the harbor sounds fill you again. Gulls, engines, rope against wood. Each noise a wire connected to a decision waiting downstream.

You breathe; the knot of responsibility is no less tight, but it has an outline now — a plan with margins and footnotes and a meeting tonight at the Resilience Lab.

[Visual: Close on your hand tightening around the notebook; a smear of graphite on your thumb]
[Music: Cello deepens, a low sustained note that implies coming pressure]

You want to stay and walk every pier, gather every neighbor, prove every model. You also know the calendar is an unforgiving thing. The meeting tonight might be the thin hinge between being proactive and being boxed out.

You let that thought sit at the base of your skull like another small tide.

[Visual: The Kelp Gardens recede into morning activity as you lift your chin]
[Music: The piano becomes a single, unresolved chord — a question]

You start walking toward the Resilience Lab, the plans in your mind like a map drawn in quick strokes. You do not know yet if your return will save the places you love, or simply buy time.

You think of Noah's promise to "make sensors sing" and that single sentence germinates into something both hopeful and fragile. You think of Elena's paintless sigh and Tomas's reef-memoried caution. You think of Arman's smile on a billboard and how the city might fold around it.

A gust of wind carries salt and a ruffled paper — a campaign flyer perhaps — and it slaps against your leg like a reminder: the clock started before you arrived.
