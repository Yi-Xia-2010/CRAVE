[Scene: East Strand Harborfront | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide, wet cobblestones reflecting pale light; peeling seafoam paint on shuttered storefronts]
[Sound: Low gull calls, distant hydraulic thumps of crane arms, the soft slap of gentle waves against a battered pier]
[Music: Sparse piano with low, sustained strings — melancholic undercurrent]

You step off the last bus and let the harbor air take you. It smells of salt and diesel and an old, green rot you learned to recognize as a child: kelp and driftwood and the copper tang of floodwater settling into wood. Your trench is damp at the shoulders; the yellow hood is rolled and tucked because the dawn is only a promise of storm. The compass pendant against your throat is cool; you rub the tarnish between your thumb and forefinger until the chain leaves a faint, familiar groove at your collarbone.

The storefront where your mother sold hand-forged lures and lamp oil is a memory folded into reflections. Puddles scrawl the faces of buildings; the puddles keep what the paint has given up. You walk the slope slowly, notebook pressed to your ribs, hydrophone in its sling against your hip like an old talisman. The notebook is full of margins—sketches, tide notes, angry underlines where funding amounts were reduced to footnotes. Your handwriting trembles in one corner and then steadies into lists: modular living wall, kelp nursery expansion, public terraces on buoyant frames. You feel the knot in your stomach tighten and sharpen. Responsibility sits there like a stone.

Elena's stall is a bright smear against the washed palette of the promenade: crates of hand-painted signs, jars of pickled greens, a half-finished mural across a plywood panel. She spots you before you reach her—paint-splattered overalls waving like a flag that refuses to quit—and grins the easy, stubborn grin you've always known.

Elena: "Look at you. Thought you'd sent yourself a postcard instead of coming back for real." (She ducks under a string of bunting to reach you.)

You: "I sent myself the weather report and the Resilience Lab's polite version of 'not yet'." (You let a dry laugh out that tastes like seawater.)

Elena: "Polite? That's their euphemism for 'we need more data and fewer faces in person.' I got the notice about the surge advisory." (She motions to your phone with a thumbstuck smear of ultramarine paint.) "Twenty-four hours?"

You: "Twenty-four hours." You flip the phone open and read the terse advisory again, then slide it shut like you can close it on the horizon. "They're calling it a 'surge advisory,' not a storm, like words can change the water."

Elena studies the marina as if it might be embarrassed into being different. "You're here early. I saw the billboard on the way in." She nods toward the crane-line where a new banner flaps, metallic and precise: ARMAN KADE — BUILDING SECURITY. (The contractor's smile is the color of polished brass, all teeth and angle.) "His face is getting frequent flier miles around here."

You: "He bought billboards before he bought permits." The comment comes out sharper than you intend. Your fingers go to the compass pendant again, as if you can find a bearing with a touch.

[Visual: Close-up on your worn notebook — sketches of interlocking living modules, notes in the margins about community workshops; a pressed kelp blade stuck between pages]
[Sound: A text alert — soft, nearly polite]

[Direction Cue: Show phone screen with a message preview]
[Text on screen: Noah: "We can make sensors sing. Talk tonight?"]

You watch the preview and let the words sit. Noah's message is small, efficient—promising possibility—but it carries the weight of a question you already know the answer to: can tech bought and patched fit into the slow, messy work of a neighbourhood that remembers every loss? You think of Noah's hands fiddling with a sensor, the way he laughs when a prototype finally blinks the right color. You also think of grant cycles and timelines and the neat spreadsheets that can swallow a plan whole.

Tomas is at the rail where the promenade gives into moorings, every crease of his weathered face catching the dawn. He squints at the horizon like trying to read a story the sea hasn't finished writing. Dew beads on his white stubble. His sweater smells of fish and wood smoke, and his cap is the same battered cap you remember from lessons on how to tie nets without watching the tide take them.

Tomas Belmar: "You back early, eh?" (His voice is the slow oar stroke of the old ways.)

You: "Couldn't sleep. Came to see how the bay remembered me." You step closer; your boots make wet, soft sounds on the cobble. "How's the nursery doing?"

Tomas Belmar: "Rows of rope, more green than I've seen in years. But the moorings are tired. Storms are lighter on memory, heavier on lineage." He taps the edge of the pier as if warning it not to forget its own weight.

You feel the old responsibility flare—like a wick you forgot to wet. "If we expand the kelp beds and stitch them to living modules, we can slow the current, break wave energy... give people places to fish and work and not be priced out." You know your enthusiasm reads like a plan being born and a plea being offered in equal measure.

Tomas Belmar: "Plans are fine. People need places to keep what matters." (He fixes you with a look that says he remembers the storefront and your mother.) "Don't let it become paper that waves in the wind."

Your throat tightens. You remember you were sixteen the night the last big surge took the storefront—a board that still smells faintly of varnish if you breathe the right way. The memory is a slap wrapped in salt water. It hardens your resolve but also presses damply against your ribs. You draft a quick margin note: "Prioritize livelihoods + access. PI: community co-design." The pencil feels like a small, stubborn anchor.

(You allow yourself the choice and the small relief of choosing. The harbor doesn't judge; the tides answer with the same impartial, necessary motion.)

You move toward the kelp gardens along the floating platforms, boots angled carefully on the gangway. The platforms rock with a slow, honest rhythm; hands—callused and patient—pull lines taut and tuck seedlings into nurseries. The air changes: less diesel, more brine and the damp, living scent of kelp and loam. The greenhouse planks creak as someone shifts a tray; laughter bubbles—brief, bright—out of a group repairing a seedling basket.

Priya is there, tablet glowing in the muted morning light. She looks up from a spreadsheet and raises an eyebrow, the posture of someone who believes in facts as armor but knows they must be made to sing for people.

Priya Anand: "You brought the hydrophone. Good. We need the baseline recordings before the next tide—can't model without them." (She taps a line on the tablet.) "Also—there's a note from the Lab. Bureaucratic smile attached."

You: "Bureaucrats, the lesser-known tidal force." You hand the hydrophone over, but your fingers linger. The hydrophone vibrates faintly with the harbor's low note—a felt, underwater hum like a city breathing. The sound is almost prayer.

Priya: "They approved seed funding for pilots—but with conditions. Matching funds, timeline revisions, and partial oversight by the contractor's office." She doesn't say Arman's name, but you both carry the same understanding like a barge carrying heavy cargo.

Your mouth goes dry. The bureaucratic footnotes have the appetite of an undertow—quiet, then all-consuming. You can imagine how Arman's teams will love 'partial oversight.' They'll nominate certainty while redirecting the promise of community control into an annex on a glossy plan.

Noah's message buzzes again—shorter now: "Tonight? Lab meeting. Bring that hydrophone. I can demo the sensor sync." You want to answer with everything: the trust you feel, the wariness at letting tech meet people whose futures are at stake, the fear that your best intention will be flattened into a deliverable. You instead tuck the phone away. Conversation continues.

Elena: "Don't let them cage the nursery in legal language." (Her voice is small and fierce.) "They'll give you a ribbon and call it participation."

You: "We codify community ownership into the contract." You say it like a plan and a prayer at once. "Public access, shared governance clause, transparent data. Noah's sensors can be part of an open archive—no proprietary lock." You know how fast Noah can build bridges between code and field data; you also know how fast private money can make a bridge one-way.

Priya: "Maps and clauses are one thing. Enforcement is another. We can build models, designs—" She taps the tablet with a short emphasis. "—but you, Mara, will need political playbooks and allies. Cass will listen, but she's on a razor with re-election next year and developers hollering in her ear."

The mention of Mayor Cass brings the political geography into focus. Somewhere between the kelp beds and the city banners, power shifts like weather—sometimes predictable, sometimes brutally sudden.

Tomas Belmar: "You always had the stubborn of your mother." (He lets that sit between you.) "Stubborn's good. Use it smart."

You let the harbor sounds fill the short silence: a gull's ragged call, the distant click of a winch, the soft slap of kelp against floats. The prediction of the surge is a small printed line on your phone; the real prediction is that people will look to leaders and plans and promises and judge them by who keeps homes and who builds walls.

[Music: Strings tighten, a single mournful violin line]

You look back up the slope. The billboard's grin seems to approve of efficiency above everything else. You wonder at the cost measured in displaced doorways, in murals scraped bare, in livelihoods turned into line items. The knot in your chest doesn't loosen; it reconfigures into a different tool—a blade sharpened into resolve.

You tuck the notebook back into your coat and let your fingers trace the compass. The storm advisory is a clock. Your to-do list is a map. Noah's message waits like a hinge: tonight's meeting could be a place to align, to make sensors and soil and policy hum together; it could also be a place where compromises creep in dressed as necessity. You can already feel the tension: how much do you ask of your neighborhood, how much of your own heart are you willing to stake on a plan that might buy time but not permanence?

Tomas's hand falls on the rail near yours, rough and warm, an anchor that has kept more than nets afloat. He doesn't say anything new; he doesn't have to. His face holds the long, patient judgement of someone who has watched tides alter fortunes and still kept calling people back to the mooring.

You breathe in the harbor—salt, kelp, diesel, and the faint, hopeful trace of paint—and you prepare to move. The Resilience Lab awaits with forms, with official stamps and with people who will tell you precisely how much of a dream is fundable. Behind it, the city council and Arman's machines press. Ahead, a meeting tonight where Noah will demo, Priya will bring models, Cass may listen depending on who called her at breakfast.

You step from the platform back toward the promenade, the community's breath around you like a mantle. The sky has thickened; light is a bruise. You can feel the surge advisory turning from line on a screen into a beating drum in the streets.

[Visual: Close-on Mara's hand over the compass; the camera pulls back to show the kelp gardens and the billboard in a single frame]
[Music: The violin resolves into a steady, unresolved cadence—an invitation to move forward despite uncertainty]

You let the harbor sound into you one more time: gulls, distant engines, the steady creak of moorings. The thought threads through your mind and refuses to leave: save what you can, and refuse to let them call it inevitable.

You start walking toward the Resilience Lab, notebook under arm, hydrophone slung like a promise. The day's work is a tangle of logistics and emotion, and you carry both.
