[Scene: Decaying Pier Platform | Early Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the pier—concrete cracked, ropes looping like knots of memory; kelp lines tethered to a trembling scaffolding. The city skyline is a pale smear behind low fog.]
[Music: Low, brooding cello under a thin percussion—an uneasy heartbeat]
[Sound: Brackish water lapping at pilings; distant gulls; the metallic ping of tools against mooring clamps]

You stand with your boots half in the tide wash, the rubber soles sucking at the mud. Salt and diesel and the green, vegetal tang of seaweed hang in the air. Your fingers are cold where they pinch the rope; the hydrophone in your sling vibrates faintly against your ribs like a small, patient animal.

Noah crouches at the sensor array, sleeves rolled, palms dark with grease and sea. He has his prototype mounted where a plank has rotted through—three modular segments clamped to a buoy frame. A strip of tape with a scrawled note says "temp." He thumbs a connection and the little LED on his device blinks: a steady, hopeful pulse.

Noah: "Okay—listen. Frequency sweep now. Marsh mats engaged. Tide cycle starting."

You slide your notebook free and watch as he patches a readout to the margin where your pencil half-sketched a cross-section of living wall anchors. Numbers print—tiny, decisive lines that mean something whole people can understand: attenuation curves, energy dissipation, erosion rates.

Priya leans in behind you, tablet glow painting her face green-blue. Her voice is careful, the kind that measures both possibility and consequence.

Priya: (whispered) "Look at the damping at 0.7–1.2 hertz. That's where the big swells bleed energy. If we tune the buoy spacing, we can get another ten percent."

You feel the graph translate into a shape under your hands—something you could point to in council and say: this is how we hold them close to their own shore.

Tomas, older than the pier and twice as sure of his feet, thumps a hand on a piling as if blessing the experiment.

Tomas: "My grandfather used to set lines just so. If the current asks for something, you can't give it something else. You give it the right thing."

Noah: (to Tomas) "We built on his idea—distributed damping. Open materials, local labor. Not a wall that locks people out."

You say nothing first. You map their faces—Priya's steady concentration, Tomas's weathered certainty, Noah's cautious smile—and you let a small, brittle relief pass through you. The crowd that has come to help claps—soft, almost disbelieving. The buoy flutters; the kelp reeds behind it sway as if the tide itself is coming to listen.

Narration (internal): This is the work you promised: hands, experiments, people. For a flicker the future looks less like a ledger and more like a shoreline you remember. But the flicker is thin. You know calendars and investor memos consume thin things quickly.

Noah meets your eyes after the second sweep—his expression careful, unreadable in the way people become when more than a prototype rests on a single set of readings.

Noah: (softly) "We can show this. But it'll be a demo. They'll ask for numbers and timelines. They'll want guarantees we can't—"

Priya: (cutting in) "—guarantee in a year. No. We give them confidence intervals, not promises. We don't rebrand hope as a contract."

The conversation pulls and tugs; you push it toward clarity because that's what you do—translate grief into scaffolding, memory into plan.

You close your notebook slowly and let the damp smear of ink dry on your thumb. The smell of wet paper is like a small, private ritual: a promise that what you record will outlast panic.

[Scene: Decaying Pier Platform – Later Morning]
[Visual: Close-up on your face; the sky has thinned—light that used to be golden now reads as a weaker, colder silver. The assembly around you talks in knots.]
[Sound: A phone buzzing in the crowd; a vendor muttering about deliveries; the low hum of a diesel generator powering the testing station.]

Your phone vibrates. Cass's name eats the screen with municipal authority. You answer with a thumb that leaves a salt-streak on the glass.

[Music: A single descending piano motif—uncertain]
Cass: (voice careful, official) "Mara. Thank you for picking up. We need a public demonstration—fast. The council calendar is moving and the investors are loud. If we can't point to something that buys time, they're pushing me to take a more 'decisive' option."

You feel the sentence as a trap you already know the shape of: the city needs cover, and Arman's plan fills a political hole fast.

You: "A demonstration? Cass, the pilot's promising but it's not a finished product. We're not ready to guarantee—"

Cass: "You don't need to guarantee. You need to show immediate, short-term protection. A staged demo at the promenade, concrete reinforcements with our mats to absorb the next swell. It won't be perfect, but it shows action. It will keep the vote from swinging to Arman."

Your throat tightens. You can imagine a dais, a ribbon, an applause that rings the sound of people being told to leave. You can imagine investor eyes looking for the next headline, for the thing they can monetize.

You: "If we do it like that, we'll be compromising the whole design. We'll be saying the cheapest, fastest thing is the safest—"

Cass: (exhale) "Mara. I inherited a city with less time than I'm legally permitted to hope for. I want your work to win. I need something the public can grasp tomorrow."

Priya, standing behind you, murmurs, "She has to balance votes. She has to survive the week to push the long plan." The words are not accusation but arithmetic.

Before Cass can answer, someone on the pier exclaims and a tablet lights with an incoming article—Arman's op-ed. The headline shouts in the same breathless font the investors use: Delay Costs Lives; Reckless Idealism Threatens the Bay's Economy.

Arman's voice is practiced even in print: the city can't afford experiments when infrastructure needs certainty. He frames urgency as moral clarity. You can picture the suit, the smile, the polished cufflink catching podium lights.

Noah crushes the tablet slightly in his hands; the action is small and private.

Noah: "He's framing us like we're sentimental. He always frames anything slower as sentimental—a luxury in the face of 'real' risk."

Priya: "He's selling certainty to people who want not to feel guilty about leaving neighborhoods behind."

You feel the op-ed like a concrete block nudged against the living thing you are trying to grow. The crowd goes quiet; the tide seems to take a breath. The air tastes metallic.

Elena comes up the pier, apron thrown over one shoulder, face raw. She clutches a folded stack of paper—rent notices, each a small, official excision. She looks smaller in daylight, like a carved figure concerned with trivial things and still trembling with everything.

Elena: (voice breaking) "They're on my door. Nino's bakery got two notices. Tomas—he said they gave him a week. The landlord's been—"

Tomas moves to Elena as if he could lay his weathered hands over official stamps and cancel them by force. He does not succeed. He only steadies her by the elbow. You step forward because you always step forward.

You: (to Elena) "We'll do what we can. We'll—"

Elena: "Do what we can? Mara, they're already sending papers. They don't need your good plans. They need someone to tell them they can keep their bakery without lying about what a wall will do."

Her eyes catch yours—accusatory and pleading. In them the weight of your promises lands, and you feel it as both armor and chain.

Narration (internal): Your responsibility swells—it folds itself around every name on those pages. You promised the neighborhood. The promise is no longer an abstract; it's stamped and legal and urgent. You know what victory would look like and also what the city will accept as victory. The two do not match.

Noah: (quiet) "We can keep testing—show the numbers to Cass, ask for a public pilot at the promenade—get a short-term shield out there. Or we can rally people and make Arman's contractors think twice. Or—you think about evacuations. Prioritize the families with the least margin. Move what we can."

Priya: "Each option is a trade. Public blockade will get attention but bring penalties. Phased pilot buys time but cedes control over materials and schedules. Evacuations keep people safe but admits that displacement is already happening."

You feel the tide of the calendar again: council deadlines like incoming waves with teeth. The pier crowd murmurs like seaweed. Somewhere the city press feed pings; the generator thrums; gulls squawk and fold away.

You look at the people around you—Tomas, who has the town's memory in his bones; Priya, who can turn models into morality; Elena, who is standing with a rent stack and a small, sharp grief. Your chest tightens until your next breath is thin.

Narration (internal): The decisions are all practical. They are also moral. They will say who you are. They will say what you value when coins and timelines shout louder than the tide.

A patrol boat passes in the channel, a small gray slice that seems to set the scale for how small you feel. On the shore, a flyer flaps in the wind: ARMAN FOR RAPID DEFENSE. In the space between the buoy bells and the murmuring crowd, the shape of your next move hardens into three possibilities—the only viable vectors that code for different moral futures.
