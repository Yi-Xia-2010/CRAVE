### SCRIPT
# Route: Route B — Ending: Sad
# Context: Start

[Scene: East Strand Harborfront | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide, misty shot of the harborfront; peeling seafoam paint and puddled cobbles reflect a bruised sky]
[Music: Low, slow piano; undertow of industrial hum]
[Sound: Gull calls, distant engine idling, soft slap of water against a pilings]

You step off the last remaining bus and the city meets you like a thing with a memory. Damp salt air threads under your collar and the olive trench clings to the swell of your shoulders. The compass pendant at your throat is cool where your fingers find it—tarnish in the grooves, the tiny dent from when you were sixteen and the storm came too fast.

Your boots sink into a shallow pool that holds the bay's gray light; the taped heel gives once and then steadies. Your field notebook is under your arm like an old friend—pages already half-full with diagrams, margin notes, a scrawl that reads: "living modules / kelp + terraces / community ops?" The hydrophone hangs in its sling, a talisman and a tool both, smelling faintly of seaweed and ocean oil.

Your phone buzzes in your pocket. The notification is blunt: SURGE ADVISORY — 24 HOURS. No headline, no promise. Only cold numbers you already know how to translate into sleepless nights and hurried evacuations.

You breathe the harbor in: diesel and damp wood, the sweet rot of kelp, someone's coffee left too long in the open. The city hums—cranes like distant insects, a new billboard turning the morning with a face you recognize even before you read the name. Arman Kade smiles from the advertisement in a suit too perfect for these salt-soft streets. His eyes are steel-blue on a vinyl gloss sixteen feet above the waterline.

You don't let your hands tighten on the notebook. You never let them tighten too soon. There is a knot around your ribs that used to be called guilt; lately it shapes itself into plans.

Elena waves from a stall before you can find the words to greet her. Her overalls are splashed with fresh paint, bright strokes like stubborn flags. She is smaller than you remember and wound in the same fierce practicality—where your hands map plans, hers make things. The market around her is a collage of patched tarps and crates. Tomas stands a little apart, cap brim low, squinting toward the horizon with a gaze that has counted tides longer than either of you have been alive.

Elena: "You're late."
There's a smile pushing at the corner. She reaches out and squeezes your forearm. Paint comes away on your sleeve in a small, stubborn smear. "You look like you slept with a buoy."

You lift a shoulder and offer the nearest thing to a joke you can muster.
Mara Evans: "Dawn kept the bed warm; it didn't keep the storms away."
You try to make your voice light; it folds into the morning.

Elena: (She blows a strand of hair from her face.) "Noah texted? Or is that just wishful thinking from your pocket?"

You check the screen while Elena organizes a stack of hand-lettered flyers. Noah Ríos's name hovers: "We can make sensors sing. Talk tonight?" Short, familiar, a line that carries both promise and his tendency to phrase technical urgencies like invitations.

Mara Evans: "Tonight," you say out loud to the empty air, to the sea, to whatever god lives between tariffs and tides. "Tonight, yes."

Elena presses her lips together. Her eyes flick toward the billboard. "Arman Kade's banner went up at dawn. White paint, blue eyes, the same speech. He looks like he was born in an office." She tastes the words—not a complaint, more a map of why you're both awake here.

You: (You notice the way she says "his" as if it were a gust.) "He's buying optics," you say. "He's not buying the bay."

Elena: "Buying everything else, though." (She folds a flyer with practiced fingers.) "Mayor Cass has meetings this week. People are already getting letters about 'relocation support' that read like eviction notices in a nicer font."

For a moment the street noise dips as if the harbor is listening. A gull wheels twice and the creak of a mooring fingernails under the sound of the town.

Tomas ambles over with slow certainty, his cap damp at the crown. His hands are calloused from lines and knots and decades of knowing where the sea will cough and where it will whisper. He smells like smoke from the kettle and the salt on his skin smells like history.

Tomas Belmar: "You came back."
He tips his head, eyes creasing. It's not a question. He doesn't need to ask. The piers remember you the way an old dog remembers a voice.

Mara Evans: "I'm here."
You let your gaze rest on the ruins of the storefront—only its threshold is evident, a ghost frame against the newer plywood. You don't let your voice wobble when you say the words. You've practiced this; practice keeps you useful.

Tomas Belmar: (He gestures with one weathered hand toward the Kelp Gardens bobbing beyond the harbor.) "If the surge hits like they say, those kelp lines will be our first shield—if they're healthy. The east moorings flex, but the nursery's not as stocked as it should be. We lost volunteers last month—work, money, the usual. Old habits die slow, but folks leave fast when the roofs go."

You feel the old guilt press in—an old ledger opening with pages already filled in. The room between your ribs fills with calculations you have been making since you left—module spacing, integration points, funding timelines. Your pen already has a rhythm on the edge of your notebook.

Mara Evans: "We can scale nursery beds fast enough to make a difference. Modular terraces at key points, bio-anchoring with kelp roots. Noah Ríos can patch sensor arrays—Priya can model the flow. If we get the Lab to forward the grant without strings—"

Tomas Belmar: (He grunts softly.) "The Lab sends letters that read like good intentions and small print. The contractors send checks with teeth. There's a difference."

The sound of that last sentence is a small sharp thing. The billboard's grin seems to lean down toward you as if tasting the remark.

Elena: "Don't say it like that here."
Her voice is almost a croon of anger—you know the sound. She hates fear dressed as inevitability. "Tomas, we don't have time for grudges. Mara—" She snaps her fingers. "If you can get sensors online tonight and folks on the moorings tomorrow, we can at least buy the time to reinforce nursery stakes."

You and Noah Ríos and Priya and the Lab: the list is a ragged chain of hope you keep adding links to. The funding letters you've received are real—ink, signatures, the warm bureaucratic language that always manages to sound both hopeful and hollow. The footnotes are a chokehold: matching funds, deliverables due, an option clause that hands control to whoever pays last.

You press the pendant until you can feel the little circle of metal. The memory of your family storefront collapsing like a swallowed tooth flashes—salt water arching up the street, the stove's brass knob cold against your palm, your mother's face in the doorway. You are sixteen in the flash and thirty-two in the damp present, and both tides pull at you with equal force.

Tomas Belmar: "If Arman Kade gets the council to sign quick, they'll build the wall from north to the pier and call it 'protection.' They put fences at the edges and promise a view. But the edge is us."

You look at the harbor and watch a small boat push free of a mooring, its pilot making for the kelp lines. The morning light cuts the water; it makes the city look like a photograph someone had overexposed on purpose.

Mara Evans: "We need to make a counter-proposal that looks like control but is actually community-owned."
Your pen scratches the margin: LIVING SEAWALL: modules + kelp + public access. Underline, underline. "If we can show the model—fast, measurable, people-first—Mayor Cass will have to sit with it."

Tomas Belmar: (He pauses, then offers a slow, skeptical smile.) "Cass listens when the people shout loud enough and when there's a map to keep them from panicking. You ever notice how politicians like a plan that can be drawn on a napkin?"

Elena: "And Arman Kade likes plans that fit in a ledger."

A laugh fights up from somewhere dark and doesn't make it. The air suddenly feels too thin with things that used to be simple truths.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Walk the slope to the Kelp Gardens with Tomas"
> Choice B: "Head straight for the nursery lines to check kelp health yourself"
[Outcome A: You fall into step with Tomas, moving slowly down the slope. His stories come out between the boards—old mooring lines, where the nursery took root, which post the tide eats first. The narrative steadies you; you make more tactical notes.]
[Outcome B: You break away and wade shallow into the nursery pontoon. Cold spray hits your face; you run gloved fingers down a kelp frond, testing its pliancy. The kelp's scent is a small, fierce comfort—green and alive and stubborn.]

[Scene: The Kelp Gardens | Dawn]
[Visual: Close shots of tethered platforms, green swaths of kelp swaying; community members bent in work]
[Music: Subtle strings; a distant drumbeat of footsteps]
[Sound: The creak of ropes, soft plipping of water droplets, distant radio chatter]

The gardens are a patchwork of hope stitched onto crumbling concrete. Tethers creak in a chorus and the kelp blades flash like banners in a sea-sun. Volunteers move with a ritual calm—there's an economy to their motions, the kind that develops where things have to be kept alive by habit and care.

You move through the rows, notebook open, fingers sketching module placements that would hug the coastline without cutting the public off. Your hydrophone, when you set it for a moment against a buoy, picks up the low heartbeat of the harbor—machinery, moving life, the long slow slap of current against wood.

A teenager from the nursery hands you a jar of soaked seedline; his palms are stained dark with nutrient slurry. He watches you with the attention of someone who has heard your name around these docks and hopes the person attaching it is the kind who can keep promises.

You crouch to tie a provisional anchor. The rope is rough against your skin, the salt stinging a little in a nick on your thumb. It grounds you in the queer arithmetic of now: hands on rope, head full of parameters. You cannot plan everything; you can only stitch what you can reach.

Elena: (She leans on the railing, watching your hands.) "Do the Lab's footnotes mention community-centred ownership?"

You glance at your notebook. The grant language was polite. It used words like "partnership" and "efficiency" and "scale." It did not say "your neighbors' names." That absence tastes like copper.

Mara Evans: "They say 'stakeholder frameworks,'" you answer, and say the phrase with an edge. "They mean 'where can we shift liability.' We have to define the stakeholders as people, not pockets."

Elena: "Name the pockets, then." She says, "If you hand them a list that'll put people in the frame, they'll have to look."

You nod. It feels like a tiny, necessary violence—naming the vulnerable so they can be protected rather than priced out. You would do it anyway. You always have.

Tomas appears at your shoulder and points at the kelp. Nothing sanctimonious in the gesture—just a man asking about a living thing.
Tomas Belmar: "Those beds by the eastern mooring—if they go, it's not just biomass. It's nursery, it's fishway, it's year-one protection. You plan the modules, make the terraces, but if the biology's brittle, the wall will be a pretty lie."

You feel the shape of a plan form between your ribs like a new bone. Living seawalls depend on living things. Living things need time. Time is the one currency you don't have.

Your phone buzzes again with another short message—a map pin from Priya, a forwarded bureaucratic note from the Lab, an image of a construction permit stamped in corporate blue. Noah Ríos's earlier sentence repeats in your head like a refracted echo: "We can make sensors sing."

You tuck the hydrophone under your jacket briefly and feel the damp against your ribs. The pendant is warm now from your skin. For a second, you imagine the seawall finished—green terraces stepping down into the water, kids on the promenade, the storefront's threshold filled again with a tired but stubborn life. The image is a small, bright thing. Then you imagine Arman Kade's wall—monolithic, tight-lipped, clean—and rows of turned lots where people used to live.

Your chest tightens. The mood here is not only cold; it's honest and descending—like the light before a storm turns to a bruise.

You make one more note in the margin: "Meet Noah 1900. Bring sensor schematic, pitch modular financing, include community-ownership clause." You fold the paper over and crease it hard.

A volunteer calls your name across the platforms: they found a glint under a piling—an old tin sign with a faded storefront logo. You pick it up; the edges are rough with salt but the paint is stubborn. For a breath you are sixteen again and the sign is the crossroads of your life.

You run your thumb over the faded letters. They are not legible. Memory fills the rest.

You look up at the city, to the billboard with Arman Kade's face, to the cranes that scent the air with hot metal and decisions, and you let the harbor sound into you—gulls, distant engines, the creak of a future being negotiated in private rooms. The surge advisory is a thin drumbeat under everything.

A thought sits sharp and terrible beneath your ribs: You came back to save this place. You also might only buy it time.

You tighten the pendant, close the notebook, and let the cold salt air slide down the back of your neck.

For a moment you simply stand, the kelp swaying like an uncertain applause. The world holds its breath with you.

You need to be ready for a meeting, for a negotiation, for the work of convincing a mayor and a city that the beautiful answer is the right one. That you might be able to keep people here without selling them to a different kind of safety.

You exhale. The sound of the bay answers.

You step back toward the harbor path, toward the town that remembers your name and the Lab that sends polite admonitions, toward an evening where Noah Ríos will try to make sensors sing and where, already, other voices will try to make other narratives louder.

You trace one more tentative line in the margins—a page-turn you cannot ignore.

[Visual: Close on your hand closing the notebook; the pendant catches a sliver of cold dawn light]
[Music: A single dissonant chord held thin, stretching forward]

You look at the horizon as a low murmur builds in your chest—resolve, dread, responsibility braided together. It is not a clean moment. It is not an ending. It is a hinge.

You tuck the hydrophone back into its sling and begin to walk.

[Music: Fade Out]
