A reporter pushes a microphone forward. A volunteer passes the looped testimonies to camera crews—clips of neighbors naming lost stairways, a child's drawing of a future marsh, a woman naming furniture she remembers by the sound of it being moved.

Samir appears at your elbow, flour-dusted and beaming, ladle in hand. He has organized teams like a conductor — food, muscle, paperwork.

You take in the smell of salt and stew, the tactile warmth of shared blankets, the sound of low conversations stitching the night. The pulse of recovery is slow and steady — not triumphant fanfare, but a persistent, communal heartbeat.
