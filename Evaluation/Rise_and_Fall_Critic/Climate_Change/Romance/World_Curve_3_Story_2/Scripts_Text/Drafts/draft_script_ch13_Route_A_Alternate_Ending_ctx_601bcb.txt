# Route: Route A — Alternate Ending


[Scene: Sea Barrier Construction Site | Morning — Weeks After Signing]
[Visual: Cranes arc like slow, obedient beasts against a pale sky; steel beams drip with saltwater. Half-built concrete segments line the shoreline like teeth.]
[Sound: Distant metallic groan of winches; the city exhales in collective chatter and the occasional relieved laugh]
[Music: Quiet, hopeful strings under a steady heartbeat percussion]

You stand with your raincoat rolled over your arm, watching a crane lower a slab into place. For the first time since you made the call, relief has a shape — hulking, noisy, and embarrassingly material. The grant arrived. The machines arrived. The city, for a little while, can breathe.

Your compass pendant rests against the hollow of your throat, cold where the wind reaches it. You find yourself twisting it absentmindedly, as if it can be wound for luck. Your notebook peeks from your pocket, pages jangling with lists you promised yourself you would not forget: community liaisons, temporary shelters, clauses to hammer after the immediate things stop burning.

Elias steps up beside you, boots clumped with mud. He doesn't offer the big, practiced smile he uses in press; it's thin and private today, and when his hazel eyes find yours they carry the small, brittle relief of someone who believed that competence could buy time.

Elias Kade: "They've reallocated the second tranche. They're pushing two shifts. We'll get the north segment closed in by next week if there are no supply delays."

You: "And the community audits?"

Elias Kade: "Phased. You'll have audits. I promised them. I promised you."

His voice is steady, but a tremor moves under the words—something like the rattle of a car engine that has lived too long. You see the man who grew up among blueprints and backfilled promises. You also see a man learning that promises cost people.

You: "Phased isn't the same as binding, Elias."

Elias Kade: "I know. But binding could have stalled everything. We have a narrow window to stop displacement tonighting. I did what I always do — I pushed for what keeps people dry."

You swallow. The seawall's shadow slants across your boots. In your chest, gratitude and guilt arrange themselves into a familiar, heavy knot.

[Scene: Promenade — Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Market stalls more orderly than usual; people trade in canned beans and sieved hope. A banner reading 'KEEP SOLACE ALIVE' flaps above a cluster of folding chairs.]
[Sound: The soft clatter of cups, a child's laugh, tide hitting the outer quay]
[Music: A single, clarion piano motif; small choral swell]

You go to the Promenade because the seawall is useless without the people it's meant to serve. Mrs. Ebbett's hand is papery as she presses a photocopied evacuation map into your palm. Samir is there, sleeves rolled to the elbow, pulling a rowboat into a makeshift dock.

Samir: "You did what you thought you'd have to. It's done. We can use it; we also need to keep the boats running. We get folks out fast if the north steps fail."

You: "We will need the outreach lists, the volunteer roster, and a dry spot for pets. Professor Bhat is working on fast-scan assessments for structural risk."

Samir grins, but the grin doesn't reach his eyes. His laugh turns into work the same way the day turns into night here: suddenly and necessarily.

Professor Anika Bhat (arriving with a waterproof case): "I ran the new tide models. The seawall changes flow vectors. It buys time at the headlands, but the old quarter may see redirected surge if we don't shore up redistribution points."

You: "So it helps some and harms others. That was the trade we took."

Professor Anika Bhat: "Trade is a polite word for triage. But triage can be humane. It depends on the people manning it."

Her eyes, behind wire frames, find yours with something fierce and practical. You feel a resolve settle like a toolbelt around your shoulders.

[Sound: A far thunderclap, low and indifferent]

You look toward the harbor. The sky tightens. People go on speaking, bargaining for fish and rice, moving with rhythms you recognize as defiance.

[Scene: Sea Barrier Construction Site — Storm Night]
[Visual: The sky folds into a single, bruised color; rain becomes a sheeted assault. Cranes groan like sea-monsters.]
[Sound: Howl of wind through cables; concrete coughing as slabs collide]
[Music: Percussion becomes urgent; brass flares like alarm horns]

The models underestimated the storm's teeth. Winds come sharp and vindictive. Half-built sections, proud and heavy, heave and shudder. You stand where you shouldn't and feel the seawall's unfinished joints give, one by one. A section collapses with the sound of a thousand plates breaking.

You: "Get back! Clear the—"

A wall of water finds the path of least resistance. It carr ies the northshore's aging houses like paper ships. You watch porches unmake themselves, kitchen chairs spin, a garden trellis dive and vanish. The smell that rises after is a cocktail of diesel and rot and something older, like old books left by the sea.

You stand on a ruined porch with the compass at your throat humming a little against wet fabric. Your fingers clamp around it as if it could pull decisions free of consequence.

[Sound: Distant sirens; a child's cry cut off and then muffled under wind]
[Visual: The silhouette of a neighbor's roofline splintering, a lamplight swallowed]

You do not have luxury for self-recrimination. You have hands. You give them orders. You hand Samir a rope. You direct volunteers to the boat launch. You mark the still-warm spots where people might be trapped, where rescuers will be needed.

[Scene: Ruined City Hall Atrium — Aftermath, Dawn]
[Visual: Glass spiderwebbed. Floodwater has puddled on the marble floor, reflecting shattered neon flood models. Paper floats like dead leaves.]
[Sound: Low, angry murmurs; the slap of water against a damaged planter]
[Music: Slow, aching strings resolving into a tentative major chord]

You and Elias meet again beneath a ceiling that still holds the city's burned promises like dust. He is covered in ash and rain, his hair plastered to his temple. His hazel eyes are raw, red-rimmed with sleeplessness and something heavier than regret.

Elias Kade: "We thought the models were conservative. I recalibrated as new data came in. We didn't—"

You (interrupting, voice low, steady): "You didn't predict the surge path shifting. You didn't feel the call of the neighborhoods the way I did."

Elias Kade: "That's not fair. You wanted an immediate yes and I gave it. Then we watched sections undercut, curve-loads fail. I thought I was doing the responsible thing."

You: "Responsible for keeping some dry at the cost of others. That's a kind of responsibility too, Elias."

Elias Kade: (after a beat) "I thought it would be enough. I thought we could fix the rest afterward."

You: "After the fact isn't prevention. But if we don't start fixing 'after' into 'during', we hand people a schedule of grief."

He inhales sharply. The two of you circle the same truth like wary animals: that intention and outcome are not the same and that both of you carried a piece of this ruin.

Elias Kade: "Do you want me to apologize?"

You look at him. His face is worn in a way you have only ever seen after someone has realized their expertise hurt human beings.

You: "I want accountability, Elias. I want a plan that doesn't treat people like variables. I want to learn from this and build differently."

Elias Kade: "Then help me make that plan."

Your conversation spills into the atrium like the tide: rough, eroded edges, then slow smoothing. You argue. You interrupt each other. You also begin the careful work of naming failures and listing repairs. The dialogue between you stretches, fracturing and knitting, long enough for both to land where the city's people need you to be.

[Sound: A distant shout; Nova's footsteps like a drumbeat]
[Visual: Nova enters, face streaked with rain, eyes steel-gray and unyielding]

Nova Duarte: "You signed a contract, Mara. You gave them a lever and a deadline. They pulled with all their strength without a plan for the rest. People died because of an arrogance dressed as urgency."

You: "Nova—"

Nova Duarte: "Don't 'Nova' me. We warned them. We tried to force them to think about retreat corridors, to listen to people who live on these streets. This is what happens when you put capital and speed before life."

The room burns with accusation. You could answer with a thousand justifications. Instead you step toward her, rain beading on your eyelashes.

You: "You're right to be furious. We are owed fury. We are owed answers. I'm not asking you to forgive me. I'm asking you to help me make something that doesn't leave us here again."

Nova's jaw tightens. For a moment her expression is unreadable — complex, layered: grief, anger, a thread of something that might be hope.

Nova Duarte: "If you're really asking, then you start with honest accounting. No smoothing the edges. No hiding who got left behind."

You: "We will publish everything. We will fund emergency rehousing. We will open the contracts to oversight. I will not sign another blank check without community legal representation."

Nova (voice lower): "You know I'm not the sort to sit at red ribbon ceremonies."

You: "Then help us keep them honest."

There is no grand reconciliation. There is a meeting of purpose, fractured and pragmatic. You and Elias speak with Nova, with Samir, with Professor Anika. You make lists and assign names and share pain into tasks. It doesn't erase the smell of rot, but it turns rage into motion.

[Scene: Flooded Neighborhoods & Promenade — Weeks Later]
[Visual: Tarps and new plywood patched across windows. Community kitchens smoke. Small gardens replanted in raised beds. A temporary memorial of sea-glass and candles on the promenade.]
[Sound: The steady clink of work tools, someone humming as they stitch, water gutters running calmer]
[Music: Warm strings and a steady, gentle beat — an upward arc]

You spend the days like a human engine: paperwork in the morning, field visits in the afternoon, organizing relief by night. Samir runs the flotilla for displaced families; you watch him ferry an old woman and her cat to a dry shelter, his hands sure and quick.

Samir: "We'll make space at the hall. We'll get the soup boiling. We'll keep listening."

Professor Anika Bhat organizes volunteers into assessment teams, her white hair plastered to her forehead, eyes bright with exhausted focus.

Professor Anika Bhat: "We can stabilize some foundations with pilings and temporary cofferdams. But long-term, we need to integrate wetland buffers, redistribute loads, and accept that some parcels must be converted."

You: "We will pair engineering with active restoration. We'll fund buyouts fairly and use reclaimed materials for housing."

Anika nods, and for the first time in weeks you feel the slow easing of despair into a plan that includes the people it affects.

Elias arrives every day to coordinate logistics. You and he share a ruined office — two desks, one rule: speak clearly, keep to facts when emotion might collapse schedules. The arguments come, fewer now; they are sharper but more productive, full of corrections and shared accountability rather than blame.

Elias Kade: "We're putting clause language for legal community oversight into the next contract. I'm negotiating with the mayor's office directly. This time, we don't leave the language vague."

You: "And you will make those public."

Elias Kade: "Yes."

It is not romance made easy. Your relationship has bruises that do not disappear. You have, between you, new expectations and a carefulness that feels less like protection and more like respect. The hurt is not flattened; it is acknowledged and folded into work.

[Scene: Promenade Market — Months After the Storm, Morning]
[Visual: Sun through a thinner, cleaner sky. Reclaimed wood stalls, painted in communal colors, sell seedlings and salt-preserved fish. A small plaque names those lost and lists community resources.]
[Sound: Children's feet on repaired cobbles; someone plays a slow tune on a battered guitar]
[Music: Gentle, hopeful piano; strings rising into a soft crescendo]

A new hybrid pilot is underway: engineered berms where needed, broad tidal wetlands elsewhere, community gardens ringed with living shoreline plantings. Nova sits at a folding table, handing out pamphlets and seedlings; her voice is still fierce but now has a laugh that catches in the sunlight.

You help plant a marsh grass plug at the edge of a newly dug swale. Your hands are muddy. The compass at your throat is warm now, as if the metal remembers sun.

You: "We did the accounting. We made the oversight public. Some people moved, some people stayed. We rebuilt with more eyes and more care."

Nova Duarte: (offering you a seedling) "This is for the people who stayed to fight and the people who left to build with others. We don't need to pick one kind of rescue."

You: "We need both."

Samir tosses you a steaming cup of soup. Professor Anika waves from a clipboard, Erlis from the contractor's tent nods as he passes by. The city is not the same; the loss is a hollow place in the map that will never fully fill. But something sturdier is layered over it — a coalition that includes engineers who testify publicly, activists who sit on oversight boards, neighbors who check on each other's foundations.

You stand at the promenade, the market noise soft around you. Your optimism is no longer naive; it's deliberate, chiseled by what it has seen. It is optimism that builds structures that survive storms and also builds the social scaffolding to carry grief.

You press your thumb to the brass compass. It ticks like a small, private metronome.

You (to yourself): "We will keep building. We will keep listening. We will not let this be what defines us, only what changed us."

[Visual: Camera pulls back slowly — the promenade humming with human life against a horizon rimmed with restored marsh and careful concrete]
[Music: Swells into a gentle major resolution; a note of persistence]

You watch children plant marsh plugs with sticky fingers. You watch Nova teach an elderly man how to tie a proper gardening knot. Elias and you exchange a look across the busy square — not the serenade of lovers but the promise of colleagues and companions who survived and chose to continue.

The compass cools, then warms again with the press of your skin. The day goes on. The work does, too. You do not have neat endings — only the honest, ongoing business of repair.
