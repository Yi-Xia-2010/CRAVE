The storm comes like a test you have already been rehearsing for. Samir's rapid-response teams wheel out sandbags and temporary sluices, neighbors with sleeves rolled and flashlights in their mouths. You stand on the edge, hands raw and blistering, watching the living breakwater take its first real measure.

Water hits the pilot sites with a kind of hungry curiosity and is turned—slowed by the grasses and the zigzag alignment. The surge that would have lapped higher up the promenade finds itself deflected, losing teeth on silt and stems.

You hear a neighbor shout: "The eastern cul-de-sac—it's holding!"

Somewhere down the line, a house that had been on the edge of the retreat map sees the water slide away from its threshold instead of through it. People cheer, a rough, astonished sound.

Nova is beside you, hair plastered to her forehead, eyes fierce and wet.

Nova Duarte: "Look at them go. We did that."

You turn and see the team—Samir's volunteers, neighbors, a few university students wrapped in borrowed raincoats—working like a single organism. The pilot doesn't stop the whole sea; nothing could. But it transformed the event into a thing that could be weathered.

You feel your chest unfurl. This is what you wanted: practical work that honors people and landscape.
