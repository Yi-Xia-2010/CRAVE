You step into the atrium with a wet jacket and the taste of iron on your tongue. Engineers hover around a schematic projection of the pilot site—vectors, valves, lockdown sequences. Their voices have the clipped certainty of people trained to reduce variables: close valves, isolate tideways, temporary closures to minimize structural strain.

Elias stands a little apart from them, sleeves rolled, watching the projection like it's a puzzle he can solve with the right angle. He looks composed, even now: neat, measured, the outline of someone who's been taught to trust the ledger over the rumor. When he sees you, his expression doesn't relax so much as recalibrate.

Elias: "Mara. The models are converging on a conservative path—immediate shut-offs at the pilot nodes, rotational closures of tideways. It reduces load by forty percent in worst-case surge. It's the safest engineering call."

You: "It protects infrastructure—yes. But those tideways are livelihoods. Fishing launches, the morning vendors—closing them bluntly throws people into precarity. We need graduated measures. We need exemption corridors."

Elias: (sighs) "Exemptions are failure modes. They create uneven pressure and then a cascade. If we don't make clean closures, we risk structural failures that would shut down everything longer."

You feel the argument in your chest: a balance between two kinds of harm. It's not a philosophical debate; it's someone's day job, someone's income, the fragile choreography of the city's marginal economies.

Professor Anika moves between you like a steadying rod.

Professor Anika: "Neither of you is wrong. But mistakes now have outsized political cost. If the pilot collapses under mismanagement, the hybrid argument dies. You both know that."

Elias: (quiet, precise) "We can mitigate social costs with temporary supports—stipends, relocation of vendors to inland stalls—but those take coordination and money, and they take time."

You: "Time we might not have while the storm is still organizing itself."

The engineers look at their screens; one points to an overlay and whispers the word that makes your stomach drop: evacuation threshold. The monitors whisper of water lines climbing, of pumps laboring at capacity.

She wears the same patchwork resolve that has chased you through town halls and alleys. Her eyes, even at a distance, are an unreadable fuse.

Samir appears at your side in the feed, soaked and rain-creased, waving at the camera. He hustles someone forward—an elderly woman with a small, battered envelope clutched to her chest. The camera pans close enough to catch the tremble in her hands and the cobwebbed loops of her handwriting.

Samir: (on a crackled line) "Mrs. Ebbett insists she won't leave the alleys. Says the house holds all that's left of her sister. She won't be moved for a week."

You feel the letter before you read it: the weight of a neighborhood's memory, the wobble in pen strokes that says refusal and dignity in the same breath.

You: (to Samir) "Bring it here. I'll read it. Tell her—tell them—we won't close their livelihoods without a plan."

Samir: "They don't want plans right now. They want assurances you can't give them in a sentence."

You open the envelope with fingers that smell of rain and battery acid from the monitors. The handwriting is a small, looping insistence.

Mrs. Ebbett (voice on the feed, unamplified but clear): "I've buried enough. I won't be moved by men in suits or storms. If the water comes, it will have to take my porch, not my pride."

The sound of that sentence cuts through your practiced scripts. It is neither stubborn nor heroic—it is human and terrible and utterly final for her.

Nova steps into the camera frame then, rain like a curtain behind her.

Nova: (loud, precise) "We will not be used as sacrificial margins for 'pilot projects.' If agencies won't protect people, people will protect themselves. Close the site? Or close the city to those who profit from it?"

You: "Nova—" You begin, because you have words of reassurance and procedural safety, because you have spent months trying to fold movement energy into legal scaffolding. But the rain eats the syllables.

Nova: (interrupting) "We negotiated. We gave you the opening. But headlines are the city's true tidal force—today they're washing the pilot as rumor. People are scared. Scared people don't trust soft promises."

Her look toward the cameras is complex—part performative demand, part plea. You cannot read it as a single thing because Nova is many things, all at once. She is furious, protectively fierce, and also, infinitesimally, tired.

Elias (over the comms, clipped): "We can't let protests complicate the closures. The machinery needs clear windows of operation. If protesters are in the tideways, we risk both work stoppage and safety breaches."

You: "If we clear them, we betray them publicly. If we leave them, we risk equipment damage. There is no clean move."

Engineers are calling through; the feeds overlay structural risk levels in a new layer of red. Someone shouts that the surge is accelerating on a linked model. You can feel the atrium's air press against your lungs like the sea against a bulkhead.

You feel the pilot as a fragile boat again—straining lines, a small sail, and a sea that has shifted into a more ruthless cadence.
