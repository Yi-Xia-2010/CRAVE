[Scene: The Strand | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: Empty boardwalk washed in salt-gray light; the horizon a thin bruise of color. A stack of patched planks and fishing nets sag against the railing.]
[Sound: Distant gulls, a low, steady creak of mooring lines, the soft hiss of surf undercut by wind in the reeds.]
[Music: Sparse piano, slow, minor-key — the kind that makes breath feel heavier.]

You wake before the town does — because the sea never really sleeps. Your shoulders are still warm from the thin sleeping roll you left on the bench, but the air is already damp enough to carry the day's work into your bones. Salt blooms along the edges of the wood like a pale lichen. Somewhere beyond the fog, the harbor breathes.

You stand and take inventory, not by eye but by memory — the way your hands know rub-marks on the compass pendant, the ragged corner of the scarf where Ivy tucked a coin last winter, the corner of your field journal warped from too many damp mornings. Then you walk the line.

The Strand reads like a ledger of small violences. Boards from the old fishing stalls are mismatched; a buoy, half-buried in seaweed, lists postal numbers that no longer exist. A row of planters meant for halophytes sag with stormwater. Where the co-op hung a banner last month, the ropes are cut clean — municipal cleanness, or intimidation. You can't tell yet.

You pause at the place where your father's boat used to rest on the low moor, its name peeled away by sun and salt. You don't need the hull to remember the last storm — you remember the way it sounded, the tilt of the pier, the last smell of diesel and wet rope. Guilt is not a single shape; it's a ledger you keep open on the bedside table of your mind. You close your fingers around the pendant as if you can press the page together.

Footsteps — quick, familiar — arrive from behind.

Ivy: "You really didn't mean to sleep through dawn again, did you? I mean, those gulls were practically calling you out."

Your sister is a small bright thing in a muddy jumpsuit, paint on her palms and a bag of seedlings slung over one shoulder. She smells like compost and citrus peel. Even this early, she moves with the impatient optimism of someone who thinks the world can be fixed with two hours and a paintbrush.

You: (soft, exhaling) "You dragged me out. Which is proper. The Strand has been talking since midnight."

Ivy: (tilting her head) "It's gossip, not prophecy. Unless the council is sending us another prophecy in concrete."

You bristle at the word, not because of the substance but because the concrete prophecy comes via Jun Park's plan — the mayor's staff calls it "managed resilience." The co-op calls it "managed surrender." You haven't said those last two words out loud for good reason.

Rosa arrives from the Basin path, shawl wrapped like a shield. Her braid is threaded with reeds the color of old rope. She carries a thermos and a small stack of community petitions bound together with sun-faded twine.

Rosa: "Morning, Mara. Ivy. The tide left us a letter in the drains. Little gifts of plastic and more little holes in the planters."

Rosa's humor is a thin paper over something tougher. When she smiles, it doesn't erase the lines at the corners of her eyes. She sits with you on the planks, the three of you a small, stubborn island.

You: "How are the petitions looking?"

Rosa: (handing you the stack) "Heavy. Names from the Basin, from the old shipwright's family, from the night-shift lads at the warehouse. A few had to scratch with blisters. They trust you to make their voice into something the city will hear."

You feel the weight like iron. To them, you are the point on a map where hope is supposed to be tied. To yourself, you are mostly ledger and ledger-keeping — the lists of who has shelter, who owes who diesel, who will have to go if the plan passes.

Ivy: "You should put a face on it when you speak, you know. Not just numbers. People remember faces better than math."

You: (bitterly) "People remember faces — and then politicians decide which faces count."

Rosa: (quiet) "Jun believes in counting well. He will present numbers that don't make people's backs feel the arithmetic."

You: (the ledger blurring) "Numbers that save the downtown and drown the Basin."

Ivy: (fisting a seed tray) "Don't let the Basin drown. Promise me you'll say the Basin's name."

You: "I will say it. That's not the same as changing a vote."

There is a pause thick enough to taste. The Strand hums with small industry: an early fishernets repairer muttering to himself, two municipal workers carting signage labeled with the city's seal, the distant thump of a hydraulic press at the harbor. The town is awake enough to make theater.

You choose to open your journal. The paper is damp at the corners; ink spreads like small tides. You read the first lines aloud. Your voice is thin at first but finds the current.

You: (reading aloud) "We are not lines on a map. We are people who have learned how to read the weather on our bones."

Ivy: (soft) "Yes. That's it. Make them think about the bones."

Rosa: (holding your hand) "Speak it like you mean it, child. Tell them about the ledger. Tell them how the Basin taught you to plant on high days and hold the water steady on storms."

Your voice falters when you picture your father's face. You have never said the part about the last night onto the Strand in full company; it's a private ledger entry. Guilt edges every sentence now — that if you'd decided differently then, if you'd pushed harder for funding, maybe the boat wouldn't have gone. Maybe it wouldn't have happened on your watch. But this is not only about your past; it's about choices now and whose losses the city can be made to bear.

[Scene: The Basin | Dawn]
[Visual: Low-lying garden bowls; puddles mirror a ragged sky. Community art rises from salvaged metal. Seedlings stand like small, damp teeth.]
[Sound: Wet footsteps, the close hum of a pump, a child's distant laugh muffled by fog.]
[Music: A single cello note under the wind.]

You lead them down the slope into the Basin. Mud clings to your boots like reluctant witnesses. The Basin is quieter than the Strand, closer to the water-table, and smells of soil and old salt. The mangrove saplings you planted last spring lean like tentative hopefuls.

One of the plots is trampled — a boot mark the size of a present worry. A stake snapped, and someone left a municipal tag nearby: "Property survey: Proposed relocation boundary." The tag flutters at the foot of young mangroves like a small paper accusation.

Rosa: (kneeling to the mangrove) "They measure with rulers and call it objective. But you and I both know a ruler doesn't know how long a person can wait."

Ivy: "They didn't even bother to ask whose beds they were trampling."

You pull the tag into your palm. The surveyors didn't leave a name, only a barcode and a date. The ledger in your head ticks. This is where the choices will be counted, the margins drawn.

You: "We can file a complaint. We can push the map back. Or we can make them explain why this patch is expendable."

Rosa: "You must do both. While they write the letter, you also remember the people who drop their kids off here. Remember the face of the boy who learned to plant back when his mother couldn't."

You think of faces, of ledger lines that become human — bills paid with favors, shifts cancelled so someone could stand at a pump. The Basin is a living spreadsheet and an altar.

You make the list, and in the quiet the names settle into a rhythm. The community's accounting is always irregular and always human — a ledger held together with braided twine and stubborn hope. You tuck the list back into your jacket, the paper soaking in the smell of salt and peat.

[Scene: Harbor Edge / Tideworks Fringe | First Light]
[Visual: A scattered cluster of instruments: a tri-pod sensor blinking faint green, a transparent tank with bioluminescent algae, a converted container labelled TIDEWORKS in flaking paint.]
[Sound: The faint beep of a sensor, an occasional metallic ping, wave-slap against pilings.]
[Music: Low synth hum; the cello lingers.]

Theo's solar pack sits like an eager dog by the container; he gives you a thumbs-up and returns to calibrating something that smells faintly of copper. Elias is there, crouched over a sensor with his wristband pulsing its pale green beat. He looks less like the man on a poster and more like someone who hasn't slept enough to let his hair lie. When he sees you, his face does what it always tries not to do — it opens.

Elias: "You came earlier than I expected. The sensors finally stabilized last night. We've got three consecutive days of lower wave energy in the models… but the sample variance is still high."

You want to be angry and grateful in the same breath. Data is Elias's way of loving the sea; it's his way of saying he can fix something without promising what he can't guarantee.

You: "Lower wave energy for three days isn't a promise, Elias. The council will want certainty. They'll want numbers that buy votes."

Elias: (sighing) "I don't want to give them promises either. I want to give them evidence they can stand on. Living breakwaters attenuate waves, rebuild habitat — the models show that. But the city funds a single mechanical barrier in a month, and that shifts the conversation."

Elias's watch ticks softly against his wrist. He meets your eyes, and for a sliver of time the scientist drops the lab-coat politeness.

Elias: (quieter) "If you tell their stories — the Basin, your father's boat, the kids who plant — and I bring the maps and the sediment results, we can make a case that isn't just theoretical."

You: (raw) "People's stories are already on the ledgers. I could speak until my voice is hollow, Elias. Will the maps make them care, or just give them a reason to do different math?"

Elias: "I'm trying to bridge that. I—I brought a reconstruction of the living breakwater effect. I can show before-and-after projections, sediment deposition… the habitat returns. It will be technical, but I can make the graph visible."

You study the sensor, then Elias. For all his measured speech, there's a tremor under the surface — a reluctance you know too well: his fear of promising too much because a failed promise would break trust, and he knows what broken trust does to people who need to rely on one another.

Theo: "And if it's not enough, we make noise. Not the loud kind that scares donors, the kind that makes them have to answer in public."

Elias: (half-smiling) "Theo thinks in megaphones."

You: (a brittle laugh) "I need facts and faces. Not megaphones. Though Theo's megaphones are helpful at rallies."

There's real tenderness in the way Elias reaches out, as if offering the data and the steady hand of method as a kind of shelter. You almost accept it as an apology for your shared past — for the things neither of you could save then that you now try to guard against.

Elias: "You carrying this alone worries me. Not because you can't, but because you shouldn't have to."

You: "I don't know how to ask."

Elias: "Then practice, here, now. Say the line you plan to say to the council. I'll tell you what hits like a rock and what lands like a hand."

You take a breath and read another paragraph of your speech. Elias listens like a scientist listening for an anomaly.

You: (reading) "This isn't about nostalgia for a coastline we remember. It's about deciding which lives the city values when the sea rearranges us."

Elias: (after a long beat) "That is good. Direct. Don't follow it with 'we deserve better' — show them what better is. Show them the Basin's seedlings, show them sediment charts, show them that retreat without care costs more in human suffering."

You: "And Jun?"

Elias: (tightening his jaw) "Jun will show them a plan that keeps the downtown floating. He will call it efficiency. He will call it fairness on municipal terms. He will ask for sacrifices the city can live with."

You: "And that 'can live with' sentence is the problem. Who lives, and who doesn't."

The words hang like a rope under tension. You feel the gravity of the council's approaching vote — a mechanism that will translate these morning conversations into policy that either holds neighborhoods together or compels their dispersal.

You test the ledger again — not only the paper lists but the ledger of feelings, names, debts.

The harbor wind lifts and slaps your jacket against your ribs. You choose, and the decision sparks a small rearrangement: planning aligns around one pathway. The town hums its private work.

Elias steps closer and for a heartbeat speaks about structure and waves, then pauses, and says something else:

Elias: (soft) "When the hearing starts, we don't get to go half-way. People will listen to a rhythm: beginning, proof, call to care. Make the beginning human. Make the proof undeniable. And—" he swallows— "if you want me there, I will stand behind you."

There's more in his eyes than the offer of technical back-up. You keep your face still so the harbor doesn't read too much.

You: "I'll take the proof. But don't make promises we can't keep."

Elias: "I won't."

The sky is brighter now, a thin, pale promise. But the color is washed, like watercolors left in the rain.

Jun's name is not shouted, but you see his shadow in the banners clipped to the lamp-posts near the road: sleek fonts, diagrams of engineered floodwalls, smiling renderings of downtown cafés that would survive. The posters are tidy and anonymous. Political design is tidy in a way that the Basin is not.

Rosa places a hand on your shoulder, grounding you as if offering a lever to lift the town by small, stubborn motions.

Rosa: "There is fear in the council chamber. Not all of it is bad. Fear can make us clever. But the ways they count us matter. Make them count us as people, not as pixels on a plan."

You close your journal and tuck it into your jacket. The pendant against your sternum feels like a pulse. You look at Elias — his wristband's faint blue beat; at Ivy — her hands stained with paint; at Rosa — steady and patient as tide-lines in old wood.

You are, all at once, ledger and salt and resolve.

Page-Turn: The hearing is hours away. As you walk back up toward the Strand, the Strand's light seems to hold its breath for you. You replay your opening line again in your head and the sound reverberates against the boards, the Basin, the harbor. Outside the city chamber, decisions will be made that will feel like weather — slow in approach but full of force. You can feel the precise, terrible, intimate scale of what is at stake: not just infrastructure, but the ledger of people's lives. You press your palm into your journal, feeling the damp paper and the pulse beneath the cloth.
