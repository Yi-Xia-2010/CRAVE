You repeat the words — a quiet hammer in your chest — and wonder if "behind" is technical support or something warmer, something that might stay when the room goes cold.

The Tideworks container smells of algae and solvent, warm and marine. Inside, lamps pool light on jars and devices; a long bench holds half-assembled reef modules, strips of living mesh threaded with young oyster spat, and dozens of tiny sensors — Theo's handiwork, you think, because only Theo loves tinkering to the point of obsession.

Theo looks up before you finish taking off your scarf. He has the grin he keeps for hopeful things, the kind that tries to insist this morning will bend to them.

Theo: "Mara. You made it. Coffee? Or—" (he gestures at a battered thermos) "—saltwater tea, and the usual moral support?"

You: "Coffee. And yes. The moral support platter, please. We're going to need it."

Theo watches you with a frank sympathy that feels like a friend holding an umbrella over your shoulders even when the sky is only threatening. He pulls a chipped mug toward you, the steam carrying a sharp edge of citrus and scorched grain.

[Visual: Close-up — bench with reef module, juvenile barnacles clinging; sensor wristband glows faintly on Elias's table]
[Sound: Soft beeping from a monitor]

Elias is crouched at the far workbench, sleeves rolled, sensors spread like a geologic field of tiny lighthouses. His watch is on the bench beside a sketch of a breakwater cross-section, annotations in his neat hand. When he looks up, there's a slow, careful measuring in his eyes — the way someone reads a tide chart for hidden patterns.

Elias: (quiet) "You look tired."  
You: "You have no idea." (You clear your throat.) "How are the tests?"

Elias: "The modules are holding against the scaled wave tank cycles. The biofilm adhesion rate is within our projected tolerance for three months of storms." (He rubs a thumb along a sensor wire.) "But scaling is where money eats time. And —" He hesitates, the sentence unfinished and heavier for it.

You step closer, smelling the salt of his parka and the faint metallic tang of the lab. Up close his hands are steady; his voice is too. He is always steadier in labs, you think, as if scientific certainty is a kind of armour.

You: "And the funding?"

Elias: (flat) "Shifts. The council's priorities just moved on a new timeline." (He keeps his gaze level, but you see the small clench in his jaw.) "Their projection models want numbers for immediate impact. Living systems don't make neat graphs on the timescale they want."

He shows you a tablet with a chart. The shaded region where your breakwater would dampen wave energy is promising but thin: the confidence interval still hugs the lower bound. Theo leans in, fingers tapping a section.

Theo: "We can tweak sensor calibration to show more immediate effectiveness on the pilot buoys." (His voice holds that mischievous conviction that believes in workarounds.) "We can demonstrate localized reduction in erosion within weeks."

You: (inward) The word "demonstrate" tastes hollow — a demo for cameras, not a life saved. Your chest tightens. The vote is hours away; the city wants a policy that will show immediate results for fundraising and political capital. You feel the old ledger of promise and loss slide under your ribs.

Your stomach tightens. The presentation is a polished thing — the fonts, the rhetorical cadence, the animation of rising costs followed by falling risk. It is persuasive in the way a winter storm is persuasive: inevitable if you accept certain premises.

Elias: (murmurs) "They're pitching hard."

Theo: "Propaganda-grade slides." (He half-laughs, then frowns.) "If people see that and hear the numbers, it will sway the votes."

You: (inward) You remember Rosa's hand on your shoulder, the way she said the ways they count you matter. On the screen, residents are reduced to projected outcomes and budget lines. It feels like erasure.

You: "Do it. Anything that keeps this from becoming a choice about decimals and not lives."

Elias' hand hesitates over "send" like someone holding a leash taut over a cliff. There's a look between you — neither of you spell it out, but the lab holds your unvoiced agreement.
