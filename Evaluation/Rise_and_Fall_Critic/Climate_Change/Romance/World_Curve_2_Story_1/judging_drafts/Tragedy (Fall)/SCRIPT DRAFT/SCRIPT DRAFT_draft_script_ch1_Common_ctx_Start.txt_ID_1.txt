[Scene: The Strand | Before Dawn]
[Visual: The boardwalk blurred in pre-dawn haze; gulls silhouette the low sky]
[Music: Low, mournful piano with distant percussion]
[Sound: Soft creak of wet wood, distant horn of a returning trawler]

You wake with salt in your throat and the taste of tomorrow already on your tongue. The Strand is a strip of patched memories — planks different widths, nails driven in at angles someone swore they'd fix last year. Your jacket smells faintly of diesel and seaweed; the collar of your sweater is rough against your jaw where you pulled it up against the wind. A thin gray light is spilling over the horizon, turning puddles to low mirrors. Your field journal, heavy with pressed seaweed and scrawled lists, rests on your knees like a legal document you can't afford to lose.

You sit up and take inventory by the feel of things: damp leather of the journal, the compass pendant cold against your throat, a pebble of grit in your palm. Outside, a planking somewhere farther down the boardwalk flexes and sighs. The taste of old guilt rises with the morning chill—remembering the harbor that couldn't hold your father's boat, the ledger of promises you keep balancing in your head.

You rub at the sleep stuck behind your eyes and stand. Your boots leave wet prints across the salt-streaked wood.

Narration
You walk the Strand like someone scanning a shoreline for survivors: measuring the damage in the broken railings, in the nets draped like ragged sails, in the new scar where the storm took a support beam. The community's handiwork is everywhere — different woods, different patches — and every repair is a story of someone bending to make do. You know each story; you helped stitch many of them. Today, those stitches feel thin.

[Scene: Boardwalk Meeting Point | Dawn]
[Visual: A cluster of salvaged chairs around a patched tarpaulin; rope hung with a faded community banner]
[Sound: Soft voices, the slap of a distant dinghy against hull]

Ivy is waiting beneath the banner, hands tucked into paint-streaked pockets. Her hair is a messy bun, and she's humming to keep her teeth from chattering. When she sees you, relief softens the lines around her eyes like rain on metal.

Ivy: "You're up early even for you. Thought maybe you'd sleep through the vote deadline."

You: "Sleep doesn't help when the tide's taking more than it gives." (You pull your scarf tighter, feeling the moth-eaten corner brush your cheek.) "What did you find at the Basin?"

Ivy leans in, voice small but steady. She speaks like someone who has to say the words aloud to make them true.

Ivy: "Mangrove saplings survived in the south bed. A lot of the young volunteers worked through the night to shore the racks. Rosa says they did good work, but the inlet's got scoured edges—roots hanging like exposed fingers. We can hold it for a bit, but it needs more than rope and hope."

You close your eyes for a moment and picture the roots, the way you used to run your fingers along your father's boat's hull and find the places the sea had already planned to take. You feel the old paper-thin guilt—if you'd been younger, faster, if you'd listened when he said to sell the boat—it's a familiar ache. But the Strand needs a plan, not self-flagellation.

Rosa approaches from the stilted walkway that leads to the Basin, her shawls gathering damp like talismans. The smell of warmed tea and salt follows her; she moves with the deliberate cadence of someone who learned patience as a form of resistance.

Rosa: "Mara. Ivy." (She takes your hands in both of hers, a small ritual.) "They'll listen to the numbers at City Hall. They will hear Jun speak with charts and a steady voice. They always believe the charts."

You: "Charts don't tell who will pack a box or who will stay. They don't know how a child learns to fish from a parent or how we build a night-shift of volunteers from scraps." (Your voice is low; you feel it pull at something tight behind your ribs.)

Rosa: (quietly) "True. But numbers move policy, and policy moves money. We need both — the heart and the ledger. You speak tonight. You will say that."

The wind picks up—sharp as a question—and your scarf whistles against your collar. Rosa watches you as if weighing the bruise inside you against the steadiness you show the rest of the community.

Rosa: "You carry the burden well, little Kestrel. But burdens stone the rivers if you carry them alone."

You: "I know." (You don't say that you don't know if you can carry it differently, if asking for help will make you feel less or weaker. You look at Ivy and Rosa, and the ledger in your head shifts from a list of debts to something else: a map you might still redraw.)

Ivy studies your face like someone reading a tide chart.

Ivy: "We can do both. We shore the Basin and get Ellen to talk to her contacts at the cooperative. But Mara—" (her voice softens) "—don't take all of it on. Rosa's right."

You: "I'll try." The promise is brittle but sincere. You tuck your journal closer, feeling each line as a strap around what you care for. You can see the hearing in the not-yet-light: City Hall's glass atrium, screens blinking budgets and forecasts, Jun with his polished coat and ribboned charts, Councilwoman Lian's neutral gaze. You sense the machinery that can flatten a neighborhood into a one-line bullet point.

[Scene: The Basin | Mid-Dawn]
[Visual: Low-lying garden cradled in a shallow basin; puddles reflect a brittle sky]
[Sound: Wet boots, distant caw of gulls, a soft murmur of volunteer voices]

The Basin is quieter than the Strand. Mud clings to the edges of your boots. Young mangroves lean together like a congregation, leaves trembling. Your hands smell of wet earth and something older—tea leaves burned to keep the volunteers warm through the night.

Theo is setting up a temporary drip system on one of the raised beds, muttering to himself about valves and the patience of plastics. He looks up when you pass, offering a half-grin that doesn't reach his eyes. There's a fatigue in the volunteers today—an exhaustion layered on top of worry.

Theo: "Elias stopped by before dawn with sensors. Said he had to calibrate them against the incoming swell. He left this on the crate." (He hands you a small sensor node wrapped in oilcloth. There are faint water stains on the cloth.) "Said you'd want to see the baseline before the hearing."

You: (taking the node) "Thank you. Tell him—tell him I appreciate it. And that...we need numbers as much as hands." Your fingers trace the outer casing, feeling the faint green pulse of the device as if it had a heartbeat.

Theo: "Will do. Also, you okay? You look like you haven't slept since the last storm, maybe longer."

You: "I'm fine." You don't say that 'fine' is the same as papered-up fear. You don't say that you tuck yourself into the margins of the boardwalk at night because it's the only place you can watch the harbor and not fall apart in front of people who need you. You don't say that when you stand under the lighthouse ruins you can hear your father's laugh and the sea at once.

[Scene: Harbor Edge | Dawn]
[Visual: Low-slung containers and a converted Tideworks Lab shipping container glowing faintly with internal light]
[Sound: Metallic clank of a winch, a low electronic beep, the slap of a moored hull]

Elias is at the container, instrument wristband faintly pulsing green. He looks up as you approach; for a moment his face holds a quiet calculation, then something softer breaks through—like sunlight shifting through cloud. The harbor smells like algae and copper.

Elias: "Morning." (He folds his hands together like someone trying to slow a storm.) "I ran the sensor sweep twice. The living breakwater model shows lower wave energy in sheltered zones, but it's sensitive to placement and density. We can show them a controlled estimate, Mara—proof that mangrove-assisted setups and reef units reduce force at the shoreline. We just need to present the phased data."

You: "Phased data is good." (You mean 'good' as in actionable, like a spade. You also mean 'good' as in 'I can stand behind this.' But there's a part of you that knows showing numbers won't stop Jun's charts from swallowing the narrative.) "Will the council accept a pilot-based approach? Or will they see it as delaying the inevitable?"

Elias looks away toward the city lights across the water, where the glass atrium of City Hall glows like a cold heart.

Elias: "They like certainty—and Jun offers the kind of certainty budgets love. But our models are rigorous. If you speak tonight about community implementation pathways and show the pilot map, we can anchor public sentiment. Evidence plus a clear schedule is persuasive."

You: (quiet) "And if they aren't persuaded?"

Elias: (after a beat) "Then we keep refining. We insist on transparency. We don't let corners be cut." His jaw sets. There's more in his voice—a memory, perhaps, of a mentor lost to decisions made too quickly. He hesitates, then adds: "I'm coming to the hearing, Mara. If you want me there."

The admission lands in your chest like a small repair patch. You don't know whether it's professional solidarity or something softer; in the battered light it looks like an offered hand.

You: "Good." (Because you do want him there; because the idea of standing at a podium with him present steadies something inside you.) "I'll need the pilot map printed for the council. And your testimony on ecological uplift. Make it clear the pilot starts in neighborhoods like ours."

Elias: "I will." He hesitates, then looks at you with an expression that feels like confession posed as a fact. "Mara—if we do this, we do it together. For the community and for accurate data. No promises we can't back."

You: "That's all I can ask—for now." The promise in your voice is a small, steady thing.

Narration
Your fingers find the compass at your throat. You don't consult it for direction now; you use it to steady your pulse. The harbor hums with small mechanical life, and somewhere in the distance a large vessel blows a long, tired horn. You feel the weight of the day like a tide: predictable for its turning but devastating in its reach.

From somewhere on the boardwalk, a council flyer flutters and sticks to a post—Jun's face is crisp in the municipal photograph, the plan's headline in bold type. The words "managed retreat zones" glare back at you like an accusation. You want to storm City Hall and tear the paper down. Instead, you tuck your journal under your arm and walk back toward the Basin, toward the people who will listen for more than a chart.

Rosa catches up to you as you cross the wet wooden walkway. She hands you a thermos. Steam coils from the spout, smelling like lemon and something bitter.

Rosa: "Drink. Speak clear." She looks at you with an intensity that is both blessing and prophecy. "And remember: you are not the storm."

You: "I know." The statement sounds smaller than it is. You want to add that you have been the storm sometimes—that anger can clear rubble in ways kindness cannot—but you swallow it. It's not the morning for confessions; it's the morning for preparation.

Narration
The morning light lifts a fraction. It doesn't warm the day; it just clarifies. You bind your journal closed and feel the seams in your hands where promise has been too often stretched. The city is waking, and along with it the machinery of decisions that will carve futures. You are carrying the ledger—literal and not—and you know how to balance books. Tonight you'll read the entries out loud.

You look once more at the harbor, at the recovered mangroves in the Basin, at your friends who have come to stand on the edge of the map with you. The hearing is a line in the distance. Ahead of you: paper, maps, pilot models, the peculiar courage of asking a room to trust a community's hands.
