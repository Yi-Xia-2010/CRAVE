You climb the berm alone. The wind finds the dipped tips of your hair and tugs lightly. Below, the boardwalk is dim, people moving like small shadows toward makeshift shelters and donation points. You have learned a terrible calculus—that saving many sometimes means hollowing out the periphery where one's life felt anchored.

You fold the compass into your palm and close your fingers around it. The compass doesn't tell you what to choose next. It sits steady, an heirloom of direction more than instruction.

You breathe in the sharp salt and the cleaner scent of repaired machines. Somewhere down the shore Jonah's laugh—thin, brittle—breaks against the night. You want to go to him. You look out over Tidehaven—the safe channel, the lonely coves—and let the weight of both settle. The town will not be the same. You will not be the same. The sea will keep changing its mind, and you will keep making choices in rooms that smell of peat, salt, and the stubborn hope that another morning will bring repair.
