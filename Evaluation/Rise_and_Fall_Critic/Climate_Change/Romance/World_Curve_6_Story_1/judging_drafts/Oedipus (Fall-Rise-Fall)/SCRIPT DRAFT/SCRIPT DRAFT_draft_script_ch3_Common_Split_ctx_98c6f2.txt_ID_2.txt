[Scene: Tidehaven Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Sound: Bell toll, low and distant]
[Music: Slow, minor strings; wind-swept undertone]

You step back onto the boardwalk with your field jacket pulled a little higher against the wind. The planks beneath your boots smell of damp wood and tar; salt scratches at the corners of your mouth. Fog hangs low like a soft bruise, swallowing the farther piers into gray. The harbor is a flat sheet of silver, the horizon a seam you want to mend with something other than policy.

You thumb the seam of your jacket because your fingers want a map. Your hands have learned to read other people's losses like topography — who was here before the water came, what can be rebuilt, what will always have a scar. The town's lines feel like ridges under your skin: the fisherman's alley, the greenhouse's heat, the old shipyard's shadow. You carry those lines with you like tide charts folded into memory.

Bento's voice is in the back of your head, a dredged-up sound from the hallway: You left, Mira. It settles like silt under everything—simple, unavoidable. You don't know how the word hangs in others' mouths now: accusation, fact, a wounded observation. It is complex in everyone who hears it, including you. The accusation and the truth are both heavy and true, layered like the town's sediment.

You let the fog press on your face. The shore smells of peat and iron, and gulls wheel with a soft, disapproving cry. Your phone vibrates in your pocket with a visit-from-everyone list of messages: council follow-ups, a note from Dr. Zhou, an emoji from Kai that reads like insistence. You let it buzz; for a moment the hiss of the tide is louder than any notification.

[Sound: The low creak of planks; a distant engine coughs and dies]

You walk toward the town hall because you promised to present more than uncertainty. The building's bricks smell of old coffee and paper petitions pinned with candle-creased corners. Council maps are taped to corkboards near the entrance, their edges curling like shells. Evelyn waits in the lobby, the same tired faith in her posture that you remember—hand on a folder, eyes on you for the answer you do not yet have.

Evelyn Sato: "We need it by next week, Mira. People are already asking where their vote went. I can't stall them—funders won't wait for consensus that never forms."

You: "I won't stall. I just—" You swallow the sentence before it finishes; honesty tastes like a salt-soaked coin. "I need to make sure whatever we recommend isn't just viable on paper."

Evelyn Sato: "Viable on paper doesn't always help someone whose house is on the line." (She folds her hands; worry is a soft sound at the edges.) "But neither does indecision."

Her eyes flick to the bell tower across the square. The bell's last toll vibrates in your sternum. You feel the gravity of the town's expectant faces already pinching your shoulders.

[Scene: Tidehaven Town Hall — Corridor | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Narrow hallway lined with bulletin boards; light tinged amber]
[Sound: Footsteps echo; a low murmuring of council members dispersing]

Bento Morais is leaning against a pillar when you pass, his fisher's coat smelling of smoke and old nets. He doesn't raise his head at first; his gaze sinks into something far away. When he looks up, the expression is not simple. It is a collection of weathered lines—pride, disappointment, memory.

Bento Morais: (low) "You left, Mira."

You have known he would say it. You have also known that hearing it again will not dull the ache. The corridor tastes of lemon cleaner and pencil shavings. The words land the way a wave lands—insistent and ordinary.

You: (quiet) "I know."

Bento studies the way your jaw tightens, the way your hands flex as if to anchor something. He doesn't step closer; he has never been demonstrative. Still, the silence between you is not empty—it's full of shared storms, of the boat your father loved, of the years you walked away to learn how to measure the sea's mood.

Bento Morais: "We need someone who understands both the land and the ledger. Don't let the letters on a council report be all you remember."

You: "I won't. I—" The sentence catches. "I don't plan to forget what it means to lose a harbor."

He studies you with a look that could break or bless. You can't tell which, and that ambiguity steadies you in a way.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Research Lab | Evening]
[Visual: Glass panes streaked with salt; warm lab lights against the dim outside]
[Sound: Hum of monitors; the distant drip of condensate from greenhouse tubing]
[Music: Subtle electronic pulse under wind noise]

The lab smells of wet soil and clean plastic. Dr. Lian Zhou stands over a monitor, her sleeves rolled, a smear of sediment at the cuff. The screen shows topographic layers in teal and ochre, animated timelines flicking forward like a heartbeat. A five-year projection runs across the center; wetlands in teal fracture into tatters as the slider moves. You glance at the numbers; they read like an indictment.

Dr. Lian Zhou: (calmly) "These are the median scenarios. Under current funding and intervention levels, we're watching a steady retreat—marsh edges recede by nearly thirty percent in high-tide seasons. If we assume increased storm frequency, those numbers become steeper."

You lean closer, breath fogging the edge of the screen. The colors feel less like data and more like places losing their edges. Your chest tightens as you imagine those lines crossing someone's front stoop.

You: "Can localized restoration slow that curve? Buy us time?"

Dr. Lian Zhou: "It can, depending on scale and maintenance. The models are sensitive to sustained volunteer effort and regular sediment augmentation. But coral-scale protections like berms change the flow; they can preserve certain tracts at the expense of others."

Her words land like small, precise stones. You realize each solution shifts the problem elsewhere—what you save someone gets taken from someone else. The models do not account for love; they count acreage and elevation.

[Sound: Door opens; footsteps approach]

Asha Verma arrives with that efficient stride of someone used to carrying a plan across rooms full of people who expect her authority. Her coat is slate-gray; the smart fabric picks up the lab's light. She does not waste a gesture; her eyes go immediately to the projections, to the timeline, to you.

Asha Verma: (direct) "We can build a sea-gate pilot here. Reinforced berms along the most vulnerable corridor. Enforceable retreat zones for the lowest parcels. It will cost, but the model shows it's the fastest way to reduce exposure."

You: (measured) "Enforceable retreat zones—people lose property, memory. How do you sell that to a town whose history is its shoreline?"

Asha's lips press into a line that is almost sympathy and almost impatience.

Asha Verma: "You sell it with certainty. You sell it with fewer evacuees in an extreme storm. You sell it with numbers that mean fewer lost lives. Sometimes salvage requires hard, definitive action. We need someone to lead the technical deployment."

Her gaze on you is not pleading; it is operational. There is an implicit offer tucked into the sentence: lead this, and you help write the rules. The weight of the offer sits in the room like an unlatched gate.

You: "And what of culture? Of the harbor? A sea-gate is a clinic diagnosis—correct, but blunt. It amputates parts of what people live by."

Asha Verma: (softening fractionally) "Cultural loss is real. I don't deny it. But if this town is washed into a cautionary tale, there will be no culture left to mourn."

The words are clean, devastating in their clarity. You feel a lump forming at the base of your throat. There is logic in Asha's proposal, and an ethical hardness you cannot ignore.
