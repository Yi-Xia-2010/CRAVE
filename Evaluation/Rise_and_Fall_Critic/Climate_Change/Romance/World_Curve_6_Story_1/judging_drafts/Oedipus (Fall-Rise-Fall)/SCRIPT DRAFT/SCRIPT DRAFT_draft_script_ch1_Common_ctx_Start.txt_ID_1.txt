You step off the bus and the world rearranges itself into the scale you remember: wind, salt, and the tired rhythm of a place that has learned to keep going even when the ocean answers back. Fog eats the edges of the boardwalk, softening neon into ghostly pools. The air is a braid of salt and diesel, and it smells—exactly—like the last thing you did before you left. Your palms press around the weatherproof notebook at your chest; the cover is scuffed, the tide charts inside folded to familiar margins. Holding it feels like holding a heartbeat.

The wooden planks are slick with last night's spray. You slide one foot forward, feel the give underboard, the cold seeping through the soles of your boots. Your hair—sea-green tips tucked into the knot at your nape—keeps catching the wind. You are, for the first real time in years, very small in the middle of everything that made you leave.

Across the boardwalk Jonah is leaning against the iron rail, elbows planted, scarf flapping. He looks like he never quite learned to grow up: sun-bleached tips, the same slow grin that used to get him into trouble. When he sees you he nods, that skeptical smile curving at the corners of his eyes—half welcome, half appraisal. The ache behind your ribs tightens in reflex; the memory of your father's boat and the storm that took it slides in like a cold current. The town folds that history around you; you can't not feel it.

Jonah: "You finally decided to show up, Mira. Or did the bus drivers union stage an intervention?"
You: "It was the bus, not them. It made a stop. I'm here."

Jonah laughs like he's trying to iron something out with warmth; his hands are steady and salt-dark. He shifts, and a small Polaroid peeks from his coat pocket—one more photograph of the harbor, taken last month; a testament and a worry. He tucks it back with a grimace.

Jonah: "Good. We need you—well, the town needs you. I mean. The lab needs you. And me? I need you to stop promising the town you'd fix everything and then vanishing."
You: (You hear your voice and dislike its brittle edges.) "I never promised—"
Jonah: (His brow softens, then hardens.) "No. You left. You left to learn, then you came back to tell us how to save ourselves. Some people—some people want to hear that from someone who didn't leave."

His words are gentle, but they land like stones. You open your mouth to argue and instead fold your fingers around the compass at your throat. The brass is warm from your skin. Your mother's compass—small and decisive—sits against your sternum, a counterweight to the guilt.

From the rail near Jonah, an old figure straightens. Bento—Old Bento—is a weather-line itself: stooped shoulders, hands like carved driftwood, cap pulled low. He holds onto the railing, eyes on the water as if reading some shifting script.

Bento: (gravel-voice) "You come back with the city smell on you, Mira. Salt can't be scrubbed off, but city plans try. They got a smell, too. I hope it ain't the scent of retreat."

You: "I'm not here to tell people to run, Bento. I'm here to—" (you hesitate) "—figure out what keeps people here without them getting swallowed."

Bento studies you with slow patience, the sort that measures years not minutes.

Bento: "Words are cheap as slatwood. You fix things with hands or you fix them with maps. Both have merit. Don't forget the people who remember each net, each anchor. They teach you where to put the hands."

His pragmatic tenderness bruises in the place where your guilt sits. You find yourself explaining more than you intended.

You: "I ran because I thought knowledge would make up for being gone. I came back because I couldn't let Tidehaven drown on someone else's schedule. I—" (you close off, because confession is a tide you don't trust.)

Jonah: (soft, reluctant) "Then show us, Mira. Not to impress the council. Show us where to put our hands."

A shout from further down the boardwalk catches the thread of conversation. Mayor Evelyn Sato stands near the old bait shop’s flickering neon—placards slung over one arm, breath warm against the cold morning. Her hair is silver-threaded, scarf a faded campaign color; she spots you both and approaches with the quick step of someone who has spent years running toward crises instead of away.

Evelyn: (calling) "Mira! Thank goodness you're back. We were afraid—" (she stops herself) "—I mean, your timing might be—difficult, but necessary."

You: "Difficult is... the word I was expecting."

Evelyn's face is both relief and ledger. She looks at Jonah, then at Bento, then at you with the practiced softness of a politician who has learned to hold a room while weighing votes.

Evelyn: "Council wants to hear your views for the pilot restoration. Asha Verma's team sent pamphlets this morning—big plans, concrete, quick timelines. Dr. Zhou's model shows options. We have to choose what we pilot this afternoon."

You feel it then: the slow, compressing weight of decisions. Asha's logo—the city planner's emblem—stares up from a pamphlet Evelyn hands you, minimalist and clinical. The glossy paper smells faintly of printer ink and urgency. Where your notebook is ragged, the pamphlet is immaculate. The presence of it in your hands is a small, physical sting.

Evelyn: (lowering her voice) "People are nervous. Some want walls, some want marshes, and the developers are circling. The meeting might get loud. I need you there, Mira. You understand the data and the people."

You: "I'll be there. I'll—" (you hesitate, tasting the edges of what 'there' might cost.)

Evelyn nods, but you can see the ledger keepers in her eyes. Support is conditional; every good word is balanced against votes and funding.

[Sound: A gull cries; the faint whisper of distant machinery on the horizon]

You cross the boardwalk toward the harbor, Jonah falling into step beside you. The harbor is quieter than in your memory—boats tied down, many with patched planks and tarps. The ebb shows the mud, the ribs of dockwork. A single flickering neon sign over a shuttered bait shop throws a dying pink over algae-slick ropes.

Jonah: "You going to the lab first? Because if you bring the lab into the meeting without checking how the fishermen read the models, you're going to get a room full of angry folks and a slammed door."

You: "And if I don't bring the models, the council will say we're guessing and they'll hand the grant to someone with a prettier brochure. I need both sides present, Jonah."

Jonah: (frustrated, earnest) "That's exactly the problem. Everyone's an expert from a different angle. We need someone who can stand in the middle without being flattened."

You take in the row of small boats, the way someone’s hand has repaired a hull with a metal patch that gleams like a scar. Your notebook is a rosary of tide marks and notes; each page is an apology and a plan.

You: (thinking) Maybe you can't stand in the middle without getting pulled apart. Maybe trying is the kind of bravery that looks like stubbornness.

A youth—Kai—scurries past, a safety vest unzipped and seed packets peeking from his backpack. He slaps Jonah on the shoulder and rushes off to the Greenhouse Collective, breathless and full of heat.

The building is both ruin and potential, and your chest feels cramped with the knowledge that all of Tidehaven is trying to hold two truths at once: that the sea is an enemy when it floods, but that retreat is a wound if it erases the stories that taught people how to live here.

At the lab, Dr. Lian Zhou's greenhouse extension gleams like a promise. Through salt-streaked panes you can see benches cluttered with sediment cores and monitors that light up teal and ochre topographies. You can almost hear the models humming, patient and implacable.

You: (to yourself, quiet) "Data doesn't forgive, but it doesn't forget either. Maybe that's how you reconcile memory—turn it into something that prevents another storm from taking what my father lost."

A lab tech opens the door and hands you a data printout. It's dense, full of lines and careful legends. Your fingers smell faintly of printer ink and marsh. You press the printout to your chest for a second, the way you pressed other things for comfort when you were young.

Then Evelyn's earlier words come back: council, meeting, choices that might need to be made before sunset. The pamphlet with Asha's logo sits in your bag like a splintered plan. You flip open your notebook; the tide charts stare back with neat, patient rows.

The salt-thin light is growing; the lab smells of peat and warm electronics. You stand on the threshold, the world tilting toward an afternoon that could reorder everything—funding, lives, who stays and who leaves.

You touch the brass of your mother's compass again. It is steady. Your notebook is heavy with possibilities and debts.

A shadow crosses the lab's glass—a sleek silhouette of someone moving inside—and for a second the town's future is a blurred outline where two pathways converge: slow, patient marsh restoration and fast, decisive infrastructure. Both have faces. Both have price tags. Both will demand that people give something away.

You step in.

You set down your notebook, breath fogging the glass, and look at the models on the big screen. The meeting is hours away. The weight of returning is not a moment but a colliding of obligations—family, memory, science, and a town that will ask you to pick a side or, impossibly, to make space for both.

Your heart is a slow, stubborn drum. Outside, Tidehaven waits with counting hands.

You walk toward the workbench to organize your notes. There is no grand epiphany—only the steady narrowing of the day and the knowledge that by evening some lines will have to be drawn.
