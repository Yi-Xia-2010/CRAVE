Beside you, Jonah's hand is warm and tight around yours—an anchor that feels, in this moment, both fragile and necessary. Behind you, the shipyard's silhouette is ragged: coir logs, burlap bundles, half-erected planting mats like defiant teeth against the gray.

Salt fills your mouth when the surge hits. The air smells of peat and something harsher—burning diesel from a tug, smoke from hurried fires on low lawns. You taste ash that has nothing to do with fire and everything to do with the part of you that has been waiting to be blamed.

You thought the seedlings might be enough. You thought neighbors would see the slow work and understand that living shorelines ask for time. You thought—because you had to—that small, local things would stitch into safety.

The first coir log snaps in a white, sudden sound you almost miss because the surge is louder. Water eats the low yard beside the Old Navarro place, curling in secret fingers until a favorite chair lists and floats away like a confession.

You open your mouth and close it. Words would be a ledger right now, not a comfort.

Neighbors call across the wreck: names folded in wet voices. Someone mentions cottages that have never flooded before. [Scene: Shipyard — Night Aftermath]
[Visual: Lanterns hung on scaffolds; makeshift tents; a line of quilts drying in the wind]
[Sound: Murmured prayers, the clink of a kettle, intermittent sobs]
[Music: Low hum; a cello holds a single, mournful note]

By dawn the storm's teeth are blunt. The harbor looks smaller—not by distance, but by what can no longer claim the title of home. Low-lying parcels that were the harbor's margin are now a new inlet. The shoreline redraws itself in the neighborhood maps tacked on the town hall's doors.

You make rounds—check on Mrs. Navarro's sagging porch, help with a child shivering in borrowed wool, carry a damp photograph from a neighbor's soaked box to a dry corner. Each task stitches small shelter into the long, ragged wound of loss.

Jonah watches you move and then sits on a salt-dark pallet, hands clasped like he can contain the rage in a bowl. He doesn't say the harshest thing. He looks instead at the harbor and then at you with something unreadable layered over the anger—love, exhaustion, a future that suddenly feels unsteady.

Jonah Reyes: "We built what we could. God help me, Mira, I wanted more."
Mira Kestrel: "I wanted more too." (You say it and mean it. It is the simple arithmetic of regret.)

You replay the council room in your head—the clipped exchange with Asha, your insistence on local control, Bento's slow nod, Evelyn's weary compromise. Whether the installations were fewer or larger, the outcome has the same shape tonight: tide rearranged, dear things moved or gone. The town is smaller, mosaic-ed into what could be defended and what could not.

Bento finds you near the Polaroid in your wet notebook. He presses a callused finger against the saltwater stain where the photo curled.

Bento Morais: "You did what you could. It won't fix the porches. It won't pull old boats back from the mud. But it kept doors from opening in places we could not—"
Mira Kestrel: "And it failed others." (The admission breaks you in steady pieces.)

He doesn't argue. He only sets a hand on your shoulder—a weight, an unspoken permission to be both proud and sorrowful. Bento's grief is not theatrical; it is the concentrated grief of someone who has seen entire seasons of tides and lives and knows how both erode.

You fold your hands around the compass at your throat. It is cold, a small anchor you have always trusted to point you toward something true. Tonight it points at a horizon you cannot fix with plans or good intentions.

Jonah rises and takes a breath as if to speak, then collapses back into silence. The space between you is not empty—it is full of all the things you could not prevent and the ones you tried to preserve.

Mira Kestrel: "What do we say to them? To the people who lost more than I can bear to list?" (You ask aloud what you have asked yourself in private for months.)
Jonah Reyes: "We say the truth. We say we tried. We say we'll rebuild what we can—and we'll keep counting the cost." (His voice breaks at the last few words but holds.)

You know the words will not be enough. They are the first ember toward a longer fire: rebuilding, negotiating reparations, petitions for adaptive funds, the interminable paperwork that will follow grief. You picture future council meetings where the maps are redrawn, where the town may shrink or shift, where someone like Asha will weigh lives at the scale of many and not the sentiment of a few.

Your mind returns to the Polaroid—a document of what used to be, now a wet relic. You slide it into your notebook and feel the grain of paper under your thumb like a pulse.

You stand alone for a moment, looking out across what remains. The town's silhouette is honest: awkward repairs, barricades, prayer-heaped piles of salvaged wood. Tidehaven is smaller tonight, rearranged. The word lingers—smaller—like a bruise you rub with thumb and hope it will fade.

You think of your mother’s compass pendant, of the reason you left and the reason you returned. You had imagined this work would be a ledger you could balance with time and care. The ledger today is stained.

You inhale the cold; the salt and smoke and peat make a fragrance that belongs to no holiday. It smells like endurance.

Bento's quiet voice comes from behind you.

Bento Morais: "People will write into this season the story they need—blame or praise. But stories change. They are not the water." (He offers a small, crooked smile.)
Mira Kestrel: "What will we do with the stories?" (You ask.)
Bento Morais: "Keep them honest. Keep them full." (He taps his chest where memory lives.)

Jonah approaches and stands slightly behind you, not quite close enough to erase the night's barbs but near enough to be a presence you can feel.

Jonah Reyes: "We hold on to what we can. We teach the next to move sand and plant cordgrass and read a tide line." (He meets your eyes finally.)
Mira Kestrel: "We do the work—again. We make room for those we could not save tonight." (You say it as both promise and penance.)

The town around you is already doing just that: neighbors offering beds, boats being redirected, petitions drafted, a council meeting scheduled that will taste of salt and anger and the careful arithmetic of loss. The communal muscle bends toward repair even when the heart is raw.

You let the compass hang heavy at your throat, and for the first time in a long while you do not try to turn it toward a neat answer. It is only a tool; it points. You will follow the pointing into the messy tallitudes of civic life.

The losses will be named. The small victories will be catalogued. Some relationships will fray and some will bind tighter. You cannot make the harbor what it was. You can steward what is left.

You turn to Jonah and Bento, then to the harbor—a landscape of salvage and small, stubborn lights. You think of all the hands that will come back tomorrow. You think of the council chairs and petitions and the long, unromantic work of policy and prayer.

Bento Morais: "We'll rebuild—differently, maybe smaller. But we'll do it together." (There is no triumph in his voice, only the hard, necessary constancy of mornings after night.)

You stand there in the brittle light. Tidehaven is changed and smaller, but it is not gone. The town will define this season in many ways—some will call it failure, some salvation. You will carry the bruise of responsibility for as long as the compass sits against your skin.

For now, you breathe. The harbor breathes. People hammer, someone sings an off-key song to a child, someone else weeps into a coat. The world is unromantic and exactly as it should be—full of people who will refuse to let loss be the only story.
