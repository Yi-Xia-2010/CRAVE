You watch him: the way his jaw tightens, the impulse to leap into labor coiled in his shoulders. You know his logic — direct requisition can shore up ten blocks by next tide if the crew moves fast. You also know that moving fast without careful sealing and pumps invites failure.

Elias Hart [softening]: "So you go ask the engineers for a favor. You think she will slide a pump under our noses and wink?"

Asha Rivera: "Not wink. A conditional pilot. Hands-on oversight. Shared logs. No exclusivity." (You glance at him, an unspoken admission between you.) "And — Elias — I want us to stay in control. Not sidelined."

Elias exhales, a laugh like a half-release. He steps closer, lowering his voice.

Elias Hart: "Okay. If this is your line, I'm with you. Just—be careful, ash. Don't let their machines become their rules."

Asha Rivera: "I won't." (You mean it. You feel the steady pull of responsibility, the familiar knot of stubborn optimism that got you through the last storm.)

[Scene: Skyward Market (Rooftop Greenhouse) | Twilight]
[Visual: Glass domes fogged with breath and steam; hydroponic beds glow faintly turquoise; strings of home‑made lanterns sway]
[Sound: Soft murmur of growers closing up, the hiss of an automated drip system, distant city hum]

The greenhouse smells like damp earth and lemon peel — every plant a witness. Hana is already there when you arrive, standing by a table of seedlings. Her AR monocle catches the light, a pale coin. She wears her usual precision like armor: sleeves rolled, notes open on a tablet. But when she looks up at you, there's a crease at the corner of her mouth that isn't entirely clinical.

Hana Kim: "You came alone." (There is a small way she says it — not accusation, more like a check of weather.)

Asha Rivera: "I wanted it to be quiet. I wanted to be honest first." (You let that sit: a soft admission that still feels risky.)

Hana Kim [a breath]: "Honesty is part of engineering, in its own way. Tell me what you want."

You explain the terms you imagine: a limited pilot along the south stretch, composite mesh anchored to living posts, a pair of smart pumps integrated with community-operated valves, and—most important—an open dashboard that the barrio can read and audit. You voice the safeguards that will prevent corporate overreach: community maintenance protocols, local hiring clauses, an agreement that data ownership stays with the neighborhood.

Hana Kim listens, not interrupting. Her fingers tap an absent rhythm on the tablet; occasionally she narrows her eyes and maps your words into architecture in real time. Her breathing is steady; the plants behind her lean toward the humid light like patient witnesses.

Hana Kim: "Those are strict conditions."

Asha Rivera: "They need to be." (You meet her gaze head-on. You want this to work because you understand both halves: the value of engineering and the soul of the neighborhood.)

Hana Kim: "I work within systems that require measurable outcomes." (She gestures at the tablet.) "If I bring Aegis in at all, I will have to satisfy modelers, risk assessments, corporate boards. But models can be tuned, and boardrooms can be steered. I can make the tech work to your constraints — if you give me the parameters I can translate."

You both pause. The greenhouse hums: a living chorus. In the space between your words, something else passes — a long-dormant intimacy that isn't loud but is solid in its own right. It is the kind of hush that comes when two people who once built things together realize the scaffolding remained.

Hana Kim [softer]: "I didn't expect you'd ask. I... I thought you'd go loud. You always surprised me." (Her voice carries a small, careful hope.)

Asha Rivera: "Maybe I surprised myself." (You smile, and the smile is a small, honest thing.) "I want to keep what we have. I want the barrio to stay rooted. If that means learning your models and teaching them how to listen, then that's what I have to do."

Hana Kim [a rare unguarded glance]: "And if I open doors that smell like suits, you'll tell me when I'm crossing a line."

Asha Rivera: "I will." (The promise is steady; the words weigh right when spoken aloud.)

You accept the binder with two hands. It is full of minutes, of legalese shaped to community needs, of official nods to local oversight. It is small and dense and exactly the kind of thing that will keep this scheme alive when tides threaten.

You walk the seawall edge one last time before noon; the pilot hums beneath your boots. The living posts sway faintly with the current of the water, their roots beginning to knit. You think of the children who will run along these walkways years from now and the stories they'll tell about the day the barrio braided itself into a living wall. You think of Jun's charts, of Mayor Ortega's binder, of Leon's inevitable phone calls. The work continues. The pilot is the first stanza of a longer song.

You close your eyes for a breath and feel, like a tide turning, the steady return of hope.
