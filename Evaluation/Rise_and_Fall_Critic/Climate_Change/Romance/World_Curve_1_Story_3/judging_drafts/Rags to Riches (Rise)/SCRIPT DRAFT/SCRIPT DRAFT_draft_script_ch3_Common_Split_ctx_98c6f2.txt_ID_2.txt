The gavel taps once. The hearing begins.

A hush collects. You set your notebook on the lectern and glance at the slide queued behind you: an image of a living seawall — layered native marsh plants woven into biodegradable frames, roots taking hold, children playing on the raised walkway behind it. It is both demonstration and promise.

You feel Hana's eyes (cool and unreadable) settle on you, then Elias' (open, impatient) burn like a sun. In their faces you read histories: collaboration and fracture, shared summers in the lab and shouted arguments at midnight. But whatever you once were together, today you are each standing in front of the city, offering visions.

You start with a breath, letting the strings in the room swell with you.

You: "When the first storm took my family's home, it wasn't just a house that disappeared. It was a set of routines, the smell of sofrito on the stove, the neighbors who'd show up with tarps. Living seawalls—" (you tap the screen) "—are about restoring those routines. They're engineered ecosystems that recover with us; they give time and habitat back to the shoreline, and they invite the community to be the ones who maintain them."

Hana leans forward, chin tilted with professional curiosity.

Hana: "Ms. Rivera, your community engagement is clear. But scalability is the issue. We can prototype living systems, yes — but when the next ten-year storm arrives, can we guarantee protection at a citywide scale without automated barriers, predictive pumps, and rapid-response tech?"

Her voice is precise, but not unkind. The question is practical; the subtext is a bridge she is offering and simultaneously testing.

You: "Guarantees are promises; they break. What we can build is resilient, distributed defenses that are maintained by people who live here. They don't require perfect uptime; they require presence. And presence is already here."

Elias can't help himself — he leans in, eyes bright, voice carrying the cadence of the street.

Elias: "And presence scales, Mayor. It's people. Teach ten neighborhoods to weave their seawalls together and you get citywide impact without begging for corporate-controlled systems that put labs in charge of our lives."

Leon Voss smiles thinly across the room, fingers tapping the arm of his chair.

Leon Voss: "Community energy is admirable. However, the city's exposure is measured in billions. Our firm can deploy semi-autonomous pump grids and modular barriers that complement living systems. We can fast-track deployment and offer maintenance guarantees."

Sofia interrupts, voice steady and warm, the immediate weight of homes in it.

Sofia: "Guarantees mean little if we lose the places we remember while 'fast-tracking' happens behind closed doors. I lost a stall last year. I need to know my granddaughter can play on the boardwalk next spring."

Mayor Lila listens, her hands folded like a ledger; a flicker of fatigue, but also of resolve, tightens the lines around her mouth.

Mayor Lila: "There are votes to consider and funding to allocate. Ms. Rivera, can a community-led approach integrate with company resources without ceding control?"

The question hangs. It's not a trap; it's a lever. Your throat tightens, not for lack of words but for the gravity of choosing one for the moment.

You: "Yes — if control is negotiated up front. If community oversight is codified. Living seawalls require local knowledge. We can build governance structures: shared maintenance schedules, transparent data access, joint audit processes. If Aegis or any provider wants to contribute, it must be under those terms."

Hana's expression softens a fraction — complex, unreadable — as if your mirror held not just a plan but a memory she negotiates with herself. Elias folds his arms, scanning you for signs of compromise or betrayal; his face opens when you emphasize the community thread.

Elias: "Make that explicit. Put people at the center. Strengthen volunteer brigades with training funds, not conditional contracts."

Leon: "And where I can, I will fund training. But the city needs assurances beyond goodwill."

You're aware of the clock, of the thinness of the moment. It could go sideways if you choose a word too sharp, or it could grow into something that lets both engines of the city run in sync — slow, living hands and precise, scalable tech.

Elias: "We teach people how to read those sensors. We don't let them become tools that only technicians understand."

Mayor Lila: "Before I call for public comment, is there anything the community wants to say?"
