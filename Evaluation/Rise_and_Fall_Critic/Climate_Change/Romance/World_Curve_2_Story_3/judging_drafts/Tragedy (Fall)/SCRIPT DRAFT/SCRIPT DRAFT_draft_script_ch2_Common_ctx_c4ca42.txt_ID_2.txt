Your jacket is crusted where the tide mud dries into salt; dirt has worked its way into the cuff where you tie seedlings. You can feel the faint ridges of the compass pendant against your sternum when you tilt your head.

Ivy is already here, laughing at something small and private; her braids bob as she hands you a length of biodegradable twine. Her vest is neon against the leaf-green terraces, and the sound of her laughter cracks the heavy thing inside you for a second — a good crack, the kind that lets light in.

Ivy Morales: "You're late. Or just dramatic with timing. Either way — perfect. Come look at this scaffold."

You: "Late considers itself dramatic, also guilty." (You lift a hand and brush soil from your palm.) "What's the panic this morning?"

Ivy's grin softens into a satisfied, hands-on seriousness as she points to the buoyant lattice they've been assembling — a modular scaffold of ropes and reclaimed plastic lattices, studded with pots of kelp and halophyte seedlings.

Ivy Morales: "If we can anchor these in a sheltered curve, they'll take wave energy and seed the living wall. Think nursery + armor. We test it here, scale later."

You crouch and let your fingertips trail over the wet edge of a planter. The plants are slick and young and full of the terrible, stubborn hope of life. Your notebook is tucked inside your jacket; the cover is scuffed, leaves pressed between its pages like tiny fossils. You can feel them there when you breathe. The memory of the boathouse roof — the way you watched it buckle and finally give — is a weight in your hands that refuses to be ignored.

Ariana arrives like a bright idea wrapped in a coat. Her eyes find the scaffold and light up as if the scaffold has just told her a joke she understands. She moves with the impatient joy of someone who thinks time is a tool to be bent.

Ariana Voss: "Morning. Look at this — modular frames could let us phase in beds gradually. If we pair them with sediment traps and community-run maintenance shifts, we could show a model that isn't top-down."

You: "Phase in how fast? There's logistics, materials, volunteer hours—"

Ariana Voss: (cutting in, breathless with possibility) "—and funding. And we could prototype at the north terrace, where the flow's calmer. Weeks, not months. If we take the boardwalk group with us, show them the prototype before the council meets, the visuals could change minds."

You feel the impulse to say yes — to ride the energy, to prove something real and immediate — but your fingers find the page with pressed leaves and your hand trembles when you turn it. Saying yes right now would stretch that promise thinner.

You: "Weeks is... fast. Rapid rollouts can look like miracles until something fails. If the scaffold's not vetted, or if maintenance falls through, we could make new vulnerabilities."

Ariana Voss: (eyes narrowing; she softens when she sees the tremor) "I know. I know. I'm not asking you to jump blind. I want you in because you think three steps ahead. Because you see the people behind the plan. Help me make the guardrails, not just the scaffolding."

A pause gathers between you, full of unsaid debts and obligations. Ivy leans in, planting a hand on your shoulder with a quiet, practical pressure that says she trusts you to steer.

Ivy Morales: "We'll run the volunteer training. Elder Tomas will talk to the older fishers about how we secure lines. You know how to stitch this together."

You: "I do." (A small, brittle affirmation.) "I know how to stitch. I also know how fast stitches fray if you don't make room for repair."

Ariana's optimism flickers — for a heartbeat it's impatient, then it becomes careful, deliberate, like someone rereading a sentence to make sure the meaning is clear.

Ariana Voss: "Then we build in the repairs. Scheduled checks, community accountability, transparency. We pilot the maintenance rota with real incentives for participation. We can make it sustainable."

You want to tell her about the exhaustion that moves like a shadow behind your ribs. Ariana smiles — not the bright smile she uses for crowds, but a smaller, private one that looks almost as if it remembers your better mornings.

Ariana Voss: "That's exactly the kind of thing I needed from you. Your lists are... calming."

You want to laugh, because lists are also a way you avoid saying the things that really matter. Instead, Lina's phone buzzes in her satchel like a clambering bird.

Lina Park (off-screen, voice crisp): "On it. I'll run a piece if there's something actionable. But Marin — be careful. Tideguard's framing will matter at the hall."

Ariana Voss: "If Tideguard's narrative is already seeded, our visuals need to be stronger. Proof-of-concept, community testimonials, Elder Tomas at the center. We make it about people, not just engineering."

You: "Elder Tomas will speak, yes. He remembers the old ways. He'll remind them what gets erased when walls go up without hearts in them."

Ivy: "And we can show them this nursery. Let them run hands through the soil. Let them smell the salt and the difference."

Ariana: "Exactly. We make it tactile."

The plan grows on the table between you — a living seawall that doubles as nursery, maps scrawled in brown ink, buoys sketched with anchor points, volunteer rosters, and a list of talking points. It feels real enough to touch and fragile enough to shatter if the wrong person frames it.

Your hands move over a map you've traced a dozen times. The pencil smudges the edge of a pressed leaf; for a breath you see the boathouse's bowed timbers again, hearing, impossibly, the metal rasp of a roof lifting in wind. You know loss intimately; it is a lesson that teaches both caution and urgency. You also know what it is to let the desire to protect harden into an iron will that ignores the people who will have to live with the fix.

Ariana reaches for your hand — a quick, tentative touch meant for solidarity more than romance — and for a moment you want to let her stay there and tell her everything. But there are people to call, points to map, a delivery schedule to confirm. You press your thumb to the page instead, anchoring yourself in ink.
