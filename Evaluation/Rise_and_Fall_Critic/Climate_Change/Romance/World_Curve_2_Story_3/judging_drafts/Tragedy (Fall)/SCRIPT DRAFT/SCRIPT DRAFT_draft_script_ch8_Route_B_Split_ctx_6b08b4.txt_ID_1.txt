[Scene: The Old Boathouse | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The single lamp hangs low, casting a honeyed circle over a scarred plank table. Papers fan out beneath its glow.]
[Sound: The distant slap of waves against patched pilings; a gull cries once, far off. Inside, the boathouse settles with a soft, wooden creak.]
[Music: Low, mournful cello; a slow, descending motif underlines the conversation]

You spread the compact across the table like a map of futures: folded clauses, typed margins, Tideguard's clean block of logo staring back at you. The paper has the thin, corporate sheen of things meant to be both final and forgetful. Salt and oil have dulled some ink at the corners; someone has annotated the margins in a tidy, small hand—Cassian's. The annotations look like logic attempting to be mercy.

You feel the lamp's heat on the back of your wrist. Your thumb finds the brass of your compass beneath your collar and turns it, once, twice, as if checking whether the needle still points to the same stubborn north.

Ivy sits across from you, the neon of her vest dimmed by the lamp, reading aloud in a voice that keeps splitting at the edges.

Ivy Morales: "(reading) 'Easements shall allow for the installation of barrier segments along prescribed stretches...owner relocation clause activated upon storm threshold...'"

Her mouth tightens around the last words, and she looks up with something raw—hurt folded into a civic sense of betrayal.

Ivy Morales: "They've written 'activation' like it's a button."

Elder Tomas taps his carved stick against the map unfurled next to the paper. The tap is a small percussion: salt on wood.

Elder Tomas: "Paper does not remember salt." (quiet, like complaint and counsel at once)

The line lands between you and Cassian. It sounds truer than any of the legalese—salt remembers everything: boats, names, the taste of the boathouse rafters when you slept there as a child. Paper doesn't talk back; it can be made to look like consent.

Cassian Rhee: "(soft, exact) We can cover the most vulnerable stretches immediately. We can stop erosion, reduce displacement risk, and buy time for your pilot projects."

Cassian's voice is smooth; his fingers rest near the margin, where his handwriting forms an almost architectural neatness—small loops, precise arrows. You imagine his hand drafting scaffolds on paper the way he drafts them in conversation: neat plans that leave out the small human seams.

Marin Sato: "(you speak, measured) The compact has clauses for emergency relocations. Who decides when 'activation' happens? Who gets priority? The words are careful, but they carve a map."

Cassian leans forward, not aggressive—deliberate.

Cassian Rhee: "Operational triggers are objective, Marin. Measurable thresholds. We can't base emergency deployment on a neighborhood's press appeal."

Lina Park: "(impatient, leaning forward with her satchel beside her) We can't pretend this is only technical. The town deserves to see the terms. If they know what's at stake, they can hold people accountable."

Ariana Voss touches the edge of the compact with a fingertip like she might bruise it. Her camera hangs by her hip; she looks between the clauses and the faces.

Ariana Voss: "(soft urgency) Publicizing this could spook investors. If funding withdraws, the protections vanish. We all stand to lose everything. Transparency is right, but timing matters."

Lina snaps back, sharper than you expect.

Lina Park: "Timing matters when it helps you sleep, Ariana. Timing matters when it preserves power. The town's voice isn't a variable to be scheduled."

A pause blooms. The cello thickens.

You can feel the weight of the ledger in your chest—the inventory of costs and gains you have to balance. Each clause is a small calculus with human bodies as variables.

Ivy exhales and reads another clause, and this one lands like a stone thrown into still water.

Ivy Morales: "(reading, voice small) 'Priority allocation for barrier adjacency shall be determined by asset valuation and continuity of critical infrastructure...'"

Elder Tomas: "(hard) That is a paper that values beams over bodies."

Cassian lifts his hands, palms open—an attempt at reassurance.

Cassian Rhee: "We base 'priority' on objective criteria because it's the only way to avoid arbitrary decision-making. Marin, you know arbitrary favors those with loudest voices and deepest wells."

Marin Sato: "(you) Objectivity on paper can still come with bias in practice. Who writes the formulas? Who audits them? A 'threshold' can be designed to ignore a fisherman's pier and favor corporate docks."

Ariana's jaw tightens; she starts counting off points on an invisible hand.

Ariana Voss: "We can demand transparency: independent auditors, a community oversight board with veto power, public dashboards. Those are legal clauses we can add."

Cassian's eyes flick to her, then to you. His expression goes complex—calm, then a blink of impatience, then something unreadable.

Cassian Rhee: "(careful) You can ask for a lot of protections, Ariana. But each added clause is leverage for investors to walk away. I won't pretend there isn't a hard line where funding meets feasibility."

Lina: "(bitter laugh) So our people's lives are a bargaining chip. Lovely."

Marin's palms go slick; the compass against your sternum feels like a small, false anchor.

You want to bargain for guarded trust. You want to file an amendment with teeth. You want to tear the compact and make the town decide in roar and daylight. Each impulse is a different kind of courage and cowardice.

Elder Tomas taps his stick again, this time slower.

Elder Tomas: "When their buildings fall, they'll call it natural. When our nets tear, we will call it theft."

Ivy: "(quiet) And what do we do while we call? If the storm comes before we finish calling—do we mean to stand on the shore and shout?"

A silence settles so thick your breath sounds loud. Outside, a gust makes the boathouse groan.

You can see the possible futures laid out in the compact like a row of stones crossing a river. Step wrong and you drown someone. Step wrong and you strand families on a different shore. The lamp's circle feels narrower; the rafters of the boathouse press closer, as if the structure itself is holding its breath.

Cassian folds his fingers, an elegant bridge.

Cassian Rhee: "If you sign, we secure immediate work. Crews mobilize, concrete pours, barriers go up where we agree. If you demand binding oversight, we can write it into the compact—if Tideguard can accept it. If you push for publicity, there is a real risk the company will pull funding."

Lina leans in, voice a low, dangerous thing.

Lina Park: "If they pull funding because the town demands to know, then we know who prefers quick walls over accountable walls. The question isn't only what protects the shore; it's who gets to decide what 'protection' costs."

Ariana: "(pleading) The pilot shows a different way. We can use the funds for hybrid measures—living seawalls, kelp nurseries, community-managed maintenance. But we need time and some guarantee to keep investors from panicking."

You press the compass between finger and thumb until the metal warms. The scuff feels like an old argument: loyalty vs. pragmatism, memory vs. machinery.

Marin Sato: "(soft, because the words must carry all of you) I can sign this and secure work immediately. I can push for oversight language and risk losing the deal. I can give these papers to Lina and hand the town the truth, raw and dangerous."

Cassian watches you, the calm of a man measuring tides by charts rather than stories.

Cassian Rhee: "(gentle) Whatever you choose will define us for decades. Make the choice you can live with."

Your hands tremble. The compass is weight and decision both. The boathouse feels smaller; the town outside holds its breath. You realize the decision is not only about structures on the shore—it is about what kind of people you will be to one another under pressure.
