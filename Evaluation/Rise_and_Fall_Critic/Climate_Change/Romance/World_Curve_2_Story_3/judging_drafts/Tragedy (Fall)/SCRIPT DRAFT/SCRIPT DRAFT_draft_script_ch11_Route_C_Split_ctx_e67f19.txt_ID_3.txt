[Scene: Greenwave Pilot Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Golden-hour light thinning; kelp fronds ripple like green lace across a grid of floating planters. A few engineering buoys bob bright orange against sullen water.]
[Sound: Low wind, gulls calling; the slap of wet rope against the scaffolding.]
[Music: Understated cello, a slow pulse under the conversation.]

You step onto the narrow gangway and the smell of wet seaweed fills your nose — rich, iron-sweet, the smell of something trying to hold itself together. Your boots thud on planks that have already been patched twice this season. Hands on the rails, you watch the kelp, watch how small life finds angles in the mesh: translucent larvae tucked into frond folds, tiny mouths open like questions.

Ariana is crouched at the edge of one planter, her hand hovering over a blade of kelp as if it might snap if she touches it. Her raincoat is streaked with spray; one blue-streaked strand of hair clings damp to her temple. When she sees you, her face brightens for a fraction, then the light slips away.

Ariana: "Look — the settlement rates are better than our baseline. The larvae counts are up by twenty percent since last week. Even with the surge, the microhabitats held."

You watch her explain and you want to believe in the quickness of her words the way she does. You feel the compass at your throat, the brass warm against your skin, and you fold that warmth into the shape of a promise you still mean.

Ivy comes up behind you with a bundle of rope over her shoulder, breath steaming in the chill.

Ivy: (half-laughing, half-warned) "You two sound like a grant reviewer and their star witness. It's good, but —" (she taps a buoy) "— I don't like how thin the tethers are. That wind's been strange all afternoon."

Ariana: (brisk) "We can reinforce. We've got the tensile data. If Tideguard backs us, we can upgrade connectors in a week."

You feel the word Tideguard like a weight in the air. Their name hangs between the two of you, both a lifeline and a blade.

Ariana steps closer, so close that the dampness off her coat beads on your forearm.

Ariana: (soft) "We can't let this be the thing that teaches us humility the expensive way, Marin. We fixed the biology — now we need the build."

You want to tell her you agree and that you want to move faster, to string bolts and bring enough hands to make a difference. But the frayed splice you felt, the thinness Ivy mentioned; they don't let you say that without a cold knot forming in your chest.

You: "I know. But speed without margin is a kind of gamble. People can get hurt."

Ariana: (voice quickening) "Not on my watch. Not here. We have the pilot data — if Tideguard sees this as scalable now, the funding and materials come. We can reinforce everything."

Ivy: (arms folded) "Or you hand the keys to someone who will want it scaled by their timetable, not ours."

Ariana snaps her mouth shut as if she's just remembered a rehearsal line. Her hand curls, then unfolds. For a second, she looks exactly like the person who once dragged mangrove seedlings up three flights of stairs to prove a point: luminous, unstoppable.

[Visual: Clouds thicken along the horizon; a distant rumble. The water darkens to a bruise-blue.]
[Sound: Wind lifts; a loose plank creaks.]

You taste salt and iron. The day is tilting.

[Scene: Greenwave Pilot Site — The Storm | Evening]
[Visual: The sky blackens. Rain arrives in a sheet; the orange buoys bob like hearts in a wave.]
[Sound: Rope straining; a metallic snap; people's shouts cut off by the wind.]
[Music: String tremor rising to a dissonant peak.]

The first tear is a sound you feel in your ribs — a dry, final rip. One of the outer planters breaks free, then two. Volunteers scramble, boots sliding on wet decking, hands reaching for rope that slips from sodden fingers. You move before you think: the compass at your throat feels like a stone you should plant or throw.

Someone recorded it — a rope slapping an exposed splice, a planter tumbling into the surf, bright orange buoy trailing like a wound. Ivy grabs at a tether and swears; Lina is already filming from the high ground, her face pale and small against the rain. No one is hurt. No bones snap. Still, the footage has the exact quality the internet eats for breakfast: a slow arc of failure with your project at the center.

[Sound: A distant horn, digital alerts chiming from pockets.]
[Visual: Lina's phone screen blooms with a notification: the clip is already shared; comments multiply.]

Lina: (voice tight) "It's viral. People are asking if we were reckless. 'Were they ready?' the first comments say."

You feel something slide under your ribs — not guilt exactly, not yet; something made of pressure and the realization that the brittle things in life break all at once.

Ariana stands in the rain, rainwater sheening on her lashes. For a moment she looks stunned, and then anger flares, bright and raw.

Ariana: (shouting over the wind) "We can fix this. We tighten the specs and roll out reinforcements. We show them we can adapt faster."

You: "And if we move too fast, and skip community sign-offs or safety redundancies, then what? Do we fix the coastline or trade it for a public relations victory?"

Ariana's jaw tightens. She breathes, and the breath trembles.

Ariana: (quieter) "I didn't start this to be careful. I started this to save things."

You: "So did I. Saving doesn't always look like the same thing."

Ivy stands between you like an old hinge, eyes flaring with local outrage wrapped in charity.

Ivy: "This town remembers what it's like to wake up to nothing. They need promises to be real, not headlines. But they also need roofs."

[Scene: Coastal Research Hub — Emergency Lab Meeting | Later That Night]
[Visual: The lab is lit in cool blues: rows of touchscreens, drying racks of gear, a wall of monitors repeating the viral clip in a looping echo. Shadows pool in corners. A single lamp over a worktable throws a honeyed circle — the only warm spot in the room.]
[Sound: Low hum of servers; chairs scrape; cups clink. The rain drums the exterior panels with steady insistence.]
[Music: Sparse piano taps; a low synth undercurrent, heavy.]

You stand at the center of it: the compass weight at your throat, breath shallow, the room full of expectant faces. Lina is at the monitor, fingers restless on the keyboard; Ivy leans on a counter, hands stained with rope fibers. Ariana stands across from you, hair still damp, eyes wide and unblinking.

Cassian is on the video feed — his image crisp, the background of his frame hinting at polished offices and rain-sheened glass. He watches with an expression you cannot parse. There is competence there, and a something like calculation. It reads as complex, not outright hostile, but you feel the distance between his world and the one you walk.

Cassian: (measured) "I saw the footage. It can be spun into an argument for speed: instability under pressure is precisely why hardened infrastructure is necessary."

Lina: (leaning in) "Or it's a failure of our testing regime. People will want to know why bolts sheared. They'll want accountability."

Ariana: (bristling) "We tested everything we could. These were extreme conditions — a freak—"

You cut across, because you are tired of explanations that layer over what matters.

You: "This wasn't a freak. It was a storm. A storm we will face again."

Ariana: (voice thinning) "Then we scale. We get Tideguard to help. We don't have time to be timid."

You: "Scaling under a conditional contract where specifications get fast-tracked isn't the same as scaling with the town's consent."

Ivy: "And if Tideguard pulls funding because you try to slow them down?"

A silence that isn't empty hangs between the names — Tideguard, funding, consent — each a chord that can snap the whole arrangement.

Lina: (hard) "People will demand transparency. If we bury data to appease an investor, they will smell it. Trust evaporates faster than money."

Ariana's face shifts, something like fear shadowing the edges of her optimism.

Ariana: (low) "If we pause and release everything — the raw footage, the logs — we could force a public reckoning. Maybe that's what we need to rebuild trust."

You study her. The idea of radical transparency sits raw and hungry between you. It could be a cleansing. It could also be the axe at the roots of everything you've built together.

You: "Or we accept conditional funding and fast-track the build — risk structural bypasses for the safety of scale. Or we insist on community consent and slow trials — risk investors walking. Or we go all-in on transparency and pause."

Cassian's face on the screen tightens imperceptibly. His hands, in the frame, fold and unfold.

Cassian: (calm) "Every path has trade-offs. If you can't pick one, pick the one that preserves the most options."

You: "And whose options are you prioritizing?"

Cassian: (complex, unreadable) "The ones that keep the shoreline intact."

The room hums with that imprecise phrase. It is a goal shared in rhetoric but split in method.

Ariana looks at you with something that isn't just hope or fear — it's a mirror of your own fatigue.

Ariana: (voice breaking) "Do we choose momentum or do we choose community? I don't know anymore which is braver."

You press the compass into your palm until the scuffs bite. The brass is cold, grounding. You remember nights on the boathouse floor, listening to Tomas tell you about storms he couldn't steady with his hands anymore. You remember protests, meetings, small victories: a repaired sluice, a nursery saved. You think of the volunteers who laughed as they waded in ankle-deep to tie down a planters' net, and you think of Lina's headline already forming in your head: "Pilot or Pipe Dream?"

Ivy crosses to you and rests a fist against your shoulder — solid, immediate.

Ivy: (soft) "We are not just managing a system. We are stewarding trust. Don't let that get lost."

Lina taps the screen; a list of social comments scrolls by, sharp and impatient.

Lina: "The public's question is simple: were we ready? Answer that, and you start to steer the narrative."

You: "But answering it one way or another closes doors."

Ariana: (whisper) "And closes us."

Your chest aches. Duty pulls a direction; love pulls another. The pilot's promise — kelp like green lace, larvae sheltered in fronds — sits beside the viral clip of failure. Two images, equally true.

You can feel the room holding its breath in the pause that follows. Every path you imagine tethers to consequences that will ripple through the town, through friendships, through how Ariana looks at you tomorrow.

[Sound: The rain eases into a steady hush; the servers' hum persists like a pulse.]

You look to the faces that matter: Ivy's stubborn, practical presence; Lina's journalist's hunger for truth; Ariana's fragile, urgent hope. Cassian's image is still pinned to the screen, distant and complex. The lab light makes small flecks of salt shine on your skin.

Everything narrows to a choice you cannot escape.
