You move through the terrace like someone trying not to break an inheritance. The pressed-leaf notebook is heavy in your pack but your hands are full: coils of rope, a jar of damp clamps, a clump of kelp seedlings with roots like small, trembling fingers. Volunteers dot the terraces—neighbors, teenagers, a pair of visiting students—faces washed in the soft light of a week where things felt, briefly, like repair rather than collapse.

Ariana Voss is everywhere and nowhere at once: instructing a small group with a bright, efficient cadence; laughing as a kid splashes mud across their yellow boots; then, when she feels you behind her shoulder, turning and closing the small distance between public urgency and private ease.

Ariana Voss: "We need the outer line at a two-meter spacing—no less. The growth meshes faster if we keep the nutrient trays angled toward tidal flow. Marin, Jonah can take the south run if you can show him the mounting brackets."

You feel the name land like a weight. You don't correct Ariana to your own name—it's smoother to let her call you what she knows you by in the work. Your hands brush the brass compass at your throat out of habit and for steadiness.

You crouch beside a floating planter; the air is damp and metallic in your nose, the soil rich and cold. Your jacket smells of algae and the tarred wood of the old boathouse. The buoy next to you bobs, tethered and bright, like a small, defiant buoy of color against the gray sea.

Ivy Morales moves past with a wheeled cart of tools, grinning and unflappable.

Ivy Morales: "You okay? You look like you haven't slept in a week." (she says it half-chiding, half-worried)

Marin Sato: "I've slept in fragments. Enough to keep moving." (you offer a small smile that doesn't reach your shoulders)

Ivy Morales: "Good. Don't go hero-ing this one, Marin. We need you for the long haul, not just headline snapshots."

There's a sting in that—exactly the thing you keep trying to forget: your tendency to do too much, to be into the breach alone. Ivy's words are a net; you let them catch you.

The exchanges are soft but sharp; no one is shouting, but every sentence carries an edge. Your role is to bridge—your training in ecology, your roots in the harbor. You're halfway between the lab and the planks, and the strain of that middle position presses at your ribs.

The town, for a week, has felt stitched back together—laughter and dirt and the small machinery of hope. The stitches wobble at the edges.

A crowd gathers near the outer terraces—skeptical fishers, a couple of council aides, Lina Park with a notepad half-hidden behind a tablet. Ariana taps the tablet; a map expands, color-coded growth zones pulsing like living things. She explains growth curves to a cluster of fishers, and you watch the way she simplifies without losing rigor—every sentence a bridge between jargon and practice.

Ariana Voss: "We ran the microdosing on a smaller batch. The kelp acclimatized within five tidal cycles. The living-seawall modules attenuated wave energy by an observed twelve percent in the last surge."

Fishers: "Twelve percent? That's not much for the money and the fuss."

Ariana Voss: "It's significant—especially when layered. The point of the living seawall is redundancy. We don't replace a barrier that isolates the coastline; we create a system that breathes with it."

Old Fisher [After a beat]: "And who pays when—when your 'agency' doesn't pay the bills?"

A silence follows you like a small fog. It's not just about science. It never was. It is about livelihoods, histories, lineage. Ivy places a steady hand on your shoulder; Tomas watches from the edge with his walking stick like an anchor of older certainties.

Elder Tomas Hale [Soft, Unyielding]: "We did a living bank once, when I was a boy. Took a decade to get right, and even then we learned things the hard way. I want to believe, but I want the town to know the costs."

Ariana Voss: "We can stage this. We can scale in verified steps—"

Lina Park: "Staged steps mean slower data. Slower data means Tideguard's timeline passes. Marin, if you—if you push for staged proof instead of rapid scaling, the narrative shifts. Tideguard will say the community couldn't move."

You feel the sentence sharpen like a hand on your throat. Lina's words are clean and unflinching; her articles will shape the town's perception. The pilot's success needs visible metrics fast. Tideguard's funding isn't neutral; it's a clock with teeth.

[Visual: Interior—cool white light, screens casting clinical blue on faces; various boots line the floor, a kettle forgotten on a bench.]

We move into days that fold into nights—data streams, volunteer rotations, hurried code pushes to get sensor arrays talking. The lab smell is antiseptic touched with salt. You and Ariana work side by side under the precise glare of monitors; the world reduces to pixels and algae samples and the steady, bureaucratic cruelty of a funding deadline.

Ariana Voss presses her camera into your palm in the hum of a midnight break—a small, warm weight. Her coppery breath fogs the air for a second.

Ariana Voss: "Take one. For the record. For the kids who got mud in their socks today. For us."

You want to say all the wrong things—the grand lines about futures and vows you don't trust—but you keep it smaller.

Marin Sato: "If this fails, it'll be on all of us."

Ariana Voss [Quiet]: "If it succeeds, it will be on us, too."

There's a softness in her eyes you didn't expect tonight. Maybe it's the exhaustion, or the fragility of what you've been building together. You trace the line of her undercut with a fingertip, a ridiculous, tender thing to do in a lab between datasets.

Ariana Voss: "Do you think I'm reckless?"

Marin Sato [Hesitant]: "Sometimes. But not in the way that makes me stop trusting you. You try big things and don't always wait for slow proof. That can hurt. It can also be the only way things move."

Ariana Voss: "I can't always slow down. There are windows that don't reopen."

You swallow. Her hand lingers near yours on the bench, an electric almost. There's heat, and there's also the cold plainness of a reality neither of you can change: funding windows, tide lines, a corporate timeline that will not wait for the town to catch up emotionally.

Lina Park slips in, rain on her coat, eyes heavy with copy.

Lina Park: "We need a narrative thread. The town is watching. Tideguard is sharpening its message. Cassian's team briefed someone this afternoon—quietly. They're talking about 'urgent stabilization' and 'minimizing economic disruption.'"

At the pronouncement of Cassian's name the room contracts. Cassian Rhee is not a monster; he is a force with resources and a clarity of purpose that terrifies by its efficiency. You imagine his polished shoes walking the pavilion, his graphs measuring everything in units that forget people.

Marin Sato: "Did they offer—"

Lina Park: "Not to us. Not yet. But the story is being written. We either give them cause to write a different one or we try to beat them at the story they prefer."

Ariana Voss: "We don't have the luxury of waiting for perfect data. If we can compress the calibration and get field metrics showing load-bearing and biodiversity uplift, we can argue for a hybrid model. But it's not ethical to rush experiments without proper consent and oversight."

The sentence pulls taut—ethics versus necessity. You think of the volunteers who've given hours, the kids who've smeared mud on their boots, the fishers whose nets depend on a shoreline that remains accessible. You think of Tomas's patient warnings and Ivy's relentless energy. You think of the compass pendant at your throat, scuffed to softness.

You open a data feed and the charts bloom: one array shows promising kelp growth; another shows a sudden spike in cellular stress markers in a subset of samples subjected to accelerated nutrient dosing. The numbers are not catastrophic; they are a bruise—an early sign that pushing faster has costs.

Your chest tightens. The sea, patient and indifferent, offers no counsel. The plants will either adapt or they will not; people will either be convinced or they will not. Your job has been to translate between biology and belief and the two are not always compatible.

Ariana Voss [Soft]: "We can run a targeted rollback on the dosing in the affected modules. It will slow us, but it may save the argument later."

Marin Sato: "If we slow, we risk not meeting the window. If we don't, we risk doing harm."

The lab hums. Outside, the tide climbs with a small, relentless certainty. The choices do not present like forks—they slide like small avalanches waiting to begin.

You know what waits: a choice that will not feel like choice, because whatever you decide will be judged by a tide that has no ethics. You are trying to steward both a community and a relationship. The two can pull away from each other on inconvenient hours.

You breathe and the air tastes like salt and unresolved things.

You feel the meeting—the push to scale, the argument with ethics, the possibility of being hailed for bravery or blamed for haste—coming close like a storm front. The lab clock ticks. Your fingers hover over the tablet, over the draft of a public statement, over the rollback command, over the contact list that includes Cassian when you most wish he were a different kind of man.

A single, simple thought rises in you with a weight you cannot shrug: if you are wrong, people will get hurt; if you are right, you might have saved them. The sea will not tell you which outcome it prefers.

You look at Ariana. Her face is pale in the monitor light; hope and fear etch lines there. She meets your eyes with something unreadable—encouragement and accusation braided together.

You close your hand around the compass until the metal bites.

There is no clean answer. Only a next move.
