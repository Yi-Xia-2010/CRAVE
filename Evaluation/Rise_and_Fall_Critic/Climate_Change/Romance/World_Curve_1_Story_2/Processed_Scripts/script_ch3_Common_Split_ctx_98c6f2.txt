The salt air tastes like memory — your mother's hands, the green-sour tang of kelp, the smell of the spray when the sea decides to reclaim a seam of the town. The gathering has thinned, but the pulse of tonight lingers: plans, promises, the weight of decisions pressing in like tidewater against a bulkhead.

Soren Voss: "Mika Hoshino. I'm not asking for empty trust. I'm offering infrastructure that buys us time — raised roads, reinforced docks, a staggered seawall with integrated transit corridors. Think of it as an investment in the town's bones."

You study the shimmer. The model is precise, mercilessly tidy in its solutions. It smells of ozone and factory polish, not of the salt and rope you grew up with. Your fingertips itch to touch the projection and feel the cold clarity beneath the promises.

Mika Hoshino: "It looks… clean. Efficient. But lines on a screen don't tell you what we lose when we raise a street or move a workshop. History gets boxed up. People get priced out."

Soren Voss's pale eyes narrow just enough to be human. He lets the projection shimmer, then folds a corner of the model with a flick.

Soren Voss: "Change has costs. So does inaction. I can secure funding that keeps businesses afloat while we rebuild. We can design the raised areas with ecological buffers — living systems incorporated. You'd get influence at the table, Mika Hoshino. Real power to shape how it's done."

You sense the offer's teeth: partnership with leverage. It's tempting in a way that flickers behind your ribs — the ability to protect what you love fast. But his phrase, "you'd get influence at the table," carries weight: it assumes concessions, slow compromises, the politics of 'how much' rather than 'whether'.

Elias Maren steps forward, voice steady even when his hands tremble with adrenaline and hope.

Elias Maren: "Or we scale the kelp pilot. Living breakwaters seeded at key points will slow erosion, rebuild substrate, and — in time — restore fisheries. No raised streets, but resilience built from the sea outward. If you let me use your workshop as a hub, Mika Hoshino, we could train locals, create jobs, and show the town a functioning alternative."

You watch his pendant catch lantern light — it looks like a small patch of ocean held against his chest. Elias's optimism isn't naive; it's patient work with a faith in processes you both learned are stubborn and true.

Mika Hoshino: "How long until it matters? Trials and pilots sound good in a greenhouse, Elias. But king tides don't wait for promises."

Elias Maren meets your gaze square on.

Elias Maren: "It won't be instant. But the kelp grows faster than people expect when the conditions are right. We can start at dawn, test plots next week. Show, don't tell. If results come in — increases in nursery habitat, attenuated wave energy — the town will listen. People follow success more easily than speeches."

He swallows. "And Mika Hoshino — your workshop has the tools and trust. If you lend it, we get more than a lab. We get a center people will come to."

Riv's voice slices through the careful plans with an edge of fever.

Riv: "That's all well and good, but while we're bleeding time, Soren will corral votes and sign deals. We block the bulldozers — loud, visible, a blockade — and force real concessions. People need to see that we're not handing over our town."

Jun, always the counterweight, exhales and props his hands on his hips.

Jun: "Blockades make headlines, Riv, but they also make enemies. Funding dries up when the risk premium spikes. If you've got a lever, you use it—whether it's public pressure or a seat at the table. We need to be strategic. We need receipts, not just righteous anger."

Mayor Amina folds her hands, the gravity of civic duty plain on her face.

Mayor Amina: "Mika Hoshino, I'm asking for your recommendation. Whatever you advise will carry weight. The council wants options; the town wants leadership. We need something practical but defensible."

The boardwalk creaks underfoot — an old rhythm, dependable in its complaints. All around you, the harbor breathes: the soft slap of rope, the metallic scent of tide-mixed with wood-smoke from late-night heaters. Your breath fogs in the cool air. You keep thinking of the greenhouse light, of the warmth in the rooftop lab where seedlings thrive against the odds. You think of your mother's dented key in your pocket, an anchor to a past that taught you repair as prayer.

Elias Maren: "If I had the workshop, we could run community workshops: tide monitoring, kelp propagation, boat repair. People learn skills and earn a stake in the system. Soren Voss interjects before you can answer.

Soren Voss: "And yet there's the practical truth — grants move with schedules. Contracts have windows. A town needs shelter, roads, jobs. We can integrate your kelp pilots into construction if we plan together. That way —"

Elias Maren, voice sharpened by worry and affection, replies: "Or we refuse to let ecological measures become lipstick on concrete. If you speed everything up without community buy-in, the result is fragile. You're talking about our homes as if they're portfolios."

A beat. The projection between you flares; the kelp pendant glows faintly in Elias Maren's chest. The argument isn't a duel so much as two maps trying to overlay: one diagram of efficiency, one map of living processes. Both hope to save the same town. Both speak truth.

Jun rests a hand on your shoulder, solid as a joist.

Jun: "There's no clean path, Mika Hoshino. But there's one thing I know: whatever you choose, start with something small and measurable. If you back Elias, set a pilot schedule and contingency funding. If you back Soren, get binding ecological clauses. If you lead a coop, secure seed capital and legal counsel. People will follow clarity."

Riv looks at you, eyes bright with an almost worshipful urgency.

Riv: "Lead loud or lead smart. Either way, don't be quiet. Don't be nice when nice costs the town."

It tightens when you imagine signing papers that will redraw property lines and move families. There is no neutral place here — only choices that trade one set of risks for another.

Mayor Amina's voice breaks through, lowered and earnest.

Mayor Amina: "Town will listen to you, Mika Hoshino, because you speak from doing. Tell me what to recommend. I'm asking you to name a path."

You close your eyes for a second and remember the nights your mother sewed nets by lantern light, the smell of resin, the sound of laughter over plans that never worked at first. You remember taking apart a water pump and being surprised at how many small pieces had to fit together exactly. Decisions are like that: small tolerances, exacting patience.

Your hands find the dented key in your pocket and rub its cold edge. Your wrist patch buzzes faintly with a tide alert — a reminder that action has a schedule.
