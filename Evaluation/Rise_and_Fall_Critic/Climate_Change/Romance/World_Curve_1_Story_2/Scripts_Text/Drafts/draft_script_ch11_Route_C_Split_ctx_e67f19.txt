### DRAFT
# Route: Route C / Ending


[Scene: Greenhouse Rooftop Lab | Dawn]
[Visual: Condensation beads on curved glass; rows of salt-tolerant seedlings glow under amber grow-lamps]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the soft slap of water against pilings, and the low murmur of the town awakening]
[Music: Warm, rising strings — hopeful, steady]

You step into the greenhouse and the air wraps around you: warm, damp, threaded with the sharp green scent of sprouting leaves and an undernote of salt that slips in from the harbor. Your palms still smell faintly of ledger ink and rope. Dawn cuts a pale strip across the floorboards; droplets on the trays catch it and scatter little suns.

Elias is already here, sleeves rolled, a tablet balanced on one knee as he scrolls through tide logs. The bioluminescent pendant at his throat catches the grow-lamp's glow and throws a tiny, laughing sparkle against his collarbone. He looks up when you enter; his smile arrives before words, unhurried and steady.

Elias: "Morning. I ran the monitoring draft by last night's signatories. It's short enough for the volunteers and detailed enough for the funders. We can start training the watch rotation this week."

You feel your chest loosen. The cooperative's name is still new, but hearing it in Elias's voice frames it into reality — something that breathes and keeps hours.

You: "Good. Keep it simple. No jargon. If people can log a tide and a net patch in the same form, they'll actually use it."

Elias: (a soft chuckle) "Practical as always. I like that the form fits your language."

Before you can reply, Jun's heavy steps thud on the ladder as he comes up onto the roof, carrying a toolbox and the smell of sawdust. His grin is the kind that makes plans feel doable.

Jun: "If we're showing anyone anything, it better not just be seedlings and spreadsheets. Bring 'em to a pier we fixed with our hands. Let them smell the tar and taste the sea biscuits."

Riv barges in next, bandana askew, waving a stack of flyers and an ear-to-ear grin that refuses to be practical but is infectious anyway.

Riv: "Flyers, chants, a couple of camera angles—bam. We'll have the story. People show up to spectacle. Young folks show up to be seen. Old folks show because it's familiar. Old money shows because cameras are there. It's momentum."

His voice quickens like a wave building speed. You feel the tendency—in you and in them—to ride that momentum. You also feel the ledger's weight in the metal box in your head, the schedule you wrote in looping handwriting that promised patience as well as action.

[Visual: Close on your hands smoothing a seedling's leaves; the braided string on your wrist twitches as you breathe]

You think of your mother's voice — not the exact words she used, but the cadence: "Do the work, and it will gather people." That memory steadies you. You can be blunt; you can also be tactical. The two aren't opposed.

Elias watches the back-and-forth, eyes like slow tides. He steps closer and lowers his voice so the plans feel like something shared between you two, not a public declaration.

Elias: "Noise works. Demonstration works. Policy lasts. We don't have to pick one because we can do them in sequence, but… we do need a lead plan. Which one do you want us to lean into?"

The question lands on you like a clean net over tuna — purposeful. You feel every face around the table waiting for the decision that will shape the next movement of the cooperative. You taste coffee and salt and the faint metallic edge of adrenaline.

You: "We need a plan that keeps the cooperative moving forward and brings the town with us, not just in headlines but in habit."

Jun: (nodding) "Right. If the mayor can see real numbers — harvest weight, volunteer hours — she can't be as casual about slapping a development down the line."

Riv: (pushing a flyer into your palm) "And if she's not listening fast enough, we can make sure the cameras do."

A beat of silence stretches, full of possibility. Mayor Amina's office called earlier and asked for a sit-down at the municipal building later today. Her voice on the phone was warm but contained that tired, measured authority that holds a town together. You remember the silver-gray braid and how she pauses before she commits, counting implications like driftwood weighing an idea.

[Sound: The greenhouse door opens on a flit of colder air from the boardwalk; gulls call like punctuation]

A thoughtful friction starts between Jun's practical lean toward visible repair work, Riv's appetite for public pressure, and Elias's faith in evidence and demonstration. Each sees the ladder up the same cliff but imagines a different foothold. You find yourself enjoying the friction; it sharpens the plan. Your bluntness is no longer a peel of anger — it's a chisel.

You: "We have three things: urgency, evidence, and the town's trust. We need them all. But this week—this week we have to pick a lead."

Elias: "If we lead with a demonstration — a cooperative harvest that's big enough to make numbers and small enough to succeed — we can bring the mayor a thing she can touch. Jun's crews can make the pier ready. Riv can marshal the crowd to be there in a way that looks like community, not spectacle."

Riv: (grinning) "I like being the crowd whisperer."

Jun: (crossing his arms) "Or we can escalate: a series of direct actions, staged at municipal meetings. Force them to either legislate or be publicly shamed. It gets results faster, but—" (he glances at you) "—it gets messy."

A pulse of unease flares: legal risks, arrests, the strain on volunteers who have families and jobs. But the rising energy in the room presses against that unease and makes it feel manageable rather than paralyzing.

You: (thinking) Your mother would have grounded a plan in people's rhythms. Fast is fine if it doesn't break people. Bold is fine if it builds infrastructure to hold the change.

Elias catches your glance and, with a small, almost shy motion, reaches out to touch the back of your hand — a brief, careful contact like testing a tide. There's an implicit question in the pressure: Do you trust this will work? Do you trust me?

That tiny touch lands more meaningfully than any speech. It isn't a guarantee; it is a promise to stand. You feel your resolve steady in the contact.

You: (softly) "We do this together. Whatever path we choose, we don't let the cooperative be a stunt or a bargaining chip. It's a thing that keeps working."

[Music: Strings rise into a warmer, brighter motif; hope gathering like light behind clouds]

Jun: "Okay. Logistics then. If we stage a harvest, I need a crew to shore up the pier, nets ready, and a place to weigh and display. Elias, you'll need to bring monitoring to the table as a legit metric. Riv—" (he smirks) "—you'll have to make it look like a town party, not a protest."

Riv: "Party with a mission. Got it."

Amina's upcoming meeting hangs in the air like an appointment on a calendar you can still fill. You picture the municipal office — clinical light, folding tables, the mayor's steady face. You picture the boardwalk with banners and the pier crowded with people who'd rather fix than film. Both images are possible; both are good in different ways.

You: (weighing) "Mayor Amina asked to meet. She'll be more receptive if we come with evidence and a narrative she can carry into city budgets. But if she drags her feet, we'll escalate. We have to be ready for both."

Elias: "I can finalize the monitoring protocol today and start training two volunteers to do data entry. That gives us a week of numbers if we schedule the harvest soon."

Jun: "I can get the pier structurally sound by Friday with a volunteer day. The town will see the repaired pier and the seedlings in carts by Saturday."

Riv: "I can get volunteers and press for the harvest day. We'll make it feel like everyone's invited, including the reporters Mayor Amina cares about."

You feel the plan forming under your hands: a lattice of work, each node held by a person who knows one thing and trusts others to know the rest. It is ordinary, communal, stubbornly human — and it is effective.

The small choice hangs for a beat and then folds into action — Riv scribbles on a flyer, Elias taps keys, Jun whistles a rhythm and begins inventorying supplies. The greenhouse hums with motion. Work is the language that unites you; the town answers that language in hands and hours.

[Scene: Floodplain Boardwalk & Reedbeds | Later Morning]
[Visual: Overcast sky reflected in puddles; a long line of reeds sways while volunteers carry crates across the boardwalk]
[Sound: Wooden planks creak, footsteps, the soft chatter of many voices planning and joking]
[Music: A light, insistent motif — energetic but warm]

You walk the boardwalk with a clipboard heavier than it should be, the cooperative's name stamped on the top in your handwriting. The tide is out enough that the reedbeds glisten like a spread of coins. You hear snatches of conversation: someone teaching another how to knot a line; a child laughing as a volunteer lifts them onto a bale of straw.

Riv finds you, breathless with excitement and a little damp from the mist.

Riv: "We've got three local photographers willing to do pro-bono shots. If we put the weigh-station by the pier and invite the mayor, the photos will be on every feed she checks."

You: "Good. Make sure the narrative isn't just 'look at our harvest' — make it 'this is community work, this is livelihoods, this is resilience.'"

Riv: "Got it. Framing, not fluff."

Jun joins, wiping hands on a rag, and leans against the railing with a look that says he's both exhausted and ready to keep going.

Jun: "We're on schedule. Amina's assistant confirmed the meeting time. She wants numbers, not speeches. If we can show volunteer hours and yields, that's concrete."

Your stomach flips at the political hinge of it: evidence in exchange for political muscle. You feel like an operator setting a lever into motion.

You: (internal) You are both steward and strategist now. Your bluntness has become a tool that shapes policy, and your stubbornness is teaching people to keep showing up. You think of how lonely it felt before, how you kept the metal box closed, how the ledger sat like a private inventory. Now the ledger is a public narrative.

Elias appears beside you like a steady shadow and offers a thermos; the steam smells faintly of sea mint.

Elias: "You haven't had breakfast, I don't think. You keep giving away your sleep to meetings."

You: (smiling despite the tightness) "Someone has to keep the schedule honest."

Elias: (earnest) "You don't have to carry all the honesty alone. Let me run the volunteer training for the data, and Jun can lead the pier work. Riv will handle messaging. You—" (he hesitates, examines your face) "—you'll be the voice that turns all of it into a story the town wants to join."

The warmth of his certainty is a buoy. You realize how trust has threaded through these days: in your hands, in the ledger, in the way people show up for early mornings and late nights. Romantic trust has not been an abrupt confession; it's threaded through logistics, through reliance on small promises kept. That steady weave feels more intimate than any sudden declaration.

[Music: Strings swell brighter; the town's pulse quickens but feels together]

Later, at the municipal offices, the air is cooler, clinical, but there's a hum of possibility. Mayor Amina receives you in a room of folding chairs and a whiteboard scrawled with budgets. Her eyes are warm but direct.

Mayor Amina: "You built momentum fast. The grant matches could free up funds for equipment and stipends. But matching requires municipal buy-in. How are you proposing we structure that ask?"

You lay out the plan: a cooperative harvest this month, monitoring data collected and presented, a matched grant request for seed funding and pier reinforcement, and contingency plans for escalation if the policy route stalls. You speak plainly about risks and benefits; you speak in that language your mother taught you — plans that can be built and tended.

Amina listens, nods slowly, then allows herself a small smile.

Mayor Amina: "This is the kind of plan that can survive a council vote. Bring me the numbers and the schedule. If it's credible, I'll put it on the agenda. But understand — I'll need to hear that the town supports it, not just that you'll make a spectacle."

You: "We want more than spectacle, Mayor. We want structures that last."

She folds her hands, the kind of gesture that contains both the exhaustion of municipal life and a ready, guarded hope.

Mayor Amina: "Then give me those structures. I'll do my part. But make sure your volunteers know what they're signing up for. Promise me responsible escalation, not reckless publicity."

You: (decisive) "We won't burn bridges for headlines."

When you step back outside, the sun finally breaks a little through the clouds, striking the wet boardwalk and sending a ribbon of light across the harbor. The cooperative hums with activity. The plan is written in your head now, fleshed out into tasks and names and times.

You feel the tide under your feet: rising, not just in water but in possibility. The town is choosing to act. You have chosen to convene that action with a hand that is more strategic than you might once have imagined.

He already pictures the volunteer badges and the clean spreadsheets that will follow." ]

The choices you make now will shape tone and tempo, but none of them feels like a betrayal of the cooperative's purpose. They are flavors on the same dish: urgency, evidence, and demonstration. All of them can help the town.

[Music: A lingering hopeful chord, held like a breath before action]

You gather the volunteers into a rough circle on the pier as the morning's work day begins. Hands are counted. Roles assigned. A sense of competence fills the air like the smell of fresh-cut rope.

Your voice rings clear.

You: "We show them what we can do. We show them we are worth investing in. We start with a harvest, we bring the numbers to the mayor. If the city moves, we keep building. If it doesn't, we make ourselves impossible to ignore."

The volunteers nod, not because they love the politics, but because they love the town. That feeling — civic, stubborn, tender — is the current that moves you.

[Scene: Greenhouse Rooftop Lab | Midday — Return]
[Visual: Golden strip of sunlight through greenhouse glass; volunteers come and go like musical notes]
[Sound: Laughter mixed with instructions; the count of volunteers ticking like a metronome]
[Music: Steady, ascending strings]

You sit for a breath and run a hand along the ledger's cover in your pocket, feeling the imprint of the town's name. The decision now is not whether to act — it is whether to lean one way or another to get the action the traction it needs. There is courage and consequence in every route; there is also a rising faith that you are not the only one who can carry this.

Your jaw unclenches with the simple knowledge that whatever you choose will be shared. The cooperative has become a muscle built by many hands. The town is learning to lift with you.

You look up at Elias, at Jun, at Riv, at the volunteers moving through the greenhouse like a chorus. You feel a clean, fierce hope. This is the place you refused to leave. This is the life you are shaping.

[Music: Swells into a bright, expectant motif]

You breathe once, steady, and offer the options to the group — to yourself.
