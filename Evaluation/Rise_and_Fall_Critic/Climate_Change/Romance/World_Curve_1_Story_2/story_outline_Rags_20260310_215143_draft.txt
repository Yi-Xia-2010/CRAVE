## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Threads

---

## World View
Atera is a near-future coastal town (circa 2038) that lives in the margins between old fishing economies and glassy redevelopment. Sea-level rise and more volatile storms have redrawn shorelines; king-tide marks are painted on pilings like scars and stories. Politically, Atera is a small municipality negotiating with regional developers and grant-funded scientific teams. Amina Bakar, the mayor, mediates between developers who promise infrastructure and grassroots actors demanding ecological stewardship. Class and identity are intimate and visible: multigenerational fishers and craft workers, younger activists learning community science, and outside urban planners with polished solutions. Culture blends practical mutual aid (workshops, shared meals, repair collectives) with a faintly wary appetite for outside capital. Religion is diffuse — rituals are civic and ecological rather than doctrinal: ceremonies mark the planting of breakwaters as much as funerals for lost docks. Geography centers on harbor piers, reedbed boardwalks, a rooftop greenhouse above an old workshop, a glass planning office on a bluff, and subtidal kelp beds. The atmosphere is melancholic but resolute: salt in the air, patched nets drying in the rain, and conversations that pivot between whether to preserve history or to rebuild for safety. Everything feels like an argument made in the voice of someone who believes repair is love.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I narrate as Mika Hoshino, a pragmatic repairswoman inheriting her late mother's workshop and the moral responsibility to keep Atera whole. The story is a romance woven into a town-scale response to climate threats. Beginning in a shared crisis moment, I choose whom to trust: Elias, the gentle marine biologist advocating living systems like kelp farms; Soren, the charismatic urban planner promising engineered elevation and investment; or the community-first path of collective action. Across the Rise arc, each branch is a steady progression toward resilience, not a descent into defeat. Choices determine what kind of success we achieve — grassroots governance, scientific-model replication, hybrid public-private partnerships, or scaled redevelopment with ecological safeguards. Along the way my reluctance to ask for help loosens; I learn to delegate, negotiate, and anchor love alongside duty. Romances deepen through collaborative work: quiet nights in the rooftop greenhouse with Elias, tense negotiation dinners with Soren, or joyful protests with Riv and Jun. Endings vary in texture — stable cooperative governance (True), hybrid but preserved town fabric (Happy), negotiated compromises with costs (Bittersweet) — but all follow an upward curve as Atera becomes more resilient and my relationships mature into partnerships based on shared labor and trust.
