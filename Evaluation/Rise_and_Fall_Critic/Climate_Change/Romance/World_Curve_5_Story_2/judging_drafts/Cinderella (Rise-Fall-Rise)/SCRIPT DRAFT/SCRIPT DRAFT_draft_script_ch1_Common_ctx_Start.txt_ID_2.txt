You are in the lab before the meeting. The notebooks on the bench smell faintly of peat and iron; pressed marsh grasses slide against the battered leather cover when you flip it open. Your hands are clumsy with gloves and habit: cataloguing seed trays, labeling sedge and cordgrass with a steady script, scanning the tidal model readouts until the numbers look like a language you can translate into safety.

The LED on Jun's data tablet blinks: new runs finished overnight. You steady your breath and let the machine's calm precision argue against the tightness in your chest.

Aria: "Jun—walk me through the new resampling. Are we looking at a two-centimeter margin here, or is that confidence interval still skewed?"

Jun Park: (peering over his glasses, tapping the tablet) "The interval tightened. We ran the estuarine coupling using the updated roughness coefficients from the marsh cores you sent. Two to three centimeters—statistically significant when you run the ensemble with the buffer vegetation modeled as porous—so: stable trends in storm attenuation, assuming we keep planting density above the tipping threshold."

You listen to Jun's voice and let the technical terms settle like pack ice finding its place. The models are good. The models mean you have a case to make tonight. It helps, and it doesn't fix the ache in the hollow behind your ribs but it steadies your grip on the ledger.

You: "Two to three. That's enough to argue for the living shoreline approach, not a stopgap seawall."

Jun: (a small, pleased lift at the corner of his mouth) "It is. And the variance narrows if we include the community-managed check dams—your proposed staggered berms reduce scour in the channels by a measurable percent. Also —" (he hesitates, glancing up) "—if Marta can secure early volunteer plots, we can show a pilot this season."

Your mouth wants to say yes immediately. Your chest wants to walk out and start spooning mud into trays. Instead you fold your hands and talk about logistics—permits, schedule, a concise ask for the meeting.

You: "Then we'll focus the talk on practical milestones: three pilot check dams, a volunteer planting rota, and a living-market component to offset costs. Short, credible steps."

Jun: "Short and credible. That's persuasive. People trust something they can see happen."

Eli stands a little way behind you with his tool box open, fiddling with a length of rope. He doesn't crowd you. He never crowds you. His presence is an offer: help, proximity, not a demand.

You: "I want people to be safe. I want them to have work that lasts."

Eli: "And love, maybe, in between the practical bits." (He says it with a lightness that isn't careless; it's a wish.)

You allow the hope in his voice to settle with the models and the plans. The rising arc of the day gathers people—Marta already posting flyers, Jun checking a tablet, Lina scribbling questions into her folder.

You stand, the boardwalk creaking like an old promise. Tonight you will speak. Tonight the community will hear a plan that has the chance to become something more than a blueprint: it might become a life.

You breathe in the brine and ledger of your life in this town—salt on skin, ink on pages, earth beneath the nails—and your resolve consolidates into a simple, clean sentence you can carry like a lantern.
