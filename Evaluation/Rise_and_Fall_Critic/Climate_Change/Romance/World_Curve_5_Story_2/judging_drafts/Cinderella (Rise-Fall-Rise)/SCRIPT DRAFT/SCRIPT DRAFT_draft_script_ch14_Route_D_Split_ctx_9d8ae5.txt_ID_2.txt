[Scene: Old Pier Market | Early Morning]
[Visual: Lanterns gutter to pale day; tarps sag with last night's rain. The boardwalk still smells of peat and smoke. People cluster in smaller knots than usual—faces set, hands busy with nets and crates.]
[Sound: Low murmur, a cart wheel dragged over wet wood, gulls distant and impatient]
[Music: Minor piano motif, a single low cello underpinning tension]

You stand where the market meets the boardwalk, the coil of rope heavy at your shoulder. The beam from the horizon: it hangs in your memory like a bright bruise. The market's life halos that bruise—vendors lifting their heads, the rhythm of barter a thin skin over something raw. You can taste salt and smoke on your tongue and an ache under your ribs that isn't just fatigue; it's the sense that whatever started here will be measured now in other people's calendars.

Marta pulls you aside without ceremony, her paint-stained hands more steady than her voice. "They called a special session," she says. "Council's meeting at ten. Lina sent a message—there's been a vote next door. Emergency approvals."

Old Man Rafi stands near a stall where dried kelp hangs like prayer flags. He doesn't speak at first; he only looks out toward the water, the lines around his eyes deepening. When he does, the words are thin and precise. "When the shore is taken that quick, the people go with it or are left with ghosts. We don't have many ghosts to spare."

You close your fingers around the rope. Momentum has felt like promise before; now it tastes like a test.

You step from the market toward the Pavilion. The boardwalk feels narrower than it should—crowds pressed into conversation, eyes flicking toward you, expecting. You are the one who asked the town to take on a different relationship with the shoreline. Now the shoreline answers back with decisions that will not wait.

[Scene: Redevelopment Pavilion / Council Chambers | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Stainless atrium, rows of chairs, a long table with microphones. On the wall, a live feed shows a neighboring district's shoreline construction: cranes, concrete pours, a prefab seawall rising like a wound.]
[Sound: Murmur grows to a roar when the feed switches; beneath it, the steady click of a projector and the shuffling of paper]
[Music: Low, insistent drones; heartbeat percussion]

You step into the room with Marta and Eli at your side. Jun is already at the end of the table, scrolling through datasets with a tautness to his jaw that makes you uneasy. Lina stands by the projector, face half-lit, hands clasped as if they might keep trembling if she lets them go. Caden arrives last, perfectly composed—his suit still dry, a slim case in one hand, a practiced placidity in his smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Caden: "Good morning. I know the visuals are stark. It's fast; that's the point. Investors moved quicker than expected, and the neighboring council used emergency powers. We're… being asked to respond."

Caden's voice is even, the kind of evenness that can be a comfort—or a trap. He speaks like a machine converted to persuasion.

Lina: (quiet, urgent) "They presented a funding package tied to immediate approvals. If we stall, the money goes elsewhere. The argument is that if we don't accept, our people will be left without relocation options when the next storm hits."

You watch Lina's fingers. She presses the pad of her thumb to her notes until the paper creases. You know her—she is not a person who intends harm. She is a person wringing a compromise from a rope that keeps slipping.

Eli: (leaning forward, palms flat on the table) "So 'relocation options' become a two-tiered deal. We give them permission, they get the land, they get the ports. The co-ops get, what—a voucher and a vague promise? That's not safety, Lina. That's displacement dressed as aid."

Eli's voice carries the market in it—practical, blunt, threaded with a heat that people rally to. He looks at you, at Jun, at the vendors packed into the back rows.

You want to meet his eyes and say something that will bend the room. You reach for your notes and feel the familiar steadiness of your plan under your fingers. But there are facts you cannot change with a clipboard. The neighboring district's seawall is a fait accompli; the investors are already presenting models on the live feed; permits are stamped in other offices.

Jun: (tapping a tablet) "The modeling shows higher surge risk where they poured concrete. It's short-term protection with long-term erosion downstream. If they succeed there, pressure will mount here for similar quick fixes. The funding timeline they're using to justify emergency approval—it's aggressive and legally questionable, but it's structured to look time-sensitive." (He lets out a breath.) "We can challenge the legal basis, but it will take time and money."

Dialogue flows like a current: confrontation, rebuttal, plea. The room is a net where every argument catches on somebody's history.

Marta: (standing) "People on the pier are terrified, Aria. They came this morning with their laundry and their babies and a brand-new fear. If you ask them to resist, what do you ask them to bet on? Talent? Time? You asked us before to make hard asks—this feels harder."

You feel Marta's words as a pressure against your ribs. You are the person who translates science into community action; your instinct is to map out steps, to name risks and mitigations. Yet the scale of the choice here is more human than technical. It involves livelihoods, memories, where people bury their dead.

Old Man Rafi: (when finally he speaks) "There's a line we cross when we treat a coastline like a ledger. You mark what stays and what goes. The shore keeps memory—if you pull families and anchor them on new ground, those stories loosen. You can build a wall, but you can't force the stories to come with the stones."

His words hit the room like salt. Someone coughs. A vendor in the back—young, his hands still smelling of fish—shakes his head slowly.

Vendor: "My father's catch is tied to the channels that run near the reed beds. If they rebuild with more ports and less marsh, my nets catch nothing. What do you say we do then?"

Caden: (folding his hands) "We can make room for micro-enterprises in the redevelopment. Public-private partnerships include community quotas. The fast-track is not an eviction; it's a reallocation of funds to secure people's safety. Think of it pragmatically: immediate housing, jobs in construction. Stabilize first. Save lives."

The word "pragmatically" rests on a table between you like a knife. Your internal calculus spins—save lives now at the cost of cultural ruin, or resist and risk immediate suffering.

Your internal monologue runs rapid, a metronome of what-ifs. You think of the sibling you lost to a storm—how the tidal mark cut through the family house—and of the stubbornness that turned grief into project. You think of Eli building floating farms and of Marta patching tarps. You feel the community's expectations like a mantle you were not sure you wanted until you had it on.
