[Scene: Brineheart Wetland Corridor | Early Morning]
[Visual: Low silver light across reeds; raised boardwalks slick with overnight mist. A shallow semicircle of disturbed water glints where new work was begun. Small flags mark pilot plots; a ring of flattened reeds fans out like a bruise.]
[Sound: Distant motor hum from the barge, the soft, anxious murmur of vendors gathering, a gull’s single, keening cry]
[Music: A low, strained cello line — steady, taut; under it, a piano that refuses to resolve]

You come back to the corridor thinking in habits: check the gauge, read the sediment lines, listen to the water's breath as if it will tell you whether something is wrong. The compromise you helped shape sits like a thin skin over everything — flexible, recent, and vulnerable. The pilot was supposed to buy time: staged interventions, matched funding, community oversight. It is also, increasingly, a timetable someone else can accelerate.

Jun meets you near the first check dam, his tablet a pale rectangle of data under his arm. He doesn’t look up from the screen when he speaks; his voice is dampened by the reeds.

Jun Park: "We expected settlement settling, not this—look at the scour depth here. These readings are out of sync with planned flow rates."

You crouch down, the board under your palms groaning wet and familiar. The peat smells sharp, like old tea and salt. Your fingers come away dark, staining the leather of your notebook.

You: "How far off? Could it be a miscalibration from the contractors? Or—"

Jun Park: (shakes his head) "Not just miscalibration. The clamp torque on the temporary bypass was set intentionally outside spec. The fracture pattern suggests a sudden loosening. Mechanical failure doesn't usually look like this."

You feel the sentence land like a stone. "Intentionally." The syllable opens space in your chest where memory and fear sit side-by-side: a sibling’s laughter cut short by a wall of water. You have learned to translate fear into checklists; this feels different — intimate and accusing.

[Sound: A single, metallic snap — far at first, then close; a vendor calls out in alarm]

You look up. Where the temporary bypass meets the market-side channel, a clamp that held a sheet piling assembly flashes and shears. Water finds new paths, carving into the reed roots, sending muddy fingers under vendor stalls.

[Visual: Slow motion of water spilling through gaps; wet tarps whipping; seed trays and jars tumbling into grey water]
[Sound: A chorus — the slap of falling crates, people shouting, the hollow crash of wood giving way]

Eli is there in seconds, hair sticking to his forehead, hands already reaching for a young woman who clutches a ruined basket of seedlings. His jaw sets like a line of rope.

Eli Navarro: (to the vendor) "Hey, breathe. Give me your hand, I’ve got you. Marisol, cover the trays—no, lift them away from the flow—"

You want to move with him, to be met and steady the way he does, but the corridor calls you to other things: trace the path of the scour, note the clamp, photograph the fracture for Jun. The decisions you made in the council — to allow staged infrastructure under investor timelines — press at the back of your eyes like winter.

[Scene: Old Pier Market | Mid-Morning — Aftermath]
[Visual: The market is a frenetic collage: sodden awnings sag, lanterns swing, steam rises from overturned kettles. Seed trays lie half-submerged, cloths cling to warped boards. Vendors stand in clusters, faces written with loss and fury.]
[Sound: Low, angry voices; wads of paper slapped against tablet screens; a distant drone of a council security line being called in]
[Music: Sparse strings—minor key; a heartbeat of percussion beneath the murmurs]

Marta is the first to find you in the crowd. Her braid is a gray rope, her hands smeared with mud and dye. She doesn’t insult; she simply stands and lets the words press into you.

Marta Quin: "They rushed it, Aria. They said the timeline was non-negotiable. You stood in that room and… you signed it." Her voice is worn like the edge of a well-used tool.

You can taste the accusation before it forms. The room narrows to a point where every face is a question. You can see where Lina stands at the margin, phone clapped tight to her ear, eyes wet. Caden is farther off, composed as always, but his smile is thin now, the practiced politeness of someone counting losses.

Caden Holt: (to a reporter nearby) "We are convening an immediate review. My teams have been transparent from the start. Any suggestion this was a predictable failure is unfounded." He looks at you on the last word, just long enough for the photographers to catch your name in the same frame as a trouble headline.

You: (voice small, measured) "We set parameters. We expected gradual settlement. This pattern—" (you look at Jun) "—isn’t that what you said?"

Jun Park: (brisk, but with an undercurrent of alarm) "The mechanical evidence points to enforced stress at the clamp junction. Whoever tightened or loosened it did so with intent. That’s beyond the scope of our model's assumptions."

A vendor steps forward, a man whose stall bore the smoke-curing racks for small fish. He spits at the ground between you.

Vendor: "You gave them permission, Aria. You let the deadlines in. My wife had three months of work there. You took that from us."

Eli intercepts quickly, stepping between you and the vendor, palms up as if to dissipate heat.

Eli Navarro: (to the vendor) "I know this is raw. We all know what’s at stake. Blame helps no one—"

Vendor: "No—no, he needs to feel it. You were the one in their meeting, Aria. You shook hands with the plan."

You feel each accusation like a pebble landing on your chest. Your throat clenches around a memory you have not let yourself hold lately: the swell of water, the terror of being swept, the absolute helplessness of watching. You’d promised yourself you would not let that happen again, and here you are, fingers still smelling of peat, implicated by proximity and the compromises you brokered.

[Scene: Council Chambers / Redevelopment Pavilion | Afternoon — Emergency Session]
[Visual: Glass doors are micro-frosted with condensation. Inside, the room seems narrower than before—polished, clean, designed to corral emotion into sound bites. A line of council members sits with tablet screens glowing. Camera crews cluster near the back like small, impatient birds.]
[Sound: The hum of fluorescent lights, the scratch of pens, the murmur of digital polls populating]
[Music: A sparse piano, descending motifs—an unresolved cadence that leaves the listener unmoored]

Lina stands up as you enter, her face washing with wear. She lays a hand on the transom beside you, an anchor of someone trying to bridge two burning things.

Lina Voss: (softly) "Aria, thank you for coming. We need the technical briefing from Jun, and… we need you to address the community. People are scared."

Caden slides his gaze across the room, tactical, then lands briefly on you. You can feel the stakeholders leaning inward like a tide. Investors on a side screen tighten threads of messages. Whispers about "capital protection" and "contingency clauses" thread the edges of the session.

Caden Holt: (measured, distancing) "We are initiating emergency repairs. Contractors have been engaged. We will ensure swift compensation to affected vendors. This is an isolated incident, and it will not derail the broader project."

You want to ask: "Who authorized the clamp configuration? Who tightened the torque beyond spec?" But every question you raise courts implication. Your advisory stamp is visible enough to be used as an arrowhead.

Jun Park gives his briefing with the steady cadence of someone who has watched data betray quiet certainties.

Jun Park: "The failure signature indicates external manipulation. The component wasn't corroded—it was adjusted. Whoever changed the torque altered the hydraulic load path, and that was the proximate cause of the scour. That is to say: this was engineered."

Murmurs ripple. Lina’s jaw tightens. Caden’s hand flexes around a pen.

Lina Voss: "If that's true—"

Caden Holt: (cuts in) "Then we will pursue whoever is responsible. We must be careful with language until we have full forensic analysis. Speculation invites panic and capital flight."

You can see the logic: keep the optics tidy, avoid triggering a withdrawal of funds that could leave the community destitute. You can also see the cost: silence and hush buy quick stability while letting the truth blur.

A council member at the far end, someone who had once nodded along to the compromise, now speaks with a new, raw edge.

Council Member: "Aria, you were in those negotiations. You signed off on the pilot timeline. Were you aware of any of these shortcuts being presented as acceptable?"

Silence searchlights across your skin. You feel the room's pressure like a low tide receding, exposing the raw underside of your choices. Jun looks at you with something like pity and something like professional urgency.

Jun Park: "We can model repercussions. We can outline immediate mitigations, emergency funds, and forensics, but—"

Eli's voice cuts through the formal theater, louder than the mics, personal and dangerous.

Eli Navarro: (to the council) "Mitigations are afters. People lost their livelihoods today. You can't call this a 'risk' the way you'd call bad weather. That's a human cost and you all have a hand in it."

Caden turns toward Eli with a practiced smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Caden Holt: "Mr. Navarro, we are working to—"

Eli Navarro: (interrupting) "Working isn't enough. Not when the machines are set to speed."

Your chest tightens until you can barely breathe. The cameras are on you. The vendors are in the hallway outside, voices pressed against glass. The coalition you helped hold together feels like salt-stung rope: frayed in places you can see and held taut by a few stubborn hands elsewhere.

You think of what stewardship asked of you—small daily acts that stitch community to coast. You think of the deals struck, the times you told yourself that compromise could be a kind of fidelity. You taste peat and salt and the metallic tang of responsibility.

You realize, with a slow, helpless clarity, that the next move will not simply be tactical. It will be moral and public and possibly ruinous.
