[Scene: Harborfront | Early Morning]
[Visual: Mist-thinned dawn over a row of low wooden piers; salt crust on ropes and nets]
[Music: Low, hollow cello; distant gull calls undercut by the hum of a diesel generator]
[Sound: Water lapping against worn pilings, a loose shutter clattering once and falling still]

You wake to the harbor's hush — the kind that holds the night's weather in its lungs and exhales it slow. Your jacket is damp where you slept against the rail; the hem of your sweater still smells of yesterday's rain and the resin of tar. The storm has scrubbed the sky clean, but it has left the town marked: a ribbon of debris, a boathouse door hanging at an angle like an open wound, nets that used to cradle a season of fish shredded into salt-slick ribbons. The harborfront bulletin had promised less. The harborfront is not what the bulletin promised.

Your waterproof notebook is heavier under your palm than it should be; pages swollen from spray, margins crowded with the shorthand of worry. You thumb it open and the ink bleeds into the familiar lines of plans and lists. In the top margin of a page with tidal diagrams you scrawl, in a hand that feels both steady and jagged: Protect the harbor, protect the people.

The memory comes with the smell of wet kelp and diesel: another early morning, another unpredicted current, and the absence that followed. You count the cost like one who weighs fish — careful, pragmatic. A net costs a season's catch; a boathouse door, the means to claim a livelihood. But what you measure in currency is threaded with a deeper count: names, birthdays, the laughter of a life that used to move along familiar routes. Guilt presses at your collarbone the way salt presses at a wound. You carry it because you promised, once in the fog of grief, to do better by Hearthbend.

From the boardwalk across the inlet comes a different hum: the click of polished shoes on engineered concrete, a sound you know well in theory. The Skyhub delegation will arrive mid-morning. Words that used to be whispers — "regional program," "managed reinforcement," "rapid adaptation" — have hardened into a proposal with glossy pamphlets and metrics. Seren's name glints on the front of those pamphlets the way a blade glints when someone else draws it.

Your loyalty to the town tightens like a rope in both hands. You can already feel the tug between small hands that mend nets and the machine-hands that promise quick salvation. The rope burns. You breathe salt and tar and hold the carved coral pendant under your shirt, feeling its roughness between knuckles. The pendant is small, warm from your skin, an heirloom that has its own grain of salt in the pores. It steadies you.

A shadow detaches itself from a pile of driftwood: Ilias. He walks the dock the way old boats remember tides — sure but careful. The tide-watch bracelet on his wrist catches the weak light; its braided threads glow as if remembering daylight. He carries a cracked jar in both hands, the seal imperfect, and something dark and slick shifts inside.

Ilias: "Morning." (He glances up too quickly, like he wasn't sure you'd be awake.) "The storm did a number on the eastern berths. I—" He lifts the jar a little. "—collected whatever eelgrass I could salvage. If we can nurse it, the restoration trials might still get what they need to test resilience."

You: "You shouldn't have lugged that along the boardwalk." (The wind plucks at your fringe.) "It's fragile."

Ilias: (He shifts, adjusting the jar so water doesn't slosh over his boots.) "Fragile isn't a reason to leave it. Not when it's about to tell us what can live here. And you looked like you needed a cup of actual human coffee, not just seawater." (He smiles, then it fades.) "I heard about the boathouse. Etta sent me."

You: (You press your thumb against the coral pendant and feel the tooth of it, grounding yourself.) "I've catalogued damage. Nets, doors, the eastern net shed—" (You hesitate, because the ledger is both practical and intimate.) "—and people. I need to know who will be able to fish next month."

Ilias: (He nods. He leans on the railing, peering at the grey water.) "Dr. Hana says the eelgrass trials could scale if we can protect the beds and seed them in sheltered pockets. If SeaHub's plan funds controlled nursery expansions, we could—" He stops, searching your face. "—we could make a compromise that keeps people working while we build resilience."

You: (Inside, something tightens. The word compromise tastes metallic.) "Scaled by whom? Who sets the rules for 'sheltered pockets'?" (You choose the sharpness of the question, not because you want to cut him, but because you want to know where he stands.)

Ilias: "By people who measure and by people who mend," he says slowly. "By communities who are at the table with scientists. Nobody wants your fishing grounds turned into data rectangles, Maya."

You: (You want to believe him. You want to believe the slow arc of his optimism holds against the quick promise of Skyhub's capital. The jar in his hands feels like the hinge between those two futures.) "Promises in pamphlets don't anchor nets. The town needs something that won't be taken apart next season."

Ilias: (He meets your gaze, and meets the unsaid in it.) "I know. That's why I'm here. I don't want to deliver data to a boardroom and walk away." (He taps the jar as if it were a metronome.) "I want to build with you. Even if the work is slow."

The air is cold enough that exhalation becomes a visible thing between you — two breaths in a landscape that is learning to shake itself with every storm. You feel steadied, for a moment, by Ilias's presence. That steadiness is fragile too.

You turn your attention back to the harbor. The market stalls are shuttered, tarpaulins flapping like quiet flags. A group has gathered by the mayor's bulletin board — half curiosity, half obligation — and already the conversations carry the sweeter, more dangerous word: grant. If Mayor Ansel's office offers funds, it will be a relief; if those funds come with strings, they will strangle more than they save. You feel the old rhythm under all of it — survival, negotiation, the bending of people into protocols that weren't made for them.

Etta's voice, when it comes, is a rope thrown over the wind: sharp, steady, not quite brittle. She appears from behind a stack of lobster crates, shawl damp, eyes like flint.

Etta Muir: "Maya!" (She doesn't need to say your surname. Her voice is enough to pull a dozen memories into the same room.) "You going to stand with them, or stand with Skyhub's glossy maps? I don't want a wall where my daughter's nets used to be."

You: (Your jaw tightens. Etta's memory holds the town like an old buoy.) "I won't let them take what keeps people fed and afloat. Not without a fight."

Etta Muir: (She steps closer, the shawl rustling like dried kelp.) "Words are good. Action is better. When the mayor opens his mouth about grants, I want you at the front. Tell them what a boathouse means. Tell them what fishing means."

You: "At the meeting?" (You feel the weight of public voice like a rock pulled from a pocket.) "You know how people listen to me, Etta. But speeches don't seal nets. They change minds if they're heard. I can do it, but they need—"

Etta Muir: (She cuts you off gently, but with force.) "They need you. That's the truth. They need someone who'll say what 'we' mean, not what 'they' will decide. You don't have to speak brave for the town — you already are. Speak smart as well."

You: (You want to argue the logistics — you always want to keep the ledger and the people's stories in alignment — but Etta's certainty wraps around your plan like rope. It is a comfort but also pressure. You feel your ribs tighten under it.)

Before you can say more, Mayor Ansel steps out of the shadow of the council boat with a folder under his arm, tweed cut wet at the shoulders. He smiles — the practiced kind that sits a distance from his eyes.

Mayor Ansel: "Maya. Good to see you up early. The storm was rough. We're lucky no one's been displaced." (He opens the folder the way one opens a gate — with an even hand.) "There is funding on the table from the regional initiative. It could shore up some private piers, fund immediate reinforcements. It will require a formal partnership with Skyhub and a memorandum of understanding."

You: (You feel each of his words press like a stamp, certifying a future you would be accountable for.) "Partnership with strings, then."

Mayor Ansel: (He meets your gaze, the practiced politician acknowledging the hurt behind your measured skepticism.) "There will be oversight. There will be standards. It's not the only option, but the town needs access to cash, Maya. The council will want answers today."

You: "Cash that requires ceding oversight won't be protection, Ansel. It will be red tape and relocation policy in disguise. We need local control in any agreement."

Mayor Ansel: "Local control where feasible. But we also have liability to consider. If larger infrastructure prevents another disaster, we can't dismiss it out of principle." (He sighs; you hear strain under the civility.) "I need someone who can make the case for Hearthbend's needs — balanced, pragmatic. Will you present the community's position at the meeting?"

You: (The rope tightens again. If you say yes, you step into a public role that will shape how the town is governed. If you say no, someone else will, and the argument may not protect the nets you care about.) "I'll be there."

Mayor Ansel: (Relief flickers across his face.) "Good. I'll see you at High Tide Boardwalk then. And Maya…" (He leans in, lowering his voice.) "There is pressure from the regional office to approve quick measures. If Hearthbend delays too long, the window for certain grants might close."

You: (Time curves like the harbor wall, rising faster than you want. You touch the coral pendant again, and the ledger in your notebook flutters in a sudden gust like a small bird startled.) "I understand."

The morning presses forward. People bustle in small knots, counting losses and saying names aloud like offerings. You walk the pier with Ilias at your shoulder and Etta's admonitions echoing behind you. The cracked jar of eelgrass sits in your chest as much as on the dock — a small, stubborn life that might become a root for something bigger. Or it might be catalogued and forgotten in a meetingroom file.

You open your notebook and write in the margin beneath the scrawl you made earlier: Protect the harbor, protect the people. The ink blurs slightly where the page is damp, like a promise being tested by water.

You know action is overdue. You know too that every choice now will not merely redistribute resources — it will rearrange lineage and livelihood, who stays and who leaves, which names remain spoken at the docks. The weight of that knowledge sits in your palms like wet rope.

A bell rings from the harbor hall — a summons for the meeting, a caw of gulls, a town constructing the outline of its next day.

You breathe, listening to the hush that comes before a public thing, and the harbor holds its own breath with you. The storm has cleaned the sky but not the consequences. The ledger is heavy, your guilt is honest, and the decisions are impending.
