You tighten the collar of your patched field jacket and feel the old brass compass charms against your sternum. Your notebook is tucked under your arm like a promise you haven't had time to break. Today is loud because it must be — not performance but insistence. You let your breath match the rhythm of people arriving: careful, then eager, then steady.

Rafi moves through the crowd with his camera like a tide—never still, always finding the small moments that say everything. He points the lens at Etta before you can, and she holds an old net like a relic offered to strangers. The net's fibers smell of brine and fish oil; light catches on the frayed knots and makes them look like threads of history.

Etta Muir: "Ain't nothin' we got that wasn't earned in salt and sweat, Maya. Don't let 'em hand that away."

You hear the oldwoman's voice as you do the rest of the people here: as ballast. Her certainty makes your ribs feel less hollow.

You stand at the head of the march with the other organizers. Neighbors you inspected boats for years—hands that know rope and repair—carry seedlings in battered milk crates. Someone is handing out lengths of rope and strips of remnant tarp to make banners. The slogan curls across weathered canvas in uneven letters: HANDS NOT WALLS.

Your planner's notes, once margins and messy sketches, have become a folded petition in your breast pocket. The plan you've scribbled into nights and back alleys now has a backbone: stepped berms where they fit, community nurseries for kelp and eelgrass, and a decentralized chain of care that doesn't siphon decision-making away from those who live here.

Ilias is at the Harborfront with a portable propagation table. Mud crusts his elbows; kelp fronds sag in trays, green and patient. He catches your eye and grins in the way that almost always makes the day feel manageable.

Ilias Navarro: "Got enough starter lines for a hundred suckers," (he says, wiping his hands), "and Dr. Sato's samples survived the overnight chill. You're sure about this—about going public?"

Maya Kestrel: "We are sure," you answer, and even saying it makes the sound real. "If the town doesn't decide what we protect, someone will decide for us."

Ilias pauses, then leans forward with that gentle, measuring look you know too well. He remembers the equations and the patience of lab hours; today he chooses shovels and seedlings.

Ilias Navarro: "Alright. Then let's make proof with our sleeves on. I'll run the propagation demos. You run the march."

At the boardwalk you find Mayor Ansel standing on the raised promenade, the hem of his tweed damp with mist. He looks like someone who has made his life into a set of compromises; today those edges show. Behind him, a Skyhub bus arcs silently along the far road, sleek and distant—Seren's machines, polished and patient, orbiting like gulls that never land.

Mayor Ansel: "Maya. A moment?"

Maya Kestrel: (You) "If it's to offer support, I have a list. If it's to ask us to stand down—"

Mayor Ansel: "No. I'm not asking you to stand down. I'm asking you to be reasonable. Seren's offer brings funding, engineering. It'll alleviate immediate risk."

You can see the political map in his tone—the ledger he keeps balanced on his lap. Your hands tighten on the banner rope.

Maya Kestrel: "Reasonable to you is often expedient to others. We can accept resources without ceding our future. We don't want walls that move us and not the water. We want solutions that let us do the work ourselves."

Mayor Ansel: (He exhales, and the sound is almost a laugh of frustration.) "You ask a lot of me, Maya. The council is losing patience with the delay. But—" (he hesitates, looking at the faces behind you) "—they listen when their neighbors show up. I'm not promising votes, but this… this helps."

Mayor Ansel's admission feels like a small, improbable opening. He isn't offering everything, but he's offering a crack.

Etta steps up and slams the heel of her palm against the rail. Salt flecks her knuckles.

Etta Muir: "We ain't asking for the moon. We ask to be seen. We ask to keep our nets and our nursery beds. We ask that if you build, you do it where our children can still fish."

A cheer rises, rough and earnest. You feel the pitch shift beneath you—anger smoothing into something tender and stubborn. It is a warmth you recognize: the town's body moving to protect itself.

You climb onto a low crate to address the crowd. The wooden box rocks under your weight; someone steadies it. Your notebook feels heavy in your pocket, full of names and maps, but the sound in your chest is lighter than it has been for years.

Maya Kestrel: "We aren't naive. We know the sea is hungry, and we know what it takes to keep it fed correctly. We also know what we've lost when decisions are made for us without knowing the shape of our days. Today, we show that we can do this—together."

Crowd Member: "What do we start with?"

Maya Kestrel: (You) "Kelp nurseries at the Harborfront. Raised path berms at the most vulnerable points, built with community hands. A shared maintenance rota so the responsibility doesn't sit on a single person. And a covenant with the municipality: no relocation plans without consent."

Conversation spreads like warmth. Someone calls for tools, and immediately they appear—shovels, wheelbarrows, buckets. The air smells of wet wood, coffee, and the peppery tang of kelp.

Ilias catches your eye and points at a provisional station he set up. He doesn't need to say the words; you understand. Propagation schedules, tides for planting, volunteers for the nursery—proof that small experiments build resilience.

Ilias Navarro: "If we nail these plots—if the kelp takes—then Dr. Sato has the data. Then the argument isn't just moral; it's demonstrable."

Maya Kestrel: "Then we show them both—the moral and the measurable."

Rafi comes up beside you with a breathless smile.

Rafi Soto: "Mayor's office called—some of the council members are watching this live feed. You're trending in the nearest two counties. Etta's got a fist in the air and my camera is full. Also—" (he grins) "—people want you to say something that isn't a policy word."

At dusk the march dissipates into clusters of people who keep working. You walk down to where Ilias has spread out burlap sacks and trays. His cheeks are streaked with mud and salt; his smile is the kind that says the day mattered.

Ilias Navarro: "We planted the first nursery line. It was shaky, but we have it. People showed up—more than I thought would, actually."

Maya Kestrel: (You) "More than we dared hope. It feels… possible."

Ilias studies your face like he's cataloguing light.

Ilias Navarro: "You look like you slept last night."

You almost laugh. You did—fitfully, but with a steadier heartbeat for the first time in a long while. The warmth from the day still sits in your shoulders like sun-warmed stone.

Etta ambles toward you both, her shawl damp and smelling of the sea. She puts a hand on your shoulder, rough and sure.

Etta Muir: "Don't let the bus scare you, child. They'll come 'round. Folks like you give 'em no choice but to reckon."

Rafi appears with his camera dangling from his neck and a USB stick in his hand.

Rafi Soto: "Public's eating it. Comments are mostly good. A few trolls, but mostly… honest interest. Mayor's office had a quiet council meeting earlier—he said your presence made them hold off finalizing anything tonight."

Mayor Ansel steps out of the fog, hands in his pockets. He seems smaller in the low light, less a man of tweed and more a man who knows the weight of towns.

Mayor Ansel: "I'll be frank. Votes and budgets and the press—they don't move like a tug. But people do. I can't promise an outcome yet, but I can promise patience—more time to consider community-led plans."

You feel the promise like a faint lantern extended across a darker path. It won't light everything, but it shows where to walk.
