[Scene: Harborfront | Early Morning]
[Visual: Mist-thin morning light over wet piers; nets and tarpaulins sag with last night's rain]
[Sound: Gulls wheel and cry; a distant diesel engine coughs; the creak of damp wood underfoot]
[Music: Low, somber piano with a slow, tidal pulse]

You wake to the harbor's hush like a bruise—muted, cold, and full of promise that something is already wrong. The storm scrubbed the sky clean but left the town raw: salt crust in the joints of the pier, puddles that mirror cloud, and the metallic tang of wet tar in the air. Your field jacket is still damp where you slept under the lee of a stacked crate; your fingertips remember the notches of the notebook inside your breast pocket before you pull it free. The waterproof cover is scuffed. The pages inside feel heavier than they should.

You move along the pier, notebook pressed to your ribs like a small, stubborn ledger of care. The bulletin said "minor impact" and a list of quick repairs—words tailored to calm the market. The harborfront tells a different story. Nets hang in shredded ribbons from a stall where Hannah's family sells bait. A boathouse door—your childhood neighbor's—has been warped on its hinges, the metal tongue twisted until it no longer meets the frame. A skiff lists at an angle, its name half-submerged and shivering in the grey water.

You count the cost, and the counting always becomes memory. You think of the flash storm years ago and the sibling whose face you have tried not to press into the ledger of causes, the weight that suspected choices can carry. Your coral pendant, rough and salt-bleached, rests against your palm as you thumb its notch. It grounds you and pulls the past into the present: protect the harbor, protect the people. You write it in the margin of today's entry with the same blunt pen your hand has trusted for years.

You breathe in brine and oil and the small, living smell of seaweed. Each inhale is an accounting: how much can a town shoulder before its lineage—its docks, its nets, the old ways—become collateral? The civic meeting is today. The Skyhub delegation arrives. Whispers that used to live in the market now have suits and talking points; the word Seren has started to feel like weather itself, an inevitable front approaching.

Footsteps join the planks behind you. The sound is measured, familiar: rubber on wet wood, the soft clinking of that tide-watch bracelet you once spent two summers helping Ilias repair after it cracked against a reef.

[Ilias: (soft, trying to be light) "Morning, Maya. You look like you wrestled the tide and lost."]

You let a breath out that comes out smaller than a laugh. Ilias carries a battered jar in one hand; inside, eelgrass pulses like green lung. The glass has a hairline crack and sea salt has salted a rim. He sets it down on a low crate as if placing a delicate vote.

[Visual: Ilias at the dock, hair damp at the temples; the tide-watch bracelet catches a stray shard of sun]
[Sound: The faint bubbling of water inside the jar]

You want to ask one thing first—the state of the eelgrass. It matters. It matters because kelp and seagrass are not abstract lines on a report; they are nursery and bank and the soft scaffolding that slows a surge. They are the difference between a net that returns and a season of empty lines on ledgers.

You let the question unfurl instead as a gesture, fingers brushing the jar where the crack catches light.

You: "Is it salvageable?"

Ilias kneels, palm hovering over the water as if feeling an unseen current. He speaks slowly, the way you both have learned to speak when the stakes are biological and the language precise.

Ilias: "Some of it. The holdfasts are weak in patches. Salinity spikes peeled off the newest fronds. But there are runners — still viable lines if we can secure them soon."

You hold his gaze for a long breath. His optimism is tethered to experiments and fieldwork; it steadies you, but it is cautious—more protocol than sermon. He nods toward the sound of trucks—Skyhub's trailers, a glinting line against the fog—and you see the worry flicker behind his smile.

Ilias: "They're bringing models, Maya. High-tech scaffolding for eelgrass restoration, nutrient-flow sensors. Scaled restoration—if it works—could accelerate recovery across sections we don't have manpower for."

You taste salt and something bitter. Scaled restoration is desire framed as solution, but you have learned that "scale" often comes with a price: standardization over local practice, efficiency over lineage. Your hands, trained to mark lines on maps that honor old currents and old rights, clench at the edge of the crate.

You: "And the people who fish behind those lines? How do we keep their say?"

Ilias rubs a thumb along his bracelet as if drawing an answer from it.

Ilias: "That's the tricky part. We can design co-op protocols—data-sharing, maintenance labs run here. I want it to be led here, Maya, with us running the trials."

You want to believe him with the whole of you. You have to believe him. But promises whispered in a lab can sound very different when mapped to mortgages and nets and the memory of houses that turned into slick little altars of orange life jackets in flash storms.

You move through the harbor, cataloguing damage like a rhyme—what needs wood, what needs rope, who needs a wage advance to tide them through repair. The town's rhythm is a ledger written in living things: a gull snags a bit of netting, a child flings a rock into a puddle and the splash fissures the mirror-sky, revealing the seawall's silhouette. The Skyhub trailers park at the head of the quay, their reflective sides bright and alien. You can see men and women in clean outerwear stepping down, clipboards and tablets bright against the grey. They carry an efficiency that smells faintly of citrus disinfectant and money.

A voice like gravel in a man-made suit interrupts the murmur. Mayor Ansel emerges from between two vans, tweed wrapped against damp as though to prove that dignity can hold off water. He gives you a careful smile, the kind that counts votes.

[Mayor Ansel: (measured) "Maya. Thank you for coming down. We've got an opportunity—funding from the region. It's sizeable. We can shore up critical points; Skyhub's program could match it if we accept the framework."]

His hands are open, palms visible. You see strings in this offer before he speaks them: data access, easements on certain shorelines, a management model that centralizes permit power. You already know what centralization smells like—fast permits and lost local control, like a barn door closing with a lock you can't pick.

You: "What's the catch, Ansel? There's always a catch."

He gives a small, almost painful laugh that doesn't reach his eyes.

Mayor Ansel: "There are terms. The region wants a place for pilot projects. There will be oversight and reporting. They'll need assurance they can scale the model. In return, they'll provide materials, crews, and a grant to cover livelihoods during the works."

Etta's voice cuts in from the market, high and not polite. The elder fisherwoman has the harbor's history mapped into the curves of her shoulders; she moves like a current everyone knows to read.

[Etta Muir: (sharp) "Pilot projects. That's corporate-speak for, 'We take what works and leave what we don't.' Tell them Hearthbend isn't a lab rat. Tell them our graves are not collateral."]

Etta's eyes find you and hold like an accusation wrapped in warmth. She smells of sea and smoke; her shawl is still damp from last night, and her hands show the network of lines a lifetime of knots etches into skin.

You: "Etta, they—"

Etta Muir: "Don't 'they' me, Maya. You were there when we lost Marie. You remember the ledger that didn't account for her kin. If you stand up and give them room to redraw our maps without us, whose ghosts are we inviting back in?"

The public exchange ripples outward. A vendor halts with a half-opened tin, eyes darting. Ilias steps between you and the mayor, palms out in a small, placating arc.

Ilias: "Mayor, Etta—look. We all want the harbor safe. If Skyhub's tech can restore eelgrass beds faster, that's less strain on the netters this season. But it has to be a co-created plan. We need binding clauses for local oversight."

Mayor Ansel looks at Ilias like a man trying to weigh a scale with two plates that never balance. He nods, showing the map on his tablet as if the pixels will answer for him.

Mayor Ansel: "We can negotiate oversight. We can recommend community liaisons in the pilot agreements."

Etta's laugh is a small, dry thing.

Etta Muir: "Recommend. People write 'recommend' and mean nothing. You'll need teeth, not recommendations."

The conversation unspools—words heaped and reshaped—while in the harbor the gulls argue above. You listen, counting the costs that the mayor's small concessions might not cover: ancestral fishing rights, access to seed lines for eelgrass, the unquantified value of a dock where names are carved on the beams. Ilias speaks about scaled restoration, threaded with hope. Etta demands public stands and ironclad promises. Mayor Ansel offers grants with bureaucratic collars.

Your notebook is open now, the margin filled: Protect the harbor, protect the people. Beneath it, a small list: local oversight clauses; seed bank storage in community custody; stipends for fishers during trials.

You feel the rope of loyalty tighten in your hands—toward your fishermen, toward Etta's grief-made-guard, toward the fragile biology of eelgrass and the unquantified worth of places named for ancestors. The storm has lifted the surface of things and revealed the seams. Choices are here already, before any papers are signed: trust the glossy hands that bring quick relief, or hold the town's authority close and risk missing the materials that could accelerate recovery.

You press the coral pendant between your thumb and forefinger again. It tastes like salt and like a memory you haven't fully paid. Your notebook's pen blurs as a breeze crosses the harbor, making the words waggle like flags. There is pressure in the air that has nothing to do with weather—the kind that pulls at loyalties and lineage and asks you to name what you will protect, and how.

[Visual: The Skyhub delegation arranges themselves near the trailers; a glossy banner flaps. Far bank workers begin to unload crates stamped with a corporate seal.]
[Sound: The metallic clatter of crates; a distant, rising conversation that feels like a tide returning]

You write, in a firmer hand than before: Protect the harbor, protect the people.

Your throat tightens. The civic meeting will be held at High Tide Boardwalk—the wall itself a contested monument of what some call progress and others call a wound. The town will be watching. Etta will speak with salt and memory. Mayor Ansel will offer language of balance. Ilias will show you samples and models and promises.

You feel the pull toward the meeting like tide toward a moon that doesn't answer.

Your lungs draw the cold air once more, and you let the harbor's damp hush fold over you like a cloaked promise: action is overdue. You are overdue.

A truck door slams; the Skyhub delegation steps forward under a banner that glows faintly in the wash of morning. The town breathes faster.

You close your notebook and tuck the pen into its loop, coral pendant against your palm, and take one last look at the warped boathouse, the torn nets, the eelgrass in its cracked jar, and the faces that will remember how you choose to stand.

[Music: A single low piano chord sustains and begins to swell — unresolved]

You step toward the Boardwalk. The decision that will ripple from today's meeting waits like a cresting wave.
