### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Ending


[Scene: Community Rooftop Garden | Early Evening — Several Months Later]
[Visual: Rows of raised beds glow under strings of solar lights; new eelgrass trays line skylights like small green seas. People move with practiced, quiet urgency; mud on knees is a badge.]
[Music: Warm, sustaining chord — tempered by a minor string undercurrent]
[Sound: Soft murmur of conversation; distant surf; the creak of a rain barrel settling]

You remember the night you hit "publish" — the protocol spread across the town like an open window. Maps, watering regimens, volunteer rosters; the whole thing, raw and stitched and free. For a while the world answered. Rooftop beds bristled with seedlings. Elders in oilskins came back to tend eelgrass plots as if recognizing a long-lost kin. Fishermen traded once-secret tips for anchor points and seedling months. The waterline, ridiculous and slow, hesitated in a place you could point to and say, "Look."

You sit on the low wall beside Ilias. His jacket smells faintly of kelp and dryer lint from a night sampling at the breakwater. His camera rests against his thigh, lens cap off like an eye attentive to everything. When he smiles at you, it doubles in the crinkling at his eyes — a small, human geography you could memorize.

Ilias: "You did something the town needed. You made it possible for people to try without waiting for permission."

You: "We made it possible. Volunteers stayed up longer than they should have. Etta braided the scheduling like a net."

Ilias: (Laughing softly) "Her nets are better than municipal timetables."

You: "She'd say the same thing about your data sheets if you weren't so earnest about plotting contour lines."

Ilias: (He nudges the notebook you keep tucked beside you) "Your notebook? It's a poor thing to hold against the sea. But it gave people a place to start."

You: (You feel the usual swell — pride braided with that old, sharp guilt. You think of the ledger, of all the names that might have been written in different ink if you had acted sooner.) "We did start. Sometimes that's the most honest thing to do."

Ilias watches you, ocean-blue eyes leveled and steady. There is a modest, earnest pride in him that makes your chest ache with something both warm and fragile.

Ilias: "Promise me... small things. A picnic on the High Tide Boardwalk when the spring storms hold off. Teach me to patch a floor so I don't scrape my knees on your boat when I'm clumsy with timber."

You: (You picture a picnic blanket flapping at the edge of the boardwalk, thermoses and sandwiches, the town in soft focus. You imagine mending a floor together, mud and laughter and taut rope.) "A picnic. And I'll teach you the stubborn way to nail oak — not because it holds better, but because it remembers."

Ilias: (He tucks a damp strand of hair behind his ear, meeting your gaze) "That's all I need."

[Scene: Inlet — Night of the Storm]
[Visual: Cloud like a physical thing — low and bruised. Streetlamps blink and the sea takes on a metallic sheen. The new berms stand in sober rows, patched much too quickly in places.]
[Music: Low, throbbing bass; sparse percussion like distant heartbeats]
[Sound: The wind, a different voice now — a pressure that seems to breathe; the slap of surf hitting freshly piled sandbags]

You feel it before the radios pick it up: a shifting in the smell, the sea moving with an odd, slow inhalation. Dr. Hana's alert was terse on the channel hours before: "Check seams and anchor points. Unusual low pressure moving up the coast." You thought you had covered the weak joints — volunteers anchored where you could find rope and time. But the funding that could have bought the marine-grade anchors never came; you traded ideals for speed, and speed will always show its seams.

Etta is there at the inlet's edge, shawl whipping like a banner in an ancient gale. Her hands are caked with the same mud as everyone else's, but she moves with a speed that belies age.

Etta: "We hold the line here. Keep the southern plugs weighted. Get that child’s playhouse up the bank."

You: "On it." (You taste metal in your mouth. Your boots sink; the berm yields a little under your weight.)

Ilias: (Shouting over the wind) "I can check the southeast seam — there's a run in the new geotextile!"

You: "Ilias—" (You want to tell him to stay safe. You want to tell him that the seam is the seam — but the tide has a mind now.)

Ilias: "If it tears, the inlet takes the marsh. I'll bolt a line."

You: "You don't have the anchors—"

Ilias: (His voice cuts off as a sound tears the night: fabric and peat giving way, a noise like a giant tearing a page.) "Hold—"

The wind grabs your jacket like an old friend pulling you toward something you both know is dangerous." || Choice B ("Stay to coordinate from the inlet's ridge and direct volunteers") -> Reaction: "You plant your boots and shout instructions, your voice carrying over the storm. Somebody needs to keep eyes on the whole inlet and you are what people look to."]


[Visual: A black seam opens where a child's playhouse had been anchored in summer; the structure is swallowed in a single, furious gulp. Water vaults through like a fist. Two small fisheries along the inlet — places where nets were mended and stories told — lift and tilt and are dragged into the churn. The berm, thin and compromised where budget saved time, parts like bad stitching.]

You: (You hold the photos later — edges blurred with rain, faces half-seen in headlights — and count out the miscalculations. Every anchor you couldn't buy is a place you now know by its absence. You mark those places in your notebook like a geometric confession.)

Ilias: (He comes back soaked to the bone, hands raw where he worked. He smells of salt and something like defeat.) "We plugged where we could. We saved people. But the seam— it opened faster than we expected."

You: "The anchors—"

Ilias: "Would have held longer." (He swallows, looking at the water that just took a shape of your town's memory.) "We can study it. We can catalog and publish and make sure it's not in vain."

You: (Your throat tightens. The word "study" is both a comfort and a knife.) "We already published the plans without conditions. We taught them to listen to the sea. We believed people would be enough."

Ilias: (He looks at you with the quiet, painful squeeze of someone afraid his best won't be enough.) "You did the right thing. The right thing doesn't always get you through a night."

[Scene: Harborfront | Gray Morning — Aftermath]
[Visual: Wet cardboard boxes line the quay. A rented van's back door is open; a woman tucks a framed photograph into a blanket. Boats bob listlessly where they can still float. The sky is the color of old pewter.]
[Music: Sparse piano — each note spaced like slow footfalls]
[Sound: The low, continuous swell of the sea; distant calls; the hum of engines loading up]

You stand on the slick planked harborfront, the coral pendant resting against the bone at the base of your throat. Salt tastes bitter on your tongue. Families move past with the careful economy of people who wrap precious things slowly: heirlooms, a patched sweater, a box of shells. Some faces you know already are hollowed by a small, private courage.

Etta approaches, hands balled behind her back, voice flattened like weathered rope.

Etta: "You held to the town's hand. You told them the truth as it is. People know what they lost because they were told the truth in time to pack something that mattered."

You: "They've lost more than anything our protocol could protect. The anchors—" (The sentence cuts off; there's no way to make the ledger line up.)

Etta: (She studies you like sea glass) "You didn't choose the storm. You chose how we face it. That matters."

You: (You should feel that as balm. Instead a hollow grows under your sternum — for the fisheries, for the child's playhouse, for the families piling belongings into an anonymous van.)

From somewhere down the quay you hear a voice that makes your chest constrict, low and uncertain.

Ilias: (Standing a few yards away, his coat buttoned too high, camera at his side) "I have an offer. A fellowship to quantify what went wrong across three coasts. They want the data, the failures, everything. It's a place to... make more than a report. To scale understanding."

You: (You feel the room tilt. This is not unfamiliar territory — him offering a way out to fix the pattern at a scale you could only dream of. It feels like being handed a map that takes him away.)

Ilias: "I thought I could do both. But the timeline they'd give me would take me from here for a long while. They'll fund a team, equipment. They said they'd support the people we've already helped if I take it."

You: (You try to hold the pain from showing. The town needs him in the slow, patient way it needs hands. But the world outside wants the failures cataloged. You remember the nights of calculating risk, the ledger closing and opening like a mouth.)

Ilias: (He steps closer, and for once both of you are quiet for the time it takes a gull to lift.) "I'm not abandoning what we started. I promise you that. I want to make sure our mistakes teach others — not just here, but where no one else will have them first."

You: "Promise me you'll come back if you can." (The words are small; they are all a person can ask when the horizon feels so full of leaving.)

Ilias: (He hums, a rueful sound.) "I promise. But I can't promise the timeline."

[Visual: He reaches out, fingers brushing the coral pendant at your throat with an almost apologetic reverence — a touch that says more than an argument could. Then he steps back toward the van idling by the quay.]

You: (You count — not spreadsheets this time, but moments. The picnic that didn't happen yet, the unpatched floor, the lab notes in his waterproof sleeve. Each one becomes a kind of ledger all its own.)

Ilias: "Take care of them," he says softly, nodding toward the town.

You: "Keep your hands steady, wherever you go."

Ilias: "I'll try." (He smiles the small, private smile you've loved, then turns away. He walks to the van, lifts his camera bag, and then — without ceremony — leaves you with the harbor's breath.)

Etta: (She stands with you, both of you watching the van's taillights disappear down the quay.) "People go. People come. Some of the things that leave take pieces of us to fix places they've no business mending."

You: (You press your thumb against the carved coral — the coolness anchors you. The pendant had been an heirloom; now it feels like a ledger you carry on your skin.) "We rose. We built what we could with our hands. We were honest with the town. That honesty... it wasn't enough tonight."

Etta: (Her voice is quieter than the sea.) "No. But it will be the story people tell. They'll know they were given a try."

You: (You look at the harbor, at the boats bobbing and the quiet snap of ropes. You hold the photos in your open notebook — the torn playhouse, the empty nets, the faces of people who will leave and those who will stay with a new, harder kind of courage. You catalogue the missteps in margins and blot them with tears you don't let fall.)

[Visual: The sky opens up a fraction and light falls like a benediction on the wet planks, not forgiving, simply witness.]
[Music: One slow note resolves into nothing, leaving the world to exhale]

You stand a long time, the pendant warm now from your touch. The rise had been real. For a while there had been sun in the gutters and green in the trays and a map you could read like truth. The fall is absolute tonight — a seam ripped open at a child's playhouse, fisheries claimed, neighbors loading up boxes and leaving with the last of the tide.

You do not have easy answers. You have only the work you did, the people who still look to you with something like hope, and a notebook full of photographs and plans and places you miscalculated.

You fold the notebook closed, the leather creaking like a hinge that has been opened too often. You tuck it against your ribs, against the place where guilt and care live together.

[Scene fades: Harborfront | Dusk — The town moves; some leave, some stay, and the sea continues with an old indifference]
[Music: Fade to low, empty sustain]
[Sound: The constant, small music of tidal water retracting]

[ENDING]
