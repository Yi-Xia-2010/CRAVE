[Scene: Municipal Hall — Planning Office | Late Morning]
[Visual: Rain streaking down the tall glass; fluorescent lights hum above; corkboards pinned with maps and budget sheets]
[Music: Low, insistent piano; a slow, minor-key swell]
[Sound: Distant traffic, the muffled slap of rain against the facade; a copier whirrs in the corner]

You take the back row because the seats closer to the table feel like they belong to people who already measure the town in risk matrices. From here the fluorescent light washes everyone in the same thin pallor; the room is quieter than the Greenhouse Commons ever was, but it presses in more. Paper smells of toner and old coffee, and every damp coat carries its own seaside salt.

Your tablet sits face-down on your knees, notes folded into a rhythm you've practiced until the edges are soft. You breathe in — humid, metallic air — and remind yourself that you are here with a plan, not just a history.

Ravi sits to your left, fingers interlaced above the table. His eyes are tired the way a lamp gets tired after too many nights. Across the table, council members flip through packets. Someone outside the frame coughs, apologetically loud.

[Visual: Marin enters — slate-gray trench coat buttoned, hair a little loose from the rain; they move with the precision of someone who's measured every entrance]
[Sound: Chair legs scrape as Marin sits; the rain creates a steady, accusing rhythm on the glass behind them]

Marin: "Good morning. Thank you all for the time. Let's keep this tight — we're on a schedule with the regional bond review later."

Their voice is measured, the kind that folds in the room and makes margins smaller. When they turn toward you, pale gray eyes find yours, and you feel the familiar tug: a mix of professional appraisal and something harder to label. Unreadable, complex.

You: "Thank you, Marin. I appreciate everyone's time. I'll keep it brief."

You push the tablet to the center, and the projector sputters to life. A simple slide appears: rooftop gardens as storm buffers; modular, community-owned seawalls with open-source plans. The images are almost embarrassingly earnest — hand-drawn schematics, neighborhood photos annotated with proposed green infrastructure — but they are backed by numbers you can taste in your mouth.

You: "Rooftop gardens reduce runoff at the source, and paired with low-cost, modular seawalls, we get both near-term protection and community control. The designs can be built with local labor and open-sourced so other towns won't have to reinvent what's worked."

Kai is at the table's far edge, sleeves ink-smudged from an overnight prototype. He lifts an index finger, not yet asking to speak but brimming with it.

Kai: "We can demo a module. Small, stackable, made from recycled composites. We did a load test last week—"

Marin: (interrupting, politely) "—and what does an initial deployment cost look like? We need bond-compliant figures, not concept-year optimism."

Ravi: (softly) "We also need to consider maintenance over five years. The state will ask for that."

You feel your throat tighten. The room narrows into an arithmetic of doubt. Numbers are needed — you know that — but you've seen how slow numbers can be when measured against an incoming storm.

You: "I have costed initial deployments and community labor. Maintenance can be a cooperative-managed line item. The social value—"

Council Member A: (cutting in) "Social value doesn't pay invoices, Ms. Alvarez."

The barb lands sideways; something hot and old rises in your chest. You think of the pier festival that used to be the town's heartbeat. You think of Etta's garden beds and Tom's grease-smudged hands. You think of the way the promenade smells in summer. Responsibility is a lens that warps distance — you remember farther things as if they are near.

The choice ebbs, and whichever way you framed it, the next hour becomes a grind. People who are paid to calculate do so; those who are paid to care keep circling back to the same vulnerability: money, but also power.

Marin leans forward when you finish, hands folded. Their tone is low and deliberate.

Marin: "Iris — you propose a local-first model, which I respect. My job is to make that practical within the bond's legal framework. I can ask for a pilot clause, but there will have to be oversight and assurances the regional board can accept."

You: "Oversight we can plan for. Transparency and community governance are part of the model."

Marin: (after a moment) "Good. I'll work with Ravi on the language. But know that the bond's priorities are brittle; lawyers will tighten them. We must have contingencies."

Ravi: (rubbing his temple) "We can draft oversight terms, but we might need a public-private component to placate the bond holders."

The words 'public-private' are a bruise forming. One of the councilors — someone with a developer contact in their Rolodex — clears their throat.

Council Member B: "There is also an alternative on the table. A developer has proposed a 'protect-and-privatize' plan: raise the promenade, retrofit the shore, and create commercial leases along the boardwalk. It funds itself and gets insurance companies off our back."

Your chest tightens like an animal catching scent of fire. Protect-and-privatize. Build walls, then let the wallet determine who walks behind them.

You: "That would put public access at risk. It would change the character of the promenade and concentrate control."

Council Member B: (sourly) "It would save properties and generate revenue. It solves the ledger."

Marin watches you. Their face is a sea chart folded into weathered lines; you can’t read which way the wind will turn. Your knee bounces under the table. You feel the old tendency — to shoulder, to fix, to become the loudest voice until someone else has permission to follow.

You: "We don't have to monetize our home to make it safe. Community-run solutions keep culture and safety aligned."

A murmur moves around the room — sympathy, skepticism, the grinding realism of budgets. Ravi looks at the ceiling as if numbers might stick there.

Ravi: "If we push only community funding, we might lose the chance to shore up essential infrastructure quickly. There's a balance."

Marin: (quiet, then) "Balance is what we need. But balance doesn't mean giving the promenade away."

The discussion begins to fold into committees and subcommittees, the language of deferral. That word — defer — is a small, cold thing in your throat. You want to reach across the table and show them the pier at dawn, the way the boards sing under foot. Instead you outline a pilot schedule and a phased budget, less bright than you'd hoped but solid enough to put in front of a bond manager.

Kai taps his notebook, restless energy shifting into action.

Kai: (leaning toward you) "If we can get just one roof or one seawall funded, I can run a live demo. People understand a thing that moves and resists. They relate to that."

You catch his eye; his optimism is a thin, flammable thing tonight.

You: "We need that. A visual, tactile example makes the abstract tangible."

Marin: (looking between you and Kai) "Fine. A pilot. Legal language to be drafted by Ravi and my office. But if bond counsel rejects it, we proceed with alternative measures, which may include private partnerships."

There's an edge to that last clause that tastes like basalt. You don't like it, but you nod because Marin's "if" is not a promise. It is a conditional: life will offer you alternatives, some of them ugly.

[Visual: The meeting disbands; chairs scrape. Rain sounds louder on the façade, as if the weather itself is listening harder now]
[Sound: Thunder low and patient, a distant siren beginning to thread through the city noise]

You gather your papers, all the while noticing the room's smell — ozone and the faint metallic tang of policy. People filter out in polite clusters. A few offer quiet words of encouragement; others avoid your eyes.

[Scene: Municipal Hall — Rear Stairwell | Midday]
[Visual: Narrow stairwell lit by strip lights; rainwater streaks down the stairwell window and blurs the harbor]
[Sound: Rain heavier now; the muffled rumble of a city swelling with storm]

You descend into the damp outside, and the cold air hits like a rebuke. The municipal steps lead into an alley that smells of wet wood and gasoline—a short cut you and Kai have used a hundred times. He catches up to you before you can reach the corner, his beanie pulled low, hair damp.

Kai: "Hey. That was—" (he exhales) "—hard. You handled it well."

You: "We kept a pilot in. That's something."

Kai: (paces a small circle) "Something is fragile, though. The developer pitch landed. People like a one-stop number for 'security.' We need something visceral. I can bring the module tonight. I know it's late, but—"

You: "Tonight? With the storm coming?"

Kai: (rushes) "It's modular. It's small. If we put it in the hall for a few hours, people will see the mechanics. They'll touch it. I'll explain load tests. Visual beats spreadsheet."

You feel the pressure of his optimism pressed against the damp night. It's contagious but also reckless in its way.

[Scene: Damp Alley Behind the Promenade Shops | Early Evening]
[Visual: Narrow, cobbled; tarps flapping, lighted shop windows like warm beacons; rain threads down the alley]
[Sound: The storm is a living thing now — gusts, the slap of rain on tarps, a far-off car alarm wailing intermittently]

You and Kai work quickly in the alley for an hour — bolts, straps, a compact demonstration module that smells faintly of resin and sea-spray. Passersby slow, frowning at the weather and then at a thing that doesn't belong. Kai talks in bursts; you hand him tools and steady pieces. Your gloves are damp, your hair clings to your forehead. The demonstration, small and honest, moves like a promise.

Kai: (checking a bolt) "If we just get it in front of people—one councilor, one property owner—"

You: "Then they'll have to reckon with the possibility of a community alternative."

His fingers are quick, precise. The storm pushes harder; a gust snaps a tarp, and for a second your heart jumps like a trapped gull.

[Sound: A siren crescendos close now; voices shout; the wind changes direction]
[Visual: Lights flicker down the alley; a cold flash — a window somewhere up the street shatters in a bright, startling noise]

Kai freezes, face drained of color. "Did you hear that?"

You: "Yeah."

You both run toward the promenade, boots slapping the slick stones. Sirens converge. A municipal alert buzzes on your phone: Severe storm warning upgraded. Then another ping: emergency maintenance report incoming.

[Scene: Promenade & Pier | Night — Storm]
[Visual: Wind lashing lightposts; the pier now a dark, serrated silhouette as waves climb; lanterns snuffed and gutters throwing sheets of water]
[Music: Tense, low strings; a single cello holding a sorrowful note]
[Sound: Crashing waves, the splintering of wood, urgent sirens]

You stand at the edge where the promenade meets the water. Rain cuts into you; your jacket is soaked through. Emergency crews move with headlamps, voices clipped and efficient. You can't see the pier clearly — just the suggestion of boards breaking off like old teeth.

A maintenance worker's shouted update reaches you across the wind.

Maintenance Worker: "Lower promenade—critical structural failure at the east approach. Pier supports compromised. We're cordoning the area. Send damage report."

Your chest collapses with the words. Not could be, is. Not hypothetical.

Ravi finds you, rain beading on his collar, eyes wide in a way that has nothing to do with sleep deprivation.

Ravi: "We have preliminary damage reports. Critical sections of the pier and the lower promenade are compromised. Insurance will call this an act-of-God window; funding decisions get complicated if the narrative becomes reactive."

You: (voice small) "How long until a full assessment?"

Ravi: "Teams are out. But right now? We have to assume longer repairs, higher costs. If we wanted to push the pilot, we'll be talking about triage priorities."

Marin arrives, coat plastered to their frame, hair damp. They look at the scene and then at you with the same unreadable expression as before — a mix of calculation and something that might be worry.

Marin: "This will change the conversation at the bond meeting. Emergency damage influences bond counsel. We need a consolidated response — immediate safety, then a prioritized plan. That plan could include the pilot, but it will be weighed against emergency restoration."

You want to say: 'We told you so' and 'This is exactly why we tried to act before it worsened.' Both thoughts rise and choke each other. The falling tone of the world is not a single event but a sequence: warnings ignored, measures delayed, and then the weather turning decisive.

Kai stands beside you, rain dripping from his beanie, eyes rimmed red with the sting of the wind.

Kai: "We can still show them the module. Even in damage control, a demonstration could steer the narrative toward community resilience instead of private contracts."

You: "If the town is in emergency mode, people will vote for quick fixes. The privatize argument is stronger when fear narrows choices."

Marin listens, jaw working.

Marin: "Then we fight the narrative. We must frame repairs as an opportunity for shared stewardship. But that requires fast coordination and evidence the pilot reduces future risk. We need storm-condition load tests, insurance statements, and a legal pathway that doesn't get legally tied up."

The list is long. The timelines are short. Your palms are numb from the cold; your throat tastes of salt and iron.

You think of the pier, the festival, Tom's shop, Etta's garden. You think of the way the town's stories are spread along the boards and benches; some of those stories are already splintering.

[Visual: The camera pulls back, rain blurring the edges of the pier and promenade into a loose watercolor of loss]
[Music: A single cello note hangs, unresolved]

You open your phone to check messages, to find a dozen new pings — council channels lighting up, concerned neighbors, an image of a splintered board from a local's upload. The town wakes to this new fact.

A damage report is transmitted to the council inbox: "CRITICAL — Pier East Approach & Lower Promenade: Structural compromise. Immediate action required. Estimated timeline pending full assessment." The words appear flat and brutal on the screen.

Your options narrow into a funnel. There are technical responses, political maneuvers, and human costs. There is no single right answer, only a sequence of trade-offs made under pressure.

You breathe in the storm, let it fill your lungs with cold, and feel an ache that is equal parts grief and fierce resolve.
