[Visual: A long raised wooden walkway threaded above tidal channels; salt-smeared glass panels of the lab reflect a pale, shifting sky.]
[Sound: A low mechanical hum grows in the distance, interlaced with gull calls and the slap of water against pilings.]
[Music: Warm, hopeful strings underscoring steady forward motion]

You follow the hum like following a remembered tide. The boards underfoot creak with the damp patience of old wood; your boots leave dark imprints that dry almost as soon as they're made. Salt prickles at your throat and the locket at your collar warms against your skin. The air tastes of wire and wet stone—ozone threaded with kelp and recently turned soil from the Skyfarm terraces below.

As you approach Saltglass, the building resolves: glass panes streaked with salt, condensation freckling the corners, and inside—green strips of moss and small, stubborn pots with seedlings. The lab feels alive in the way a conservatory does, clinical light softened by living things. It is both a hospital for ideas and a greenhouse for hope.

[Visual: Cut to interior through a large salt-streaked window]
[Sound: The hum sharpens; a finger-tap cadence on a compact tablet is audible, a soft metallic click every beat.]
[Music: A subtle swell—anticipatory, kind]

Elias is there before you. He sits at a bench by the main window, shoulders relaxed in that slate-blue jacket you know so well, hair falling into his forehead as if gravity were another variable to be engineered. The tablet is strapped to his forearm; his fingers move with the kind of ease built from repetition and faith. Light catches the pale irises of his eyes and throws a gold fleck like a promise.

He looks up as you step through the door and, for a moment, the rest of the lab recedes—the humid panels, the moss strips, Serena's clipped posture at a rear workstation, even the low, steady whirr that drew you here.

Elias: "You're early," (he says, a smile that is both apology and invitation) "or you're right on time. Depends how you look at it."

You: "You sound like Ibe when he says 'on time' for lunch." (You let the remark land, letting the banality anchor the hum in human rhythm.)

Elias: (laughs, a quick loosen of his shoulders) "Then consider this lunch for the future. Sit. I want to show you—" He taps the tablet. The display blooms in the air above it, a holographic lattice of channels and nodes that looks like a living map.

[Visual: Holographic TideGrid simulation unfurls—modular turbines shimmer at strategic inlets; animated flow lines show redirected currents; sediment clouds shimmer where controlled redistribution occurs.]
[Sound: Soft clicking HUD sounds, simulated water gurgling]
[Music: An uplifting chord progression]

He narrates like someone showing you the parts of a beloved instrument: precise, tender, almost reverent. The TideGrid animates: turbines rotate in sequences, sluices bloom open, sediment paths thicken and thin. Where water pressure peaks, ghosted barriers lift. Where neighborhoods dip, modular platforms offer staggered relief.

Elias: "Modularity is the key. We don't flip the city with a single lever—we stitch in pieces. Local operators keep authority over deployment windows. The simulation runs with thousands of permutations, and the failure modes? Fewer than what we face now. Fewer lives lost."

You watch the simulation more than you watch his face. The idea has scale; scale feels like rescue. It is hard not to be taken by the beauty of something engineered to hold the city like a palm.

Serena stands a few feet away, arms folded, wristband flashing a diagnostic pulse. Her skepticism is clinical but not unkind.

Serena: "The control loops introduce feedback lags. Tidal resonance in narrow channels could amplify instead of mitigate if we misjudge phase. I want redundancy layers—local fail-safes independent of networked commands."

Elias: "Which is in the model. We add decentralized nodes—community-run control centers. That's why I brought you here, Maia. We can build both: the system and the local oversight."

Your municipal badge feels heavy against your chest. The weight isn't just brass and thread—it's memory: Old Man Toma's slow cough, Ibe's laugh at a splintered plank, the heat in the Skyfarm kitchen on nights when the generator hiccups. The lab's clean air tastes both of possibility and of too-small, sterile solutions that ignore the stubborn, human mess of neighborhoods.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' appears in the doorway as if the building exhaled him out. Sawdust still clings to the cuff of his apron; his hands hover halfway between a half-built railing and clasping his own mug.

Ibe: (grinning) "I came for coffee, but now there's promising scaffolding and you two look like adjectives. Explain it to me without the fancy pictures."

Elias: "Think of it as a set of coordinated maneuvers. We don't take everything—only what needs to be nudged. Turbines here to pull sediment so the canals stop swallowing the promenade; small, incremental rises at strategic points, driven by predictable physics."

You: (trying to translate the simulation into home) "And the neighborhoods? The kitchens on Skyfarm, Toma's house on Dock Six—will they still —"

Elias: (reaches for your wrist gently, an anchor) "They'll be safer. That's the point. Maia, I'm asking you not because I want to override you, but because I need the people who will live with this to help shape it."

There is a pulse between his words and the touch of his hand—the kind that asks for trust without demanding it. Your instinct bristles: community autonomy, place-memory, the slow growth of resilience that sews people together. But beneath the bristle is a thread of something else—could this scale of thinking actually protect those tender knots of neighborhood life?

Serena: (interjecting, eyes on the hologram) "Even with community centers, centralized funding and maintenance create dependency cycles. Political capital will concentrate—"

Elias: (cutting in, softer) "Which is why governance is part of the design. Open-source controls, civic councils with veto power, transparent maintenance logs. We build accountability into the system."

You watch the exchange and see both of them bending toward compromise like two poles learning to hold a new tent. It doesn't erase your caution, but it reshapes it into curiosity. The hum that brought you here no longer feels distant; it hums like a living thing that might be taught to listen.

The small decision to lean or to stand guard yields immediate, soft consequences: whether you invite Elias's optimism into scrutiny directly, or whether you give Serena the floor to sharpen the design. Either way, the room fills with the sound of problem-solving—an orchestra of practicality.

Elias notices the way you hesitate. He doesn't demand an answer; instead he begins to explain a different slice of the plan—something quieter: sediment redistribution as an act of repair rather than conquest. He draws lines where rivers once braided; he traces the long patience of sediment building and shows how, over seasons, redistributed silt could raise foundations under the promenade.

Elias: "It's not just about holding water back. It's about giving the city back its substrate—soil to plant roots into, terraces that won't collapse under people's weight. Think of the terraces you make on Skyfarm, Maia. Imagine them anchored in substrate that isn't guaranteed to slide away next season."

You: "It sounds like—" (you search for a word that sits between machinery and memory) "—a scaffold for life."

Elias: (smiles) "Exactly."

Serena: (a half-smile) "If the models hold. Which means we need pilots. Small, monitored sections where community groups actually run the nodes."

Ibe: "You mean our teams? I can get a crew. We'll test bolts, brackets, and if someone tries to cut a corner, I'll know which one of them to glare at until they do it properly."

The lab laughs—light, human, slightly relieved. Laughter cracks open the tension you arrived with and lets warmth pour in. The hum becomes background to voices and footsteps, a machine learning to be companioned by human habit.

You find your hands unclenching without thinking. The hard line between 'top-down' and 'local' eases into a seam you could stitch. You imagine the Skyfarm kitchens safe from sudden swallowings, Old Man Toma's steps steady on a raised threshold, Ibe's hands steady on hammer and wood. The vision does not erase the risks; it simply sets them into a plan that includes people at every stage.

Elias reaches for your hand again, slower this time, as if to test the temperature of your willingness rather than to claim it.

Elias: "We don't have to do this alone. I want you in—help design the pilot governance. Your voice gives it meaning. Your networks give it legitimacy."

You feel the tug between the ethic that has driven you—slow, participatory, stubborn—and the momentum of a new possibility that could preserve those very things on a larger scale. Hope rises in you not as certainty but as a working hypothesis: what if scale could shelter the small, not erase it?

The lab waits for your answer in the soft way that trusted spaces do—they know decisions are made slowly. Outside, light breaks through a cloud and the moss on the lab's inner walls glows a brighter green for a moment. The warmth is literal and metaphorical; it presses at your face and inflates the possibility in your chest.

You think of the long work ahead: piloting nodes, drafting governance charters, convincing skeptical elders, training crews, weathering failures and celebrating small survivals. It feels suddenly vast and suddenly possible.

Elias watches you with a careful optimism that doesn't presume. Serena prepares a printed briefing, her movements efficient and steady. Ibe rubs sawdust between his palms like he is already starting to measure what will be needed.

You are at the edge of a decision that could tilt your whole project one direction or another. Standing there, your locket warm against your sternum, you feel both the pull of systems and the gravity of neighborhoods. The hum that pulled you here has shifted—it is not a threat or a promise, it's an invitation.

You breathe in, tasting brine and chlorophyll and the metallic bright of new plans.

You reach for your multi-tool bracelet, fingers worrying the familiar cold metal, and the lab's conversation threads around you like hands ready to help lift something heavy.

You can feel the moment when a choice could be made. The room narrows to the space between your palm and Elias's. Outside, the city holds its breath with you.
