# Route: Route A — Alternate Ending


[Scene: Saltglass Research Lab — Pilot Bay | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the pilot bay — a vaulted space of glass and metal perched over a tidal channel. A compact TideGrid mock-up sits on a waist-high plinth: articulated turbines, miniature sediment sluices, and tiny terraces linked by threaded cables. Salt-streaked panes throw a wavering, golden light across wet concrete and moss panels.]
[Music: Low, buoyant synths swell into a hopeful string motif]
[Sound: Soft mechanical whirring, distant gull calls, the gentle slap of water against pilings]

Narration
You walk in with Elias at your elbow, his tablet a warm rectangle of blue light against the slate of his jacket, his hair still wind-tousled from the morning demonstration. The air smells faintly of ozone and wet metal, threaded with the green, earthy scent of the living moss panels someone installed to cut the lab's carbon draw. There is a brightness here that feels almost like sunlight concentrated into a machine — not harsh, but promising.

Elias: "We got the final specs through last night. Serena pushed the diagnostics into production. I… we couldn't have done this without the community models."

Narration
He says 'we' like it includes you, and the word lands as both an entitlement and a benediction. Your municipal badge hangs loose at your chest — you unclipped it this morning on purpose, leaving it in your pocket during the announcement. It felt strange to step into a space of other people's money and machinery with your badge unclipped: a small refusal and an honest way to keep your voice from being swallowed by logos. The badge is warm from your body; when you rub your thumb over the municipal seal, you taste the metal of responsibility under your tongue.

Elias (quiet, hopeful): "Come look."

[Visual: Close-up on the mock-up as Elias gestures; the miniature turbines rotate lazily as a controlled current passes through the model channel]
[Sound: The model's micro-motors click with tidy precision]

Narration
You step closer. The turbines are clever and delicate — thin blades that bite the current, adjustable sluice gates, and little terraces that could, if scaled, let neighborhoods set their own waterlines. It's both engineering and choreography, a physical poem about flow and access. Your chest opens a little at the sight. There is an almost childlike joy in watching an idea spin into motion.

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (grinning, hands dusted with varnish) "We retrofitted the anchor points this morning. They're lighter now—easier for the market crews to haul. If this pilot goes, the platforms won't sink like the old rigs."

Narration
Ibe's laugh fills the bay — solid as timber. The smell of sawdust and salted varnish follows him. He hands you a small, rough-hewn cleat he'd shaped himself, its edges sanded by a thousand small, necessary touches. You take it: the wood is warm and slightly tacky from a recent oiling. You close your fingers around it like a promise.

Elias: (handing you his tablet) "Serena ran the stress sims overnight. You'll like the transparency layer—community inputs, real-time fail-safes. We promised oversight. We put your clauses right into the contract language."

Serena Qiu: (leaning in, professional but softer than usual) "Maia — if you want, I'll show you the diagnostic strings. We built public hooks for civic review. It isn't perfect, but it's auditable. If we peer together, we patch faster."

Narration
Serena's voice is clipped, technical. But when she offers you the tablet, there's an unease smoothed by a small, trustful look — the kind that says she's willing to let you carry the ethical ballast with her. You remember nights in the terrace porches, mapping community data with a lantern and a hurried handwriting. There is more at stake than simulation scores; there are names scribbled in margins, elders' memories, children's routes to school. Serena knows that, and for a breath she lets formality fall.

Narration
You move through the room like someone who has learned to do many things at once: listening to engineers, answering the quick, human questions from neighborhood volunteers, keeping one eye on the contract language printed on glossy sheets at the edge of the table. Rin's emissaries hover nearby in tailored coats, neutral faces folded into polite engagement. They offer quiet murmurings about funding tranches and deployment timelines; their voices are smooth and efficient, like a tide coming in on a well-ordered schedule.

Rin's Emissary #1: (formal) "We can accelerate the pilot funding by thirty percent if we clear the permitting window next week. TideGrid needs momentum; political winds are fragile."

Narration
You notice how their words are all cadence and no reckoning. But the money will buy more iterations, more community sensors, better materials for anchor points that won't strip neighborhoods of labor or agency. You think of Toma's stoop, of the mosaic of bowls on his table, of the stories he keeps like salt in the seams of his fingers.

Old Man Toma: (pressing a weathered palm to your locket) "May the tide learn our names. May it carry our stories as well as it carries the grain. Let them not forget the songs."

Narration
He murmurs the blessing and for a moment you are nine again, watching tides suck pebbles in and out, learning which shells the water would choose to carry away. His fingers are knuckled and steady against the copper. The locket warms under his touch — a private, impossible closeness that translates into a public commitment. Your throat tightens with hope and something like a quiet grief that is finally being answered with action.

Elias: (squeezing your hand) "We can do this together. Not just the machine, but the way people stay in charge of their water. I want you at the center of the oversight board. I want—" (he hesitates, voice roughening) "—your voice in every deployment panel."

Narration
His thumb rubs the back of your hand the way it has when plans go well and worse; the motion is both comfort and an argument. You see in his narrow, squinting eyes a future where neighborhoods sleep easier. You also see the engineer's impatience: an insistence that motion, any motion, can save lives now. That insistence is why you stood with him today at the announcement, why your unclipped badge felt like a bridging knot between civic duty and technological hope.

Serena: (more forceful) "Maia—I've run the failure cases. There are edge scenarios where community controls prevent a cascade. We built interlocks. But they only work if people have the tools and skills to use them. We'll need training, a bug-bounty for reporting edge cases, and continuous funding for maintenance."

Ibe: (grinning) "We can teach crews. The market crews are hungry for work that pays. We'll make it a neighborhood industry. Keep the money local."

Narration
You feel the plan knitting itself into being: engineering, community labor, legal scaffolding, and civic transparency. Each thread is imperfect, but when woven together they make a net. Momentum tastes like a bright salt wind — sharp and clean — and sunlight splinters across the wet floor in prismatic promises.

The syllabus becomes a living thing by the end of the hour." || Choice B ("Insist on a public town-hall before finalizing clauses") -> Reaction: "You raise your voice and call for a public town-hall. There's a small, appreciative murmur. Elias nods reluctantly; Serena taps a note into her tablet. The plan becomes more cautious, but also more anchored to consent."] 

Narration
You spend the next hour splitting your attention between legal phrasing and the smell of oil and moss, between the clinical glow of Serena's diagnostic overlays and Ibe's practical jokes about rope knots. Conversations pour into one another: technical terms folded into metaphors for neighborhood rituals; dry contractual paragraphs softened by Toma's stories of the old docks; a corporate emissary's neutral cadence interrupted by a volunteer's blunt question. Dialogue here is a pattern — push-and-pull of hearts and spreadsheets.

Rin's Emissary #2: (softening, unexpectedly honest) "We want to ensure no one else loses their home. If this pilot proves scalable, many lives are safer. We're willing to fund local labor programs."

Narration
The words hang, earnest and weapon-like in their simplicity. You think of the terraces you helped plant, of the children who learned to salt-proof seedlings, of neighbors who braided ropes for buoyancy. The funding could strengthen those threads instead of cutting them — if the contracts keep their promises.

Elias: (quietly) "If you want me to, I'll help shepherd the oversight panel. I can’t pretend I don't want this to scale fast. But I also… I want it right."

Narration
He looks at you with a kind of open, pleading optimism. You notice the furrow at his brow — the same furrow you see whenever simulation constraints force uncomfortable arithmetic. For an instant you allow yourself to imagine rescue as a clear, uncomplicated thing: turbines humming, rooftops safe, Ibe's smile wide. You close your eyes and let that image settle — wind on copper, the lab's hum stitched to the neighborhood's laughter.

Old Man Toma: (softly, with a conspiratorial wink) "Promise me one thing. Teach them the songs. Teach them how to speak to the sea, even if it's with a chart and a sensor. The sea remembers those who remember it."

Narration
Those words make everyone laugh, and the laughter is wet and honest and from the throat. The pilot date is set on the whiteboard — a neat, blue marker slash across a calendar square. Someone snaps a photograph; elsewhere, a volunteer starts marking maps with the neighborhoods chosen for the initial rollout.

The bay beyond is a smudge of gold and grey, and the real tide moves in slow, patient rhythms. Your lungs fill with salt and sunlight; for a moment the hope that built in the room feels like a steady thing." || Choice B ("Stay at the table and help redline the consent clauses") -> Reaction: "You sit back down and lean over the printed drafts, red pen in hand. Narration
Momentum hums through the room like an electrical charge. You draft consent frameworks and oversight clauses with a care that feels both bureaucratic and tender. Each clause reads like a pledge: transparency obligations, a community veto for deployment in sensitive zones, commitments to local hiring, public bug-bounties, and requirements for open-source telemetry. Legalese becomes a tool of care.

Serena: (satisfied, a small smile breaking through) "That's the kind of guardrail we needed. Auditable, public, and enforceable. We'll run the first rollout in three weeks, with the community crews trained and the sensors live."

Ibe: (clapping you on the shoulder) "Three weeks. We'll have boats, ropes, and enough coffee to fuel half the island. You'll see. The terraces will be singing."

Narration
The lab hums like a thing coming to life. Rin's emissaries move toward the doorway, their faces composed but their steps quick with purpose. They offer one last careful congratulations and a formal handshake — an action that tastes of both alliance and negotiation.

Rin's Emissary #1: (offering a smile that is both corporate and oddly warm) "We look forward to seeing the pilot succeed."

Narration
You accept the handshake. The grip is firm and leaves an impression that echoes the moment's gravity. You feel the world tilt toward possibility: community knowledge stitched into a machine that could scale, funding that could provide decent wages and better materials, safeguards that might keep agency where it belongs. For the first time in a while, possibility feels like a rope you can hold and use.

Elias: (soft, almost to himself) "Thank you. For saying yes."

Narration
You turn to him. The lab's light pools over his face, catching the gold flecks in his eyes. He looks tired and ferocious and utterly, impossibly hopeful. You squeeze his hand back, steadying both of you in the middle of this new tide.

Narration
Outside, a gull argues with the wind. Inside the bay, community members laugh as they tally training modules and volunteers. Old Man Toma hums an old harbor tune into the room. Momentum tastes of salt and sunlight and the kind of weary joy that follows long, necessary work.

[Page-Turn Moment]
You step back from the mock-up, the whiteboard, the tablet, and the people whose lives you have pledged to protect. The pilot date is written in sharp blue ink; the clauses sit in your palm like knots ready to be tightened. For a moment you close your eyes and imagine a future with fewer midnight rescues, with terraces that hold dinner tables instead of tombstones, with a city that learns to steer both tide and policy together. It feels fragile and real — a thing built of hands and signatures and songs.

You unclench your fist, feeling the outline of the cleat Ibe gave you. The lab's hum threads into your bones like an insistence: move forward, tend the stitch, keep the people at the center. Outside the glass, the channeled water glints with the same gold that caught the mock-up's turbines. The future is not finished, but it has begun to take shape.
