# Route: Route C — Alternate Ending
# Context: Start

[Scene: Marenport Flooded Promenade | Morning]
[Visual: Pale gold light breaks through lifting fog over a shallow, glassy channel; broken string lights reflected in tidal pools]
[Music: Soft, hopeful cello with distant percussion]
[Sound: Low mechanical hum of pumps, gull calls, the metallic tap of shutters closing and opening]

You stand ankle-deep on the Flooded Promenade. Cold salt laps at the seams of your waterproof boots; the water tastes of iron and old stories when it beads at your lips as a fine spray. The sky above is the color of pewter glass, but where the fog parts a warm thread of sunlight slips down and gilds the edges of the boardwalk. Your dark chestnut hair is loose at the nape of your neck; the frayed blue ribbon knots into the hollow at your collarbone and presses there like a memory you can feel.

The municipal badge clipped to your field jacket rubs at your ribs when you breathe. It is light — a stamped rectangle of authority — and yet the weight you carry today feels heavier than any brass token. You tighten your fingers around the multi-tool bracelet at your wrist, feeling the cold metal bite through fabric and skin. The bracelet makes a small, reassuring clink. Practical things steady you. People do not.

[Visual: Old Man Toma steps forward from a patch of woven netting, his patchwork cloak heavy with morning damp; he unfolds a brittle map]
[Sound: Cloth rustle, a soft inhalation from you]

Old Man Toma: "Here."
(He spreads the map on a slat of dry wood held above the water.) "This was Canal Twelve before the tide took the market. Look how the street used to run—straight as a promise."

You lean in. The map smells of salt and tobacco and a faint tang of seaweed — the scent of things kept in pockets for too long. Stitches along the cloth trace faded street names. Isles that once hummed with carts and bicycles are now lines of floating stalls and rope bridges. Toma presses a thumb to a corner where an old pier used to jut out; his thumb is calloused, trembles only a little.

Maia Soler: "You remember it like this, Toma?"

Old Man Toma: "I remember the night lanterns. I remember your mother carrying a fishmonger’s boy on her back, laughing like we had all the days in the world."
(He chuckles, but there is a fine pain beneath it.) "Maps are stubborn. They keep trying to tell you what belonged where."

Maia Soler: "We keep trying to put things back where they fit," you say, voice soft. "Not the exact places — that would be foolish — but the feeling of them. The markets, the terraces. People who know their neighbors."

Old Man Toma: (His eyes, bright as driftglass.) "Good. Then keep them close. People forget faster than the sea forgets a scar. You—"
(he taps the little copper locket at your throat through your jacket) "—you keep the right ones close."

The copper locket hangs warm against your sternum, a tiny photograph curled like a held-back tide. You think of the coastline it contains: a street now dissolved into water, a single lamppost leaning like a sentinel. The image is small, impossible to map onto the present, and yet you carry it as if it were a map that matters.

[Visual: Ibe appears at the end of the boardwalk carrying a length of salvaged timber over one shoulder; flakes of old paint cling to his apron]
[Sound: Heavy, friendly footsteps on wet wood; a plank creaks]

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (Grinning, breath steaming) "You'd better have tea if you expect me to work under your optimistic plans today. My hands still smell like resin from last night."

Maia Soler: "Tea, dhowsong, and a lecture. Your usual wage."

Ibrahim 'Ibe': "Ah — and your charming leadership, of course. Don't tell me you've been letting Toma soften you with stories of pre-flood pastries."

You laugh, and it comes out bright, surprising even yourself. The laugh unfurls something in your chest that feels like relief. Ibe sets the timber down with practiced care, splinters catching the light.

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (More serious, tapping the timber with a forefinger) "So which pilings do you want to try? The northern string's half-rotted, but the midline has those old iron clamps—I can rig them. Or we go for the floating garden frames; those planks we salvaged last month will do nicely."

Maia Soler: (Counting, imagining) "Midline clamps sound good. They hold weight. But the floating garden frames — we plant hedgerows there first. Salt-tolerant hedges will help slow water and give people places to gather."

Ibrahim 'Ibe': "And where will you be planting the stubborn souls who still believe in terrazzo staircases?"

Maia Soler: "Right where people want them. On roofs, on floats, on the tilted stoop of that bakery that insists on a single stubborn oven."
(You tap the multi-tool as you say it.) "We'll anchor memory into soil."

Ibe grins, then his face softens in a way that makes your heart lurch cleanly — half-joy, half-worry. He always knows when to stab the mood with a joke and when to carry the load beside you.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Examine the map's pier markings"
> Choice B: "Ask Toma about the stories behind the map"
[Outcome A: "You trace the faded ink with your fingertip, feeling the cloth's rough threads. Each notation — an 'X' where a market stall once stood, a dot for a communal oven — becomes a small responsibility. You imagine where to put pilings so a floating terrace can anchor without disturbing the remnant eelgrass beds. The plan in your head grows roots: scaffold, clamps, young hedgerow saplings tied into burlap. You feel steady."]
[Outcome B: "You tilt your head and let Toma's memories lead. He tells you of lantern-light and a night a boy chased a stray dog into the market, of a storm that came soft and then enraged. Each story outlines not only places but reasons those places mattered. You folder the anecdotes into your argument for community terraces: people will tend what they love."]
[MERGE: Old Man Toma watches you choose, his eyes unreadable for a breath, then warming with approval. Whatever map or story you settle on, he gestures toward Canal Twelve, where nets and ropes make a patchwork of solutions.]

Old Man Toma: "Whatever you do, keep them where they can be found. Make places so no one has to ask to belong."

Maia Soler: "We will."

[Visual: A soft breeze ruffles the map; sunlight threads through and makes salt crystals on the boardwalk glitter]
[Sound: A distant, rhythmic pumping becomes a tiny drumbeat in your chest]

You feel the city's hum: the steady thrum of pumps, a metallic click as a lab shutter opens in the distance like a blink, someone's violin practicing across the water, a dog barking twice and then silence. These are the scaffolding of daily life here — not grand designs, but persistent noises that say people are still present and still trying.

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (Pointing toward a cluster of half-sunken pilings) "If we salvage the second and the fourth from that cluster, we can anchor two new frames and give the gardeners room to plant in the spring cycle. You'll want the heavier clamps—I can forge a brace by midday."

Maia Soler: "Good. I'll fetch the seedlings and talk to the Resilience Collective about the permit for raised beds. They'll like the idea of living terraces that double as storm buffers."

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (Wry) "The Collective will say 'borrowing space' and then give you seven forms to fill out. But they'll also bring the pump technicians if it looks like a good plan. They always like plans that breathe."

You think of the Marenport Resilience Collective — a ragged orchestra of civil engineers, patchworkers, community organizers — drafting a new set of floating terraces and rain gardens for Canal Twelve. Their blueprints float like promises between municipal offices and the sea. That mix of weary hope and responsibility that sits like a stone in your gut swells toward something like conviction. You can feel the edges of a day lined with work and small victories: a salvaged piling here, a hedgerow there, neighbors bringing seeds and cups of tea.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Sketch a quick layout on Toma's map"
> Choice B: "Call the Resilience Collective from your comm-badge"
[Outcome A: "You take a stub of charcoal and sketch where pilings might sit, where the terrace reaches will kiss the water. Your lines are practical, tidy. Toma nods and murmurs his approval, pointing to a spot to leave an access ramp for old feet and for the market carts that might come back. The plan is rough and will change, but the act of drawing makes it real."]
[Outcome B: "You lift your comm-badge and press the coil; the badge's faint hum answers, and you leave a brief message with the collective coordinator. They respond with a time and a promise to send two volunteers. The coordination eases a pressure behind your ribs — the city will show up if you ask it to."]
[MERGE: Ibe watches you make the call, eyes crinkling in that warm, private way people use when they watch someone do the right thing. The sky grows kinder; sunlit dust motes drift between planks like a scattering of permission. You feel lighter.]

You and Ibe fall into a rhythm: measuring, marking, lifting. Your palms grow warm; the scent of timber and salt stitches into the fabric of the morning. Toma stays a little while longer, sharing names of old shopkeepers and the exact place where a baker once left boiled fish for the children. When he leaves, he ties a small strip of his cloak to a post — a talisman, a map-stitched promise — and waves without looking back.

[Visual: Close-up of your hands fastening a clamp; water reflects the movement like fragmented glass]
[Sound: The soft chorus of neighbors moving about, someone hammering in the near distance]

You promise yourself — out loud, because promises spoken to the boardwalk feel truer — that you will keep this neighborhood together. You promise Old Man Toma his lantern-light, and you promise the kids who still play on broken steps that there will be space to grow. Your breath goes slow with that promise. It grounds you.

A small thing — the metallic tap of a lab shutter — catches at the edge of your day, a reminder that not everyone here trusts only ropes and hands. The Resilience Collective drafts ideas while labs test possibilities. The city hum holds both: the workshop's clamor and the lab's quiet beep. Both are needed, you tell yourself. Both will be coaxed into conversation by people who care enough to listen.

You tighten the multi-tool bracelet once more. The clamp catches; the plank settles. Beneath the surface, water swallows shadow and reflects the gilded edges of what you are building. Hope comes as work, not as a sudden miracle, and the work begins with one salvaged piling at a time.

[Visual: The boardwalk extends toward Canal Twelve; floating stalls bob gently. Light is warmer now, promises gilded in the surf.]
[Music: A gentle swell of strings, affirming]

You look out over the water, map folded in your hands, and imagine the terrace — a place where people meet, where seedlings root into borrowed soil, where stories get told again. Your chest unclenches in a small, fierce way.

A gull cries, and you feel like answering it with the only thing you truly know how to give: steady, practical hope.

You know what you have to do next. The day will be long, and it will be full of small calculations — sand, salt, seed, clamp, and neighbor. For now, you let the light settle on the map.

[Page-Turn Moment: You hear the distant whir of something larger moving — a vessel or a research platform — its silhouette briefly catching the sun on a reflective panel. For a fraction of a second the horizon seems to hold a choice larger than pilings and hedgerows. You tuck the map into your jacket, feel the locket warm at your throat, and step toward the work with hands already stained by the day's first tasks. You do not yet know which will be needed more — the slow weave of community terraces or the sudden arrival of big machinery — but you promise that whichever comes, you'll face it with the people at your side.]

[Visual: Fade to Black]
[Music: Fade Out]
