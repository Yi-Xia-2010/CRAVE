But you also feel the weight of other people's livelihoods in the air — the fishermen who come in at dawn, Rosa's café counter, Miriam's volunteer crews — and you know this is not just a ledger exercise.

Miriam (leaning forward, voice edged with urgency): "Redlines are only good if they bite. We need teeth — escrow in an independent trust, rehousing budgets written as irrevocable obligations, and a clause that forces a pause if coastal displacement exceeds projections."

Marco (smiles; it doesn't soften): "Pauses cost money and momentum, Miriam. I funded this because I want solutions that move. If everything's conditional, the project stalls and the town stays exposed. We can build faster than the sea can take us."

His watch blinks faintly as he speaks; his reticence to slow the schedule is visible in the way his fingers tap the table. You meet his eyes. He isn't wrong about speed; he isn't wrong that slow responses can mean lives lost. But speed without accountability will rewrite Seabright in someone else's image.

You: "Speed and accountability are not opposites. They are obligations to the same outcome." (Your voice is steadier than you expected.) "We can agree a fast timeline — but it must be tied to enforceable milestones, escrowed maintenance funds, and a rehousing guarantee that can't be bypassed by a corporate rebrand."

Marco: "Enforceable how? You want something that can stand up to courts and investors."

You: "We want covenants recorded with the county, a maintenance endowment under a community trust, and a public grievance mechanism with binding arbitration. Hal (voice thick with memory): "When we rebuilt after '29, we didn't ask for the right guarantees because we wanted to move on. We ended up with promises on letterhead and storms five years later. If you're going to put your name to this, make the architecture resist the kind of revisionism that money buys."

Eli: "I can make the technical triggers measurable. Sensors, tide gauges, stress bands on the pilings — all feed into a ledger. If a metric trips, it triggers the fund release. But legal teeth have to be written by someone who knows municipal code."

Miriam: "We get community representation on the auditor board. We set term limits. We make sure repair contracts require local hiring and transparency. And rehousing isn't a line item you hope gets funded — it's an escrowed requirement, with independent oversight."

Rosa (softening the room with a practical note): "People want to know their shops won't be the collateral for a 'modern Seabright.' Can we write protections for small tenants? Rent controls? Priority access to any rehousing units?"

Marco: (a beat) "We can prioritize current tenants for rehousing allocations. We can set aside a fund. But rent controls are a municipal policy, not a private contract."

You inhale, balancing the immediate legal limits with what the town needs morally. The political and legal frameworks are messy; your job is to stitch together a package that works in practice and law.

You (leaning forward, palms flat): "We structure adaptive management into the contract: scheduled, documented adaptation windows tied to monitored outcomes. It isn't endless rework if it's funded and transparent. It is how you make infrastructure that doesn't break the community."

Hal: "Record everything. Covenants recorded, trust legally incorporated with community trustees, and criminal penalties for falsifying inspection reports. That's how you stop promises from being polished into something meaningless."

Rosa: "And language that preserves small businesses: right of first refusal on reconfigured ground-level storefronts, and relocation grants for those displaced during construction."

Marco's jaw tightens, then loosens. Compromise is a craft; he started as a developer who believed force of capital could fix everything. Now he is sitting at a table where human detail is being specified in ways that would have embarrassed the version of himself who only saw blueprints.

Marco: "I didn't come here to erase Seabright," (he says quietly) "I came here because I grew up in a place that got washed away. If this ends up protecting people and preserving the town's soul, I want to be part of it."

You feel the word "want" in his voice; it's a small, human thing that tilts the balance. It doesn't erase the risk of later maneuvering, but it opens a door.
