[Scene: Town Hall Awning | Late Morning]
[Visual: Wet awning, rivulets tracing paths on the canvas; the Town Hall's brick facade behind a line of rain-dark flags]
[Sound: Distant crowd murmur; the steady slap of sneakers on wet pavement; a megaphone's soft echo from the plaza]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings — a careful, optimistic swell]

You pull your jacket tighter against a chill that smells of salt and damp stone. The decision you made at the council meeting — to open the door to Marco's partnership, but only with strict terms — has arranged the morning like a list you can finally start checking off. It is an awkward relief: the weight of choosing, transferred into paperwork and faces instead of the lonely ache of indecision.

Eli stands beside you under the awning, folding the collar of his reflective vest against the rain. His satchel is wet at the corners; the brass compass at its flap catches a sliver of light. Hal leans on the low railing, shoulders stooped under a thrifted coat that has seen more tides than most people in this town. Miriam paces by the plaza below, her teal hair flashing like a signal; a cluster of volunteers waves hand-painted banners: "COMMUNITY FIRST" and "NO DISPLACEMENT."

You watch her for a moment. Her megaphone is tucked under her arm, but her face is open and fierce in the way you've come to trust: angry, yes, but sorted — organized grief. The people she has drawn here are not your opponents in the abstract. They are your neighbors, the barista who shouldered extra shifts last month, the elderly couple whose stoop sits too low.

Eli lowers his voice. "You ready for this?" His question is practical. The tide-monitoring band on his wrist flickers; a data pulse like a tiny heartbeat. He keeps asking practical questions when your chest tightens — it steadies you.

You nod. "We ask for redlines. We keep community oversight. We don't sign away relocation without rehousing guarantees. We bring Hal into the technical review and Miriam into the advisory board." Saying the list aloud is a ritual. It pins the day to something you can hold.

Hal snorts softly. "Practical. Measured. Good. Tell Marco you'll want clauses on maintenance funding and a clawback if prototypes fail." His voice rustles like a newspaper in a breeze. "And tell him you remember what 'temporary' meant the last time someone called a levee 'temporary.'"

[Visual: A cluster of protesters below; Miriam's banner flaps in a gust]
[Sound: Miriam's distant voice, cutting through the rain: "We want a seat at the table! We won't be sold out!"]
[Music: A hopeful piano motif threads under the strings]

You step down from the awning and into the plaza. Rain beads on the brim of your scarf and the soles of your boots whisper on wet cobbles. Miriam meets you halfway, eyes sharp, a damp smear of paint on her knuckles where she—somehow—keeps volunteering at everything. There's gratitude in the crook of her mouth.

Miriam: "You're giving them a door. That's not nothing." (She tests you with the sentence, not to judge, but to see whether you can hold the balance.)

You: "I know." (Honesty feels like an offering today.) "I'm trying to keep the town whole. That means leverage — not a sellout. I need you in that room, Miriam. Your voice changes the math."

Miriam's jaw tightens. "I'll go in if Hal's on the technical review and the oversight committee gets veto power on evictions."

You: "Then we'll ask for both."

She exhales, a small, fierce laugh. "Good. Don't let him charm you." There's a beat where she looks past you, toward the doors, and for a flash you imagine Marco's face there: tailored, practiced, the kind of smile that has been used too many times.

[Scene: Town Hall Entrance | Moments Later]
[Visual: Tall heavy doors, varnish dulled by countless hands; a red-carpet runner has been set out and plastic sheeting taped to protect it from rain]
[Sound: The doors open with a hollow creak; muffled chanting softens inside to the low thrum of conversation]
[Music: A single held violin note, then quiet]

Inside, the air is warmer — a mixture of heated breath, brewed coffee, and the faint, metallic tang of wet umbrellas. Marco waits beside a small conference table, flanked by two aides whose nametags glint. He is exactly as the rumors describe: smooth, measured, a man made to look most at ease on a stage or a shipping manifest. His blazer is dry; of course it is. Rain adheres to other people.

Marco: "Amara, Eli, Hal. Thank you for coming." (His voice is even; there is a practiced softness.) "We both want Seabright to survive. We can bring resources you can't raise from volunteers and grants."

You: "We're here to define how those resources will be used. Not just to take them." (Your words are direct but not blunt. You have rehearsed this on the ride over and in the quiet hour after midnight.)

Marco smiles — small, appraising. "And we appreciate that. Contract clarity is important to us too. We can move quickly if the terms are reasonable."

Eli folds out a sheaf of his own notes and taps a diagram. "We don't need a glossy seawall that isolates people. We need modular, maintainable structures with budgeted maintenance, and funds designated for small-business protections during transitions."

Marco: "Agreed on modular. Efficiency is in our interest too — nothing worse for PR than a failed seawall." (He leans in.) "But speed requires scale. We can finance microgrids, elevated foundations, workforce training programs. Think long-term jobs."

Hal: (low, gravelly) "Talk is cheap if the maintenance fund isn't locked. You say 'workforce training' — are those jobs permanent? Or just the sort of contract that vanishes when the numbers look unattractive?"

Marco's eyes narrow — not quite menacing, more like a man recalibrating the chessboard. "We structure wages and contracts in phases. Initial construction, then upkeep. We can include incentives for local hiring."

You notice the phrasing — "incentives" — a pivot that leaves room. You press: "And displacement? How will people who lose their homes be rehoused? And who decides what's 'redevelopment' and what's 'preservation'?"

Marco: (hands open, palms up) "We can earmark housing funds. But there are trade-offs. Not every structure can be preserved if it's a safety hazard and draining the budget. We work with the town to identify priorities."

Your internal monologue ticks through scenarios — apartments converted into low-income housing, a small bakery shuttered because foundation costs skyrocket, the old fisher's wharf reimagined into a tourist promenade. Each image is a ledger entry with a human face attached.

Eli interrupts before you can erupt. "Let's talk contingencies. If a prototype fails, who repairs? If a flood exceeds expectations, how do we activate emergency housing funds? We'll need trigger thresholds, data feed ownership, and an independent review panel."

Marco: "Independent panels are fine in principle, but they slow things down. Investors need clarity."

Hal: "Independent panels also keep your investors from walking off if things go sideways. You know that. You've seen it in other towns."

Marco's smile tightens. There's a beat — a small, complex unreadability around his mouth that you can't entirely place. It could be calculation, nostalgia for battles won, or something that would complicate the picture if you knew it. You store that ambiguity away as a datum: useful but not decisive.

Marco reaches for a packet on the table — glossy, stock paper with renderings of promenades and clean lines. He slides it toward you like an offering. "These are preliminary concepts. They can be adjusted. But they need a lead partner to underwrite them. We can structure a fund that locks in maintenance for ten years and offers grants for small business resilience."

You examine the renderings — bright, breathable, unimaginably neat. The artist has smoothed the edges of a town that is lived-in and ragged and turned it into a brochure. In the margins, scribbled notes from Hal and Eli begin to appear in your mind: "material choices," "storm attenuation metrics," "local labor clause." Actual adaptation is less photogenic than the renderings; it is a slow stitching.

Marco watches you like a man waiting to see which rope you'll tie first. "Amara, your endorsement matters. A public voice in favor lets us move money quickly."

Your chest tightens in a familiar pressure — the old fear of being the solitary pivot between a town's future and a developer's timeline. But under it is something else now: a network you have called into being. Miriam below. Hal's stubborn memory. Eli's calculations. The Beacon's volunteer list. These are leverage.

You speak with a steadier cadence than you feel. "We'll negotiate redlines. Oversight, rehousing guarantees, transparent procurement, and a maintenance lock. We make the town a partner, not a product."

Marco taps a pen. "Redlines are negotiable if backed by enforceable mechanisms. Put them on paper and we'll consider them."

Eli leans forward. "And we should define what 'consider' means. Timelines for replies. Mediators agreed in advance. That way 'consideration' isn't a stalling tactic."

Marco's gaze flicks to Eli with something close to respect. "That's reasonable. Timelines are acceptable as long as they allow for proper due diligence. We both want speed — but we need it to last."

Hal clears his throat. "Then let's put it in language. No weasel words about 'temporary' protections. No vague 'community benefits' that end when the project's first profit report comes in. Make the guarantees explicit."

The conversation stretches — clauses discussed, contingencies named, a small argument over a blueprint's drainage slope turning into a thoughtful examination of maintenance supply chains. It is, in its own way, the work of love: tedious, granular, anchored in the messy business of keeping people alive through storms.

[Visual: A beam of weak sunlight breaks the clouds; rain slows to a gentle scatter]
[Sound: The protest below settles into a rhythmic chant; Miriam's voice carries upward, then softens into laughter at something a volunteer has said]
[Music: Strings swell into a warm, hopeful chord]

You feel something shift — not resolution, not yet, but a movement toward shared terms. It's not perfect. It will never be perfect. But where you had feared being boxed by impossible choices, you now see a corridor: hard-won language, technical thresholds, community oversight mechanisms. It is negotiable space, and negotiation is, paradoxically, progress.

Marco: (quietly) "If we can agree on this framework, I can have my legal team draft an initial memorandum within a week. It will outline funding, milestones, and oversight structures."

You: (allowing yourself a small, cautious smile) "A week is better than a year."

Eli: "And we'll circulate redlines to Hal and Miriam for comment before we accept any drafts."

Marco inclines his head. "Fine. Transparency is good business when it's built into the plan."

Hal coughs a laugh that sounds almost like a bark of approval. "Build it into the plan and hold them to it."

You step back from the table for a second, letting the room spin into practicalities. The day's work had been to open the door; the next day's work will be to make the hinges reliable. You glance down at your hands — ink-smudged from notes, warmed by the coffee the council clerk thrust into your palm — and for the first time in a long stretch, you feel the promise of a real lever in your fingers.

You carry the morning's notes in a damp packet, and inside those pages live possibilities: redlines, timelines, and a fragile, growing architecture of trust.

The next step is the paperwork: iron-clad language and clauses that turn promises into obligations. You have a week to write the town's protection into a legal future.
