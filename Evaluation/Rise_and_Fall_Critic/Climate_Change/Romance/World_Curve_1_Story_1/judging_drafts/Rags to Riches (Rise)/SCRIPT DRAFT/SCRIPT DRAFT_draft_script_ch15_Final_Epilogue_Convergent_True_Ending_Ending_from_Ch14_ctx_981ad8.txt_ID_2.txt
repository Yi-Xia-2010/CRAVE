Amara Vale: "Hal?" (You smile at the memory, tasting salt and sawdust.) "He still has the knack for making steady work feel like a ritual."

Hal: "Rituals keep the tide honest." (Hal's voice comes from the short distance behind you — closer than you'd expected. Narration
Hal's thumb leaves a chalky print on the rail. You think of the long ledger he'd shown you in the Beacon, spidery notes of past repairs and missed warnings. His presence is a braid of knowledge and time — the kind that keeps plans honest.

Amara Vale: "It holds because people insisted on oversight." (You let the sentence carry the town's work — Miriam's campaigns, Rosa's lobbying of small-business owners, Hal's quiet records.) "Because we set conditions that the funding had to meet."

Marco Voss: "And those conditions turned my pitch into a promise. We pay for infrastructure; you keep the stewardship. Fair."

Narration
You watch his hand flex around the word promise. The bar between cynicism and generosity is thinner than it seems, and Marco lives along its edge. Still, tonight his words sit in the air as easy as any seal on a contract.

Each small decision bends the evening: an inspection, a conversation, a pie. These are the stitches that will keep the town together through weather and politics.

Eli: "You did something risky, you know." (He turns to you, earnestness softening his features.) "You trusted enough people to let them help. That's not small."

Amara Vale: "I learned not to carry it alone." (You meet his eyes. Between you is the map of months of back-and-forth — arguments, compromises, nights of data and coffee.) Eli: "That's why the hybrid governance matters. Public oversight, private funding bound by clear thresholds, community stewards with legal teeth. Not a hand-off. A hand-in."

His words settle into a quiet that feels like the night falling. You think of families upstairs in raised houses, of little shops that smell like coffee and fish, of Old Man Tor and the carved buoy he keeps by his door.

Eli: "Durable and upward." (He repeats your words as if tasting them, then adds:) "We still have to face funding cycles, storms, politics. But for the first time I think the town believes in a route forward that doesn't erase itself."

Hal: "And I'll keep the ledger." (He taps his cane, grin hidden.) "Someone's got to keep scolding the future."

You look at each face — the small constellation of people who held the town upright while it rebuilt its spine.
