# Route: Route C — Sabotage


[Scene: Boardwalk | Dawn]
[Visual: Wet wooden planks, salt-bleached and warped; low lamplight from cafés; pale fog lifting off the water]
[Music: Sparse, minor piano with distant, mournful cello — slow tempo]
[Sound: Slow, rhythmic slap of water; gulls calling far off; the scuff of your boots]

You breathe the town in the way you always do at dawn — like taking inventory. Salt on your tongue from yesterday's breeze, the oily whiff of fried coffee from Rosa's kitchen, the sharp metallic tang of old nails and sea air. Your Moleskine is tucked under your arm, its corners softened with tide-drips and pencil smudges. The compass at your sternum is cool against the fabric of your jacket, the dent in its rim a familiar weight; you press your thumb over it as if you could steady what the sea keeps shifting.

The boardwalk tilts under your boots. Where the planks have bowed and split, thin rivers collect rain and tidal spray; they mirror neon from closed shop windows and your own reflection, hollowed and narrow. You catalogue everything automatically: salt stains like faded constellations, the way some railings sag toward the surf, a child's sand-splattered raincoat snagged on a lamppost. It is a ritual that steadies you — observation as prayer. You make a small list in your head, then sketch a rough cross-section in the margin of your notebook: plank, gap, rust, algae bloom — note: propose recycled plastic replacement? — but the motions feel more brittle than they have in months.

Ahead, Rosa's café glows like a fragile lamp in a storm. Its front window fogs with breath and steam; inside, string lights toss a honeyed pool across mismatched tables. The sound of the espresso machine is steady and human, a heartbeat you can trust.

Finn is at the railing, sleeves rolled, hands in the knotwork of a new line. He looks up as you approach, his jacket a patchwork of scavenged fabric and proud repairs — a personal map of every job he's ever taken for the town. He is humming under his breath, fingers looping and tightening; he always hums when he's thinking of knots, as if the rhythm helps him compute forces and futures at once.

Finn: "Morning, Maya. Tide's higher than the chart said. Could be wind, could be the—" (he gestures to the sea as if the ocean is a cranky neighbor) "—the ocean deciding to be ornery."

You: "Or the ocean doing what it always does. You're up early."

Finn: (grins) "You know me. Kisses the dock, calls it by name. You look like you carried the tide in your teeth." He gestures at your notebook. "Find anything good?"

You force a smile but your chest tightens. "Salt patterns, warped boards. A new leak in the boardwalk past the old bait shop. I sketched the seam."

Finn: (tilting his head) "The mayor's team coming by the hall today, right? For the funding thing?"

You: (your stomach drops) "They were supposed to present the matching grants decision today. I—" Your mouth makes the shape of an explanation that doesn't exist yet. "I was counting on that."

Finn's hands pause mid-knot. For a second his practiced optimism looks like armor that might slip.

Finn: "Hey. If it gets delayed, we can do the emergency shore reinforcement with the volunteers. We have pallets and sandbags, and Rosa's got hot coffee. We can stretch it."

You: "Stretching works until someone needs a new roof." Your voice is steadier than you feel. "The matching grant isn't just plywood and sandbags. It's the engineers we need, permits, paid labor for people who can't volunteer away their rent."

Finn: (softly) "I know." He finishes the knot with a flourish and hands you the loop. "You always say don't fight the tide alone."

You curl your fingers around the rope, feeling the salt grit under your nails. The compass pendant is heavier now, a small, private gravity.

Finn watches you with the kind of patience that makes you guilty for the silence between you.

You: "Don't give up the pots on the rooftop garden. If we lose them, that's community food gone." You shift the subject because you don't want to say the thing sitting heavier than the compass — the neighbor you couldn't save. The memory arrives anyway: wind like carved glass, water dragging the porch like a hand, the terrible small face in the dark. You swallow. "We can't afford that to be a lesson again."

Finn: (jaw tight) "We won't let that happen." He looks away for a beat, then smiles, small and stubborn. "You should come by later. I fixed the net on the boathouse. Bring the notebook. We'll go over the volunteer roster."

You nod. "I'll be at Rosa's in ten."

[Scene Transition: You step away from the railing; the sound of the sea is louder in the shift of the camera. The lamplight from Rosa's grows warmer.]

[Scene: Rosa's Café | Dawn]
[Visual: Close interior — steam curling from cups, a faded mural of a fishing boat on the far wall]
[Music: Quiet acoustic guitar notes under the ambient hum of conversation]
[Sound: Espresso machine, the clink of ceramic, low conversation]

Rosa is at the counter, apron tied, hair wrapped in a bright scarf. She lifts her head and squints at you like you're both a customer and a kin. There is a kindness in her face that makes the room breathe easier.

Rosa: "If I didn't see you every morning I'd worry. Sit. You look like you've been negotiating with a windstorm."

You: "I have. I lost that negotiation." You hold the notebook up out of habit. "How's Eric's scones today?"

Rosa: (laughs, but it's brief) "Burned the first batch. He cried into the second. It's been a week." Her smile falters. "You heard? The city's press release — delay on the grant. Something about additional review, budgetary hold. They put it in the same paragraph as a tourism incentive. I hated that sentence."

You, internally: Your mind skitters along the edges of policy language: merit review, contingent matching, political calendar. The words are thin paper over a deep cut. You feel the knot in your chest tighten again, not only for infrastructure, but for the people whose names fill your tide logs.

You: "Do you have the release? I need to see the wording. If it's tied to tourism incentives they're rearranging priorities."

Rosa: (wiping hands on her apron) "Here." She slides a printed snippet across the wood. It's bureaucratic, the kind of sentence designed to numb. You read the line twice, slowly. "Delayed pending review" — a phrase that tastes like postponement and dismissal.

Rosa: "Mayor Cho's office hasn't said much except that they're 'assessing fiscal risk,'" she says, lowering her voice. "My cousin said she saw Lillian on the news with a smile like someone waiting out a slow leak."

You: (the memory comes back anyway — the neighbor lost — and you press your thumb into the dent of the compass until you see stars behind your eyes) "Lillian's office sent a terse update to every municipal listserve this morning. 'Further review required,' it said." Your jaw tightens. "Hull repairs and human lives are different budgets in their spreadsheets."

Rosa: (her voice softens) "Maya—"

You: "I could call Elias." The name is a small flare of impulse. Elias, with his camera and bandana, the way he rallies. When he gets a fire in his throat, it spreads. "He'll rope the camps together, maybe push a protest before the council meets."

Rosa: "Or you could draft a counterproposal, run it by Dr. Hana, send it to the mayor's inbox before noon. You know your numbers better than anyone." She folds a napkin and presses it between her palms, thinking as a neighbor and as someone who depends on the town remaining habitable. "Either way, don't burn yourself out."

You feel both options like raw edges. Calling Elias is a live wire — electric urgency, unpredictable but alive. Drafting a counterproposal is meticulous, slower, but maybe harder for the system to ignore. You imagine Elias on a speakerphone, voice lit up, hands flung wide. You imagine Mayor Lillian's inbox, polite and poised, a place where your careful paragraphs might be skimmed and set aside.

Rosa pours you coffee without asking. The first sip is hot and metallic, a little too strong; it grounds you in the body: taste, warmth, the way the mug bruises your palms.

Rosa: "Finn stopped by with the nets and said you'd be here. He left the list for the volunteers on the table." She leans in, voice small. "Maya, whatever you choose, don't do it all. Let people carry it with you."

You: (laughs, but it cracks) "You always know what to say."

Rosa: "I know you. And I know the town." She straightens, the proprietor again. "People will pull together if they feel the call is worth it. But calls need answers. We need you to tell them how to help without losing their roofs."

You close the notebook and run your thumb along the edge of the page with a line of tide logs. Your handwriting is steady when you are calm and jittery when the stakes get too sharp. The dented compass sits against your sternum like a question mark.

From the corner of the café, someone murmurs about the Mayor's next press conference — an item that feels like a distant bell. The radio under the pastry case coughs into life with a municipal bulletin; a voice you don't recognize reads the official line. Your chest tightens with the weight of an entire town suspended on a clause.

You pace your choices in your mind the way you pace the boardwalk. Delegation feels like giving away a piece of the plan you have borne alone. Asking Elias feels like inviting volatility into a controlled emergency. Drafting the counterproposal feels like admitting the problem could be bigger than you planned for — that you need institutions and allies to change.

Your memory of the neighbor returns, uninvited: rain turning to a sound like someone tearing fabric; a porch light gone dark. The compass heats against the skin of your chest, and the red thread on your wrist rubs raw against your pulse. That thread was knotted the night you promised yourself you'd do everything you could to keep Saltbridge together. You had meant "everything." You had not meant "alone."

You breathe. Your hand hovers over your phone. The café's warmth presses around you like a temporary shore.

You think of Elias's laugh, how it gathers people. You think of Mayor Lillian's practiced calm, the way policies move like glaciers: slow, inevitable, and sometimes cruel. You think of Finn's steady knots and Rosa's lamp. The town is a map of humans balancing on thresholds.

Your plan — small, meticulously catalogued in your notebook — feels suddenly insufficient, not because it's wrong but because it might not be enough.

You fold the notebook closed, the pages like a promise and a ledger at once. The decision sits in your throat, heavy as the dented compass.

[You stand at the edge of the café's light, the boardwalk beyond it dark and slick. Outside, the sea keeps time with a relentless patience. Inside, the town is waiting for a nudge that could become a shove.]
