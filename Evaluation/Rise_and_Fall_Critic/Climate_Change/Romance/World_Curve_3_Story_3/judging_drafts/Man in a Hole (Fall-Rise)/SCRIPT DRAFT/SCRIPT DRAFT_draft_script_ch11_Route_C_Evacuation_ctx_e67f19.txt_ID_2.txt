[Scene: Rooftop Sanctuary | Dawn]
[Visual: Dew-laden solar fabric sagging between reclaimed rafters; raised beds glisten, steam rising from a tray of coffee; the mall's glass bones loom beneath]

[Music: Sparse piano, a low melancholic cello]

You are already awake before the sun reaches the horizon. The sanctuary smells of damp soil, frying oil from Rosa's thermos, and the metallic tang of salt that hangs in everything in Saltbridge now. Your notebook is open on a crate-turned-table; a half-crossed timeline blurs where condensation caught the ink. You should be calm. Instead your chest is a sternum of small, sharp decisions.

Finn moves like a problem with hands: rope coils, duct tape, canned goods—everything organized into piles you can map. He looks up when you put your palm on the compass at your throat, and for a beat there's a softness that almost undoes you.

Finn: (quiet) "We're on Phase One. Nurses at the community hall, two relay teams up north, and we can push extra water purifiers out on carts if Rosa's volunteers can spare—"

You cut him off with a fingertip on a list. Interruptions now are kindness; everything is timing.

You: "Run the relays with Mateo and Laila. I want the purifiers on the second shift; leave the first to the hospital volunteers. If we lose the hospital grid, those purifiers move to the Rooftop net."

Finn: (nodding, brisk) "Got it. I'll mark the caches. Elias is pulling volunteers for the walk lists."

[Visual: Elias, damp scarf wrapped twice around his neck, is at a folding map with a marker]  
Elias's smile looks both tired and relentless—he's already translating moral urgency into routes, turning heat into a roster. But there is something in his jaw that your eyes catch and your heart can't read cleanly.

Elias: "Most people are ready. A few are stubborn—old Mrs. Halvorsen said she won't move until her cat's found. We'll handle that."

You: (soft) "Don't promise things you can't keep. Send two people who can get the cat carrier and a ladder. And make sure they're wearing life vests."

Elias: (half-grin, then serious) "You know I will. You always think three steps ahead."

His fingers brush the map; whether that touch is to steady something physical or impressionable, you can't say. The map is the only thing you can measure.

Rosa arrives with crates of breakfast and a stack of flyers that have been smudged by hundred hands. The sanctuary has become a dispatch point and a confessional; people arrive with lists and grief in the same breath.

Rosa: (hands on her hips) "We're short-handed if Hannah's team goes to the shore. Mayor's office called—trying to push a shuttle route that skips the old quarter. Says it's 'efficient.'"

You: (bitter laugh) "Efficient for whom?"

Rosa: (quiet, almost a whisper) "Not for people who sleep above the bookshop."

Your stomach turns at the bookshop's mention. You pictured the narrow shelves and the scent of old paper stitched into the building's bones. That picture lines up with the promise you tied to the red thread on your wrist and suddenly feels childish.

[Visual: The sanctuary's radio crackles; a tinny voice from Dr. Hana comes through, steady as an anemometer]
[Sound: Radio hiss; a low alarm from the east channel]

Dr. Hana (over radio): "Maya, I'm reading unexpected elevation from buoy seven. It's spiking faster than the model. We're rerouting maintenance to the channel; I need the east gate cleared for boat access."

You: (to the radio) "Copy. Finn, open the east gate. Get the pallets down. Rosa, can you spare someone to flag the channel mouth?"

Finn: "On it. I'll take a team."

Rosa: "I'll go, but I'm taking Mateo."

The coordination feels like stitching a wound that keeps bleeding. Each motion you arrange is an attempt to close the map's jagged edges.

[Scene: Mobile Research Boat | Dawn — later]
[Visual: Sodium lamps wash the deck in angry orange; bundles of instruments hum; fog eats the horizon]
[Sound: The creak of hull, the slap of ropes, low voices clipped with concentration]
[Music: Tense low strings; heartbeat percussion]

You step aboard the clanging research boat, the boots-on-metal smell mixing with coffee and the clean ozone of testing equipment. Dr. Hana is a silhouette against a bank of instruments, her face lit by a pale screen. Her hands move with the tenderness of someone tending a sick animal.

Dr. Hana: "We picked up the spike before you did. A gust surge funnelled into an unreinforced channel—likely a scouring effect near the eastern sluice. We can divert with temporary booms but the timing is tight."

You: "What's the window?"

Dr. Hana: (flat) "Thirty minutes before impact. If the booms deploy, we buy twelve hours. If we don't—"

Her sentence hangs like a tide line.

You: (decisive) "Divert everything to buoy seven. I'll marshal the east teams and we'll lay the boom from shore."

Dr. Hana: (soft, a reprimand that almost sounds like relief) "Good. And Maya—no hero shit. We need you intact to run logistics if things get messy."

You swallow the urge to retort; there's truth in her tone. "Intact" feels like a fragile treaty.

Noah Kestrel radios in from the Kestrel Office launch: his voice is clipped, professional, a scalpel. You always found his calm disconcerting when the world was falling into string and water.

Noah (over radio): "I can spare two mechanical winches. They'll help with the boom anchors. We can stage them at the east gate."

You: "Yes. Send them now. Coordinate with Finn on anchor points."

Noah: (pause that stretches) "Acknowledged."

You hear nothing of softness in him; you hear the sound of systems aligning. Whether that's comfort or distance is not yours to judge now.

[Scene: Rooftop Sanctuary | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Volunteers shuttling between roofs and stairwells; condensation runs in rivulets down canvas; patched signs reading 'Sanctuary' flap in a steady wind]
[Sound: The murmur of strained voices; an ambulance siren far off; pages of your notebook being thumbed quickly]
[Music: Sparse, aching violin]

The timeline begins to crack. A family on Larkin Street—two kids, a small dog, and a stubborn father—refuses to leave until their paperwork for temporary housing is clarified. A teacher insists she'll stay because her classroom holds irreplaceable supplies. Members of a neighborhood watch accuse you and the sanctuary's volunteers of selecting who can be saved.

Man from Larkin Street: (shouting) "Who decides this? You? What right—?"

You: (raising your voice because it's the only immediate tool you have) "No one decides. We're offering routes and resources. If you want to stay, we can't force you—but if the levee fails, we will come back to recover bodies. I won't be the person who leaves you to die."

The words land harder than you expected. Someone gasps. Rosa's jaw is tight; Finn's hands flex at his sides. Elias steps between two arguing neighbors, palms raised like a mediator who has run out of magic.

Elias: (breathless) "Listen—this isn't about control. It's about time. We can get you to the hall, we can—"

Man from Larkin Street: (cutting him off) "You said grassroots! You said we get to choose! This is top-down!"

Elias: (hurt, stumbling) "I didn't—"

You: (soft, but iron) "No one here wants to strip anyone of choice. But there are lines of safety. We're trying not to decide who lives. We're trying to decide how to keep as many of you alive as possible."

The argument becomes a scapegoat. People want a figure to pin their fear to, and you are conveniently frontal.

The clock eats minutes and your margin of error shrinks. You send teams out; Finn locks supply caches and writes names on boxes with a blunt, mechanical hand. You and Rosa run a roster that is part whim and part triage. The radio reports the boom deployment has begun, but so has the tide.

[Scene: Larkin Street — later]
[Visual: Narrow street, water already creeping into doorways; a small bookshop with a lace curtain and a hand-painted sign reading 'Halvorsen & Sons']
[Sound: The rush of water through a hidden inlet; a child's shriek muffled by the rain]
[Music: Single piano note repeating, a metronome for dread]

You remember promising Mrs. Halvorsen you'd get the cat and check her safe box of ledgers. You trusted that promise because she used to keep the town's oral records behind that counter, telling stories to anyone who would listen. The bookshop's floorboards are already dark with the sea's black fingers.

You bang on the door with two volunteers. The tide is higher than you'd forecast; a channel you assumed secure has become a conduit. Mrs. Halvorsen's voice is thin and panicked from inside.

Mrs. Halvorsen: (yelling) "My ledger! My ledger's—oh God—"

You force the door. Cold seawater explodes under your boots, the bookshop filling like a mouth. You see the ledger floating, a slim dark knife of paper between shelves. Then you see Mrs. Halvorsen slip as the floor gives way under her feet; the water—quick, traitorous—takes her ankle and pulls.

You lurch. Someone grabs her sleeve. You grab her other arm. The world narrows to the scrape of fabric, the wet sting in your palm, the weight of someone's life depending on the split-second the town has left.

You: (commanding) "On three—pull!"

Their hands are small around hers; calloused, shaking. You heave and the bookshop gives you a flood of air and wet paper. Mrs. Halvorsen is alive but coughing salt and mud into your sweatshirt.

You: (panting, voice small) "Get her to the net. Warm blankets. Now."

You manage to save her. You win something that should feel like everything and find it tastes like iron and wet wood. When you look back, shelves sag, paper curling into unrecognizable black shapes. The ledger is gone, nor will it come back dry. The bookshop will not survive this season.

[Sound: People murmuring in grief; someone is furious]
[Music: Cello low and unresolved]

Back at the sanctuary, the mood has shifted from focused urgency to raw blame. A cluster of neighbors, their faces knotted with loss, press forward. Accusations are direct and bright as salt.

Neighbor #1: "You decided who gets help and who doesn't."

Neighbor #2: "You took away our agency. You made maps and sent people away. Those houses didn't have to go."

You try to explain logistics, triage, the constraints of pump capacity and volunteer hours. Your explanations are technical, and here technical talk is gristle.

You: (soft) "We didn't want to decide who lived. We tried to make options. The booms—Dr. Hana—"

Neighbor #1: (cutting you) "People with money get shuttles. We get boxes. That's what 'options' look like."

The charge stings in ways a tide never could. You hear Elias in the crowd, trying to re-weave the argument into something useable. Noah stood near the edge of the sanctuary, hands folded. His face is a plan, unreadable; whether he blames you, admires you, or sees this as inevitable policy fallout you cannot tell.

Elias: (to the crowd) "We are—"

Neighbor #2: (bitter laugh) "We are what? Saved by people who never had to choose where to live?"

You want to be everywhere at once, to stitch every torn thing. But there are only two hands on a ledger, one pair knotting rope, and a chest that keeps tightening with a guilt you cannot vent to the sky.

Finn comes to your side and simply stands there, his shoulder a small, steady presence. He does not speak for you; he offers steadiness. Sometimes steady is enough. Sometimes it isn't.

[Visual: Rain begins harder, a thin curtain between faces]
[Sound: The sanctuary's radio bleats with new alerts; someone curses; a distant alarm wails]
[Music: Violin climbs, unresolved and aching]

You think of the lost ledger, the bookshop's smell, Mrs. Halvorsen's hand in yours. You think of choices you made that were reasonable on paper and felt monstrous in practice. You think of the way the town's social fabric—bookstores, lamplights, the small trades of neighborliness—frays under salt and time.

Loneliness presses into your ribs with the taste of iron and wet wood.

You step away from the crowd and open your notebook. The ink there is a confession and a map. You write a single line and the movement feels both ritual and useless.

You: (to yourself) "We did what we could. Was it enough?"

No answer comes that isn't complicated.

[Visual: Close-up of the compass at your throat; the red thread dull from salt]
[Sound: A single gull cries, cutting the wet air]

This is not the end of the plan. It is an unraveling in public. It is a ledger burned by water. You have saved a life and lost a place that held stories the town will miss. People look at you now with something you do not know how to bear: wounded love and sharp accusation braided together.

You fold the notebook closed, and for the first time since you tied the thread around your wrist, the promise feels like a decision made by someone else.
