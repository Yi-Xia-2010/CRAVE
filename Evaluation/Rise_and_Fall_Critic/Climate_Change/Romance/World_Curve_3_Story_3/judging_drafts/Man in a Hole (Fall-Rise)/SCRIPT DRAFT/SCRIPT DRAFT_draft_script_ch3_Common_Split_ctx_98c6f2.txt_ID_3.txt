[Music: Low, hollow cello; distant industrial thrum]

Narration: You leave the warm smear of the Community Hall like a stone pushed into cold water — the ripples run out toward the sea. Your coat smells of coffee and damp paper; the notebook in your bag is gone quiet. The boardwalk behind you recedes into gray light. Ahead, the barricade waits: an improvised spine of sandbags, pallets, and slogans meant to make the Mayor and whatever comes next look into town and feel guilty.

Narration: The air tastes of metal and old rain. Salt clings to your lips. Each footstep sinks with a soft, angry sound; your boots pick at the seam between land and tide.

[Visual: Close-up — a protest sign nailed to a post: "HANDS OFF OUR TIDES" — letters uneven and deliberate; spray paint glossed with salt]
Narration: The signs flapped themselves into messages as if they had mouths. Someone had painted hearts beside the letters; someone else had crosshatched them with sharp black strokes. It looks like a town arguing with itself.

Narration: Your fingers find the red thread on your wrist. It is a tiny compass of its own: a promise felt more than read. The thread pulls at something in your chest and the promise contracts into a single question — what will you risk?

[Sound: A hollow beat of distant chant, then nothing]
Narration: You came down here hoping solitude would make the answer simple. It doesn't. It makes the edges sharper.

[Visual: Mid-distance — a cluster of figures rounds the dune path; a colorful scarf and an old camera bob in the gray]
[Sound: Voices, low and urgent; a laugh held like a spark]

Elias: "Maya." (he keeps his voice to the width of the wind) "You came down."

Narration: He arrives with a handful of others — neighbors with armbands, someone carrying rope, someone else with a stack of handbills. Elias moves like he was born in public: open, immediate. Up close, his amber eyes are bright with fatigue and urgency. They find you and settle, but you can read the way they hold back too—the way someone used to leading a crowd learns the value of listening.

You: "I needed to see it alone first." (you say, then add, softer) "But I knew you would come."

Elias: "You write things in your head and they don't always like to be shared." (he gives a small, crooked smile) "We could get twelve people here by dawn, fifty by noon. Sit-ins, block the pier access, live-stream the council meeting. Pressure. Shame. Make them decide with us watching."

You: "Pressure helps," you say, because it does. You feel a rush of warmth at the memory of his laugh, at the way he folds strangers into a plan, and then — almost immediately — the old ledger reasserts itself in your ribs. "But pressure can burn people. Arrests mean families short a paycheck."

Elias: "I know that." (he drums his thumb against a signpost) "I don't pretend arrests are clean. But what else moves them? Petitions? Another study? We have to make it costly for them not to act. For us to wait is to grieve people we could've saved."

You: "And lines get drawn," you say. "Not everyone here will be able to keep up with escalations. People have kids, rent, meds. You know that as well as I do."

Elias: (hesitation) "I do. I do—" (he searches your face) "I just think the greatest risk is doing nothing."

Narration: There are no shouted promises in his voice now; only the pressure of a moral geometry he believes in. Your chest tightens at the honesty of it.

Narration: He leaves some of his group to reinforce a corner of the barricade, someone to hammer, someone to sew. He lingers with you a moment longer, an unreadable look in his face that could mean pride, or pleading, or something that lives in the gray between them.

[Visual: Headlights cut through dusk — a low-profile corporate vehicle eases up the dune track, precise and dry against the weather]
[Sound: Engine hum, the click of a door closing; a different kind of order]

Noah: "Maya." (his voice is even, almost too calm for the wind) "Good to see you out here."

Narration: Noah steps from the car with the controlled gait of someone who measures everything. His coat sheds rain in perfect beads. He speaks like a person used to balancing ledgers and people, and his words smell faintly of metal and clean paper.

You: "Noah." (you try to keep your voice level) "I didn't expect you to come down to the barricade."

Noah: "I didn't expect to find a town improvising a defense that could make them liable," (he answers) "but politics have a way of bringing planners into odd places. I have a proposal — a fast-track contract. Kestrel's firm can put up a modular seawall, prioritized for the blocks with the highest risk and economic need. It will buy time, and it will be durable."

You: "At what cost?" (the question is older than the two of you; it tastes like rust)

Noah: "Rezoning for certain parcels, relocation subsidies for low-lying homes we can't protect, and a maintenance plan that — for now — relies on a public-private partnership. It's not perfect, but it's something immediate. It keeps people dry and preserves jobs in the short term."

You: "You mean buyouts, Noah." (there is no softness in your voice anymore) "You mean some of our streets become corporate property. You mean we lose histories, kitchens, front porches."

Noah: (small, pained nod) "Yes. There will be losses. But there will also be fewer funerals if the storms keep coming. It's the calculus of triage. We can argue until the water gets higher."

Noah: "And," (he adds, quietly) "I don't want this to feel like an end. We can build community oversight into the contract. We can set affordable housing clauses. We can phase things to preserve cultural landmarks when possible."

You: "Promises on paper are not the same as the smell of a kitchen saved." (you hear your own bitterness) "How do I tell someone their home is a line item?"

Noah: (after a breath) "You don't. You help them make the decision with as much information and support as we can give. You help them choose what to save."

Narration: He looks at you then, and there is an unreadable thing in his steel-blue gaze — not hostility, not warm plea, but a measurement. You sense that to take his hand would be to sign a contract that would change many people's maps.

[Visual: The sky deepens; a light flashes from a small tablet — Hana's face appears in the glow, grainy but steady]
[Sound: The soft chime of an incoming call; static at the edges]

Dr. Hana Park: "Maya, I'm watching the tide data. It's spiking sooner than projected." (she folds her arms as if to hold herself together) "I won't romanticize this: the evacuation plan you sketched on that napkin—it's the kind of choice I call 'strategically traumatic.' It's a surgical loss. It saves lives but it will eat at people's sense of place."

You: "How soon are we talking?" (you grip the thread on your wrist until it draws a line of white)

Dr. Hana Park: "Possibly within three seasons for the worst-affected blocks. The models are clear: incremental fixes will slow the inevitable but do not stop the encroaching baseline. You can hedge with infrastructure, or you can plan a managed retreat. Either way, those decisions will cost the town parts of itself."

You: "And if we do nothing?" you ask, already knowing.

Dr. Hana Park: "The carrying capacity of the place changes. People die. Social networks fray. Emergency responses become triage by default, not design."

Narration: Hana's words are not dramatic; they are the measured cruelty of scientific clarity. You feel the red thread tighten until it is a wire. Each plan she names arrives with a ledger of human weight.

[Visual: Finn appears at the edge, breathless from the run; his twenty-something face is smudged with sand]
[Sound: The echo of hurried footfalls; a breath like wind through rope]

Finn: "Maya." (he tries to smile, but the smile comes out thin) "You okay? Rosa said you'd be here."

You: "I'm here," you say. You reach for him; his hand is warm and steady. "What did you hear at the docks?"

Finn: "People are talking about moving if the buyouts go through. My crew's worried. I patched a net and a neighbor's roof for now, but..." (he trails off) "I don't want to move away from this place, sis. I know it's—

You: (interrupting, because the list of possibilities presses) "—not a single thing to fix." (you think of the neighbor who is gone, of the compass like a hot coin at your throat) "I know."

Finn: "I don't have answers. I just... trust you, Maya." (his voice catches) "Whatever you pick, I trust you to try to keep people here."

Narration: Trust is a fragile, aching thing in the wind. You want to wrap Finn and the rest of the town in something solid, a shelter you can hang from nails and keep calling home. Instead you have plans and models and people whose faith in your shape of thinking is both a gift and a burden.

[Music: Heartbeat-like thrum beneath the cello; the wind rises]
Narration: The three strategies stand before you like paths cut into the same dune. Each one is blunt and real:

Narration: Elias wants mass mobilization — pressure, protest, community-built defenses that force visibility and moral accountability. The risk is escalation, arrests, burned bridges, and the raw, fierce solidarity that might change minds.

Narration: Noah offers mediated negotiation — a fast-track contract for durable infrastructure, with buyouts and rezoning as the price. The risk is displacement, sold heritage, and power ceded to corporate hands in exchange for dry floors.

Narration: Your plan — an independent, community-led evacuation and relocation — would save residents by sacrificing beloved blocks. Hana calls it "strategically traumatic." The risk is fracture: alienating allies, grieving public spaces, and shouldering blame for hard choices.

Narration: You imagine each street in Saltbridge as a hand on a map. Choosing one route is like closing a fist. The muscles ache.

Narration: You look at Elias — his shoulders hunched with the slow wear of organizing. You look at Noah — steady, exact, holding offers like a tray. You think of Hana's blunt clarity and Finn's faith. None of them moves in the same weather. None of them will be entirely satisfied by what you choose.

Narration: The sky presses down. The barricade sighs and flaps. The sea keeps perfect, indifferent time.

[Visual: Close-up — your hand unconsciously tightens on the red thread; the burlap of a sandbag rubs the knuckles raw]
[Music: The cello drops to a single, unresolved low note]

Narration: The decision isn't theoretical anymore. It is a physical thing at the base of your throat. The town you grew up in will be different after you choose. They will forgive you, resist you, love you, or turn their backs. You will be called a hero by some and a traitor by others.

Narration: The tide is coming. You inhale salt and grief together and let the inhale be a page-turn.
