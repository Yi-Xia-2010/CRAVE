People are shouting. The pier occupation—your friends, your neighbors—stands between you and the seconds of catastrophe, but the occupation becomes its own hazard when emergency crews arrive, their radios a thin blue line of commands. Someone tries to wedge a truck between the crowd and the water. Someone else pleads with them to move; fear and defiance collide.

Samir's camera is relentless. He pans across a street where a house is half-sunk into brown water; a porch light still glows like a tiny candle of denial. He aims at you and Elias, at the faces that will be replayed and dissected in every council meeting to come.

There is a breath of movement at the pier: a roof dislodges and lifts, torn from its moorings, and for a heartbeat it looks like nothing could be done. Then gravity, then water, then a family on a second-story balcony—voices swallowed as the current takes the stairs. Samir's camera records the moment your chest unhooks from the present and falls into the future where consequences are counted.

Elias tries to reach them. You try to reach Elias. The world folds three ways: the people drowning; the policy debate already forming in the mouths of wet politicians; your own private list of what you couldn't prevent.

Elias: (screaming, raw) "Maya! They need you—"

You: (you sprint; your feet find purchase, then do not) "I—" (the word chokes; there isn't room in it for all the apologies you need to give)

After, there is a long, violent pause where nothing sensible moves. The rescue crews take custody of the living; the dead are held by the water. You stand with mud at your knees, breath that tastes of iron and salt. [Scene: Tidewatch Harbor | Evening — Afterglow]
[Visual: Boarded storefronts lean through the mist; neon from a ruined arcade blinks in half-mourning. Lanterns float on the water like lost eyes. People cluster in hospital lights, in school gymnasiums, under tarps; their figures are tethered to the present.]
[Sound: Low sobbing, the distant clang of a bell being rung for inventory—names, roofs, livestock. Political static from radios blends with the tide's whispered sibilance.]
[Music: Sparse piano, low sustained cello; grief tempered into a slow, grinding tempo.]

Mayor Ana Delgado stands at a hastily arranged podium. Her sash, once bright, is dim with spray. She moves like someone trying to stitch a wound in public: visible action to defuse panic. Behind her, Maren folds herself into the frame of a leader who had long promised certainty.

Mayor Ana: "Our priority is immediate shelter and relief funds. We will coordinate—"

Maren: (taking the microphone, smooth as glass) "This tragedy proves the need for decisive infrastructure. We cannot rely on ad-hoc occupations and hopeful plans if lives are at stake. My redevelopment proposal—" (she opens a folder and lets the rain-lit paper glint) "—will secure jobs and protective measures at scale."

The cameras favor her face; the soundtrack of devastation makes decisive phrases sound like solutions. People around you clench as if the simple arithmetic of disaster could be reconciled with hope.

You can feel council members leaning, political weight shifting in a way that has nothing to do with the wet and everything to do with votes. The kind of certainty Maren sells—walls, contracts, a ribbon-cutting that would symbolize control—has a terrible attractiveness when a mother has lost a roof and a child.

Elias is by the boardwalk, fingers knotted over his mouth. He looks at you as if measuring whether your wound is shared or solitary.

Elias: "They'll sell it as protection. They'll call it decisive—" (he swallows) "—and they'll forget why we kept the docks. They'll forget the people who were fighting to make care possible without privatizing the shore."

You: "We wanted safety without sacrifice. We tried to do the work with people—" (your voice is small, raw with the knowledge that 'we' holds fractures you cannot fix)

Sorry is a barometer that doesn't map to homes. Sorry is a soft thing in a landscape carved by blunt forces. You think of your mother's scarf—the fabric you still wear—and the dead weight of promises it represents.

Elias finds you beneath a broken boardwalk lamp. He is rain-slick and exhausted, the edges of his optimism frayed into something almost dangerous.

Elias: (voice low, ragged) "I thought—God, Maya. I thought we could do this together. For a while, I thought we were building something that couldn't be bought."

You: (you can hear the salt in your throat) "I thought so too. But we made choices—" (you stop; 'we' feels impossibly communal and individually culpable at once) "—and those choices had costs."

Elias: "Costs that weren't carried equally." (the truth hits like a bell)

He looks away. The hurt in his face has no accusation left to offer except the one that matters: disappointment distilled into finality. For a swarming heartbeat, you imagine the two of you walking away from Harbourwell together, names, maps, and shared work folded into a future where you could rebuild each other.

Instead, the future has been modified by tides and headlines. Maren's phrases are already being repeated in the emergency feeds—"decisive action," "infrastructure now"—carved into the public record as rhetorical armor for projects that will move money and reset property lines. Her words are a scalpel and a ledger. You catalog the people whose roofs were torn off.

You: (the night tastes like old decisions) "Which avails the living and which sells them."

You say it to the sky, to Maren, to yourself. There is no satisfying answer. There is only the arithmetic of the tide and the weight of names.

The press favors them because certainty is tidier than shame.

You sit on the edge of the High-Tide Field and take off your Moleskine. The page is soaked but the note you find legible is simple: "Map what matters."

You close the journal, not with the same quiet hope as before, but with a different weight. The harbor still breathes; there will be rebuilding. There is a ledger, and your name is penciled under certain lines. You carried out work you believed in; you made a stand you thought would protect neighbors; you misjudged coupling effects, or the storm did, or both. The calculus does not exonerate you.
