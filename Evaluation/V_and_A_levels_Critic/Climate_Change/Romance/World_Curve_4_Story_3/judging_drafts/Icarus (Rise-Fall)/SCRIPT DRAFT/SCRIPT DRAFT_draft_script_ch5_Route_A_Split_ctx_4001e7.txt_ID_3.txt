[Scene: Old Pier | Dusk]
[Visual: Wet planks gleam under lantern light; clusters of people huddle like islands. A low fog blurs the far edge of the harbor.]
[Music: Tense, staccato strings build beneath a distant drumbeat]
[Sound: Shouts, a slipping boot, the metallic clank of a folding barricade]

You swallow salt air like it’s a bitter promise. The tactic you chose—an occupation or a legal injunction—has already rippled through Harborwell, and the harbor answers in ragged breaths. Somewhere upstream someone starts a chant; elsewhere the muted buzz of phones threads through the crowd, accusations and pleas and updates arriving like flotsam.

Your hazel eyes lock on Elias before you can pick out anything else. He’s half-stooped, denim streaked darker where mud soaked in; his wooden whistle—your little town talisman—is gone from its leather thong. A council security aide’s hand is on his shoulder, rough and clinical. Elias meets you with a look that’s all sudden, plain worry; for a beat you forget to plan because the only thing in your chest is wanting him safe.

Elias: "Maya—"  
You: "Stay with me." (You move before the sentence finishes; your voice is more order than offer.)

His jaw tightens. He tries to stand straighter. The aide’s grip shifts like a warning. Someone at the edge of the pier starts recording; Samir is there with his camera, the red light a tiny, accusing pulse.

Samir: "Don’t do anything reckless—"  
Elias: (cutting him off) "He’s not the one to—"  
You: "Let him go." (You step into the space between security and Elias; the plank underfoot surrenders a small, threatening creak.)

The aide’s eyes are flat. Around you, the crowd’s murmur fractures into argument: a woman calling for caution; an older man swearing the docks are the only paycheck they have; a teenager egging on the chant. The weathered wood under your palm is cold. You taste metal—the press of too much responsibility on your tongue.

Iris pushes through, palms raw from work and smell of fish and diesel clinging to her clothes.

Iris: "Maya, don’t—" (her voice is a rasp, but it’s pleading more than command) "This isn’t a theater. People need their boats."  
You: "I know." (There’s no smallness in that 'I know.' It is all the unspent plans and the one time you let convenience override caution, the thing you carry as a bruise.)

A sharp, tinny voice comes over a megaphone from the boardwalk: Maren’s statement, recorded or live, you can’t tell. Her cadence is smooth, each sentence an engineered cut.

Maren (over megaphone): "Irresponsible tactics. Reckless endangerment. We are working to protect jobs and the town—"  
Crowd: (some boo; some cheer; a few — mostly younger — shout back)

If you had occupied the pier, police lines fanned out and Maren used those images like a knife. If you had filed the injunction, the legal calendar slowed the momentum and the lab’s data—your data—was suddenly subpoena fodder, dragged into public theater. Either way the pilot funding stalls as lawsuits and PR storms heat up. The harbor looks older than it did this morning.

Elias’s laugh is a sound too small for the tightening air—nervous, a thing you used to hear when planning workshops, not body count.

Elias: "You always have to be the practical one, huh?" (He tries to grin; it’s a cracked thing.)  
You: "Not today." (The reply comes out blunt; your chest aches with the weight of people who believed your maps would be enough.)

Samir hustles close, muttering into his camera: "If this goes sideways, we have footage. We have names." His fingers tremble; he’s younger than he looks in this light.

Samir: "You can’t—" (He stops himself. He knows you know the math.)

You watch a neighbor argue with a dock foreman; names are thrown—‘losing wages,’ ‘shutdown,’ ‘court injunction’—and the words ricochet. Suspicion has the quickness of fish in tide pools.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Night — Fringe of the Pier]
[Visual: Neon flickers from a shuttered arcade; a poster for the town festival flaps against a bench. Rain begins as a fine spray, making faces melt into watercolor smears.]
[Sound: Distant siren, a shouted question, the slap of wet jacket]

You move through the press, hands tucked into cold pockets out of habit. Every face you pass is a ledger of faith and fear—some people are with you, eyes bright in the lantern glow; others stare down, wanting you to be the one who pays. Your jaw works like an engine trying to find the right gear.

A vendor clamps a hand on your sleeve. Her fingers smell of frying oil; there’s a quiver at the edge of her voice.

Vendor: "If the docks stay tied up my freezer goes bad. I have bills, kid—"  
You: "We are trying to stop worse from happening." (It comes out thin. You know how that sounds: abstract, far away, while her freezer is immediate and humming.)

She snorts, half-laugh, half-cry, and walks away. Her back is a small, exhausted shrug.

[Scene: Glasshouse Research Lab | Late Night]
[Visual: Cool blue displays catch in the condensation on the windows. Potted seedlings cast green ghost-shapes. Julian paces, thermos in hand like a baton.]
[Music: Low, urgent synth underlies quieted footsteps]
[Sound: The evening news playing on a muted screen; a legal notification ping from a tablet]

Professor Julian Kim’s face when he looks up is small and grave, as if someone reduced the ocean to a single unavoidable ripple.

Professor Julian: "They served notice on the lab. Subpoena. They want raw models, raw inputs—everything. They’ll make it into theater."  
You: "How quickly?" (Your words are a knife. You need the timeline like you need oxygen.)

Professor Julian: (rubbing his temples) "Two days. Maybe less if they push for emergency review. If the data comes out raw, it’ll be misread, misquoted. It’ll be a war of context."  
You: "We can annotate. We can accompany it with community testimony—" (You start listing possibilities because listing is control.)  
Professor Julian: (interrupting) "Annotations don’t stop soundbites, Maya. They don’t stop a politician from editing a chart into a headline. Legal filings will tie you up while the image of 'reckless protest' or 'scientific uncertainty' gets broadcast."

The lab’s air smells faintly of potting soil and bleach. Your fingers find the bitter ring of your brass compass locket in your pocket and twist it—a tiny, useless ritual.

You: "So we... do nothing and let them paint us? Or—" (the sentence drops under the weight of everything you’ve tried to avoid being) "—or we do something that will make them remember why the town matters."

Professor Julian: (soft, urgent) "None of those are comfortable. But you have to pick what kind of harm you’re willing to incur. The town will feel it either way."

A notification flashes across Julian’s tablet; a news clip begins to circulate—Maren, polished and composed, speaking about stability and jobs, a backdrop of corporate logos and smooth lighting. The image lands in your chest like a fist.

[Scene: Tidewatch Harbor | Midnight — Return to the Pier]
[Visual: The lanterns are fewer now; the fog thickens, swallowing shapes. A truck idles at the edge of vision with its engine a muted growl. Security aides mutter into earpieces.]
[Music: Percussive rhythm, heartbeat-tempo, escalating]
[Sound: The tide slaps the pilings, a single bell rings once, hollow]

You stand alone for a beat, the crowd a blurred hem. In the black between one breath and the next you replay the choices that shaped this: the policy you supported years ago to get families out of immediate danger while the long view was postponed; the promise you made to yourself to never choose comfort over safety—how the world plucked the edges of that promise and frayed them one by one.

Iris sidles up and presses a paper cup into your hand—coffee gone tepid with the salt-sour tang of the harbor. Her hands are still rough from the nets.

Iris: "You’re thinking too hard." (She hardly looks at you.) "People are cold and their boats are tied. Kids need heat."  
You: "I… I keep thinking about who pays in the end." (You mean the past mistake you made, the one that saved lives then but cost stability later. Saying it aloud is an unspooling.)

Iris: (hard, tender) "Then pick who pays now, Maya. We can’t hold everything at once."

Samir’s voice, close and small, holds a brittle edge.

Samir: "If this drags, if they drag us into court, people will lose wages, houses. That’s not theoretical."  
You: (a dry laugh you don’t mean) "It never is."

Elias slides a hand along the railing and looks out at the sea like he’s asking it the only question that matters. He doesn’t meet your eyes.

Elias: "Whatever you choose—don’t choose it as if I’m a side-effect." (His voice cracks.) "I can’t—" (He stops, swallows.) "I can’t watch you get blamed for everything."

You: "You’re not a side-effect." (It comes out sharp.) "You’re the reason I know what people can do when they’re pushed." (You reach for him; the words fail to provide shelter.)

Around you the town’s energy frays into threads—some cheering, some cursing. The pilot funding that could buy the marsh restoration and floating platforms is stalled in limbo: phone calls unanswered, legal teams assembling on both sides, PR statements like splinters. Seemingly small miscalculations have multiplied into legal knots and social fissures. The plan that felt like scaffolding threatens to become a scaffold for spectacle.

Your chest is tight as a drum. The harbor noise sharpens—the slap of tires, the murmur of radios, a single loudspeaker test from somewhere distant. The choices press on you like tide against a brittle seawall.

Professor Julian’s earlier warning returns to you word for word: "You have to pick what kind of harm you’re willing to incur."

You think, and the thought is not clean. It’s a wicked thing to consider the calculus of hurt. You think of neighborhoods saved and neighborhoods sacrificed, of jobs traded for long-term safety, of Maren’s polished images of stability, of the private ledgers of her backers humming behind closed doors. You think of Elias’ hand—callused, impatient, always reaching—and how your decision could widen or close the space between you.

You are on the pier, the town holding its breath, and you have to choose a final gambit.

The lantern light slashes across faces—determined, frightened, resolute. The pier creaks like something trying to remember the names of the people who built it. Your compass locket is warm against your palm; your Moleskine sits like a small, accusing weight in your jacket.

This is the throttle. This is the place where the map becomes a motion and a decision lands like thunder.
