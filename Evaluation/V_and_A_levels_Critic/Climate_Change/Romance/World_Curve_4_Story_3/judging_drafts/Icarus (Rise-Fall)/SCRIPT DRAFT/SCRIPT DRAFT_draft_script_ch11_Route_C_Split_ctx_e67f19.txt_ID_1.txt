You are here because the platform rose; because hands cheered; because someone wrote a check with terms in the fine print. The papers lie between you and a future you had hoped to sculpt—white margins crowded with clauses that feel like a tideline etched onto a house: where the water will reach, who pays, who decides.

Your fingers curl around the edge of a contract. The paper is too warm from being held; a coffee ring mars the header. You read the clauses for the third time and your chest tightens like a fist. They speak in legal sunlight—"development rights," "priority parcels," "governance panels with external representation"—polite words for something that will rearrange how the town breathes.

Philanthropic Representative: (smiling, smooth) "Maya, this structure ensures longevity. Our partners bring capital, supply chains, access. We want to see Harborwell succeed; these terms are standard for scale."

You inhale; the lab smells of wet earth and disinfectant. The sound of waves under glass, your watch ticking tide data—small, steady—against the thrum in your throat.

You: "Standard for whom? When you say 'priority parcels,' do you mean entire neighborhoods will be earmarked for commercial redevelopment?"

Philanthropic Representative: (tilting head) "Priority parcels mean efficient service delivery. The mixed-use parcels—commercial hubs—allow local co-ops to subcontract. It's a win-win; you keep the co-ops involved and we secure long-term maintenance."

You: "Subcontracting isn't the same as agency." Your voice is quieter than you intend. "It sounds like a plan that keeps jobs—temporarily—but cedes decision-making to interests with no stake in our lived lives."

Philanthropic Representative: (hands steepled) "Maya, you understand the difference between idealism and feasibility. You wanted scale. This is scale."

A reporter's laugh filters from a distant corridor—someone polishing the version of events they will sell as a triumph. Your watch buzzes with a message from Samir. The buzzing is a small electric pulse that summons the harbor's memory: last week's floods, the way the boardwalk heaved like a living thing.

You: "I read it. They call it 'governance support.'"

Elias Hart: (bitter laugh) "Governance support. They mean control. They mean the co-ops will be subcontractors for a developer who'll own the waterfront. They'll take our stories and repurpose them as advertisements."

You feel a cold opening inside your sternum, a place you thought you'd filled with data and diagrams. The harbor's wind whips at Elias' hair; his eyes are ocean-green and furious.

You: (careful) "They brought funding, Elias. Without it, the projects stall. People will lose jobs now."

Elias Hart: (voice near breaking) "People are already losing jobs when they can't fish. This isn't about jobs if the jobs come with strings that choke the rest out. You promised—"

His sentence fractures. You keep your mouth closed because the rest of the town crowds the space between each word: the single parent who patched nets until dawn, the aging fisherfolk who remembered when the sea yielded more and gave less, the small bakery that reopened after the flood because neighbors pooled faith.
