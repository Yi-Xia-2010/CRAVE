## Visual Novel Title
Harborwell Rising

---

## World View
Harborwell is a mid-sized coastal town in 2035, a place where the sea is both livelihood and menace. Salt-scarred storefronts and thrifted signage cluster around Tidewatch Harbor; the town breathes in storms and exhales reluctant resilience. Political power is split between municipal technocrats and emergent grassroots coalitions; Mayor Ana Delgado occupies a fragile center while Maren Voss channels institutional authority and private capital into large-scale redevelopment. Local fishers, co-ops, and community organizers — led by Elias Hart — press for adaptation rooted in local knowledge. Scientific institutions such as the Glasshouse Research Lab bring technical legitimacy but limited political capital; Professor Julian Kim's research plucks at the frayed thread between evidence and policy.

Culture mixes nostalgia and urgency: festival banners fade at the boardwalk, rooftop gardens attempt to reclaim failing marshes, and the abandoned sea wall serves as a weathered ledger of past bets. Class divides shape exposure — long-time residents like Iris Tanaka and the Reyes family live proximity to risk; new investments promise jobs but threaten community fabric. Religion and formal ideology are muted; civic religion here is stewardship of place. Geography is intimate and precarious: the tide and salt dictate daily rhythms. Atmosphere is wind-washed, fog-diffused, and tension-laden, where romance and responsibility intersect with climate choice.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I play Maya Reyes, a coastal resilience planner returned to Harborwell to help her hometown survive rising tides. The narrative follows an Icarus arc: we rise quickly through a string of tactical successes — galvanizing citizens, winning concessions, and attracting attention — but those gains precipitate a catastrophic reversal as political pressure, economic desperation, and one storm expose the fragility of short-term fixes. Romance with Elias Hart threads through choices that bind my heart to community tactics; Maren Voss offers institutional solutions that promise security but demand harsh trade-offs. My past guilt — a miscalculation that once prioritized relief over resilience — colors every choice. Across branching decisions, I must choose between grassroots adaptation, institutional compromise, or opportunistic funding tied to moral costs. Each route builds hope and momentum (the rise) then collapses into consequential loss (the fall), yielding outcomes that range from a morally honest, difficult survival to a painful exile. The emotional core is the tension between love and the long-term duty to Harborwell's most vulnerable.
