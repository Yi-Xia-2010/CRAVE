The room smells of ozone and salt and something burned—paper singed at the edges of your notebook where a drip found purchase. Your palms are wet, not just from the spray but from the sweat that has nothing to do with cold. The air presses against your lungs like a hand asking for more than you can give.

You were thinking in ledger-weights: thresholds, fail-safes, who gets called, who gets forgiven. That lifetime of calculus condenses now into a single, terrible instruction: hold the circuit closed long enough for the remote handshake to finish, or the panel tears open and the flow cascades. The math is clean in the mind you carry—the kind that learned to measure risk and accept the balances. The ethics are not clean. They stick like salt.

Mina is a blur of motion, voice high and tight. "First-aid kits, now. Rafi, film the routing. Sora, strings on the exits—people, out, now!" Her hands move in the air as if knitting the room into safety; you can almost see the network of care she always makes visible.

Rafi's recorder clicks; the camera light bobs like a small, stubborn beacon. He is up against the handshakes his profession offers: witness, record, name. His breath is quick. "This is... this is live," he says, voice narrower than you're used to. "We have—" He swears, the technical phrase choking to a human sound. "We're getting signal loss on sector four. More water."

Elias is at the lip of the tilting panel. He looks at the mechanism the way he used to look at a stubborn storm drain in your neighborhood: with impatience and a quiet certainty that if enough hands tried, it would be fixed. He reaches for a cable, then withdraws because it sparks blue and hisses. For a second he looks at you in a way that breaks the professional calculus into small, jagged pieces—something raw and unedited.

Elias Chen: (voice raw) "Go. Please—get out. We'll—" His sentence ends on nothing, the grammar eaten by the noise.

You feel your boots slide. A record of all the ways you've learned to stand comes flooding back—sea-anchored feet, toes jammed against wet concrete, a stubborn muscle memory. One boot throws you a fraction; your balance is a promise you can no longer keep. The panel tilts more. A fail-safe requires human contact; a mass to keep the switch depressed against the shear of seawater pushing through a torn pipe.

Rafi: "If we lose this, the barrier cascade—it's not just us. The Beacon's buffer will fail. People upstream—" He swallows. "They'll lose their shelters."

Mina: "Then we move them first. We can't—" Her jaw sets. "We can't make heroes of ourselves."

Sora stands near the stairwell, hands on the staff that has steadied generations. She watches you like a vessel being inspected; her face is unreadable in the half-light, a history you have always been able to read only in fragments. Age has taught her to hold grief like an instrument, careful and precise. For a long beat she says nothing.

Sora Watanabe: (soft, unyielding) "Aiko—remember the old ropes. Decide where the ledger goes. Choose the sentence you will accept."

There is a small, ridiculous memory in your mind of ledger pages fluttering in the Beacon's lamplight like leaves, and you want to laugh because what you have is not a ledger so much as a scrap that will be read after you are done speaking.

Your body knows what the instrument requires. Your head catalogues the outcomes with cold, administrative efficiency: if you stay, the handshake completes; remote handlers can lock the mechanism and prevent the surge; the system might survive. If you pull everyone out, you prioritize life but risk a cascade that will displace hundreds. If you attempt a manual override with the team, maybe you buy enough time—maybe you don't.

The room narrows to the percussion of choices. Each breath is measurable, each blink a calculation. You want to think of every person in the neighborhood and the ledger they have contributed: Mina's jars of soup stacked like promises, Sora's oral maps of the older tides, Rafi's recordings that name the harms. You also want to think of Elias' laugh, reckless as wind through rope. You cannot hold both the ledger and the laughter without tearing something.

Elias reaches out, fingers grazing your cheek—too quick to be a goodbye, too intentional to be nothing. His eyes are complicated, a mix of pleading and fierce adoration and that impatience that made him move whole communities with a single slogan. He does not ask you to die. He asks you to choose him.

Elias Chen: (whisper) "I love you. Don't make me—" He cannot finish.

Your throat closes. Your mind, trained on models and probabilities, fractures along the edges of what love wants and what duty requires. The sea is loud enough to drown out the rest of the world. The clock buried in your chest moves with clarifying cruelty.

You notice the small things—the way Rafi's camera light stutters, the smell of Mina's scarf trailing of roasted garlic and lemon from the community kitchen, the sting of seawater in your eyes. The world is a collage of textures you know intimately: the rough braid of Elias' rope bracelet, the salt-splattered sleeve of your jacket, the smooth metal of the panel warmed by sparks.

You find the failsafe handle with your other hand; it is slick and warm, vibrating with electricity. Your grip finds the place where human flesh must meet engineering to keep a machine from unmaking so many lives. The thought of your notebook—the ledger you carry—flashes, a cluster of pages that will be read after. Rafi's camera catches your face for a fraction of a second; his lens finds something you cannot name and hold.

Mina: (voice cracking, practical) "Aiko—if you stay, we get witnesses. Rafi will get this on record. We will carry the story. If you go—" Her voice breaks and then repairs. "If you go, you get to be with us. Choose what the ledger remembers."

Elias: (sharper now, trying to make a bargain with sound) "We can do the manual override. We can—together. We won't lose you to a statistic."

You think of the ledger's future fingers—legal hands, protest signs, quiet meals thrown in backyards for remembrance. You think of how words become policy and how policy becomes the shape of neighborhoods. You think, like a map, of futures layered upon futures.

The alarm above you pulses a red rhythm. The remote console flickers: handshake pending, thirty seconds and counting—twenty-nine, twenty-eight. The models on the monitors bloom like painful flowers—red zones spiking, threads of predicted flow. Your breath is a tool now, metronomic, measured.

Sora grips the staff with both hands and says something you can only hear as a vibration against the bones of your teeth: "What will you let be your end?"

The panel gives a louder groan. Cables snap and whip, arcing blue light like a storm's teeth. Your footwear slips again. The room tilts a fraction, gravity a new author writing quick sentences across wet concrete. Around you, voices thin to a chorus.

Rafi: (low, steady) "People will watch this. They'll decide what happens next. Whatever you choose—" He chokes on the next word. "—they'll record it."

You feel Elias' fingers again, softer this time, as if he is asking permission rather than pulling you away. Mina's hands are on your elbow. Sora's eyes are steady and old as the coastline. The world contracts to the span between your thumb and the panel. The timer, indifferent, still counts.
