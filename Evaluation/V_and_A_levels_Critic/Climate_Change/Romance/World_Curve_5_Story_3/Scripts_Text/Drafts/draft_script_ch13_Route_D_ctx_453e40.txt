### DRAFT
# Route: Route D


[Scene: City Streets | Midnight]
[Visual: Narrow streets slick with recent rain; banners tied between lamp posts ripple in a salt breeze]
[Music: Tense, driving percussion — a steady heartbeat under a rising synth]
[Sound: Muffled chants in the distance, the snap of fabric, boots on wet concrete]

Narration:
You move like a current through the crowd — not the loudest voice, but the one others orient toward. The city is awake on its edge: a ribbon of people threading down the promenade, hands full of rope, paint, and a hundred small, useful tools. Neon reflections sheared across puddles. The air tastes of salt and adrenaline; it clings to your throat.

You can feel the shift already. This is no polite meeting at the Beacon. This is a pickup of momentum — a decision made visible. You did not choose the cautious ledger. You chose to make the ledger unreadable to those who would rewrite it behind closed doors.

[Visual: Close on your hands tying a banner; your field jacket sleeves are damp, the silver streak at your temple flattened with rain]
[Sound: Elias' laughter, quick and bright, behind you; a camera shutter clicks like a small drum]

Elias: "Look at this, Aiko — they're calling us a 'threat' in the feed. Cute. Gives us drama points." (he grins, but his fingers are steady as he helps knot the banner)

You (Aiko Mori): "Keep the camera steady, or it'll be more dramatic than we need." (you don't let go of the rope) "We need to control the message. Not spin it — the facts. The people, the data, the contracts."

Elias: "Facts, then theater. Got it." (he bounces on his heels) "We'll make sure someone streams the contracts being read out loud. People should see the clauses that promise whole neighborhoods to HelioCorp."

Narration:
His optimism is oxygen. It spreads. Mina moves through the group like heat; her apron has been swapped for a windbreaker tonight, but she still smells faintly of broth and basil — comfort to anchor the agitation. Rafi skirts along the curb, recorder out, eyes flicking through his notes. Sora stands a little apart, hands wrapped around a steaming thermos, watching the younger faces as if they were seedlings in a storm.

Mina: (quiet, practical) "We need med stations at every corner. If the city sweeps, people will need exits and water. No one gets left behind because someone's nerves frayed."

Rafi: (low, urgent) "I've got the contractor schedules. Two deployment crews rotating the east barrier. If we delay one day, we can force a public hearing long enough to slow them down."

Narration:
You digest their voices like quick data. Delay is a lever. Exposure is a wedge. Sabotage is a last resort; a public wedge can make that last resort unnecessary. But you've bled a different kind of patience. Sitting at the Beacon with memos in your hand felt like being a museum docent to disaster — cataloging, but not stopping progress that would hollow communities out. Tonight, the ledger is no longer enough. Tonight, the pen becomes a picket.

[Visual: Banners unfurl — hand-painted slogans about commons, about sea rights; a silhouetted HelioCorp logo crossed out with green paint]
[Sound: The chant rising — a layered chorus, not rehearsed but fierce]

Narration:
You pick up both roles in sequence — first call, then map. You are at home in the small, practical gestures that make large things possible: a tied knot, a mapped alley, a kept promise. There's a dangerous elegance to that. The city's teeth show for a moment as municipal lights sweep; the police presence has edged up since the afternoon. You can feel the weight of helmets like future headlines.

Elias (leaning in, voice lowered): "If they try to run a crane tonight, we stand in front. If they try to plug a pipe, we clog the access. We make it costly to pretend the city can ignore us."

You (soft, straining): "Costly for who, though? I won't let us become what they're called. We don't sabotage lives. We obstruct tech deployments — the infrastructure, not the people."

Elias: "Agree. Infrastructure first. People always first." (his hand squeezes yours; the gesture is both promise and accusation)

Narration:
He looks at you — that look is complex and open. It holds hunger and fear and something like worship. You return his squeeze in public, but your head is already a dozen steps ahead: the data pipelines that feed HelioCorp's models, the subbasement nodes where tidal models get translated into directives.

[Scene: Old Beacon | Shortly After Midnight]
[Visual: Lantern light warms cracked stone; a fold-out map is spread on a tarp, marked in bright pins]
[Music: A taut string section underlines whispered plans]
[Sound: Wind through the ruined lantern room, a distant siren that resolves into the city's breathing]

Narration:
The Beacon is where plans are made honest. You set the map under your palms like a ritual. Sora comes close, the thermos held between both hands as if balancing history. Her face, mapped with decades of storms, is unreadable for a beat.

Sora: "This coast has birthed people and lost them and borne them again. You must ask — what do you carry forward?" (she pauses, eyes flicking to yours) "Anger can be fuel. It must also be framed into what the land and the people need."

You: "We won't burn the village to stop a company." (you say it like a vow) "We will break machines that take land, not lives. We will break access to the contracts, the data feeds, the central nodes that let them overrule community voice."

Sora: (a soft exhale) "Then make sure your hands remember how to heal. Sabotage that can't be undone must be weighed like a grave."

Narration:
Mina folds a stack of small bandages into a waxed tin and tucks it into your palm without looking at you. A gesture as practical as any agreement. It steadies you.

Mina: "If you're going to go under, Rafi and I will be your eyes. He'll feed you open-source feeds; I'll feed folks. No one goes cold tonight."

Rafi: (with a rough laugh) "Don't make me poetic, Mina. But yes — I can loop the city cams long enough to hide a drift, and I've got a line on an old utilities hatch near the subbasement lab. It hasn't been used in years."

You: "We won't do this blindly. We map the human flows first. We stage exits, we post med teams, we make every action reversible if we can."

Narration:
You lay out the plan: a banner drop at the East Barrier to draw press; a coordinated sit-in at the contractor staging lot; a small team — quiet, technical — to go underground at the utilities hatch to disrupt the data pipeline that would authorize deployments. Your voice is crisp, the cadence of someone who has built models and can see where action ripples.

Narration:
He wants to be visible; he also wants to be effective. You decide — momentarily — to split roles: Elias will lead the public wave; your small, trusted few will go to the hatch. The sub-basement is a mechanical throat; you picture valves and routers as if they were organs. You imagine placing a hand over them and insisting they breathe a different pattern.

Elias (quiet, half-joking): "Promise me one thing."

You: "What?"

Elias: "Don't make me the hero who gets arrested on a rooftop because he couldn't help but climb higher."

You: (a short laugh despite everything) "Promise is a dangerous word. But yes. No rooftop heroics."

Elias: "Good. Because I'm tired of funerals — of harbors and of men who think they can decide for everyone."

Narration:
His conviction is raw and brittle in the same breath. You sense the border of collapse and courage. In your chest, something bright and hot swells — not unlike the tide pooling behind a barrier before it breaks. Very positive, you think. Very human.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab — Exterior | 1:30 AM]
[Visual: Service grates and rusted frames; the city's hum is a low electric ache]
[Music: Percussive ticks, urgency increasing — a metronome that speeds]
[Sound: The click of your respirator, distant voices, a van idling then driving off]

Narration:
Rafi's directions are clean and merciless. The hatch is old, half-swallowed by a pile of seaweed-stiffened debris. Your hands — steady from years of wiring and charts — find the seam. Someone brushes your shoulder: Mina, offering a damp cloth and a fistful of energy paste.

Mina: (low, fierce) "Remember why. Remember who's under this." (her voice trembles, but only barely) "If there's a choice between stopping the machine and hurting someone, choose to stop the machine another way."

You: "Always."

Narration:
You do not know the passwords to the lab. You don't need to. The goal is simple and immediate: disrupt the automatic authorization stream that converts HelioCorp's models into deployment orders. You will not be smashing servers for spite; you will be breaking the pipeline that allows unaccountable decisions.

Rafi: (softly) "There's a timed relay between their model compute and the city's barrier control. We can fuzz that relay. We delay them. We force oversight time."

You: "Delay becomes a public window. We buy hearings. We buy witnesses."

Rafi: "And in that window, the public pressure makes prosecutions more visible. They can't quietly sign homes away."

Narration:
You crawl through the hatch into stale air and the smell of oil and old paper. The subbasement is compact, a rumbling chest of servers and ductwork. The LEDs are a constellation of arrogant certainty — teal and red badges of calculation. Your fingers find a junction box and the cable loom; the plan is surgical.

[Sound: A faint radio at the surface — someone is chanting. Distant applause. Then, the sudden metallic scrape of a boot on the stair above the hatch]

Narration:
High arousal snaps into sharpness. The stair creaks; a shadow passes the grate. Your throat tightens, but your hands do not stop. There is no panic, only quick, precise pressure. You reroute a single feed through a small homemade shunt — enough to scramble the timestamping, to make the deployment require manual confirmation. Not destruction. A bureaucratic jam.

You: "Hold the line up there. If the city comes down, we execute Plan B — banners in the way of vehicles, humans between cranes and the shoreline."

Elias (voice through the comm, a low roar over the muffled sounds of a crowd): "They're trying to push a truck down the lot. Ten officers at the gate. We're right in their eyes."

You: (a breath) "Then be visible. Be louder."

Narration:
You finish the rewire. An LED hesitates, flickers — then reasserts itself. Nothing dramatic explodes. The servers keep humming like a caged sea. But the timestamps stutter; an authorization fails to reconcile. In the lab, a soft console chimes an alert. Outside, the contractor's crew frowns at a suddenly paused deployment app. Eyes pass to phones. The network slows. Something in the city's hulking machine hiccups.

[Sound: A swell of cheering from the plaza; a heavy, triumphant beat under the chant]
[Music: Swells to a brief, exultant chord; then undercurrent returns — tension maintained]

Narration:
For a breath, the city listens to you. The air in the subbasement tastes metallic and sweet. You feel triumph — a clean, almost sacred warmth. This is not vindictive; it is corrective. You have given the public time to see the clauses, to talk, to decide.

Then the radio picks up a new thread: sirens splitting the night, voices shouting through megaphones, and over it all a new, colder tone: a HelioCorp liaison insisting on "restoration of network integrity" — the phrase a diplomatic sop to a shove. The company's logo flashes on news feeds, damage control already narrating their side.

Rafi: (breathing hard) "They're calling it sabotage. The contractors are framing it as anti-technology violence. We need the documents and the crowd — now. If we flood the feeds with the contracts and the city's own memos, we can flip the narrative."

You: "Do it. We amplify the ledger we refused to write under their lights. We fill the air with facts — with people."

Narration:
You feel it — the crowd a living algal bloom around the Beacon, swelling with a hope that is audacious and immediate. It's very positive: people standing for a thing greater than themselves and taking the responsibility of action. Your chest is tight with it, with a satisfaction that this night meant something tangibly changing.

But beneath the satisfaction, darker ironies hum. The city does not like being forced into the spotlight. Contracts hide in plain sight; powerful people rewrite urgency into necessity. Your action pushed the needle, but needles move back when pressed.

[Visual: A red alert flash on Rafi's screen; a municipal cruiser turns the corner fast]
[Sound: The crack of a megaphone; orders being barked; a crowd's murmur hardening into a focused roar]

Elias: (on comms, voice raw with effort) "They're moving on the barrier. They have police lined. Some of our people are getting shoved."

You: (sharp) "Hold them. Keep them recording. Push the camera to the lawyers in the crowd. Get Mina to shepherd the med teams. If they arrest — we need witnesses and legal names."

Mina: "We're already moving the kits. People stay on the edges. No baiting the police. We don't want a martyr — not tonight."

Narration:
You want to believe that you can control the momentum you've unleashed. You want to believe your moral architecture will hold in a storm of human tempers and corporate muscle. The feeling is very positive — a conviction that people can act for the common good and hold themselves accountable even in the spill of adrenaline.

Yet the high arousal tightens: sirens grow, lights glare, and the municipal presence sharpens like a blade. The city's breath becomes shallow and quick. You stand in the subbasement for a long second, listening to the chorus above and the dull hum below, aware that every choice from here curves forward into consequence.

Page-turn thought:
You have given them a delay and a spectacle. You have handed the public the ledger and the light. The city will decide whether to look. You will decide, too, in the next moments, how far you will press that gaze.

[Music: A single, charged motif resolves into a held minor chord; tension coiled, ready]
