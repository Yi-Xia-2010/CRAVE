[Scene: Tideward Promenade | Afternoon — Two days before the forum]
[Background:"ch3_98c6f2_2"]
[Music: Light acoustic guitar — steady tempo]
[Sound: Water lapping against improvised levees, distant laughter from children planting beds]

You meet Rafi at the edge of the Promenade, where the sea smells brine-heavy and the banners feel like flags raised against forgetting. He keeps his voice low; there is a journalist's hunger under the caution.

Rafi [tapping his recorder]: "HelioCorp's presentation tonight is polished for a reason. The models are persuasive. But their legal team—there are clauses that concern me. Exclusivity, eminent-control language layered in thin print."

You feel your jaw set. Words like 'eminent-control' and 'exclusivity' land like stones. They don't explode the room immediately, but they change its acoustics.

Aiko Mori: "Show me," 
you say. "Let me see the wording. If there is a way they put hardware over governance, we make that visible."

Rafi slides a photo from his sleeve—an angled shot of a draft contract, small type and confident signatures. The letters on the page are clinical, patient.

Rafi [soft]: "They can buy a pilot and lock out others. They say 'urgent' and 'scale' and they mean it. You know what urgent looks like; you also know what irreversible looks like."

Mina hovers nearby, watching the exchange. Her expression is complex—neither agreement nor dissent, just a knot of loyalties and fears that make you ache for a solution that keeps both life and dignity.

Elias Chen [rushing in]: "We can't be slow. We can't be polite while they decide where our people will live." He paces, a tremor of energy. "We pull the crowd, we chant, we force transparency publicly. Make them answer in the light."

You hear the heat in his words, and you imagine the atrium glass reflecting a throng of neighborhood faces. There is righteousness in that image—potent and immediate.

Aiko Mori: "And I imagine something complementary," 
you counter, steadying the tempo. "We bring the crowd, Elias. We also bring the audit trail. We bring the open models. We make their promises verifiable in the same room."

Rafi nods slowly. "If you can get the data stream open—if it goes live and citizens can see it—that shifts power."

You think of the Subbasement Data Lab then, a place you visit when the city is tired and the machines hum like second lungs. This is where numbers become maps people can touch.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Late Afternoon — The day before the forum]
[Background:"ch3_98c6f2_3"]
[Music: Low electronic hum with an undercurrent of piano — work and warmth]
[Sound: Fans, the soft click of keyboards, the occasional beep of incoming packets]

You descend into the lab with a thermos and a purpose. The air is cool and damp; condensation beads on the concrete like a reminder of the sea above. Monitors throw teal light across your face, and for a moment you are the person who makes prediction legible.

You sit, fingers steady, and begin to build the presentation you promised: graphs that demystify, a simple live feed that could show wave heights and barrier performance in near real-time. Your goal is straightforward and stubborn—make the pilot impossible to monopolize.

Mina brings soup down and sets it beside you. She watches a tidal graph for longer than you expect, and when she speaks it's as if the numbers are also stories.

Mina: "If they want to know how the kitchen fits into plans—show them. If your data leaves out doors and meals and who tends a bed, they will make decisions that don't see us."

You add a layer to the presentation: community-maintenance logs, a patchwork of names and times that tie technology to people. It is small, human, and it warms the sterile feed in a way Rowan's models will not.

You rehearse until your voice is hoarse, until each slide is a hinge that opens into something accountable. In the lab's dim light you feel, for the first time that week, a confidence that is more than hope—a practical readiness.

[Scene: HelioCorp Glass Atrium | Evening — Forum Night]
[Background:"ch3_98c6f2_4"]
[Music: Crisp orchestral flourish that underlines confidence]
[Sound: Murmured applause, the rustle of brochures, the distant hiss of climate-control]

You step into the atrium and the air shifts. It smells faintly of citrus disinfectant and polished metal; the light is controlled so that nothing surprises the eye. HelioCorp's displays shine with a kind of perfected calm: animated renderings of modular sea-shield arrays unfurling like mechanical reefs; a cloud-seeding adjunct illustrated as a halo that could soften storms.

Dr. Rowan Hale [smiling, precise]: "We do not offer miracles. We offer engineered mitigation—scalable, rapid, and backed by the latest modelling.With modular sea-shields and targeted atmospheric interventions, we can blunt the force of surges and buy communities time."

The visuals are slick; the crowd leans in. People who have known storms their whole lives make small sounds of wonder. You feel it too—the immediate relief of a concrete promise—then anchor yourself. This is where the ledger of the Beacon meets the ledger of HelioCorp.

Aiko Mori [quiet, to Elias Chen]: "Focus on public questions. When they present numbers, ask where oversight sits. Ask about access—who can see the data?"

Elias Chen nods, eyes bright. He nudges you, a private signal: the crowd is receptive. He wants to stand up during Q&A and name the grief that raised him—he wants theatricality and truth.

Rowan's presentation shifts to timelines and deployment schedules. He places a hand on the mock module projected in hologram, and the audience can almost feel the weight of it.

Dr. Rowan Hale [firm]: "We intend to offer pilot programs to neighborhoods that demonstrate readiness. We will fund deployment and maintenance during the pilot phase."

Murmurs ripple: funding sounds like manna; for a city on the knife-edge of tides, funding is oxygen.

From the side, Mina watches with an expression you cannot read. It is, by design, unreadable—layered with relief, fear, and calculation. She catches your eyes for a beat and something unspoken passes—someone who cooks, plants, and keeps watch, weighing the warmth of soup against the cold of displacement.

Rafi leans close to you, breath warm with urgency.

Rafi [whispering]: "Listen to the contract language. They say 'pilot'—but in other cities, 'pilot' became 'dominant platform.' 'Maintenance.' 'Exclusive deployment.'"

You remember the draft he'd shown and the thin type that promised too much power.

The Q&A opens. Hands rise and fall; some are raw with loss, others polished with institutional discipline.

Elias Chen is first to the microphone—he speaks of neighborhoods, of nets and meals and small things that stop families from leaving.

Elias Chen: "We don't need solutions that make us invisible. We need systems that amplify our voices, not replace them."

Dr. Rowan Hale smiles, practiced and not unkind.

Dr. Rowan Hale: "Agreed. Our models incorporate local participation. We imagine partnership."

The word lands like a bridge. It is brightly lit and appears to connect two shores.

Then Rafi stands, slightly shaking but steady. He asks—direct, journalistic—about control, about data access, about whether HelioCorp intends to hold veto over local decisions during deployment.

Dr. Rowan Hale [smooth]: "We will require certain guarantees to ensure system integrity. That is industry standard."

Your chest tightens, not with despair but with the familiar, workable anger of someone who knows the levers of a system. You see an opening: the pilot can be a lever in your hands if you design the fulcrum right.

You step forward when a lull comes. The atrium light catches the line of your jaw.

Aiko Mori [measured]: "We appreciate funding and engineering support. But our terms are non-negotiable: public data streams; community oversight; explicit clauses preventing unilateral control. If HelioCorp wants a pilot, you will agree to transparent metrics and an independent audit."

Rowan's eyes narrow—just enough to be human, not enough to be unkind.

Dr. Rowan Hale [inclining his head]: "We can discuss oversight. However, our deployment models depend on unimpeded maintenance windows. Too many stakeholders can delay response. Who ultimately holds the authority in an emergency?"

The room hums. It's a practical question that is also a philosophical toothpick—used to probe structure.

Elias Chen bristles. "You can't explain away people as delays," he says heatedly. "People are the fastest responders we have."

Dr. Rowan Hale [calm]: "People are vital. Also, coherence is vital. We must balance."

You feel the scale of the choice: urgent action versus distributed governance. It is the hinge on which your romantic life pivots as much as community power does—Elias's insistence on immediacy; your insistence on systems that don't erase people.

Mina steps up to the mic, voice smaller but fierce in its clarity.

Mina: "If your models don't account for the ones who boil soup at midnight, they will fail the ones who matter first."

Dr. Rowan Hale [softening]: "I acknowledge that. Our designs are not blind to community needs. We'll integrate local maintenance teams."

Rafi, not satisfied, presses: "And the clauses that allow for eminent-control during declared emergencies? How will you prevent that from becoming permanent?"

Dr. Rowan Hale's smile remains, but there is a sharpness now. "Emergency provisions exist for a reason," he says. "When infrastructure fails lives are at stake. We cannot have a system delayed because of contested jurisdictions."

His logic is ruthless in its simplicity; it is also salving to the immediate fear in the room. The crowd's emotional tide rises with the promise of safety. You can see faces soften, a breath exhaled.

Inside you, however, a different calculation takes shape: the ledger you keep is made of neighbors' names and kitchens and ten-year plans. The offer on the table is fast, funded, and carrying a seat at Rowan's table—if you accept it with conditions you shape the rules; if you refuse, you maintain autonomy but risk being outflanked.

Your heart is not frantic; it is purposeful. The decision will define the next step not only for policy but for whom you love and how you love them: a practical partnership with a technocrat, the unvarnished street-level movement Elias embodies, or something that threads both.

You picture the Subbasement Data Lab's live feed, Mina's maintenance logs, and a clause that says "open access." You imagine how a public pilot could be an instrument of empowerment if oversight is written in the light.

The moderator leans into the mike and offers a pause. The room waits like a held breath.
