[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Midmorning — Fall]
[Visual: Wide shot of the plaza under a low, rain-thick sky. Banners ripple along the railings; umbrellas cluster like a field of dark mushrooms. Etta stands near a makeshift podium; Mateo's Wave Lab booth is a scatter of prototypes under a tarp; Elio's suited reps hand out glossy pamphlets. Councilor Rosa watches from the municipal steps, a measured presence among the press of bodies.]
[Music: Tense BGM — strings interlock with a rising percussion]
[Sound: Murmuring crowd, distant gull cries, rain tapping on canvas]

You step into the plaza and the air tightens around you — salt, wet wool, the bitter tang of someone’s thermos coffee. The photograph you tucked away in your tote feels heavier than the camera did earlier; it's a weight of memory and proof, but right now your lungs taste of adrenaline.

Voices fold over each other: chant, counter-offer, the low hum of diesel from a corporate generator at Elio's booth. Each sound is an argument made audible.

Etta turns when she hears your steps. Up close, her hands are soil-dark and steady; the sea-glass beads at her throat catch the gray light. She does not need to say your name for you to know that her look asks for alignment — not only political, but moral. The crowd seems to lean on that look as if it could tip a scale.

Etta: "Ari. Thank you for coming." (her voice rough with weather and worry) "They need to hear someone who knows the ground."

You: "I brought the recordings, the mapping." (you feel the words tangle; you force calm) "We can show them the sediment losses, where the marsh will collapse next winter if we don't act."

Etta: (a soft, sharp laugh) "Show them. Then ask them to choose people over pavement."

Her tone is fierce but frayed. Behind her, a cluster of neighbors — fishermen, teachers, young parents with damp scarves — nod or fold their arms. Their faces are the townsfolk you promised to keep in mind when you started this campaign. Their fatigue is as alive as their resolve; it presses on your ribs.

A man near Elio's table holds up a pamphlet with a smiling rendering of a seawall and a shiny promenade. The image is sterile: clean lines, tidy lighting, no reed grass, no patched boots. A corporate rep offers it to you with a polished smile.

Corporate Rep: "Fast, effective protection. Jobs during construction. Long-term stability. Open to public-private partnership—"

You: (you hear the tautness under his words) "—and displacement, often." You do not say it aloud at first; the thought cuts off as if you spoke it in the marrow. You know what managed relocation has meant in other towns, and you know the look of promises that come with buyouts.

Mateo appears beside his booth, sleeves rolled, hands full of damp sensor housings. He doesn’t approach with the performative distance of a pitchman; there’s a tired set to his shoulders, as if every prototype has been carrying him since he came back. When he sees you, something in his face shifts — brief, unreadable. There is warmth there, and also the silent calculation of someone whose work might hinge on your words.

Mateo: (low) "Ari. I— I can cut the demo down, show the buoy array working in a real swell. It's not just hardware; it's a tool to keep people here and power small pumps when they need it." (he tap-taps his notebook, fingers jittery) "If we can prove community-scale deployment, we can get microgrants, municipalities won't need to sell the waterfront."

You: "I want to believe that, Mateo. You know that." (your voice is steadier than you feel) "But I've seen the promises that begin as 'community-friendly' and end up with fences."

Mateo: (fast) "Then help me keep it from becoming that. Help me write the community clauses. Make the pilot participatory. You know the people who'll turn buoys into a town project, not a corporate product."

Before you can answer, a shout cuts through the low-level debate. A young man in a faded fisherman's jacket — Jun — pushes past a line of pamphleteers, slamming his palm on a table. Rainwater arcs from his sleeve.

Jun: "We don't need a prettified wall! We need the marsh! We need work that doesn't sell our access!"

A corporate rep steps in, voice polished but thin. Elio, watching from a careful distance in a charcoal coat, smiles the kind of smile that reads as policy rather than sympathy. His hand gestures are minimal; his speech is cadence and evidence.

Elio: "We all want safety. Managed relocation reduces risk in high-exposure zones; the modeling is clear. If we act quickly, we can prevent the loss of life and infrastructure."

Murmurs ripple. Somewhere near the steps, a placard — 'HANDS OFF THE MARSH' — catches a gust and flaps like a wounded thing. The mood tightens: some people clap; some hiss. The plaza is a live wire.

Councilor Rosa steps forward from her place on the steps, palms lifted in a plea for order. Her shawl is windblown; she smells of tea and the municipal paper that never leaves her bag. The council chamber's tall windows behind her leak gray light; inside, the long table where policy is made seems small compared to the volume of human need in the plaza.

Councilor Rosa: (voice carrying) "We all know this vote is advisory. We are not deciding final policy in the street, but we will hear the community. I am opening a symbolic vote to guide our consultations. We will take brief statements. I ask for civility."

Her eyes sweep the crowd and land on you — not accusing, not pleading, but naming you. In that look is the unspoken: you have influence. You have organized. You have footage and relationships. You have, in the eyes of the neighbors, a kind of responsibility.

Mara pushes through with her recorder out, hair still damp from the mist, the lens of her camera catching glints like tiny accusations. She corners Etta first, then Mateo, then Elio's reps, lancing questions like small nets.

Mara: "Elio, will your plan require buyouts?" (she presses) "And you, Ari — can you commit publicly to a plan tonight?"

Etta answers in a ragged, compelling turn of phrase that makes the crowd lean forward.

Etta: "We have hands in the mud. We raise seedlings. We teach kids to plant cordgrass. We know every inch that holds life. A seawall is a line that says 'this is not for you.' We will fight that."

Voices overlap — some in agreement, some in anger. You feel your pulse in your throat. This is not an argument in the sterile room with slides. This is raw. Here, neighbors remember whose docks were taken last winter, whose attic still smells of salt, whose child learned to walk on patched boards.

The choice is small — immediate, physical. It does not change the fact that the plaza is a pressure cooker. But it shifts the detail the crowd will remember: the sight of you beside the grassroots table, or you examining the tech that promises to bridge compromise.

Etta's speech runs longer than anyone expected. She names dead marshes and living children, recites years of oral history like a ledger of debts. People clap and sob. In the press of it, a corporate rep gives an earnest, rehearsed talk about engineering certainty. Elio listens, hands folded, a weathered calm about him. When he speaks, his voice is softer than the rep's, and its clarity is dangerous.

Elio: "I lost a sister to surge. I've seen what hesitation does. We can model and build at scale. We can buy insurance for neighborhoods. We can manage the move with dignity."

A woman near you laughs — not unkindly, but with the brittle sound of someone who has already lost dignity to contracts. Someone else hollers that 'dignity' has a price.

Mateo's face—when he speaks—is close and raw.

Mateo: "Look, I'm not saying my buoys are a silver bullet. They are small, iterative. But if we couple them with living sills, if we anchor them to community labor and shared governance, we can be both practical and just. We can avoid wholesale buyouts by giving people protection that scales from the shoreline in."

You: (your mouth is dry) "Mateo's worked nights for months on this." (you cannot help the small defense) "He wants these to be run by the town."

An older neighbor shouts: "And who keeps them when funds dry up? Who stops the company that makes them from turning them into tolls?"

Mateo: (lowering his voice) "We can write that into the pilot. Open source controls. Community-maintained docks. Trustees from the collective."

The argument curls inward, between hope and fear. Your breath comes faster. Every option carries a wound. Each solution contains the possibility of harm.

The plaza hums and spikes. A shove from an agitated attendee sends a paper cup flying; someone yells. Security — municipal and corporate — moves like a shadow along the edges. You can feel, physically, the pressure of people looking for anchors. Your skin prickles.

Jun approaches you, rain beading on his hair, voice close and harsh in a way that is almost tender.

Jun: "Don't let them sell us out, Ari. Not again." (he locks eyes with you) "People listen to you."

You: "You're not asking me to be perfect." (you say, honest) "You're asking me to be brave enough to choose."

Jun: (a half-smile) "Choose. Just choose what keeps people here."

His words land and don't leave room for the soft answers you have rehearsed. The crowd has shifted from hearing to expecting.

Mara steps in front of you, recorder held like a small weapon. Her eyes are bright with the journalist's hunger but also—I think—hope. She asks you directly, when the chatter calms just a fraction.

Mara: "Ari, you're on the record. Which plan do you support?"

You feel the plaza shrink to a narrow focus on your chest. The taste in your mouth is metal; your heart beats like a fist against your ribs. You think of Etta's seedlings, of Mateo's late nights soldering, of Elio's steady clarifying arguments and the towns that were moved 'for their own good.' You think of the families who have nowhere else to go if shorelines fail.

Your internal list runs like a tide: people's faces, proofs, promises, compromises. Each one is a vector of consequence. Choosing is not a tidy moral act; it's a triage of loyalties.

The space between inhalation and exhalation lengthens. Your palms go slick. Around you, the plaza presses like a living thing, waiting. The rain thins to a mist that tastes faintly of the estuary.

Councilor Rosa meets your gaze; for a long second she is an island of steadiness in a sea of faces. Her hand brushes a stack of ballots — symbolic, advisory — and she looks to you, to the person who has shown up, who has organized, who has footprinted the marsh and the models alike.

Councilor Rosa: (quietly) "Ari, would you make a statement? It will guide how we frame the advisory vote tonight. The town listens to you."

You search your mind for the neat speech you practiced on the municipal steps: the map, the seedlings, the sensor feeds. You find instead a tangle of what-ifs and obligations. The plaza waits as if it could be rewound to reconsider every lost decision.

Your chest tightens until speaking feels like choosing between lungs.

You can hear each option like a separate tide: the earthy drag of Etta's plan; the bright, mechanical lilt of Mateo's hybrid; the polished inevitability of Elio's negotiation. All of them promise safety and risk in equal measure. All of them will leave someone behind.

You taste salt on your lips and know that whatever you say will ripple.
