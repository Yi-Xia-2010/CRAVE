[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse | Dusk, pre-storm]

Etta moves like a weathered reed through the aisles, securing trays, knotting bungee cords around racks. Her movements are efficient and stubborn. The greenhouse is humid, tipped toward life, and under its glass the world is a different temperature—a pocket of persistence against the cold's advance.

Etta: "We got a thousand signatures today. We got a temporary injunction on construction. That's not nothing, Ari."
You: "It's not nothing. But it's not a defense against water either."
Etta (trying to keep warmth in her voice): "We bought time. Time is all we asked for. We will make it count."

You crouch and water a tray of salicornia, fingers cold through plastic. You think of the vote left hanging, the emails piling in with legalese, the children who learned to plant with Etta's patient scolding. That memory is a small, bright thing you try to keep from being extinguished.

[Scene: Municipal Plaza / Storm Night | Hours later]
[Visual: The plaza emptied and half-heartedly barricaded; streetlights throwing drowned halos through sheets of rain]
[Music: Full orchestral crash—brass, percussion, strings screaming]
[Sound: Wind tearing at banners, the ocean like a distant thunderhead, sirens wailing closer]

The storm arrives like a verdict. It is sudden and enormous, a thing that does not negotiate. Rain knocks the sound from words. The town's barricades shudder. Your stomach tightens into a cold stone.

You run—sandbags, blankets, the motion of people who learned long ago that slow, steady labor is all that steadies a breaking world. You shout directions into the wind; your voice is pulled away, chewed by the storm.

Mara appears, soaked through, recorder lost to the night, eyes ringed with exhaustion and the raw need to witness.

Mara: "We have to get people to high ground. Councilor Rosa's trying to coordinate evacuations—"
You: "Jun—his boat—where is Jun?"
Mara (shouting over the wind): "He was out trying to haul lines. Last I saw, he was heading to the pier."

The surge hits with a monstrous intelligence. Water eats the plaza first—the asphalt dark and slick, streetlamps snapped like matchsticks. You see, with a new, terrible clarity, the places where life is kept—the pier ladders, the low stoops, the nursery—vanish under the wall of sea. Your camera flies from your tote as your shoulder is slashed by a gust; it hits the pavement and the coral strap thins and frays. For a second you see the world as a photograph of itself in motion—grainy, impossible to hold.

[Sound: A singular cracking sound—something structural failing]
[Visual: A wooden pier section ripping free, splinters like bone]

You sprint toward the greenhouse, because that is where Etta will be, because she will not leave seedlings to the water. You find her legs braced inside the doorway, arms full of trays, face streaked with mud and salt.

Etta (yelling): "Get inside! Get everything high!"
You (helping): "There aren't enough tables—"
Etta: "Stack them. Stack everything."

You are two people performing a rescue that feels too small. The greenhouse windows bow; the lights flicker and hold like small stars.

Jun's shout slices through the night—a different kind of panic. You pivot and see him clinging to a post as the water claims the dock. Mateo is there too, a dark shape against the storm, hauling a line with raw, aching force.

Mateo (yelling at Jun): "Tie off—tie off now!"
Jun (straining): "I can't—it's pulling—"
Mateo (to you, furious and breaking): "We should have had more buoys. We should have—"

His words are not an attack on you so much as on the whole impossibility of the moment. For a breath, between the roar of water and the scream of wind, the town's chosen path and the sea's indifference collide in one unbearable sound.

[Scene: Workshop (temporary shelter) | Dawn, After the Storm]
[Visual: A room of tarps and wet gear, scattered personal items half-buried in silt; morning thin and ashen]
[Music: Sparse piano notes, distant, like someone counting losses]
[Sound: Dripping water, low murmurs, the crunch of debris under foot]

Dawn reveals the arithmetic of damage: homes with washed basements, a collapsed porch, the café's front window gone. People move through the wreck with a stunned, mechanical grace. Mara interviews neighbors, her voice flat as a blade.

Mara: "How many families lost homes tonight? We don't have a full count yet—"
You (to Mara): "We have the signatures. We have the injunction. It doesn't help when the sea comes down like a creditor."
Mara (soft): "You did what you thought was right."
You (sudden, sharp): "Did we? Did we protect anything but our paperwork?"

The question eats at you. It is both accusatory and pleading. You taste like metal.

Mateo appears in the doorway with a small pack slung over one shoulder. His hair is still wet, clothes stained with seaweed. He looks older in the raw daylight, frail in a way that makes you want to hold him and also let him go.

Mateo: "They're offering me a deployment—an immediate rollout for a neighboring coast. They have funding. Real deployment. I—"
You: "You'll leave?"
Mateo (shaking his head): "I don't know what else to do. I can't stand here while lawyers tie this place up and watch it drown."

There is a silence between you that has depth and weight. Around you, people pivot between practical grief—wiping off silt, salvaging a child's toy—and a deeper, angrier disbelief.

You: "You promised—"
Mateo (cutting in, voice fraying): "I promised to try. I didn't promise to watch everything burn to keep a principle on paper. There's a program that can put buoys in tonight in another place. I can help them save people immediately."
You (brittle): "And us? What about the people here? The nursery? Etta?"
Mateo: "I don't know if staying here is the only way to help them."

The argument cracks into accusation, then into the exhausted round of two people who love each other but are being ground by different urgencies. Mateo looks at you as if mapping the contours of a loss he cannot yet name.

Mateo (quietly): "I won't be gone forever. I can't promise that where I go won't bring me back. But I can't be paralyzed by hoping the system will catch up in time."
You (voice breaking): "So you leave because the system moves faster than patience?"
Mateo: "I leave because the sea doesn't care about audits."

He steps closer, as if to close the distance with something more than words—a touch that smells of salt and solder—and then he does the opposite. He picks up his pack with hands that are steadier than your heart feels, kisses your forehead in a motion that starts like intimacy and ends like evacuation, and moves toward the door. He pauses.

Mateo: "Take care of Etta. Promise me that."
You (swallowing): "Promise me you'll write. Promise me you'll—"
Mateo: "I'll do what I can. I'm sorry."

He leaves before you can answer. The doorway snaps shut like a decision made.

[Visual: Mud tracked into amber light; trays half-salvaged, soil clinging to shoes]

Etta sits on a collapsed bench, hands deep in a bucket of dirty water as she resuscitates seedlings. Her mouth is set; her eyes are a map of refusal. Around her, volunteers move with a tired, sacred determination.

Etta: "We still have the legal win. The audit will force transparency. Investors will be scrutinized. They'll have to account for what they knew and when."
You: "That's something. Paper isn't nothing—"
Etta (cutting you off gently): "It's not nothing, Ari. It is exactly what we fought for. But the sea paid in roofs and boats today, and signatures don't raise a porch."
You (tears hot and unnecessary): "I wanted to save everything."

She looks up at you, and the aunt, the teacher, the woman who taught you to plant more fiercely than any storm, speaks like the tide itself.

Etta: "We saved a thing that matters. We did not save everything, child. We could not. But we made it harder for them to sell our losses as inevitable. That matters."
You: "It matters to whom? To the families who have nowhere else to wake up tomorrow?"

Her silence answers like a bell. You move through the greenhouse—touch damp leaves that survived and wilted stems that didn't—feeling the measure of action and its cost.

[Scene: Workshop / Final Hours | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The workshop is a gallery of what remains—Mateo's half-built buoy, Etta's seed labels, your camera with the coral strap frayed and taped]
[Music: Sparse piano, lower register; a thin, dissonant choir]
[Sound: A clock somewhere ticking the hours, the whisper of paper as a legal notice is unfolded]

Mara sits across from you, pen poised. She is writing, but her face shows the fatigue of someone who records calamity so it might be remembered without ornament.

Mara: "Your campaign slowed the corporation's timetable. The audit uncovered undisclosed risk models. It changed the conversation, nationally even—it exposed a playbook."
You (bitter, low): "It exposed what we've been saying. And the town—"
Mara: "The town paid. There were losses. People had to leave. Mateo left to go where he felt he could save more people. Etta keeps planting."

You think of the sound of Mateo's footsteps leaving, the way the door framed his back and made you feel both abandoned and relieved. You think of Jun's boat, of the nursery's drenched trays, of a landlord who will now raise rent under the logic of loss. The list is long and sharp.

You: "So what did we win?"
Mara (meeting your gaze): "You won proof. You won precedent. You won the ability to point at documents and say 'we were right'—and in doing so, you may have saved more lives later. But right now, you are here, and parts of Tidebridge are gone."

The truth lands like a stone and you do not have the energy to throw it back.

You move to the bench and pick up your camera. The coral strap is glued with duct tape; one stitch of leather stubbornly holds. You lift it, and the lens fogs with your breath. The camera is heavier than it should be. You feel the history of every picture you've taken—each frame a plea, a record, a hazard.

You think of words that might absolve, might explain, might build a bridge. None arrives that will stitch a roof back onto a home.

Etta (from the doorway): "You'll be needed. All of you will be needed. Not in the way you wanted, maybe. But needed."
You (closing your eyes): "I thought if we kept the line of proof clean, we'd keep the town whole. I misread what 'whole' meant."

You place the camera down carefully, as if doing so might keep the memory safe. Outside, somewhere in the distance, a bell rings—not in triumph, but to mark the hour like an accounting of time and absence.

You breathe. The sob is small and private. The arousal of the day—the panic, the running, the storm—has exhausted itself; what remains is raw and unprocessed grief. You fold it inward, a habit of survival that has always kept you afloat and now somehow keeps you from drowning.

You walk outside. The sea is a savage color—gunmetal and bruised—and it has taken more than it should. But in the bills that will come to light, in the audits that will follow, the record of those losses will not be invisible. There is a small comfort in that bureaucratic honesty, and it tastes like ash.

You have preserved a principle. You have paid for it. The balance sheet of your life is uneven, with debts in friendship and shelter.

You hear a soft footstep behind you. Etta stands there, hands folded, eyes luminous in the grey.

Etta: "We begin again. Not because it's easy. Because we've been given something to fight with now—truth. Use it, Ari. Even when it feels like it's not enough."

You want to promise her everything—homes rebuilt, Mateo back, a town undiminished—but your voice only manages the quiet of what you can do next: plant, document, speak.

You nod because nodding is all you have. You will keep the record. You will keep the tally of what was avoided and what was lost. You will continue in the place where action meets reckoning.

Narration
The audit will name names. The court filings will be public. Some policies will change; some investors will be shamed into better disclosures. These are real outcomes—but they do not stitch roofs, nor do they bring everyone back. In the ledger of climate and consequence, the page you turned today will read like a moral victory whose price was paid in homes and in a love that could not reconcile its timelines.

You stand there, between the greenhouse and the water, knowing you did not choose ease. You chose scrutiny. You ordered truth at the cost of immediate safety. The choice was deliberate, and its pain is precise.

You look at the horizon where sea and sky blur. The town is smaller now in ways that matter. Names of people who left nick your daily thoughts. You measure yourself against what you could have done, what you did, and the things that were never in your hands.

You step away from the water, clutching the camera as if it contains the ledger of your culpability and your courage both. You walk back toward the greenhouse where the seedlings that survived will be pruned and replanted. The work continues. The love continues, changed.

You are tired beyond knowing, but your feet move. You will write. You will testify. You will stand in the municipal record and insist that the numbers be honest. That will be your legacy—and your torment.
