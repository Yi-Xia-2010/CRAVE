[Scene: Municipal Plaza & Town Council | Days Later — Storm Watch]

[Music: Crescendo—brass slashes and pounding timpani]

The lawyers file motions; the firm responds with rapid public-relations campaigns and thin apologies. Local businesses, stretched thin, waver. The fundraisers you engineered fall short, a ledger of good intentions but insufficient cash. The town organizes sandbag lines and late-night watch rotations, because practical work still presses in despite everything.

Then the weather does not wait for paperwork.

When the first surge comes it arrives like a beast with no mercy. Wind hammers roofs, water sneaks under doors faster than promises can be read. At the high tide the sea takes hold of yesterday's margins and drags them toward a new horizon.

You run with a lantern and a rope. Your camera hangs around your neck like an amulet; you take pictures because the world seems to need bearing witness. In a half-light of rain and adrenaline you are everywhere: helping haul a tarp, yelling into a wet megaphone, threading your way through collapsing scaffolding.

Mateo finds you beneath a sign that shudders from its bolts, his hair plastered to his forehead, his hands raw.

Mateo: (shouting over the storm) "We can't keep doing this, Ari!"
You: "We can't hand them our future without knowing who it's for!"
Mateo: "We are losing people now. Not tomorrow—now!"

His voice breaks on the "now." He hauls a soaked beam into place with you. His movements are precise, desperate. Somewhere behind you both a fisherman's shack—one you've known since childhood—gives a long, awful groan and collapses under the water's pressure. The sound is an animal dying.

You make a decision in the span of a breath: you will not let the town's fate be written by paperwork while people drown. But the next legal hearing is set, and the injunctions will keep teams from fortifying certain shorelines until the court says otherwise.

You and Mateo wrestle a loose boat anchor free, and for a suspended moment something cracks between you: not love, not trust exactly, but the brittle string they shared. He stares at you as if asking permission you can't give or deny.

Mateo: (voice small now) "I'm going to take a job on the southern program. They're deploying tonight to where the hits are worst and they have a timeline we can follow. We can help more people from there."
You: (your throat closes) "You—are you leaving?"
Mateo: "For now. I can't watch this town be ground into paperwork while people sink. Maybe I can make more of a difference where things move faster."

There is no yelling, only a rain-scrubbed quiet and the relentless sound of water. You want to demand he stay, to remind him of promises. Your mouth opens and closes; the words turn to salt in your throat. You touch his sleeve as if to anchor him and his hand is warm and then gone.

Mateo walks away into the night, a silhouette swallowed by rain and the sway of the pilings. He leaves his field notebook behind—half full of diagrams and half full of pressed clippings—and when you pick it up his pencil slips out onto the wet boards like a small, surrendered thing.

Mara: "You'll be the story people point to—'They stood up for transparency.' But you'll also have to live with who couldn't wait."
You: (voice small) "Was there ever a right move? Or just different ways to watch things burn?"

The legal victory arrives as a file in your inbox when the spring is supposed to be warming. It is dense, signed, archived.
