[Scene: Shoreline — Night — The Storm Hits]
[Visual: Black water, white caps rising like teeth; the modular barrier outlined in fog, seams revealed in staccato lightning]
[Sound: Sirens overlapping; the groan of metal; the ocean detonating against concrete]
[Music: Full-throttle strings, low brass, heartbeat percussion; everything moving too fast]

The storm does not arrive like a single thing. It arrives like a verdict, a series of small betrayals that add up. Wind drives salt and rain into every seam, into eyes and lungs. The barrier — the very structure meant to protect — begins to show seams where the contractors patched for speed, where plans met deadline pressure and investor impatience.

You see it first as a ripple along the engineered face: water finding the seam between modules, then a pressure, then a small buckling. Then another seam lifts, and a sluice line forms, angling toward the Ramos lowlands like a hand.

Elias is at your shoulder, face smudged with salt. He has the sensor unit in his palm, lights blinking code he is trying to read like a prayer.

Elias Park: (Hollow.) "We accounted for wave height and wave energy. We did not account for redirected eddies when a seam—"

You already know the answer even as he tries to compute it. The structure was built to buy time but not to bend in the way it is now bending. It is an incomplete promise, and promises are now drowning neighbors.

A radio stutters — static, then a voice you know too well.

Responder: (Crackling, panicked.) "Boat three down near Ramos! We need air support! We've lost an engine—"

The name rattles in your chest. Your feet feel like stepping stones gone under the tide. Mateo's voice had been steady, warm; it had been certain. Now it comes like ocean on broken glass.

You run. You run because you cannot not run. Volunteers are already moving, bodies forming lines to pass sandbags like a human tide. Someone screams, something wrenches you sideways, the world reduced to hands and weight and cold.

[Scene: Shoreline — Early Dawn — Aftermath]
[Visual: A washed palette — gray light, torn banners, the barrier jagged like a set of teeth; a line of coffins under a tarpaulin]

The storm recedes like a slow, reluctant apology. It has done what storms do: revealed what was weak, rearranged the map of homes and hearts. Where once there were steps and docks, there are now new channels and a ruinous geography that will take years to understand.

You stand at the edge of what remains and feel the weight of all that is lost press into your bones. Mateo's absence is an ache with teeth. The town has a hole the size of his laughter.

Iris approaches, her coat still flecked with rain. She is not softened into warmth; she wears grief like armor that has been dented.

Iris Varela: (Quiet, almost stripped of formal hardness.) "We will hold a council. We will demand oversight. We will restructure who pays what. I will—" (Her voice breaks, the only sound like a rope fraying.) "I will make them accountable."

You look at her. There is victory there, but it tastes like ash. There is also a terrible truth: her hard choices saved some. Her agreements bought some lives time. They also cost others irrevocably.

Abuela Rosa stands nearby, hands folded, eyes large as if storing the town's history in their wet brown depths.

Abuela Rosa: (Tender.) "Names are not arguments, niña. Names ask us to remember people beyond policies."

You feel the town reconfigure itself in your mouth: blame and forgiveness braided together like kelp. People will shout at meetings; people will place flowers on the pier and curse the contractors; people will look at you and Elias both as architects of fate and as neighbors with blood-stained boots.

Elias moves close. You hear the catch in his breath, a wordless plea.

Elias Park: (Soft.) "I don't know how to ask you to stay. I only know I am here. If you want me to leave, I—"

You feel the old intimacy and the new impossibility. Love used to be a place you could return to. Now it is a field of broken things you both walked across.

You: (Low, steady.) "We were trying to do right. Trying was not enough."

He opens his mouth, then closes it. The two of you stand at the edge of a changed coastline and a rearranged future. There will be hearings and lawsuits. There will be apologies and indictments. There will be a map of responsibilities and a ledger of losses. None of that brings Mateo back.

You kneel at the shoreline where the water reaches and withdraws like a wound considering its edges. You make a mark in the wet sand, not a plan, not an argument, but a small private ritual: a name, a footprint, an acknowledgment.

The town will rebuild, in some way. It may never look the same. The social fabric has been torn; some stitches will be quick, some will never match. You will shoulder some blame and some forgiveness. Elias will carry his. Iris will carry hers. Abuela Rosa will carry the stories that bind the past to the future.

You breathe. The open sky is a pale ledger of the night before. You have done what you could, and it was not enough. The grief is a country you are now a citizen of.
