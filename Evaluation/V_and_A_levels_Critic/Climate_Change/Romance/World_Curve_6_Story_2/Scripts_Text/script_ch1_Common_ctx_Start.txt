### SCRIPT
# Route: Final Alternate / Sad Ending
# Context: Start

[Scene: Puerto Alba Marketboard | Morning, low tide after last night's high tide]
[Visual: Fog-thinned morning light; salt-streaked awnings; puddles reflecting pale sky]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a soft, constant creak from the raised walkways]
[Music: Sparse piano, slow and hollow]

You wake with the taste of brine in your mouth and the memory of the tide — how quickly the water licked into the stalls, how the market's laughter turned into shouted orders and the clatter of sandbags. It is morning after the high tide; the town moves like someone who has stopped breathing and is not yet sure how to start again.

Your windbreaker is still damp where you slept on a borrowed bench; the copper tips of your bob drip salt like slow coins. Your fingers fumble for the compass on its leather cord out of habit, then find the waterproof notebook instead. You flip it open because you always flip it open — a ritual that steadies you — and the page takes tide sketches the way skin takes rain: messy, quick, necessary. Ink smudges along your thumb; the same thumb presses too hard and leaves a blot that looks like a map of a broken coastline.

The marketboard smells like yesterday and today folded together: brine, frying dough, and a faint diesel tang from a generator someone left idling overnight. Stalls sag under tarpaulins; ropes hang limp, braided with seaweed. A blue fishing crate lies on its side, half-full of water and a silvering of small fish trembling in the shallows. You walk because there's work that will not wait for your sleep.

Mateo: "You're up early. Or late, depending on how you look at it."

Mara Serrano: "Somewhere in between. Did we lose the pastry stall?"

Mateo: "Mostly soaked. Rosa's boy hauled what he could. The vendor's boy swears the dough's salvageable if we can get an oven to dry it without burning the crust."

You laugh because the thought of pastry rescue feels absurdly gentle for the morning, and the sound catches in your chest like a small, stubborn buoy. You tell Mateo what you sketched in the notebook — the tide line where the water sat against the northern railing — and he traces it with a weathered finger as if reading a familiar wound.

Mateo: "You can't keep doing everything, Mara. Not that I'm saying you do —" (he flinches, halting) "— but you shouldn't."

Mateo [flinches, halting]: "— but you shouldn't."

Mateo [shrugs]: "Different kinds of stubborn. Yours is the one that bruises."

Mara Serrano: "It's not stubbornness, it's... I keep thinking if I map it, label it, patch it, then maybe—" Your voice disappears under the gulls. "—maybe it won't happen again."

Mateo: "Maybe not. Or maybe we'll be better at cleaning up. Either way, you don't have to be the whole crew."

The conversation lingers, and you let it. He shifts topic, practical as always: volunteers list, who can bring blankets, whether the generator can power the bakery enough to salvage what remains. Your hands move while you and Mateo talk; a knot of salt-sodden netting, a loop of rope, a pile of shattered ceramic tiles. You fold them into inventory in your head: people first, then tools, then arguments.

You do not make a grand decision; you move in the small increments that habit has taught you. Voices carry over the market: Abuela Rosa calling vendors by pet names like old anchors throwing lines into the past. When she sees you, she folds you into a look — warm, assessing, full of the patience anger often lacks.

Abuela Rosa: "Mija," she says, and the term holds both scold and blessing. "You are always with the notebook. Draw for me what the tide did. Show Rosa where the sea bit deepest."

You hand her the page. She squints through the same brown-glass eyes that have watched tides before you were born. Her hands are smaller than you expect, knuckles like knots, and she presses where the ink is smudged.

Abuela Rosa [softly]: "Ah. See here." (she taps a crescent) "We learned to plant higher. We learned to move the granary. We learned. But our memory is short when we have gold at our feet."

Mara Serrano: "I know. I know the stories. I just—"

Abuela Rosa [cutting gently]: "Stories won't hold the wall. But they teach what walls cannot: where to leave room for the sea to breathe." She folds open a damp, faded map from inside her apron — the paper makes a small, library-like creak. "This map. Your abuelo marked the old marsh channels with blue ink. They used to flood differently."

You take the map with reverence and fear. The paper smells like the inside of the church and oyster shells. Your fingers trace a channel drawn in careful, almost invisible strokes.

Abuela Rosa: "Quick fixes bring quick relief. Then they bring the wrong kind of memory. Remember the pier they poured fast and proud? The fish left within two seasons."

You feel the warning like a small tide tug beneath your feet. You think of Iris Varela's stern face, of her insistence on structural certainty. You think, too, of your father's ruined boat — a monument and a blame — half-swallowed by the marsh where it once brought in clams.

Abuela Rosa: "You are young, but you listen. That is good. But listen also to the land, mi niña. Ask before you build."

You want to argue — you have plans, sketches, a stubborn belief that careful engineering plus community watch can make a difference. But Abuela Rosa does not need convincing. Her look is unreadable and full: a bundle of history, grief, and refusal. You fold the map back into the apron, the ink seeping across fibers like time.

[Sound: A soft thud — someone posting a notice on the market bulletin board]

You turn and see the Council Hall's flyer pinned to the board by a heavy thumb. The paper is official: bold letters, municipal seal. Your stomach slides toward something hollow.

Vendor [distant, tongue-in-cheek]: "PUBLIC ANNOUNCEMENT: EMERGENCY MEETING TONIGHT - COUNCIL PROPOSAL FOR RAPID STRUCTURAL DEFENSE. SPEAKER: IRIS VARELA. ALL RESIDENTS ENCOURAGED TO ATTEND."

You inhale as if the notice were a physical thing. Iris Varela. The name lands in your chest with a slow, steady weight. She is decisive; she is weary; she is working from a ledger of loss that has sharpened her until corners cut. The notice is brief, bureaucratic, but its certainty rubs against your nerves like dry cloth.

Somewhere between Abuela Rosa's map and the notice, your phone vibrates — a short, practical buzz. It's Elias. You don't think he knows the notice yet. The message is clipped: "At Saltmarsh Labs. Have a modular prototype. Can you stop by?"

You look at the market in the way one looks at a ledger: lines of need, columns of ability, a gap at the bottom where numbers don't add up. Elias offering a prototype feels like a necessary thing: engineering in service of shelter. But the shape of that shelter is not neutral; it will change how the town looks and who belongs where.

You make your way along the raised walkway, boots splashing through ankle-deep puddles that reflect a sky too thin for its own blue. The labs are a warehouse of warm lights and damp concrete, and the smell shifts to hot metal and solder. Inside, Elias Park is there, sleeves rolled, hands a little grease-marked, an earnestness about him that is almost a physical light.

Elias Park [smiling, but his eyes have a restlessness]: "Morning, Mara. I brought you coffee." (he offers a cup, hands almost brushing yours)

Mara Serrano: "Thanks." (you take the cup; it is too hot, comforting in that small, mundane way) "What is it? The prototype?"

Elias Park: "It’s small. Modular barriers, stackable, with planted pockets for marsh grasses. Low footprint. Quick to deploy. Sensors to map salinity and wave pressure. We can test it behind the promenade and show the council numbers." He speaks in concise sentences at first: numbers, benefits, timeframes.

You study the device: compact, made of recycled composites, pale engineered greenery sprouting from a seam like a hopeful graft. It looks like a promise that could be disassembled and hoped away. You notice Nima's logo stamped on a corner — the ethical-tech collective — and you think of investors, of timelines that are sometimes more persuasive than facts.

Mara Serrano: "It looks... considered."

Elias Park: "We tried to keep it small so the town can decide scale. This isn't about a wall; it's about giving back room to marsh and protecting doorways." He hesitates, voice thinning. "I know how much you... how much this place matters to you."

There is a softness under his words — affection moving like tide under sand. You want to tell him the full weight of what "matters" means: the ruined boat, the night you held your father's jacket and whispered apologies to a hull that would not answer. But the confession gathers like low clouds and doesn't break.

Mara Serrano [careful]: "I didn't say I trusted prototypes."

Elias Park [a small laugh]: "Fair. I don't expect blind trust." (he leans back, more open) "I want to show data. I want transparency. But I also don't want us to argue from opposite sides of the same problem."

Mara Serrano: "What do you mean?" Your notebook tugs at your sleeve, impatient.

Elias Park: "I mean — I know there are decisions that will change people’s lives. I want them to be informed. I don't want engineering used to bulldoze local voices." His eyes search yours, earnest and a little desperate. "Can you help me? Help me make it a demonstration, not an imposition?"

He says it quietly, the sort of question that is also a reaching hand. You feel something in your chest, a recognition that his optimism sometimes glosses over what he can't measure: grief, memory, the slow erosion of trust. Yet, beneath that, there is a warmth — the sort that pulls at you because he believes problem-solving can be humane.

Elias Park [softer]: "If Iris is proposing a fast structural plan tonight, I want to be there. To answer questions. To make sure the town can choose, with eyes open."

Mara Serrano: "You'll speak at the meeting?"

Elias Park: "I'll try. But I need you there. You know the town. You tell me what questions make people bristle. Tell me when I sound like I'm selling something."

Mara Serrano [a small, rueful smile]: "That's an easy list."

Your conversation stretches into practicalities: what to demo, who to call for volunteers, how to arrange a site that won't look like a corporate showcase. There are pauses where both of you think of the same things — the boat, the bakery, Abuela Rosa’s map. The lab hums quietly around you; the prototype waits like a small, obedient animal.

[MERGE: Your exhaustion sits behind your ribs like a cold stone.]

Your exhaustion sits behind your ribs like a cold stone. You can feel the limit of your energy as a physical barrier: you can rally volunteers, you can sketch lines on paper, you can argue at council meetings, but there is a ceiling to what one person can hold. Perfectionism — your way of trying to shoulder everything — threatens to become its own disaster: plans unfinished, relationships strained, promises made to yourself broken by omission.

Abuela Rosa's warning and Iris Varela's impending speech loop through your head as you walk back toward the marketboard. The notice seems louder now; the letters edge into your thoughts.

At midday, the bulletin boards around Council Hall have folded the same message into different fonts: an official email, a printed handbill, a whispered rumor at the bakery. The municipal language is crisp, technical; it smells like a closed room where difficult things are decided with stamps and signatures. You can already hear the cadence of a procedural meeting — Iris's measured voice, the clack of tablets, the soft murmur of someone noting expense lines.

You stand for a moment on the terrace outside Council Hall, the wind threading through the municipal banners. The town below moves in small repairs: a children’s bicycle with a new tube, two women retying an awning, a man sweeping silt out of a doorway. Everything is being mended while the question of whether to rebuild differently presses close.

Inside you, the arousal of the day is low — a slow-burning worry rather than a flare of panic. But the feeling grows: a tightening in the jaw, a carefulness in your breath. The marketboard's patched awnings and the labs' prototype are different kinds of answers to the same problem. Each answer has an absent piece: who decides, who benefits, what is given away. That spool of possibilities tightens as dusk moves closer.

You sit on a bench facing the harbor, notebook across your knees. You draw again, because you always draw when you are trying to make sense. The tide marks, the footprints, the places people huddle: your sketches are small, exact, annotated not with flourish but with a slow steadiness. The town smells like frying dough and salt, and the light lowers as if someone pulled a curtain across the sun.

A hand falls on your shoulder — Mateo, passing with a thermos and a tired smile. Abuela Rosa calls to someone about where to store saved boxes tonight. Elias Park texts: "Iris got the hall. They'll expect numbers. See you at dusk?" You look up at the sweep of the sky, a thin band of gold over bruised cloud.

Nothing is resolved. The notice sits in your pocket like a pebble: small, weighty, edged. You imagine Iris Varela in the meeting chamber, sharp and certain. You imagine Elias Park there too, trying to translate numbers into something human. You imagine the town leaning in, listening, deciding.

You close the notebook and tuck it into your windbreaker's inner pocket. Your fingers are smudged with mud and ink; the smudge is a small, visible proof of work done and work yet to do. You stand, the world around you moving with the slow, deliberate care of people mending things they love.

Page-turn feeling: You walk toward the market's main lane as the first evening shadows thin the air. The announcement will hang over everyone tonight — and you will have to choose how loudly to speak, how hard to press on every side. A single meeting cannot hold the town's whole truth, but it will tilt the balance. You breathe in, tasting salt and frying dough, and feel the low hum of obligation settle into your bones.
