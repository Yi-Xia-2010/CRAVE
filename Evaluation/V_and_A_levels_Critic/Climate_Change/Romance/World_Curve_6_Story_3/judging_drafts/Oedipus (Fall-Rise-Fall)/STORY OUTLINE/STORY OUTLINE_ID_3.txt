## World View
Brinehaven is a near-future coastal town where weathered wooden boardwalks meet municipal glass towers; salt and hardware oil sit side-by-side with community gardens and instrumented marsh restoration plots. Politically, a pragmatic mayoralty negotiates between corporate funders and grassroots councils; civic meetings are noisy and televised, and planning offices keep holographic waterlines that follow tidal math like scripture. Culture privileges intergenerational memory — fishers, storytellers, and repair collectives — even as engineering firms offer large, fast fixes that alter shorelines and livelihoods. Religion is secular, with ritual centered on repair and remembrance: lantern nights, seed-sowing, and boat blessings. Class lines are thin and lived: third-generation fishers live beside municipal employees and visiting engineers; tensions center not on wealth alone but on authority and whose knowledge counts. Geography is immediate: the boardwalk and docks, the saltmarsh fen, a lighthouse-turned-field lab, a municipal planning office, and a rooftop seed-and-repair garden that smells of peat, oil, and hope. The atmosphere is tactile, painterly — sun-gold or bruised storm-blue within an hour — and every policy, gesture, or embrace has measurable consequence as storms become more frequent and the sea insists on being listened to.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Mira Kestrel, returned scientist and Brinehaven native, torn between loyalty to the town’s traditions and the promise of engineered security. The story follows an Oedipus (Fall → Rise → Fall) emotional arc: you fall into escalating crisis as storms and political pressure reveal past failures; you briefly rise with collaborative solutions and intimate bonds that promise stability; then the final fall arrives as choices, compromises, and unavoidable consequences culminate in communal loss or moral victory. Romance is entangled with policy: Jonah, the warm, stubborn fisher, asks you to preserve livelihoods and local identity; Cass, a polished coastal engineer, presses for scalable defenses. Secondary figures — Tessa (your sister), Old Man Rafi (the storyteller), Dr. Saira (mentor), and Mayor Lynette (political force) — push, comfort, and judge. At three structured split points, you choose how to allocate trust, whose plans you endorse, and how transparent you are with the community. Multiple endings (Happy, Bittersweet, Sad, True) are reachable based on combinations of these decisions, each staying within a six-chapter depth from the opening, echoing the brutal arithmetic of climate politics: small choices cascade visibly into the town’s future and your intimate life.
