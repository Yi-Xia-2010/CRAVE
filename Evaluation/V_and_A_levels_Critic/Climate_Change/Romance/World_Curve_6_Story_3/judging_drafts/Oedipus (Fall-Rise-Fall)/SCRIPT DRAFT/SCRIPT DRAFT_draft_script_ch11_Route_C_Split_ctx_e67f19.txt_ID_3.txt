Dr. Saira N'Goma stands beside you with a stack of laminated graphics, her braided hair pulled back into a practical knot. She does not smile, but there is a steadiness in the set of her shoulders that steadies you.

Dr. Saira: "We need to make the models speak plain English. If the town understands the trade-offs day by day, they won't be swayed by glossy promises."

You glance at the first projection: waterlines layered like fingerprints, cost curves, and timelines. The math is clean and brutal. Civil engineers in the audience nod; fishermen in the back frown. You know numbers can either empower or alienate.

Jonah pushes through the crowd, rain beading on his wool sweater. He plants himself opposite you, amber eyes burning with a quiet, fierce heat.

Jonah: "They're trying to bury the truth in jargon. We'll hold assemblies on the docks tonight. Everyone gets a copy, and we break it down."

You want to tell him how much you already owe him — for the crews, the town meetings he's organized, for hauling your boat out during the last storm — but the room is alive with expectation. There's no space for private debts right now.

Mayor Lynette: (to the room) "This hearing will proceed. Witnesses will testify. Counsel will be allowed. I ask for civic calm."

A scoff escapes from someone near the back; the room hisses and refocuses. The air tastes metallic, like the moment before lightning strikes.

Cassian: "Because most projects are adaptive — timelines change when the tide or the market changes. That's… that's how large projects operate."

Jonah: (cutting in) "That's bureaucratic language for walking away when it gets ugly. Who enforces those safeguards?"

The hearing stretches into the afternoon like a pulled net. Legal teams circle around clauses; auditors trace funding lines like fingers across a fishing map. You and Dr. Saira translate model outputs in lay terms: 'adaptive holdbacks' becomes 'reserve funds that cannot be touched without town approval'; 'phased construction' becomes 'sea walls with living marsh buffers.' Each rephrasing changes how people imagine their options.

Cassian is questioned again, softer now. When the attorney asks him about the ethics of prioritizing cost-effectiveness over cultural sites, he hesitates.

Cassian: "I love coastlines. I… I don't want to bulldoze memory. I grew up near rock groves in a city that built and then realized they had nowhere to put the past. My fear—" (his voice cracks once) "—is not about profit; it's failing the people my work is meant to protect."

You hear the tremor of someone who spends nights imagining worst-case scenarios and sleeplessly designing against them. For a second you feel the romance of shared duty: two people, different tools, the same fear of failing a shore.

Jonah: "Words are cheap at a lectern. I heard a firm once promise the world and leave with the scaffolding while the town cleaned up the broken nails."

Cassian: (wearily) "Then make me prove it. Set oversight. Put community people on the review board. Audit us. If the firm fails, make us responsible on the record, with penalties."

Mayor Lynette eyes you. The room tightens with the smell of wet coats and the electric edge of decision.

You step out for a breath. The night will come soon. The hearings will resume, subpoenas will be filed, and the grant money will still be frozen until someone decides what 'public' actually means in practice.

[Scene: Community Seed & Repair Garden | Evening]
[Visual: Rows of raised beds under lantern strings; soft faces tilted toward you]
[Sound: Bees have quieted; the crackle of a small firepit; a kettle hissing]
[Music: A swell of optimistic brass over a steady drum]

The garden is full in that intimate way of a place where everyone has a stake. There is the smell of grilled fish and wet wood; the lanterns make skin gold and eyes bright. Old Man Rafi sits near the edge, hands folded, watching with the kind of patient pressure elders perfect. People cluster in small knots: fishers with salt-stiff collars, mothers with bandaged hands, volunteer engineers with rolled plans.

Jonah has already organized a rota: translation tables, plain-language sheets, and a sign-up to read contractual clauses aloud. He hands you a cup of tea; steam fogs between your faces.

Jonah: "We can break the grants into stages. We can start with small, high-impact projects—marsh restoration, emergency roof reinforcement. If we get the oversight seat, we can control future disbursements."

You nod. The idea is tempting: redirect funds into hands that know their own tides. You think of small victories — a repaired net, a reinforced shed — accumulated into long resistance.

Dr. Saira: "Or we can renegotiate the contracts with enforced oversight clauses. That gives us broad protections, but it's political and legal, and it will bruise relations. It buys structure but not the same immediate autonomy."

Tessa is restless, tracing a finger over a packet of example budgets. She looks at you like someone waiting for permission to set a fire where the ground is dry.

Tessa: "Stop talking about what we might do. Tell me what to do. Who do we call to get crews moving tomorrow?"

Your heart hammers. The crowd leans. Lantern light makes everyone luminous and small at once.

Cassian arrives later than expected. He walks the perimeter, watching faces as if learning to read a dialect he didn't know before. When he speaks to you, his voice is tired but earnest.

Cassian: "I don't blame the town for distrust. If you'd seen the contracts… there are clauses I fought for, and clauses that were... baked. I can push for oversight, but I can't promise miracles overnight."

You sense the pressure in him — his timeline, his funding deadlines, his fear of being the architect who cost people their homes. His multifunction watch glints under the lanterns, little lights counting time and tide.

You: "You stood in the hearings and admitted there are gaps. Will you sign an enforceable oversight clause now? Will your firm accept community seats with veto power on phased draws?"

Cassian looks at you, then at Jonah, then at Tessa. His breath fogs in the cool, lanterned air.

Cassian: "Yes — if it's meaningful. But legally meaningful means renegotiation language, corporate counsel, boards of reviewers. It will take lawyers and weeks. It will strain relationships, perhaps irreparably. I want to help, Mira. I will not walk away from this if I can help it. But I need you to understand the institutional friction."

Jonah's jaw tightens. He looks at Cassian with a tidal mix of mistrust and the oldest kind of hope: that someone from outside might stay and do the right thing.

Jonah: "If you sign, you don't get to go back to the firm and smile about it. You sign and you stay accountable in public."

Cassian: (soft) "Then hold me accountable in public. I will take it."

The garden hums. People exchange looks — some skeptical, some relieved. The lanterns bob like the town's small, stubborn heart.

Inside you, the arousal grows: this is the crescendo moment where governance splits into pathways. Each breath feels like a metronome. Your decisions will set the tempo for months, maybe years.

Dr. Saira puts a hand on your shoulder — a quick, steadying anchor.

Dr. Saira: "We have three realistic pathways. We can redirect funds to community-managed resilience projects; we can force renegotiation with oversight clauses and attempt a hybrid; or we can pursue legal cancellation and open bidding. Each has a cost and a heartbeat of its own."

The garden hushes. Lantern light catches on the coral at your throat and on Jonah's rope-and-shell bracelet. Cassian's watch ticks, precise and unblinking. Tessa's fingers drum impatiently against her knee.

Your chest is tight with urgency and a strange, luminous optimism. This is what you came back for: to convert outrage into design, grief into contracts, fear into governance. You can feel the town leaning on you like a mast in a storm — not to hold it alone, but to set the course.

You imagine the marsh pushed back into health, children running along repaired boardwalks; you imagine oversight boards that include fishers and elders; you imagine legal battles and empty construction seasons. You imagine all three futures at once, each with its own ache and its own salvation.

Your voice, when it comes, is steady because you have mapped the contours of consequence and care.

You: "We must choose how to institutionalize transparency."

The crowd waits like a lungful of held air, every face an argument and a hope. The lanterns seem to dim and then flare as if in answer. The music swells to its highest chord, a very high, very bright pulse.
