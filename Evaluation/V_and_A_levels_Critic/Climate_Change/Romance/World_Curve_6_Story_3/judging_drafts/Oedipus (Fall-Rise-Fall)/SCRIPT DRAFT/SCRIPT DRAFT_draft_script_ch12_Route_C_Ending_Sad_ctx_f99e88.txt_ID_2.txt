[Scene: Saltmarsh Fen | Late Morning]
[Visual: Sunlight slanting through broken cloud, dew beading on reclaimed barrels; strings of lanterns sag between poles that smell faintly of salt and smoke]
[Music: A tentative, bright motif — plucked strings with low sustained cello]
[Sound: Bees hum; children's laughter; the distant slap of water on reeds]

You stand with your hands full of woven baskets — glasswort, sea purslane, a fistful of peppery samphire that snaps between your fingers — and for a few slow breaths the town feels like the small thing you always loved: stubborn, practical, alive.

Tessa is nearby, knees muddy, eyes bright as someone who has been waiting for a spring to prove itself. She waves you over with that impatient, absolute look you know too well.

Tessa: "You should see the kids with the dragonfly nets. Old Man Rafi's telling them the marsh will teach them secrets if they listen."

You: "You mean he'll spout a riddle and then hand them a thermos." (You smile; the action feels like proof that something has been saved.)

Dr. Saira approaches carrying laminated maps — simplified cross-sections of the restored fen — and drops them into your hands with a practical gravity.

Dr. Saira: "Numbers look good. Sediment accretion's holding where we planted — the co-op did well. It bought us time this season."

You: (You press your thumb to the edge of one map, feeling the damp paper) "Bought us time—yes. We won this season, but the models still show stress points down the inlet."

Dr. Saira: "We did what we could with what we had. Transparent hiring, local oversight, the restoration co-op—people stepped up. That matters."

Tessa: "It matters because it's ours. No ribbon-cutting, no glossy render. Just us and the mud." (She laughs, then sobers.) "And you. Mira. You did this."

You: (The coral pendant at your throat warms, familiar as a heartbeat. You want to correct her, to name every sleepless night and bureaucratic battle, but the moment doesn't need the ledger. It needs the harvest.) "We did it together."

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Weather-stained windows streaked with salt; instruments humming; projection maps hover over a table like ghosts]
[Music: Low, urgent percussion enters the score; a two-note motif repeats]
[Sound: Distant gulls replaced by the hollow clank of gear and the quiet, relentless tapping of rain to come]

You climb the narrow spiral once more, notebook under your arm, and watch the town from the lighthouse's shoulder. On the projection, the next storm is a slow, fat thing mid-ocean — warm core, rapid deepening. Models have margins; you have spreadsheets and the memory of loss.

Jonah waits in the doorway, his sweater plastered with damp, a rope slung over one shoulder. He smells of diesel and the sea—familiar, steady; the kind of smell that anchors you against the horizon.

Jonah: (soft, practical) "We can pull more of the small boats higher. Secure the pontoons to the new anchors. If we double up the lines—"

You: (You cut him off, not unkindly) "Jonah, the co-op already scheduled volunteers for moorings. You won't be alone out there."

Jonah: (He gives a half-grin that doesn't reach his eyes) "I know. I just— I want to check the low-side houses. If the surge hits worse than the models, those old pilings won't hold. I can get to them before the crews."

You: "Those houses are mortar and memory. If you go out there alone when it's still building, you could—" (You trail off, because the warning is both practical and terribly personal.)

Jonah: (leans forward, earnest) "Then come with me, Mira. Help me. Or tell me no and I'll go anyway."

You: (You want to argue deadlines and risk matrices; instead you touch the bracelet at your wrist that Tessa braided for you last year.) "Let the crews take the boats. You check the houses with two men and a radio. Promise me radios, Jonah."

Jonah: "Promise." (He pockets a small waterproof handset and looks at you for a beat that contains more than weather talk. He says, "Be careful," like a benediction and a dare.)

[Scene: Boardwalk & Docks | Late Afternoon — Storm Arrival]
[Visual: The sky flips — a bruise of violet and green; the sea heaves like a living thing]
[Music: Percussive drums and high violins; the tempo accelerates into a frantic, storming rhythm]
[Sound: Wind tearing at canvas; the roar of water; wood groaning; desperate shouts]

You do not have time to frame the moment; it hits instead like someone slamming both hands into your ribcage. The storm deepens faster than any of the models predicted — a compound surge born of warm waters and a converging coastal eddy. The marsh-colored horizon becomes a churning wall.

Old Man Rafi stands on the boardwalk with his cap clutched to his head, voice ragged as he yells instructions. Tessa is at the prow, hauling a soaked pontoon with more muscle than sense. Volunteers shout over the wind; lines snap like brittle promises.

Old Man Rafi: (to anyone who can hear) "Pull inland! Take the children! The pilings give!"

You: (You taste iron on your tongue and the salt on your lips; your notebook flaps uselessly in the wind. For a second the past compresses — the houses you've seen fall on the coast in other places, faces you've known folded into water. You move.)

A high slab of spray hits the boardwalk and the first small boat shudders free. A chain snaps. The noise is huge and animal. People shout names into the white roar.

Jonah appears at the edge of the water, chest set, eyes gone hard with the old stubbornness. An angry wave throws a wet sheet over his boots.

You: "Jonah!" (You try to be practical even as your throat closes.) "Get back! The line—"

Jonah: "The people down near Crow's Bend— they'll be cut off!" (He doesn't wait for you to agree. He wades into the water, boots sucking at mud, rope coiling from his shoulder.)

You: (You run toward him, but the water's pulse is faster than your feet. A piling gives with a splintered crack. A low house on the boardwalk tips, then slides like a sigh into foam. Screams tear the air; someone is calling Tessa's name. The world narrows to white noise and the cold of seawater on your calves.)

[Visual: Slow-motion close on a dropped radio sliding into a puddle]
[Sound: Electronic static, then silence]

You: (You remember every promise you've ever made to this town. They feel absurd and tender all at once. You don't reach Jonah in time.)

[Scene: Boardwalk — Immediate Aftermath]
[Visual: Night falls as if drawn down with a wet cloth; lanterns sputter, a sheen of oil on puddles reflecting desperate light]
[Music: Sparse, mournful cello; a single note held and then cut]
[Sound: Weeping, the cracking of wood, the faraway groan of metal being ripped free]

The rescue takes on a shape of blunt, painful choreography: hands passing ropes, people hauling with a grim, mechanical rhythm. Old Man Rafi is crying openly now — the sound of him is a small, breaking thing that makes everyone else’s shoulders fold.

Old Man Rafi: (voice shredded) "My brother's steps were there. The door… it was open and then it wasn't."

Tessa is frantically moving pontoons, her face white with a frozen disbelief that looks like numbness. She strains, muscles raw, and when she looks up her eyes find you and then drop away.

Tessa: (to you, barely more than air) "They said like this wouldn't happen. We— we did everything we could."

You: "We did. We routed funds. We hired our people. We learned the marsh. We did it on purpose." (Your voice sounds small to your own ears. You are yelling at the sea and at the ledger of the world.)

Tessa: "Then where is Jonah? He went out with a rope—"

You: (You run to the edge where the water has milky edges, scanning, calling. Your radio is dead. For a long, terrible minute the tide gives you nothing but the echo of your own voice. Then a volunteer pulls him in by a stray line, dripping, coughing. Relief rocks you; your knees almost give.)

Volunteer: "He's getting pulled toward Crow's— another swell took him—"

You: (You lurch toward the sound. Then the line goes slack; a board rolls free; the volunteer's face becomes a question. They shove, they pull, a hand surfaces and then is gone again under a curl of black water. Jonah vanishes into the sea's indifferent throat.)

Sound collapses into a single terrible note. There is no dramatic rescue. There is only wet, and the keeping of breath, and a small, incomprehensible absence.

Old Man Rafi: (the cry that comes from him is not a word but a tearing) "No—no—"

Tessa: (she collapses against the railing, her hands working as if to undo the world by muscle) "Help—help—"

You: (You can't make a plan fast enough for the speed of loss. You pull at your coral pendant until the cold bites the skin; the bead is cold as a grave. Your notebook, open in your hand, streams a line of numbers that mean nothing against the violence of the water. For the first time the data are mute; physics has rearranged the pieces.)

[Scene: Lighthouse Ruins / Sodden Boardwalk | Night — Hours After]
[Visual: Rain thinning to a fine, relentless sheet. The town is a silhouette of lost porches and scaffolded grief]
[Music: Very slow, low strings; a distant brass note like a bell]
[Sound: The sea's constant, lower voice; low murmurs; the soft, wet creak of wood]

You sit on a sodden log the town hauled from some ruined dock, the hem of your jacket clinging to your knees. Around you, people move with the wooden, mechanical motions of those who have performed the impossible and been found wanting. The harvest baskets are ruined; the green smell is gone.

Old Man Rafi sits two log-lengths down and cradles a crusted thermos as if it were fragile. He looks at you without the storyteller's performance, his face open and raw.

Old Man Rafi: "We did what was right. We chose the marsh and the people. We taught the children how to read the tides. We kept the books open and the hands in our own pockets."

You: (You close your notebook because the numbers are both accusation and confession. Your thumb leaves a salt circle on the page.) "We redistributed the grants. We trained the co-op. We named every expense in public. We followed every principle we could think of that would let people steer their own future."

Old Man Rafi: "And yet the sea took what it wanted."

Tessa comes and sits beside you, water dripping from her hair, her fingers numb from the hauling. She doesn't cry; she moves with a practical, terrible efficiency, checking knots, counting ropes like someone trying to find an order that will make sense of the chaos.

Tessa: "They will ask us questions. The mayor will demand reports. The funders will send lawyers. They'll want accountability. Maybe they'll ask 'Why weren't we safer?' and it will sound like punishment." (Her voice is flat; you know the look she's shielding from you — the one that asks whether rightness is enough.)

You: (You press the coral pendant so hard it hurts.) "Rightness is not a shield against physics. It never was. But if we stop being right because the world is loud and cruel—then what do we have left to give people in the morning?"

Tessa: "You mean, keep doing the hard thing even when it doesn't save everything?"

You: "I mean we owe the dead truth. We owe the living a plan that doesn't pretend this is enough. That doesn't pretend our honesty is a lifeboat."

[Scene: A Week Later — Boardwalk Memorial]
[Visual: Small candles on the ruined pilings; a single wooden marker with names scrawled; the marsh in the distance, bruised but holding some green]
[Music: A single, clear note repeated on a flute; wind under it like breath]
[Sound: Quiet: low murmurs, a few practiced sobs, the sea as background]

The town gathers. It's not a celebration; it's a remembering. You stand at the front because that is where your life has placed you: notebook in one hand, pendant cold and heavy against your sternum. People touch your sleeve and offer names as if they could anchor you.

Old Man Rafi: (slowly) "We told the children the marsh remembers. It also keeps its ledger. We will write in that ledger differently now."

You: (You think of Jonah: his laugh around a pot, the way he insisted on fixing a thing himself even when others wanted to help. You think of the rope he carried, the radio that quieted. You think of the unsent text you still have open in your phone.) "Jonah is—"

Tessa: (stops you with a look that asks you not to speak to fill the space) "We don't have the words yet."

You: (You fold your hands and let the lack of words be its own offering. You remember the models, the co-op meetings, the children catching dragonflies. You remember the way the community read the town like a body and tried to heal it.)

A woman in the crowd — someone whose stall you repaired last season — hands you a sprig of glasswort and bows her head. The gesture is small and worldly and sacred.

You: (In that moment, something like a verdict settles in your chest. You cannot claim you saved everyone. You cannot say the right moral choice would have kept every house upright. The physics of an angry ocean have no regard for the purity of method. You do, however, know that the work you did mattered — to the families still standing, to the children who will remember how people pulled together.)

[Visual: Close-up on your coral pendant, the bead dull and gray in the candlelight]
[Music: A single drawn chord, then silence]

You: (You open your notebook and write three words across a blank page: "We tried. Continue." Your handwriting is uneven; the ink blurs with salt. The three words are not comfort. They are a covenant.)

You stand, place your notebook into the inner pocket of your field jacket, and feel the weight of everything it contains: names, plans, failures, small triumphs. The town is broken in ways that will take years to knit. People will point fingers and file papers and maybe they will forget the exact shape of the choices made here. But when night comes and the wind tastes of iron and brine, the marsh will still remember the hands that planted cordgrass and pulled at ropes.

Tessa finds you at the edge of the memorial and links her arm through yours without speaking. Old Man Rafi puts a callused hand on your shoulder — not consoling so much as acknowledging that sorrow is a tool you all now carry.

You: (In the hollow where grief nests, you ask the question you have been carrying since the first storm: Do righteous choices outrun the physics of a changing sea?) The answer does not arrive as absolution. It arrives as a fact: sometimes they don't. And yet — in the face of that cold truth — there is only the small insistence to keep making the choice that honors people, even when it fails to be enough.

You step away from the candles. The town disperses slowly, each person gathering what they can. The marsh breathes in the dim light, a living thing with memory and capacity and pain.

[Visual: Fade to wide shot of Brinehaven at dusk — missing porches, stacked pontoons, lanterns reflected in new channels of water]
[Music: Low, resolute cello; no uplift, only the long, honest tone of endurance]
[Sound: The sea's distant, endless exhale]

You close your eyes and for a moment you imagine the ledger unwritten — blank and glowing. Then you open them because there is work: the co-op must rebuild, the town must record the failures so others won't repeat them, names must be kept alive. You fold the page of your notebook over the three words and leave it closed for the walk back to the lighthouse.
