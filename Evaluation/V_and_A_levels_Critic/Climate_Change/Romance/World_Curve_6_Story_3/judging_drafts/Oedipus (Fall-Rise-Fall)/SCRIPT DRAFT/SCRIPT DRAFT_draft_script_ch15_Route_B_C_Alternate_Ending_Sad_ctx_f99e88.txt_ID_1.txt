[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk & Docks | Night — After the Surge]
[Music: Rapid, dissonant strings; distant keening brass; undercurrent of high, repetitive percussion]
[Visual: The boardwalk in ruin — planks splintered, ropes snapping like thin thunder; a half-built seawall gapes where cranes once hummed; rain still falls in sheets, washing salt and mud into the gutter grooves]
[Sound: Sirens in the distance; people shouting; the sea hitting the breached wall with a new, vicious rhythm; the metallic thud of falling hardware]

You walk the length of the docks with the soft slap of saturated boots against wood and a chorus of small, angry noises around you. The town is a mouthful of grief — voices frayed and sharp, the smell of diesel and wet timber, the copper tang of seawater dragged through broken basements. Your coral pendant swings once, twice, catching a flash of emergency light and then going dark.

Everything you did to slow this, to channel it, to force transparency — lawsuits and delays that you convinced the town were necessary — sits in your hands like wet paper. Contracts frozen, pumps idle, a seawall half-exposed to the sea's new hunger. The temp you all feared — that impossible, warm pocket out past the shelf — arrived like a fist.

You know the outline of who did what. You know the legal logic that slowed machines and the municipal meetings that traded time for certainty. You know the arithmetic everyone pretended was abstract. Now the math is a series of empty boats and broken doors.

[Visual: Close-up on a pump array overturned in a shallow pool; seaweed threads the intake; a contractor's tag flaps, barely legible through grime]
[Sound: A hurried cry; wood snapping; a child's muffled weeping enclosed in a blanket]

Jonah comes at you from down the walk, salt in his hair, knuckles raw where rope cut his palms. His face is rubbed of jokes; there is only the hard line of blame and exhaustion.

Jonah: "You were the one who put the hatches on the contracts, Mira. You called for oversight. You wanted transparency — and now half our pumps are sitting idle while water eats through people's floors."

You want to answer him with the careful catalog of reasons — the clause numbers, the hours saved if we'd known sooner — but your throat is raw with a different thing: the knowledge that the town's heartbeat did not keep time with bureaucracy.

You: "Jonah, listen — I didn't want this to happen. I thought—"

Jonah: (interrupting, voice sharp) "You thought what? That a judge would stop a storm? That paperwork would plug a hole in someone's living room?"

His finger points toward a collapsed boathouse where Tessa and two others are hauling sodden crates into a pickup. The accusation is a physical ache, hitting you where grief and logic meet.

You: (low) "I thought we could make sure no one profited from half-measures. I thought oversight would protect people from being sold out."

Jonah: (laughing, half a sob) "Protect? We protected the contracts. We didn't protect Mary O'Hanlon's house."

You stare at him. He looks like the harbor at low tide — exposed, raw, all the things he keeps under water laid out to the sky.

Jonah doesn't wait for you to pick either path. He moves past, shoulders squared toward salvage tasks, and his absence is loud. You feel the doors of relationship close the way seawater closes a breach — swift, implacable.

[Visual: A municipal van glows under the lamps; Mayor Lynette's people stand near it, voices clipped, defensive; a legal placard half-blown along the railing]
[Sound: A harsh, synthetic voice from a loudspeaker requesting volunteers for emergency shifts; a file cabinet collapsing in the distance]

Cass arrives on the far edge of the pier, his trench coat plastered to his back, tablet in one hand — now inert and useless under running water. He moves with that measured urgency that once felt like certainty and now only has the rawness of being late.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Mira. The injunction — they froze the contracts because of the filings you supported. I told them we'd need contingency clauses. I told them to keep a phase-one emergency budget out of litigation reach."

You: "I asked for oversight because there were questions about who stood to gain. You read the filings. You know what transparency means here."

Cass pulls in close; up close you see the tremor you once mistook for professionalism. Something human and jagged under his composed exterior.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (voice taut) "I know what it means. I also know what the sea does when it's fed by a meteorological freak. I thought we could combine things, Mira. I thought we were doing that."

You: "We tried. We argued. We tried to get hybrid clauses in. We—"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (cutting you off, softer, not quieter) "We didn't get there. The firm, the town, the lawyers — everything ducked until the lawyers stopped ducking. We didn't move, and water did."

There is a silence that is not calm; it is a pulling taut before something breaks. Cass's eyes are glassy, not with blame but with an exhausted culpability. He looks at you the way the town looks at its ruined pilings: searching for a face to hold accountable.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Do you think I wanted this? Do you think I'd choose a model that cost lives?"

You: "I don't think any of us wanted this. I think we wanted to avoid the very thing that happened. I thought—"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (bitter) "Thoughts don't man the pumps."

Cass walks off before you can finish, and you do not know whether you are relieved or bereft. He leaves a small, technical pocket of the world unattended — precise answers and the inability to stop the sea. On the pier, the mayoral aides argue openly; the political center splits along the seams you've all spent months widening.

[Visual: Old Man Rafi kneeling by a small, private fire of driftwood under a tarp, his cap low; flames throw amber across his weathered face]
[Sound: The fire snaps; someone sharpens a broken oar; a group nearby starts to chant the names of missing pets and lost boats as a litany]

You wander toward the small fire. Old Man Rafi sits, hands cupping heat, as if needing it to remember solidity. He looks at you without surprise — the town's sorrow is expected, and he has the tired kindness of someone who has seen too many tides.

Old Man Rafi: (quiet) "Sea takes things. It always has. People think it's change made new, but I remember sixty, eighty years. The sea's memory is long. What we forget is what we give it willingly."

Tessa is there, hair plastered to her forehead, dirt under her nails, eyes red. She sits beside Rafi and leans into your shoulder with a small, burning weight.

Tessa: "They lost the bakery's upstairs. Mrs. O'Hanlon's curtains are in the water. Jacob's boat — all the gear gone. How do you say 'I did this to save us' and mean anything?"

You put your hand on Tessa's back. She is smaller than you remember in this wet, immediate way, but her anger is enormous and blameless.

You: "I can't make those losses un-happen. I kept thinking trade-offs would be the worst of it, not the only thing. I thought if we held funding to a higher light, we'd keep people from getting sold off. Instead we bought time on a paper horizon while the world moved on a different schedule."

Old Man Rafi: (smoking the air through his teeth) "Paper never kept the waves out. People did. But people aren't a machine; they get tired. They get hungry. And when the law slows what hands can do, the sea doesn't pause for statutes."

You feel each of Rafi's words like a stone in your chest. The town's moral calculus — what you were protecting versus what you delayed — collapses inward. The legal victories you sought are tiny refuges swallowed by a tide that takes everything not nailed down.

[Sound: A sudden crack — a support beam on the damaged wharf giving way; people shout; the orchestra of disaster swells]
[Music: Percussion rises into a frantic, breathless pulse; screeching strings]

Someone screams that a family is trapped in a basement reached only from the water side. Volunteers look at the law on their phones and at the locked gate. A municipal band of officials argues over jurisdiction, then overrope, then over nothing as hours pass and pumps sit unanointed by contract.

You run without thinking, boots slipping in the mud, and find yourself at the place where the seawall was being built. Steel reinforcements jut like broken teeth where tide has found the soft spot. The cranes tower impotent; their power sources unhooked because of frozen procurement. The breach widens with each tide, and the water rushes in like a ledger being cleared — inexorable, indifferent.

You: (to yourself) "Inertia killed us as much as the storm."

That thought is a simple fact and a burn. You think of the nights you stayed up redlining clauses and the morning you told the town that transparency would save them in the long run. You think of the families whose morning now begins under tarps and salt.

[Visual: A small group of neighbors hauling soaked furniture onto a flatbed; a child's stuffed seagull droops, sodden and mute]
[Sound: Low, shared sobbing; the sound of a lock being broken open; a bell rung for volunteers]

Jonah returns briefly — exhausted — and he doesn't look at you. He mouths something you can't hear, or maybe he doesn't mouth anything at all. Cass is gone into the fog of contractors and emergency crews, his tablet abandoned in a puddle. Mayor Lynette stands by the van, a public face thin and raw, a handful of aides pulling papers free from soggy folders.

Mayor Lynette Cole: (to the crowd, voice brittle) "We will file for emergency funds. We will rebuild. I—we will look into why pumps failed during injunctions. There will be inquiries."

A chorus of anger rises — not satisfaction. Questions without comfort. Mayor Lynette's words land like a press release over a crater.

You sit on the edge of the ruined dock and open your notebook. The pages are wet at the corners. You close your eyes and see the faces of everyone you tried to protect: Jonah's callused hands, Cass's precise stubbornness, Tessa's small voice, Rafi's slow wisdom. Loss is a heavy, granular thing; it accumulates and then sets.

You close the notebook. The weight hits the hollow beneath your ribs like concrete.

[Music: The strings collapse into a single, long, mournful note that hangs over the harbor; the percussion slows into a heartbeat]
[Visual: The pendant on your chest glints once in the lamp, then disappears under rain]

Old Man Rafi moves close and lights the small driftwood fire again, pushing embers into a tin can he produces from his coat. He looks at you with a clarity that is almost kind in its cruelty.

Old Man Rafi: "We will rebuild. Some things will come back. Some won't. The town will be different, Mira. Maybe safer in some ways. Maybe not in others. You asked for truth. Here it is: truth doesn't mean no one gets hurt."

You think of the trade-offs one last time, and the meaning of them: that inaction and action both cost lives in different registers. Your chest empties with a new, terrible knowledge — that there are no purely right choices here, only different weights and measures of harm.

[Sound: The tide presses against the damaged wharf; a child's laugh, shockingly small, cuts through the noise; someone starts singing — a thin, defiant line of a sea shanty]
[Music: Low, patient hum beneath the ambient field; the final chord holds and does not resolve]

You stand. The town presses forward into the mud and rain, a body that can't help but move. Jonah straps a harness and climbs to secure a sagging roof. Cass speaks into a radio, voice grainy, coordinating a private crew. Tessa hands out dry shirts. Mayor Lynette signs papers with shaking hands and passes them to volunteers. Old Man Rafi throws a small, steady arm around your shoulders like a weathered mast.

No tidy romantic closure comes. No single person is absolved. You share looks with Jonah, Cass, Tessa — small, broken connections that won't stitch themselves overnight. There is grief and also a terrible, slow beginning: neighbors forming chains to move furniture, someone boiling water for tea, someone else starting to tally losses aloud.

You tuck your notebook under your arm and feel the weight of choices, the heavy residue of justice and delay. The town is a ledger written in wet ink.

[Visual: Dawn struggling to push through low cloud; gulls wheel, indifferent and bright]
[Music: A single, sustained cello line — sorrowful, steady]

You know what you must decide next. You know that every possible next step will carry consequence and blame and small mercies. The sea has reasserted itself. People have paid. The ledger must be balanced somehow, and the arithmetic will not be clean.

You stand on the ruined dock, the town moving around you — raw, human, unadorned — and the night does not promise solace. It promises work. It promises choices that will continue to bruise.

You close your eyes, feeling the weight of the concrete of consequence pressing down, and think of the map you used to draw in your head: possibilities as trade routes, losses as inlets. You had wanted to be precise. The world taught you a different lesson: precision does not always save.

You open your eyes and turn your face to the cold wind coming off the water.
