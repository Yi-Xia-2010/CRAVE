A thin sheet of printed contracts fans across the table like driftwood — clauses, redlines, footnotes that promise safety and hide costs. Your waterproof wristband vibrates against your skin; a confidential message curls across its edge and then vanishes. You breathe anyway.

You: "Okay. Let's see what the city and the firm don't want us to read."

Dr. Saira: "You found all the procurement files?" (She leans over the monitor, fingers steepled. Her voice is low but bright — the kind of voice that keeps people awake with ideas.) You scroll. Numbers swim into narratives: inflated labor margins, vague maintenance schedules, clauses tying indemnity to third-party subcontractors. There are lines that make your mouth taste of copper.

You: "There are termination clauses here that let an outside contractor take over maintenance with no local oversight. And these 'contingency fee' entries—they're calibrated to make repairs profitable for the firm, not affordable for the town."

Tessa: (snorts, half a laugh that doesn't reach her eyes) "They love their euphemisms. 'Contingency.' Like that's a cozy word for 'we'll bill you later.'"

You: "We need context. Not just the numbers: who signed off, when, who stood to benefit."

Dr. Saira: (tilting her head) "Public records requests, redactions where legally required, a careful legal primer. And fingerprints — metaphorical ones — linking these addenda to the municipal liaisons. I can run the sequence analysis on financial transfer dates and show patterns. But you'll need someone to parse raw code if there are obfuscated entries."

You press your thumb against a corner of a page until it creases. Midnight is buying you clarity at the cost of sleep.

[Scene: Municipal Hall | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Glass facade catching the first hard light; a line of townspeople begin to gather like weather patterns]
[Music: Brass percussion under a rising choir of strings — urgent but purposeful]
[Sound: Murmurs, footsteps, the rustle of newspapers; a reporter's recorder being clicked on]

Mayor Lynette sits at the polished conference table, presses and municipal aides flanking her. Her face is an even plane, professional, but the color at her temples betrays pressure. You carry the dossier under your arm. Dr. Saira's presence feels like ballast.

Mayor Lynette: (cool, precise) "Mira. You understand the implications of releasing internal procurement materials to the public?"

You: "I do. I also understand the implications of silent contracts. The town deserves to know where money funnels and how decisions are made that will change our livelihoods."

Mayor Lynette: (leaning forward) "There are legal constraints. There are grant conditions. If we jeopardize funding, we risk construction that—"

You: "—that may not benefit the town. That's exactly my point. We can protect funds and ensure transparency. They are not mutually exclusive."

Cass (arriving, coat still damp from rain): (his steel-gray eyes scan the room, then land on you with something complex — concern, wounded pride, an appeal to reason) "Mira. You understand how fast a rumor can derail a project. These contracts have contingencies because of liability matrices. If you publish partial context, you hand opponents a lever."

You: "Then help us provide context, Cass. Walk with us through the clauses. Maybe there's a way to keep funding and insist on oversight."

Cass: (his jaw tightens) "I tried that route. I pushed for community seats; I fought for incremental releases. But there's pressure—board members, investors—they expect deliverables. If you blow this open publicly, you could stop everything."

You: "Or you could force the deliverables to include the town."

Mayor Lynette: (voice like polished walnut) "You can't threaten funding in the town's name. We need stability. We need work crews to rebuild before the next winter."

Old Man Rafi: (arriving quietly, the room scenting of salt and wool; he speaks in a voice that carries both the spit of the sea and grandmother story cadence) "Lynette. Stability built without trust crumbles in the first honest storm. Truth is a tide. It erodes wrongness. Make of it what you will."

A reporter asks a quick question. Cameras tilt. The conference room leans forward like whetted knives.

You: "We've prepared a dossier. It shows mismatched cost-benefit analyses, clauses that remove local oversight, and payments routed through third-party accounts. We are proposing a transparent oversight board, immediate suspension of any fast-track procurements until independent review, and public hearings with all contracts posted."

[Sound: A low, stunned murmur grows. Some relief — a woman at the back cries out softly. Others mutter anger. The room is suddenly a shoreline: waves of voice rising and retreating.]

Cass: (softly, to you) "You could have come to me with this instead of publishing."

Jonah: (from the doorway, voice like rope and comfort) "Consequences are worth facing if the town gets a say. I'm with her on this."

Dr. Saira: (quietly) "You did it."

You: (the truth feels like a wind through an opened door) "I did what I could no longer not do."

Old Man Rafi: (smiling with all the creases in his face) "A tide rememberin' itself. Good."

[Sound: The municipal phone lines begin ringing in earnest; Mayor Lynette's aide scrolls through a feed of social posts and donor letters. There is a warmth there that steadies you like a held rope.]

You: "I slept badly last night. My chest — it was tight. But now, lucid lightness. This is the first time in a while that I feel like the town isn't just being patched. We're choosing how to be."

Tessa: (whispering, eyes bright) "We made a thing happen. This is scary and holy."

Mayor Lynette: (meeting your gaze) "The political heat will be real, Mira. You must be prepared for lawsuits, delays, and for the firm to double down."

You: "I know. I also know the town deserves to see the choices laid plain."
