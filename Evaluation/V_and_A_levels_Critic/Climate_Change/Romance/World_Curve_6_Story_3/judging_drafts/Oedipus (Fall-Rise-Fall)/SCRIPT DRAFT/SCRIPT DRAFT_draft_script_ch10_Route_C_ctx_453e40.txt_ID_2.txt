[Music: Tense, propulsive strings]

You are already awake when the lighthouse clock says it's too late to be human and not late enough to be ghost. Your navy-blue hair is knotted at the base of your skull; a loose strand sticks to your temple with sweat. The field lamp throws a cone of warm light across a clutter of printed contracts, a tablet speckled with annotations, and the notebook you keep pressed to your ribs like a talisman.

Your wristband hums against your pulse — a soft, confidential thrum that threads through the night. Someone on the other end of town has sent another message. You read it with one eye on the code window where Dr. Saira's scripts parse redactions into plain English.

You taste the salt from the windowsill on the air and the bitter of too-strong coffee on your tongue. You breathe in, measure the space between urgency and intention. You do this because the ledger will not scrub itself; because there are clauses that favor contractors and models that smooth inconvenient costs into quieter footnotes. You do this because you have been the one to look at a town's ruins and know what unexamined contracts can do.

Dr. Saira: "There — see how they've nested the indemnity? If the storm damages the old docks, liability shifts three levels down, practically to the town." (She taps a paragraph; her voice is even but tempered with something like quiet exasperation.)

You: "They folded maintenance into 'contingent capital expenditures.' That lets them delay repairs for profit cycles." (You point; your finger leaves a salty smudge on the margin.) "We can make that visible."

Tessa (half a laugh, half a sob): "You make it sound like I'm watching accountants drown my grandfather's boat."

Dr. Saira: (soft) "You are watching the structure they built to make that possible. Visibility is the first tool."

Your hands move almost without thought: copy, highlight, annotate. The lighthouse hums with the small mechanical noises of equipment and the larger, patient noise of rain. Outside, a buoy light blinks like a slow accusation.

The wristband buzzes again. A text from Jonah: Be careful. Another from Dr. Saira: Are you ready to publish? You inhale, and the lighthouse seems to press its ear to you, wanting the answer.

You pick up the phone and dial Cass because you want him to know what you are about to do. You want to hear his voice; you want the sound of someone whose processes you respect to witness your step.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (on the line; measured) "Mira. It's late. You shouldn't—"

You: "I'm publishing the dossier at first light." (You are not asking. You are telling.)

Cass: (a pause; the clack of keys in the background) "Mira, I agreed to transparency — you remember that. But if you release unverified claims without the firm's context, the narrative will skew. We can present this together."

You: "You've had time to show me those contexts, Cass. I asked for the procurement breakdown three nights ago. You promised—"

Cass: (voice tight) "I didn't promise secrecy, Mira. I'm saying process matters. You're playing in a space where the mayor will weaponize anything that looks like chaos."

You: (the lighthouse lamp makes the coral pendant at your throat glow dimly) "Process matters, Cass. People dying is not a process I can wait on."

Cass: (a brittle sound, not quite anger) "You're making me choose between my firm and the town's trust. Don't make me the villain for trying to keep things orderly." (His steel-gray voice softens at the end, and for a second you hear a different timbre — a hurt that is mostly about being misunderstood.)

You: "Then don't let me be the scapegoat for inaction. Help me be accurate. Help me be clear."

Cass: (a low exhale) "I'll send what I can. But Mira—" (he stops) "—be careful."

You hang up with both the warmth of Jonah's earlier text and Cass's fractured compromise caught in your throat. The lighthouse feels smaller; the rain sounds larger.

Jonah's message arrives as you close the call: I'm with you. Not always in how, but with you. You feel Jonah's presence in that sentence — amber, steady, like a hearth you can step back to measure your heat by. You touch the wood-carved compass in your pocket and imagine him on the boardwalk, boots planted, jaw set.

Old Man Rafi joins the small cluster at the lighthouse by sunrise, soles making a soft slap on the wet planks. He carries the smell of seaweed and slow speech.

Old Man Rafi: "Truth is a tide, Mira. It don't care who shouts first. It just reshapes what it meets." (He looks at you for a long moment, the blue bead in his beard catching the wake-light.)

You: "I want it to reshape things that keep us from deciding for ourselves."

Old Man Rafi: (a small smile) "Then set your rudder true."

[Scene: Municipal Hall | Morning]
[Visual: The municipal atrium fills with a thin, anxious light; projection screens cast spectral waterline models on the glass]
[Music: Rising percussion, urgency]
[Sound: A building murmuring with voices; printers; a distant, insistent ringtone]

The dossier is a physical thing now — a folder, carefully redacted copies, and a digital packet pinned with Dr. Saira's verified annotations. You walk into the Municipal Hall carrying it like contraband and civic scripture in equal measure.

Mayor Lynette: (cool, practiced) "Mira. This is quite the morning." (She takes the folder with fingers trained to steady tremors into the press.)

You: "It's all here. Cost-benefit analyses, scoring models, and the clauses that shift risk onto the town. We've cross-referenced procurement schedules with payment timestamps."

Mayor Lynette: (a flicker — the political mask tightening) "You understand the ramifications. There are donors, bonds, approval windows. We cannot simply—"

You: "We can and should know what we're agreeing to." (Your voice is steady but high with momentum. There is a clarity inside you now that feels like a blade: sharp, hazardous, necessary.) "This is governance, not theatre."

Dr. Saira: (interjecting) "We are offering the community the data to make an informed decision. That's the point. We're not condemning without evidence." (She meets the mayor's gaze; neither of them yields.)

Across the hall, reporters gather like gulls around a freshly spilled fish. Phones lift. A camera clicks. Your wristband picks up a ripple of messages — some from sympathetic councilpeople, others from unknown numbers with clipped, legal-sounding language. You scan the emails, your thumb trembling just enough to betray you.

Tessa: (quietly, beside you) "People are going to be mad. People will be relieved. You know this, right?" (Her freckled face is paper-thin with worry and something like pride.)

You: "I know." (You let the certainty be a small, private light.)

You decide — not from an emptiness of consequence but from a place of calibrated courage. You hit send on the uploaded packet. The file transfers, a white bar creeping across a screen as if time itself were a progress indicator.

There is an almost superstitious hush, like the calm before a gale. Then, a sound: a low, mounting roar that begins as a few choked exclamations and becomes a town-wide voice. From the reporters' side, a chorus spikes — questions, exclamation, the rustle of notepads. From the hall's back benches, someone stands and shouts a single fierce line; another voice answers, softer and relieved. Your wristband vibrates so hard you think it will fall off — texts, calls, the first ripples of a tidal notification that you released into the world.

Mayor Lynette's posture changes. The political calm has been punctured; you can see the calculation rearrange on her face, the way a captain adjusts sails when the wind itself shifts course.

Mayor Lynette: (cooling into strategy) "We'll convene an emergency session. Public comment will be open. We need to manage this carefully—"

Tessa: (cutting in) "Manage? Or hide?"

Mayor Lynette: (a razor) "You won't force my hand, Tessa." (Her eyes flick to you, then away.) "Mira — you may have started a conversation you do not control."

You feel the truth of that like an electric shock. You did start something you would not control. That was the point. The ledger was always meant to move the conversation from closed rooms into moonlit porches and kitchen tables.

Jonah arrives at the hall as the first crowd begins to gather, his sweater clinging with humidity, his rope-and-shell bracelet catching the light. He finds you and takes your hand — a solid, uncomplicated touch that steadies rather than guides.

Jonah: "They're calling for you from the boardwalk. People want to hear the facts from you." (His voice is low, almost private, even as the hall swells.)

You: "I didn't want to be the spectacle."

Jonah: (shrugging) "You gave them a mirror. They'll do the rest. We can handle a little spectacle."

You exchange a look with Cass across the room; he stands with his tablet like an island of measured calm. His eyes meet yours for a split second: steel-gray, tired, and — beneath the professional mask— an ache of betrayal blurred with concern. He moves through the crowd as if it's an experiment that just turned unexpectedly social.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (quiet, when he reaches you) "I sent my notes. I didn't want to be the firm stepping in uninvited." (He pauses, searching for a word.) "I wanted to do this with you."

You: "You could have been more forthcoming three nights ago."

Cass: (a quick intake of breath) "I know. I can't change that. But I'm here now." (There is tension between apology and professional defensiveness.)

You: "Then be here for the whole thing." (You say it without malice; it's a demand for accountability, not reconciliation.)

The hall begins to feel like a living organism: oxygen-rich and sharp. People talk, shout, clap. The first waves of opinion roll in from social feeds, from fishermen at the docks, from grandchildren who read the files aloud in bright, disgruntled voices. Some neighbors come with gratitude; others come with fury, accusing you of scaring away investment. The soundscape is complex — cheers like gulls, howls like the surf during storm season.

Old Man Rafi appears again, stepping through the press clamor as if the noise simply parting for him. He catches your eye and gives you a small nod. For all his sayings, his gaze is steady: approval and a measured warning bundled together.

Old Man Rafi: "You pulled a thread, Mira. The cloth will be different now."

You: (to yourself, a breath) "I'll live with different."

You find a corner of the hall where the light from the projection screens throws waterlines over your arm, and you sit for a breath. Your chest is taut; your throat raw from speaking and not speaking. You have watched raw documents bloom into public fury and public relief in the same morning. You feel a strange, clean exhaustion — like you've finally let something that was rot out into the sun.

You check the tide of messages one more time. The dossier has been downloaded scores, then hundreds of times. A thread you started in the town forum is already growing with submissions and testimonies: people naming what they see in their own ledgers. There is a noise in the world now where silence had been the official policy.

You lie awake later — the town's roar still ringing behind your eyes. Sleep is thin; you wake to phantom vibrations on your wrist. In the dark, the coral pendant at your throat is warm and steady, the small, local heirloom against the broader, stranger ledger you've opened.

Your closing thought before you drift — a thought that is not fear so much as an acceptance of consequence: you pulled a thread that could unravel funding and protections, yes. You also unlatched a door that lets people see what they've been given and take part in choosing what to keep.

You are not done. The cost will be high. But for the first time in a long time, you feel the lightness of having chosen in accordance with your moral compass.

You breathe, and in that intake you can feel everything pivot.
