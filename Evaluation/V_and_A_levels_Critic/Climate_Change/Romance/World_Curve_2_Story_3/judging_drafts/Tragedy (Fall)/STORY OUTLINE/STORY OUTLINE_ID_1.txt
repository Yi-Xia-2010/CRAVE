## Visual Novel Title
Salt Lines

---

## World View
Verdante is a mid-sized coastal city in the late 2030s where tides and policy collide. Economically diverse, it blends historic wooden rowhouses along Mariner's Row with glass towers and municipal modernism. Power is split awkwardly between grassroots neighborhood councils and a technocratic municipal planning apparatus; private capital, embodied by the Vale Consortium, exerts leverage through contracts and media. Culture is civic and pragmatic—people value place, kinship, and local craft—but a growing class tension has emerged between risk-bearing coastal residents and inland stakeholders who prefer decisive engineering. Religion is private and diffuse; civic ritual centers on town-hall debates and mutual aid. Geography compresses politics: reclaimed piers, tidal markets, rooftop conservatories, and a research pier (Beacon Station) create sites for argument and tenderness. The atmosphere is painterly and precarious—salt-bleached palettes, misted light, and a mix of hope and brittle fear. Conflicts center on who decides adaptation: community-led living shorelines vs. quick, top-down engineered defenses that displace residents. Social hierarchy privileges technical expertise and capital, while neighborhood networks retain moral authority over daily life. The result is an intimate political drama where romance and civic duty overlap, and every choice ripples through the shoreline.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mira Santos), a resolute community organizer from Mariner's Row, must navigate the moral geometry of Verdante’s climate crisis while an old, warm connection—Elias Moreno, a visionary municipal planner—arrives with a city-scale solution. Cassian Vale, a polished magnate, promises a signature seawall and redevelopment that will "save" the city but at the cost of displacing vulnerable neighbors. The common route (Chapters 1–3) establishes stakes: rising king tides, community vulnerability, Elias’s plan, and Cassian’s political pressure. At the split, I choose whether to ally with Elias, confront Cassian, or double down on grassroots resistance; each path branches further. Across all branches the emotional arc is a fall: small compromises or miscalculations beget larger losses; what begins as hope erodes into failure, betrayal, and displacement. The structure favors horizontal branching so every decision leads quickly to escalating consequences. In some routes I try collaborative moderation, in others I escalate protest or investigative exposure. The endings converge on tragedy: communities fragmented, homes lost, relationships strained or broken. The romance with Elias is tested; at times it deepens into sacrifice, at others it dissolves into complicity. The city’s adaptation becomes a ledger of moral losses—an elegy for what we could not hold.
