You cross the threshold of your childhood block as if returning from a mission that already shows its costs. The patched elbows of your olive jacket catch on a low nail; familiar aches press under your shoulder blades. Around you, neighbors set up folding tables and a folding whiteboard, tacking laminated maps to a clothesline between two lamp posts. Kids kick at a puddle and send a silver arc across the boardwalk—an incidental demonstration of the problem you and everyone here are fighting to solve.

Hands come to you in quick, grateful squeezes. Lina's grin is sun-bright but her jaw is tight; Tamiko arrives with her camera like a canary, alert, watching for the first sign of a spark. Dr. Arun moves among the plants, his pockets heavy with samples and annotations, the smell of wet soil and crushed saltgrass clinging to him. Elias—high-visibility vest slung over one shoulder, slate trousers rolled up at the hem from the damp—carries a stack of printed guarantees and a tablet humming with overlays.

You feel the choice from the greenhouse still warm in your throat: negotiate, not capitulate. It made you a promise to try. Today, that promise lines everything—workshop stations, the marker-smudged tables, the maps beneath a protective plastic sheet.

You set down your water-stained notebook and unroll a living-shoreline sketch. The paper smells faintly of brine and coffee; your grandmother's cadence of sewing sandbags threads the edges of every plan.

Mira (you): "We'll start with the community maps. Everyone mark the places they won't leave. Not for me—so the council sees it. For us."

Lina: "You're serious about that, right? Not another 'consultation' where they redraw our history into boxes."

Elias: (smoothing a plan with a fingertip) "I'm serious. The land trust clause—if we can secure funding through the city's bond and attach a phased construction schedule, we can guarantee tenancy protections during each phase."

You watch his mouth as he says it, the practiced certainty there. For a moment, it feels like the greenhouse: warm, collaborative. Then the county lines on his tablet blink—an administrative countdown you can almost hear.

You nod, the weight settling in like a stone. The clock isn't theoretical. Cassian's consortium—his polished renderings of a continuous seawall—keeps tempting the council with a single promise: faster delivery, fewer bureaucratic headaches. Faster means flood gates first; tenure rules later, if ever.

[Scene: Workshop Site — Community Tent | Midday]

[Sound: Low murmur; pages rustling; a distant municipal truck idling]
[Music: Cello rises to a sharper string line; a percussive tick like a clock]

The workshop hums with energy. People trace contour lines with blunt crayons, arguing softly about where sand will move and where a child's bedroom will be in a decade. Tamiko films as Lina narrates a story about a woman two doors down who has lived in the same house for forty years. Dr. Arun kneels to measure tidal marks against a waterlogged ledger. You walk the room, catching fragments: "Phased construction," "binding tenancy protections," "community land trust," and the interjection of a municipal liaison—an officer with a clipped badge—who keeps returning to the timeline.

Officer: "The council needs commitments. Projects delayed cost more. The procurement team favors Vale's bid because of delivery guarantees."

Your hands curl around your notebook until the paper creaks. The legal walls you suspected are not abstract; they're mortar and code and fiscal calendars. Elias sits near the tent edge, tablet open, his jaw anxious. He has been translating community language into municipal language all morning, and the strain shows.

Elias: "We can make the tenure clause stronger—if we can find matching funds. The bond covers infrastructure, but it doesn't easily lock in long-term tenancy language. That's usually separate."

Mira (you): "Can the bond be conditioned? An escrow?"

Elias: (hesitates) "Conditioning bond language is messy. It invites legal challenges, delays. The bond-holders want a clean capital flow."

A neighbor's laugh—short, brittle—cuts through. You feel something shift under your feet, like a tide pulling at footings.

Elias: (quiet, steady) "I want guarantees as much as you do. But if we push too hard on bond conditions, the procurement timeline could flip to Vale, and then—"

You don't need him to finish. Vale equals fast seawall equals displacement if untempered. You can see Cassian's advertised renderings in your mind: a clean, sanitized promenade where the market used to be, with security cameras and boutique kiosks replacing the warm clutter of life.

Lina: (voice rough) "So what you're saying is, we vote 'yes' on compromised guarantees to get the work started, or we hold out and wait for legal counsel that'll take months."

Mira (you): "That's not how I want to frame it."

Lina: "No? How do you want me to tell my neighbors their lives aren't bargaining chips? You're negotiating with 'messy' on his tablet like it's a personality trait."

Her words land like a blow. People look at you—expectant, fearful, tired. You remember a photograph of your grandmother's hands, brown and rough, tying rope around a mattress to keep it from washing away. Negotiation is necessary. But bargaining away people's security feels like betrayal.

[Scene: Mariner's Row — Evening Community Meeting | Dusk]
[Visual: Lanterns bob over flooded steps; faces are half-shadowed; the sea flashes dark on the horizon]
[Sound: A strong offshore wind; the low grinding of a generator]
[Music: Strings sharp, percussion urgent—heartbeat tempo]

You stand at the podium with Elias and Dr. Arun flanking you. A small camera from the local station tilts toward the stage. Tamiko is conspicuously camera-ready; Lina sits forward in the front row, arms crossed. The municipal liaison sits to your left; a representative from Vale—the polished sheen of his suit showing even in shallow light—sits at the edge of the crowd, an unreadable smile.

Reporter: "Ms. Santos, the city has a deadline to meet. What will you recommend?"

You feel Elias's hand on the back of your chair—brief, supportive. He smiles at you, but you can see the calculation behind it: if you push too hard publicly, he risks his position; if you soften, the guarantees weaken.

Mira (you): "I recommend the city fund projects that bind protections to every phase. Not an addendum. Not a promise. A legal safeguard."

Elias: (careful) "We're drafting language for phased fiduciary guarantees and a transitional protection clause. It will—"

Lina: (cuts in) "It will what? Let them displace my aunt while 'transitioning' her into affordable units that don't exist?"

The room tightens. Tamiko's camera focuses; even the wind seems to hold its breath. Elias's hand lifts almost subconsciously; he reaches for composure.

Elias: "I know what's at stake, Lina. I'm trying to stitch the municipal process to community needs. If we delay for further counsel, we risk Vale stepping in with a faster, unilateral plan."

Cassian's representative stands, his voice smooth through the megaphone he didn't need.

Cassian Rep: "Speed saves homes. Delay damages them. Vale's offer ensures the city's frontline is secure within months, not years."

You can almost see the headlines: 'City Acts Swiftly; Seawall Secures Shoreline.' The subhead that never appears: 'Longtime residents relocated.'

Dr. Arun: (soft but firm) "Engineering without consent is coercion. The science supports living shorelines as a complement to bulkheads—it's not mutually exclusive. But protecting tenure is a social engineering problem, not just a fiscal one."

Elias: (his voice cracking slightly) "The bond timeline is a hard constraint. We can try to enshrine tenure language, but legal teams push back. If the city postpones awarding contract parts tied to tenure, we could lose competitive advantage."

Your chest constricts. Each syllable from Elias narrows the space in which you can breathe. You wanted negotiation; you didn't want your values rationed.

Lina stands, face flushed.

Lina: "Mira—you're bargaining our lives. Are you selling us out for a ribbon-cutting?"

The accusation is a thrown stone. It shatters the polite shell of the meeting. People murmur; you taste salt and iron in your mouth.

Elias: (defensive, then pleading) "I haven't— I haven't sold anyone out. I'm trying to build a durable program that can be implemented citywide. If we force the council into a public war with Vale now, they will retreat into a binary—cash payment or inaction."

Mira (you): (internal) His words are methodical, almost clinical. You can see the spreadsheets in his mind like an overlay. He believes in outcomes measured over time; you believe in people measured in present moments. The friction between those truths splits the air.

Tamiko: (low, to you) "If he goes public, he risks losing his job. If he doesn't, we risk losing our homes. Both are real. Both hurt."

You think of your grandmother again, of the house that smelled of lemon oil and salt, of the way neighbors braided rope together during the flood. You came back to Mariner's Row to keep those braids from unraveling.

Your attempts to harden the guarantees have collided with municipal procurement logic and Cassian's shiny offer. Legal counsel tells you clauses can be strengthened, but only if the bond is reworded—an action that invites legal challenges and might flip the tender to Vale. Valley of tradeoffs.

The meeting dissolves into clustered conversations. Elias approaches you, eyes raw.

Elias: "We can push for a public framing that calls Vale's approach into question—raise the social cost of their timeline. Or we can accept a compromised guarantee to get construction started and then litigate for tenure protections later. Or we can pause and bring in outside counsel to refine the language—at the risk of losing momentum."

His breath fogs in the cold air; his fingers twist a corner of his vest. You can see he is weighing credentials with conscience and finding both wanting.

Mira (you): (internal) The pressure spikes like a wave hitting a seawall—resonant, thunderous. Your chest tightens; the cello hits its apex. Trust, that fragile plank between you and Elias, creaks.

Lina finds you and stands close enough that you can smell the lemony soap she uses for her hands. Her eyes are molten with fear.

Lina: "Which side are you on, Mira? With us, or with the plan?"

You think of every neighbor who scribbled their home on the community map this morning. You remember Dr. Arun's measured warnings and Tamiko's distrustful camera lens. You think of Elias, earnest and terrified of being the one who fractures his career. The choice is a fracture—and you are the seamstress expected to stitch it without blood.
