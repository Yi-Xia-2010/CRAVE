Tamiko faces legal threats; she stands firmer than the smear campaigns expect and, exhausted, wins a partial injunction that forces a public review. The city council adopts emergency tenant protections influenced by the trust model Mira Santos pushed—language clumsy but usable. Some families secure right-to-return guarantees tied to rehabilitation funds; others, already relocated for the promise of immediate safety, do not return.

Mira Santos shepherds the Row in the months that follow—coordinating legal clinics, teaching people to read procurement language, tending to a garden bed that survives king tides because of the berm she built with neighbors. The wins are structural and real. They are also excruciatingly slow, and there are always new notices on stoops where old children's chalk drawings once were.
