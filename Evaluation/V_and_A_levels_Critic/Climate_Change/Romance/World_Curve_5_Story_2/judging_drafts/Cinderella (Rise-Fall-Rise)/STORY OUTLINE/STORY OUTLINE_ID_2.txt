## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Promise

---

## World View
Marisma Bay is a weathered near-future coastal town reshaped by rising seas and managed retreat policies. By the 2040s, municipal governments, private restoration firms, and grassroots collectives compete to define the shoreline's future. Economically, Marisma Bay sits between struggling fishing neighborhoods and a revitalized urban strip that lures outside investment; class tension threads through conversations about relocation, rent, and livelihoods. Politically, City Hall brokers between public funds and private contracts — Councilor Nguyen embodies the pressure to pick single, scalable solutions. Culturally, the town cherishes oral histories and intimate place-bound knowledge: elders like Old Man Rohan keep tidal lore; community hubs like the repurposed aquarium ("The Aquarium") host seed banks and harvest dinners. Religion and ritual are small, local: tides and storms mark communal calendars, and funerary rituals honor places lost to the water. Geographically, salt-bleached Low Street, a reclaimed Tidepark greenhouse, and a cliffside promenade form contrasting sites — human tenderness against elemental force. The atmosphere is tactile and bittersweet: fog on morning streets, algae sheen on boots, soft LED hum in the greenhouse. Climate here is treated like a character: morally ambiguous, indifferent but decisive, forcing choices that test love and civic responsibility.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Aiko Navarro) return to Marisma Bay to shepherd a community-led marsh restoration that could protect homes and critical habitat. Early scenes build hope as community and municipal collaborators sketch solutions; I reconnect with Mateo Ríos, whose steady planning and sea-green eyes are both anchor and mirror. A corporate restoration director, Dr. Selene Park, arrives offering rapid engineered fixes promising measurable protection and lucrative contracts. At a pivotal split, I choose a path: partner with Mateo to fight bureaucratic inertia together; accept Dr. Park's scalable biotech to secure immediate defenses; or lean into grassroots direct-action led by Tala. Each route follows the Cinderella emotional arc: rise to a brief peak of optimism, a sudden fall when a storm, betrayal, or unforeseen consequence arrives, and then an ascent toward a durable resolution. Across branches, romance with Mateo, ethical friction with Dr. Park, and loyalty to neighbors (Ivy, Old Man Rohan) color practical decisions. Endings vary: community-led triumph (happy), partial preservation with necessary relocations (bittersweet), corporate techno-fix with ecological loss (sad), or a "true" systemic sacrifice where I choose broader change at personal cost. Visual motifs (emerald streaks in my hair, Mateo's damp man-bun, Dr. Park's silver lob and AR spectacles) anchor emotional beats.

### Chapter 1: Returning Tide [Rise] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I step off the bus onto Low Street and feel the bay before I see it — salt air, algae gloss on puddles, the particular ache of a shoreline that remembers. My raincoat is damp at the shoulders, the two emerald streaks tucked behind my left ear catching the morning light. I carry my waterproof field notebook, pages weighted with pressed marsh grasses, and the silver locket with a map of the childhood shoreline presses at my collarbone. Old Man Rohan nods from the dock, his beard salt-sprayed; Ivy waves from a crate-stacked doorway, overalls paint-splattered and fierce. The scene is ordinary and fragile: patched roofs, a market stall selling preserved kelp, a tide line painted on a masonry wall like a bruise. I visit the community hub, The Aquarium, where string lights blink over beds of salt-tolerant plants; Tala is handing out flyers, purple hair cropped like a flag. I meet Mateo again at the edge of a reclaimed lot — sea-green eyes, damp curls in a man-bun, blueprint tube at his side. We trade a quiet, practiced warmth. My goal feels immediate: convince people that a community-first marsh restoration can still work. I record measurements, but mostly I listen — to conversations, to the small political currents that will determine everything.

Mateo and I grow closer in the quiet, practical hours — sharing thermos coffee and blueprints, fingers smudged with soil. A surprise storm surge breaches a temporary dyke, washing away weeks of fragile work. Volunteers stand mute on the ruined beds. The suddenness hits like cold water — our peak optimism falls into scramble and self-doubt. I look at the ruined seedlings and wonder if hope alone can weather the next choice.

* **Arousal:** High
