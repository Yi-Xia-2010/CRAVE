[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Glass-and-steel conference room. Holographic models float above the table; warm lamp pools contrast with the cool glow of projection screens. Mateo sits in the front row, hands folded around a waterproof tube. Tala, Old Man Rohan, and Ivy are clustered near the aisle. Councilor Nguyen presides from a raised chair. Dr. Selene Park appears as a crisp holographic overlay at the side screen.]
[Sound: Low murmur of attendees, the soft click of a laser pointer, distant gulls carried faintly through an open vent.]
[Music: Quiet, buoyant strings—steady, hopeful]

You inhale and notice everything at once: the metallic tang of the city-hall air, the roasted coffee from the side table, the salt that lingers on your lips like a promise. The notebook at your hip is warm from your hand. Mateo's presence in the front row is an anchor—familiar, steady—two breaths in the same rhythm as yours.

You step to the podium. A hush shifts the room closer to you. The hologram above the table pulses; your slides bloom in soft color—maps, before-and-after photos, a simple budget line that does not hide the hard math.

You (softly, to yourself): "Numbers with names. Stories with models."

[Visual: Close on your hands as they rest on the podium—faint salt smudge on your thumb; a pressed blade of marsh grass peeks from the notebook.]
[Sound: The click of the microphone; the far-off sound of a truck on wet pavement.]

You begin.

You: "Thank you, Councilor Nguyen, and thank you all for coming. My name is Aiko Navarro. I grew up where the marsh meets the docks—where tides told us what to build and when to leave. Today I’m proposing a pilot for a community-led marsh restoration: simple scaffolds, locally sourced materials, and training so neighbors can maintain it themselves."

Your voice is steadier than you expect. You lead with the human picture—a photo of Rohan on a younger pier, a child with mud-caked knees—and then you fold the models into that frame: erosion rates, projected shelter for shore homes, biodiversity gains measured in small, repeatable tests.

You: "This is not just mitigation. It’s stewardship. Low-cost prototypes we tested in Tidepark reduced sediment loss by measurable margins—enough to protect key spawning grounds and give small-scale fishers more time to adapt."

Mateo leans forward in the front row and murmurs—just loud enough for you to hear: "Clear, human data. Perfect."

You exchange a look and feel a small spark of shared satisfaction. There is asking and answering in it: you pitched the warmth; Mateo supplied the scaffolding; together you made sense.

You continue into specifics—cost breakdowns pared into single lines, maintenance timelines that emphasize local employment, and a training plan led by Tala’s groups and local fishers. The projection switches to a layered map that shows fragile parcels and community hubs, color-coded in a way a planner would love and a neighbor could read.

Councilor Nguyen: "You propose completion in what timeframe?"

You: "A phased pilot—six months to deploy the first scaffolds and training, a year for monitoring and adaptive adjustments. We’re asking for materials, a one-time training grant, and use of the Aquarium’s workshop for initial fabrication."

Councilor Nguyen narrows his gaze but not unkindly. The room leans. You can hear proofs being weighed in other heads: costs, political capital, voter response.

Dr. Selene Park (hologram, cool): "Aiko, your model is compelling at this scale. However, for municipal risk exposure and measurable flood reduction across larger neighborhoods, my team’s engineered scaffolds and seeded barriers reduce peak surge by double on model runs. We can deploy at scale quicker than grassroots training can be scaled."

There is a crispness to her voice—the confidence of a company built on algorithms and rollouts. The hologram flickers; behind her, a clean schematic unfurls: engineered mangrove scaffolds, nutrient-seeding algae barriers, a timetable with million-dollar figures pulsing in a corner.

Corporate Rep (from the back): "We can begin within six weeks. Funding and full deployment in three months. Measurable insurance metrics, real-time monitoring, and guaranteed contractual performance."

Tala’s voice cuts in, bright and forceful: "Speed matters, but community ownership matters more. When outside teams leave, who cleans the nets? Who keeps the scaffolds from becoming liabilities? Our people have the knowledge—Rohan’s knowledge."

Old Man Rohan (leaning forward, voice rough like rope): "There’s more to the tide than numbers. The tide remembers where the eelgrass used to hide. If you plant for the wrong current, it’ll take what you meant to save."

You watch the interplay—data against memory, scale against belonging. The room is not hostile. It is alive. That liveliness, that argument, feels like progress.

Councilor Nguyen: "Dr. Park, your proposal is attractive to the budget office. Aiko, we hear your community model is cost-efficient and brings employment. Council needs to know: who would you invite into a pilot? Whose stewardship do you trust to lead the first phase?"

Heat of anticipation slides across your shoulders but it feels more like purpose than pressure—an opportunity rather than a trap. You feel Mateo's presence in a different way now: proud, taut with hope.

Dr. Selene Park (hologram): "I’m not dismissing local knowledge. We can integrate community liaisons. However, contractually, large-scale engineered interventions require centralized oversight to ensure uniform performance."

You (responding): "Performance matters. But so does resilience that people can maintain when contracts end. Our tests show certain species recover better when local harvesting patterns are considered. That knowledge is not optional—it's data, if you collect it."

Mateo interjects in a low, even voice—deft and conciliatory, exactly what you need.

Mateo Ríos: "Council, there is a middle path. A municipal hybrid could centralize funding and provide oversight while committing to local leadership in maintenance and adaptive decisions. A shared governance model binds incentives—performance metrics on the short term and community-benefit clauses for the long term."

Councilor Nguyen: "Shared governance? Spell that out."

Mateo Ríos: "An oversight board including community reps, municipal engineers, and an independent scientific auditor. Funding from municipal bonds or grants; technical assistance from firms like Dr. Park's under clear co-governance agreements to prevent unilateral control."

Dr. Park's hologram tightens—an almost-imperceptible shift—and then softens. Dr. Park: "We’d consider such partnerships if contractual terms protect deliverables. Our investors demand performance guarantees."

Tala (passionate): "And our people demand voice, jobs, and a guarantee that we don't get displaced. Contracts mean little without teeth that secure local stakes."

Ivy (speaking up, small but hard): "Been renting the same stall for twelve years, Council. I don't want a four-month check to be the only legacy of a 'solution.'"

The room hums with forward motion now—energetic, earnest. The arguments cut vivid lines through the air, but there is a common current: everyone wants the marsh to survive and the town to endure. No one is trying to win against another—mostly they seek to be part of a durable answer.

You feel the rising cadence inside you: confident, present, warmed by the sense that the community and institutions are finally negotiating rather than retreating into their silos. The arousal is mid—focused and energizing, not frantic.

You (quietly, to Council): "If Council asks for a recommendation, I will be honest: I want the marsh to belong to the people who live with it. That said, I also understand the need for resources and technical support that scale. I think we can design a pilot that tests those exact mechanics—who manages, who funds, who maintains—so we can learn fast."

Councilor Nguyen: "Aiko, we're asking you now: who should be invited into the pilot? The Council wants your recommendation—they say it will help shape the initial allocation of support."

You feel every eye settle on you. Mateo’s fingers tighten around his compass tube; Tala’s jaw sets with eager expectation; Rohan watches as if the tide itself has asked him a question. Dr. Park's hologram inclines, unreadable in that thin, corporate light. Ivy bites her lip, watching you like someone counting on an oar.

You inhale. The decision field opens in your mind—values cleaving into options—and the town's future feels like a hinge that swings where you push.

You are ready to answer.

Invite a community-led consortium—us, local fishers, and Tala's groups.
Recommend Mateo’s municipal hybrid plan—public funding plus municipal oversight.
Open the pilot to Dr. Selene Park's firm—the fastest funder and tech.
