You say it like a calculation, but your chest warms when people nod. You have always trusted numbers to be honest; today the numbers like the plan so far.

You wade where the ground permits, the burlap scaffold—a prototype you stitched late into the night—already laid in a shallow trench. Its fibres smell faintly of the sea and compost. You press it into the mud and listen to the small pop of air escaping as the material beds in.

Neighbor: "Feels sturdy. Like it wants to root."
You: (softly, to yourself) "That's the point."

Footsteps behind you are measured; Mateo arrives with his usual patient gait, a rolled tube of blueprints clutched under one arm. The parchment unfurls with the sound of careful paper.

Mateo Ríos: "Morning. Sorry—was held up at the desk. How's the prototype?"
You: "We set three lines. This one is the mid-salinity band—meant to catch seedlings and reduce shear stress. If the root mats take, the scaffolds will interlock and slow erosive flow."
Mateo Ríos: (kneeling, fingers brushing wet burlap) "Your stitch pattern distributes tension—smart. If we angle low-engineered breakwaters along this contour, it should dissipate wave energy before it strips the beds."

You both map with quiet gestures: palms tracing invisible lines in the air, eyes flicking between the marsh and the rolled blueprints. His voice is steady, explanatory—measured like a tide.

Tala: "So that's the plan—beds, staggered planting, and the breakwaters. The town can build these in stages."
You: "And community beds give people a hand in the restoration—stewardship and livelihoods."

Mateo studies the marsh, then looks at you. There's a softness in his expression that isn't quite spoken: it's gratitude, and a sense that he believes this could work.

Mateo Ríos: "We can simulate the projected accretion over a season and present the worst- and best-case timelines to Council. If the models show even modest gains, it's a compelling ask."
You: "We need the stories too. Old Rohan's charts and the fishermen's routes. Numbers plus memory."

Mateo nods, and you sense how the two halves—data and story—fit together in his mind as neatly as the blueprint tubes he keeps.

You and Mateo sit on a crate with the blueprints spread like a small sea between you. He talks through a phased rollout, and you sketch notes directly onto his plans—annotations, concessions, the balance between achievable milestones and aspirational reach.

Mateo Ríos: "If Council green-lights a pilot for three plots, we can show community labor and low-cost materials, plus the model's projections. It makes the ask tangible."
You: "Three plots, staged over a year. We'll need permit wording that protects community stewardship. And we should budget for teaching sessions—Tala can run them."
Mateo Ríos: (smiles) "Tala will do more than run them. She'll make it contagious."

You share a look—shared exhaustion and the quick, gentle lift of being understood. The plan is no longer only a schematic in your notebook: it is scaffolding for other people's days and futures.

Ivy arrives with a small basket of sea-baked rolls. She offers them without fanfare and the act cuts through the technical talk like light.

Ivy: "Council's scary, but rolls are not. Eat."
You: (biting into a warm roll) "Council's scary and rolls are necessary."

Laughter in the wind. It isn't blithe; it's the buoyancy of people holding onto small comforts as they make large asks.
