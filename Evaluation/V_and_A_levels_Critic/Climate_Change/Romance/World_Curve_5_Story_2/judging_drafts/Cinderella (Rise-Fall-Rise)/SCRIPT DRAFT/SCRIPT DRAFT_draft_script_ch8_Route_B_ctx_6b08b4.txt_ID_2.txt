[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Morning]
[Visual: Dew beaded on polymer skylights, rows of young marsh scaffolds in staggered bays]
[Sound: Hum of dehumidifiers; distant gulls; technicians' voices clipped with urgency]
[Music: Tense, propulsive percussion—heartbeat under glass]

You arrive before most of the technicians. The greenhouse smells like wet peat and rubber: the peculiar, honest smell of things being coaxed back into shape. Your boots sink slightly in the damp path between beds. Light filters through the translucent panels in thin, careful bars; every surface is splattered with the Atlantic's fingerprint — salt crystals, a smattering of algae.

Technician 1: "Morning, Aiko. We replicated the hex-scaffold design overnight. Municipal crew is here on shift two; they're running calibration now."

You nod, palms already itching for the instruments. There's a stack of sensors on the cart — moisture probes, salinity meters, tiny accelerometers for scaffold flex. Mateo is there too, sleeves rolled, the blueprints unfurling like a map of a future you're supposed to trust.

Mateo Ríos: "We got the technicians from Public Works trained in the protocol last night. They'll be able to deploy the first ten units within the week if the metrics hold."

You feel the sentence in your chest like a small, brittle thing: hope tempered by the knowledge of how quickly systems can unspool. The sensors blink to life under your fingers, LEDs pulsing like a nervous animal.

You crouch over the first scaffold bay and press a probe into the soil. Data streams across the tablet's screen with the immediacy of a heartbeat — salinity down a few points, water table responding in microcycles, root-binding improving in the pilot bed. The numbers are good. Too good, briefly, until the history in your bones says "trust but test again."

You: "Run a sixteen-hour drift and then a stress cycle. I want to see response to a simulated storm surge — not just the lull."

Technician 2: (breathless) "We can — but Public Works wants deployment numbers by Friday. Council's asking for early wins."

Mateo: "We need a balance. Public confidence and municipal deliverables have to coexist with rigorous testing."

You find yourself saying the obvious — and necessary — aloud, because everyone needs to hear it: the rush to scale can't erase the slow, local knowledge these prototypes must honor.

[Scene: The Aquarium - Community Hub | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: String lights sway; tile murals of fish dim under protest banners; people cluster in warm pools of light]
[Sound: Murmured arguments, brass of a distant bullhorn, folding chairs scraping]
[Music: A rising, anxious motif—strings pulled taut]

You return to the Aquarium carrying two things that now feel like fragile structures: the greenhouse data and the political promise you and Mateo made to the community. The air inside smells of candle wax, old tile, and the faint, fermented tang of kelp tea. Tala is already there, jacket pins bright against her chest, eyes like a winter sky — sharp and ready for gale.

Tala Kumari: "They rolled it. Public Works is calling it a success story and running headlines. Where's our seat, Aiko? Where's the clause for community control?"

Her voice is a blade wrapped in concern. People are leaning in. Old Man Rohan stands by the mural, hands balled into the sleeves of his cap, face weathered into a map of tides.

Old Man Rohan: "They talk about 'scaling' like it don't take memory. They can't measure the smell of when the marsh felt safe. You know that, girl."

Ivy bustles in with a stack of flyers, breathless, the practical timbre of someone who measures outcomes in rent and shifts.

Ivy: "If Public Works gets the contracts and the city ties it to new zoning, my shop could get evicted for a seawall. We didn't sign up to be models in some textbook."

You feel the room compress around you — the social geometry of expectation, hope, and frankly, accusation. Mateo stands at the edge of the circle, hands folded, an anchor that wants to be steadier than he is.

Mateo Ríos: "We negotiated for an oversight seat on the municipal committee. It's a start. It will let us push for living monitoring frameworks and community hires."

Tala scoffs aloud. "A seat? Like a paper ornament. They'll pat us and move the levers anyway. You know how this goes."

You taste bile. The pilot's early metrics are good enough to be a headline, blunt enough to be a weapon in the wrong hands. You've watched the municipal technicians replicate scaffolds with care, but the narrative that arrives on the town square is already sharpening into an image that sidelines the people who built it.

You: "We demanded co-authorship. The monitoring plan was amended. Mateo — we have signatures."

Tala: "Amended doesn't mean binding. You gave them the playbook and kept the chapters."

The exchange stretches, then snaps into a dozen crosscurrents: people offering stories, referring to waterlines on kitchen walls, a child who no longer recognizes where the tide used to stop. Voices layer — protective, furious, pleading. Dialogue ricochets; each turn reveals more worn edges.

Old Man Rohan: "They put a fence where a path used to be. You can't fence the sea."

Mateo Ríos: "No. But fences won't hold forever either. We bought time. Time to get people to safer ground, to retrofits—"

Tala: "Time for who? You speak for 'people' like we don't already live here. This isn't about 'buying time,' it's about who gets to decide which time."

You try to mediate, to fold the conversation into something that can become a blueprint again, but your words feel like small stones while the room wants a fortress.

A cluster of technicians, brought by the municipal team to observe community feedback, stand awkwardly at the periphery. Their presence is a visual proof of cooperation — and also a symbol of the very shift that has everyone here on edge. One of them meets your gaze with an expression you can't parse: professional neutrality or private discomfort.

Technician 3: "The data will keep coming. If thresholds are exceeded, we'll trigger adaptive responses."

Tala: "Adaptive for who? For systems or for people? You're speaking from someone hired to build things, but it's not the same as living here."

The night condenses into questions that don't have easy answers. Every word you give seems to cost something. Mateo reaches for your hand across a space crowded with tension; the contact is tiny and fragile.

Mateo Ríos: (low) "We got them to add community monitors into the plan. It's not nothing."

You: "It's not nothing," you agree, the phrase a hinge that doesn't fully close. The phrase both comforts and indicts you.

[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Night]
[Visual: Glass tower lit against storm-slate sky; a conference room with holographic models revolving in blue]
[Sound: Distant siren; the low murmur of late-night staff; the click of a digital pen]
[Music: Percussive strings intensify—tension into near-anticipatory crash]

Councilor Nguyen sits at the head of the long table, lamp light making the wet pavement outside ripple like a moving thing. Dr. Selene Park's presence is felt even before she arrives — a polished AR brief glows on the table, the kind of certainty that eats nuance. Mateo and you file in with Tala, Rohan, Ivy, and a smattering of technicians. The municipal legal counsel is already shuffling documents.

Councilor Nguyen: "Thank you for staying. The city recognizes the value of this pilot. We've agreed to a municipal rollout with Public Works handling deployment. We've also secured a community seat on the oversight committee."

A few polite noises. Tala's jaw tightens into a line you know from past storms.

You: "Is the seat binding? What's the enforcement mechanism?"

Councilor Nguyen: "It is an advisory seat. It will allow for input into monitoring. Implementation decisions remain under municipal authority. The contract includes dispute-resolution clauses — arbitration by municipal standards."

The words fall like a gavel. Your stomach drops; the oversight seat — the concession you and Mateo fought for — is ceremonial when placed beside the city's authority to bind the contract as it prefers.

Dr. Selene Park: (smooth) "Advisory frameworks allow action at scale. We cannot delay engineering responses for every local preference. The model proves rapid deployment saves property and lives."

Tala: "Your 'lives' have a price tag. You say 'scale' like it's a cure. It isn't."

Dr. Selene Park: (cool) "Scale is survival. Local traditions inform implementation, but in emergency tolerance, algorithms help us prioritize quickly."

Old Man Rohan: "My memory ain't an algorithm." He spits the words into the room. The sound is raw.

Your chest tightens the way the tide tightens around a pier. You had envisioned something different: not just a seat but a stake. Not a concession but an equal hand on the wheel. Instead, you watch legal language fold community voice into advisory notes that can be overruled.

Mateo touches your shoulder, a small human engine trying to steady both of you. His hand trembles just slightly.

Mateo Ríos: (quiet, to you) "We bargained for access to data streams. We can expose anomalies publicly, make enforcement politically risky to ignore."

You want to believe him; the municipal dashboards could be a lever. But the clause Nguyen recited has the finality of timber: authority is not the same as collaboration.

Councilor Nguyen: "We move to sign tomorrow morning. Public works will start deployment after the ceremonial press release."

Your throat constricts. The word "ceremonial" is a small, private knife. You imagine the sunrise over Low Street and the crews carrying scaffolds across thresholds that used to belong to people's memories. You think of Ivy's apron, of Rohan's hands, of Tala's knives of rhetoric. The greenhouse data sits in your bag like a relic.

You: "If the oversight is advisory, what safeguards exist to prevent unilateral changes once the contract is executed?"

The municipal lawyer opens a folder, eyes sterile and efficient.

Municipal Lawyer: "Change control will follow the contract. Emergency clauses permit the city to expedite modifications if model thresholds recommend it. Public recourse is through the municipal grievance mechanism."

The room exhales — a small, collective surrender. Your ribs feel like they might split with the pressure.

You realize, with sudden, sharp clarity, that what you helped set in motion will proceed with your fingerprints but under someone else's authority. Your hands tremble. The locket is heavy against your sternum.

Tala's voice is a low, dangerous thing now.

Tala Kumari: "We didn't give you a blueprint to hand to them like a gift. We wanted co-authorship, not a footnote."

Dr. Selene Park: (measured) "We can collaborate on post-deployment adaptation. The company has funds for community liaison programs."

The word "liaison" tastes like a polite resignation. You feel betrayed and complicit at once.

Ivy: "What do we do when the city says 'we did our part' and then evicts my landlord to make the retrofit easier? Where's the guarantee for jobs and rent protection?"

Councilor Nguyen: "We will discuss mitigation measures in the coming meetings."

You notice the hedging. "Will" and "discuss" are words that defer to future acts — often the most dangerous place for promises to live.

Your mind races, inventorying options like tools: public leaks, protests, legal challenges, appeals to media, direct action. Each feels necessary and insufficient, like bandages on a wound that will reopen when the tide comes.

Mateo: (raising his voice, not breaking) "If we can run an independent audit of the first deployments and publish results — transparently — it will force accountability."

A municipal tech: "Independent audits can be scheduled, but deployment timelines are fixed for certain stages."

The argument curls tighter. The night grows thinner, oxygen-starved. You feel a surge of panicked clarity: metrics matter, and the numbers already show early wins that will be amplified as proof and used as a justification to proceed without binding community control.

You inhale and the room seems to tilt. The world outside is a wet blur of streetlight and sea. You sense a breaking point — a place where you can either ratchet up resistance or try to preserve what influence you have left.

Your phone buzzes with Mateo's earlier message echoed: "They're signing at 8. We need a plan."

It lands like a bell. You have minutes to choose posture, to marshal allies or to accept another notch of compromise.

You press your thumb against the locket, as if to steady the weight inside.

A single, sharp thought slices through the din: you must decide what kind of sacrifice you're willing to make — for the marsh, for the town, for the people you love. The options are small and terrible; the consequences will be large.

You stand with the legal copy of the contract open in front of you, the phrase "advisory oversight" glaring like a wound.

The air in the conference room thickens. Outside, the tide is rising.
