[Scene: Low Dock | Twilight — Rising Tide]

[Visual: The boardwalk is a patchwork of wet planks and resin seams; salt-proof fairy lights blink like constellations in a low cloud. Children paddle by on makeshift rafts. The prototype panel sits on a patched dolly, metal glinting under strings of bulbs.]
[Sound: Water lapping against pilings, a chorus of muffled conversation, the occasional metallic clank as someone tests a shackle.]

You shoulder the wet end of the panel and feel the give of soaked wood under your boots. The harbor smells of kelp and frying oil, a humid salt that sticks to your lips. Your chestnut bun is loose at the nape; a stray curl has stuck damp to your temple. The solar pendant at your throat flickers once when a passing shadow cuts the light, tiny as a heartbeat.

Neighbors gather in a rough semicircle beneath the lights. Voices cross like tides — practical questions, small jokes to move fear out of the way, someone singing off-key as they test a rope. Sami stands ankle-deep in the brackish water, hand raised, rallying volunteers like a conductor. Elliot is crouched beside the prototype, fingers in the circuitry of the micro-irrigation unit, eyes small and focused behind the smudge of his drafting glasses.

You take a breath that tastes like salt and hot metal and start calling logistics.

You: "Okay—anchor points two and four. Run the irrigation manifold on the east side; that'll feed the birch pockets and keep overflow toward the drains. Two people on watch at the northeast corner—rotate every thirty minutes. Noor, where are we on current tide windows?"

Dr. Noor (offscreen, from the crowd): "Pinged you in the thread — revised high-water at 19:12, not 19:30. That window tightens us by exactly six minutes. Hold steady."

[Sound: A phone ping, distant and bright. Sami's shoes slap wet planks as she comes closer.]

Sami: "Volunteers ready. I've got three teams for anchoring, two for the irrigation tent, and Lia for first aid. Folks want to help, Maya. They came out for you." (She grins, but there's tension in the set of her jaw.) "We can move fast if you call it."

Your mouth wants the words that make everything definite. You can feel the room compress into a single hinge: your decision.

Elliot looks up from the manifold, eyes bright. He doesn't hide the twinge of exhaustion around his mouth, but he smiles — an honest, small thing.

Elliot: "If we recalibrate the pulse width, we can get the seep irrigation to respond faster to sudden salinity spikes. It buys us buffer time if the tide shoves a surge through the market."

You: "How untested is 'faster'?"

Elliot: (ease in his voice) "It's a parameter tweak. If it bails, it resets to baseline. I can run it in manual watch mode for tonight. But—" (he gestures toward the children paddling near the fishmonger stall) "—it reduces lag. We have to be aggressive to prove viability."

Sami: "Aggressive or reckless?" (she spits the word out like a challenge, then softens) "Maya, we're not here to lose anyone. You know that."

You: "I know. That's why we plan for redundancies. Two mechanical anchors on each side, rope backups, and watch teams wearing comms. Noor, your correction — does it change anchor deployment at all?"

Dr. Noor: (she thumbs a tablet, the tide chart scrolling in faint blue) "It means we need the panel in the water no later than 19:05 to let the sensors cycle before the crest. There's a six-minute cushion for calibration. If the crest comes earlier, we'll be reactive, not proactive. That's a risk I must state clearly."

[Music: The score tightens — snare rolls under a hopeful chord; very high arousal begins to pulse.]

You feel the old instinct pull like a buried rope in your ribs: refuse any private hand. Your brother's face arrives with the force of a tide — a laugh, a sprint, a sudden absence in rising water. The image is a measurement you keep: what you cannot afford to lose. But beneath that, something else hums — the ember of trust that lit in the Spire weeks ago. It's small. It's stubborn.

Elliot reaches up, brushing salt from his sleeve. "If we get this right, we can show the city the living system works at scale. We need that proof to keep control at a grassroots level."

You: (quiet) "I need that, too. But proof won't help if we get strangled half-way by red tape."

[Sound: A low engine approach; a sleek rubber-dinghy eases to the pilings. A man in a tan suit—Luca's liaison—steps up with a waterproof folder and a practiced smile that doesn't quite reach his eyes. The crowd hushes as he clears his throat.]

Luca's Liaison: "Maya Rios, Elliot Chen — Luca Marin asked me to offer a proposition on behalf of Meridian Coastal. We've been watching your pilot. He believes in your work. He'd like to provide engineered anchors and fast-track permit assistance, in exchange for limited access points to monitor structural load and integrate with city-level systems. Minimal data sharing. Full deployment scale possible within weeks."

You hear the hope in the hush shift like a current. Funding. Scale. Permits. The need in the neighborhood for reliable defenses pushes at your sternum like another tide.

Sami is the first to react aloud. Sami: "Access points? Like surveillance."

Luca's Liaison: "No surveillance — structural telemetry, metric feeds for load and cohesion. We can guarantee expedited permits, certified materials, and a crew to secure the anchors to code. This is the fastest way to get installations city-wide, with minimal disruption."

Dr. Noor steps forward, tablet in hand, expression threadbare but alert. Dr. Noor: "They'll want data. You must be precise about what 'limited' means. There's precedent for that word being elastic."

Elliot's jaw tightens. Elliot: "If we accept that, we get scale. But we hand over control, even if just a sliver. Their anchors could change how the living wall integrates—"

You cut him off because pause is another kind of weight.

You: "Show me the spec." (Your voice is steady, but your fingers tremble as you tuck a damp curl behind your ear.)

Luca's Liaison opens the folder, a glossy schematic sliding out like a promise. "Four engineered pylons with load-distribution arrays. We attach monitoring nodes at intervals. We propose a MOA that allows access only under emergency declarations and requires community oversight."

Sami scoffs. Sami: "Emergency exceptions are loopholes. We all know that."

Dr. Noor: "Questions first," she says softly. "What safeguards on data sovereignty? Who audits the load? And—importantly—what's the stop clause if ecological indicators show damage?"

[Sound: A distant thunderclap rolls over the harbor; someone laughs, nervous. The tide, already higher, washes a cold line across the boardwalk and laps at your boots. The fairy lights tremble.]

Elliot looks at you. His hands still smell of solder. He leans in so his voice is only for you.

Elliot: "We can pilot with their anchors on a perimeter—keep community control over the core—use the data to argue for more living infrastructure. It will speed everything. I think it's salvageable."

You: "Or we say no, build with neighborhood labor, and carry the timeline ourselves. Maybe slower, but with consent everywhere. Maybe more resilient because it's owned."

Elliot: "Or—" (he swallows, eyes flicking toward the prototype) "—we run the open-source adaptation I sketched last night. Push the firmware to force quicker response. If it works, we prove viability without external anchors. If it fails, legal risk and ecological unknowns."

[Music: Percussion rises to a near-sharp edge; the scene accelerates. Voices layer — neighbors murmuring, children laughing nervously, someone calling a child's name over the rafts. The air is charged with salt, hope, and danger at once.]

Your hands ball into fists around the strap of your field notebook. The pendant at your throat catches the light and flashes. For a sliver of a second you are your brother again, running — and then you are not. You are present. The neighbors' faces are lit by fairy lights and resolution. Sami is waiting for an order. Dr. Noor folds her arms in a manner that hides—accurately—how desperately she wants a clean, evidence-based win.

You run through the practical ledger in your head, quick as a technician running diagnostics: anchors cost, permits, watch teams, ecological unknowns, the legal exposure on the modification, the moral weight of accepting a private hand. Each metric is a tide chart; each choice will change the coastline of lives.

You: (soft, decisive) "We have three paths. One keeps the work entirely in the community. One takes Meridian's anchors to scale faster but with strings. One tries Elliot's firmware tweak to speed things, risking unknowns."

Elliot presses his palms together, the way he does before he pitches an idea. "I believe in the firmware," he says. "But I won't ask you to gamble your neighborhood for my optimism. I'm not asking for blind trust. I'm asking for a shared bet: you put me on a short leash. Full transparency, rollback, and public logs."

Luca's Liaison folds the schematic back with the calm of someone used to sealing deals. "We can limit access. We can codify oversight. Meridian wants to be a partner, not a predator. Consider the scale you could reach in weeks, not months."

Sami: (bitter) "We hear 'partner' and we remember displacement."

A child paddles past, her oar sending a small spray that glitters in the fairy lights. She looks to you with the blunt clarity of someone who doesn't know "compromise" as a word yet understands what a wet floor is. Her parents are nearby; their faces are set with the resolve of people who will either shelter or be uprooted depending on what you call next.

You sense the room leaning with you. The tide brushes the inside of your boots, and the sound of water on wood is a steady metronome. Time compresses: Noor's correction, Sami's teams, Elliot's tweak, Luca's offer — all compress to three possible breaths.

[Music: Swells into a high, urgent chord — the moment is at its apex.]

You draw a breath, the harbor's salt in your throat. Your voice is steady because steadiness is something neighbors can lean on.

You: "All right. We decide now."
