You are up on the Spire's deck because the work is simple enough to be honest: lift, hold, test. But the night has weight — two weeks to convince a council that a living wall can do what concrete promises. You lift a reed panel and feel its damp weight in your palms. Soil scratches your fingers; the smell of wet peat and salt rides the wind. Elliot laughs at something you say and the sound tightens like a line being pulled taut — it steadies you.

Elliot Chen: "Okay, ready when you are. Anchor this end, I’ll tension the mesh." (He grins; a smudge of soil sits like a star in his hairline.)

You answer with a clipped sound that could be a yes because actions make words unnecessary. You set your feet against the wet decking, shoulder the panel, and together you hoist it toward the piling. The world narrows to the muscles in your arms, the wedge of cold between your ribs, the precise angle where the root-mesh must meet the corroded bolt.

[Visual: Close on your hands — callused, notebook-cord mark on your palm; moss and reclaimed glass glinting under the worklight]
[Sound: The scrape of rope, a small, delighted hiss from Elliot as something lines up]

Elliot mutters measurement numbers under his breath; you counter with an adjustment. The number of times you've argued over millimeters in the field gives you muscle memory — you don't argue to be right; you argue to be precise. His hand finds the same groove on a plank as yours do; for the half-second the world allows, the motion is perfectly in sync.

You braid root-mesh with a rhythm that steadies you — left hand, right hand, tuck-and-pull. When a stubborn vine loops itself around a knot and refuses to let go, you snap a laugh out of surprise at your own relief. The sound comes out lighter than you expect. Elliot grins, mud at his knuckles.

Elliot Chen: "You're too precise for your own good, you know." (He says it like a compliment. His green eyes catch the worklight and around them the tired, hopeful lines of a person who has stayed up nights making small things hold.)

You roll your eyes, but the tension in your shoulders loosens. Precision has always been your armor; tonight it is also your language. You counter his tease with a jab about engineering aesthetics — a private barbed joke — and he meets it, not deflecting but leaning into it. Your banter becomes a tool in itself, shaping the way you fit together on a task.

[Sound: A squat drum of thunder, closer now; the deck pitches as a gust skims the harbor]
[Music: The strings rise faster — an ascending pattern like a tide racing a seawall]

You test tension on a salvaged piling. Water pours into the prototype's basin — Elliot's tinkered irrigation doing its little rehearsed trick. You watch the reed panel drink, roots absorbing, mesh flexing, the whole thing humming with wet life. The design is rough: reed panels lashed to reclaimed glass frames, irrigation lines braided from old garden hoses, a patchwork of hope. And then it holds — not perfectly, not the engineered calm of a machine, but alive, breathing, managing the water with the cleverness of plants.

Neighbors on the deck — faces glowing in the worklamp: a woman with a looped raincoat, an older man with a handkerchief, a couple with a kayak lash across their shoulders — clap quietly, the sound more like a breath than a bang. Sami drops from a rope-lifted crate like he always does: energetic, damp, carrying thermoses that steam into the cold air and a folded list.

Sami Alvarez: "List says twelve folks can come tomorrow, maybe more if the kids can skip school." (His eyes find you for a beat, then Elliot, then the prototype. He's all grin and urgency and the kind of hope that can't wait.)

You take a thermos with both hands. It’s hot and slightly sweet; the mug steams against your cheek when you blow because you are tired enough to want that small care. Sami taps the list and then pats your shoulder with a friendly, fierce pressure.

Sami Alvarez: "Also, there's a neighbor who'll trade reclaimed corrugated metal for a spot on the wall. Says his aunt says it's 'properly resilient'." (He winks; he knows the city's language of survival.)

You laugh, sharp and relieved. It bubbles up because people have actually shown up. Because lines on paper are becoming material. Because your work is catching — not only water but attention.

The prototype sings when it holds water: a small, wet sound, like a chorus of breath. You test more panels — lift, angle, secure — each pass faster than the last. Your hazel eyes narrow into that familiar lens of concentration; in that narrow band you forget the political calculus, the council's clocks, the ledger lines that always feel like knives. All that matters is the integrity of this living thing under your hands.

Elliot Chen: "We should run a continuous cycle before dawn. If it weathers two tidal runs, Dr. Noor has the graphs she'll need." (His voice is quick, excited; his words are both technical and warm. There's a tilt in them — as if he's handing you both a plan and a question.)

You offer adjustments: anchor depth a centimeter deeper at the south face to counter the eddy, irrigation pulse at thirty-second intervals to mimic natural rain patterns. You argue softly — there’s friction, but it's productive friction; it sharpens the idea into something that will survive storms and scrutiny.

[Sound: Rain intensifies, drumming faster; the worklight lens flares as a rogue gust throws a spray of harbor water across the deck]
[Music: The heartbeat under the strings syncopates — faster now, exhilarating, breathless]

Elliot pauses, palms on his knees, breathing in the same rhythm as the night. He wipes his hands on his smock and reaches into his pocket. The vial of dune grass seeds on a leather cord taps once against his chest as he moves. He looks at the prototype, at the way the panels flexed and gave and held. Then he looks at you.

Elliot Chen: "Maya —" (He uses your name like a small anchor; it lands.) "I could keep doing this as the designer, and you could keep being... what you are. Or —" (He inhales, searching for the right balance between plan and confession.) "Or we co-lead the pilot. You bring the neighborhood, the practical rig; I bring the builds and the training. We make it impossible to ignore."

There is nothing showy in the way he asks. It's technical, political, practical — a neat package of responsibility. And then the edges shift; it is personal in the seams. The room shrinks to the distance between you and him. The rain drums faster, the harbor seems to inhale, and something inside you — the old habit of taking everything yourself — tightens like a fist.

Your chest is a map of old losses. You think of your brother's laugh carried off by a storm, of nights you stood eye-level with water and thought you could hold it back alone. You taste iron and salt and fear. The ask is an invitation to share burden, which feels like risking the one thing you've told yourself will keep others safe: your capacity to shoulder things alone.

You look at Elliot's paint-stained hands. You see the soil under his nails, the way his knuckles move when he measures and the easy light in his soft green eyes when he talks about living things. You feel the laughter you had with the stubborn vine hours ago like a tether.

Elliot Chen: "I want you on the plans. I want you in the meetings. I want… to do this with you." (He lets the sentence hang; the technical contains the personal and the personal contains the technical.)

[Visual: Tight on your face — rain beads on your lashes; your breathing matches the weather: fast, then steady, then charged]
[Sound: The rain cuts to a sharp, almost cinematic staccato as a gust passes; someone on the deck lets out a small, hopeful whoop that sounds like agreement]
[Music: The strings surge into a culminating chord — very high arousal, bright and urgent]

You taste fear — of responsibility, of letting someone else hold a corner of the weight that has become your identity. The part of you that learned to repair things when the tide took them whispers that leaning on others is a luxury you cannot afford. Another part — newer, tentative, tenacious like moss in a crack — imagines a different architecture: one where load is shared and therefore lighter.

Sami nudges you with an elbow, wordless. The neighbors shift closer. Elliot waits, the question clear in his posture and the gentle lift of his eyebrows. Your pen — the one that maps out tidal runs in your notebook — sits against your ribs, an absent weight.

You feel the city watching through the rain-sleeted air: a tension of possibility stretched as taut as a rope. The prototype glows under the worklight, moss-filtered pools pooling at your boots. The plan exists now not only as a technical scaffold but as a social promise. Someone must agree to carry it into the council and into the tide.

Elliot Chen: (softly) "Will you co-lead this with me?"

[Music: A single, sustained note that hangs just beneath the hum of the worklights; the strings hold, bright and insistent]

Your heartbeat is a drumbeat you can count: one, two, three. The arousal — your stomach, your lungs, your hands — tightens and then readies. You are at the threshold of answering the question that could change the pilot, the neighborhood, and something quieter and deeper between you and Elliot.
