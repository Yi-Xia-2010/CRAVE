[Scene: The Green Spire | Early Evening]
[Visual: Exterior — a repurposed shipping tower draped in vertical gardens; dusk light puddles on reclaimed glass]
[Sound: Soft drip of irrigation, a distant gull]

You cross because Elliot texted two words: "Prototype tonight." The Spire opens before you like an answer — warm air that smells of damp soil and sawdust, a living muffling from the mossed skylights above. Your boots hit a mat of reclaimed pallet wood. The place feels alive in a way most municipal rooms never do: small, persistent organisms turning light into oxygen, people turning scraps into structure.

[Visual: Interior — tool benches, jars of water cultures glowing faintly, a mockup seawall on a low table]
[Music: Low, hopeful synth — steady, unobtrusive]

Elliot is exactly where he said he'd be: half over the mockup, fingers still marked with sand, drafting glasses pushed up on his head though one lens hangs at a funny angle. The vial of dune-grass seeds bobs at his throat like a talisman.

Elliot: "You made it." (He wipes his hands on an oily rag and grins.) "I wasn't sure you'd—never mind. Come see."

You step closer. The mockup is small, a patchwork of salvaged timber and woven root-mat, strands of living plants threaded through tensile cables. He describes it as if he's teaching you a poem.

Elliot: "Roots don't just anchor; they share load. Think of the wall as a neighborhood of plants—each one taking a bit so the whole doesn't fail. The cables are sacrificial; they'll take the storm first, and the plants will regrow."

His voice is bright with metaphors that press against something hollow in you. You listen with the professional part of your head—evaluating tensile loads, drainage paths, maintenance cycles—while another part catalogues how easily his eyes light up when he talks about living things.

Internal: You want to be skeptical. Partnership asks you to hand over a kind of control you haven't risked since your brother's storm. Skepticism keeps you safe; risking trust feels like a fragile pilot light.

Elliot notices the hold in you and smiles softer.

Elliot: "I kept the prototype simple. Test the root-mesh with the tidal pump tonight — see if we can get consistent saturation without collapse. If it works, we scale. If not, we iterate. With you on community logistics, I think—" (He searches your face.) "—we can actually get neighbors to build it."

You let the sentence land. It's both an invitation and a gentle push.

Around the room, other presences give the moment context. Dr. Noor's weather logs lie open on a bench, tabs of dense graphs and annotated projections. The paper smells faintly of toner and old rain. A tablet in the corner leaks Irene Voss's glossy renderings; a polished model, wrapped in Luca Marin's glossy corporate sheen, gleams on a shelf like a coin.

You pick up Dr. Noor's most recent printout. The numbers are as elegant as they are unforgiving.

Dr. Noor: "If we only reinforce the boardwalk, return periods stay the same. You need porosity, gradient dissipation. Living infrastructure buys time and biodiversity." (She pushes her glasses up the bridge of her nose.) "But it requires maintenance and social governance. That is the harder variable."

You nod; this is a calculation you know intimately. Maintenance isn't sexy to funders, and social governance is the sort of slow, messy work that people with power avoid.

Sami arrives like a weather front: neon lifevest flapping, wet hair stuck to one temple, cheeks still wind-burned from deliveries. He slams a stained clipboard down on the bench — protest locations, times, a list of volunteers who've committed boats and kayaks.

Sami: "They're moving equipment through the South pier tomorrow. Luca's guys said they 'need' to mobilize early. We can't let them set pilings without a community alternative in place." (He looks at you; there's that old easy camaraderie, and something rawer underneath.) "We need noise, Maya. Pero, we need plans on the ground so we can show folks what else looks like."

You read the list. Names scrawled in different inks. The neighborhood is patched together in handwriting.

You start to map tasks on the paper in front of you, because that's how you think: names to roles, materials to sources, time to milestones. Your pen scratches while people argue softly about timelines and permits. Elliot floats ideas—sensor arrays to show real-time erosion, a pop-up demo at the Boardwalk Market, plant giveaways as proof of concept. Dr. Noor counters with measured caveats about seasonal variability and the need for longitudinal monitoring.

Dr. Noor: "Demo exposure is valuable, but make sure your sample sites are representative. Otherwise you sell a photo, not a solution."

Elliot: "Representative — yes. And theatrical. People need to see the living thing that will save their houses, not just a spreadsheet."

There is friction and it's generative. You feel the conversation spin threads together—science, showmanship, mobilization.

A neighbor—an older woman with salt-streaked hair—leans in, voice low.

Neighbor: "If you do this, will it keep water off our lower steps? My son can't climb them no more."

You meet her eyes. There is nothing theoretical about the way her fingers curl on her handbag.

Internal: This is why you do it. The big plans live in charts, but the consequence is the step of a neighbor's son.

Elliot turns the mockup so you can see the underside where the cables and root-mats meet. He points to a small sensor attached with a strip of rubber.

Elliot: "We can put a live readout at the next hearing. Tide, saturation, displacement—real numbers. People respond to touch and to data. This is both."

You think about how to thread that needle: the council wants certainty and deadlines; the neighborhood wants safety and a seat at the table.

The room gathers into a single resolved rhythm. Voices layer—Elliot's buoyant drawings of possible shorelines, Dr. Noor's measured timelines, Sami's map of urgent actions, your own notes that stitch them together. The polished model and glossy renderings remain at the edge of the Spire's light, a visual counterpoint: what Luca and Irene sell looks like order and finality. The living wall offers resilience and maintenance, messy and communal.

Irene's brochure sits on the bench like a shadow you can't ignore. You flip it open with the tip of your finger and read the headlines: "Protect the Waterfront — Jobs & Security." The tone is efficient; the images are drone-perfect. Luca's model catches the light and returns it in flat, persuasive flashes.

Luca (voice from a speaker-phone someone set up): "We can accelerate. Private partnership means immediate mobilization. Compensations for displaced residents are on the table."

Sami reacts with a curse that is half laugh, half fury.

Sami: "Compensation doesn't replace what they're taking. They want to trade us for concrete and call it progress."

You put your hand flat on your notebook and begin to write the operational list: who can build what, where to source materials, whether the community hall can host a build-training, who will monitor sensors. You find a rhythm familiar as breathing — logistics is sheltering; it is how you steady your pulse.

Dr. Noor pulls you aside and lays a small stack of annotated logs and a note: "Council dates and probabilities." Her look is both advisory and grave.

Dr. Noor: "This is the window. You have a hearing in three weeks. Data alone won't suffice; you need demonstration, constituents, and a clear maintenance plan. That last piece is why you."

There is a quiet ferocity in her voice — the tired kind that comes from years of watching slow betrayals. It settles in you like a second skin.

Elliot steps close, voice dropping so the others edge away in respectful privacy.

Elliot: "If we can have a working sensor array and a demo at the market, we show two things: that this works, and that the community can own it. That flips the political calculus."

You meet his eyes. For a moment you let yourself imagine what trust looks like: sharing credit, sharing burden, waking early to check a pump with someone whose shoulder you might one day lean on.

Internal: Partnership is not a single act. It's the steady, often boring repetition of being there. It terrifies you because absence hurt you once, and you still test the horizon for the familiar absence.

Sami slaps a hand on the table — the sound sharp enough that the jars tinkle.

Sami: "We can do the market demo this Saturday. I can get three boats, two volunteers for outreach, and the kids from the raft crew as an attention grab. But we need anchors, fasteners, and the sensors—no bullshit."

Elliot: "I can get the sensors wired tonight. We have a battery bank and a pump that can simulate a surge."

You read Dr. Noor's timeline again, then the protest list, then the names of neighbors willing to host pilot segments. The plan condenses into a line you can follow.

Neighbors murmur agreement. The room's mid-tempo energy hums; conversations are purposeful, neither frenzied nor slack. You taste metal and salt in the back of your throat, the familiar seasoning of preparation.

Your pen slows as you write the final note: "Council hearing — present demo & ask for provisional permit; show sensor data; propose maintenance co-op." The words feel authoritative on the page but fragile in the world.

Elliot watches you finish, then steps back to the mockup, smoothing a stray bit of moss with satisfaction.

Elliot: "So — stadium of plants, small sensors. Demo at market. Training at the Spire. You run the community side, I take the technical build. We ask council for a provisional window to show efficacy."

You look up at everyone assembled: neighbors, Sami's blunt confidence, Dr. Noor's steady hands, Elliot's hopeful hands. Even the glossy brochure and the model at the edge of the light feel like part of the same conversation now, not an enemy but a barometer of the stakes.

Internal: This is the moment. The plan sits on the table between you and a room full of people who will make or break it. It's both promising and terrifying.

[Music: The synth holds — steady, expectation without alarm]

You breathe in, the salt and sawdust filling your lungs. The decision is not simply a vote on a tactic; it's the point where you either take the community's voice to the council as a living, breathing thing, or you fold the plan back into yourself and keep building in quieter, smaller increments.

You straighten the page and stare at the sentence you've circled: "Present demo & ask for provisional permit."

The room's conversation lowers into a respectful hush. There is a thin expectation now — not pressure from any single person but the kind that gathers when a group awaits the next step.

Your pen hovers. The world narrows to breath and the scrawl of ink on paper.

Page-Turn: The decision is not between safe silence and reckless display. It is between a public test that could change minds and a quieter, slower proof that may never be heard. You feel a small, familiar hope — the hard kind that creeps into cracks like moss — and it nudges you toward one word: "Okay."
