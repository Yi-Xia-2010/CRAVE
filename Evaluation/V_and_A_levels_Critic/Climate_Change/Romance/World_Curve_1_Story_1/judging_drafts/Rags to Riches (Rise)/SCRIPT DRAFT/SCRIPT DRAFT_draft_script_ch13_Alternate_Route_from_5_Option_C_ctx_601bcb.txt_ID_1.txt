[Scene: Flooded Dock | Night — The Surge]
[Visual: Sky brightens at the horizon; a band of weather approaches like a distant wall]
[Sound: A rising rumble; wind gathers salt and voice]
[Music: Crescendo into full, urgent percussion — drums, brass staccato]

The spring surge arrives earlier than all your models predicted—an inexact thing that smells like dug-up mud and old rope. Water slaps against the pilings with a new, hungry rhythm. The panels shudder as the first wave hits, flexing like the ribs of a living thing.

For a terrifying half-second you see everything as numbers and stress charts and the face of your brother in a sodium streetlight. Then the pulse kicks in—a sequence of micro-irrigations synchronized with the waves. The hydrogels bloom and give, releasing measured wetness just as the panels flex. Roots, sodden and thin, find one another across seams. Fibers knit.

Neighbors shout and run lines, tightening anchors. Someone tangles with a sandbag; someone else lances a blocked valve. The work is a chorus of wet hands and shouted coordinates.

Elliot yells over the wind: "Hold your side! Don't let the midline go!" His voice is almost drowned, but you feel it down to your knuckles.

You: "I've got it!" (you haul on a ratchet, a bolt groans, and the frame bites like a jaw)

[Sound: A massive wave slaps the living spine; water curls where concrete would have shattered. The panels flex, then spring back, a muscle remembering its function.]

There is a heart-stopping creak. For a breath, you wait for failure. Instead, the frames settle into new compression. Sensors chirp: nodes across the line report increased cohesion. The tablet flashes green.

Sami's laugh breaks the tension into shards of sound that land like coins.

Sami: "It's holding! It's actually holding!"

Neighbors clap each other wetly on the back; a kid hoots and splashes in a puddle that used to be a street. The jubilation is bright and immediate—wet palms, sloppy hugs, faces scraped with salt and tears.

[Music: Release—triumphant, fast, the strings racing upward into a bright fanfare]

Elliot Chen: "You did that." (his voice cracks around both the science and the risk)

You: "We did it." (you say it because it's true; you say it because you're no longer only yourself)

He grins—a sheepish, luminous grin that crinkles the soil at the corners of his eyes. You find yourself smiling back until your face hurts.

[Scene: Low Dock — Aftermath | Night -> Early Dawn]

By morning, the city is louder. Video from a neighbor's drone has been shared in enough channels to make the mayor's assistant call for an emergency review. Luca's team has photos, legal drafts, and a fury lubricated by offended profit margins. City Hall sends an auditor; Irene Voss's office issues cautious statements promising both accountability and study.

You read the headlines with dripping gloves: "Unpermitted Living Seawall Holds Through Surge"—and underneath, "Emergency Review Opened." The sentence sits like a badge and a bruise.

Dr. Noor Patel calls just after dawn, her voice a steadying hand.

Dr. Noor: "You bought us time and data. That surge gave us a real-world stress test—better than any simulation. Expect pushback, but expect interest too. This will force an actual conversation at council." (soft, precise)

You: "They could criminalize what we did." (the fear is blunt as an exposed nail)

Dr. Noor: "They could. Or they'll have to adapt policy to match reality. Politics moves faster when something is demonstrably working." (a brief, almost tender pause) "You did more than hack hardware. You hacked the timetable."

Elliot stands at your side as you listen. He tucks his smudged drafting glasses into his smock and watches the harbor. For all the noise of legal threats, his expression is almost foolishly calm.

Elliot Chen: "They'll send letters. Maybe a lawyer or two. Maybe Luca will try to spin it as 'reckless'." (he shrugs) "But they also can't unsee a living wall that bent the tide."

You think of your brother—the loss that taught you to split work from want, to armor your edges. Tonight the chord between you and Elliot hums with a different possibility: partnership folded into purpose. The success is messy and precarious; it has enemies and lawyers in suits and the risk of being unmade by policy as easily as it was made by sweat.

Neighbors come by in trickles—old Mrs. Hales with tea handed in a chipped mug, teenagers who spent last night hauling lines returning to point and laugh at the memory of how terrified they were. Someone wraps you in a soaked blanket. Your skin prickles; the pendant on your chest catches a streetlamp and flashes once, like an exclamation point.

You scan the living spine: webbed roots that now hold the line, salt-tolerant foliage that flexes and drinks. The city will respond in its own slow, political way—threats, hearings, possibly criminal charges—but you've done something immediate and undeniable. You altered the timeline.

Elliot slips his hand close enough to yours that fingers brush. It is not a promise of forever, not yet, but it is the quiet, insistent proof that someone else will stand in the water with you.

Elliot Chen: "They can try to criminalize us. They can try to shut it down. But they can't pretend it didn't work." (firm)

You: "And we can't pretend there won't be consequences." (a straight, honest edge)

You let the tidal wind take both statements away into the city. Triumph and liability sit together like wet stones—heavy, cooled by surf.
