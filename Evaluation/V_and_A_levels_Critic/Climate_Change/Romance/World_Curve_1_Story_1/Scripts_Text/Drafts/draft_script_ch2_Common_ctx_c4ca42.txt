# Route: From Chapter 11, Option B


[Scene: Green Spire | Evening]
[Visual: The shipping tower framed against a bruise of low cloud; vertical gardens drip along patched glass panes]
[Sound: Soft drip of water cultures into jars, a distant gull, the creak of pulleys]
[Music: Warm, low-key ambient — steady, not urgent]

You come up the walkways with salt and diesel still in your throat because Elliot's message was short and theirs always are: Prototype tonight. The Spire smells like damp soil and sawdust, and the air inside is warmer, humid and alive — the kind of warmth that makes your fingers remember how to untie knots. Light passes through moss-filtered skylights in a soft green wash; shelves of jars glitter with cultured water, and a tangle of rope and reclaimed rebar leans against a bench like sleeping animals.

You set your field notebook back into its strap because tonight the plans are physical. You can feel the loosened thread of the crowd’s attention from the previous hour still knotting around your ribs. Saying things in public was a test; getting your hands dirty is another. Both require you to leave the safety of maps and go where people can see you falter.

[Visual: Elliot at the center bench, half-turned toward you; a small mockup sits between his hands]
[Sound: A soft tap of measuring calipers]

Elliot: "There you are." (He pushes his drafting glasses up with two fingers; sand flecks like constellation dust in his fringe.) "I wasn't sure you'd come."

You: "You sent three words and a blinking emoji. That's not—" Your voice narrows; you are sharp because this is easier than the other kind of ask. "—an invitation."

Elliot: (He grins, small and apologetic.) "It's a prototype night. A prototype is a promise that you can measure. Easier to debate than feelings, right?" He taps the mockup, a strip of living matrix woven with root bundles and salvaged polymer seams. "Roots as tensile loads. Living riprap. The model's only half the story."

You crouch across from him, fingers hovering near the mockup without touching. The miniature roots look stubborn and earnest. Elliot's eyes brighten when he talks about them; his sentences come out in green metaphors that make you want to roll your eyes and then, against expectation, listen.

Elliot: "Plants distribute force in ways we don't always give them credit for. You let them hold tension, and they hold memory — if we bind old pilings to living crowns, the wall becomes a neighbor, not an appliance."

You: "Or it becomes more competent at failing in untested conditions." You let the skepticism in your tone do the work that caution usually does. "What are your failure modes? How does it respond to a ten-year surge? To debris impact?"

Elliot: (He nods, patient.) "We've simulated for surge envelopes and debris strike. The matrix flexes; the root mass dampens shear. We still need field data. Which is why—" He taps his patched smartwatch as if it can make the data pop into being. "—we need tonight's sensor run. The array, the flow gauges. Noor's logs will tell us what to watch."

[Visual: Dr. Noor at the bench, leather folio open; tide graphs and annotated field notes spread like a scientific fan]
[Sound: The soft whisper of flipping paper]

Dr. Noor: "I ran two retention scenarios this afternoon." (Her voice is even, clinical, a counterpoint to Elliot's warmth.) "If we prioritize modular installation at the storm thresholds, projected run-off lowers by seventeen percent in the modeled grid. But that's conditional — soils, compaction, maintenance. The community has to commit to long-term upkeep or the model decays."

You: (Quiet) "Maintenance is the hard line. Not building it — keeping it alive after the cameras leave."

Dr. Noor: (She doesn't look up immediately; when she does, her gaze is steady.) "Exactly. Which is why coupling with local labor programs and modular design matters. This isn't about hero projects; it's about durable practices."

You run a thumb along the edge of the mockup, thinking about rot and the loyalty you owe to neighbors who trusted you with their roofs. Elliot watches your hand with something like wonder. The mild tension in your chest loosens a fraction; plans are things you can name.

You glance toward a corner where someone left a tablet open. Irene's glossy renderings slide across the screen in polished layers—slick animation of broad concrete swathes, tidy promenades, and tidy headlines. They look like a promise you have taught yourself to resist and also like the kind of thing people who want to be seen crave. Across the bench, small scale models with mahogany veneers glint in the lamplight; Luca's aesthetic hand is obvious even if he isn't standing at the table.

You: "Who's—" The question fades before you finish it; the Schrödinger of supply chains. Maybe Luca dropped them. Maybe a municipal aide left them for translation work. The provenance is fuzzy and the meaning is clearer: money, polished and tempting.

Elliot: (Softly) "Renderings are seductive. We can't let the gloss set our agenda." (He smiles too easily and then steadies.) "But they also tell us what language to fight in. If their pitch is 'progress as spectacle', ours is 'progress as neighborhood.'"

[Sound: A thud and a slap of neoprene; a fluorescent lifevest flaps like a flag as Sami bursts through the door]
[Visual: Sami, neon lifevest, hair in a braid, breath of wind carrying the smell of fried dough from the Boardwalk Market]

Sami: "Okay! Locations for tomorrow—Pier 6, the flooded wing of the Market, two barricade points by the old pumping station—" (She slams a handwritten list on the bench. The paper flutters and the little jars collect in its wake.) "—and we need volunteers to bring tools. I've got six kayaks and twelve people willing to chain themselves to—" She stops when the bench's attention goes quiet.

You: "Sami." The single name holds more than reprimand; it holds calculation. "We need presence at the hearing, not just a spectacle. The council moves on recorded testimony."

Sami: (She bristles, hands on hips.) "Presence, spectacle — same work. People show up more when they can see the river making them hungry. They're not going to read graphs. They'll watch a line of bodies."

Dr. Noor: "Both matter." (Her voice is a scalpel.) "Public demonstration creates political weight. Empirical evidence creates policy foundation. We need to marry them."

Sami: (She throws a look at you, all fire and history.) "You led the last sandbag run. You can lead this. Rally them, tell them what's at stake, and we'll back you."

You feel the room tilt very slightly; the stakes are not abstract anymore. Elliot slides a small vial—dune-grass seeds—on the table toward you. It's a silent offering: tactile, fragile, possible.

You write names and materials with the same steady hand you use to trace floodlines: who can weld, who has tide sensors, where to source pilings, which neighbors are willing to host workshops. The list grows, a lattice forming under the talk. In the margins you note maintenance schedules and contact windows for volunteer cohorts. Elliot asks about scaffold attachments; Dr. Noor reminds you of soil compaction thresholds. Sami argues for a rolling protest calendar synced to the council timetable.

Neighbors drift in and out — a woman who runs a solar co-op, a teen who mends nets, an older man with salt-callused hands. They don't speak all of the time; when they do, it's short and practical. The Spire feels smaller and fuller at once, like a room drawn to a single point.

Luca's models gleam again in the corner, anonymous and bright. Whoever left them traded their tact for shine; it doesn't reduce their power. You find yourself imagining the council chamber with those glossy renderings projected as gentleness — or as erasure. Dr. Noor's graphs whisper constraints: deadlines, budget lines, maintenance viability. Sami's list insists on immediacy: bodies in place, voices in the room.

Elliot: "We can get the prototype installed at Market Edge overnight. If we have volunteers and the sensor feed, we can bring live data to the hearing." (There is an eagerness under his words.) "Show the council something that isn't a render."

You: (You feel the responsibility fold over you like a familiar jacket.) "I can speak. But I won't toss a plan into a room without the facts. We need the installation to be defensible and repeatable." Your fingers press harder into your notebook; the spine creaks.

Dr. Noor: "Council hearing is two weeks." She says it like a deadline, not a suggestion. "That's our window to collect baseline runs and refine the prototype. If we can present a tested modular and a community maintenance plan, the argument becomes harder to ignore."

[Sound: A distant storm siren, faint — a reminder, not a panic]
[Music: The ambient track gains a slightly more rhythmic undercurrent — a heartbeat of planning]

You look around at the faces lit by filtered green light: earnest, exhausted, sharp. Elliot's eyes find yours and hold them; there's an invitation there that is neither dramatic nor coy. It's logistical and personal at once — help me do this, help me trust that we can keep it alive after the halo of attention fades.

You think of the line you almost spoke in public, the pull between stepping forward and fixing things alone. The room waits, not impatient but expectant, like tide-locals listening for the tide to decide its own time.

Your throat tightens with the same small hope you felt at the bench in the last hour — the kind of hope that doesn't promise victory, just possibility. It feels like moss finding a crack: not loud, but tenacious. The people around you begin to edge their chairs inward, as if the plan requires less space and more breath.

[Visual: Close on your notebook as you uncapping the pen; the tip hovers over the page]
[Sound: The click of a pen cap like the hush before a speech]

You can feel the room's attention fold into you. The window of the council date sits like an axis: two weeks to turn a prototype into proof, and you the fulcrum. The list in front of you is practical, full of names and materials and times. The Spire hums with steady, collaborative noise — a midpoint of momentum.

You breathe once, slow and deliberate, feeling the draft of the tower pull at your jacket. The decision has the shape of the bridge you are always building in your head: not heroic, not solitary — structural. Tonight is for building, two weeks are for proving, and the hearing will be for asking.
