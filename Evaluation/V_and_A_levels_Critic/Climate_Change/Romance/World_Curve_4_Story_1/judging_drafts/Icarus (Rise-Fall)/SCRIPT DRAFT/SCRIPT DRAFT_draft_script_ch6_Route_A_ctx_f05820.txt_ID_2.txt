[Scene: Promenade | Night — The Wall Gives]
[Visual: Concrete and rebar that used to look like protection tear like paper; LED floodlights strobe in wet air]
[Sound: A long, monstrous cracking, then a roar as water finds new paths]

The Promenade was supposed to be the line between town and ocean—a place of commerce, of engineered certainty. It now becomes the place the certainty breaks.

You don't have the time to think around it: a section of the seawall collapses like a mouth opening. Cars are pushed; chain-link fencing becomes sail. Concrete slabs pivot and fall. Someone on the other side is screaming a name that will echo in your head all night.

Elias is there, sleeves rolled, knee-deep in slush. He is shouting technical commands into a soaked radio while also reaching for a stranded neighbor, the practical and the immediate braided together. For a single slice of time he performs both the engineer and the rescuer, shifting roles with a speed that is part of why people trusted him.

Mara (You): "Elias—here—now! The northern slab—it's moving!"

Elias Navarro: "I see it—(we need) anchors—now—Tommy, wedge the rebar!" He hurls his shoulder into a rusted piece of metal, trying to leverage a path. Rain drives into his face; the notebook is gone, puddled in the water.

You run down the slab with him, the wood underfoot slick, and then gravity answers with a sound like a thousand doors opening at once. The boardwalk splits. People who stood on it are pitched like rag dolls.

For a breathless moment the sea and sky have replaced the language of reason. You watch a neighbor—Mrs. Halloway—lift her face to you with eyes the color of storm glass, and somewhere a child is crying for someone named "Papa."

You reach for hands. Elias reaches for the same ones. Your fingers close on the same wrist; the world tilts. The two of you haul until your muscles scream, and then something gives—a hand slips, a shoe comes off. Somebody goes under.

You and Elias manage to pull two people free. You don't get to pull them all.

[Scene: Tidewatch Lab | Night — Emergency Engineering]
[Visual: Glass lit by monitor glow; wet boots on tile; coils of cable like sleeping snakes]
[Sound: The bonk of a generator being coaxed to life; the low whine of pumps]

Inside Tidewatch, Amina moves with a calm that mechanism cannot explain—years of field triage compressed into concise commands. She accepts data like a priest accepts confessions and turns them into action.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We triage the critical nodes—power to the pumps first, then the temporary coffer dams. Elias, I need you to run the anchoring specs. Mara, can you coordinate evac points? We need unbroken human chains to the high points."

You nod and the nod becomes work. Rae shuttles radios. Volunteers strap themselves to wooden pallets to act as makeshift buoyancy platforms. Someone folds a tarp into a stretcher with hands that are small and sure.

Elias kneels over a schematic projected on a tablet—lines and numbers that mean lives. He runs fingers through wet hair, jaw clenched with the thousand micro-decisions engineers learn to hold without breath.

Elias Navarro: "If we anchor at these three points and blow the eastern coffer 0.2 meters lower, the flow will redirect into the breach and keep the neighborhoods to the west from overtopping. It's a gamble—"

Mara (You): "A gamble people are already paying for."

Amina's voice cuts through, not the soft of diplomacy but the steel of someone who has seen projects die in courtrooms and lives die in storms.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We have to act. This is triage."

Elias looks at you while he says it—eyes searching. For solace? For permission? For atonement? You don't have the kindness to give and the rage to refuse. The air between you is a rope taut with what remains unsaid.

You move with a volunteer group into the neighborhoods that did not have the luxury of elevation—rents cheaper here, histories longer, anchors older. Houses are tilted; furniture floats in swollen kitchens. People clutch papers, a family album, a cat in a damp blanket.

[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Dawn — Aftermath & Fracture]
[Visual: Dawn is a bruised bruiser; the Commons is a gutted mosaic of mud, salt, and discarded signs. The sky is a flat pewter.]

You walk the Commons in the thin light. The seedlings you planted last spring are sodden and unrecognizable, the boardwalk that once carried laughter now a line of grief. Volunteers sort through belongings—mattresses, a wet box of letters, a kettle that seems obscene in its intactness.

Tommy comes up beside you without speaking. He hands you a damp photograph: a younger you, hair windblown at a summer fair, smiling with someone who is not present tonight and will not be because the town has moved the axes of life around like capricious weights.

Tommy: "We did what we could." He looks unable to make it mean enough.

You fold the photo into your palm like a talisman and feel a stone settle in your stomach where hope used to be.

Elias approaches with Amina at his back. She looks tired, but the lines around her mouth are set with the quiet of someone who has seen the calculus and tried to feed it to survival.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We prevented a collapse in the west. We lost property in the north and east sectors. We'll catalog damage, prioritize relief."

Mara (You): "And then what? We come back to council meetings and talk about phased relocations while people sleep in gymnasiums?"

Amina's answer is the sober one: she speaks neither for Celeste nor for you but for the ledger of lives.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "There will be funding for emergency relief. There will be a review of the Promenade's design, an inquiry. We need a plan that includes buyouts, alternative housing, and large-scale restoration. It will take money and time."

Mara (You): "Time is a currency we just discovered we don't have."

Elias says nothing for a moment; his face is a map of the night. Then his voice is small but steady—an engineer switching to human-level honesty.

Elias Navarro: "Mara. I—(I am) not leaving. I will help rebuild. I will help write the grant applications, stand in the hearings, do whatever is—"

Mara (You): "You will do what your institutions ask of you. That is what you promised."

Elias's mouth goes tight, the old reflex of compromise and measured language. He tries to reach a bridge.

Elias Navarro: "I promised people I'd try to keep them safe. I tried, and it cost us. I didn't—(I didn't) expect the sea to take so many corners."

You realize the accusation sits in your chest like a living thing, hungry for something to feed on.

Mara (You): "You think you can be the person who signs the grant papers and still sleep in the same bed as an organizer who remembers what we've already lost?"

Elias's face hardens in a way he seldom lets the public see; then softens. This is not a contest of wills for him but a fracturing of a promise neither of you wrote neatly.

Elias Navarro: "I don't know how to be both without breaking. I thought I could. I thought—"

He doesn't finish. Neither of you finishes.

Elias Navarro: "If forgiveness is a spreadsheet, we've already failed. But if it's built on work, then I want—"

He closes his jaw and turns away. He walks down toward the Promenade with Amina calling after plans and volunteers. The distance between you is measured not in feet but in the collapse of shared assumptions.

You stand alone in the Commons. The mud squishes between your boots. You are a person who keeps showing up, who adopts other people's tired causes like plants seeking sunlight. You have done more than that tonight; you have hauled, shouted, begged, and held. And still, the sea has taken what it wished.

You think of the sibling you lost to a storm years ago—the memory like a scar pressed into place. Tonight the scar spreads.

You cannot make the water go back into the place it came from. You cannot instantly repair the trust that collapsed with the seawall. Elias will do his work in the rooms that smell of finance and policy. You will do your work in the streets. You are not the same person as the one who climbed the rooftop earlier. The night has rearranged you.
