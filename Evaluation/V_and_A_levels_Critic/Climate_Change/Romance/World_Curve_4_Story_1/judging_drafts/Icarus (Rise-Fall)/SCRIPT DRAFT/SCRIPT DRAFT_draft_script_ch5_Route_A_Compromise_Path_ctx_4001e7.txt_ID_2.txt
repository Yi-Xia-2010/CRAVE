Celeste Park: (leaning forward) "Scour is manageable. We can add geotextile reinforcement and accelerate compaction. The timeline is critical; delays mean cost overruns and the cooperative withdraws contingencies."

You want to answer, to point out that "manageable" often translates into "done without consent," but Elias speaks for you both.

Elias Navarro: "We can add reinforcement, but Amina's right: a full subsurface investigation is prudent. We also need contingency language that doesn't let funding dry up if we ask for longer testing."

Celeste's jaw tightens—a micro-expression you catch, a calculus taken against public optics.

Celeste Park: "We can write contingency allowances. But community leadership needs to commit publicly tonight. The council needs a headline: forward motion. Private delays will look like obstruction."

Her eyes slide to you, measuring whether you'll be the obstacle or the bridge.

You feel the room narrowing—every word a lever. Your chest is a drum. Outside, the sky has gone a blue-gray; the promenade sketch on the projector looks like an invitation and a threat at once.

You crouch, hands on cold rock, fingers bloodying on an old nail. The risk is concrete and immediate.

Elias Navarro: "We're not talking about preventing a future someday—this is now. We can patch locally and continue the timeline, or we can pause and reinforce comprehensively."

Celeste Park: (into the dark) "Fast fixes exist. They add cost, but the cooperative can absorb initial repairs. In exchange, we tighten oversight and accelerate the first phase. If we stall for a full retrofit, costs double and momentum dies."

Elias Navarro: "Or we bury a long-term risk under a quick fix. That's not acceptable to the community, not to me."

Celeste Park: (cool) "Acceptable to some, perhaps. We can't be paralyzed by worst-case speculation. The cooperative's model depends on predictable rollouts."

You are pulled between outcomes—public momentum versus engineering prudence. The salt air tastes metallic in your mouth. Somewhere a gull cries like a question.

You push the point because it matters; because your memories—and the memory of your sibling—are an internal firewall against vanity projects that call themselves salvation.

Mara Lin: "We want co-management. We want local oversight with teeth. And we want safe, enforceable emergency stop conditions that protect lives and beds alike."
