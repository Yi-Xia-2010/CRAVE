[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Dawn — hours after the storm]

[Visual: Wide shot of the Commons — boardwalk splintered, raised beds overturned, tidal pools black with oil-sheen detritus]
[Sound: Distant sirens; gulls calling once, subdued; the steady, wet slap of tide against broken wood]
[Music: Percussive, frantic strings; low brass like a held grief]

You move before you think. The first step is over a plank that used to be a bench; the next is through mud that feels like someone else's memory. The Commons is a body that has been pushed through a narrow throat: ribs of boardwalk jut up like broken teeth, seedlings float like refuse, and the smell — rot and diesel and the sharp bite of fresh rain — hits you and keeps hitting you.

Your hands remember tools. Your hands remember the rope-ladder and the rooftop tiles and the way you taught a child to plant with two fingers. Now they are sticky with salt, trembling enough that you can feel it in your teeth.

Tommy is there, hunched over a collapsed raised bed. He looks up when you reach him; his face is a map of salt and lines carved by years at sea. He doesn't smile.

Tommy: "Morning... if you can call it that. We pulled three families out near Harbor Lane. Two were—" (He stops, jaw hardens.) "—lost in the lower terraces."

You don't need him to say the word. It hangs anyway, heavier than the wind.

You: "Names."

Tommy: (He exhales as if pushing water out of his lungs.) "Maris and her boy. Old Mr. Halvorsen. The clinic took what they could. Rae's with the triage—she's shaking." He looks at you with a question that has no good answers. "What did you hear on the radio?"

You remember the green light, your own voice through static. You said words that offered a map. You did not, could not, say everything.

You: "Plans. Coordinates. We tried to prioritize." (Your voice is a thread pulled taut.) "We did what we could."

Tommy stares a long time, then nods, not comforted. Around you, volunteers move with an animal focus — wrap, carry, shout — the town operating on muscle and memory. The Commons smells of uprooted life.

Rae moves like a person wearing someone else's skin. She sees you and her mouth opens, a question, then closes.

Rae: "You were the one on the radio. People listened. Some of them—" (Her breath breaks.) "Some of them didn't make it, Mara."

You: "We need names, lists. We need—" (You cut yourself off. The word 'need' sounds small.) "We need blankets, chainsaws, the med kit from Tidewatch."

Rae: "Chainsaws are here. There's a lineup at the boathouse for the vans. People keep asking if the Promenade plan is what broke them or fixed them." (She laughs, a sound like a snapped wire.) "Nobody wants that answer. Not yet."

Her eyes find Elias before you do. He's at the edge of the Commons, near the ragged fence where survey flags used to flutter like promises. He is up to his elbows in mud, sleeves rolled, looking more like a man who is trying to understand how the world bent than like the architect of solutions. He holds a tablet; the screen is scrambled with water and code.

You walk to him because you always walked to him when the world gave you puzzles instead of consolations.

Elias: (Quiet, near-broken) "We rerouted three teams. The sensor buoy readings were... wrong in one sector. The models didn't account for the compounded overflow at the lower terraces. I—" (He rubs his forehead.) "I mapped a thousand things and missed the one line that mattered."

You: "You missed it?"

Elias: "We all missed it. The storm did things the models didn't expect. The levee failed at a seam we thought redundant." His hands clench. "If we'd prioritized the Commons or the hospital, maybe—" He stops. His face is a geography of regret.

You both stand in that broken silence where blame is a bright knife. Celeste watches from a small mound of rubble — immaculate notwithstanding the mud spattered on her coat — arms folded. She is present like a machine evaluating inputs. Her face doesn't unfurl to grief; it tightens into decision.

Celeste: (Even) "This is not the time for recrimination. We need shelter, power, and stabilized access. We mobilize crews. We open the promenade as a line of supply if the infrastructure holds."

You: "Open the promenade? With the rebar exposed and the pier sheared? You want to invite more—"

Celeste: (Interrupting) "People need bridges, Mara. They need work and a way to get out of wet houses into dry spaces. My team's ready to coordinate emergency funding."

Something in that offer tastes like salt and coin. Her precise solutions are both balm and blade. You can see why some would choose her certainty. You can see why others spit when she speaks it.

[Scene: Promenade | Midday — the breach and the emergency lines]

[Visual: Cranes silhouetted against a bruised sky; a stretch of promenade caved into the gray sea]
[Sound: Metal groaning; men shouting into radios; the intermittent thunk of falling debris]
[Music: High-tempo strings, percussion like falling rain]

You walk along the edge where the town meets the sea. The Promenade is a wound; a splice of concrete hangs over the water like a jaw missing teeth. People are shouting, arguing, organizing—orders and grief braided into the same voice. Volunteers form human chains; a child hands packages down and doesn't ask for a medal.

Dr. Amina Bhatt meets you near a pile of supplies. Her glasses are chipped; her instruments are covered in silt. She looks at the soil probes then at you, and her face is a ledger of facts that still wants to be human.

Amina: "We need to move sediment cores now, before the next surge. The microbial activity will shift and with it the floodplain's trajectory." (She pauses, softer.) "Mara, there are banks asking whether to fortify with riprap or seed the marsh. Either choice commits decades."

You: "People are sleeping under plastic. They don't need decades; they need roofs. But they also need future rooms on dry ground. There isn't enough to do both."

Amina: "Then we triage. Life-sustaining first, then long-term. It's not pretty. It's necessary."

You: (You want to say that 'necessary' sometimes feels like an excuse.) "We tried triage in different orders for different neighborhoods. The results read like a map of who gets to live and who gets to remember."

Amina: (Her hand closes for a second, then opens.) "Remember, Mara — policy is an accumulation of choices. You shape the next accumulation."

The wind takes the word. It spits sand into your open palm.

A rail of people watches you. Some want speeches. Some want your hands in the dirt. You choose — but the choice is a seam stitched over bleeding.

[Scene: Various Neighborhoods | Afternoon — salvage, graves, and the slow counting of names]

[Visual: Narrow streets lined with houses half-submerged; neighbors stand in doorways, faces streaked with ash and salt]
[Sound: Muffled sobs, a distant dog barking, the constant whisper of the tide]
[Music: Low, mournful cello; then a sudden high, piercing violin that breaks into silence]

You go where the calls lead. You find a house you know: a red trim, a crooked mailbox. A child's toy sits in muck, half swallowed. A photograph frames a smile you remember from a picnic on a different shore. You gather the photo and the toy like evidence of what the town asked you to guard and failed to hold.

You see a cluster of people at the end of a street, a ring of bodies around a form under a soaked blanket. You're one of many leaning close to the small dark face when Rae says the name. You know the name without needing to hear it.

Elias arrives at your side. His hand finds yours — or it doesn't, depending on what history gave you — but whether contact is made or not, there is a tremor between you. His voice is closer now, raw.

Elias: "I ran the simulations every night. I thought — I told myself the models would keep us honest. I'm sorry." (Not an argument: an offering.)

You: "Sorry is not a life raft." (The words are brittle.) "It helps at the edges. It doesn't rebuild a house."

Elias: "I know." (He swallows.) "But I can help rebuild. I'll stand in the mud. I'll write the grant. I'll—" (He looks at you, searching for a place to land.) "I'll do the work you wanted. If you'll let me."

You look at him. You look at the blanket. You look at the child's toy in your palm. Your chest is a drum that won't keep steady time.

You: "What if the work you're offering is the kind of thing that changes who we are? What if it takes the Commons away in the name of 'stability'?"

Elias: "Then we fight to keep it. I don't think building something has to erase roots. I—" (He hesitates, awareness and frustration tangling.) "—I can't promise it won't be messy. I can promise I'll try differently."

Celeste approaches with a clipboard and with the same precise calm as a storm engineer. She watches the two of you, unreadable as a locked chest.

Celeste: "There are plans to erect temporary cofferdams and a supply corridor. We can prioritize community spaces in the next phase, if we secure funding now. Storytelling later, logistics now."

Her words are minefields and bridges both. You feel the old ache tighten in your throat — the sibling's absence, the funeral you had no power to stop. The world is offering calculus when you need hold.

[Music: Rapid, discordant motifs — the emotional tempo spasms high — then a single, piercing sustained note]

You watch as people pass by with lanterns and blankets and lists of names. Someone screams for help from a collapsed walkway; volunteers sprint. A child laughs somewhere, unbearably alive. The sirens grow closer; a bell rings to call people to a makeshift shelter. You taste iron and the sea and the memory of a hand you couldn't hold.

Everything is very loud. Everything is very small. The choice about what you will do next feels both meaningless and apocalyptic.

You think of the sibling again — their voice, the joke, the water taking them. The ghost of that memory is not historical; it is immediate and sharp. You feel the weight of it inside your chest like a bruise.

Elias reaches for your shoulder. This time he doesn't say "models." He says, simply, "Stay with me. Help me."

You want to. You want to climb and breathe and choose and make it right.

You also know that some things cannot be made right. Loss accumulates like silt; it reshapes the shore whether you will it to or not.

Your knees give in a way you didn't expect. You sit down on a slab of concrete, rain drumming a tempo on the edges of the world. Around you people bandage, argue, pray, and curse — the town in every note of a minor key.

A distant roar rises: equipment arriving, voices amplified, a crowd deciding. The scene compresses into staccato images — a child's shoelace, the salt on an eyelash, Celeste pointing at a map, Amina folding her hands and counting. You feel the last of your defences thin.

You place the child's toy into the palm of the person next to you — an older woman who nods and tucks it into her coat. It is a small consecration.

You raise your head. The sky is the color of bruised metal. The tide is still moving in deliberate, indifferent negotiation with the shore.

You speak, and the words are not a choice so much as a closure.

You: "We will bury them. We will name them. We will try to fix what we can. And if some things must be given up to save others, we'll learn to keep the memory alive anyway."

Elias: (His voice breaks.) "I will help you keep it. In whatever way you let me."

Celeste: (From the edge of the crowd, a tight smile there to be read as plan or victory) "Then let's get to work."

You look at each face: Rae, eyes like flint; Tommy, hands raw; Amina, cataloging; Celeste, ledger-open; Elias, offer-open. Each of them is a direction your life might take, a different grammar of repair.

You stand. Your legs are slow to obey.

You take one step toward the graves they've started to dig on the high ground, then another. You lift dirt with your hands. The motion is elemental and obscene and necessary all at once. People pass wrapped photographs and flowers; someone whispers a name. The crowd forms a rope of bodies that is both the town and the future.

[Visual: Close on your face — tear streaks, mud, a small, stubborn set to the jaw]
[Music: A single, low brass note holds; beneath it, faint strings fold into a fragile, ending motif]

In that moment, the ending is not a punctuation but a slow turning of the shore. You cannot un-choose what happened. You cannot un-summon the tide. What you can do is fold your grief into action, into tending, into the lineup of ways to save what can be saved.

You know, as your hands grow numb, that the rest of your life will be counted in increments of repair and remembrance. You will name the dead at meetings and in private; you will shout at officials and sit with engineers; you will hold a child's toy and feel the way it weighs the heart. You will be angry. You will be tender. You will be exhausted.

For a breath, for a long breath, you allow the collapse to move through you. It is terror and a kind of purging. Then you let it settle into something like a vow.

You look up once more at the horizon where sea meets sky and say, not to anyone in particular, only to the air and the salt and the memory:

You: "We will remember."
