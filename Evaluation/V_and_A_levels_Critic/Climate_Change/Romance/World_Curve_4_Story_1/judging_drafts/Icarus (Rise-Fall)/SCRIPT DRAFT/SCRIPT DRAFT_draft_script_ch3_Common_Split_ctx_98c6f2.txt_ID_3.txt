You walk into the chamber with the aftertaste of the rooftop still in your mouth — salt and tea, the warm agreement in Elias's voice like something you could fold into your coat. The room hits you all at once: the buzz of people trading short, urgent sentences, the citrus tang of someone’s hand sanitizer, the faint metallic smell that always follows a roomful of wet coats. Under those new smells, the older scent of Solhaven — brine, diesel, compost — threads through the air like memory.

[Visual: Close on Mara Lin — fingers curled around her satchel strap, soil smudges on her knuckles]
[Music: Violin motif rises softly; energy steady, purposeful]

You find a place in the back where you can watch without being a target. Rae is there, shoulders squared, camera slung like a banner; Tommy sits a row forward, jaw working as if he’s chewing the words he might have to say. Dr. Amina Bhatt is in the front, notebook open, her presence a steadying pressure. Elias stands near the podium, his tablet tucked under one arm, his expression careful but bright when his eyes find you. It’s not just that he’s here — it’s that he’s chosen to be here, beside the political clench and the possibility of compromise. That thought eases something in your chest.

Councilor Finch bangs the gavel. Light from high windows slices across the wooden benches. Celeste Park takes the stage with the precise, deliberate smile you’ve seen in press photographs — a smile cut to project reassurance.

Celeste Park: "Good evening. Thank you all for coming. The Promenade will bring jobs, tourism, and the long-term funding Solhaven has been promised for years. We propose a reinforced seawall, a periodic dredging program, and a commercial promenade that will anchor our waterfront economy."

Her words are clean, schematic: numbers, timelines, renderings on a screen behind her that show smooth promenades and neat planters. A few people in the crowd nod; a few scoff. Her voice carries as if she’s reading the future from a ledger and already balanced the columns.

You feel the room waiting for the counterpoint. The suspense is not antagonistic — it has the tone of a town holding its breath before it decides which way to rebuild.

Tommy leans forward, breath rough with the old salt of the sea.

Tommy: "Celeste — you paint a pretty picture. But where does that leave the small boats? Where do folks who make their living with lines and nets go while you build?"

Celeste’s composure doesn’t falter. Her answer is efficient, polished.

Celeste Park: "There are provisions in the plan, Mr. Reyes. Temporary moorings, compensation packages. We are designing with minimal disruption."

Tommy: "Compensation doesn't replace a season. Compensation don't teach the seas to behave different."

The exchange raises the pitch of the room just a touch; a ripple of agreement and some thin gasps follow Tommy’s words. You feel the heat from the crowd as if it were sunlight.

Elias steps up when the chair opens the floor to technical questions. He smooths his sleeves like someone gathering his thought-frames into something presentable.

Elias Navarro: "If I may — the Promenade need not be all-or-nothing. We've been modeling a hybrid pilot: a low-profile, living buffer integrated with engineered reinforcements. We test on a designated stretch, monitor sediment, community livelihoods, and economic flow, and scale what works."

His voice is earnest, and as he speaks, his hands outline a compromise as if sketching it into being. The room responds differently to him; his language feels like scaffolding that lets several hands touch it without collapsing.

Celeste’s jaw tightens for the first time. Her public mask slips into something… complex. She nods to Elias without giving away whether she sees him as collaborator or competitor.

Celeste Park: "A pilot requires funding, oversight, and strict timelines. If it's to proceed, we need enforceable metrics. I am open to a joint oversight committee — provided it doesn't delay construction."

Elias: "We can work with measurable metrics — agreed. But delay isn't the enemy here. Pilot data can de-risk the larger project and attract more investors if it shows long-term stability."

Councilor Finch: "Time is the commodity we're short on. Citizens, your voices?"

Rae pushes to the mic with that reckless, contagious energy that makes you glad the world still has people who will shout for what matters.

Rae Carter: "We don't need promises phrased in capital letters. We need the ground to be livable tomorrow. Let us test nature-based buffers now. Let the Commons be the pilot — we already have community buy-in."

Dr. Amina clears her throat, quiet but authoritative.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "From a scientific perspective, iterative tests reduce risk. If we pair living shorelines with selective engineered supports, we can measure resilience and maintain livelihoods. It's pragmatic and evidence-based."

The room is starting to lean. Not every heart is convinced, but the debate has moved from binary to something like choreography: options that can be combined, tested, and corrected.

You feel Elias’ glance find yours across the crowd — not pleading, but proposing. There is the simple, warm pulse of shared work. It colors your memory of the rooftop: agreement as sunlight, not capitulation.

Celeste comes back to the podium, smile measured again.

Celeste Park: "I will say this — Solhaven deserves durable investment. If a pilot can be shown to reduce long-term costs and stabilize taxable revenue, it would be considered. But the town needs assurances."

Tommy stands, and the room hushes for his small, plain speech.

Tommy: "Assurances only worth the breath in the paper they’re printed on if seas don't take our boats mid-season. We need to see protections that respect how we live."

There is murmur — someone coughs; a chair leg squeaks. The mood is tense but hopeful: people on different sides of the argument nodding at the same sentence like neighbors agreeing on a fence post. Your chest loosens with relief — the debate has texture, not absolutes. That’s what you wanted.

Elias glances toward where you stand, then back at the council. He makes his proposal concise, the way engineers do when they want to be understood.

Elias Navarro: "I'm proposing a hybrid pilot on the eastern stretch of the Promenade site. We would install modular revetments and living berms, funded through a blended model — municipal bonds matched with private grants and a small, conditional allocation from Celeste's development fund. Monitoring would be transparent, run by a joint committee with community representatives — including Ms. Lin, if she accepts."

The room shifts; someone near you whistles low. For the first time tonight, the word "we" feels like a plan instead of a slogan.

Celeste's response is complex — she inclines her head, expression unreadable for a beat.

Celeste Park: "The funding model will require legal safeguards. I will consider matching grants on a pilot if it meets criteria that protect investors. Ms. Lin's involvement would bring legitimacy — assuming governance is clear."

You hear the possible future in their exchange. It's not perfect, and it certainly asks you to risk local trust for testable, scalable proof. But it's a path forward. The movement in the room is toward action that includes rather than excludes, toward testing rather than bulldozing.

Your pulse quickens an even degree — not panic, but a rising confidence that the town can choose a path that holds both economy and ecology. You can feel the arousal curve building: from the hush at Celeste’s opening, through the push of Tommy and Rae, to the proposal that folds competing interests into a plausible experiment.

Councilor Finch narrows his eyes at the tablet in his hands before addressing the chamber.

Councilor Finch: "We've heard presentations, proposals, and passion. The council will not make a unilateral decision tonight. But we can direct staff to draft terms for three possible actions: fast-track construction; approve a staged hybrid pilot (as proposed); or delay for further study. Before we put anything to vote, we will open the floor for a statement from the key proponents."

All eyes turn toward you and Elias and Celeste in a triangular hush. This is the hinge you felt on the rooftop, now magnified under fluorescent lights. Your chest hums with hope and responsibility. The choice you make publicly will do more than position you politically — it will define what "we" looks like to the people who would live with the decision.

Elias steps slightly behind you, an invitation without words.

Elias Navarro: (softly) "Whatever you decide, I’ve got your back."

You taste salt and hope. You think of the marsh your hands have dug into, of seedlings taking root, of the sibling you lost and the reason you won't let complacency decide Solhaven's fate. You think of community meetings in sunlit parks and late-night strategy emails; you think of Celeste's smile and its unreadable edge. You think of the pilot — a laboratory for hope, or compromise that softens both danger and determination.

The council waits. The room breathes with you.
