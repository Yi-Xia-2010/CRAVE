[Scene: Town Hall Steps | Night — Immediately after the action]
[Visual: Rain-soaked steps; ambulance lights pulse across slick brick; reporters cluster under umbrellas with microphones like iron flowers]
[Sound: Distant siren wail, camera shutters, the hiss of rain on canvas]
[Music: Fractured strings; tempo high, urgent]

You are standing on the slick steps and the world feels smaller and sharper: a smear of light, a rope of voices, the broken outline of what you thought you could control. The stretcher has already been swallowed by the ambulance; Tommy's face is a hard, wet map of salt and something like guilt. Rae presses a phone into your hand — footage, timestamped, raw as a wound.

Your jacket is heavy with rain; the copper bracelet at your wrist tastes like cold metal against your skin. You can feel every heartbeat in your throat; the storm has left a hollowness behind the adrenaline. News vans hum like insects that found a broken window. Someone calls your name and it sounds like an accusation that isn't even formed yet.

Elias moves through the crowd toward you, soaked but steady, his notebook clutched like ballast. His expression is complicated—patience and strain braided into one. He doesn't meet your eyes at first; he scans the scene, the ambulance, the reporters, and then he looks at you with something raw and sideways.

Elias: "Mara. Are you—" (He stops. A hand rubs at his temple, rain in his hair.) "We need to pull together a statement. Clear, fast. The feeds will run in minutes."

You can hear the logic in his voice threading through the chaos: triage the narrative the way you triaged the injured. It helps. It also feels like a compass whose needle has been jolted.

You: "We need to make sure Tommy's okay. Then—" (your voice catches) "—then we make sure the town doesn't get swallowed by what happens next."

Rae presses her phone toward Elias and you both watch the first two seconds of the clip: a plank snapping, a shout, wet hands, someone going down. The frame is a confession made by light.

Rae: (word-quick) "I uploaded it. I couldn't— I couldn't sit on it after what happened. If the council keeps pretending they didn't know—"

Elias: (cuts in, softly urgent) "We don't control how people use it. We can control what we say now. That matters—"

You glance at Celeste across the square. She stands beneath a corporate canopy, immaculate despite the rain, a small circle of aides and a lawyer like a shield. Her eyes find you; they're narrow, precise. She offers a small, public smile that doesn't reach anything like warmth. Her PR reads like a promise and a threat in the same breath.

Celeste: (over the hum of mics) "Safety first, Solhaven. We stand with the town and welcome a full review. We'll cooperate."

There is canned civility in her voice that sharpens against your skin.

Your chest feels like a drum; every inhale is heavy and impatient. The leak has turned the private hurt of a ruined night into a public fever. Lawsuits and subpoenas will follow the footage the way stormwater follows a slope: inevitability stony and fast.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "There are engineering failures and human errors in many of these situations. We need an independent audit. We need custody of the chain-of-evidence—sediment cores, anchor design, logs. We need time."

You watch the flood of statements and petitions and legal filings with a kind of numbness that is almost clinical. Each one slams into the town's thin defenses and leaves a bruise. There is policy at the epicenter of a wound.

[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Early Morning — After the first wave of coverage]
[Visual: Boardwalk saturated, seedlings bent under rain; the Commons feels smaller, more intimate against the backdrop of national attention]
[Sound: Distant helicopter; the Commons' wind chimes sound like frightened glass]
[Music: Low, anxious cello; heartbeat beneath strings]

You walk the Commons with Rae at your elbow. The reclaimed beds look like someone stitched together a memory with rope and soil. People who have known one another their whole lives watch you with the careful faces of people who are trying not to show how afraid they are. The leak has pushed headlines into their kitchens.

Rae: "They cropped the footage to make it sensational. They put Tommy's name in the crawl. Someone's calling the council to sue for public nuisance—do you believe that? For saving something?"

You: "They'll say anything when the cameras are hungry." (You kneel to check a seedling; damp soil clings to your fingers.) "It's not about Tommy's name. It's about the way attention rearranges everything. We need to be precise."

Rae's jaw tightens. She looks gutted—exhausted and defiant in equal measure.

Rae: "Precise or not, people are scared. They want answers now, not in committee reports."

You can hear the truth in that. The Commons was built for slow patience; social media demands immediate resolutions. The tide of public opinion is both a tool and a chain.

You and Rae walk toward the boardwalk where a small group has gathered: Elias, hands spread in a trying-to-be-reassuring stance; Dr. Amina with a folder of printouts; Tommy's sister, face hollow but resolute. Celeste's team sends in a representative who takes notes like an archivist cataloguing advantage.

Elias: (urgent whisper) "We can push for a temporary restraining order to stop work while Amina's team audits the anchor points. It'll buy time."

You: "A restraining order gives us legal breathing room. It'll also invite legal counters—injunctions, claims of interference. They can say we obstructed emergency work."

Amina: (cool, precise) "Evidence is the best weapon here. Independent custody of site logs, a pause in construction, and a transparent audit. If we frame it around safety and data, the court language favors pausing."

Celeste's representative intercepts the conversation with a practiced smile. The smile is a thin membrane.

Celeste's Rep: "We encourage rigorous review. We only ask that such processes be timely—overly prolonged halts can harm livelihoods."

You sense the unspoken pressure: deadlines, bonds, funds tied to timetables. The town's fragile economics hum beneath the official language.

[Scene: Town Hall — Hearing Day | Morning]
[Visual: The council chamber jammed; folding chairs packed, wet coats draped over backs, livestream cameras set on poles]
[Sound: Murmurs like gulls, pages turning, the scrape of shoes; a public address system that buzzes when someone speaks]
[Music: High strings, staccato; the tempo hurries toward breaking]

The hearing is an arena squeezed into fluorescent light. A legal pile—motions, affidavits, emails—sits like a small mountain on the clerk's table. Reporters angle for the best shot. A local broadcaster relays the proceedings; a national feed runs a sidebar about civil unrest and public safety. The room is warm in a way that makes your skin itch; bodies packed together exhale a damp, anxious heat.

You stand at the back of the chamber while Dr. Amina gives testimony. Her voice is steady, but under it there is an urgency that makes the hairs on your arms stand up.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "My team has documented design deviations at the anchorage points. There is evidence that some specifications were altered in the field without adequate review. Until an independent audit confirms stability, continuing heavy construction near tidal zones is a risk."

The developer's counsel—slick, practiced—pushes back with hypotheticals and risk calculus.

Developer Counsel: "Temporary setbacks do not equal systemic failure. The work in question is vital to the town's economic recovery. An injunction based on speculation can throttle necessary progress."

Celeste is at the table opposite you, eyes like a cold ledger. When she speaks, her tone smooths the room like a blade.

Celeste: (calm, clipped) "Our objective has always been to protect Solhaven. We regret any injuries; we are prepared to work with oversight." (She looks at you, the public listener, and there is a molecule of challenge in that glance.) "We will not allow obstructionists to hold this town hostage to performative delays."

Her use of the word "performative" lands like a small, hard stone. The chamber reacts—some nod, some bristle. The word includes you whether or not you acted.

You want to stand and tell the room what the Commons is: that each bed of marsh grass is an argument against thoughtless speed; that soil remembers when it has been treated kindly and when it has been forced. Instead you let your fists curl and force yourself to breathe.

Elias leans toward you—his voice a low thread.

Elias: "If we stay emotional, they'll label us as obstruction. If we stay clinical, they'll say we lack heart. We have to do both."

You: (half-laugh, half-cry) "When did every appeal for safety become a test of theatricality?"

Elias: "When cameras learned to eat nuance and call it content."

Testimony continues. Rae reads a statement about Tommy—clear, blunt. Tears wet the paper; she steadies herself, voice not as small as she feels.

Rae: "We were trying to stop harm. We weren't trying to make headlines. The injuries we saw—"

Developer Counsel: (interrupting) "Objection. Emotion is not evidence."

The judge—a tired figure with a gavel that looks like an ancient, small thunder—calls for order. Lawyers trade legal barbs like knives. Every interruption is a new headline waiting to happen.

Then, the press finds another seam: a leaked internal memo from the developer is circulated—brief, technical, and damning in tone. It talks about "expedited timelines" and "mitigating PR risk." The phrase "cost-saving substitutions" is highlighted in someone else's overlay. The leak lands in the public feed like a thrown stone into a quiet pond.

The room stirs. Celeste's professional face flickers—no more than the shutter of a window—but it is there.

Celeste: (voice even) "That was an internal draft, speculative. We operate under strict guidelines. Any allegations will be addressed."

You feel the air tighten. The leak has turned administrative caution into a moral argument. Lawyers begin to speak faster; counsel for the town pounds the small lectern with legalese and fury.

Town Counsel: "We move to place the project under independent oversight and a temporary halt pending full audit and chain-of-custody verification."

Developer Counsel: "We will file to dismiss those motions. We will also file for damages should this be spurious."

A jurist on the bench speaks quietly to the clerk. The room leans in without meaning to. You can taste metal when someone changes the temperature of a conversation.

Elias takes your hand under the table for a moment—his fingers braced and real. His pressure is a promise and a plea. He looks at you, and for a second the world reduces to two people in a storm-lit auditorium.

Elias: (whisper) "However this goes, we'll show the data. We'll show the harm and the chain of custody. Let Amina lay it out—method, samples, timeline."

You: "If we lose on procedure, we lose on remembering what was at risk." (You swallow.) "We need the record intact."

Amina: (to the bench) "If this court allows an injunction to halt activity until evidence is secured, the town will have preserved life and a path to a methodical assessment."

The judge's pen hunches over a pad. The chamber waits as if breath itself were a currency the court might spend.

The clerk says a name: "Order in the court." A lawyer stands; another leans in. Phones buzz like distant wasps.

Your palms sweat against the table; you can feel bone and tendon and burned fiber of responsibility. The scale of things—homes, livelihoods, the person on the stretcher—tips and balances in a way that feels like the next decision could split the town into before and after.

You look at Elias. You look at Celeste. You look at Rae, face drawn, and at Amina, who has become the small, steady fulcrum of the entire hearing. The moment contracts until it is nothing but potential: the continuation of the promenade, or a pause that could be the one chance to prevent worse.

This is the hinge.
