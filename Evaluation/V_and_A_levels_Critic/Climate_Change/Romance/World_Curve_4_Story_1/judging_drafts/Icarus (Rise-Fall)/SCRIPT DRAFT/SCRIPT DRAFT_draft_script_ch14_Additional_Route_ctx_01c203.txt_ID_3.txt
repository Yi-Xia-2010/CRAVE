[Visual: Floodlight-glossed faces around a long folding table; rain streaks the window like a hundred small knives]

You are still wearing the damp where the magistrate's gavel landed. The last sound of the chamber — that hollow, final strike — has not left your bones. Outside, the world has started to unzip itself: wind clawing at the eaves, rain punching the pavement. Someone's forwarded photo of the promenade — bulldozers lined like teeth — lives behind your eyelids when you close them.

A fluorescent bulb buzzes. The coordination center smells of wet wool, antiseptic, and a tinny tea someone forgot to finish. Monitors show tide models, camera feeds, and a scrolling list of incoming calls. Voices overlap: calm incident-command language braided with panic. The room is small for the size of the decision it needs to make.

Elias stands by the array of screens, hands curled around a steaming paper cup as if it anchors him. His jaw is tight; there's mud under his nails. He doesn't meet your eyes at first. Dr. Amina Bhatt is half-turned toward the tide model, pointer in hand, voice zeroed in on numbers you can feel in your ribs. Tommy leans against the map table, mouth set, salt in his nostrils and grief thin as a thread behind his eyes. Celeste Park is immaculate in a water-sheened coat, one precise glove on her hand, an unreadable patience settled into her face.

You inhale. The air tastes of iron and the raw, living thing that is water. Your heartbeat steps up against your throat.

You: "Status."

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (without looking away from the model) "Surge projection just updated. Worst-case crest in ninety minutes. Promenade—sheet flow advisory; Saltwren Commons sits in an inundation corridor if winds shift east. The low-lying residential strip will see interior flooding within an hour. We have two small rescue launches available, one volunteering skiff from the marina, and three crews—"

Elias: (interrupting, voice low) "And the coastal authority is insisting we prioritize the promenade corridor. Infrastructure first—control points, equipment, to prevent catastrophic washout to the north. That's the directive."

Tommy: "They're saying we'd let our neighbors drown to save a strip of concrete?"

Celeste: (calm, precise) "Not 'drown.' Minimize systemic loss. If the promenade collapses, the economic hit is catastrophic for Solhaven. Funding dries up. More people suffer later."

The word 'later' hangs in the air like a forecast. It tastes like ash.

You feel that old pressure behind your sternum — the one that tightened the night you lost your sibling to a flood. Memory is not a thing you can shelve when the tide is on its way; it is a torque. It moves you.

You: "Who called in from Saltwren?"

Amina: "Rae's last ping: children's group sheltering in the upper boardwalk hut. Tommy reported Mr. Reyes's mother can't move from her back room—oxygen concentrator, needs evacuation. Promenade has five trapped contractors reported by site lead; heavy equipment, risk of breach if tanks topple. North residential strip has a dozen calls flagged as priority—seniors and families."

Elias steps closer, voice smaller now. "Official channels—coastguard, county emergency—they've signaled a single priority corridor. They're worried about cascading failures. If we divert assets off their advised pulls, we complicate mutual aid."

Tommy flings his hand at the map as if he could move the tides with it. "Mutual aid is fine in the books. In practice, it means our folks wait while the lawyers and engineers argue."

Celeste smooths the corner of a paper like a makeshift dam. "We need to be strategic. Save the spine, secure the funding, then the neighborhood teams can mop up with grants and support."

A cold laugh escapes you, sharp and sudden. "Grants won't steady a roof while someone's lungs fill with water."

[Visual: Close on Elias's hands around the cup; his knuckles blanch]
[Sound: Radio bursts — a child's voice, distant, thin: "—help—boardwalk—water—"]

The radio cuts out and comes back like someone's breath held too long. A new alert slides across the monitor: "Saltwren Commons — high priority: structure breach. Children reported." The letters are black on orange. Your chest compresses. Everything accelerates.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (exact, unyielding) "Based strictly on risk vectors, the Commons is likely to trap people quickly due to shallow boardwalks and rapidly rising backflow. Promenade failure would be catastrophic for infrastructure but slower in terms of immediate human entrapment."

Elias: "But if the promenade fails, there are secondary breaches north. That's not just infrastructure; it's a multiplier." He looks at you finally. "If we follow the county, we hold the line where they tell us. I— I can call it in."

Tommy: "Or you can listen to us. To people who live there."

Your radio crackles. A volunteer dispatcher reports a collapsed boardwalk at Saltwren and a screaming name that means something. The sound is not filtered; it is raw and brittle. Your thumb feels the radio's plastic like a pulse point.

You: "How many craft? How fast can they reach the Commons vs. the Promenade?"

Amina flips a tab on the model. "Two launches to the south—Commons accessible in eighteen minutes at best through the inner creek, but it's tight and the tide will push them into the marsh. One skiff to the north; that'll hit the promenade in twelve but can't carry more than three people at once. Crew endurance is a factor: hypothermia risk—"

Celeste: "Then we send the skiff to secure the walkway defenses. Stop the breach before it chews through the rest of the shoreline."

Tommy: (spit) "And leave Rae's kids to the marsh. You'd stand there and fence our grief into a spreadsheet."

Elias flinches. "I'm not—"

Tommy pins him with a look. "You could be on that skiff. You used to know this water like the back of your hand."

Elias's mouth opens, closes. The room tilts. He is a man caught between manuals and memory, between a professional oath and the small, stubborn gravity of this town.

The room answers differently depending on what you do. If you go outside, the wind shoves words into your ear that feel like prophecy. If you stay, the decisions etch themselves into ink and radio static. Neither is safe. Neither is clean.

Elias steps forward, voice rough. "If we split resources, we risk both sites. If we commit to one, we abandon the other. If we follow protocol, we risk losing people who won't survive the ten minutes between a call and a rescue."

You can see the calculation behind his eyes like a slide rule. He's balancing probabilities against faces. You remember how he once told you about a mentor who taught him to measure risk in lives and infrastructure as if they were numbers on a page. Now those numbers exact a cost he has to feel.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (soft, urgent) "The physics are immutable. The crest arrives faster than models predicted when the wind vector shifted. Tide will backflow into the Commons faster than we estimated. If we wait for mutual-aid confirmation, doors will lock with people inside. If we redeploy without sanction, we risk losing support from county command."

Tommy claws at the map. "So we break the rules."

Celeste: (cold, clipped) "Breaking protocol invites liability. We can't operate in chaos and expect funding and partnerships to hold. People will be angry, and rightfully so, but we also have to think five years out."

You: "Five years doesn't take a breath for those underboardwalk beams tonight."

Celeste: (a fraction too smooth) "And under no circumstance do we let unchecked emotion put the whole town's recovery in jeopardy."

The accusation in her tone lands like a stone. You feel a hot, bright anger — the old one that does not help boats move faster but makes your voice sharpen.

You: "What do you suggest, then? We let funding be the arbiter of who lives?"

Celeste's eyes are level. "I suggest we act strategically. Stabilize the promenade to prevent fuel or chemical tanks from breaching, then allocate community volunteers to shore-side extraction points. It’s pragmatic."

Tommy spits again, louder. "Pragmatic for whom, Celeste? For the portfolios on your lapel?"

A silence opens. It is the kind of silence that tastes like the bottom of a well. The storm drums louder at the glass.

Elias steps between your two realities. His hand finds your shoulder for a moment — grounding, quick, almost apologetic. "Mara, whatever we call it — strategic, pragmatic — you made me promise I wouldn't let this be only about my head. Help me find a line that saves people now without burning the town later."

You think of the marsh coordinates on your bracelet, the year your family came back to rebuild, the way mud smells like history. You think of a small bed that will never need a shovel again. The room narrows to the radio microphone in front of you, the list on the screen, the faces around the table.

A new alert slices the air: "Multiple casualties reported at Saltwren Commons. Immediate evacuation requested. Promenade—structural failure imminent on pier three." The words pile, not unlike waves: they want to bury you.

Both reactions happen in your head, an endless looping rehearsal. In the room, people prepare to move either way. Boots stamp, gear is slung; someone straps on a life jacket with hands that barely stop trembling.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (sharply) "We have to be explicit. Whoever issues the command assumes the liability when mixed-agency coordination fails."

You feel the responsibility like weight in your chest. It is a question that has no clean answer and carries consequences in every direction.

The thunder cracks as if on cue. A child's voice wars through the static again, closer now: "—Rae—can't—boat—"

Your thumb curls over the radio's transmit button. Your hand is not steady. The station's hum fills your ear. Outside, the storm leans in, enormous and indifferent.

You are a second from speaking into the radio and ordering which direction will get the town's scarce hands and boats. Your decision will tilt the evening into a new geometry of loss and salvage. The room waits. The tide waits. The town's small lives, your sister's memory, everything you tried to hold in neat plans — all of it tilts.
