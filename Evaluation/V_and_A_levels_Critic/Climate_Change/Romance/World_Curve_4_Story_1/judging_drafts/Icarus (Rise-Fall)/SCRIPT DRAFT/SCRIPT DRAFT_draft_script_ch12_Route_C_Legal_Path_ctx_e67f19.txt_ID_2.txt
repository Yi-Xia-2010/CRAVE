Elias Navarro: (tightening) "I do. That's why I testified the way I did. I said what I had to so the judge could act. I thought—" He stops, the rest of his sentence curling into the space between you two. "I thought this was the best way to prevent real harm."

You: "Best for whom, Elias? The injunction keeps cranes idle; it keeps labor unpaid. It keeps investors skittish. It keeps railing open as a narrative: 'NIMBYs stall progress.'"

Elias Navarro: (softly) "I didn't come here to be an executor of an institution's PR. I came because I remember what unmitigated flood did to neighborhoods. I remember a mentor who said 'don't let bureaucracy be an excuse to do nothing.'"

You study his hands—engineer's hands, steady, the knuckles a little raw. There's an earnestness that doesn't resolve into a single truth. His reaction is complex. You want to trust it; you also know what distrust has saved.

Attorney Vega intercepts, brisk and businesslike.

Attorney Vega: "Mara, Amina, we have to prepare the evidentiary schedule. They may move to appeal, and if they do, we need to show the judge we've timed assessments sensibly. Elias' deposition will be called again."

You: "I'm going to Tidewatch. We need to archive raw samples, chain of custody intact. If they appeal, they will try to poke holes."

Amina Bhatt: "I'll be there. Bring two more techs. We move quickly and we document double."
