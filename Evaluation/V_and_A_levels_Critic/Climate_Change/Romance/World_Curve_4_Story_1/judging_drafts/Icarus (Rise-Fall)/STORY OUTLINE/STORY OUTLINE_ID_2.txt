## Solhaven Rising (Visual Novel Title)
Solhaven Rising

---

## World View
Solhaven is a small coastal town in 2035 caught between salt and concrete. The temperate sea laps against weathered fishing-era buildings and newer glass-front proposals; reclaimed wetlands rub up against survey flags and corporate trailers. Politically the town is a tangle of municipal council meetings, grassroots co-ops, and outside development interests — a local democracy tested by climate-driven storms and investors promising stability. Culture blends longshore craft traditions and emergent ecological activism; there is a tacit religion of place — ritualized maintenance of marshes, memorials for storm losses, and seasonal festivals tied to tides. Class lines are not stark but real: fishermen and gardeners, white-collar engineers and city consultants, retirees who remember the town before the storm. Conflicts center on how to secure livelihoods and landscapes: engineered floodwalls and promenades backed by corporate capital versus living shorelines, buffer wetlands, and community gardens. Geography is intimate — boardwalks, algae-slick boathouse steps, the Saltwren Commons, and the exposed Solhaven Promenade. The atmosphere is melancholic and resolute: frequent storms, salt in the air, activists painting stencils, and councils lit by fluorescent heat lamps. Romance is braided into politics; love and policy collide as people choose the town’s future.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
Icarus in miniature: I rise with the town — a surge of collaborative energy between Mara (protagonist) and Elias (engineer ally) that promises a hybrid resilience plan combining nature-based restoration with targeted infrastructure. Early successes — community gardens expanding into pilot buffer wetlands, pragmatic engineering proposals, and grant momentum — create a sense of triumph and a believable pathway to stability. But the ascent is precipitous: political compromises, Celeste’s polished promises, and a big public unveiling ramp up stakes. The fall comes quickly when a violent storm (and/or legal maneuvers) strips away gains, exposing the limits of quick fixes and the emotional toll of decisions. Branching choices let the player decide whether to embrace compromise, resist hardline development, litigate, or prioritize emergency measures; choices shape whether Mara and Elias remain partners, drift apart, or reconcile with a wary long view. Endings range from a celebrated hybrid victory (tempered, costly), to bittersweet sacrifice, to the pain of development overriding local will, to a patient, long-term resolution that accepts uncertainty. Across routes the emotional arc rises to a hopeful peak and then collapses into consequence, forcing players to weigh love against communal responsibility.

* **Summary:** I open on the Saltwren Commons, mud caked on my boots and soil under my fingernails as the tide retreats. I—Mara Lin—move through raised beds and boardwalks, naming plants: a salt-tolerant sedge, a resilient samphire. My dark chestnut bob brushes my jaw; the copper bracelet engraved with Solhaven's marsh coordinates is warm against my wrist. The reclaimed wetlands breathe: gulls wheel, wind chimes rattle, and Rae’s stencil posters flap on driftwood. I check the clipboard in my frayed canvas satchel, tallying volunteers and a shipment of recycled lumber. Tommy shouts from a boat—his beanie and weathered coat a silhouette against the gray sea. I think of the sibling I lost to that one storm and of vows made in its wake. My work is small gestures that add up: a new berm here, a raised bed there. I feel both fierce and careful—impatient with puffed promises, protective of places others see as empty lots. The morning is ordinary in a way that feels like a blessing; the idea that we can stitch the town a little tighter sits warm and dangerous in my chest.

The town is not “saved” in any tidy sense — people have moved, businesses shifted — but there is a durable architecture for resilience. I stand on the Saltwren Commons and watch new seedlings take hold where old wreckage once lay. The future is neither clean nor certain, but it is ours and rooted in both care and compromise.
