[Scene: Tidewatch Lab | Morning]
[Visual: Rain-soft sky over the harbor, Tidewatch's glass panes beaded with droplets]
[Music: Quiet piano, slow tempo]
[Sound: Distant gull cry; kettle faintly boiling]

Mara Lin steps into Tidewatch and the smell hits her first — a warm, metallic tang of damp earth and tea, threaded with the sharper, clinical scent of ethanol and inked labels. Shelves of jars wink in the gray light: sediment tubes, a half-dozen cores laid out like small, stratified stories. The lab feels like a slow heartbeat of the town — patient, persistent, full of records you can touch.

Dr. Amina Bhatt looks up from a light table. She smiles without flourish, the way someone does when they're quietly glad to see a familiar collaborator rather than to announce anything dramatic.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Mara. You're right on time. We pulled the latest cores overnight after last week's high. Thought you'd want to see the surface compaction."

Mara Lin brushes salt and grit from her jacket, fingers finding the frayed cuff of her canvas satchel. Her heartbeat is even; being here steadies her. There is work to do, and work is where her grief and her stubbornness make sense.

Mara Lin: "Show me everything."

Dr. Amina Bhatt nods and gestures toward a long table. Elevation maps are spread like folded tides: pale contour lines, color gradients from marsh-bronze to pale sand, pins marking houses and berms. Elias Navarro stands at the far end, tablet glowing in his hands. He looks up as Mara approaches — reserved, attentive. You can't tell if he expected Mara to bring the extra urgency she wears when Saltwren is on her mind, but his expression is soft, cautious, in that way he has when he's listening to more than one argument at once.

Elias Navarro [lifting his tablet]: "We overlaid the updated LiDAR; Dr. Bhatt adjusted the compaction factors. It smooths some of the noise near the old docks. If we curve the promenade inland by—" (he points) "—we gain half a meter of effective elevation for smaller storms."

Dr. Amina Bhatt interrupts, not unkindly.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "That's assuming the re-compaction doesn't accelerate under load. The cores tell us about the sediment's memory: previous storm deposits, organic horizons, where marsh recovered and where it didn't."

Mara Lin draws closer. A core is unrolled on the table: a dark ribbon of compressed peat, a lighter band of shell fragments, a thin line of gray silt stamped by the last surge. Her thumb hovers over the band where old roots still cling like clinging stories.

Mara Lin [internal]: The core is a timeline you can press. Each dark band holds names — the ones she's said out loud in meetings and the ones she hasn't. It's why she fights. It's why she won't let tidy numbers erase the margins where people live their lives.

Mara Lin: "What does it mean for living shorelines here? For Saltwren?"

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "It means the marsh is holding, in patches. The organic layers indicate resilience where we work with sediment supply and native planting. But it's spotty; where human-made armoring has severed sediment flow, recovery stalls."

Elias Navarro taps a sequence on his tablet, bringing up a slow, gentle animation of projected shoreline with and without a hard wall.

Elias Navarro: "A floodwall buys predictable protection for high-traffic areas — the promenade, main shops — in the near term. Living shorelines require time and maintenance, and in places with currents that strip sediment, they might not hold alone."

Mara Lin: "Time is a luxury for people who can't afford to be out of a boat during the season."

Dr. Amina Bhatt's eyes are steady on Mara; the lab light catches the silver at her temples.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "That's the hard part. The science doesn't hand you moral answers. It hands you trade-offs. We can model probabilities: how often a place will flood, what the economic exposure is. But we can't model the weight of a family's pantry or a fisher's next season."

Elias Navarro [softly]: "So it's not about choosing a technology. It's about choosing a distribution of risk and responsibility."

The three of them fall into an easy cadence: maps, models, memories. The lab hums around their words.

If we can restore sediment inputs at points A and C here, the marsh can accrete over a generation. But it is not instant."

Mara Lin rubs her thumb over the map's contour, imagining Saltwren's wooden boardwalks and Rae's spray-painted signs. Her mind files the practical: volunteers, seedlings, tidal timing. She also holds the memory of her sibling's absence like a compass needle; it points her away from false promises.

Elias Navarro looks up, measuring Mara.

Elias Navarro: "I know you prefer living systems. I do, too, in principle. But I think we can design hybrid solutions — low-profile berms that look and function like dunes combined with strategically placed hard points to protect the market access."

Mara Lin: "Hybrid is the word everyone loves when they mean 'compromise.' What do you actually want, Elias?"

There's a small hush. The lab is warm; outside, the tide is a slow heartbeat. Elias's fingers find the edge of his tablet as if he's threading a thought through his hands.

Elias Navarro [after a breath]: "I want to keep people safe. I want to keep their livelihoods intact. I want to find the middle that doesn't erase memory. If that means we prototype a living shoreline at Saltwren's northern edge while reinforcing the promenade access with a discreet berm — then that's what I want. But I need the community on board, Mara. I need you to show me the practices that matter."

Mara Lin feels something like relief — small, contained, hopeful.

[Scene: Town Hall | Noon]
[Visual: Sun wagging through cloud breaks; the town bulletin board stands under a small awning]
[Sound: Paper rustle; a child's laughter from the square; distant jackhammering]
[Music: Light cello, contemplative]

Mara Lin walks to Town Hall with the core's images still behind her eyes. The bulletin board is a ritual: neighborhood notices, thrown-together flyers, the official press of municipal type. Rae's posters are pinned by a thumbtack she recognizes; a new, glossy notice is posted under glass.

She tilts her head, feeling a small, precise tightening. The notice is short, official language in bold: Celeste Park's Cooperative has lodged a formal Promenade Development Plan. A council hearing is scheduled; a public vote will be placed within the month.

Her first breath after reading tastes like metal. But it isn't panic; it's a sharpened focus. Celeste Park has played by the rules — permits filed, partners named, a polished render of a promenade that gleams like an advertisement. The lab's slow hope and the town's civic clock now share a deadline.

Mara Lin [under her breath]: "A vote. Soon."

A passerby — Tommy Reyes, probably checking nets — brushes past and gives her a nod like passing a buoy.

Tommy Reyes: "You all right, Mara?"

Mara Lin: "I'm fine. The council put a vote on Celeste's plan. It's on the board."

Tommy Reyes's face knits. He doesn't need the full explanation; the stakes are what they are.

Tommy Reyes: "You'll get people in the street if that's what it takes."

Mara Lin [small smile]: "You'd be the first to hand out flyers."

He grins and wanders on. Mara remains at the board a moment longer, tasting the town's mixture of worry and resolve like salt on her tongue.

[Scene: Rooftop Garden of the Old Mill | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Golden-hour light; mason-jar lanterns glint; the air smells faintly of wet rope and rosemary]
[Music: Slow, hopeful strings]
[Sound: Wind through hanging planters; a faint radio in the distance]

Mara Lin climbs the rope ladder to the rooftop because it's where she can think in an unhurried way. The city shrinks and the sea breathes; Saltwren's boardwalk is a line low and patient in the distance. The rooftop holds her making: salvaged tiles, hanging planters, a small bed of marsh grass she planted last spring.

Elias Navarro is already there, leaning against the low parapet. He offers Mara a mug of tea by way of greeting. His posture is relaxed but deliberate — like someone easing into the role of being present.

Elias Navarro: "I thought I'd find you up here."

Mara Lin takes the tea. It warms her hands and blunts the edge of the day's notice.

Mara Lin: "You did. Did you want to talk strategy or try to get me to like your berms more?"

Elias Navarro warms into a small, genuine laugh.

Elias Navarro: "Both. And to say — thank you. For the lab walkthrough. For not writing me off when my slides looked...architectural."

Mara lets the laugh sit with him. The conversation here is quieter, less about plans and more about threads: where her work and his intersect, and whether that intersection can hold two stubborn wills.

Elias Navarro: "I don't have all the answers. I do have, maybe, a way to make proposals that the council will take seriously and that doesn't bulldoze the commons. But I need to know you'll be part of the framing — that community knowledge isn't sidelined as 'local color.'"

Mara Lin: "I don't want to be color. I want to be a partner. You know that — in pieces. Will you show up to the Commons, not as a consultant but as someone willing to get dirt under his nails?"

He studies her. There's complexity in his face that can't be flattened into a single emotion — professional care, personal interest, an unspoken willingness to be held to account. It fits the "complex" she has learned to expect from people who cross between systems and neighborhoods.

Elias Navarro: "I will. If you let me."

Mara Lin feels the chapter of the day folding into a new page. The lab gave her facts; the board gave her the implacable tick of civic time; the rooftop gave her a moment of agreement that felt like sunlight through clouds.

Her pulse has risen a degree — not alarm, but the forward pull of momentum. A vote is coming. Plans are lodged. People are ready to act. The town needs a frame that fits engineering and ecology and the messy, stubborn lives that depend on the water.
