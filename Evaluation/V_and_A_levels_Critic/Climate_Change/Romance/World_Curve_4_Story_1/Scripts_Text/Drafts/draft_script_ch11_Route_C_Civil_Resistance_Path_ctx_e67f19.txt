# Route: Additional Route / Ending


[Scene: Solhaven Promenade | Dawn]
[Visual: Fog-bleached banners and survey flags whipping; chain-link fences catch damp light]
[Music: Tense BGM — a low, driving pulse with discordant strings]
[Sound: Distant horns, the thud of boots, a shouted count]

You moved here from the Commons with a rope looped over your shoulder and the taste of metal on your tongue — the memory of the tide pulling at foundation posts, the way it had taken a life once and left a hollow behind your ribs. You chose to escalate. The choice throws a current that pulls everyone into motion.

The promenade is a narrow strip of shore crisscrossed with construction markers and temporary floodlights. Concrete ribs jut from the sand like the exposed bones of a plan someone thought would outlast the sea. People cluster in ragged groups: volunteers with wet hair and handmade signs, local fishers in stained coats, college interns in bright vests, a few legal observers with badge lanyards. A line of uniformed security and municipal officers forms along the fencing, faces set like flint. Between the two, you stand with Rae, Tommy, and a handful of others who look at you as if you are a map for what comes next.

Rae bounces on the balls of her feet, stencil boards under one arm, breath hot in the cold air. "We can block the trailer access. If we lock down here, they can't stage heavy equipment," she says, voice sharp with adrenaline.

Tommy rubs his hands together, the motions more about habit than warmth. "We don't want people to get hurt, Mara. You know that. If they push, the tide's near high. That ramp's slick."

You feel the damp of the morning in your boots, the salt-sour smell of exposed seaweed and diesel. Your hands remember the boardwalk planks of the Commons, the places you planted seedlings that survived a hundred tides. Each image is a small, stubborn proof that place matters. You breathe and the air feels like paper — fragile and easily torn.

Elias stands a little apart, tablet tucked under his arm, sleeves rolled, the lines of his face softened by fatigue. His eyes find you and then look away as if measuring the space between intent and outcome. His expression reads layered — concern, calculation, something quieter you can't pin down. He doesn't step in front of you; he doesn't need to.

Elias: "Mara, we can still—(he searches for the right term) —apply pressure through the injunction. Media coverage helps. A staged blockade risks escalation and arrests."

Rae snorts, not unkind. "Staged? This is real life, Elias. They're moving dirt tomorrow unless we physically stop them. Laws don't stop a bulldozer that's already in."

Elias: "I know. I'm not saying don't act. I'm saying plan the action to protect people. Get a legal team on standby, map exit routes, keep medics close."

You feel the word plan like a glove — useful, but not always a fit. Plans in the courtroom mean delay; plans at the water's edge mean paperwork while the tide rises. You remember the promise you made, the one that sits under your copper bracelet: to make space that saves lives, not just scores. That promise is heavier than any banner you could hold.

[Visual: Security moves forward; a clipboard glints under fluorescent light]
[Sound: Handcuffs clicking; someone crying out; the crowd chanting]
[Music: Percussion rises — heartbeat tempo]

When the first line of officers steps in, the crowd swells. You lock eyes with a woman two rows back — Mrs. Halvorsen from the Commons collective, who planted marsh grass with you last spring. Her jaw is clenched, and a small child clings to her skirt. The officers start taking names. They begin with those nearest the fence.

Celeste appears as if she's stepped out of a brochure: trench coat buttoned, portfolio under one arm, eyes like silver coins catching light. She walks the line with an executive's measured pace, the crowd's noise folding around her. Her presence is immaculate and jarring against the grit. She doesn't need to shout to be heard.

Celeste: "This is regrettable," she says, voice smooth. "Construction delays cost jobs and funding. You must understand—businesses are watching Solhaven. The promenade secures the town's future."

You want to tell her about the things she'd call 'regrettable.' About the tidal breach that ate a porch and a brother. About the marsh you planted where kids learned to hide fiddler crabs in cupped hands. But Celeste's voice is made to convince and to close space for doubt.

Mara: "Your 'future' is built on foundations you don't trust to stand in a storm. You call us regrettable while you pile steel on the shore."

Celeste's mouth thins. She inclines her head the smallest amount, polite, unreadable.

Celeste: "Then we disagree about what trust looks like, Mara. I would prefer it not turn into chaos."

Her hands are empty; her words are not.

An officer steps forward and grips a young volunteer by the arm, hauling them toward a police van. Shouts rise. Rae plants herself and starts chanting, a rhythm that feels like a heartbeat. A line of bodies links arms. The first arrests are messy, public and humiliating. A man you recognize — a volunteer who'd taught kids to build bird boxes at the Commons — is pushed to the wet sand, cuffs biting into his wrists. The scene compresses into a single painful image: sunlight on salt and the dull red of a scraped palm.

Your chest tightens until the world narrows to the sound of your own breathing and the sharp edges of anger.

The officers begin to back off as cameras click. Someone calls the press. A surge of adrenaline makes your hands tremble and your teeth ache from clenching. Elias moves through the crowd, hands raised in a pacifying arc, and for a moment you think he will take the handcuffs with the others to shield them. He doesn't. He kneels beside a girl with a cut lip and helps staunch it with a clean bandana he pulls from his pocket.

Elias: (soft) "We need to document injuries. Get names. If we file complaints, this will help the injunction."

Mara: "We can document it, sure. But documentation doesn't stop a tide."

Elias: "It can keep people out of harm's way while we build the tide defenses."

You want to throw the bandana across the sand, to demand his blueprint be dragged into the open and shown to everyone who trusts charts more than hands. Instead you put your palm on the girl's forehead and feel the heat. She squeezes your fingers, trust exchanging hands like currency.

[Scene: Courtroom | Midday — Days Later]
[Visual: High-arched windows, sunlight cut into panels on linoleum; people crowd the benches]
[Music: Low, metallic hum; legal-leaning motif]
[Sound: Murmurs, gavel thuds, pages rustling]

The courthouse smells of old coffee and disinfectant; voices echo under the vaults. The arrests have been processed and the legal machinery has started grinding. Photographs of the promenade and the arrests are already in the files. The prosecutor's face is a flat study in patience; the defense attorney moves like a man meant to deflect waves with polite words.

Dr. Amina Bhatt sits a few rows behind you, a stack of sediment reports and tide-monitor printouts on her lap. Her hands fiddle with a pen cap. She looks at you with the same steady kindness she offers that rarely reaches the courtroom's formal chill.

Dr. Amina: "If we present the monitoring data together with eyewitness testimony, the judge is more likely to consider an emergency stay. But we must be precise. Emotional testimony has its place, but it needs to be grounded."

Rae: (bitter) "Grounded. The sea doesn't care if the testimony is 'grounded.' It finds the weakest seam."

Tommy: "We held the line. Some people are proud. Others worried about kids and harvests. The court thing is fine, but it's a paper fix if they flout it on site."

A court clerk calls names. One by one, people stand and speak into the microphone. You listen to them describe being shoved, the feeling of boots on sand, the cold as an officer's hand closed around a wrist. Each story is a small catalog of indignity. When it's your turn, you walk to the stand and look at the jury as if they were the shoreline: a line of neutral faces that will decide what weight the town's grief will carry.

You speak, and you say what you always say — about living shorelines, about people tethered to place — but your words carry the gravel of fatigue. You are exhausted, and the court records taste like salt-scraped soot.

After the session, a man in a suit representing a corporate legal team approaches with a smile practiced enough to be uncanny.

Corporate Attorney: "We sympathize with your concerns, Ms. Lin. Our client is also committed to Solhaven's resilience. Perhaps there's room for a mediated solution."

Mara: "Mediate how? While they sink posts into the bed?"

Corporate Attorney: (smile) "Our timelines and your community's needs aren't mutually exclusive."

You want to tell him about the bruised wrist of the volunteer, the girl with a bandaged lip, the rusted rebar turned hazard. You only manage a look heavy enough to say it.

Elias finds you in the hallway, away from microphones. His face is raw with the sleepless geometry of someone trying to reconcile impossible things.

Elias: "They offered an expedited review if we agree to sit at the table. We can bring Amina's data, push for independent oversight. That would slow them down."

Mara: "Slow them down, or let them keep their tools on the shore while the glacier moves? You keep talking about oversight like it's a lock."

Elias: "It's a lock if we make it one. We have allies in the institute. Quiet pressure can buy the time we need to make a physical change."

The corridor smells like paper and the warmth of bodies. Your argument isn't just with Elias; it is with the memory of what the water does to people when plans outrun prudence.

You leave the courthouse with the weight of precedent on your shoulders. The town hums with rumor and rumor hardens into lines drawn in front pages. Social feeds boil with clips of arrests and interviews; families argue over breakfast tables. The Commons becomes a triage hub for outrage and medical kits. People bring cups of tea and wrapped sandwiches; their hands are smaller and older, and there's an underlying fatigue like a low tide line no one wants to cross.

[Scene: Construction Zones & Saltwren Commons | Night — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Horizon bruised purple-black; streetlamps flicker; tarps flapping]
[Music: Violent strings, high and unrelenting; percussion like distant thunder]
[Sound: Wind tearing, rain increasing, the metallic racket of loose equipment]

The storm arrives like something that had been writing at the edge of your awareness, finally pressing its hand down. Rain comes first as fine needles and then as sheets that slap the town's face. The tide is high. The promenade, interrupted under the glare of halted work, looks like an unfinished mouth.

Power crews have been ordered off the site because of safety protocols. Still, there are half-embedded pilings, cables strung and loose, an anchoring point where the contractors had started to set a brace. The brace is incomplete — a steel tendon waiting on a bolt that was supposed to be tightened tomorrow. The watchful pause birthed by court filings and protests has left dangerous half-measures in the elements' path.

Rae and a handful of volunteers are at the Commons organizing a wet-weather shelter for those the storm displaces. Tommy is coordinating fishermen for emergency response. You split your time between the rescue tent and walking the perimeter where the promenade sits like a wound.

Dr. Amina stands near a lamp, stabilizing a volunteer with a makeshift splint. Her face is a map of focus, every line a measured decision.

Dr. Amina: "This storm isn't like the others. Pressure is building faster than predicted. If that anchor fails, it could whip. The gusts are throwing pieces like projectiles."

Celeste appears at the fringe of the construction zone again, flanked by a small crisis team. Her coat is soaked but immaculate in a way that seems to take effort. There is a new hardness to her face as she watches people moving like prey or protection, depending on where you stand.

Celeste: "Evacuate this perimeter now. Safety first."

The words are reasonable. They come too late to reconcile the physics of promises already made.

You step closer to a temporary cable run where a line carries tension between piles. The wind takes it like a bowstring being played badly. You reach for the bolt — instinct, not calculation — and your fingers slip in rain. In another life you'd have tightened that bolt with a proper torque wrench and watched the numbers settle. Tonight, a loose plate catches the wind.

There is a crack like a gunshot. The anchor gives.

Metal screams. A supporting beam — a half-fastened strut — frees itself and whips like an angry limb. There is no slow motion here, only the suddenness of physics. The beam slams into the wooden scaffolding and stars of splinters fly. Someone is struck and goes down with a sound that rips the night. The smell of wet wood and hot, metallic blood hits you all at once.

Panic breaks loose. People shout names, the word "medic" like a prayer. Someone kneels over the fallen, hands that had been hands of protest now floundering to apply pressure and keep the airway clear. A child cries, hysterical and small in the world of screaming wind.

You run. Your boots churn in sand and puddle. You reach the injured person — it's a volunteer you know by face, not by name, and that anonymity makes the scene more terrible. Their breathing is jagged; a wet bloom spreads across sleeve and sand. Dr. Amina is there in a second, voice steady but brittle, barking instructions that slice through the chaos.

Dr. Amina: "Pressure here. Tourniquet. Call for evacuation. Someone get a blanket!"

Elias is beside you, hands shaken, and he keeps trying to keep track of realities — names, numbers, where the nearest ambulance will cut through the barricades. He presses a compress into the wound like someone trying to fix a mechanical failure with a patch.

Elias: "Stay with me. Stay—(his voice breaks) —you're going to be okay."

You reach for the injured person's hand and find it empty of the expected warmth, shock cold under your palm. The answers you have are not enough. The volunteer's friends circle, faces raw, and one of them looks at you with an expression that sears: accusation braided with the same fear that twists your gut.

Rae is crouched nearby, eyes wide, chalking out an image of the scene through adrenaline. She says nothing for a long moment, then the words come like a knife.

Rae: "We tried to stop this. We did everything right, and it still—"

Mara: "We tried to stop it," you repeat, but your voice is small under the storm. It sounds like a denial and a confession both.

Sirens wail and cut through the rain. Hands pull the injured onto a stretcher with a rough efficiency. A medic mutters the calm phrases used to keep a body from folding. Someone shouts at the construction crew to secure tools. Someone else calls the police. The floodlights swing, washing the scene in a clinical glare that makes everything feel raw and exposed.

Celeste is at the perimeter again, watching. You think you see something in her face — perhaps a flicker of regret, perhaps the calculus of liability. She turns to speak to one of her team and puts her hand to her mouth. Her reaction remains complex and unreadable. It is not the absolution you want.

You feel the town shift beneath you. Reporter cameras catch hands and blood, people and policy tangled in the frame. The footage will play on repeat. Headlines will simplify grief into columns. The courtroom will have new evidence; the legal files will swell with names and medical records. The Commons will become a meeting place for funerals and for accusations. People who once cheer at your speeches will ask whether the urgency you chased turned into tragedy.

Elias kneels beside you for the first time since the first arrests. His hand hovers over yours, then grips it. His fingers are shaking; the wristband with sensors is slick with rain.

Elias: (voice rough) "Mara—what if we pivot. We can document everything, use this as evidence, push for an emergency stop with Amina's data. The risk—"

Mara: "Or we double down. We make sure no one goes back to that site. We make the cost of doing this without consent unbearable."

He exhales, a sound that is half breath and half surrender.

Elias: "If we push them hard now, people could go to jail. People could get hurt more. If we back off to legal channels, we might save lives but lose momentum—and maybe they'll finish anyway."

There is a noise somewhere that is not the storm — the collective intake of breath from a town forced to reconcile intention and consequence. You feel the pull in both directions, as if the shoreline were splitting in two. The weight of the injured volunteer is a new axis for everything you thought you could bear.

Your palms are raw from holding rope and from holding hands. The taste of iron hangs in your mouth. The storm makes the choices feel clearer and crueller by turns: activism that flirts with danger; legalism that flirts with defeat.

You stand in the rain while the world around you behaves like a thing that will not be made neat again. The emotional tide has crested; the music of urgency and clashing strings reaches its highest note. The town hums with questions that have no easy answers: Did your action shorten a timeline or create one? Were you brave or reckless? Can you live with the answer?

You look at Elias. You look at Rae. You look at the partly finished anchor that failed and at the line of communities frayed by the night. You feel the impossible geometry of responsibility: to the living, the injured, the future, and the sea.

The choice you make now will shape not only the next few actions but the moral architecture of the movement — whether it is defined by spectacle and sacrifice, or by caution and cold technique. Either road bites.
