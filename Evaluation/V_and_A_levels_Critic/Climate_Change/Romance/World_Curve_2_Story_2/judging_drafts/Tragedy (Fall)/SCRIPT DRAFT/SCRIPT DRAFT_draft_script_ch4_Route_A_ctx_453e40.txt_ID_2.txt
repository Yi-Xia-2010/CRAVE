[Scene: Rooftop Orchards | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Golden-hour backlight slices through wind-curled leaves; rows of raised beds and shallow ponds gleam. In the distance, the Blackbrand staging yard is a cold silhouette across the brackish channel.]
[Sound: The steady drip of irrigation; distant gulls; voices layered—instructions, laughter, low arguments.]
[Music: Tense, rhythmic percussion under a low string ostinato]

You promised the town a fight. The promise is a shape in your chest—too large to sit comfortably in your ribs. Today the shape has edges: sit-ins at the yard, rallies on the promenade, a kelp-planting demonstration that says, plainly, we can do this without a concrete hand closing over our shore.

Hands pass you a coil of biopolymer twine—warm from someone's pocket. The twine smells faintly of sweet resin and salt; it leaves a resinous tack on your fingertips. You tie a knot the way your father taught you to tie a line: quick, sure. The knot is small and ordinary and it steadies you.

Kai is there before you—hair pulled into a loose knot, sleeves rolled, a smear of dark mud across his cheek. He grins when he sees you, the kind of grin that tries to hold off the weather. It steadies you in return, but it doesn't erase the tightness between your shoulder blades.

Kai Navarro: "You look like someone who forgot to sleep on purpose." (he wipes his hand on a towel and hands you a stack of laminated instruction cards) "Here—workshop two. Oyster clusters. Don't let Leila scold you about placement."

You: "I didn't forget. I just... prioritized." (you force a smile and start handing out cards) "Leila's placement rules are good. We need them to hold when the tide comes. Anchor three, then weave—"

Kai: (drops his voice) "And the sit-ins? Nita says the first group leaves at dusk to stake out the promenade staging access. We have volunteers for the rooftop demo tonight, but the yard people—some of them are jittery. Security patrols were spotted earlier."

You: "So we keep it visible but nonviolent. Demonstrations, workshops, public meeting—pressure without escalation." (you look out across the water at the long, clinical curve of Blackbrand's tarps) "That's the line."

Kai: (hesitates) "Do you think that'll be enough? Evelyn's team is already running a narrative. I saw a ticker on the municipal page—'Delaying critical infrastructure.'"

You: "We don't become what she sells." (your voice tightens) "We show the town how to protect itself, not hand it over."

Kai looks at you—open, worried. His fingers hover where they almost touch your forearm; then he pulls away, practical hands solving knots instead of giving comfort. The gesture is small and complex; it reads as every possible history between you and him at once—comfort, restraint, the memory of shared plans—without committing to any single meaning.

[Scene: Saltflower Greenhouse (makeshift workshop annex) | Early Evening]
[Visual: Vines climb rusted beams; translucent solar film patches glow faintly. Under the hull's curve, neighbors crowd around tables stacked with oyster clusters and strips of biopolymer matting.]
[Sound: Muted chatter; the rasp of scissors; a soft electric hum from battery banks; distant rumble of heavy machinery across the water]
[Music: Urgent piano motifs, increasing tempo]

Dr. Leila Osei stands at the head of a folding table, sensor packets clipped to her lapel. She speaks fast and sharply—data as a kind of prayer—because numbers here are not cold; they're armor.

Dr. Leila: "If we plant the kelp lines in a staggered grid and pair them with the oyster clusters along the shallows, we can get a fifteen percent reduction in surge energy within the first three seasons. The proof-of-concept at Crescent Wharf—"

You: (interrupting, because there is no time for waiting) "And if the current shifts? The models—"

Dr. Leila: (softens, but only slightly) "Then we reinforce. We adapt. The point is: this is scalable and replicable. It's visible work that people can participate in."

Your voice shakes—partly from the cold, partly from the pressure that has been building for weeks. You imagine petitions, public hearings, Evelyn's polished talks about efficiency. You imagine the boardrooms where her diagrams look like inevitabilities.

Nita bustles in, carrying a cardboard box of handbills. A laugh escapes her, half joy, half disbelief.

Nita: "You lot look like season three of a hopeful disaster documentary. Good. Keep that energy. The market will be out front handing out snacks to keep folks from fainting."

You: "If legal tries to shut this down, we need to be ready. Scripted statements. A phone tree. Legal observers."

Dr. Leila: "I've got contact with a community legal clinic. They'll observe and document—"

Nita: "And I'll get Sol to start the blessing thing that doubles as a press moment. People listen to him."

Reverend Sol appears in the doorway like an old tide—slow, inevitable. His coat smells of oilskin and tea. He carries a small bell carved with a wave motif. When he speaks, the room tilts.

Reverend Sol: "You remind them why we care. Not just houses, but histories. Not just property lines, but the names of the streets where children were taught to swim by their grandparents. Bring your grief—that's honest. But bring your neighbors, too."

The room hums with planning energy. Yet even in the hum there's an undertow: a courier from town hall slips a thin envelope across Leila's workbench. The seal is corporate, the paper cool.

[Scene: Kelp Farm Platform / Rooftop Edge | Dusk]
[Visual: The sun slides low; kelp lines undulate between posts. The water pockets between platforms catch the sky like mirrors; across the channel, the Blackbrand yard's cranes are hunched shapes against cold light. A ring of newly installed security lights glows faintly even now—an island of white in the falling gold.]
[Sound: The slap of kelp against posts; whispered instructions; a distant mechanical thud from the yards; now, the low electronic hum of security lighting powering up.]
[Music: Drums quicken; strings tighten to a fine wire]

You lead a demonstration line—hands in wet gloves, fingers pressing oyster clusters into porous baskets while others weave biopolymer mats to anchor young marsh shoots. Volunteers chant softly—words half-prayer, half-instruction—and the work forms a rhythm that quiets your mind. For a moment you are purely present: knuckles raw, breath steady, the smell of sea and compost filling your nostrils.

Then the hum becomes a presence. High-mounted security lights across the channel click to life one by one, casting long, clinical shafts over the muddy lot. Someone from Blackbrand steps onto the yard perimeter carrying a tablet; a camera drone sweeps lazily low, then higher. A van idles with its door open, a logo pin-sharp against white vinyl.

A town official's phrase flitters through the crowd—"legal injunction"—and all the familiar elastic tension snaps taut. You are aware of your hands wrapped around a strip of biopolymer as if it were a lifeline.

Kai comes up beside you, breath visible in the cooling air.

Kai: "They've got PR feeds rolling. Corporate press release: 'Opponents endanger lives.' Evelyn's posting a video tour of their design in twenty minutes."

You: "So they preempt us. Make our actions reckless before we act."

Kai: (tries for levity, fails) "Evelyn—always dramatic."

You: (bitter laugh) "Evelyn's not dramatic. She's efficient. And now efficient means 'we speak first and loudest.'"

Your phone buzzes—legal counsel forwarding a scanned notice. Evelyn's counsel has drafted a politely worded cease-and-desist and mentions possible trespass charges should the sit-ins 'interfere with critical infrastructure operations.' The wording is thinly veiled, but the teeth are sharp.

Reverend Sol: (in your ear) "We must be careful. Courage is not imprudence, child."

Nita: (across the platform, shouting cheerfully but her hands shaking) "We got the crowd to the promenade. They're singing. But there are more vans. And a camera truck just parked near the boardwalk."

Dr. Leila: (checking her watch) "The sensors—if security storms the platform, we lose months of work. We need observers documented—phone timers, open-source live stream, legal IDs. We need to make this public in a way that protects people."

The social heat builds—messages pinging through shared channels, neighbors calling in, volunteers swapping worried looks. Your chest feels raw, each breath a rasp.

[Scene: Floodplain Market (raised platforms overlooking the channel) | Nightfall]
[Visual: Tarps flap; lanterns bob like slow stars. The promenade is a patchwork of signs and faces. People gather in the cooling air—there's a festival atmosphere that sits oddly with the tension: casseroles on folding tables, kids with hand-painted placards, elders with folding chairs.]
[Sound: Raised voices, a beatbox rhythm someone is drumming to keep people's energy up; alternately, the thin static of a live-stream feed]
[Music: A single tense chord held long, then a rapid cluster as tempers pick up]

Evelyn's representative arrives in a tidy black coat, flanked by a small team that smells faintly of dry-cleaning and certainty. They hand a copy of the legal notice to Reverend Sol; the bell in Sol's hand makes a small, brittle sound.

Evelyn's Rep: "We understand the community's concerns. However, until proper permits are in place, we must advise against activities that may impede construction."

Reverend Sol: "This is not just a permit dispute. It's people."

Evelyn's Rep: (cool) "We have been authorized to ensure safety."

Nita: (step forward, voice sharp) "Your 'safety' is fences and walls. Our safety is oysters and kelp. Our children."

Evelyn's Rep: (tilts his head) "We're funding relocation grants, if needed."

A hush. Someone near the back murmurs, bitter: "For who?"

Kai moves forward, trying to make space between the tidy-coated team and the crowd.

Kai: "We invited you to discuss hybrid options. You could've come to the table. Instead you sent lawyers."

Evelyn's Rep: (a faint smile) "We are open to engagement, but we must proceed with the timeline. Delays cost lives."

You step up onto an empty milk crate—old habit of public speaking, hollow sound underfoot.

You: "Delays cost different people different lives. Your timeline sacrifices the low-lying blocks—where my father once kept his boat—because it's easier to protect the same neighborhoods that already have the means to be protected."

A ripple goes through the crowd. The rep's face doesn't change; polished. From where you stand, his indifference is like a wall.

Evelyn's Rep: (calmly) "We are trying to prevent a larger disaster. Hard choices are unavoidable."

Your heart is a drum inside your ribs; heat spikes and drains in a staccato that makes your fingers tingle.

Reverend Sol: (quiet, but steel beneath) "Hard choices are one thing. Ignoring us is another."

Someone at the edge of the crowd holds up a laminated copy of the legal notice and reads an excerpt—'trespass'—the word landing like a brick. Phones lift, cameras roll. The security lights at the yard's perimeter throw white blades across the water, making the surface glitter like blade-edges.

And then, as though timed for maximum pressure, a PR feed pops onto a neighbor's phone: Evelyn at a ribbon-cutting simulation, her voice full of assured cadence, talking about 'swift protection' and 'scale.' The juxtaposition is a slap—her composed certainty against the ragged reality of your community.

Kai grabs your arm—not hard, urgent.

Kai: "They're pushing narrative. They think if they drown out our footage, they win."

You: (low, deliberate) "Then we make sure they can't drown it out."

Your mouth tastes of salt and iron. The choice hangs in the air—not yet a decision but a pulled string that will snap into motion. People look to you. You feel the town's weight, a map etched into your palms.

All around, the mood frays: some want to escalate now, to walk the plank and sit on the yard's gate until they're arrested. Others plead for caution—document, legal observe, mobilize pressure through visibility. The air feels thin with urgency; the night seems to press closer, as if it were another actor waiting for its cue.

You can feel the moment wanting to split into action.

You breathe through salt and fear and resolve. Whatever you choose next, someone will be hurt, or saved, or both. The balance is brittle.
