You are awake before the town. The dawn is a wetted gray, the kind that makes colors tired. The planks under your boots hold the cold of night and the faint, abrasive grit of salt; each step makes a dry, brittle sound—like paper folding in on itself. You keep your fingers wrapped around the compass out of habit at first, and then because it is the only object with your father's dent still visible, the only one that knows how you tilt toward problems and away from them.

You count loss the way other people count calories or expenses. Juno: gone. Kai: gone for work that took him beyond the harbor, his last goodbye carried away in a knot of words—support, but distant. The market faces you used to recognize have become ledger entries and legal names; Nita's laugh is separated from its owner by a little more caution now. The seawall stands in patches: tidy modules protecting certain blocks, a cold geometry against the messy, human shoreline you wanted. Threadbare resilience survives in corners. The town is less whole.

You breathe salt into your lungs until your ribs ache. The compass is cold enough to burn under the skin of your palm. You imagine its needle shivering with the same tremor that lives behind your teeth.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands; the brass is scuffed, the leather strap thin from years of use]
[Sound: Your breath, shallow; the low distant thump of a pump at the Blackbrand site; a floorboard creaks behind you]

You are not entirely alone. Footsteps—firm, familiar—meet yours on the boards. Nita—bandana bright even in dawn—arrives before you can decide whether you wanted company. Her market apron is damp at the hem. She stops a step past you and doesn't look at the sea.

Nita: "Morning, Mara." (Her voice is work-worn; there is curiosity in it, but also a practiced reserve.) "You look like you could use someone to tell the truth to."

You: "I—" You stop because speaking feels like opening a seam. The words that turned public meetings into lawsuits and quiet choices into fractures are too large to hold in the space between two people on a boardwalk.

Nita: (a slow exhale) "We all watched you hide things. You knew what happened with the kelp lines and you didn't tell us the whole of it. We fixed other things. We saved some roofs. But you carried a secret, Mara. Secrets make holes."

You hear accusation and exhaustion braided together. It lands like a blow you already expected, and yet you flinch.

You: "I thought..." (You sound smaller than you feel.) "I thought if I could limit the damage, if I could keep us safe from the headlines, we'd keep enough of ourselves. I thought—"

Nita: "You thought for us. You thought for you. There's a difference." (Her words are not cruel but precise, like a knife used to cut away dead tissue.)

You: "I thought I'd get lucky." The lie tastes metallic. "I thought it would hold."

Nita shifts her weight. Her hands find the edge of the boardwalk and the wood is warm with last night's sun. "Luck is not a plan. Not for storms."

A pause stretches, taut as a reef line. Nita looks at you, and for a breath you find something like the old market's warmth—someone willing to name a thing and then keep you in the frame with it.

Nita: "Listen. You messed up. But you didn't do it because you wanted to lose us. You did it because you were afraid. People get afraid. People get dangerous when they're afraid. You need to stop doing things alone."

You: "I don't know how." The admission is a weight dropping from your chest. It rings hollow. "I don't know how to not be the person who makes the choice and takes the blame."

Nita: (a short laugh, not unkind) "Then start sharing the weight before someone else decides for you."

Nita steps off before you can answer. She doesn't leave in anger; she leaves with a kind of set understanding—one person stepping back so the wound can be looked at. The wind turns and for a moment all you can hear is the sound of your own blood.

[Visual: The light grows stronger—edges sharpen; the sea's surface is a sheet of dull mercury]
[Music: A quickening—violins staccato; a low percussion like distant falling debris]

You keep walking because staying gives you the opportunity to think too much. Every house you pass has one face turned away, or a window blind that won't quite open all the way. The seawall pieces look like teeth—efficient, fearsome, and not meant for a town that sings. The waterlaps at the stilts murmurs a verdict in a language you almost understand.

Reverend Sol appears next, slower, cane tapping in a metronome you used to obey. His coat smells faintly of oilskin and cedar. He stops a long pace down from you and looks up, not at the sea, but at you. There is a weathered kindness to his face now that has been tempered by the months of hard listening.

Reverend Sol: "Mara." (He pronounces your name like an invocation.) "You carried us through a season of storms. You thought you were protecting us."

You: "I thought I was choosing the least terrible option." The phrase knots in your throat.

Reverend Sol: (his voice softens and hardens in turns) "Least terrible is still terrible. And terrible has consequences. People will not forgive the calculus you used without being asked to. They will not simply understand the math of survival."

You: "They don't understand it now." Your voice climbs high and breaks. "They only see what we hid to spare them. They see the parts that were made public and the rest becomes rumor."

Reverend Sol: "We are a church of hands, Mara. We bind wounds and we stitch sails. But when the stitch is hidden, the wound rots under the cloth." (He pauses, as if choosing whether to step back or press forward.) "You must learn to put the wound in the light."

You: "And if the light shows the work was a failure?"

Reverend Sol: "Then we heal in the open, not in secrecy. There will be pain either way. But pain that comes with truth can be tended. Pain that grows in the dark becomes poison."

The tide tugs at a forgotten rope, and something somewhere groans. Your hands, still gripping the compass, are suddenly too small for the size of the conversation. Anger rises like a wave—sharp, hot. You want to tell them both that their words are easy when they don't hold all the blame. You want to tell them you were trying to buy them time. You want to tell them you are tired.

Nita: (cutting through the pause) "You're tired," she says. "We can tell. But tired isn't permission."

You: "I thought—" Your voice is a thread being pulled. "I thought I was saving us from being torn apart in the press, in the courts. I thought I could manage the fallout."

Reverend Sol: "You managed consequences. You did not manage the town."

Nita: "You managed a fortress with the gate closed."

You: "Then what do you expect me to do now? Walk into every room I shut the door on and start confessing? Wait for the lawsuits? Wait for the people whose houses were saved and whose houses were not to decide I'm the villain?"

Reverend Sol: "We expect you, Mara." (There is a sorrow like an old bell in his voice.) "For better or worse, we expect you to stand with us now."

You feel the space between the expectant town and your solitude collapse inward until both sides are touching your ribs. The compass is suddenly heavy, an anchor—not to the past, necessarily, but to a gravity you have lived by: the responsibility to decide and the loneliness of the verdict.

You walk to the railing. The sea smells sharper now, like cut lemons and old pennies. Waves press and retreat against the stilts; each retreat takes something microscopic and leaves a cold space behind.

You remember the last time you held the compass before a storm—your father's voice, the taught way his hands folded over yours, the map of fingers showing where to stand and when to let go. You see Kai's face in flashes: laughing, then steady, then the day he left with a messenger bag and words that were both promise and goodbye. You remember Juno's small, furious mouth when you asked them to stay and they answered a decision with movement. The accounting of all of it presses in.

You: (a whisper that might be the beginning of a decision) "I am tired of keeping secrets."

Nita: (watching you) "Then don't, Mara. Show them the parts you hid."

Reverend Sol: "And take whatever comes in the open. That is how we rebuild."

You close your eyes because the wind is a blade and your face is raw from the salt. For a second, every sound is muffled—only the sea, only the heavy chord in your sternum that won't unring.

The option to confess, to make truth public and accept the consequences, feels like a narrowing, and yet it is a kind of relief—a path that will hurt but will not hide. The option to remain silent, to let the sea take what it wants, is a different form of surrender: you are not making the choice, the water is. Both routes finish with loss.

You feel the choice in your throat like a stone. Your breath is quick, your muscles taut. Every instinct you have trained—protect, fix, shield—pushes one way. Every new wariness—of lawsuits, of people leaving, of being the scapegoat—pushes the other. The music in your head is a siren; the wind comes harder, stitches the hair at your neck to your skin.

Your fingers loosen. The compass swings once, then twice, then holds steady against your palm as if waiting to be cast. The brass is warm from your touch; in the metal you see a thousand small failures reflecting back.

You could go back now. You could stand at the market steps and make a speech that is honest and terrifying. You could write every misstep in detail, sign it with your name, and let people decide whether you are a liar or a leader.

You could also let it go into the water and pretend the choice was the tide's—that the sea wiped the ledger clean. It would not be true, and the enormity of that untruth sits like a weight.

Your heart races until it fills your ears. The town waits in the shape of two people on the boardwalk and the absent faces you cannot pull back. Your body trembles with the knowledge that nothing you do can put the wound back the way it was. Whatever you choose will be a new wound, an honest, or dishonest, incision. Both will bleed.

You breathe in the salt and make a movement small and enormous. The compass leaves your hand.

Time does not stop. The compass drops. It slips between the cold fingers of your past and the wet teeth of the present. For a second—less than the beat of a gull's wing—you think you hear it catch on the rope below the platform. Then the tide takes it, a small, inconsequential arc, and the sound is gone.

Your chest hollows as if something old were emptied out. You feel raw. There is no triumphant catharsis. There is only the blunt ache of something decisive and irreversible.

Nita: (voice small, unexpectedly) "Mara—"

Reverend Sol: (he does not say forgive; he does not say condemn) "Now you know."

You stand at the railing and watch the place where the compass went. The water is a restless, indifferent mirror. It returns only small things to the surface—bits of kelp, a plastic bottle, a broken length of rope. The town breathes around you, not yet healed, not yet whole, and you realize the truth: endings are arithmetic. They are sums and subtractions. You can total what you saved and what you lost, but the accounting never reaches the same number as the one you held inside your chest.

You walk away from the rail because staying would make you believe that a single act could rearrange everything. It cannot. People will talk. Some doors will open; some will close. Juno may never come back. Kai may find a harbor that needs him more. Nita will fix what she can. Reverend Sol will keep gathering the towns' wounds and offering sutures. You will carry what you have done and not done like a mantle—heavy, honest, and isolating.

[Visual: The morning brightens; the town's silhouette becomes clearer; people, like small shells, move in the distance]
[Music: The urgent motif resolves down into a low, aching chord with no promise of light]

You cannot unmake the choices. You cannot make everyone whole. You can try the slow work of repair—walking door to door, telling the truth, taking whatever comes with it—but you are also the sum of the decisions you made in the dark.

You fold your hands into your pockets. Your father's compass no longer marks your direction. The town's heartbeat continues—irregular and stubborn—and somewhere between the seawall's neat teeth and the kelp's soft fingers, life goes on.

You look up once more at the line where the sea meets sky. There is a small, private grief lodged inside you like a splinter. It will not leave quickly. It will ache when it rains and twinge when you pass the market and Juno's name is spoken. It may never stop being a part of your bones.

You step off the promenade and into the town. Your boots find the familiar path; your stride is steadier now, not because you are certain of the rightness of what you did, but because you have chosen a consequence and can begin to live inside it.
