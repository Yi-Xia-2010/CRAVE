[Scene: Floodplain Market | Night]
[Visual: Tarps hang in tatters from raised stalls; a scattering of crates and overturned buckets dot the raised platforms. Mud darkens the stilts below. Lanterns gutter in the wind.]
[Sound: Distant generator hum, a staccato of shouted names, the wet slap of boots on planks; an ambulance siren knifes the air then recedes.]
[Music: Aggressive strings; percussion like a heartbeat under the tide]

You come back into the market like a body pulled through a storm—sudden, breathless, every sense raw. The place looks smaller and kinder by day; now it's a ledger of losses. Someone's banner is shredded across the rail; spray paint reads HALF A BLOCK, the letters smeared by salt and—something else.

Nita's hand is already on your arm before you have words. Her palm is warm; it smells of fish oil and antiseptic. She is blinking hard, wiping at a smear of blood on the back of a child's neck with the hem of her bandana.

Nita: "Mara—you're here. God, you're here. We thought—"

You (Mara): "I'm here." Your voice comes out hoarse, as if the salt has sanded it raw. You look past her, taking inventory by touch and sound: Reverend Sol kneeling to steady an older man with a collapsed ankle; Dr. Leila counting injuries into a small tablet, the glow of it pale against the gloom; kids with shivering arms looped in borrowed blankets. The smell—smoke, salt, someone frying something over an overturned stove—stings at the back of your throat.

[Visual: Close on your hands—salt-raw knuckles, the compass pendant heavy at your chest]
[Sound: The market's murmurs collide and overlap into indistinct hurt]

You had expected a scattering, maybe a fight, maybe a legal delay. None of it was clean. The enforcement came in fog and protocol: bright lights, dogs, men in jackets with a corporate crest, and behind them a fleet of modular segments waiting like teeth. The eviction was exact as a saw; it tore families from thresholds and pulled lanterns out of hands. Some held firm and were carried away under the floodlights. Some folded like paper.

Reverend Sol: (softly, to you) "We kept what we could, child. We kept each other."  
You: "We kept enough?" The question cracks on the wet air.

He looks at you, whole history in the lines of his face. He does not answer the arithmetic. He only squeezes your shoulder until the bones think they're being held together.

Nita (after a beat): "We have got—what, three houses promised a stewardship allotment? A corner for the nursery? It's something." Her voice tries to shape hope out of the rubble. It sounds thin.

Dr. Leila: "Something won't keep roofs above families who can't rebuild," she says. Her hands are steady as she taps a sensor's readout. "The pilot’s data is clear—localized reefs will lower wave energy in patches. The company's barrier will protect the harbor proper, but—" She stops, and the 'but' hangs heavier than the instrument.

You listen to the 'buts' stacking. The town's map unfurls in your head like a wound: bits stitched, other bits left bare.

[Scene Transition: The Promenade | Later — pre-dawn]
[Visual: Floodlights retreat but leave pale halos; a partial concrete wall gleams off to the east where cranes have already fitted segments together. The sea beyond is flat and waiting.]
[Sound: Machinery pumps; the clink of metal on metal; distant horns; gulls quieted by the light.]
[Music: Low brass; a slow, relentless thrum that tightens like rope]

You walk the boards alone at first, the compass heavy and small against your sternum. The seawall is a clean thing—too clean for this coast—sections bolted and seamless, chrome edges catching the first gray. Between the slabs, skiffs have been moored; between the skiffs, the memory of what used to be slips like driftweed.

Kai finds you midway along the promenade, leaning with his forearms on the rail, wind twisting his braid. He looks like someone who has spent the night trying to understand a map someone else drew in smoke.

Kai: (quietly) "They're building faster than I thought they'd have the nerve to."  
You: "They're building because they have the money, and because the law lets them." You feel the sentence like a stone you have to swallow.

He turns to you, searching your face. When his eyes find you, they are two bronzed questions thrown open.

Kai: "Mara—what did we get?"  
You stare at him. He doesn't meet your gaze. He reaches for your hand; you let him take it for a second, then draw back like a burn.

You: "We got time in places. We got a stewardship fund that covers some leases. We got—" Your throat closes. "We lost the row on East Hollow. The developer's lease—" You choke on the legalese. It is both precise and meaningless. You are a ledger and a memory; neither holds every face.

At the edge of the promenade, a small press contingent is swung into place. Evelyn Black is there—already in her trench, a tablet clamped underarm, the photograph in her pocket visible only to her. She moves through the light like someone used to cutting through fog. Her smile is trained to the flash, but when she crosses your path she does not smile at you. Her expression is complex, unreadable: a practiced neutrality that pinches.

Evelyn: (hands folded) "Mara. Kai." (She inclines her head like an offer.) "We both wanted to avoid losing homes. We found a way to protect the harbor, and we negotiated mitigations for affected blocks. The stewardship fund ensures long-term maintenance and a dedicated resilience office." Her tone is precise; there is no tremor in it.

You: (hot) "Mitigations don't rebuild a life, Evelyn." You are standing on the precipice between numbers and people. "You promised participation. The clauses in the agreement we saw..." Your words tumble; you should have rehearsed a hundred ways to make a hole big enough for this grief. You did not.

Evelyn: (folding and unfolding her hands) "The clauses are legal scaffolding. Without them, the project would have been delayed two years and tens of millions more—delays that would have meant more homes flooded." She watches as if waiting to see if you accept calculus instead of names.

Kai: (with a brittle edge) "And many of those homeowners don't have 'tens of millions' to fall back on. They have birthdays, and funerals, and heat bills."

Evelyn: "We prioritized lives. Where we could, we allocated community-managed pockets for living shorelines." Her gaze flicks to you, and you see something like a decision being weighed. "There will be opportunities to steward those patches. This is how we scale protection."

You want to spit in her symmetrical face. Instead you say: "A 'scale' that snaps neighborhoods in half is not protection. It's triage by address."

The crowd around you fractures into mutters. Reverend Sol has moved closer; his cane taps once, twice. He lifts his voice high and old as tide: "This town remembers who it is. We will steward what we can. We will tell the truth when the ledger reads wrong."

Evelyn (soft, almost regretful): "I do not doubt your community's devotion." Her voice is flat precise. "I do doubt your capacity to implement at the scale needed in time."

You: "Then why bring the scale down like a blade?" The question is not to her; it is to the machines, the money, to the rows of slabs that already cut the horizon.

[Sound: A scuffle. Shouts falling like rain. A security line—uniformed, faces masked—moves in a wedge. The crowd tightens. People begin to cry out names.]

This is the moment the night became a wound. The security force advances in slow mechanical steps. Someone throws a bottle; it shatters in a spray that catches the glaring lights like confetti. A child screams. A man with a fishing scar on his cheek charges forward and is scooped up by two officers. The world senses like an upturned net.

Kai steps between you and the officers. He places his hands flat on a vest like a makeshift peace sign. "We don't need violence," he shouts, but the words are a thin membrane.

You cannot let them take Nita's stall. You will not let them take Juno's street. You feel the old compass—your father's—burning at your throat like a heartbeat. You move forward. You hear your voice rise, raw and false-not-loud-enough.

You: "Back! Back—this is our town—"

A metal hand clamps on your elbow. Someone pulls you sideways. For an instant, you're face to face with one of the private security technicians—young eyes, a headset fogging in the cold air. His grip is sure; his expression is not unfriendly. It is businesslike. It is the thing that carried him through training.

Sol's cane strikes once. Dr. Leila's tablet records the angles; she murmurs to whoever will listen about violations and documentation, about the way that policy can carry blood. Nita wraps a blanket around a woman who is sobbing over a crate. The market becomes a ring of small, brave hands trying to hold the tide.

[Scene Transition: Blackbrand Development Site | Dawn to Weeks After]
[Visual: The site at daybreak is immaculate—concrete seams slick in the sunlight; corporate banners hang crisp and triumphant. In the weeks after, machines continue their choreography. The promenade's edge is a straight line now, engineered and final.]
[Sound: The steady thud of trucks; distant horns; the muted conversations of a town remaking itself.]
[Music: A funeral march whose tempo refuses to slow]

Time, you discover, is not a salve but a slow wash. Weeks paste themselves into a calendar: pieces of public land fenced off and rebranded as "resilience corridors"; press releases that use the words "community partnership"; a stewardship fund rolled out with professionally photographed check presentations and smiling faces that do not reach their eyes.

You stand at one of those check presentations. Reverend Sol reads something about dedication and remembrance. He speaks of the sea as ancestor. You hold the compass in your palm like a relic and remember the boy who walked beside his father down these docks before the world leaned in.

Kai speaks afterward. There are cameras. He talks about the living shoreline pockets and the pilots. He mentions community governance and training programs. His voice is earnest and arranges hope into a neat pile.

He comes to you when the crowd breaks up. People drift toward their remembered places, toward packing, toward decisions about who will stay and who will go.

Kai: (low) "They offered us a seat at the table."  
You: (bitter laugh) "A seat with rules written on the underside." There is no humor in it. You cannot find the currency to laugh.

Kai: "We did get funding for the kelp lines." His eyes look for purchase, for a place where you can anchor. "I can—I'll keep working, Mara. I want to build what we started." He reaches out; this time, his fingers are tentative, asking permission.

You look at him—the way his mouth holds regret; the way he tries to be the practical caretaker of both a project and a person. Love is a bright fragile thing, and after the night it is all frayed edges and holes.

You: "Kai, people lost homes. Families. Juno's street is—" Your voice cracks. For a beat you think of your father's compass, then the rusted barrette at your wrist, then the ledger of names on scraps of paper. "I cannot make the ledger balance with good intentions."

He closes his eyes for a breath too long. When he speaks, the words are small and terrible with admission.

Kai: "I thought compromise would keep us whole. Maybe I was wrong."

You want to shout. You want to slam your palm against the concrete wall newly set between town and sea. Instead you say, very quietly: "So were we both." It isn't an accusation you can lift; it is a confession.

Over the following days, some people leave. You watch neighbors fold furniture into trucks, hold a child in one arm and a cat carrier in the other, eyes like plank glass. Some stay and try to build in the pungent soil of what's left. The stewardship fund pays out in stipends and contracts that favor scalability. The patches of shoreline you fought for exist—beautiful, small, tended by volunteers and bound by legal language none of you wanted to parse on grief.

Juno packs one night in silence. You wrap your hands around the zipper of a faded duffel and try to recall the first time you saw them ride that spray-painted bike. They look older now than their years, as if the town's hurt has placed a film over their youth.

Juno: (without looking up) "I have to go somewhere I know I can breathe without looking over my shoulder."  
You: "Leave." The word is an echo that falls into the room and rattles. You mean it, and you don't. Your ribs feel handcuffed.

He steps toward the door with something like determination. Kai puts a hand on Juno's shoulder and then removes it as if uncertain whether he has a right. Juno looks at both of you, and the look is a small verdict. Then the bike groans down the street toward the bus stop, toward a horizon that has fewer walls.

The town stitches itself imperfectly. There are gardens on rooftops. There are kelp farms bobbing below the patched platforms. There are memorials—candles and driftwood plaques—where whole streets used to be. There is a new office window that reads MARISPORT RESILIENCE PARTNERS, and inside that window a warm fluorescence hides the ledger of contracts.

You walk the promenade in that slow, terrible way that is part habit and part pilgrimage. The seawall stands like a blade. The horizon is steady, indifferent. You think of names—those who left, those who stayed, those who wrapped blankets around the small and kept vigil through nights of wind. Your father's compass sits beside your heart, heavier than you remember.

Kai finds you at dusk one last time. He looks older at the edges; the repeated strain has trimmed away some of his easy brightness. He reaches out—no grand promises, no theatrical vows. Just empty hands and a face that has learned that love is not enough to hold everything.

Kai: (soft) "I want to be with you, Mara. I want to work with you. But I can't promise I won't make the same concessions you make—" He stops. "I don't know if that's a promise you want."

You study him, drink in the lines of his face, the way he is trying to be honest in a world that keeps asking for bargains. The town is a ledger; love is a ledger; neither accounts for the weight of what is lost.

You: (final) "I don't know either." You let it be both truth and shield. Then you say, with a steadiness that surprises you: "Go. Build the kelp lines. Do the work that needs to be done." Your voice is not resignation only—it is a permission and a benediction. "Maybe that will be enough."

Kai: (a single small laugh, choked) "Maybe." His shoulders drop. He leans in, resting his forehead against yours for a moment that is all present tense and memory. Then he steps back, the space between you widening like the sea.

[Visual: Close on your compass, resting in your palm; it reflects the sunset like a small, stubborn sun]
[Sound: The distant thud of hammers, the sigh of the wind. Somewhere a child laughs briefly and then the sound is swallowed.]

You stay until the light bleeds out of the sky and the promenade's LEDs hum to life. The seawall throws a thin line of shadow across the water. The town is quieter in some places and louder in others; the ledger has been rewritten in ink and in absence.

You fold the compass into the palm of your hand and feel its scuff against your skin like a small punishment. You think of your father on a boat and how he taught you the difference between a map and a coastline: one tells you where to go; the other tells you what you will lose along the way.

You do not leave Marisport today. You do not stay in any romantic sense of the word that is whole and sealed. You make lists, you attend meetings, you teach a few neighbors how to plant cordgrass in the new pockets. You move in small, stubborn increments. Sometimes the increments are enough. Often they are not.

At night, you walk the boards and talk to the sea as if it were a person who might forgive. You lay your compass down on a bench and close your eyes. The wind smells of hardware and kelp and cigarette smoke. It tastes like the town's breath after a long day of grief.

You will keep working. You will gather the children who remain and teach them how to tie knots and plant oxygen in the mud. You will file petitions that tatter into bureaucratic replies. You will sit at tables where the community's name is called out and then footnoted into legalese. You will hold a handful of people and let the rest go.

The town has been scarred. Love has been tattered at the edges. Courage—bruised, threadbare—still steps forward anyway.

You breathe in salt and ledger-paper and defeat. Then you breathe out.
