## World View
Marisport is a near-future temperate coastal town bending its life around the sea's slow, relentless climb. Its governance is a patchwork of local cooperatives, a fiscally strained municipal council, and a few regional planning offices that hold sway with permits and purse strings. The economy mixes small fisheries, rooftop orchards, kelp farming collectives, and a Floodplain Market built on raised platforms; a single powerful developer—Blackbrand—promises modular, engineered coastal armor and investments that tempt homeowners and officials. Religion is intermittent communal ritual rather than formal church power; Reverend Sol Hart anchors old values and memory. Class divides are spatial: wealthier parcels can buy temporary elevation and sealed living blocks while working-class and historically marginalized neighborhoods cling to vernacular homes on stilts. Culture is tactile, repair-minded, and neighborly—people swap seedlings, share boat engines, and plaster handwritten notices on tide-stained boards. Geography: a half-submerged boardwalk (Tideward Promenade), a converted Saltflower Greenhouse ferry-nursery, a retrofitted Old Lighthouse Solar Spire, kelp farm platforms, and the cold, clinical Blackbrand Development Site. Atmosphere: salty, melancholic warmth, patchwork resilience; the sea is always present in light, smell, and risk.

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mara Linh Alvarez) navigate a town divided by a choice: nurture living, community-led shore defenses or accept Evelyn Black’s fast, engineered seawall. The story is first-person and tragic: relationships fray as strategic compromises and tactical errors accumulate. Initially hopeful—a cooperative movement with Kai and allies draws public support—the campaign spirals when a sequence of choices exposes logistical fragility, political maneuvering, and a high-profile failure that the developer exploits. Multiple split points test my values: whether to escalate direct action, negotiate guardedly, or seek legal/political delay. Each decision accelerates emotional ruin; small losses compound into larger betrayals and displacement. Kai’s optimism strains against political realities; Evelyn’s charisma and decisive resources make her engineering solution attractive to frightened voters. If I choose confrontational tactics, a public incident turns media and municipal sympathy toward Blackbrand, and Kai and I blame ourselves. If I opt to negotiate, concessions carve away neighborhood protections. If I delay for policy, bureaucracy and storms expose vulnerability. The valence falls steadily to a tragic nadir: homes lost, trust damaged, and community dreams fractured. The endings vary (Bittersweet, Sad, True) but the arc is tragedy—failures, loss, and hard truths about what survival demands.
