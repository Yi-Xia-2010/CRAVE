[Scene: Glass Harbor Development Site | Midday]
[Visual: The cleared lot, temporary fencing, glossy renderings pinned to plywood; a small pilot habitat sits like a fragile, earnest model near the edge of churned earth]
[Sound: Sirens in the distance; a restless crowd; the steady slap of waves against temporary piles]
[Music: Tense, urgent percussion with a rising electronic swell]

You are right at the edge of everything — the smell of wet concrete and diesel undercuts the sharper salt air. Your palms are damp; the copper of your barometer is slick in your grip. The pilot habitat behind you gleams with hopeful screws and patched canvas, a small promise you've all made with your hands. In front of you, hired security in dark vests fan out like a slowly closing hand.

Tala's voice threads through the crowd beside you, bright and fierce, "They're going to try to lift the seed beds. Hold steady. Don't let them touch the beds."

You can taste adrenaline: metallic, sharp. The chant swells again, people pressing close, seed tags catching sunlight like ragged flags. Your heart ratchets faster — the music in your chest doubled by the crowd's breathing.

[Visual: Close-up on your fingers tightening around the barometer; tide of people behind you]
[Sound: A shrill bark — an order you don't understand at first — then footsteps, purposeful and heavy]

Security moves in. Two of them kneel over a low, woven bed of marsh grasses someone's grandmother nurtured for months — delicate things knitted to life from mud and seed. One of the men reaches down, gloved hand closing under the basket.

You step forward without thinking. Your voice comes out below everything but razor-clear: You: "Hands off the beds."

Security man: "Private property. Move aside, ma'am."

You: "Those beds are community property." Your words hit like a cleat catching on rope. They don't have the polish of press releases; they have the one thing that will register here — ownership born of labor.

Security man: (tight) "We have orders."

Tala: "Orders from who? To steal our work?"

Security man: "From the developer. You're obstructing a lawful repaving."

The man lifts. Fingers graze woven reed. The crowd surges. A volunteer tries to wedge a hand between glove and basket. A sharp, knuckly arm jerks back. Shouts swell. Someone screams; it might be joy or panic and it might be the same thing.

[Music: Percussion spikes into brittle staccato]
[Sound: Scuffle — cloth tears, a muffled thud]

A scuffle blossoms, sudden and raw. You move faster than thought, elbowing between a security hand and the fragile earth. A security guard shoves you; wood from a nearby pallet bangs your shoulder. A seed tag comes free and flutters like a white flag.

Officer: "This is your final warning! Disperse!"

You: (to the officer) "They're taking living things. This is theft."

Officer: (flat) "You are in violation. If you do not move, you will be detained."

There are flares of movement: two arrests, then another. Someone's voice breaks as they're cuffed; Tala swears and lunges. Captain Reyes — who has stood behind you with his carved pipe at his lips — steps forward, not to fight but to fill the air with something else.

Captain Reyes: (low, carrying) "When I was a boy, we ran a line of nets across the inlet to stop the trawlers. We stood out there all night, singing to keep our hands from shaking. The officers tried to cut the nets. We sang louder. We lost a season later, sure — but the names of those nights keep the harbor from forgetting us."

You listen. The riot of sounds — sirens, shouted names, the gritty rasp of cuffs — drops into the hollow he calls out of your chest. His voice is not angry. It is steady and weathered, and it has the weight of a tide that remembers its own pull.

You know the thing inside him: the memory of contracts he once drafted for a corporation that would have buried this marsh under engineered stone. His gaze flicks to you like a question. He doesn't move to tackle the guard. He lifts his chin instead.

Evan: (quiet, careful) "I'm here. I'm recording. I won't let them pretend nothing happened."

You: (to Evan) "We need you closer if you can. Your footage will matter."

Evan: "I'll come forward when it's safe. Or —" He hesitates, because he is wired to measure risk even in a crowd. "— I'll keep the lens steady. I go where I'm needed."

You want to tell him that being present is already what you need, but words tangle with the clamoring crowd. You nod instead, and the nod says more than a paragraph ever could.

[Sound: Two short, urgent blasts from a siren; a new line of uniforms moving to flank the site]
[Music: Rising electronic swell; rhythm accelerates]

Dr. Sora Kim appears at the edge of the crowd like a purposeful wind, lab coat smudged with mud, field notes clasped in one hand. Her glasses fogged at the edges, but her eyes are hard and bright.

Dr. Sora Kim: "We can publish a rapid-impact brief. Buffering calculations, storm attenuation models, community displacement projections. If we file it with the county now, we can ask for an emergency injunction."

Mayor Lionel Park: (from the periphery, muddled) "An injunction? That could stop work. But — it takes time. And the courts—"

Dr. Sora Kim: "Time is relative here, Lionel. The data can be rendered urgent. It won't be guaranteed — I know the legal hurdles. But I can make the case that the work is a public hazard."

Mayor Lionel Park: (rubbing his temples) "If I back it, I get headlines praising action — or I get sued for delaying development. People want jobs now."

Tala: (sharp) "People also want homes that don't wash away. Which people's jobs count if the water eats their porches?"

Mayor Lionel Park: "It's not that simple."

Your barometer swings in your palm; the little needle thunks like a heart against copper. Sora's offer feels like a lifeline and a weight. Legal work means waiting. Waiting means the site may keep moving. The arrested people are led away — faces grim but not broken. The crowd forms a slow ring; someone starts to drum on an overturned bucket.

Captain Reyes moves through the ring, his boots heavy with mud. He stops beside you and lays a hand on your shoulder, callused and sure.

Captain Reyes: "We did this before because we had to. The sea doesn't respect votes. But it does respect people who refuse to be invisible."

You: "If we file an injunction, will it actually buy time? Or will they bulldoze through and call it progress later?"

Dr. Sora Kim: "If the brief is compelling and the public outcry is large, it will force a hearing. The law loves numbers that look like danger. I can get the numbers to look like danger."

Tala: (softening) "Numbers and danger. That's not a bad song."

Mayor Lionel Park comes closer, his face a ledger of compromise. He keeps glancing toward the rendering board and the developer's glossy pamphlets.

Mayor Lionel Park: "Maya —" He doesn't call you by your full name often. "If I order the injunction, there will be fallout. If I don't, we'll lose leverage. I need to know what the town can bear."

You: "What the town can bear is not an abstraction, Lionel. It's your grandmother's porch, Captain Reyes' nets, the kids who watch the ocean and don't know if they'll have a high school reunion. We need action that protects both people and place."

Mayor Lionel Park: (sighing) "I want to do the right thing, but I need cover. Political cover."

You: (firm) "Then make the cover come from the people you're supposed to serve. Back Sora. Stand with the protesters. Make the hearing about hazard and not just permits."

He hesitates. The weight of his countersigning presses down. You can see it in the way his fingers tremble at his coffee cup.

Meanwhile, the arrested are backlit by the plaster light from a media van's spotlight as they are processed at the temporary command center. Cameras chew the scene into a thousand small stories. You notice the way Evan's camera lingers on a child's face, on a seed tag, on a mud-smeared palm — the utterly human detail the press often misses.

Evan steps closer now and lowers his camera slightly. He meets your eyes; there is a tremor of something in him, old guilt braided with new resolve.

Evan: "I couldn't sign those contracts. But I still built the machines. I'm trying to make amends, in ways I can. If I stay behind the lens, I lose my voice. If I move forward, I risk repeating mistakes."

You: "You won't repeat them. Not if you help us make this clear. Help us show how this site will fail us in a storm. Show the models. Your workshops with the prototypes — they are proof."

Evan: (an exhale) "Then I'll be proof. I'll stand where I'm needed."

[Sound: The chant becomes a steady, determined heartbeat — not a howl but a mantra]
[Music: Full, urgent swell; strings and electronic beats converge into a single accelerating pulse]

There is a flurry of whispered counsel between Sora and a civil litigator Tala found on the Boardwalk. Captain Reyes, with the slow authority of tidewater, lays his hands on two seed beds and speaks so people nearby can hear.

Captain Reyes: "They've tried to buy us with promises. Today, they try to buy our memory. If the place goes, your grandchildren won't know why you fought. That's why you stand."

You breathe, a sharp in through salt and dust. You feel the crowd behind you like a living wall. You think of the people led away earlier — not as losses, but as points of light passed along a line. Your chest is tight, but something else unfolds under the tension: a bright, stubborn conviction that the work matters.

You measure the options in your mind like weights, like different tides:

- Hold the line, refuse to move, let them arrest you if they must. Stop construction now, but court chaos and legal backlash.
- Pivot to law and science — file emergency injunctions backed by Sora's rapid science. Play in court. Delay work, but risk the developer's narrative and the slow clock of justice.
- Take a truce — negotiate an immediate, targeted protection for the most vulnerable households and run public pilot demonstrations to gather the most persuasive data. Protect families now while you build the case.

Your barometer swings once more, the little needle nearly slicing the air. The crowd's faces — hopeful, terrified, resolute — press in. The air is electric and wet. You feel Evan at your side, Tala's warm shoulder, Captain Reyes' calm weight, Sora's clipped scientific certainty, Mayor Park's indecision like a small, brittle thing.

Everything narrows to a point: what kind of escalation will you lead?
