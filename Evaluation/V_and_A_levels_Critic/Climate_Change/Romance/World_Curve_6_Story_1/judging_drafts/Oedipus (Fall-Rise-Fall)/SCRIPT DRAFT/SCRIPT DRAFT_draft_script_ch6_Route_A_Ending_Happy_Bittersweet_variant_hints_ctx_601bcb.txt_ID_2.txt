Their answers are not neat. They never are. The pilot keeps its slow, hopeful rhythm. The neighborhood cheers in bits—shouts and claps carried by damp wind—and you let yourself note each face like tally marks against future storms.

[Scene: Pilot Site — Approaching Storm | Late Afternoon]

The cyclone punches a hole in the horizon that wasn't in the neatest model runs. It arrives with a steady, devastating patience. Your living breakwaters—resilient and alive—take the brunt with grace until they can't. They slow the first assaults; water hisses, then climbs.

Sensing the limits Sima had warned about, you and Rafi move like practiced ritualists, stacking and tying and pushing soil into hands that smell of salt and metal.

Rafi: "We're holding where it counts, but the promenade—" (he spits the words into the wind) "—the older foundations are slamming."
You: "More coir mats. Hold the line. Lio, help the families move anything that can be saved up the alleys."

The surge comes higher and farther than you expected. Where your design had created pockets to absorb and translate energy, the storm throws more volume into fewer places—the margins no longer a gentle edge but a blade. Old houses shudder. The cultural shrine—the small wooden alcove tucked into a sandstone embankment that had been rebuilt by hands across generations—becomes a focal point for the water's appetite.

You run toward it because of habit and because habit is sometimes the only map you have. Your boots slip. The rain cuts at your face like thrown needles.

Neighbor: (screaming, half-prayer, half-command) "Maya! The offerings—pull them up!"
You: (you reach with other hands; offerings come free in a wet, meaningless cascade) "Get anything you can—photographs, the pendant! Lio, now!"

For a breath everything snaps into a single, harrowing frame: the shrine taking water, wood bending, the old painted figure washing with lacquered colors and floating away like a small, private apocalypse. Then the surge takes it—an entire margin of memory dissolved down the throat of the sea. You stand there, chest tight, hands numb with cold and mud.

Time dilates: water passes, roars, then recedes with the indifferent slow of a clock. The engineered pockets lessened the damage in many places, but the shrine and several very old houses—the ones that had been the soul of the promenade—have been lost or grievously damaged.
