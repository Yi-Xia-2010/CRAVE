[Scene: Old Promenade — Temporary Hearing Tent | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: Wide shot of the tent pitched along the half-submerged boardwalk; banners flutter — MUNICIPAL HEARING: COASTAL RESILIENCY — rows of folding chairs packed with neighbors, journalists, and municipal staff; a raised dais with nameplates; a projection screen at the center showing a corporate rendering of a monolithic seawall.]
[Music: Low, steady ambient pulse — a soft drum undercurrent to mirror composed tension]
[Sound: Distant gulls, camera shutters, the slap of water against derelict boards; the canvas overhead flutters with a humid breeze]

You step into the tent carrying the weight you promised yourself you'd keep light enough to lift. The map-printed pages in your hands are warm from your palms; the ink smells faintly of printer toner and rain-damp air. Your braid is tucked beneath your collar so nothing loose will catch in the mic; you feel, for a second, like a piece of field equipment — calibrated, labeled, ready.

Dr. Sima is already at the projector, her glasses catching a glare of late sun as she clicks through a slide. Nearby, Rafi moves among the chairs, whispering last-minute instructions to neighbors. Lio stands a little apart, hoodie up against the spray that rides the wind; he catches your eye and gives you the same wry nod as before. Whatever happens next, there are faces threaded behind it.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands smoothing the top page — your hybrid plan summary, header neat and decisive]
[Sound: A microphone feedback squeak, then soft silence as Mayor Velez taps the lectern]

Mayor Ana Velez: "Thank you, everyone. We convene under emergency ordinance to hear presentations on proposed resilience measures. We'll begin with presentations from the private delegation, the municipal scientists, and then community representatives."

Her voice is exact. The tent is half civic ritual, half media circus — flashes from cameras punctuate the air like small, indifferent stars. You nod to Sima; she gives a small, professional smile and motions you forward.

[Visual: The podium at your chest height; the microphone hovers; you breathe in the salt and diesel mixture of the city]
[Music: A cello line enters, patient and measured]

Before you speak, the private firm's rendering fills the screen: a smooth arc of concrete rising from the breakers, gleaming, immaculate. Its caption reads: ENGINEERED SOLUTION — PROTECTING CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE.

A hush follows the image, the sort you get when everyone recognizes a promise that already costs something unspecified.

Corporate Presenter (off): "Our model shows a ninety-two percent reduction in modeled inundation for key infrastructure zones. It's a tested, fast-deployable solution."

You feel the room through the soles of your boots — a thrum of hope, skepticism, fear, stubbornness. When you step up, the microphone takes your breath and turns it into a fact-shaped thing. Your voice is steadier than the tightness you feel.

You (Maya): "Good afternoon. I'm Maya Corvin, representing the Low Row resilience collective. Thank you for this time."

You don't launch into rhetoric. You lay the maps on the lectern, point to tidal boundaries and living corridors, and let Sima's slides follow yours: model runs that show the same reduction in flood depth when a scaled barrier is combined with restored wetlands and managed retreat corridors.

Dr. Sima: "Our simulations indicate that a hybrid approach — targeted hardening plus ecological restoration — reduces localized flood risk comparably in many scenarios while maintaining wetland function. It lessens downstream amplification and preserves biological buffers."

Her language is precise; she adds the caveat that models are simplifications and that some compromises are unavoidable. No flourish. Only margins and numbers, and the quiet way a scientist names uncertainty.

You alternate between numbers and stories — how a patch of marsh slowed a storm last winter, how a child's school is closer to sea level than anyone admits on paper. The hybrid plan isn't a slogan; it's a schematic with maintenance schedules, community governance proposals, and staged funding needs. You watch the faces on the dais: Mayor Velez's composed restraint, Elias's warm but guarded attention, Kai's fingers drumming a slow, precise rhythm on her tablet.

Elias speaks after you. He stands with that practiced calm — the municipal planner who has learned to make compromise sound like prudence.

Elias Kahn: "Maya has presented a thorough option. My office sees potential in piloting hybrid measures in targeted neighborhoods — as a staged approach. Procedural safeguards and monitoring can be built in to ensure investor confidence and community oversight."

He doesn't champion your plan outright; he couches support in administrative language, in steps and thresholds. His tone is protective but cautious. When he looks at you, it's not an open flame; it's the light a hearth gives — steady, contained. You take the subtle support and store it like a coal that could be fanned.

Kai rises like she has a blueprint in her bones.

Kai Navarro: "The city doesn't have time for staged experiments. Storm intensity is accelerating. Speed is an ethical choice. We must secure infrastructure now — quickly, at scale — to prevent catastrophic failure. Every day we delay increases risk."

Her delivery is crisp, unblinking. When she says "ethical," the word lands with a sharpened edge: she frames urgency as a moral imperative that tolerates little ambiguity. There's an undercurrent in her gaze — not personal, not exactly; it's businesslike steel.

You respond, careful to avoid the trap of turning the hearing into an argument that sounds like moral one-upmanship.

You (Maya): "Speed matters. Lives matter. That's why I propose a phased hybrid — fast enough to deliver immediate protections in critical corridors, but designed to retain and restore ecological function where it prevents long-term harm."

The back-and-forth elongates. Mayor Velez asks technical clarifying questions; Sima outlines monitoring thresholds and contingency triggers. Elias presses on implementation timelines. Kai counters with deployment schedules and contractor readiness. Each exchange adds a layer: legal constraints, funding windows, community oversight mechanisms, investor confidence. The tent feels like a pressure chamber where decisions will be distilled.

[Visual: Tight shots — a journalist's notepad, a child's hand gripping a protest sign, the shake of a technician's gloved hand as he switches slide trays]
[Sound: A rumble low in the distance — not thunder yet, but the earth remembering the tide]

At one point, a neighbor at the back — someone you've watched stitch sandbags in the stairwells — stands up. Her voice is raw and necessary.

Neighbor (Mrs. Ortega): "We don't want walls that erase where we lived. But we want our kids' bedrooms to stay dry. Can't you listen to both?"

The room quiets. Even Kai's jaw faceting into a professional line softens, if only briefly. You watch Mayor Velez catalog the responses in a ledger of expression; she does not decide in the tent, she collects.

The tension tightens but remains measured — you can sense it building toward something, like a tide lifting but not yet cresting.

Then the weather intervenes. A wind shoves rain at the canvas; a far siren starts and grows louder, threading with the slap of waves against the old promenade. The tent shakes in the breath of a storm moving faster than the forecasts predicted. Phones vibrate against knees like small, anxious hearts.

Sound technicians mutter into headsets. Someone has already flipped to live; the corporate rendering blinks against a sky gone gunmetal. The factual hum of the hearing meets the chaotic calendar of weather.

Kai's voice holds — almost steady — but the line of her mouth shifts.

Kai Navarro: "This is exactly why we move fast. Models are fine when there is time. We don't have that luxury."

Mayor Velez raises a hand to quiet the rising undercurrent of shouts and questions.

Mayor Ana Velez: "We will not decide this hour. I am calling for an expedited vote — three business days from now. The board requires additional legal review, but the timeline will be short."

A murmur — not quite applause, not quite groan — moves through the tent like a small wave. The expedited vote is a victory of certainty; it forces a timeline into everyone’s hands. It also compresses options.

You feel the surge in your chest not as fear but as mobilization. The hearing staff bustle with logistics; Elias exchanges a look with you that is unreadable: relief mixed with apprehension, as if the timeline solves some problems and makes others urgent in new ways. Kai's expression is contained — a professional at the moment of delivery. Dr. Sima collects her notes and gives you a brief, expectant look: the science is done; what happens next will be politics.

[Visual: Papers rustle, people stand and cluster, the projection blinks between corporate renderings and ecological maps]
[Sound: Rain hitting the tent with quick, merciless staccato; distant sirens fold into the drum of the rain]

Rafi arrives beside you, palms damp, already shifting into organizer mode.

Rafi: "Three days. We can rally. We can push for a referendum, run outreach, call the networks—"

You hear the possibilities listed like a toolkit. Each tool has a cost: speed versus depth, public pressure versus brokered influence, exposure versus guarded negotiation. Your shoulders tighten the way they do before physical work; you feel the mental inventory: neighbors to call, petition signatures to gather, tenders to meet, a hard conversation to have with Kai if you go that route.

Sima sidles close, voice low but steady.

Dr. Sima: "From a risk perspective, pilots give us data quickly. But the political timeline may not allow ideal roll-out. Whatever you choose, build monitoring triggers and exit criteria. That will keep the science honest."

Elias stands a little apart, watching the tent fold into action. He steps toward you before he disappears into the staff cluster.

Elias Kahn: "If you want my office to try to buy time, to thread in community oversight language, I will. But I can't promise change without compromise. We need measurable asks, not ideals."

His offer is pragmatic. There's warmth beneath the caution, and the look he gives is complex — not a simple encouragement, nor a withdrawal, but a mapped negotiation of his role between you and the city's mechanisms.

Kai crosses the space briefly and stops near the edge of the tent, the rain darkening her jacket.

Kai Navarro: "If you want to come through our channels, I'll have my team outline a joint technical brief. It would be faster than a public campaign, and it keeps the project on schedule."

Her offer is a door that swings both ways: protective and perilous. You sense the implications — access to resources, but proximity to the firm that wants broad, fast control. There is also something understated in the way she says "your channels" — a recognition that you have power the firm can't buy outright.

You feel the moment bifurcate the air: public pressure, municipal negotiation, or corporate collaboration. Each path maps to a different idea of how to do what you love and whom to trust to carry it. The rain builds and the sound of the city in crisis presses in; the decision feels urgent but not frantic. The timeline the mayor set is the engine now, and you will have to choose which gears to engage.

You look at the folded maps in your hands. They are lighter than the conversation that just happened. You think about Lio's face, about the nursery on the rooftop, about the math Sima showed — the trade-offs between immediate protection and ecological function. Nothing about this is tidy. Nothing about this is easy.

You can feel the arousal building a steady notch higher — action edged with calculation rather than panic.

You set your jaw, inhaling the brine and the electric charge of a city about to move. There are three plausible avenues to influence the expedited vote, each with its own moral geometry and tactical demands.
