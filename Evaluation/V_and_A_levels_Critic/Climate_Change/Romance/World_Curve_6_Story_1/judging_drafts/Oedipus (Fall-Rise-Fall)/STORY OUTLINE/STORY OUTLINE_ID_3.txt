## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Compromise

---

## World View
Nueva Mar is a near-future coastal city-state built atop reclaimed wetlands and steep cliffside neighborhoods. Rising seas and intensifying storms have carved the city's politics: wealthy development corridors with corporate-managed flood defenses sit beside hand-repaired terraces and rooftop nurseries. Power is mediated through civic institutions (Municipal Hall) and private engineering firms that promise rapid protection in exchange for land and regulation. Religion plays no organizing role; instead, a shared ethic of practical stewardship and local ritual (harvest days, mural-making) animates neighborhoods like The Low Row. Class frictions run along access to durable flood infrastructure vs. subsistence from aquaculture and crafts. Geography is visceral—half-submerged promenades, terraces clinging to cliffs, and engineered seawalls slicing the skyline—so policy debates translate into immediate survival choices. Culture trades in bricolage resilience: community gardens, patched sandbags, rooftop green labs; corporate zones are cool, steel, and precise. The atmosphere is humid, bustling with a painterly realism—film grain, muted pastels in domestic areas, and monochrome corporate spaces. Conflicts center on whether to accept large-scale engineered security that displaces and sterilizes the shoreline, or invest in distributed, ecologically integrated resilience that preserves livelihoods but risks slower results and immediate vulnerability.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Corvin, a coastal systems engineer turned community organizer. The story opens with the city announcing an urgent corporate seawall project that promises rapid protection but threatens the Low Row's wetlands and cultural fabric. I rally neighbors, translate technical models with Dr. Sima Raza’s help, and face political throttling at Municipal Hall where Elias Kahn mediates between factions. My stubborn empathy clashes with Camila “Kai” Navarro’s efficient corporate mandate; beneath that clash simmer ethical kinship and suppressed attraction. The narrative follows an Oedipus curve: an initial fall—escalating setbacks as the corporate timeline accelerates and a storm damages outreach—leads into a temporary rise when a compromise seems achievable (hybrid plans, political openings, small victories), and ends with a harsher fall in which structural forces, emergency choices, or miscommunications produce irreversible losses. Romance threads mirror policy: with Elias, a stable, patient trust grows but may demand compromise; with Kai, fierce attraction collides with opposing values. Multiple branches let me choose negotiation, confrontation, or tactical alliances; every choice trades immediacy for sustainability. Endings range from community-led hybrid success to bittersweet compromises, to loss—each ending explores whether love and neighborhood can survive engineering inevitabilities.
