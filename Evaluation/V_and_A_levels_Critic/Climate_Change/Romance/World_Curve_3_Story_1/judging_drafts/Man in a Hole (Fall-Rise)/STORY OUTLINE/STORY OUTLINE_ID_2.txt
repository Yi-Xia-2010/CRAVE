## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
A near-future coastal metropolis of retrofitted mid-century towers and experimental green infrastructure lives on the edge of rising seas. Neighborhoods cling to reclaimed marshes and old docks; power is divided between municipal planners, private resilience firms, and grassroots collectives. Income disparity and development pressure are constant: longshore families, fisherfolk, and repair collectives share cramped adaptive housing while global capital eyes flagship engineered defenses. Culture blends ritualized community maintenance (boat blessings, tide-mark anniversaries) with pragmatic engineering culture—data kiosks stand beside hand-knit marsh baskets. Religion and formal faiths exist but the dominant ethos is secular stewardship and collective memory: elders like Samir are moral anchors. Geography is dominated by ribboned salt marshes and the seawall promenade; weather alternates between golden-hour calm and sudden stormlight. Politically, Councilwoman Elena balances re-election, developers' pressure, and vocal grassroots demands. The atmosphere is humid, salt-thick, and tactile: walls flake with salt, gardens form vertical tapestries, and neon flood beacons pulse in puddles. The conflict centers on whether Maya's floodplain neighborhood follows a slower, community-led nature-based resilience or accepts a visible, fast, engineered seawall championed by Ari and financed by outside capital. Intimacy and policy collide: lovers become advocates for competing resilience philosophies.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Ortega: a pragmatic idealist who returned to the floodplain to lead a grassroots resilience collective. The narrative follows a "Man in a Hole" (Fall → Rise) arc. Early chapters trace the mounting pressure—funding threats, a failed pilot, and political urgency—that push me into a crisis where an urgent municipal vote looms. At the low point a storm and damaged pilot project fracture community trust and threaten displacement. From that bottom I must choose a path: double down on community-led living shorelines (route A), negotiate a hybrid compromise with Ari (route B), stand firm and sacrifice political capital to protect autonomy (route C), or accept immediate engineered protection for the wider city (route D). Each path is short (six-chapter depth from the start), and each decision entangles romance: Luca (warm, hands-on organizer) embodies local stewardship; Ari (charismatic technocrat) embodies scalable engineering. Secondary characters—Nora, Samir, Councilwoman Reyes—push the stakes. Branch routes explore love versus duty: in some outcomes I build a life with Luca and keep local stewardship (Happy), in others I partner with Ari at personal cost (Bittersweet), in another I preserve my moral stance but face solitude (True), and in the last, political compromise and infrastructural failure lead to loss (Sad). Throughout, salt, wet textures, and sunset/stormlight underscore choices—every intimate touch echoes systemic consequences.
