## Visual Novel Title
Tide Markers

---

## World View
The city is a near-future coastal sprawl where rising seas and storm-surge seasons have become ordinary weather. Neighborhoods cling to raised cobbles and reed-lined causeways; reclaimed wetlands and retrofitted mid-century apartment blocks sit beside municipal pilot projects and private flood-investment towers. Politics have shifted into urgent triage — council hearings, private capital bids, and grassroots labor compete for limited funding. Religion and ritual survive in small-scale marsh ceremonies, fishers’ blessings, and communal mending circles; there is no unified creed, but strong cultural memory anchors place-based identity. Class lines run along elevation and designation: “resilience districts” favored by investors get hard infrastructure and relocation stipends, while informal settlements press for living shorelines and community stewardship. The city’s atmosphere is humid and salt-thick, punctuated by gold-and-steel sunsets and the nervous shimmer of stormlight. Public culture admires scalability and glossy metrics, while neighbourhood cultures prize oral histories and everyday care. The tension at the heart of the city is not only about concrete versus mangroves, but about who gets to decide what “safety” means — and whether protection will cost people their homes and memories.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maya Ortega) return to my floodplain neighborhood after training in environmental engineering, building a resilience collective that experiments with living shorelines and marsh restoration. The city faces a high-stakes decision: accept Ari Nakamura’s flagship engineered seawall project (fast, fundable, but displacement-prone) or pursue slower, community-led nature-based resilience championed by Luca Chen and our neighbors. Early promise turns to crisis: a pilot failure and political pressure create a collapse of trust and a local emergency. I fall into the “hole” — blamed, out-funded, and forced to choose between pragmatic compromise, principled refusal, or tactical risk. Across branching choices, the narrative explores Romance vs Duty: I can lean into Luca’s communal, hand-hewn approach and deepening intimacy; into Ari’s polished technocratic partnership blending professional advancement with complicated feelings; or carve a solitary moral route that sacrifices recognition for local autonomy. The arc follows Man in a Hole: a fall into crisis, struggle at the bottom while deciding moral/romantic partnerships, and an ascent toward recovery — different endings yield happy community partnership, bittersweet compromise, principled solitude, or tragic loss depending on choices.

- The outline adheres to the Man-in-a-Hole (Fall → Rise) valence arc: Chapters 1–3 descend into crisis (Neutral → Negative → VeryNegative), and subsequent chapters climb toward recovery (Negative → Neutral → Positive → VeryPositive for True endings). All branches respect this global fall-then-rise valence trajectory.
- Arousal increases across chapters to build toward climactic, VeryHigh intensity in decision and ending chapters.
- All chapters are written in first-person POV as Maya Ortega and explicitly reference character visual traits (hair braid, jackets, Ari’s cuff, Luca’s pendant), locations, and the world’s tactile textures.
- Branching nodes occur at Chapter 3 (major split), Chapter 5 (Route A split), Chapter 8 (Route B split), and Chapter 11 (Route C split), providing at least three distinct splits and multiple branching decisions.
- Every route from Chapter 1 to any Ending visits no more than six chapters (path length counted sequentially), satisfying the estimated depth constraint.
- No new main characters or visual designs were introduced; all descriptions use provided character and location assets.
- The visual novel is narrative-driven (no gameplay systems), focusing on moral choices between community resilience and engineered solutions entwined with romantic consequences.
