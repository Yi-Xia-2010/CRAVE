You came here from the Atrium with your voice still a cage in your throat. The choice you made there — to pull at threads you couldn't ignore — is the current that brought you back to this room. Nora has already scoured the municipal lists; Samir's slow footsteps are the kind that arrive with a purpose, not haste. Your field notebook lies open but unused beside a scatter of printouts; tonight the paper, not the pen, will do the talking.

The air tastes of salt and old coffee. Under the lamp, ink reflects like wet tidewater. You rub the bridge of your nose and try to uncoil the tight knot of adrenaline in your ribs. Evidence is not just data; it's a geometry of possible harm. Each file you unseal redraws the map of who will gain and who will lose if you make it public.

Nora lifts a sheaf of subcontract clauses toward the light as if trying to see past the typeface into motive. Her face is stained with copier toner and exhaustion, but her hands tremble with the same sharpness you feel — calculation moving to action.

Nora: "Look at this line — 'preferred contractor' clause, passed through a shell company. It's not just a name drop. There are payment triggers tied to 'expedited relocation provisions.'"
You: "Expedited relocation — that's a euphemism for displacement. Who signs that off?"
Nora: (tapping a table) "A municipal procurement advisor. Cross-referenced with a PR firm that worked the council last election. It's messy, but the links are there."

You scan the pages again, your eyes stinging from the lamp's glare. The memos have a polite tone, full of passive verbs that hide action: 'recommended,' 'consideration,' 'favors to be reviewed.' Reading them feels like watching hands move behind a curtain, and every clause tightens your chest.

Samir clears his throat in the doorway, his shadow long and deliberate. The part of you that is always afraid of being too impulsive tightens its grip.

Samir: "You found trouble, then."
You: "Enough that it can't be ignored. Enough that it could—" (you stop, because you can't finish a sentence that names possibility as fate)
Samir: "—break people. I know this shoreline, Maya. I've seen promises float like driftwood. We need to be sure what we throw into the tide." (He steps closer, the smell of tobacco and salt on his coat.) "If you make this public, you make enemies with knives that don't bite open. If you hold it, you hold a weapon behind your back."

You feel that weapon — hard, heavy, live in your hands. The pages are a ledger of decisions that were made with other people's lives as margins. You can almost hear the hum of the Atrium's projectors, the cold, measured cadence that turns homes into metrics.

Nora: (leaning in) "We can timestamp everything. Metadata, IP trails — I can show the procurement calendar aligning with a private dinner the council aide attended. It's not just coincidence." (She exhales sharply.) "But if we leak raw, without a plan, the developer will drown us in lawsuits and disinformation. If we negotiate with it, we risk letting the process sterilize the people in the margins."

Your stomach pitches at the binary spread before you. The pilot project that failed under your name is a wet stain on your memory; you can still feel the weight of Samir's disappointed nod when the funds evaporated, the way a neighborhood learns a new word for betrayal. You don't want to repeat that.

[Scene: Old Docks Community Hub — Confrontations | Late Night]
[Visual: Lamp halos, faces carved into half-shadow. Outside, the storm sharpens, puncturing the warehouse with cold light.]
[Music: Tense strings escalate; heartbeat thump layered under percussion]
[Sound: Rain hard against corrugated metal, shouting muffled from elsewhere on the docks]

Luca appears at the door with wet hair and a wet grin that doesn't reach his eyes. He drops into a chair without asking, boots leaving a dark crescent on the floor.

Luca: "You shouldn't be doing this alone. You shouldn't be the only one carrying an arson plan in your pocket." (He laughs short.) "So what do you have?"
You: "Enough to be dangerous. Enough that if it goes public without a plan, they'll gaslight everyone. And enough that if we use it quietly, it could secure oversight — or it could be neutered into nothing."
Luca: "We don't have to be polite with them. Let it out. Let it set the conversation on fire. People need to see the rot."

Samir's hands fold in his lap like an anchor. His voice is slow but sharp.

Samir: "Fire is not always clean. It eats the things we love along with the things that feed it. If you open this without shelter, you'll torch the wrong roofs. I'll not see my neighbors homeless because of a righteous burn."

You feel pulled between two tensions: Luca's incendiary courage that honors truth by spectacle, and Samir's patient refusal to let justice be a conflagration if it destroys innocents. Nora's eyes flick between you and a folder of red-flagged contracts.

Nora: "There's a third way — staged exposure. Leak a dossier to a watchdog with a timeline. It forces transparency but gives us a legal leash. It buys time for hearings. But it's still risky."

Then, as if the room had been a sleeping animal and you were the finger that pressed hard enough, there is a knock at the side door. You leave the lamp and go answer. It's a courier with a sealed envelope and a note: 'Council liaison—request for a private meeting. Ari proposes mediation.' The paper trembles in your hands like a pulse you didn't pace.

Back at the table, Ari's voice reaches you through a call you finally accept. He speaks with that cool, deliberate cadence that always made you want to listen.

Ari: "Maya. I heard you are looking into procurement irregularities. I thought we should talk before this becomes a spectacle."
You: "You heard the right thing. Are you offering help or containment?"
Ari: (a small, even laugh) "I offer resolution. Public leaks accomplish headlines; they don't always produce resilient outcomes. We can convene an independent audit and schedule community oversight. We can fund immediate reinforcements while we sort the contracts."
You: "And whose protections are you prioritizing? The city's livability or the developer's margins?"
Ari: "Both. We can protect people and the city's economy. Binary choices rarely reflect complex realities."

You want to tell him that 'both' is often a way of making the powerful whole and the vulnerable small. You want to tell him that independent audits are delayed by footnotes and budget arbitrage. Instead, you breathe and listen to the way his words slice into the room. He is offering a ledgered path that could save lives — and maybe your career — but at what cost to the neighborhood's autonomy?

Luca's hand slams the table.

Luca: "He's offering to fund a narrative, Maya. With him in the room, the neighborhood will be reshaped into a KPIs case study. Your people will be consulted like a focus group."
Ari: (coolly) "I am offering operational capacity. You know how to run living shorelines — I know how to scale them. We are both necessary."
You: "Necessary to whom?" (Your voice is a blade wrapped in velvet.) "If we do this with you, do our people still decide where they live? Will Samir's nets still hang from the docks in ten years?"

Silence falls heavy and electric. The storm outside bangs like a judge's gavel. Every voice around the table is an entreaty, a strategy, a fear. Inside you, the pilot project's failure drums like a looped recording: promises made, parts outsourced, people left to mend roofs with nothing.

Your chest is tight enough that breathing feels like a decision.

Samir: "Maya. I have lived through agreements signed in warm rooms. Sometimes paper can save a life; sometimes it betrays one. You must decide if you're willing to risk your whole person so others don't have to lose their homes."

Nora watches you with an expression that is both pleading and practical. Luca's jaw is a cliff. Ari's amber eyes, through the phone, are cool as a tide pool in moonlight — reflective, unreadable.

The evidence sits between you all like a wound. You can hand it to the public and hope scandal will force accountability. You can hand it to Ari and hope negotiation buys safeguards. You can let it sit and gather more until it becomes incontrovertible — but waiting is a risk too: storms are not patient.

You stand and pace once, twice. Your boots squeak on the wet floor. You think of the people who will be affected by whichever route you choose: elders in the low rowhouses, kids who learned to swim before they learned to read, the hands that sew nets and patch roofs and tie each other's boats to the same battered pier.

You feel guilt sit on your shoulders like a small animal. Every option carries weight; every inaction is itself an action. The storm outside intensifies, as if the weather knows this is the hinge hour.

You press your palm flat to the top of the stack of files. The paper is soft and moist. The room leans in to hear what you will do.
