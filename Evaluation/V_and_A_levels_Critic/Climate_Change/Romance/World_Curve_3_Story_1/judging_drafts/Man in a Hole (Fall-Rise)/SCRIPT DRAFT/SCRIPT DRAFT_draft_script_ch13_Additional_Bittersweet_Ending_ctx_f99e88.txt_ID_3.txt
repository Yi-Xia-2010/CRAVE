Nora, perched at your left, twists a strand of auburn hair into her fist. Her knuckles bruise the paper. She meets your eyes with a look that says: hold firm, but finish the sentence.

Luca Chen: "If the covenants are paper-thin potholes, they'll tear. We need community-managed repairs, line items in budgets, and enforcement mechanisms that don't require a lawyer for every complaint. And we need a relocation fund for the pockets that cannot be saved — not a promise, a trust."

Ari: (a soft, almost apologetic smile) "That's reasonable. The trust should be phased: immediate mitigation money, then a long-term fund scaled to risk indices. We can write that into the funding model."

We will also create a relocation trust for neighborhoods the models flag as unsavable, designed and administered with residents, lawyers, and Samir's council of elders."

Councilwoman Reyes exhales, a professional sound that hides a private calculation.

Councilwoman Reyes: "This will be an ugly number in the budget and a headline magnet. But if we can couple engineered protection with clearly codified community guardianship, I will carry it to the floor."

Ari's amber eyes find yours. For a beat they are not the copper of municipal projection but something closer — recognition of the stakes, and, quietly, something like respect.

Ari Nakamura: "I will put my firm's team on the drafting. We will accept resident veto on relocation clauses. I expect public scrutiny. I won't ask for silence in exchange."

You: (the words feel both like settlement and surrender) "And I won't ask you to erase the neighborhoods that cannot be saved. I will insist on dignified relocation packages designed by those who will move, not for them."

Nora Díaz: "It looks like a scar, but it's a scar that keeps hearts beating inside houses. You did three rounds with legal on the covenant text. The oversight board is populated — half residents, half technical advisors, one elder seat for Samir. Funding's in escrow. Councilwoman Reyes signed the emergency ordinance this morning."

You: "And the relocation trust?"

Nora: (taps her tablet) "Seeded. Ari's firm put up the matching tranche on condition of oversight. They did the press release before dawn."

Luca Chen: "We can weave these along the promenade, mark where the old fairs were. If they're building something that erases the skyline, we can at least leave markers so people remember where they danced."

You: (the thought floods you with warmth, a tiny ember of ritual you can still tend) "I want that. If we have to build walls, we will build memory too."

He ties a ribbon to a railing, color bright against concrete gray. For a moment you let your head lean on his shoulder and breathe. The sensation is both sacrament and bargaining.

Ari arrives, dusting his blazer despite the griminess of the site. He wears a hard hat now, not the AR cuff. He watches the concrete being poured as if making a quiet calculation about flows and loads and timelines.

Ari Nakamura: "The sensors are online. They will give us near-real-time stress readings. If anything starts to fail, we divert funds from the contingency to immediate repair. The escrow is ring-fenced. Your oversight board will have emergency pull. You wanted law; you got law."

You: (you look at him and notice the faint tremor in his hand — not weakness, but the residue of sleepness nights spent in modeling) "You didn't have to put your firm's reputation on the line."

Ari: "I didn't have to. But I chose to. I believe in saving as much as we can without tearing the community out of its own history."

Luca: (his voice low, a rough tide) "And what about us, Maya? Between council hearings and press cycles, where are we on that map?"

You: "I don't know yet. I don't know if there is room for everything I promised and everything you need."

Luca Chen: "Then promise me you won't lose the small things while you're doing the big ones. Our rituals don't have a budget line. They only have people."

You feel the ache of that truth like a bruise. You nod, because your head and your heart are two committees that can sometimes reach similar motions.

It's been months. The seawall stands where it needed to. The oversight board convenes in a repurposed boathouse. You stand at the edge of the marsh, the twilight making the grasses silver. A crowd gathers — residents, council members, funders, and press. Ari stands a few rows behind you, an ambiguous presence. Luca is at your side, dirt under his nails, a ribbon still tied around his wrist.

Councilwoman Reyes steps up to the mic, face softer than it was in the chamber.

Ari Nakamura: "You were right in the council. The covenants will be a model citywide if we can keep them durable. I pushed my board harder than I thought I would."

You: (you sense an unspoken labored clause: he admires you, he partnered with you, he changed his firm's terms) "You did what I asked. You kept your word."

Ari: "We'll keep the board honest. I'll keep pushing for scalable work that doesn't callously relocate. I won't pretend this is perfect, Maya. But it matters."

You whisper to the wall and to the marsh and to yourself: "We did the best we could. We will keep doing it."
