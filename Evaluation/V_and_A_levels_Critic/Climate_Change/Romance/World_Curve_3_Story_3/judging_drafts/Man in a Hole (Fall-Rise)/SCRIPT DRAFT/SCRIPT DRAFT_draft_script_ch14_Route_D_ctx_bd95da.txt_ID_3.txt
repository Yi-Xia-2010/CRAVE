[Scene: Harbor (retrofitted) | Late Afternoon — After the Build]

You stand at the edge of the boardwalk and count the new language etched across the water: steel bulkheads, angular sluice gates, neat rows of sensor buoys glowing faint neon. They answer the practical problem—hold the flood, move the surge—and they look, from this angle, like a permanent rewrite.

Maia Rivera: (You thought the trade could be squared with careful clauses and watchful oversight. You thought speed was triage; you thought you were choosing the least-worst path. The harbor tells another version. It is tidy, fast, and unfamiliar.)

You breathe in salt and machine oil. The smell is an odd, neutral ledger: necessity annotated with cost.

Jonah Kade: "You came early." (He wipes his hands on a rag, the rag smelling of fish and rope. He ties a new patch on a net at the rail with practiced, quick knots.) "Was going to call, but I knew you'd be here watching the tide."

Maia Rivera: (You want to slide the ledger of choices across the planks and trade every heavy sentence for explanation. Instead you say,) "I needed to see it while light lasted."

Jonah Kade: "It looks...different." (His eyes travel along the line of levee—he says the word with care.) "It does what it was supposed to. Nothing drowned last month. That matters."

Maia Rivera: "It matters more than I can say." (You mean the saved houses, the people whose basements remain dry.) "I didn't—" (You hesitate because the sentence collapses into something you can't be sure you have the right to finish.) "—I didn't make the decision without counting the cost."

Jonah Kade: (He laughs, but there's no humor in it.) "Counting costs and counting lobsters are different things." (He tugs at the patched net as if to puncture the air.) "Lupe's been posting the compensation notices. People read them and rub at the old maps like trying to erase tide marks."

Maia Rivera: "How are the channels? Are the retrofit lines holding to the school of smalls?" (You ask the technical thing because it's the easiest, least-blameful seam.)

Jonah Kade: "We rerouted a channel past the old kelp bed. Some of the smaller crews are spending nights relearning where to set lines. The big boats do fine; the small ones—" (He looks at you.) "They take longer to adapt."

Jonah's mouth tenses like a caught line. The harbor hum presses at your ears—the very practical success of the build and the soft, human tally of what does not fit into new schematics.

[Scene: Flooded Historic Quarter | Evening — Demonstration Night]

[Visual: Cobblestone streets half-submerged under a shallow, reflective sheen; storefronts retrofitted with impermanent scaffolding and corporate placards]
[Sound: A swell of voices—chants, a megaphone cutting through, boots squelching on wet cobbles]
[Music: Dissonant brass stabs; rhythms intensify to a nearly frantic pace]

You walk into a crowd that smells of rain, wet paper, and baked bread—community scents bent into protest. Dana Park stands near the front, banner heavy with paint and conviction. The demonstration is not merely noise; it is a living ledger of grievance.

Dana Park: "You hear them? They think safety was enough." (She hooks the banner onto the railing and meets your eyes with the kind of accusation that stays soft to the end.) "They think a levee erases the debt."

Maia Rivera: "No one thinks that." (You find your voice in the press of bodies. You try not to sound defensive. You try to sound real.) "We built what would keep people alive through a predicted surge. We couldn't wait."

Dana Park: "You made that call." (She steps closer.) "You chose speed. You chose contracts. People lost the places they used to meet; the market stalls are reoriented toward the pumps and the company's concession kiosks. It's not only structures—it's how we gather."

Maia Rivera: (Her words land with a kind of neutral factuality; there is no melodrama in Dana's anger—only the bluntness of consequence. You feel the volume of the crowd press in. Your heartbeat quickens until it matches the tempo of the drums.) "I chose what I thought would save more of us, Dana."

Dana Park: "You chose sacrifice, not mitigation. You chose a plan with a timetable, not with our hands on the wheel." (There is a beat where the microphones catch nothing but breath. People around you shout names—some in praise, many in sorrow.)

Jonah Kade: (He stands behind you, not in front; he wants to be practical, to wedge calm into the fissure, but his hollowed expression cuts through any attempt at easy reconciliation.) "Maia—" (He says your name like a small knot.) "We can haul nets and fix boats and teach our kids new lines. We'll do it. But I'm not made of anything to make this not feel like a loss."

Maia Rivera: "I know." (You want to say more—about being wrong, about being right—but the sentence bunches. Instead you note the little details that reveal truth: Jonah's hands tremble a fraction when a chant rises. Lupe quietly counts faces. Dana barks orders to marshals. Elias's PR vans hum nearby, blue logos pulsing in sympathetic light.)

Elias Wren: "Timelines saved homes." (He offers a small, almost clinical concession.) "No solution is perfect—"

Maia Rivera: "No solution is perfect," (You echo the phrase with the flat, exhausted cadence it has taken on.) "But perfection shouldn't be an excuse for sacrifice without consent."

Elias Wren: (He pauses as if to measure the rhetorical weight of your accusation.) "Consent is complicated in crisis. Waiting for unanimous consent can cause worse harm. That is the calculus we used."

Maia Rivera: (You think of Tomas' voice when he told stories about old storms—how the town had always found some way to mend. You also think of Lupe's ledger of notices, the ways compensation failed to buy a porch. Neutrality of facts presses against the human ledger.) "You used calculus," (you say softly) "and I signed the proof."

Elias Wren: "You signed to get action." (He looks at you with a tenderness that might be real or might be strategic; you cannot tell the difference anymore. The distance between you is filled with the residue of decisions.) "We both wanted the same thing—less loss."

Maia Rivera: (The phrase stays like a ledger entry: the same thing, uncertain credits and debits.) "Maybe we wanted it differently," (you say.) "Maybe we counted different things as essential."

Your chest tightens; the day has been a series of transactions that added up to shorelines and absences. You find yourself awake more mornings with a translator's feeling: you misread the dialect of place, tempted by a clear sentence when what lived there had been a long, worn story.

Jonah Kade: (He stands with hands in pockets, the sea-glass of his eyes reflecting a steel you do not want.) "You said you'd take care of us."

Maia Rivera: "I thought taking care meant keeping people from drowning." (You hear the plainness of that defense and how small it sounds.) "I did not know taking care would feel like erasing where we met."

Jonah Kade: "We all make trades." (He pulls his cap down and looks away. You hear the tide under the boards, a low mechanical groan from the pumps. His voice is even and tired.) "But some trades don't have receipts you can live with."

"I don't know what the right receipts are anymore," (you admit, voice very small in the roar.) "I thought speed would buy lives. I thought it would buy time to fix the rest."

Jonah Kade: "Time doesn't always heal the holes we cut." (He steps back into the work—an offer and a distance in one motion.) "But we'll try. We always try."
