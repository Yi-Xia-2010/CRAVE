You come back on the low tide, when the quarter still smells like yesterday's storm and the day's first smoke. The field notebook is warm in your hand, tide charts pressed into its battered cover; the solar pin at your lapel leaves a faint heat against your jacket where it charged through the overnight. Salt has traced white veins in your dusky auburn bob; you tuck a damp strand behind your ear because the motion steadies something in your chest.

You check the sensors clipped along the living seawall as you move: salinity drift within an acceptable band, root depth steady on the planted terraces, one conductivity probe flickering and then steadying when you tap it. You run fingers over the printed graphs in your notebook and make a mark—minute, decisive. It feels like organizing your breath.

Beyond the garden terraces the horizon sits like a thin, cold teeth-edge—the cranes of VossArc cut their silhouettes into the dawn. They are always there now: a clinical geometry against the watercolor softness of Harborlight. You look at them the way someone looks at a weather system—measuring, naming, not yet alarmed. The presence of that scale sharpens the edges of the neighborhood's softer problems, but it does not change why you came back. It simply adds a line to your list.

Maya appears at the next junction, braid swinging, coat dusted with ash from the stove she keeps on the hall steps. She has that habitual squint that means she has already been talking to three people before her second cup of coffee.

Maya Lin: "You're back early. Tide-watch went easy?"
You: "Quiet shift. The swell held. A probe hiccupped but settled." (You lift the notebook, the small rectangle of paper and tape like proof that the world remains legible.)

Maya Lin: (tilting her head) "Good. Old Mara's been asking about the west terrace—said the mangrove shoots looked shallow this morning."

Maya Lin: (soft laugh) "Always with the transects. Promise you won't spend the whole morning with your head in a soil sampler."
You: "No promises." (You both smile in the way of people who have learned to measure hope in spreadsheets and saplings.)

Old Mara is on the terrace steps, wrapped in the layered shawls you remember from childhood photographs—salt-stained hems, a collection of small fishing hooks in her necklace. Her face is a map of Harborlight: weather-lines and long, gentle judgments.

Old Mara: (eyes locking on you) "You always come back as the dawn breaks." (She coughs a laugh.) "Thought you'd be in some lab teaching city planners to be less foolish."
You: "Some of them listen. Some don't." (You try to say it lightly; the weight of 'some' lands between you like a stone.)

Old Mara: (a long, unreadable look) "They'll listen when the sea takes something important enough. Until then—" (she lifts a hand, palm up, as if weighing the quarter) "—we do what we can." (Her voice is steady, a resonance that has steadied generations.)

You: (soft) "We do what we can." (Saying it out loud is both a promise and an inventory.)

Narration
You move through the morning in small, exact acts: a marker scraped clean and reset; a note stuffed into the community hall's chalk box; a quick calibration of the salinity probe with the little syringe you carry in your pocket. Your hands remember the motions the way tides remember moonlight. Each task unspools a kind of containment: if the sensors are right, then you can breathe; if the planters hold, then the children will have greens this season; if the living wall's root depth keeps pace with the marsh, you grant the neighborhood days.

You pause when you look up: the cranes at VossArc are working in a geometry that speaks of timelines far longer than your lists. They are a horizon of possibility and of pressure—you catalog them not with despair but as another variable.

Ori sidles beside you, lower voice, joking but careful.

Ori Navarro: "You ever think about leaving again? For a project that's...clean, not salt-smeared?"
You: (honest, quietly) "Sometimes." (You set your hand on the notebook like an anchor.) "But then I think of the market, of Mara's stories, of the kids who fish off the terraces. There's a tether here." (The tether is not only place; it is responsibility.)

Ori Navarro: (older than the joke suggests) "Yeah." (He lets the word float between you.) "I build things, Ava. Sometimes I build them to run away and sometimes to stay. I don't always know which one I am until it's done."

You: (meeting his amber-eyed look) "We don't have to know yet." (For now, the day asks only for small, steady things. For now, you can be enough in small increments.)

Narration
The morning stretches. You sketch a rough map of the terraces, pencil sinking into paper, translating tactile measurements into a plan that will become the afternoon's work: check anchor points, swap the flux gauge, hold a short meeting at the community hall to confirm volunteer shifts. The list is a promise, and the promise slides under the weight of your survivor's guilt—quiet, ever-present—but it does not dominate. It is a seam in your sleeve you have learned to stitch.

You run your thumb over a small margin note—WHY I CAME BACK—and don't write an answer. Instead you write: KEEP THE WALL, KEEP THE PEOPLE. The sentence is plain. Hope, in this hour, is work.

A small breeze lifts the edge of your notebook and carries the smell of wood smoke and salt. The day is not urgent; it is deliberate. You have, for the moment, everything you need to begin.
