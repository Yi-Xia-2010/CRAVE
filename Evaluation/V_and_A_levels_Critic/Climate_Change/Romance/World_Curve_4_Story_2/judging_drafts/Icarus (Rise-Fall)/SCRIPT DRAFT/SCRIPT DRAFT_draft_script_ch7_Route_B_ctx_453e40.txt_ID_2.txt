You stand in the doorway and let the room take you in: the map-pinned walls, Jun's recorder clamped under a lamp, Maya's hands sorting placards with the same calm efficiency she uses to sew tarps, Old Mara's shawl hanging like weathered reef. Your megaphone sits heavy in your palm—an awkward, borrowed thing that smells faintly of salt and machine oil. The crowd is smaller than you hoped, larger than you feared. Enough faces you know; enough to meaningfully worry.

Maya looks up and gives you a quick, sharp nod. It is the look you've learned to read for readiness and for permission.

Maya Lin: "We hit the pier in fifteen. Keep it tight, keep it loud. Jun, get the clauses on the record—every promise from their spokespeople, every timeline. Ava, you lead at the Glass Pier. Old Mara wants to say something before we march."

Jun adjusts his recorder, the red light turning a tiny, authoritative pulse.

Jun: "I'll run tape, run a snippet for the op-ed. If Voss's team speaks, I want it on mic. Public record. No off-the-record leaks."

Old Mara moves closer, her fingers working with the precision of someone who has mended nets for decades. She presses a fishing-hook bead into your palm and pins it to your lapel with a single wooden toothpick. The bead is cool and heavy, the hooks small as memory.

Old Mara: "Keep our names like you keep the tide charts. Hold to the line. They will try to change the shape of our fear."

You feel the bead against your jacket, a tiny reef against your sternum. It is both benediction and brace.

You want to answer him in equal warmth and in the more careful, measured tones you use in meetings. You do both: laughing, then sobering.

Banners unfurl like stitched reefs: jade letters shouting TENURE, RUST-ORANGE lines spelling COMMUNITY RIGHTS.

You take the megaphone, test the push-button with the practiced calm that steadies your voice. It gives a small click—but your voice, when it comes through, is both you and what you are calling into being.

You: "Saltway has lived here for generations. We will not be curated into a postcard—or priced out in the name of safety. We ask for protection, not displacement. We ask that tenure be guaranteed."

Your speech is short by design. You give the bones—tenure, public management, real transparency—and then let the chorus expand. Maya organizes the march like a choreographer of tides: who walks where, which streets to occupy, how to block access without turning the crowd into a hazard. Jun's recorder sits against your chest under your jacket; you can feel its faint buzz as it marks time.

Jun: "I'll get an op-ed ready. I know an editor who'll put it up at first light. Names, quotes, their PR lines—I'll lay them out with context. If they try their usual rewrite, we'll be prepared."

There is friction in Jun's voice; he is hungry for exposure but careful about what being exposed costs. He looks at you with the question you have felt before: how far will you press?

You: (to Jun) "Take everything. If they move, we need the paper trail. Transparency is leverage."

Jun nods, the recorder's red eye like a lighthouse in the dim.

The march becomes motion: steps syncing to breath, placards aloft, chanting that is at first ragged but finds rhythm. Children dart ahead and return with the glee of those who take resistance as a playground. The Saltway streets are a braided choreography of memory—laundry flaps, carved doorframes, salt-streaked murals. You pass the Tide Garden and watch volunteers tighten bolts on a living module; a woman who once knitted for Old Mara kisses your cheek and tells you not to do anything reckless. "Not reckless," you say to her without irony. "Necessary."

[Visual: The Glass Pier looms ahead—cranes like exclamation points against a bruised sky]
[Sound: The city's distant hum shifts to a focused mechanical thrum; the crackle of radio transmissions from VossCorp's site]

At the mouth of the pier a line of slick, corporate banners flaps—sterile blue seductively lit—VossCorp's logo folded into the twilight. Their work lamps cut white through the dusk, rendering faces sharp and thin. You imagine Calder on a raised platform somewhere—pale, poised, measuring the optics.

A woman with a headset steps forward from the Voss team, clipboard balanced. Her smile is the practiced diplomacy of someone delivering comfort and containment in equal measure.

VossCorp PR Rep: (smiling, voice rehearsed) "We appreciate the community's concerns. Calder has proposed a listening session. We want a constructive conversation. Perhaps we can arrange a moderated town hall tomorrow? We'll have our legal team present, but it will be a dialogue."

Her words land like a net: almost generous, and precise in how they sand the edges of your demands. The phrase "moderated town hall" tastes of containment in your mouth.

You feel the crowd tense. Chants flutter into sharper rhythm—TENURE! TENURE! The one word becomes a drumbeat answering her smile.

Maya slaps a hand against the megaphone, ready to speak. Her jaw is tight in a way that tells you she's balancing appetite for visibility with the need to not give VossCorp an easy PR win.

Maya Lin: (brittle with controlled heat) "Talks with strings attached are not listening. We will not be led into a room that trims our demands into footnotes."

Ori moves through the crowd like a living seam, distributing planters, hands damp and smelling of earth and salt. He catches your eye and gives a quick, defiant grin. There is pride in him—not in the camera or the flash, but in the fact that he bedded a row of seedlings in reclaimed tins and that a grandmother at the front is holding one like a talisman.

Ori Navarro: (lean, loud enough for you) "If they want a listening session, we'll give them a listening session in the square. Put every promise on record. Put every clause in the open. We'll build a crowd big enough they can't talk over us."

Jun's recorder clicks audibly as he inches closer to the PR rep, a journalistic vulture circling for exactitudes. The legal team flanking her is not invisible; black-suited faces read like liability calculators. Security vests glint. There are photographers. You can feel the media's appetite, sharp and hungry.

You take a breath and let the megaphone carry a measured call.

You: "We will not exchange our homes for glossy promises. If VossCorp wants to 'work' with us, then we build the terms—public oversight, tenure guarantees, community co-management. Anything less is betrayal."

Your voice rides across salt-and-tin and reaches people who nod and shout back. Children chant your name. Old Mara's eyes, from the front of the march, are like flint and moss—worn and fierce. The bead you wear clinks against the megaphone strap.

The air thickens; you sense a tightening elsewhere—a shift from listening to responding.

A younger man in a pressed suit, possibly from PR or legal, steps forward and reads off a prepared statement into a handheld mic. His voice is smooth, meant to reassure.

VossCorp Spokesperson: "We are committed to equitable outcomes. We propose a third-party audit of all proposals and a community advisory panel. We will open our engineers to public oversight."

Jun's recorder records everything. You watch the crowd parse the language like an old fisherman reading tide—searching for undertow.

Maya spits the word "audit" back at the suit like a rejection; Herrera—one of the locals with a voice like rope—asks a question that lands with a pitch: "And who pays when you 'audit' and say we are unsafe? Where do we go? Will you keep us here?"

The suit's smile thins. He answers with the corporate script: "All implications will be considered. No forced relocations will be immediate. We seek a partnership."

There is applause on the Voss side—engineers trained to make optics. There is a ripple of frustration on yours. The crowd's hope bends outward, becoming a pressure that will either open or be deflated by the next move.

Jun steps to your side, voice urgent.

Jun: (low) "They offered an audit and a panel—soundable concessions. But I'm getting the clause—non-displacement seems vague. We need specific tenure language. Get them to say it on record. Demand names, dates."

You can feel the high arousal in your chest now: a drumroll of chaotic possibility that tastes of salt and galvanized steel. This is the kind of pressure that forges alliances or snaps them. For a beat you imagine Calder's pale gray gaze—assessing, cool—measuring the cost of letting you win the optics versus the consequence of losing their control.

You raise the megaphone again. This time the words are not only demands; they are anchors.

You: "Names. Dates. Publicly binding tenure guarantees. An independent audit with community-chosen auditors. No confidentiality clauses. Will you commit—here, now—on the public record?"

The Voss spokesperson's jaw tightens as he consults his earpiece. His team steps forward like a net closing. A lawyer speaks into someone else faintly; the cadence from the Voss side becomes precise, measured. His answer will be a hinge.
