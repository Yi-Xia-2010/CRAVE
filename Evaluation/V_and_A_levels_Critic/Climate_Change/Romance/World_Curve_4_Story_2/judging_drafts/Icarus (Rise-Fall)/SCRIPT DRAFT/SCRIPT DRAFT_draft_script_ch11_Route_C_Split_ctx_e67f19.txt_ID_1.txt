[Scene: Ferryworks Greenhouse | Night]
[Visual: Warm lamplight spills from portholes; the converted ferry rocks a slow, practiced rhythm against its moorings. Inside, rows of living walls shimmer—mangrove roots braided into bio-concrete, circuits tucked like veins into sculpture.]
[Music: Quick, buoyant strings; a rising electronic pulse weaving with the rhythm of water against hull]
[Sound: Low hum of active sensors, the soft chatter of neighbors, a child's laugh, a camera shutter clicking]

You step over a coil of salt-stiff rope and into the Ferryworks, your boots sounding hollow against the metal grate. The air is wet and sweet with soil and seaweed, warmed by the lamps Ori soldered into the rafters. Your field notebook is still in your jacket—Mara's tenure mark pressed faint as a promise—but for now your hands are empty and the world is nothing but a bright surge of presence.

Light catches on a mangrove root where it meets sculpted bio-concrete. Tiny LED eyes—a constellation of sensors you calibrated—blink green in a staggered, satisfying rhythm. The indicator panel atop the main planter ticks from amber to steady green, and a collective breath from the crowd feels almost like applause.

You feel the arousal that started as a quiet buzz in your chest from the breaker grow hot; tonight's test is not only a demonstration of mechanics but an offering: beauty as defense, craft as tenure. Around you faces tilt toward the walls. A woman in a threadbare cardigan presses her palm to wet bark and closes her eyes as if memorizing a door she has not yet lost.

Ori is there—hands stained with algae and solder—his face lit from the living wall he riffed into a lattice of reclaimed circuits and buoyant planters. He moves between people like a current, handing out seed packets with the kind of showmanship he normally uses to distract from a harder conversation.

Ori Navarro: (grinning) "Okay, who says we can't make seawalls look good enough to fall in love with?"

You laugh before you can stop yourself. He reads the laugh like a scale and weights his next words with iron tenderness.

Ori Navarro: (low, quick) "You did the numbers. I did the jokes. Together we did the miracle."

You let the praise slide across your skin, warm and dangerous in its simplicity. Romance has always felt odd in this fight—luxury in the face of loss—but tonight, as children press seeds into soft pockets and Old Mara murmurs the names of plants, it feels like part of the work: the small freedoms you fight to keep.

Silas brushes past, carrying a tray of tiny planters—each marked with a scrawled coordinate and a neighbor's name.

Silas Roe: (hushed, urgent) "Ava—look. Mrs. Tetsu wants to pin a propagated root to the east module. She says it'll help her porch hold when the spring surge comes."

You kneel, fingers already adjusting a clamp Ori taught you last week. The sensor blinks twice; humidity recalibrates. The planter takes the graft and the little green leaf perks up like it's claimed an oath.

You: "Do it. Make sure it's tight against the membrane—watch the seal."

Silas nods, quick and bright. Around you neighbors interchange tools and stories; someone swaps salt-repaired fishing hooks for a length of braided wire. The Ferryworks fills with a soft, thrumming intimacy: of hands that know one another's bruises, of a community translating an emergency into a choreographed, joyful labor.

The crowd thickens. Someone angles a phone toward the living wall; its screen catches the mosaic of seaglass tiles and plant pockets and the ornate flip of circuitry like a necklace. An influencer you half-know—one of the young people who runs the market stall near the boardwalk—snaps a photo and, in one breath, posts it to a feed you can already feel rippling.

Jun Park is at the entrance, raincoat collar turned up despite the greenhouse warmth. He moves like someone who has learned to arrive with bad news and make it sound like a plan. He finds you, his recorder tucked like a prop—only tonight he tucks it away when he sees the expression on your face.

Jun Park: (soft) "They're loving it. The imagery is perfect. People will want this."

You: "They're holding it. They think—" You catch yourself and choose steadiness. "They think they can touch it."

Jun: (a quick, dry laugh) "Good. Viral images make hearts and bureaucrats move. But listen—there's already a ripple. The post's reached an account that flags VossCorp's watchlist."

Your breath stutters; the rising excitement hits a salt-quick edge. The greenhouse, which felt like a safe room, narrows into a stage.

You: "How fast?"

Jun Park: (measured) "Fast. Their PR bots picked it up. An influencer amplifies it, and their team's automated scanners flagged locations. I'm calling a contact—someone in legal say they want an 'emergency assessment' clause ready if anything looks like an unsanctioned infrastructure."

Ori's jaw tenses; for a moment you see him as someone who once worked inside the engine that now turns toward you.

Ori Navarro: (quiet) "If they send enforcement, they'll seize equipment. They could call it a safety inspection and—" He breaks off, hands balling into the pockets of his overalls. "They have ways."

You can feel the room tilt; the very thing that made people's faces soften—the unmistakable beauty of Ori's wall—has a new property: it makes your small, local victory legible to the whole city and, therefore, to Calder Voss's architecture of control.

You: "We knew this was possible. Did we ever imagine 'possible' would look like a social post?"

Jun slides his phone across the bench, fingers tapping. He shows you a flurry of notifications: comments, shares, a push notification from a city channel where a comment reads: "Community-led prototype near Saltway—@ferryworks looks promising #livingseawall" and beneath it the corporate account reply is a bland, impeccably neutral smile: "Monitoring."

The hum of the sensors swells; the music in your chest becomes percussion. Neighbors pull you into small conversations—a woman who grew up on a levee now wants to plant a second root; a boy asks whether he can name the LED "Hope." Each ask is a tiny plea against the machinery pressing from beyond.

Ori leans in too close; the smell of solder and sea tangles with the heat of the work-lamp. His voice is a velvet rope around the tightness in your throat.

Ori Navarro: (lowered) "We could go public. We could release the schematics—open-source the design. Fast. Big. Nobody can privatize what's already public."

You: "And we risk VossCorp coming down with—"

Jun: (cuts in, professional and quick) "—legal muscle. But it also forces a political conversation faster than their PR can manufacture safety narratives."

You: "Or—"

Silas, overhearing, plants himself between you and the instruments like a small tidal dam.

Silas Roe: (breathless) "Or we keep it here. We fix it. We teach. We make sure the people who live here know how to keep their houses together."

Each option jerks at something in you—your instinct to spread what keeps people safe, your fear of putting neighbors in the path of a corporation's legal teeth, your private fear of what exposing Ori's past might do to him and to the fragile trust between you.

Jun pulls you aside, lower than the hum and the phone chatter. His voice is urgent but buoyant—the cadence of someone who believes leverage exists even in a crisis.

Jun Park: (urgent) "If you open-source, the narrative goes wide and fast. Calder's machine will try to drown it with legal noise, but you'll get public pressure and allies. If you coordinate a careful exposé with me, we can hold some cards—get documentary proof of VossCorp's prior compromises, link their legal patterns to public accountability. If you keep it local, you protect household autonomy but you lose the chance to force structural change tonight."

You: "All of those sound like victories and threats at once."

Jun: (a flash of grim humor) "That's politics. That's climate work. It's messy, often loud, and frequently necessary. You're not choosing a tool; you're choosing the shape of the fight and how many people you carry inside it."

You turn to Ori. His face is open and raw in the lamplight, a hundred small scars and inventions mapped across his hands. For a beat, his expression goes complex—something like apology folded into resolve.

Ori Navarro: (soft) "I left VossCorp because I couldn't watch people become line items. But I also know their playbook. If this goes out, they won't only come for the plans. They'll come with the ledger of my mistakes—old approvals, projects where I turned a blind eye. I don't want to be the risk you pay. But I also don't want to stand in the way."

You: "You're not the risk. This work is bigger than either of us."

Ori Navarro: (bitter laugh) "Says the organizer who carries houses on her back like they're postcards."

Your chest knifes with something fierce and bright—pride, fear, love—each competing to be the loudest. The crowd's energy tightens into a single, expectant wire. Somewhere on the other side of a screen, VossCorp's scanners are already parsing pixels; here, in sediment and hands and light, people are deciding how much exposure they can take.

Jun's phone vibrates; he answers and listens. Four lines into the call his face drains a fraction. He ends it and meets your eyes.

Jun Park: (flat) "They're preparing legal definitions. 'Emergency assessment'—that's the phrase. If they file, they'll ask for access; they'll justify it with 'public safety' and 'unvetted structural components'. Once they move in, they can remove prototypes and archive community rights."

The moment hits like a wave: the very visibility that turned hearts is the lever that calls for corporate intervention.

You: "So—"

Jun Park: (firm) "So you decide now. Fast."

The music swells—strings and electronic pulse braided tight—mirroring the quicksilver night. Neighbors look to you, Ori's chest rises and falls as if matching your breath, Silas fidgets with a clip that might as well be a compass. The Ferryworks itself seems to hold its breath, waiting for the small, world-making motion you'll make next.

Everything in you wants to fling the design across the rooftops, to make the seawall a public tool and force the city to reckon. Everything in you wants to keep the knowledge here, close, teach it slow, watch it root deeper before risking a harvest. Everything in you wants to trust Jun to stitch together a narrative that will make Calder's machine exposed rather than triumphant.

The arousal that has been building—excitement, stakes, the intimate heat of a community's hope—edges into a white-hot clarity.
