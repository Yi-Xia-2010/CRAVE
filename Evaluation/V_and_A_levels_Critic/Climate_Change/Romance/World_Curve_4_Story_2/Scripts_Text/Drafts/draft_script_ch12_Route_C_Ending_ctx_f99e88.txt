### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Ending


[Scene: Ferryworks | Morning — two weeks after the rollout]
[Visual: Warm lamplight through portholes; the Ferryworks packed shoulder-to-shoulder with foldout tables, salvaged solar sails, and a sea of hands planting cuttings into modular planters]
[Music: Fast-paced, hopeful strings underscored by a driving percussive pulse]
[Sound: The murmur of a crowded room, scissors snipping, the soft clack of a projector, an occasional cheer]

You move through the press of bodies like someone tracing the map of consequences. Faces tilt up to you — neighbors, volunteers from other districts, a few stony-faced city planners who have come to see what the tide of people can't ignore. A small child presses a mud-slicked cutting into your palm and looks at you as if you were a talisman; you try to smile and your jaw feels raw.

Jun's piece has already begun to land. Clips from last night's interview loop on a rented screen: your hands sketching tide brackets, Ori soldering a sensor, Old Mara explaining tenure as if it were a recipe. The overlay reads: "Living Seawalls: Community Designs Meet City Risk." Beneath it, legal footnotes crawl in thin type—Jun's work, meticulous and hungry, that made the private public.

You taste the salt of the air and something metallic — the adrenaline that comes with being watched. It is exhilarating and fragile all at once.

Ori finds you at the back of the Ferryworks, grease on his knuckles, eyes bright with the same tired light that makes you want to hold him and push him away at once.

Ori Navarro: "Look at this, Ava. Workshops in three markets, a dozen new modules built, a teen in East Docks reverse-engineered our bouyancy trap and made it sing."

You let a laugh out that has no real relief in it.

You: "They built it from canned lids and a broken chest freezer."

Ori: (grinning) "Bravo, Harborlight."

You: "It's moving fast."

Ori's smile softens. He reaches, fingers brushing the tide-chart bracelet at your wrist — a small ritual you both have kept since the first prototype.

Ori Navarro: "Fast is good. Fast means we can keep a street, keep a life."

You study his mouth. There's a tremor there you have learned to read: the part of him that equates speed with escape, that would rather race than root. You want to say: slow is safer. You want to say: we can't outrun scale. Instead you squeeze his hand and find the word that steadies you.

You: "Then we'll make speed careful."

Jun corners you later, recorder in hand, fatigue in every inflection.

Jun: "The piece is clean. The law citations hold. I got past VossCorp's PR team to an inside memo—there's a release schedule on upstream gates that correlates with their testing windows. If we can show causation, we can challenge them."

You: "Causation? Jun, 'correlates' and 'causation' are different currencies."

Jun: (wry) "When the tide is in motion, people accept approximations. But yes — I need more. If we publish the upstream data alongside Voss's timeline, we can make a strong narrative."

You fold your hands around that possibility. It's not certainty. It is a lever.

[Scene: Glass Pier | Afternoon — Calder Voss's public address]
[Visual: Glass-paneled scaffolding, corporate banners snapping in a cold wind; Calder stands beneath a ring of camera lenses, palms outward in practiced openness]
[Music: A formal, hollow fanfare that the city plays when billion-dollar promises are made]
[Sound: Microphones clicking, the murmur of reporters, a thin chewing wind]

Calder Voss: "Harborlight needs scale. We cannot rely on patchwork remedies in the face of a changing ocean."

His voice is polished, the edges sanded. He speaks of models and risk matrices and the moral obligation to protect "our citizens" with "stable infrastructure." When he says "our citizens," you see the glimmer of leveed districts in his mind — tidy, controlled, behind glass. He mentions collaboration and co-design as an offer; his smile arranges itself so it can be read as sincere from any camera angle.

You watch the feed from across the water, Ori at your elbow. The Ferrryworks' projector throws Calder's image large and cold against a tarpaulin.

Ori Navarro: "He plays guardian like it's a new coat."

You: "He's selling certainty."

Ori's hand finds yours, tight, a pressure that says we know each other's faults and still choose the same shore. But under that pressure there's a question neither of you voice: will his certainty swallow the messy courage you built into the mud?

[Music: Swells; tension rises like a tightening line]
[Sound: A far-off siren, the first unsteadiness of the day]

Then the weather changes.

[Scene: Harborlight — Early Evening, sky turning the color of bruise]
[Visual: Low clouds scud across the skyline; the distant cranes are gone, swallowed by a wall of rain]
[Music: Percussive stabs and rising strings; tempo accelerates rapidly]
[Sound: A knifing wind, breaking glass from distant construction, urgent city alerts pinging through phones]

Jun's phone buzzes: an emergency alert; municipal channels flicker with a warning. Upstream reservoirs have initiated rapid releases—an emergency discharge to protect a failing dam upstream. The timing is uncanny and lethal. News anchors begin to talk over one another; Calder's earlier speech is edited into a loop: "Only engineered certainty..."

You feel the press of bodies around you pull inward, all the small human boats clinging to the Ferryworks like barnacles. The Ferryworks' hull shudders as a gust slams the mooring ropes. Volunteers shout orders. Ori moves like someone who has always found his gravity in chaos: pulling down a sail, strapping a loose module, barking quick, alive instructions to Silas and to anyone who will listen.

Ori Navarro: "Ava—check the flex anchors on Row Seven. If the shear hits the joints there, we'll lose the whole terrace."

You run, ankle-deep water beginning to slosh at the edges of the deck. The sensor lights you helped program strobe in warning patterns. The living wall breathes hard, leaves slapped flat by wind, soil sodden.

You: (breathing) "Hold. Hold. Hold."

The first crushing wave arrives not as a cinematic wall but as a low, conveyor-belt roar—water pushed faster than the city expected, bearing the upstream surge and fed by the squall's wind. You feel it hit with the sensation of an animal's weight, the Ferryworks shuddering on its chains.

[Visual: Slow motion as a corner seam shears; a module with a line of potted seedlings is torn free]
[Sound: A metallic crunch, a scream cut off, the thump of falling wood]

For a heartbeat you believe you can stop it. Then the shear point gives: a joint you had doubted, a splice you meant to reinforce next week, the place where homemade meets the ocean's appetite. The module swings, then rips clean. Water finds the right angles to multiply damage; scouring eats at pilings in the shadow of VossArc's engineered groins.

Across the bay, an image goes viral in minutes: one of your prototypes standing firm between two demolished districts, posted with a caption that reads, "Community solutions work." The next minute, a video clip circulates showing a sheared seam, a floating mass of debris colliding with a VossArc floodgate and, in the edit, a timed upstream release juxtaposed with Calder's scheduled test. Context collapses into narrative overnight.

[Music: Full orchestra—chaos; brass blares]
[Sound: Sirens, people shouting, the omnipresent crash of the storm]

Calder's PR machine is quick as a razor. Within hours, VossCorp releases footage that frames the episode as proof: ad hoc, privately-operated prototypes are unpredictable hazards that create secondary failure modes. They file emergency motions. They talk of "mandated standards" and a "citywide consolidation of coastal defenses" that will be "more efficient and safer."

You read the filings through the rain: legal language, slight and lethal. Jun's exposé that once seemed to weaponize facts is now a piece of evidence in a courtroom of public opinion—used by both sides. The city council convenes under pressure; they face cameras and polls that measure fear like currency. A few neighborhoods sprint toward the levees, signing relocation agreements in fevered meetings; others double down, bolstering with your blueprints and jury-rigged permits.

You go to Saltway where an evacuation notice flutters like a white flag. Old Mara sits on her stoop, shawl sodden, watching a family pack what they can into a scow. Her face is a map of tides you had not expected to read as grief.

Mara 'Old Mara' Tetu: (softly) "We were promised timing and time fails us."

You try to lie with facts, to say you anticipated what you could, to promise that not all will be lost. Your voice cracks. You can feel the ledger of good intentions balancing against the ledger of broken bolts and brittle bargains.

[Music: A low, relentless drum, like a heart under strain]

Then enforcement arrives. The city cites a regulation snuck into an emergency ordinance that allows them to classify privately installed floating structures as unregulated hazards. VossCorp's legal brief is polite and pointed; the city's enforcement teams are efficient. Bulldozers come with polite apologies. In some neighborhoods, crews move in with court orders to remove "non-compliant" modules. In others, neighbors push back, forming human chains that will not break for days.

You stand at the edge of one removal, hands full of a child's damp sweater, and watch as a woman you helped train in buoyancy techniques is made to watch her own wall be dismantled. She turns to you and says nothing; her mouth is a flat line, the look between you not simple anger but something more complex and inscrutable—like betrayal mixed with gratitude, like a ledger of hopes being reconciled with survival.

You feel hollow and furious. The heat of injustice burns through you; your hands shake with the need to do more or to stop everything. Words rise like tides: blame, plea, accusation. Ori steadies you with a hand at your elbow, anchoring a storm whose politics your hands cannot fix.

Ori Navarro: (voice low) "They'll use every misstep as a cudgel. We knew that risk."

You: "We built with care. We told them our limits."

Ori: "They wanted limits. They wanted you to be small."

You look at him — at the salt on his coat, the hollow in his amber eyes that the camera cannot steal but the water can. You feel the shape of a choice that is not given to you now: to keep fighting in a thousand messy, human ways, or to fold into a different kind of scale so the law will accept your methods.

There's no neat victory. In pockets, your designs hold—mangrove-breathing terraces that soften waves and keep a handful of streets dry. Children fish from new terraces. People sing as they patch leaks. But elsewhere whole blocks are moved inland behind levees, their faces erased from a map they once called home. The public narrative fractures like old glass.

You remember Calder's ring, the titanium locket with a photo of a flooded home inside. You imagine the private grief that drives him and the public moves that protect his company. Anger warps thin into understanding and then into a lane of its own: that grief beget action, and action can whitewash grief into policy.

[Scene: Glass Pier | Night — after the storm]
[Visual: A battered pier slick with rain; floodlights throw long, accusing shadows; the silhouette of VossArc glows like an operating theater across the bay]
[Music: Sparse piano, a distant choir; a slow, mournful cello line]
[Sound: Hard rain, the constant plinking of water on metal; the city settling and not sleeping]

You find Ori standing on the wet pier, coat streaked with salt and mud, eyes like drained amber bulbs. He stares out across the water where parts of the city are lit and parts are gone. He's been out here for hours, hands in his pockets, body a little too still for someone who fixes things by moving.

You: (soft) "Ori."

Ori does not turn at once. When he does, his mouth tries for a joke and fails, folding into something honest and ragged.

Ori Navarro: "We saved some of Saltway."

You: "Yes."

Ori: "And they took the rest behind a levee and called it progress."

He laughs, a small, broken sound.

Ori Navarro: "I thought— God, I thought building beautiful things would make the world kinder. I can make a garden float. I can make algae sing in a sensor. I am useless against a ledger."

You step closer. Your clothes are soaked through; the chill lives in your bones. You want to tell him that beauty is not useless. You want to tell him that every planter that holds soil and a child's foot will be a seed of the future. But your voice is small and the last hours have taught you that small truths do not always outdo legal motions.

You: "We did what we could. We were honest."

Ori Navarro: (angry, then guttural) "Honest doesn't stop bulldozers."

You: "No. But it made people look. It forced the conversation."

Ori: (bitter) "It also gave them maps for what to cut."

He turns away, the water reflecting the cold LEDs like a swarm of tiny accusations. For a moment you both stand in the rain and let the city grieve aloud. Then Ori steps toward you, close enough that his breath fogs your cheek.

Ori Navarro: (whisper) "I wanted— I wanted to run with you, Ava. I wanted to prove we could do something different together. I hate that part of me that keeps thinking the next build will fix the feeling."

You: "You don't have to run. You can stay. You can build here."

Ori's eyes flash; something like hope, or fear of it.

Ori Navarro: "And watch them use what we build to move my friends? To move your neighborhood? To call our work 'unregulated hazards' and then paint over the map with concrete? How do I—how do we—"

You: "We keep building. We keep filing complaints. We keep teaching people to anchor better, to document, to demand accountability. It's not a victory march, Ori. It's slow, and it's exhausted, and sometimes it breaks in ways we didn't foresee. But it's still ours."

He laughs again, and this time the sound has salt and sorrow braided through it.

Ori Navarro: "That's the thing— 'ours' gets messy."

You step forward and press your forehead to his, a lean that is more of a pact than a promise.

You: "Then we keep our mess. We keep the people we can. We don't let them tell us our care is a hazard."

He closes his eyes. The rain sluices down around you both, a hard, unblinking curtain. There is no triumphant music, no tidy ledger. There is only the stubborn, exhausted warmth of two bodies in the dark, and the knowledge that the night will take more than you can give.

[Music: Drops to a single, sustained cello note that trembles and then holds]
[Visual: Camera pulls back to show the pier's edge, the scattered lights of neighborhoods, the cold silhouette of VossArc; the rain blurs everything into watercolor]
[Sound: The rain asserts itself; distant, muffled voices continue the work of arguing and rebuilding]

You think of Old Mara's hands, of the child's mud on your palm, of the module that ripped free. You think of Calder's locket and his impossible grief. You catalog losses like tide marks on your ribs.

There are towns saved and towns moved. There are families in new, raised houses that are sterile and safe, and families who now live in the liminal places you always feared would be priced out or pushed back. Some of your designs are adopted by municipal pilots, their blueprints annotated with clauses that reframe community labor as "pilot data." Others are dismantled under the ordinance's teeth.

The city will call it a compromise. You call it a wound that has been stitched with uncertain thread.

You and Ori stand in the doorway between what you saved and what you couldn't. You hold each other's hands as nightly enforcement patrols pass by, as Jun texts updates and the legal battles stretch like an incoming tide. The ache is a steady presence, like a bruise you do not know how to explain without sounding like you are asking for pity.

You lift your face to the rain. It washes salt and grit from your cheeks. It feels like absolution and accusation at once.

You fold the night into the shape of a promise that is not naive: you will keep cataloging, keep teaching, keep building. You will hold what you can and grieve what you cannot. Love will not erase the logics that make cities choose levees over living neighborhoods, but it will hold you through the long, slow work of being human in a world that has begun to tilt.

[Visual: The Ferryworks' lights flicker on in the distance — small beacons of continuing effort]
[Music: A single, low chord slowly fades]
[Sound: Rain continues; the city breathes]

You and Ori step off the pier together, soaked and honest, a small and stubborn solidarity walking back into the mapped wounds of Harborlight.

[ENDING]
