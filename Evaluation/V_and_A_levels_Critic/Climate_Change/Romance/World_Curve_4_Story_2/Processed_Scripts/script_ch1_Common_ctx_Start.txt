# Route: Additional Route — Ending
# Context: Start

[Scene: Saltway Boardwalks | Dawn]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a slow, steady drip from tarpaulins; boardwalk planks protesting underfoot]
[Music: Quiet, minimal piano — a slow, patient cadence]

You come back on the tail of last night's tide-watch, soaked to the cuffs and careful with motion. Your boots slap the sodden boardwalk with the familiar, thumbprint rhythm of home — rubber soles, tire-tread giving just enough grip to remind you of the miles you put on these paths. Salt has crystallized along your jawline; rain has left pale streaks through your dusky auburn bob. The solar pin on your lapel is warm from the panels you propped against a lamp post to charge; your fingers carry that warmth like a talisman.

You carry the field notebook the way you always have: close to your ribs, edges softened with tide-chart smudges. Its pages smell faintly of salt and solder and whatever took you through the long night. You remember why you left the covers of this book open during storms — numbers to anchor you when the sky feels like it could slide off the world.

Light leaks over the quarter in thin ribbons. The Tide Garden is a suggestion of green beyond the first row of pontoons; the living wall's platforms bob with the polite, patient movement of things that have learned to live in motion. Around you, the neighborhood is waking in halting gestures: a child's laugh clipped by a yawn, a kettle clicked into life, the scrape of a bucket against a porch rung. The air tastes of brine, wood smoke, and the faint metallic echo of last week's repairs.

You catalogue, as if inventory will make everything right. Repaired pontoons — three patched with old sailcloth and adhesive, one with jury-rigged outriggers that held Silas and the kids through a night squall. A solar kite, rigged on a child's string, catches the low wind and throws dappled light across a puddle. Old Mara's shawl hangs heavy with dew in that same reflection: layered wool, salt-stained, heavy with the smell of the sea and of bayberry candles. Maya's braid is a bright slash of gray against the softer tones, and Silas' hoodie is streaked with new paint, the sort that won't leave your hands for days.

You don't linger in nostalgia. Naming faces is work: Maya—organizer, rumor-stopper, the one who yells the loudest and forgives the fastest. Silas—quick-handed, a rumor in motion. Old Mara—memory made of hooks and salt. Ori's patchwork coat anchors a cluster of floating planters like a small, warm island. He is there, as he often is at dawn: hands tucked into pockets that smell of oil and algae, watching the horizon where the cranes sit—black teeth against the pale sky, VossArc's scaffolding a distant, precise silhouette.

[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: Low industrial hum far off, like a held breath]

You breathe through the list and bury the heavier things under the logistics: check sensors, sketch root depths, note the salinity drift. The notebook opens to a page already marked with yesterday's readings; you lay the instrument on the boardwalk, fingers still catching the warmth of the solar pin. The sensor chirps softly — the sort of polite beep that seems grateful to be doing its job.

Ava Maren: "Salinity's held at last night's high, three tenths above mean. Root depth sitting a centimeter shy of the target you and I like."

Maya Lin: "That's...steady, then. Not good, but steady is something we can plan around." (She slides a thermos into your hands. The lid leaves a ring of steam on the notebook cover.) "You look wrecked. Did you sleep at all?"

Ava Maren: "A few hours. Tide kept me honest." (You flip the page and sketch a quick cross-section of the living wall, labeling the mangrove roots and the deeper anchors.) "Sensors were okay. The new node I asked Silas to patch is holding charge."

Maya Lin: "Good. Old Mara's been talking about retying the east moorline. She says the old ropes want a respectful ceremony before you cut into them." (She grins, a small, tired thing.) "She also insists you bring her a biscuit next time."

Old Mara: (From her stoop, hand drumming the bead of a fishing hook) "You bring me that biscuit or you bring me the plan, child. Both, if you're clever." (Her voice carries the rasp of someone who has spoken through more storms than the ledger of your life can count.) "No point in charts if hands don't know the knots."

You hand her the notebook like an offering. Your ink smudges under stress; your letters grow more angular when you sleep badly. Mara's eyes track the tide lines and the sketches, and for a moment her face is unreadable in the way that keeps memory safe from sentiment.

Old Mara: "Hmn." (A short sound, part-sigh, part-approval.) "Root depth's not a number; it's a living thing. Treat it so. Teach them that, don't just bolt metal to wood."

Ava Maren: "I will. I'm writing it down." (You feel, quietly, the small lift of being heard. The list in your head blots out the weight of the other things—of storefronts lost, of the letter from the precinct about VossCorp's public meeting.)

Silas shoves a fistful of cloth into your hands — a wretch of blue tarp with a fresh weld. "I fixed this with the new seam. Ori said it'd hold for the next... whatever." He looks at you, eyes bright. "You think the mayor'll come? The ones in suits? I saw a flash of LED on Glass Pier last night. Big lights."

Ava Maren: "They'll come with cameras and pinwheels and phrases about 'managed retreat,' and then they'll move on unless we make them stay." (You trace a line on the notebook, a tiny map of boardwalks and living-wall sections. Your handwriting is careful, the way someone nails a window shut during a soft rain.) "We make them stay with bodies and plans."

Ori Navarro's laugh is an unexpected thing, a looseness that folds the air like a warm hand. He steps closer, haloed by scaffolded planters and the smell of soldering flux. Up close, his amber eyes are just as you remember: mischievous, quick to forgive. He brushes a salt-streak from your hair in a motion that says both familiarity and an apology for something unnamed.

Ori Navarro: "You should have called me last night. I had an extra battery and a bag of hot tea." (He nudges one of the floating planters with the toe of his boot; a small green shoots up like a stubborn thing.) "We've been thinking about bolstering the east moorline with stacked planters—bio-silk and kelp lattices. Fancy engineering words, poor people's charm. Your name's on the prototype, by the way."

Ava Maren: (You feel the corners of your mouth lift without permission.) "I can't accept a prototype as a thanks-for-lights, Ori. Besides, I promised Silas I'd teach him to stitch bio-silk. We need everyone to know how to mend."

Ori Navarro: "Then let me teach them wrong and have them correct me." (He grins.) "It's the only way I get better."

The conversation feels small—bounded and warm—and it steadies you. The tide's patience hums beneath every word. You catalogue what they say because it grounds you to fact and to the people who will make the facts be enough. But you feel the horizon tug at the edge of the morning: VossArc's cranes are closer in the mind than they are in distance, a promise of something precise and large and, possibly, terrible. You can count its consequences as easily as you can count pontoons, but it doesn't make them less heavy.

You tap the sensor again. The screen gives you a slow, neutral readout: salinity drift, root depth marginally shallow, battery node at seventy-two percent. You make a dot on the page, then another. The motions are methodical, like bracing a house frame. The work is a partial exorcism; each number is a small altar against the shape of fear.

Your internal monologue narrows into practicalities. If the east moorline yields another inch, the market pontoons will sit oddly and the bakery will lose its lower shelf. If VossArc pushes a presentation next week, the city might be tempted by the clean lines of a corporate solution. If that happens, they will offer relocations wrapped in glossy flyers and enough capital to make some people listen. You think of the kids who fish under the Tide Garden, of Old Mara's shawl, of the way Maya speaks when she is afraid — louder and faster and with a cadence that makes you trust the plan catching in her voice.

You let survivor's guilt sit at the edge of the list like a dull stone. You acknowledge it in an essay of thought rather than in confession: you were away when the second flood took your family's storefront. The memory surfaces as a single, exact image — a window in shards, a sign flapping — and then you put it back in the drawer and write: "priority: secure tenure; map vulnerable holdings."

You choose with a half-breath — not out of indecision, but because planning has become your way of loving this place. The morning carries the small ceremoniality of people who repair because repair keeps stories alive. The conversations swell gently—Maya and Old Mara arguing about rope treatments, Silas practicing new welds in a tone that's half-boast, Ori sketching a new planter that looks like a crescent moon. You add their voices to the notebook with shorthand and arrows.

A film grain of memory edges what you write: the ink blurs the same way the edges of old photographs do. The light warms as the sun sorts itself out of the low clouds. The living wall breathes in a rhythm you can almost set your own to: inhale, tide; exhale, boardwalk. You write the day's tasks in a neat column, and beneath them, in smaller letters, a single line for yourself: "Remember: people first."

For a moment, there is only this — the smell of wet wood, the sound of tools, the exact shape of the plan you are making with other hands. It is not triumphant. It is not brittle. It is a small, steady insistence: to keep what remains whole and to do so by the slow rightness of work.

The cranes on the horizon are still there, patient as a storm. They belong to a different kind of certainty. For now, they are only silhouettes; their meaning will make itself clear before long. You finish the page with a quick sketch of the shore and a dot where your next meeting will be: the community hall after dusk, lanterns and maps and people with hands that smell of coffee and salt.

You close the notebook for the morning, the cover settling with a familiar sigh. The day will ask for plans. For now, there is an easy, very low hum of readiness among the people around you — not panic, not triumph, only the way of a neighborhood that knows how to wake.
