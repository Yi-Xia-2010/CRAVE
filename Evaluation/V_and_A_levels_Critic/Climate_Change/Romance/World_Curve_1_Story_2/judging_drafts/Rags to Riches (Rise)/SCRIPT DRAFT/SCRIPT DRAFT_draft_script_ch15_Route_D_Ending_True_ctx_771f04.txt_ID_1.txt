You taste salt and coffee; both are sharp and ordinary. The room is a contained storm—heat from bodies, the high sheen of cameras, the low hum of generators outside. Dr. Noor's chart is on the lectern, a knot of ink and benchmarks you've spent sleepless nights translating into something the public can trust. The decision you coaxed into being—binding authority with phased pilot benchmarks and community oversight—hangs between everyone like newly laced rope.

Your palms are warm against the pages of the draft. Legal clauses, rollback protections for Flooded Row, escrow language tying funding releases to transparent audits: words you chose to hold people, not just numbers.

Dr. Noor: "If we phrase the trigger points here—pilot survival at year one, community audit every six months, and an independent ombudsperson—this isn't just a promise. It's enforceable caregiving."

You: "It has to be measurable. If we make metrics vague, money moves and lives don't. If we make them impossible, no one signs. We need thresholds people can see with their own eyes."

Sora (off-stage initially, then stepping into the light, the bandana knot catching a stray beam): "And we need people in those rooms. Not just faces for cameras—actual seats, rotating, with real oversight and recall powers."

A dozen hands in the crowd clap; it's not the falling kind—it's the staccato, urgent clapping of people who've spent months climbing the same incline.

Lina stands by the podium, sleeves rolled, an expression that has learned to hold softness like a tool. When she speaks, her voice is low and steady, the tone of someone who has spent decades bargaining with systems.

Lina: "Binding authority sounds heavy. But we framed it as conditional and phased. It grows only when the pilots show success and the community signs off. Public funds move with public accountability. That is governance, not gift."

You watch her mouth the last line as if to remind herself. Her hand brushes the tablet at her hip; she doesn't reach for it. The gesture is small. Your chest, which had been tight, loosens a fraction.

[Sound: A sudden cheer from the back; someone starts a chant that folds into the room—"People decide! People decide!"]

Maya slips into the space beside you, cheeks smeared with mud from the wetland brigades, hair tucked under her fisher's hat. Her grin is wide and immediate, the kind that cracks a weathered face open to something younger. She taps the stack of pledge sheets.

Maya: "We got enough signatures this week to prove the pilots had public buy-in. We even tracked the donors—no opaque shells. People stayed up to translate the legal language into plain words. That helped."

You: "You always do the translation work no one sees."

Maya: (shrugs) "Someone's got to plant the reeds and read the fine print."

Community Member after community member steps forward. The pledges are for oversight, transparency, pilots, rollback protections. The list grows; it becomes a living thing with the heft of real signatures and the texture of a town's labor.

[Scene: Tidewatch Institute — Rooftop Greenhouse | Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm amber glow inside the greenhouse; plants arranged in test arrays; laptops open with graphs projected on walls]
[Music: Optimistic synth pads; layered with the steady pulse of conversation]

Dr. Noor and a cluster of young researchers walk you through the pilot results: side-by-side photos showing marsh growth, sensor readouts tracking erosion rates, community surveys that quantify perceived safety and cultural preservation. The numbers are not miraculous; they're real—gradual but decisive.

Dr. Noor: "At six months, Pilot A has reduced nearshore erosion by nineteen percent versus the control. Biodiversity indices show a significant uptick. Social surveys report lower displacement anxiety in households adjacent to the pilot zones."

You: "Nineteen percent is more than a statistic. It's a handful of houses that stayed put. It's a kitchen window that didn't get swallowed by the sea."

Lina: (examining a data readout) "We will present these to the regional board with a strict funding-release schedule tied to these metrics. No ambiguity."

Sora: "Make sure the presentation includes the lived stories. Data alone won't carry the emotional truth to a boardroom."

You: "We do both. Numbers and names. Policy and memory."

The greenhouse feels like a small sanctum of possibility; growth under glass. Your watch beeps with a barometric tick—an old habit. You look up at Lina; the amber light catches her profile. There is an ease now that didn't exist before: shared fatigue, shared resolve.

Lina: (quietly, not for the microphones) "We argued hard. I learned how to push without flattening the town."

You: "And I learned how to hold skepticism without becoming stalled."

Lina: "We're both better for that."

You want to say more—a confession of trust, an acknowledgement of debt—but the room is so full of other needs. Instead, you take a breath and let the work speak. Trust becomes procedural; it shows up as public audits, rotating seats, and benchmarks.

Maya leads a group teaching knotwork to volunteers. She demonstrates, and the circle winds tighter as fingers, new and practiced, copy her. A child tugs at your sleeve and asks if you can tie a bow. You do; the child's delighted squeal is a small bell in the thick evening air.

Sora: (yelling over the noise) "Remember when we thought we needed to light the town on fire to get attention? This is louder."
