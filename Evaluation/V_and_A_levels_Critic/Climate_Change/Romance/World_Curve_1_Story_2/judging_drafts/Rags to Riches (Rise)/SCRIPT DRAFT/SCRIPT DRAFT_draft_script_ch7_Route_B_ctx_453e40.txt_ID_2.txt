[Scene: Tidewatch Institute Lab | Afternoon]
[Visual: Fluorescent panels hum over rows of pinned maps and open laptops. Rain streaks the lab windows; water beaded on the glass distorts the far horizon into a smear of gray.]
[Sound: Soft equipment beeps; an exhaust fan sighs. Distant, muffled traffic on a wet boardwalk.]

You came to the lab with the decision already made—Option B folded into your chest the way a keepsake folds a photograph. The municipal pilot is no abstract now; it sits in file folders, in funding spreadsheets, and in the quiet rows of instruments that Dr. Noor has kept humming since you were a child watching tide gauges with them.

Dr. Noor: "The models run cleaner with the municipal parameters. The levee automation needs the signal latency cut by half and—" (they tap a line of code) "—we'll need an extra sensor array for the south inlet. That's non-negotiable for the failure-threshold."

You: "If we add the array, what's the delay on maintenance funding? Automated systems without guaranteed servicing become liabilities more than protections."

Dr. Noor: (exhales) "Exactly. Which is why your oversight clause matters. The municipality will set maintenance bonds, yes—if Lina can keep the council's eyes on it. Ephraim's contribution buys speed, but not stability."

Ephraim: (leaning against a counter, suit damp at the cuffs) "Speed is what saves lives when the next surge comes. We can roll panels in the next quarter. Delays—bureaucratic delays—cost people."

The lab smells of coffee, the tang of wet coats, and the faint electric ozone of the instrumentation. Your fingers find the little leather-bound Moleskine at the edge of the desk; your seagull pin rests against your jacket, cool against the salt-ring on your skin. You trace the map margins with a fingertip, and for a second numbers and council jargon recede into lines and shorelines you remember from childhood.

Sora arrives like a gust—wet bandana darkened with rain, voice raw from the boardwalk's chanting. They stand at the doorway and the room holds its breath.

Sora Watanabe: "You took Lina up on this—without telling us. Without the People's Emergency Plan in place. How is that not selling us out?"

You: (quiet) "I told you I would weigh lives first. The pilot gets municipal capital. That means evacuation corridors, desal nodes—real infrastructure that can reduce casualties."

Sora Watanabe: (the accusation sharpened by years of disappointment) "Infrastructure that centralizes control and paper-over community autonomy. Lina's contracts move people into vertical housing. They sell relocation as safety."

Maya: (voice suddenly small, a thread of salt in it) "Ari—"

You: "Maya, I know what we lose when streets empty. I know what it means to say goodbye to a place. I'm not naive about the cultural cost. I'm trying to buy time—time to build the kind of transition that keeps people whole."

Dr. Noor studies you, their expression steady and small-leveraged—wary, relieved, complex.

Dr. Noor: "Your oversight language is robust. If you hold to it, the pilot could actually mandate community-managed maintenance teams. It's not perfect, but it changes the political equation."

Ephraim: (smiles a slow, practiced thing) "And with private partners in the loop, we can deliver at scale. The optics are clean. The investors are reassured."

The room tilts with the logic of different truths: Lina's pragmatic calculus, Sora's insistence on agency, Ephraim's promise of speed. Your chest tightens with that old fault-line—compromise or betrayal—and the hum of the fluorescents becomes the metronome of the decision you've already set in motion.

[Scene: Tidewatch Rooftop Greenhouse | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The greenhouse is a warm amber bubble against the gray sky. Mist clings to the glass. Rows of potted sedges and young marsh grass reflect light like tiny, stubborn beacons.]
[Sound: The drip of condensation; the faint rustle of leaves in the forced ventilation. Cameras somewhere outside shoulder in for a photo-op.]

The municipal paperwork is a stack in a manila folder, the kind of bureaucratic thing that reads like both promise and chain. Lina stands with her trench coat buttoned to the throat, platinum bob immaculate despite the drizzle. Her amber silk collar is tucked in just so; she is a study in measured presence. When she looks at you, the expression is unreadable for a beat—then, off-camera, it softens in a way you almost miss: a quick lift at the corner of her mouth that could be mistaken for anything if you weren't paying attention.

Lina Moreau: (low) "Your tide maps are the cleanest I've seen, Ari. They make the risk legible. That's…useful."

You: (a small, surprised laugh) "Thank you. I—"

Sora Watanabe: (cutting in) "Legible for whom, Lina? For those with the paper and the power to decide where people end up?"

Lina Moreau: (measured, not unkind) "For those who need to choose where to allocate finite resources. Risk doesn't wait for debate."

The greenhouse smells of wet earth and the tang of metal from the clipboards. Your hand closes on the pen—an ordinary black felt-tip that suddenly feels like a judgement. Maya stands close enough to touch; her eyes are wet and defiant at once.

Maya: "If anyone can make sure neighborhoods get relocation funds, it's you, Ari. Don't let them erase us."

You: "I'm not erasing anyone, Maya. The pilot has stipulations for community councils. I wrote in maintenance bonds and—"

Sora Watanabe: (voice breaking on the last word) "You wrote in oversight because you trusted Lina. When did you start trusting them more than the people who raised you?"

You: (the reply comes steadier than you feel) "I haven't stopped trusting the people. I chose a path that gets resources—now—so people don't drown waiting for ideal solutions."

Ephraim: (smoothly) "People don't wait. They need structures that hold. Politically, Ari, you made the right call."

Lina watches you the way a weathered technician watches instruments: not with warmth, but with a precise appreciation. For a breath, you see something that isn't rhetoric—an actual, private acknowledgement.

Lina Moreau: (quiet, almost aside) "Your elevation contours—particularly the point on Old Harbor—were what convinced the council. They respected that detail. Your maps made the danger impossible to look away from."

The compliment lands like a small stone in still water; it ripples something inside you that is not purely professional. Your seagull pin presses cold against your chest as you sign the municipal documents. The pen leaves a black arc, a tiny, final claim. Paper meets paper. Ink dries like a small, irrevocable weather.

The photographers shift; someone readies a microphone. The ritual of municipal theater folds around the practical clauses you've insisted on. You watch the ink settle and feel the pilot's architecture materialize in the way a levee rises along a shoreline—slow, engineered, inevitable.

Sora steps closer, their hand almost touching your sleeve and then not. There are a thousand things left unsaid: the People's Emergency Plan half-built in Sora's satchel, the volunteers at Maya's co-op waiting for plant shipments, the families who will be relocated under contracts you now have a hand in authorizing.

Sora Watanabe: (soft, constricted) "So this is what you think saves people."

You: "I think it buys us the time to make better choices. I think with oversight, we can steer the politics so the people's voice isn't a footnote."

Lina Moreau: (tilting her head) "Then hold them to it, Ari. If you can make the pilot enforce community stewardship, you'll have done more than I ever expected from a municipal rollout."

There is a quiet pivot in her voice; not trust, not warmth exactly, but the kind of acknowledgment that can be the start of a different kind of bargaining. You taste the tradeoffs on your tongue—safety braided with loss, autonomy threaded with structure. The greenhouse's amber light makes the edges of each face soft; your seagull pin feels heavy in the moment, a small, stubborn relic of home.

You step back from the table, the paperwork signed. Cameras flash—professional, brief—and then the world narrows to the engendered warmth of the greenhouse and the lingering chill of drizzle on the glass.

You think of the south inlet, the extra sensor that Dr. Noor insisted upon, the maintenance bonds you fought for, Sora's charge of betrayal, Lina's rare compliment, Maya's almost-cry. The pilot exists now as both lifeline and lever. How it will be wielded remains to be seen.

[Music: A steady, mid-tempo pulse builds—no crescendo, only the sense of motion toward the next action.]

You close your palm over the seagull pin, feeling its small dented surface. The motion is private and unnecessary, and yet it grounds you like a compass.

You inhale the greenhouse humidity once more. Outside, the sea is an endless gray practice of tides, indifferent and exact.

You walk away from the signing table with the paper in the municipal folder under your arm and the knowledge that the pilot will change the town in ways you can partly foresee and partly cannot.

You know what you have authorized. You know whom it will protect and whom it will displace. You know the immediate relief it can offer and the long, stubborn questions it will leave.

You exhale, and the sound of your breath is the only small thing that answers the weight in your chest.

[Visual: Tight on your hazel eyes; the amber light reflects as a thin line in them. Outside, rain rounds the glass into pearlescent beads.]

There is no simple closure here—only the deliberate movement forward. The pilot is signed. The work begins.
