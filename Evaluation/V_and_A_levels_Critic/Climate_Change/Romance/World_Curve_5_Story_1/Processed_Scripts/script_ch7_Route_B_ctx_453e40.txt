[Scene: Harborfront Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — Storm on the Horizon]
[Background:"ch7_453e40_1"]
[Music: Driving percussion under a rising choral swell—urgent, hopeful]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the slap of a wave against a piling, the rasp of megaphone feedback]

You came down from the rooftops because Kira's phone didn't stop ringing until you promised. The choice sits in the small, solid weight of your seed vial at your throat—an honest talisman against the polished renderings that hang over the city like a second tide.

Kira: "Neighbors! Friends! This is our shoreline. Not their bottom line!"

You move between stalls that have been repurposed into testimony platforms—tables with handwritten signs, crates for small speakers, teacups offering warmth. The scent of frying fish and salt and soaked wool fills your lungs. Hands—callused, small, trembling—reach out to steady a sandbag or hand you a spool of rope. You thumb the seeds in their glass, feeling them click softly like a tiny clock counting down to the vote.

[Background:"ch7_453e40_2"]
[Music: The percussion grows; a brass motif threads through—bright, forward]

A vendor who used to sell nets climbs a crate and unfolds a photograph: a garden swallowed by a flood last year. He begins, voice low, and the boardwalk leans in.

Vendor: "This alley fed my family's table—tomatoes red as shame. Jonah's model doesn't know the taste of that soil."

Neighbor voices rise—each anecdote a stitch in a ragged tapestry. An old woman with moss under her nails speaks of rooftop bees. A teenager in a patched school jacket recounts learning to graft branch to stock on your rooftop. The crowd collects these small, specific outrages and makes them vast.

You step forward when Kira catches your eye. The megaphone is warm; her grin is brief and ferocious.

Kira [through the megaphone]: "We don't block people—we hold them to account. We say yes to safety, no to erasure. Ayla—your voice."

You inhale the damp air, taste iron and seawater. Your reply is not a speech of numbers; it is a map of lives.

Ayla Moreno: "This boardwalk is not an abstraction for you. It's where Mateo hauled his nets, where kids learned the names of every gull. We can design protection that keeps those things—our gardens, our fisheries—alive. We don't need to lose our history to safety."

Kira [through the megaphone]: "Say it. Say what you're willing to fight for."

You pause—the megaphone shakes slightly in your grip. In your chest, the old ache of the lost fence post whispers. But beneath it, a simpler, louder drum: the people here, their hands, their faces, their stories.

Ayla Moreno: "We will not trade our food, our seeds, our roofs for a glossy plaza that erases us. We demand co-design. We demand legal guarantees for community spaces. And we will stand here—together—until that happens."

The crowd answers with a roar like wind through tens of flags. The energy is high—electric, collaborative—an organism made of many lungs.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Hand the mic to Mateo"
> Choice B: "Hold the mic and keep speaking"
[Outcome A: You slide the megaphone toward Mateo. He coughs, then begins, voice thin with memory: "In my day we read tides by smell—this place remembers us." The crowd leans in as if learning to listen again.]
[Outcome B: You keep the megaphone, driving the rhythm: "Design with us. Not for us." People cheer; Kira pumps a fist beside you.]
[MERGE: The crowd's energy remains high; attention stays fixed on the speakers.]

[Background:"ch7_453e40_3"]
[Sound: Murmured chants—"With us! With us!"—build into a chorus]
[Music: Strings quicken, percussion tightens]

Mud spats at the edges of the boardwalk—Elias appears, sleeves dirty, boots caked, holding a tablet in one hand like a shield. He looks like someone who has come through a lab and into a lived sun, footprints of both worlds at his heels. For a breath, he stands on the periphery, watching you watch him, eyes searching for an opening in your face.

Elias Rook [softly, to Ayla Moreno]: "Ayla. I—"

Ayla Moreno: "You came."

Elias Rook: "I thought I could find a way to explain the models here. Prediction isn't prophecy—it's a tool. The seawall could save thousands." He rubs his thumb along the tablet's cracked corner. "But listening—" He looks at the crowd, at the vendor with the photo, and the sentence clips off. He falters in the face of human fact.

You and Elias circle a conversation that can't be contained by numbers. You want to be hard—hold him accountable—but he is not Jonah. He is a man whose hands are dirtied by both science and the place it aims to protect.

Ayla Moreno: "Your models don't get to be the only voice, Elias. They're part of the toolset—not the mandate."

Elias Rook: "I know. I know. I've been meeting with engineers who will tell anything to make a design sound inevitable. But I—" He swallows. "I came to hear you. To see what we can make together."

Kira edges closer, not unkind. Her megaphone's whisper is a razor.

Kira [through the megaphone]: "Then say something to them, Elias. Don't hide behind charts. Tell them how you see this with your own eyes."

Elias Rook (to the crowd): "My work is to reduce risk. I won't pretend models are feelings. I can show you flood pathways, return periods. But if—if the community has lived knowledge we can fold in, we can change the shape of what we build. We can prioritize where green corridors stay."

A cheer swells, tentative at first, then if the crowd trusts anything it's that promise of being heard. The arousal ratchets upward—the percussion like a pulse in the boards beneath your feet.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Invite Elias to take the crate-platform and speak his models plainly"
> Choice B: "Demand Elias pledge publicly to community co-design before he speaks"
[Outcome A: You tap the crate. Elias climbs, tablet tucked against his chest, and speaks into a borrowed mic—clear, human, translating curves into decisions that could protect—but only if they listen. People nod; some still watch him with suspicion.]
[Outcome B: You set your jaw and call for a pledge. Elias looks surprised, then solemn. "I will, in good faith," he says. The crowd erupts—an act of trust handed into his hands.]
[MERGE: Whether he speaks first or pledges, Elias's willingness to engage becomes part of the day's testimony.]

[Background:"ch7_453e40_4"]
[Sound: A hush as he approaches; then the hum of his polished voice]
[Music: A lower brass throb enters—opposing force]

Jonah Hale is all smooth angles and practiced concern. He moves through the crowd with camerawork in mind, press folding around him like a tide. When he speaks, it's to a sound system he brought with him, a counterpoint to Kira's rough megaphone.

Jonah Hale: "Friends, I come with solutions. We have to act quickly—engineering prevents loss of life. This blockade endangers the city."

His words are honey-glass. He knows optics—they are his scaffolding. He frames the blockade as selfish, a stunt that raises risk. There is a shudder of anger; some in the crowd shout back.

Mateo, hands stained by years of sea and rope, does not shout. He climbs a crate with more difficulty than he'd like and lets his voice come out small, then gathering strength like tidewater.

Mateo: "We are not blind to danger. I lost nets once. I know what a storm can do. But the answer is not to sweep us away like driftwood and call it safety. Measure, yes. Mercy, yes. Together, yes."

The crowd hangs on Mateo's cadence. In him is the weathered memory of the harbor; in his request is a moral geometry that refuses Jonah's simple equation of dollars-for-safety.

Mayor Serena arrives in a low-profile car and steps out, civic sash pinned despite the drizzle. Her face is a map of negotiations; she carries the weight of votes and press conferences in the set of her jaw. She looks at the blockade, at you, at Jonah, and for a sliver of a second, her eyes find yours and then turn away, heavy with responsibility.

Mayor Serena [quietly, to Ayla Moreno]: "This is not easily solved. But the council will see this—your presence—your testimony. The vote is hours away."

You feel the whole boardwalk tighten—an organism holding its breath. The light goes thin and violet as the storm pushes closer; every wet surface mirrors the small, human faces glinting with resolve.

Jonah Hale steps forward, voice rising with practiced urgency.

Jonah Hale: "Mayor—this spectacle could delay critical work. When delay costs lives, we cannot be sentimental."

Kira [through the megaphone]: "When 'critical work' costs lives of a different sort—of identity, of food security—we call that a trade-off we won't accept!"

A dozen voices speak at once; a hundred hands rise. The arousal crescendos: chants, clapping, the slap of boots on wood. Your heart beats against your ribs like a fist.

You feel the shoulders-braced stance settle into you—the line you will not cross, and the space you will fight to protect. The crowd's voice becomes a single worn rope pulled taut.

[Background:"ch7_453e40_5"]
[Music: All instruments blare together for a single, bright moment—joy and fear braided]

Someone shouts that the council bell is tolling in the distance—the town's signal that the vote cycle advances. It slices through the din and focuses the room like a lens.

You can feel the boardwalk as a cathedral—boards worn smooth by centuries of feet, smells of fry-cook oil and brine baptizing the area. Around you, faces—Elias's earnest confusion, Kira's fierce grin, Mateo's steady plea—are lit with something fierce and hopeful.

The tide of sound swells toward a point of decision. You realize, with an almost physical jolt, that this—this moment—might bend the city's future. Your ribs feel like a drum; your voice is part of a chorus that will be remembered.

[Page-turn moment: You step forward, megaphone in hand, the crowd quieting enough that your words could slip between heartbeats. The sky above the harbor darkens to a bruise. Sirens—or perhaps the low rolling of the sea—begin to thread into the edges of sound. You open your mouth, and the world waits.]
