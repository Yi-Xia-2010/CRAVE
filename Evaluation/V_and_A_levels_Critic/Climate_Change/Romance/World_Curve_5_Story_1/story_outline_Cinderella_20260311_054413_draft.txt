## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Seed and Stone

---

## World View
A near-future coastal city, 2042: a harbor once humming with shipping now wrestles with higher tides, ferocious storms, and the politics of survival. The urban fabric is patched — regenerated green rooftops and community-run greenhouses sit atop brick tenements; crumbling piers and flooded boardwalks bracket gleaming presentation plazas and construction sites where corporate seawalls rise like promises. Power is shared unevenly: regional climate labs and corporate development consortia wield data, models, and capital; grassroots networks organize seed exchanges, roof farms, and nightly watches against storm surges. Mayor Serena Okoye sits between lobbying and community pressure; Jonah Hale’s infrastructure firm proposes rapid, centralized fortification while people like Mateo Alvarez and neighborhood activists fight to keep gardens, memory, and livelihoods intact. The culture is pragmatic and urgent: rituals of tending, seed-swapping, and shared meals coexist with municipal meetings and televised renderings of “protected” boulevards. Religion and class shape choices: coastal elders remember the harbor’s old rhythms; investors imagine property-secured futures. Geography is intimate — layered terraces, a glass greenhouse that smells of earth and wet leaves, a warehouse lab with monitors and maps — and the atmosphere blends eco-grit and soft realism with stormwashed pastels. Romance and climate policy are entwined: love is tested where seawalls meet seedbeds.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Ayla Moreno) fight to preserve community-grown, nature-based solutions against large-scale, top-down projects promising security. I return to the harbor after founding rooftop networks and a greenhouse; Elias Rook, a climate engineer with steel-blue eyes and a dented thermos, is both collaborator and source of compromise. Jonah Hale, silvered and persuasive, pushes a comprehensive seawall that would reshape neighborhoods and displace rooftop plots. The narrative follows a Cinderella arc: we rise as community and coalition-building show promise; a sudden collapse (a political betrayal, a failed negotiation, or a storm-based setback) forces painful choices where love and public welfare strain; finally, one of several recoveries follows — from hybrid, negotiated wins to bitter compromises or resilient, imperfect collaboration. Branch points force me to choose strategy: align with Elias’ hybrid design, join Kira’s activist direct action, or negotiate with Jonah to modify his plan. Across branches I must balance sentimental attachment (seed vial necklace, the greenhouse’s humid warmth) with pragmatic scale (Elias’ models on glowing monitors). The endings vary: full hybrid victory, bittersweet preservation with loss, top-down triumph with social fracture, or a “true” ending where no side fully wins, but we build a patchwork future and preserve an imperfect bond. Every route is first-person, visceral, and visual, with choices that test my loyalty to neighbors, my mistrust of institutions, and my capacity to accept compromise.
