You step from the humid hush of the greenhouse into the lab’s cool, clinical breath. The difference slices through you like air: there is chalk dust instead of condensation, monitor glow instead of filtered sun, and a steady mechanical heartbeat where plant-scented quiet used to be. Your jacket is a map of work — patched elbows, soil in seams — and you wear it like armor.

Elias is already bent over a table strewn with overlay maps and a tablet, dark hair mussed the way it always is after a wind off the harbor. When he looks up, his steel-blue eyes light the way his hands move: brisk, precise, eager to show you something that finally fits between both your worlds.

Elias: "You made it. I—" (he gestures at the tablet) "—I ran the terrace attenuation model with your substrate specs. The retention curve improved by twelve percent when we layered the —" (he taps) "—the living substrate you recommended."

You: "Twelve percent is not nothing." (You strip off one glove, thumb worrying a smudge of compost.) "Show me where the tradeoffs are."

Elias leans the tablet toward you. The overlay morphs — color bands of risk, layered with green patches you recognize as your roof grids. The model ticks; small animations show water pooling, then spilling into planned terrace basins.

Elias: "Here. If we raise the berm by half a meter and intersperse living terraces, the peak surge in that block drops. The levee mass remains smaller. And—" (he hesitates, audio softening) "—it preserves access to the community plots."

You feel the word 'preserve' like an offered hand.

You: "And Jonahs' benchmarks? The municipality's requirement for lifecycle safety—"

Elias: "Meets them within the uncertainty range. With staged reinforcement and community upkeep, it’s acceptable for the committee." (His voice carries a smile that is mostly pride and partly relief.) "We can present a hybrid pilot. We can show it works."

The monitor hums. Your notes — pages of plant lists and soil mixes, frayed and thumbed — sit between you both like a small treaty. You run a fingertip over a pressed leaf, feeling papery ridges.

You: "If it actually holds, it’s not just engineering. It’s stewardship. It keeps people and the living parts in the same plan."

Elias: "Exactly. That’s why I wanted you here." (He watches your expression, searching for soft signs of concession or resistance.) "Your credibility—your network—makes this legible to the community. My models make it legible to the council. Together, it’s harder to paper over the human parts."

You: "And Jonah?" (You look up; at the lab edge Jonah stands, hands tucked, amber eyes assessing like a vet appraising stock. He is distant and magnetic at once — everyone's kind of dangerous when they are certain.)

Elias follows your glance. "He'll test it to see if it’s scalable. Which is good. Scaling is his language."

Jonah (from the periphery): "Scaling is everyone’s language," (he approaches, voice smooth) "but context matters. If you two can marry soil with steel and the numbers hold, that’s a story worth selling."

You (internal): Jonah’s smile is a polished hinge — opening doors, but also, you know, the kind that can swing closed on a neighborhood. Still, his presence now is not a lock but an audience. There’s work to do.

Back at the lab, the pace picks up. Models are re-run, line items adjusted, and you fall into a rhythm that is fast, close, and electric. Your soil-stained notebook and his wristpad become two halves of the same ledger. You argue details — substrate depth, plant species tolerance, volunteer roster shift schedules — sometimes gently, sometimes sharp, but always with an undercurrent of one-ness toward the outcome.

Mateo drops by, leaning on a cane and a lifetime of harbor memory. He smells of fish and varnish and the stories that salt keeps.

Mateo: "If you put kelp seams in the terraces, you'll steady the shore. But don’t forget the kids. Teach them to tend it, too." (He chuckles, voice rough with approval.) "If you let them own it, the city won't take it as easy."

You: "We’re building stewardship into the plan. Education slots, volunteer credits, a community maintenance board." (You spread your hands wide with the list like an offering.) "This isn’t a model without hands."

Mateo: "Good. Keep the hands." (He taps your shoulder with an endpoint of blessing; you feel the weight and warmth of it.)

[Visual: The lab whiteboard fills with annotations: 'maintenance trust', 'community seats', 'legal binding language'. The models spin faster; the room's hum crescendos.]

Mayor Serena Okoye arrives with her measured gait, civic sash folded into practicality. Her weariness is visible — sleepless nights, too many spreadsheets — but there’s an openness in her posture now that wasn’t there when projects started as abstract renderings.

Mayor Serena: "I’ve reviewed the initial proposal. It’s the most promising hybrid I’ve seen that doesn’t just placate investors." (She lets the words land like verdict and compliment both.) "The committee wants a vetted pilot before any larger allocation. If you can deliver a constrained demonstration with rigorous monitoring, we can justify expanding it."

Elias (quickly): "We can design a controlled demo. The lab will run continuous monitoring; I’ll publish the model traces in real time. Transparency built into the pilot."

Kira (watchful): "And community representation in governance?"

You: "Yes. Co-management written into the pilot charter. Maintenance budgets ringfenced for five years, and community seats on the oversight board."

Mayor Serena studies the exchange, then names a place that makes your chest jump — both with trepidation and excitement.

Mayor Serena: "Do it at the Twilight Festival Plaza. It’s public, it’s symbolic, and it’s a space the city values. A demonstration there, if successful, will show the public proof of concept and give the committee confidence."

Elias turns to you, breath held in that moment between plan and launch.

Elias: "Twilight Festival Plaza would be ideal for a constrained demonstration — high foot traffic, visible metrics, and community programming we can integrate." "We can stage living levees along the edge, rooftop reconnections on temporary scaffolds, and a real-time dashboard for folks to watch how green infrastructure attenuates runoff during an actual evening tide."

Jonah: "If the demo proves the numbers, I’ll support scaled funding recommendations. It’s better to build on success than on arguments."

Elias: (quiet, a promise more than a plan) "We’ll start with the festival. We’ll do it right. We’ll keep the community at the center."

You: "We will." (You feel the truth of it: this is not a halting compromise but a new synthesis.) "Let’s show them what love for a place looks like when it has data behind it."
