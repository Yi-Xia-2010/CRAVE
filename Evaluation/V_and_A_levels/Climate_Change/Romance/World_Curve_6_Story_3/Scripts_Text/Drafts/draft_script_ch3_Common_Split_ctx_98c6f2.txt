### DRAFT
# Route: Route C (Ending)


[Scene: Municipal Hall / Council Chambers | Evening]
[Visual: Rain streaking the tall windows; projection maps throw pale blue light across varnished wood. The brick exterior grows darker as the storm moves in.]
[Music: Low cello motif, steady—an anxious heartbeat under conversation]
[Sound: Soft murmur of voices, paper rustling, the occasional metallic ping as someone sets a tablet on the table]

You come in on the tail of a wet wind. The foyer smells of damp wool and salted leather; the floor at your boots makes a quiet grate when you stop. In your pocket the folded tide chart presses against your thigh like a small, private weight. You run a thumb over the edge of the paper and feel the familiar grain—a thing you can manage—while everything else feels slippery and fast.

You take the back row because the room is full and because being visible carries its own cost tonight. From here you can see faces arranged like a weather map: neighbors hardened by years of hauling nets, two teenagers with wet hoods holding a cardboard sign folded against their knees, Mira Chen in her immaculate coat—tablet edge gleaming—smoothing the screen as if wiping down a promise. Councilwoman Tamsin Hale is at the head of the table, poised as always, a polished silhouette against a glare of projection light. The mayor sits to her left, hands steepled; the microphone catches every little scrape.

Rosa slides into the seat beside you and presses her hand into yours—brief, fierce, an anchor. You feel Luca's presence like a tremor through the building: someone set down a clanking box near the back entrance and you picture him outside with sandbags, sleeves rolled, waiting for whatever comes after this vote. Across the room, Elias's eyes find yours, steel-blue and clear as a knife-edge. There's worry folded into the lines at the corners; he gives you a tiny, almost embarrassed smile, the one that always arrives right before he steadies himself.

Dr. Henrik Sato sits a few rows forward, thin and animated in his own old sweater. He leans toward you at one point and murmurs—soft, a voice that has always smelled faintly of tea and tide—"History teaches neither cruelty nor kindness by default. It teaches consequences, Maya. Don't let timing be the only reason we repeat an avoidable mistake."

You nod without turning. Inwardly, Henrik's caution uncoils the old images you carry: the roofline of your childhood home lifting under water, the way your father's hands trembled as they stacked wet photos into a plastic crate. You don't let the memory fill the room, but it sits between your ribs, heavy and exact.

[Visual: Close-up on the projection map as a new slide snaps into place—an engineered seawall rendered in stark, white geometry; annotations pulse with figures and timelines.]
[Sound: The soft click of a slide advancing; the room adjusts its breath]

Tamsin rises, and the room hushes as if a sea-wind has smoothed it flat. Her voice is precise, a well-edged thing tuned to being heard.

Tamsin Hale: "Thank you all for coming on short notice. The regional authority has offered a conditional grant to fast-track a comprehensive seawall. It's a one-time window; the funding covers construction, maintenance for five years, and job retraining for affected workers. The condition is straightforward: we accept the terms, or the funding reallocates to another jurisdiction. Time is the variable here."

A ripple—some hopeful, some scared—passes through the room. Mira stands and taps the tablet; the projection switches to renderings of clean angles and wide promenades that promise economic revival. The images are bright, almost tenderly sleek against the rain-smeared windows.

Mira Chen: "The developers we've partnered with guarantee that work could begin within months. We can protect critical infrastructure immediately and stabilize the market for local businesses. We should be realistic about what we can deliver on our own, and what requires coordinated capital."

Elias leans forward, jaw set. He speaks in a low voice that travels to you on a current.

Elias Voss: "Immediate safety isn't a talking point for me—it's a requirement. But those renderings erase who we are. Where do the fishers fit? Where's the space for tidal gardens? We lose more than Boardwalk signs."

Mira: "Mr. Voss, the plan includes provisions for fishing access. Economically viable access. We're not removing livelihoods; we're protecting your ability to plan around hazards."

Elias: "Access that looks good on a plan and access that actually keeps nets and small boats working are different things."

You can feel the council's attention narrowing—this is the edge everyone came for. People speak, voices layered: a man from the harbor wants jobs now; a retired teacher worries about long-term costs; a young mother asks whether her children's school will still be there in ten years. The microphones catch the raw, human interruptions—frustration, pleading, the brittle humor that comes before fear.

Rosa squeezes your hand again. She leans in, voice low and urgent.

Rosa Delgado: "You remember what they did up north, right? This is another 'save them now' pitch. They swallow towns. We need to show ours works."

You consider the plan you folded into your pocket—a demonstrator pilot, living seawall sections, community labor, a timeline that trades speed for stewardship. You also glimpse the practical arithmetic Mira has already put into glowing numbers: jobs created, timeline shortened, headlines that might put the project in another light.

You choose, or you do not. Either way, the microphone comes around sooner than you expected. The mayor, more conciliatory than Tamsin, clears his throat and looks over the room with the soft condemnation of someone attempting neutrality in a storm.

Mayor: "Given the urgency in the grant, and the strong opinions on both sides, we'd like a short list of speakers who can summarize positions succinctly. Ms. Ortega—would you...?"

Every small sound in the chamber seems to tilt toward you. The air tastes faintly of metal and tea. Your mouth goes cotton-dry; your hands have ridges from the tide chart. Henrik's voice—less a memory now, more a steadying press—says, "Speak the stakes. People listen to what connects to them."

You open your mouth, and the words you were planning play behind your teeth: the demonstrator's cost, the jobs it creates in a different ledger, the volunteer hours, the way the living wall can be replicated. You also feel the cold logic of the grant: time-limited funding, job guarantees, infrastructure scale. The two futures settle over the audience like shiftable weather.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Pass a quiet note to Elias: 'We can soften this—negotiate the access points'") -> Reaction: "Elias takes the note with a soft exhale. He folds it into his palm the way someone folds calm into a fist, then leans to catch your eye—his look says he'd rather argue the detail with Tamsin than lose the immediate protection." || Choice B ("Look directly at Tamsin and ask for specifics about community governance in the project") -> Reaction: "You fix Tamsin with your most level voice and ask: 'Who will manage the coastal access points? Who sits on the oversight board?' Tamsin's reply is cool—process, timelines, trustees appointed by the regional authority. The room tightens; a fisherman spits a laugh somewhere behind you."]

Tamsin answers, smooth as a cut stone.

Tamsin Hale: "The grant requires a management board for the project—regional oversight with seats for municipal representatives. The structure isn't meant to erase local say; it's to ensure accountability, expedience, and maintenance plans that small towns cannot reliably fund alone."

You don't doubt her competence. You doubt the calculus she trusts—efficiency framed as inevitable. You also see how this fits into Mira's slick slides: a manageable rectangle of progress, a press-ready narrative. It terrifies you and it seduces the town in equal measure.

Dr. Henrik exhales in a small sound you can hear despite the room: "Rapid builds are often irreversible. We should not confuse speed with sole correctness."

Rosa leans close, voice roughened with urgency.

Rosa Delgado: "We can fight this and show them our way, but it'll take time, and we might lose the grant. Or we slow-roll and try to graft things in, risking our say. Or we take it. The question isn't just what will be built, it's who gets to decide what 'built' means."

Elias steps in at that moment—two words that are both a shield and a supplication.

Elias Voss: "Maya, whatever you do—say what keeps people safe tonight, and keep a path open to fight for the soul of the place. We can push for oversight seats, for that demonstrator to be funded as part of the project. Don't rule it out without asking."

You feel the crush of choice like a tide moving in. Your chest tightens, a predictable kind of grief and responsibility. The room feels smaller, the rain louder against the windows. Heads turn toward you with the patient hunger of people watching a weathered lighthouse decide which beacon to light.

You think of Luca shoring the back door, of Rosa's knotted hands, of Henrik's quiet alarms, of Elias's held breath. You think of your old house, of the soft hollow the loss left in you—the memory a map of what "too late" looks like.

The mayor's gaze rests on you and the council waits. This is the decision point; the town's future is not only technical now—it is moral and relational and desperately immediate. You can feel the room leaning into the moment where you choose the tenor of your response.

[Visual: Rain intensifies outside; a low thunder mutters far off, like a punctuation mark. The projection fades to a neutral slide. Microphones hum faintly, waiting.]
[Music: The cello motif holds—steady but pulled taut, an unresolved chord]

You inhale. The air tastes of salt and iron and the faint sweetness of someone else's umbrella. Your hand rests on the folded tide chart you brought, the pencil tucked inside like a hinge. You picture the three paths—distinct, costly, honest in their risks. They circulate in your mind, each carrying its own grief.

You do not speak yet. The room waits as a held breath. This is where you decide next steps for the town—and for yourself.
