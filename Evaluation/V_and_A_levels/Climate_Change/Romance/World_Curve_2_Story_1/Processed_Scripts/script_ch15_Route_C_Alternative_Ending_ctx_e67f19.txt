You stand on the boardwalk with your satchel heavy at your hip, the contract papers folded into a plastic sleeve like a fossil you are about to bury or display. Elias is beside you, hands buried deep in his pockets as if he could hold himself together the same way. Aunt Lila's fingers are a taut loop around yours — steady, not stopping you, not pulling you back.

You can feel the air changing: not only the weather but the tenor of the crowd. Faces you have known since childhood—neighbors who painted the same mural, fishermen who left you coffee on rainy mornings—tilt toward you with an edge. The salt in the air tastes metallic on your tongue, as if the sea itself were warning you.

Noah is the first to close the distance, paint-streaked hoodie a banner of every build he helped raise. He arrives with Jun and two other volunteers. They do not move like attackers; they move like people who have been burned and are still hot.

Noah Rivera [voice rough]: "Mira."

You inhale. Your throat is a dry tidepool. You have answers stacked inside you—reasons, fear, arithmetic—but they will not soften the accusation.

Mira Santos [soft]: "I never wanted to run. I—"

Noah cuts you off with the gravity of someone trying to keep a bucket from tipping. He looks at Elias with a kindled suspicion.

Noah Rivera [barks a laugh that is almost a sob]: "And him? You really going to leave with that man who used to work for those people who displace villages? How long before 'we' becomes 'they' again?"

Elias Park [hands still in pockets]: "Noah, I left that life for a reason. I made mistakes. I'm trying to help—"

Noah Rivera [barks a laugh that is almost a sob]: "Help who? Your conscience? Because right now it looks like saving yourselves."

Jun steps forward, cheeks flushed, flier-carrying hands trembling.

Jun Park: "There are families moving crates to the high school tonight because they were told they'd be protected. They wanted to believe you—Mira. We wanted to believe you."

You feel Aunt Lila's palm press harder into yours, a hinge trying to close a crack. Her voice is low, threaded with something older than anger—disappointment that resembles rain.

Aunt Lila Santos: "Child, you know what's in your hands. You know what it means when a person crosses the water instead of building the bridge with folks who got wet."

Your Moleskine is zipped in your bag; the coral scarf at your throat feels suddenly too thin against the wind. Every option you've rehearsed disintegrates when a friend calls you coward.

Mira Santos [voice rising despite yourself]: "I am not leaving to save myself. Elias and I can keep working—build prototypes—get funded without turning our backs on Marrow Bay. We can go do that work and bring back what we learn."

Noah's jaw clenches. He spits a handful of words that have been gathering like storm brine.

Noah Rivera: "'Bring back' doesn't put your mother's house back on its pilings. It doesn't keep Aunt Lila's stories from blowing away. What looks like 'getting help' looks like betrayal."

There is a chorus now—murmurs, a woman scolding softly, a man who used to fix your roof turning away. The sound fills the boardwalk like surf over rock. A gust pulls at the placards and one slaps hard against a storefront window.

You can feel your heart accelerate — not the steady drum of determined work, but a sprinting, aching pulse. Your chest wants to break into all the explanations at once, to explain the calculus, the burnout, the nights you saw people you loved have to choose between mold and mortgage. Instead, you find your voice thin:

Mira Santos [bitter, fast]: "I can't— I can't be in two places at once. If I stay, I'll keep losing people to negotiations that feel like promises written in fog. If I go, maybe I can get leverage that helps everyone."

Jun Park [bitter, incredulous]: "Leverage for whom? We've seen how leverage works. It buys rights for eyes that don't look like ours."

Aunt Lila's thumb rubs a line on the back of your hand, a silent litany. She meets your eyes—not pleading, not condemning, just cataloguing. Her small face is the map of the town's memory.

Aunt Lila Santos [quiet, like pressed paper]: "If you leave, make sure it's to do the work you say. Make sure you bring what we need and not what the banks ask for."

The crowd parts as someone else appears—Mayor's aide, a clipboard, a rehearsed neutrality. It smells faintly of municipal coffee and the varnish of polished decision-making. Their presence is a line drawn in wet sand.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse & Boardwalk End | Dusk — Lantern Room Light Pooled]
[Background:"ch15_e67f19_2"]
[Sound: Waves smashing rocks; a gull caws close by, then silence beyond the glass]
[Music: A single low cello note sustained beneath anxious piano chords]

You and Elias climb the narrow stairs to the lantern room the way people climb into confession booths—seeking a place where the wind can't reach you entirely. The lantern light throws Elias' silhouette long and uncertain. He moves like someone who has learned to measure his words before he trusts them with someone else's future.

Elias Park [quiet]: "I'm sorry about how it felt down there."

Mira Santos [a hollow sort of laugh]: "It felt like being accused of theft for trying to protect a thing."

Elias's fingers toy with the turbine pendant at his collar. The small metal clicks, familiar and grounding; you know the sound by heart.

Elias Park: "I never wanted you to be the target. I know what my presence triggers."

You look at him—really look: the grease under his nails, the way his smile softens when he remembers something small and private. You are tethered to him by countless small things: a shared sandwich at the Saltworks, the night he stayed up debugging a pump until dawn, the way he jokes to make you breathe.

Mira Santos: "And I didn't want to be the reason people lose trust. Everyone thinks we're choosing escape."

Elias Park [leans forward, voice trembling with the pulled tautness of someone deciding]: "I can't promise no one will say that. I can promise I won't make the same compromises I did before. If we go, it's to build something that we can bring back—hard proof, enforceable designs, legal templates. I'm offering us a way to be useful without being swallowed."

The lantern throws their face into a chiaroscuro of shadow and lamp. In this light, the room feels small, intimate—an island in the unfolding upheaval. But the wind is a patient, insistent hand; it rattles the panes, an urgent reminder that time is measured in surges.

Mira Santos [bitter, fast]: "Maybe leaving is the only way to not lose you and also not lose everyone else to politics neither of us can beat from here."

Elias Park: "Mira—"

You cut him off with a sound that is half laugh, half sob.

Mira Santos: "Do you know what it is like to stand in a council chamber and watch projectors say 'resilience' while families get relocation forms? Do you know what it is to have Aunt Lila look at you like you've turned into tidewater?"

Elias Park [low]: "I know I failed before. I'm trying—god, Mira, I'm trying to make that wrong right. But I'm scared too. I'm scared we stay and it tears us apart. I'm scared we go and it tears them apart."

You close your eyes. The lighthouse lamp spins, making the world outside a strobe of dark and pale. The image of Aunt Lila's hands, of Noah's spit-cry, of Jun's flier clutched white-knuckled, presses into you like the cold glass.

Aunt Lila's voice, when she speaks in the doorway, is the kind of small thunder that breaks the quiet:

Aunt Lila Santos: "If you go—" (she stops, breath thinning) "—then don't let it be to hide. Promise me you will come back with pages, not with promises that sound like storms."

Elias steps closer to you, his hand finally leaving the pendant to cover the top of your hand. His thumb rubs a callus there, a tiny, human act.

Elias Park [steadfast]: "We will write every agreement down with witnesses. We'll put legal language into every prototype we spin out. We will not let 'help' be a euphemism for profit."

You feel something in you respond — not relief, not trust, but a fragile parsing of possibility. Then Jun's words from the boardwalk loop like a needle: "Leverage for whom?"

Mira Santos [whisper, almost not to yourself]: "Who are we when we leave? Are we people who took a bridge-building kit and made it ivory-towered? Or are we people who learned and returned?"

Elias Park: "We choose what kind of people we are."

Aunt Lila's hand squeezes yours twice, a rhythm like two knocks. She turns away, not giving you a blessing but a ledger.

Aunt Lila Santos: "Make it count. Make the town remember itself in the ways that matter."

[Scene: Departing Shoreline & Small Harbor Road | Dusk to Nightfall — Engines and Wave]
[Background:"ch15_e67f19_3"]
[Sound: Engine idling; distant sob; a child crying; waves banging a percussion of inevitability]
[Music: Crescendoing strings; a high, metallic undertone]

The car is a small island of packed life: boxes labeled with Saltworks, a wrapped camera, a folded raincoat, a spare pair of boots. You run your hand over the coral scarf one last time—its threads frayed where you've twisted it in worry—and tuck it into your jacket like a last lit candle.

Noah stands by the driver's side, the paint on his hoodie chalked and pale. His jaw is set; the lines around his eyes are deeper. His voice is an edged thing.

Noah Rivera: "So this is it."

Mira Santos [bald honesty]: "This is it."

Noah Rivera: "Do you know what it feels like to see someone you thought would help us pick up and go? It makes people feel smaller. Makes them think their labor is only practice for someone else's resume."

You feel the accusation like a slap that opens an old bruise. You want to argue about leverage and leverage's capacity to alter realities; you want to name the nights you sat awake drafting amendments, or the times you cried over legalese. But the words are not useful now—only the shape of what you do will be.

Jun pushes forward, no theatrics, just a small, pointed hurt.

Jun Park: "Promise me one thing. If you go, make sure whatever you bring back cannot be sold off like a new pier. Make sure the templates you make are open-source, with names we can follow."

Elias meets Jun's gaze. For the first time in days, he does not manufacture calm. He is raw and real as a wound.

Elias Park: "If we leave, everything we develop will be open, documented, with community signatories. I swear it."

Noah's laugh is a broken thing.

Noah Rivera: "Swearing counts for something until the money outbids the oath. People will call this noble and then the lawyering will start. We need someone here who will fight those lawyers in person."

Your throat constricts. The engine hum picks up; the small car's readiness feels like a ripple in the world that will propagate outward. Some neighbors move closer, as if to block or to bless. There is no clear ritual for departure unless you make one.

You step out of the car, barefoot on the cold pebbles, the sea a black throat behind you. The spray hits your face and the salt stings like truth. Aunt Lila stands at the edge of the gathering, hands empty except for the shell she held earlier. She does not call you back. She only watches, and people watch her watching you.

Aunt Lila Santos [voice that has always carried]: "Don't make this a clean story, Mira — nothing about what happened here ever was. Take the shame with you if you must. Take the plans. Remember the names."

You swallow, the words lodging like gull bones. Your hand reaches instinctively for Elias. He slips his palm into yours, fingers locking like two pieces of driftwood.

Elias Park [urgent, barely audible]: "We can leave now. Or we can go back and—"

Mira Santos [internal]: "The town is a net with holes. Some people are already falling through. Leaving might mean hands you could have held will be empty. Staying might mean the two of you are broken by the fight. You are the seam they all look at, and your fingers are slipping."

You look at Elias, at Aunt Lila, at Noah and Jun; each face is an instruction manual in how to survive loss. The car idles; the engine's little vibrations match the quickened thrum in your chest. The sky tears open somewhere beyond the lights and a thin curtain of rain begins.

You hear Jun call, softer than before: "If you go, return the way a person who promised did. Not with riches. Not with hollow things."

Aunt Lila's hand lifts, palm out, a last blessing or a benediction of sorrow. The gulls cry like a shutter being snapped.

You stand at the shoreline where the town meets the road, the ocean a decision-making mirror. The air is full of everything you didn't say and everything you will regret.

The moment tightens to a single hinge: the car door, the curve of the road, the taste of salt, the pressure of Elias' hand. Your foot is already on the gravel, the muscles in your legs bracing for the shift.

You can feel your pulse in your throat — high, sharp, the drumroll before an impossible leap.
