### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C — Ending
# Context: e67f19

[Scene: The Saltworks | Dawn]
[Visual: Wet reeds glinting; raised platforms steaming in cool morning air]
[Music: Urgent strings with a low, driving percussion]
[Sound: Distant gulls; the tinny ping of your phone repeating]

You wake to the phone that never sleeps. The screen blurs from sleep to a serrated list of alerts: messages, missed calls, the reporter's name flashing at the top. For a beat you let the sound be only noise — tide and wind filling the gaps — until the vibration becomes a drumbeat you can no longer ignore.

You touch the coral scarf at your throat. The knit is damp from last night's damped breath; your fingers find the familiar fray where your mother re-tied the end, and a small, private steadiness locks into place. You did this. You chose to make it public.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand smoothing the scarf; mud under your thumbnail]
[Sound: Phone chirp; a headline loading]

You swipe. The headline blooms across the screen like a bruise — black text over a photograph of Municipal Hall's glossy façade: "REDEVELOPMENT EASEMENTS EXPOSED: MARROW BAY CONTRACTS GUARANTEE DISPLACEMENT." The subhead lists clauses no one had seen in the glossy renderings: long-term easements, automatic rezoning triggers, indemnity language that would let developers shift costs onto residents. Below the subhead, the reporter's byline — the one you trusted — and a timestamp from just after midnight.

Your stomach drops as if the tide turned in your chest. You scroll. The documents are there, scanned and annotated; the paragraphs you highlighted like bullet wounds on paper. A comment from the reporter: "We verified signatures; we stand by our sourcing." The world you have been threading together for months is suddenly a red flag on every lawn.

Neighbors start to appear at the Saltworks without a plan, as if the sea itself pushed them toward this place. Noah's coat is half-buttoned, his face the color of rain. Jun arrives with a thermos sloshing over his palm; Aunt Lila follows more slowly, eyes sharp despite the tremor in her hands. They all crowd around your shoulders like a net, desperate and expectant.

Noah: "Mira — is this—did you—"
Mira Santos [defensive, breath loud]: "I gave the docs to Mara. I— I couldn't watch them sign away people's homes in the dark."

Noah: "You couldn't but you did. People are going to be furious. People are already furious."
Mira Santos: "They deserve to be furious. They deserve to know."

Jun: "The paper's alive. Kids at the school heard in the morning announcements. There's a thread on the town group with a thousand comments and counting."
Noah: (grimly) "And Cass will lie."

Aunt Lila [soft]: "You did right. Right doesn't mean easy."

You want to argue, to say the legal value of secrecy and the political calculus and the ways the world demands small sacrifices before change. Instead you feel the cliff-edge of consequence: once truth is shouted into the market, it rings. There is no taking it back.

[Visual: Camera lingers on a printed copy of the scanned pages, the ink smudged by last night's salt air]
[Music: Strings spike; a single brass note like a siren]

You make one move that feels like it should be small: you find the reporter, Mara, among the crowd. She stands apart, hood up, camera hanging at her side. Her eyes are tired but not surprised. She folds away from the group and offers you a cup of something hot; the steam fogs between you.

Mara [Local reporter]: "You understand what this does. You understand the pushback will be immediate."
Mira Santos: "I do. I understand that some people stay in the dark because they think it keeps them safe. I don't believe that anymore."

Mara [Local reporter]: "Someone's already posting the documents to the regional outlet. Cass has just put out a statement denying intent to displace—'mischaracterizations,' she calls it. Expect legal posture before breakfast."
Mira Santos: "Then we give them facts faster than lawyers can polish words."

Mara [Local reporter]: "You understand if this goes south, people will look for a face to blame. They'll look for yours."

Your mouth tastes of iron. You glance at Aunt Lila, who has not let go of your hand. Her knuckles are white and familiar, like someone who has been holding a ship's line for too long.

Aunt Lila [quiet]: "They'll need a place to gather. The greenhouse is open. Bring whoever can't be in the streets."

You nod and the nod is a small emergency drill. You move through the Saltworks in a blur — the micro-turbines twitching in the breeze, the tide gardens humming like a trapped orchestra — while your phone lights up with messages: "Meet at the plaza." "Is this true?" "Mira, are you okay?" "Cass says it's fake."

You feel each vibration as a small blow.

[Scene: Harbor High Rooftop Greenhouse | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Humid amber light through patched panes; condensation bead trails on the glass]
[Music: Percussive heartbeat; low choir in the background]
[Sound: Murmurs; a distant rhythm of chanting that filters down from the plaza]

The greenhouse is a held breath. Plants press at the panes, leaves sussing the salt; the heat makes your scarf cling damply to your neck. Inside, the air smells of damp earth and peppermint tea. Neighbors are gathered, some on crates, some leaning against workbenches. Eyes are bright, faces streaked with last night's salt. Jun has taped up photocopies of the headline on the glass.

Elias Park is already here. He stands by the main bench, sleeves rolled, hands smudged with grease and peat. When he sees you, relief and reproach cross his face at the same time — each more painful for being honest.

Elias Park: "You did it."
Mira Santos [flat]: "I did."

He steps closer, the way his body closes small distances in a room like this. The warmth from the lamp over the propagating tray washes his cheek in amber. You want to say that you had no other way; you want to say that you'd rather carry every storm alone than allow more secret wounds. Instead, the words come in a different shape.

Mira Santos: "They were hiding the triggers. They were making 'stabilization' contingent on legal levers that remove people from their own land. I couldn't—"
Elias Park [interrupting, voice low]: "You could have asked me to try another way — delay, negotiate—"

The exchange is not simple. Elias has always been the appliance of patience, crafting tiny experiments that could be scaled by trust. He is also the one who left a consultancy when promises to communities were traded for ROI. Those two histories collide in his tone now.

Mira Santos: "Negotiation takes time. This was a ticking clause. They could have executed within weeks."

Elias Park [running a hand through his curls]: "I know. I just—" He stops. "I don't know if my physics can fix what politics will break."

The greenhouse is hushed, every ear a static microphone. You both know the cost. At the same time, in the hush, you feel a private current pull at the day: something like regret rendered intimate. Elias's fingers brush a tray of seedlings; the contact is accidental and merciless in its tenderness.

Elias Park [softer]: "If it's about lives, then I'm with you, Mira. I always am. But I worry about you getting eaten alive by the fight."

Mira Santos [bitter laugh]: "If I get eaten, make sure someone brings the good parts to Aunt Lila for stew."

He doesn't laugh. Instead, his gaze sharpens with a different kind of plan-making.

Elias Park: "Okay. We need a secure place for the person who gave you the files. Legal counsel, evidence chain. If this is going to stick, it needs to be airtight. And public support has to look like it's coming from the town — not just from a leak."

You hear the logic in his words and the steadiness of someone who builds things out of scraps and stubbornness. It steadies you, briefly. Then the sound of shouting from below the rooftop fractures the calm like a thrown stone.

[Sound: Muffled shouting; a megaphone voice rising then splintering]
[Visual: Light through the greenhouse door jerking as it's pulled open]

Noah [bursting in]: "They're at the plaza — people are bringing placards. Cass's spokespeople— they're here, too. She says it's 'misleading.' Her legal team is on the way. Someone just posted that there's a planned restraining order—"

Murmurs swell into near panic. The greenhouse vibrates with the energy of people trying to make order out of a new storm.

Mira Santos [decisive]: "We lock the files in the safe chain. Jun, get the list of residents who need legal notices. Elias, can your prototypes handle powering a livestream? If we get the council chamber to respond, we need to be seen."

Elias Park: "Yes. I can jury-rig a feed. But if Cass files an injunction, a court order can force platforms to take it down. We need redundancy."

Jun: "There are lawyers in the town group willing to volunteer. And the regional reporters are already reposting Mara's piece."

Aunt Lila [firm]: "Then we stand where we can be seen. We sing what we know. We tell stories. Let us be the witnesses."

You feel the current picking up; panic is a clean thing in the chest now, like cold seawater. Your pulse is a drum you can't silence. The greenhouse, once a sanctuary, is a command center. You have become both witness and organizer.

[Visual: Montage — you at a laptop, your face lit by screens; you stepping into the plaza, voice raised]
[Music: Percussion accelerates to a frantic tempo]
[Sound: Megaphones, car horns, blistering chants. A dog barks. A baby cries.]

At the plaza, bodies are pressed together under handmade signs: "HOMES NOT PROFITS," "MARROW BAY REMEMBERS." The air tastes like frying oil and adrenaline. Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock's team stands at the opposite flank, neat in their coats, the reflective lines of polished shoes and tablets catching new light. Her spokesperson — a woman in a reflective jacket and an even more reflective smile — addresses a cluster of microphones.

Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock [from a projected feed]: "Any suggestion that our redevelopment will forcibly remove families is not only false, it's harmful. We intend to protect Marrow Bay. These documents are presented without context. We are pursuing legal action to prevent misinformation."

Her voice is smooth and rehearsed, a practiced tide smoothing over the ragged stones of outrage. Around her, staff hand out glossy pamphlets with architectural renderings that show a safer shoreline and happy families in raised promenades. The images are precise and pleasant and, underneath, a calculus of displacement.

You push through the crowd; someone slaps a printed copy of the contract into your hands, eyes wide. The clauses read like a cold blueprint for erasure. You feel nauseous, then furious, then wrenched into a responsibility you cannot abdicate.

Lawyer [polite, sharp]: "Ms. Santos, for the record, our client contests the authenticity of those documents. We are preparing an injunction to halt dissemination of materials that constitute defamation and breach of contract. We advise you to preserve privilege and refrain from publishing further."

Mira Santos [voice rising]: "Are you saying it's illegal to tell people what affects them?"
Lawyer: "I'm saying we will seek immediate court relief if the material continues to be distributed unlawfully."

His words are a net. Behind him, someone hands you a printed notice: a hearing scheduled, emergency motion filed. The paper is cold and clean and final-feeling, like the slap of a judge's gavel before a word is spoken.

Elias Park finds you in the crowd. His hand finds yours in a movement so familiar it hurts. There's a new urgency in his eyes, a hard-ready pause.

Elias Park: "They're trying to silence the evidence. If they get an injunction, the story gets buried and the legal machinery moves faster than we can organize. We need to escalate: community petitions, immediate affidavits, public witnesses."

Mira Santos [breathing fast]: "And if they sue for defamation?"
Elias Park: "Then we defend. We need counsel. We need public depositions. We need the whistleblower's statement preserved."

You think of the person who gave you the files — the whistleblower — faceless here because safety is a shelter you built with your own hands. You had promised protection. You keep that promise.

[Scene: Municipal Hall | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Glass and concrete reflecting a crowd like a broken mirror]
[Music: Percussion replaced by high, metallic strings; a relentless, narrow motif]
[Sound: Shouts, a gavel-like thud from a distant courtroom, the hum of police radios]

Municipal Hall is a pressure chamber; the building's glass throws back the crowd's faces in fractured patterns. Media vans ring the perimeter. Protesters press placards to the windows; counter-voices chant otherwise. The municipal dais is empty for now, but the presence of power is palpable.

Inside, Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock stands before a row of cameras. Her posture is impeccable, deliberate. She delivers a statement that reads like a disclaimer for a catastrophe: sympathetic to loss, determined to act, and constrained by technicalities. The polished cadence is a blade wrapped in velvet.

Cassandra "Cass" Whitlock [on camera]: "We mourn with anyone who fears for their homes. But we will not allow misinformation to hinder progress. Our plan creates jobs and long-term defenses. We are seeking legal remedies to protect our process from malicious leaks."

A ripple of applause — orchestrated, sterile — rises from her staff. Outside, the crowd roars in a different key. The division on the plaza is no longer a debate; it is a boil, and you feel its heat on the back of your neck.

A tall man in a navy suit — Cass's lead counsel — approaches the municipal entrance. He has the air of someone who files motions before breakfast. He carries a sheaf of papers bound with a legal clip and the implacable confidence of one who believes in the inevitability of court.

An official approaches you, breathless and gray around the edges.

Official: "Ms. Santos, legal has a notice for you. We've been instructed to inform any parties listed on the distribution that an injunction hearing will be set. We are required to serve notice."

Your hands go cold. This is the climb up to the apex you felt earlier—the moment when the machinery of power seeks to bind a community with rules that move faster than human gatherings. The official hands you another paper: a preliminary order, counsels named, a timeline that leaves almost no time.

Neighbor voices overlay the official's words: "They're taking us to court." "They'll freeze the ability to post or protest if the injunction holds." "We lose our right to speak."

Elias Park squeezes your hand like someone trying to put you back together with small, stubborn seams.

Elias Park [urgent]: "We need affidavits. We need witnesses to sign declarations affirming the chain of custody and authenticity. If we can get depositions before their emergency motions, we blunt the injunction."

Mira Santos [shaking]: "And the whistleblower?"
Elias Park: "We must secure their testimony through counsel in a secure location. They could be at risk the moment their identity is suspected."

A neighbor — a woman you know who keeps the boardwalk's lighthouse clean — grabs your arm. Her face is a flint of grief and fury.

Neighbor: "They're suing us into silence. They think law is a dam and communities are puddles. They don't know puddles can flood."

Her words are fierce and animal, and the crowd answers with a roar that pushes up against the building's glass. The noise is an animal; the glass shudders, and for an instant you feel as though the city itself might crack.

[Sound: A loudspeaker clicks on; a city official's voice runs over it, clipped and formal]
[Visual: Police tape being unrolled; uniforms appearing at the perimeter]

And then the escalation becomes physical. Officers line up like a new kind of infrastructure. Someone — you cannot tell who in the press of bodies — shoves forward, and there is a scuffle near the steps. A megaphone drops; its plastic cracks.

From somewhere ahead, a legal representative for Cass strides into the crowd, papers outstretched, a process server on his heels. He reads aloud the notice: an injunction is filed; dissemination must cease; an emergency hearing will occur within forty-eight hours. The words land like stones.

Tension spikes into panic. People clutch phones, push for livestreams, gather to shield faces. A caller in the crowd screams that threats arrived for the whistleblower's family. Someone starts to chant the whistleblower's name like a talisman. Your chest tightens into a small, keening animal.

You step between camera lights and the approaching lawyers, feeling suddenly absurd and heroic and inadequate all at once. A man from Cass's team points a camera at you and says, with surgical politeness, "Ms. Santos, will you confirm that you disseminated those documents?"

Your throat closes. Answers are weapons here. Anything you say can be used. You think of Aunt Lila's hands, Noah's paint-splattered hoodie, Elias's quiet plans. You think of the person you promised to protect and the geography of a town made of old docks and newer scars.

Mira Santos: "I did. I did because people deserved to know what was happening to their own streets."

A reporter's flash catches that admission and the air around you seems to crack. Cass's counsel narrows his eyes and reaches for a phone. Someone near the edge of the crowd hurls an accusation. The halo of television light turns you into an icon you never asked to be.

[Music: All instruments converge into a relentless, metallic cluster]
[Sound: Sirens approaching fast]

Suddenly a voice — urgent and thin with panic — reaches your ears: Jun, shouting that someone recognized the whistleblower's car plate in a parking lot nearby. The greenhouse you thought secure might not be. The city is closing in like a cold hand, and legal action is the first of many instruments they'll use.

You realize this fight is now not only political but personal and dangerous. Cass will use courts; profit will use money; fear will use rumor. The protective net of community is under strain, and the next swell will test whether it holds.

A figure in the crowd yells: "They're going to take them! We have to move now!"

People surge. Officers tense. The legal team begins coordinating with municipal officials. The moment tightens into a stranglehold of paperwork and police tape and phones that catch everything.

Your feet are planted in mud and story. Your lungs burn. The arousal you felt earlier — a building pressure like a stormfront — breaks and momentarily becomes chaos. You could feel despair creeping like cold water up your calves, but there's no time to sink. You are the point where ideology and intimacy meet; you are the one who made the choice and now must endure the consequences.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand — the coral scarf pressed against your palm — and on the Moleskine in your bag with pages thumbed open to chain-of-custody notes]
[Music: Cacophony crescendos and holds at a single, high, unresolved note]

You step forward, mouth forming words meant to steady a crowd, when a courthouse official's voice cuts through: "By order of the court, Ms. Santos, you are hereby notified an emergency motion has been filed seeking immediate injunctive relief. You may be required to surrender copies of any disseminated materials pending this hearing."

The words hang like a guillotine. For the first time since you made the choice, the reality of the mechanism designed to stop you lands with physical weight.

You grip the strap of your bag until your knuckles whiten. Around you, friends and neighbors look to you like people seeing a lighthouse fall; some are terrified, some resolute, some inconsolable. Elias squeezes your hand until your skin hurts; his eyes are soft with a plea that is both personal and professional.

Elias Park [raw]: "We protect who we can. We take this to every forum. We do not let them buy silence."

You look at him and see a future where you both keep making things, where your small prototypes become barricades. You also see a darker line where legal might not be enough and threats will cross thresholds no law anticipates.

A siren keens closer; someone cries that Cass's security detail is at the street and that legal threats have already begun to funnel through channels meant to starve out grassroots efforts. The crowd's roar becomes a single, overwhelming sound — grief and fury braided together.

You inhale. You taste salt and metal and smoke. Fear is a physical thing now, a tide that can drown. The town is not calm. It is split down the bones.

[Visual: A long shot of the plaza; police tape, cameras, the greenhouse rooftop in the background; the Saltworks off to the side like a quiet wound]
[Music: Fade to a sustained, discordant tone that refuses resolution]
[Sound: A distant gavel; then—silence like a held breath]

You clutch your Moleskine; inside a line you wrote the night before stares back at you in ink: "If we do not speak, others will decide for us." That certainty is a lantern and a fault line.

You feel the air thin and the moment stretch. The fight has moved into chambers that decide fate with stamps and time, and the people who live here are the ones called to prove they are citizens and not obstacles.

[Visual: Slow focus on your face; the crowd reflected in your eyes]
[Music: Sparse string pluck; a single low drum beat repeating]
[Sound: A phone buzzes with a new message labeled: "Legal hold — get counsel now"]

You have handed the town a voice. The town is shouting back. Cass has sharpened her response into court paperwork and public denial. The whistleblower you vowed to shield is suddenly tangible as a life you must move to protect. The machinery of law and money and security is starting to move like a monstrous undertow.

You take one last look at the crowd — at Aunt Lila's damp face, at Noah's clenched jaw, at Elias' pleading mouth — and hear the question hang between you like a storm flare: can truth alone stop a tide of legal and financial force?

[Visual: Close-up on your hand — the coral scarf pressed against your palm — and on the Moleskine in your bag with pages thumbed open to chain-of-custody notes]
[Music: Cacophony crescendos and holds at a single, high, unresolved note]

The next step is court: testimony, injunction hearings, and a quieter, colder fight that could exile people from the places they love.]
