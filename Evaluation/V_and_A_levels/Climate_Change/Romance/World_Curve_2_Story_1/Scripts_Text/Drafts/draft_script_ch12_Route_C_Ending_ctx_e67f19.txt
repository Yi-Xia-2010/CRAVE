### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Ending


[Scene: Courtroom | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of a packed municipal courtroom; protest posters folded along the benches, Aunt Lila's cardigan bright among the dark coats]
[Sound: Murmured chanting filtered through glass; the sharp rap of a gavel punctuates the room]
[Music: A taut string motif; heartbeat-low percussion building beneath]

You are standing in the aisle, palms pressed flat against the worn wood rail as if holding yourself upright will keep the room from tilting. Fluorescent lights make everyone's eyes too white. The air tastes of stale coffee and the faint metallic tang of rain from outside; beneath it, the sea's salt finds its way in at the drafty windows.

The judge reads the order without heat. His voice is flat, procedural—an instrument enacting momentum.

Cassandra Whitlock: "By provisional decree, this council recognizes Executive Stabilization Authority granted to Whitlock Coastal Partners pending environmental remediation review. Evacuation and relocation measures shall begin within seventy-two hours to ensure public safety and continuity of infrastructure."

The words land like shells thrown at a glass wall. You can feel the boardwalk—your life—crack in your chest. People stand in waves: neighbors, volunteers, faces you know since childhood pressed together in disbelief. Aunt Lila grips your hand until her knuckles hollow; her thumb rubs the coral knit in a slow, steady circle.

Aunt Lila: (soft, fierce) "They'll smooth it with words and timetables. They call it 'safety' the way they call a bulldozer 'progress.'"

You want to answer with a catalogue of technicalities, legal counters, the amendment you thought you had, the clause that would have bound private funds to community control. But the judge's papers are thicker than your resolve, and the lawyers’ pens glide clean of the messy history that lives in porch boxes and tide-stained photo albums.

Elias is beside you, jaw working as if chewing his own silence down into something small and manageable. He leans close; his breath is warm, and it smells faintly of solder and the lemon oil he uses on his camera.

Elias Park: (quiet) "There are appeals. We can—"

You cut him off because you know the sound of that sentence: the tether that keeps meaning but delays consequence. You have already seen proceedings like this eat time until memory dulls.

You: "Appeals don't pack trucks. Appeals don't sign leases."

The judge's clerk announces procedural delays and emergency powers in a voice practiced for control. Cass watches from the front bench like an architect observing their completed design; the expression on her face is a study in composed certainty. It is not a smile. It is not unkind. It is the gravity of someone who thinks she holds the map and knows which lines to redraw.

Cassandra Whitlock: (measured, public) "This will allow for coordinated stabilization. Displacement is regrettable, but necessary to preserve life and economic continuity."

A neighbor—Noah, his hoodie salt-streaked—shouts something about historical covenants and community land. His voice is raw; it scrapes at the margins of the formal language. Security moves like a slow fish, arms cutting through water.

Noah Rivera: "You can't just decide who remembers the town!"

A cluster of voices answers him: prayers, curses, the names of streets and old shopkeepers—memory as protest. The judge bangs the gavel. The court is adjourned. People spill into the hall like water finding new cracks.

[Scene: Municipal Atrium | Afternoon]
[Visual: The atrium is a cold glass box open to the bay; municipal staff set up folding tables, maps, and forms; moving vans line the curb beyond the glass]
[Sound: The diesel engines of trucks; taped voices over a PA instructing protocols; a child's small sob punctuating the announcements]
[Music: Percussive strings accelerate; a high, thin violin threads through]

You move through a choreography you didn't want to learn: intake forms, temporary vouchers, lists where names are checked off like inventory. The atrium smells of cardboard, lemon disinfectant, and a lighter undertow of seaweed from the open doors. Sun breaks in ragged curtains through storm-heavy clouds, plastering faces with short, harsh light.

Families load possessions into boxes labeled with numbers you will never be able to read like they used to read the sky. A woman you taught in the school garden passes carrying a potted geranium, its soil clinging to roots like stubborn memory. A boy drags a stuffed animal by one arm; the toy's eye is missing.

Aunt Lila stays at your side. She watches the lists, then the faces, and finally you. Her eyes are wet but clear.

Aunt Lila: "You fought so hard. I need you to promise me one thing. Promise you'll keep it close—the stories. Keep them where they can be shared."

You: (voice shaking) "I will. I—"

She cuts you off with a laugh that is almost a sob and presses your hand to her cheek.

Aunt Lila: "Don't say the word 'won' unless it's for tomorrow's breakfast. Say it only when you can taste it."

You want to argue with the forms, with the legalese that has crept into the town like ivy strangling a fence. But the facts press forward: provisional authority means parking permits, means contractors with access rights, means a timeline for relocation. The council will be paid for the work. People will get temporary housing. People will also lose porches and basements and the small altars of domestic history.

In the middle of the atrium, Elias finds you. He looks as if every quiet plan he had made has been rerouted through a storm drain—disorienting, urgent.

Elias Park: (low, quick) "They've started scheduling. Trucks at dawn. Jun says the school will host some families, but it's not long-term. Mira—there are options. I can stay and manage the prototypes, keep them running, while you—"

You cut him off because he is offering a splitting of selves that feels like a salvage operation: keep what can be saved here; take the work that can save more people elsewhere. It is sensible. It is cleaving. It is the hardest thing to say aloud.

You: "No. We can't both stay and do this— we both have different fights. If I stay, I'm in the middle of their litigation, their bureaucracy. If I go, I can work on binding policies, on legal frameworks elsewhere. It's not leaving. It's strategy."

Elias searches your face as if to find where the plan sits inside you.

Elias Park: (voice breaking) "Strategy looks a lot like exile."

You: "Maybe exile is a better word than 'stay and watch people tear each other apart.' I won't be abandoning—I'll be building an argument they can't drown in a courtroom."

The atrium hums with moving boxes and directions. Around you, people choose where to put their life. Somewhere, a radio plays an old folksong; its chorus slides into the brim of your chest and makes you swallow hard.

Elias doesn't give the promise easily—he's never been good at firm words where outcomes are uncertain. His hands twist the edge of his jacket where braided copper meets fabric.

Elias Park: "I can't promise the tides won't change my anchor. But I can promise this: what I build, I build so you can come home to something that remembers."

You can see the attempt at humor on his lips, the small, painful playfulness he uses to soften everything. But today the joke tastes like iron. You reach for him; he steps closer and then further, measuring whether proximity will cost him the courage to stay.

Neighbors bustle past with suitcases and tarps; behind them, municipal staff hand out maps that tell people where they will sleep that week. There is a bureaucratic kindness to the instructions—suggested, sanitized—while the city arranges the logistics of leaving.

A man who has run the bait shop for thirty years sits on a folding chair, hat in his lap. His hands look like maps of storms. He tells you, bitter and almost laughing, a story about the time the bay took his nephew's canoe. You hear the story and understand in a new, deeper way what displacement means: not the loss of square footage but the erasure of every small, human anchor that made a place feel like home.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse & Boardwalk End | Twilight]
[Visual: The sky bruises purple and amber; the lighthouse is a dark silhouette; waves throw themselves into the rocks in a rhythm like a fist against a door]
[Sound: Wind tearing at the lantern room's glass; gulls sounding like distant alarms; a single bell from a passing barge]
[Music: A slow, aching cello; under it, an urgent, quickening drumbeat that pushes the heart forward]

You take Elias's hand and lead him to the lighthouse because you have always come here for decisions that require a sky wide enough to hold them. The wind tastes of wet rope and the last of summer's warmth. The lantern room's lamp is gone for repairs; instead, someone has strung battery-powered lanterns that swing like small, shuttered moons.

Aunt Lila stands a few paces behind you, arms folded, face set in the same weathered determination she takes to winter chores. She watches the two of you like a harbor-master watching a ship he loves set sail.

Elias Park: (near-whisper) "I stay. I don't know for how long. I don't know if what I do will change the order of things here. But I'm not leaving because I want to keep you from going. I'm staying because this is where the people who will rely on me are."

You: (bitter breath) "And I'm leaving because there are other places that need someone who knows both the charts and how the people read them."

He swallows, and it is loud in the wind. For a moment, neither of you speaks; speech is too blunt an instrument for what's happening. You let the sea's expanse be the thing that holds this.

Elias Park: (sharp, sudden) "Mira—if you go, I will be here with the prototypes and with the micro-grids and with whatever small repairs we can make. I will—"

You put a finger to his lips because you cannot stand the staccato of words that promise everything and deliver uncertainty. The gesture is intimate and childish. He smiles with shock and grief all mixed together.

You: "Don't make promises you can't keep."

He shakes his head as if to dislodge the thought and steps forward, taking you into his arms. The wind pushes you both, a press of air that wants to separate you by force of weather.

Elias Park: (tighter) "I will try. I will stay long enough to make a case. I will keep them from erasing the things we care about. But Mira—I can't be both the person who runs away with you and the person who fixes the town while you leave."

You bury yourself against him. His jacket smells like the metal of tools and the sweetness of the camera oil; it is ordinary and anchoring. You tell him, in fragments, the places you will go: other towns, legal corridors, gatherings of people who make policy in languages that listen. You tell him you will fight in courtrooms where the law is written for movement and memory.

Elias Park: (breathless) "So we choose different fronts."

You: "So we choose different fronts."

The lighthouse's bell pounds somewhere far away. Your heart is a drumroll. Behind you, Marrow Bay's lights begin to blink on like small pledges in the dusk, and you understand the meaning of exile and of strategy both: one is personal, and one is communal; both are necessary and both wound you.

[Sound: The wind lifts into a raw, single keening note; the rain starts—first soft, then harder, as if the sky has decided to weep in time]
[Music: Cello breaks into a sharp, reedy chord; percussion accelerates to a raced heartbeat]

You kiss him. It is not a promise of forever. It is a promise of memory—of a future return folded into legal briefs and prototype blueprints, a vow held between brittle hands.

Elias Park: (choked laugh) "Promise me you will keep Aunt Lila fed in spirit, if not in person."

Aunt Lila: (from the path) "I'll make the beans; you two make the policies."

You laugh, and it is jagged and necessary. You pull away. He traces your coral scarf with a fingertip, reverent and small.

You have made arrangements with mentors in other towns. You have scheduled the first conference where you'll present a model of enforceable clauses that bind private funds to community stewardship. You have also arranged with Elias a plan for stewardship: local systems he will keep alive, data he will send, stories you will collect to make law human.

There is a hollowness to the decision that no number can fill. Moving trucks will come at dawn. People will leave houses and return them to a different economy, to contractors with permits who will promise stability in exchange for control. Cass's firm—Cassandra Whitlock—will oversee provisional authority. Her public face remains unreadable, her gestures precise, as if grief and profit are two separate rooms she enters with equal ease.

Cassandra Whitlock: (quiet, later, at the edge of the lantern light) "You led the town in its best moments, Mira. Your voice mattered. It still will, in other forms."

You study her face. There is something complex there—neither cruelty nor complete victory. Her eyes are cool, the way sea glass is cool after being tumbled. She offers no gloat. She offers no apology. She offers only an acknowledgement of an outcome she believes unavoidable.

You: (bitter) "You didn't just save lives. You decided which lives to concentrate elsewhere."

Cassandra Whitlock: (unwavering) "I am trying to prevent a catastrophe that would undo far more. The calculus is harsh. I'm sorry if the arithmetic eats what you cherish."

Her words do not console. They are a ledger entry in human form. You turn away, because you cannot negotiate grief with formulas.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands as you fold the coral scarf into your coat pocket; the knit catches at the edge like something refusing to be left behind]
[Sound: A truck horn blares in the distance; the tide presses at the rocks like a giant hand]

You walk down the boardwalk with a small bag and a notebook in a waterproof sleeve. People you love are either leaving in cars with children asleep across laps or staying to stand on porches that will be surveyed. The town is a spasm of movement and stillness at once.

Elias walks with you to the edge of the parking lot. He takes your face in his hands for a long moment, as if memorizing the lines that the sea has traced on you. The rain comes harder and wrecks the sound, but you can still hear his voice.

Elias Park: (steady) "When you write, send me the pages. When you find a clause that holds—call me. When you fail, call me worst. When you succeed, don't forget the smallest hands that built it."

You nod. You try to say something more. The words lodge behind the syllables—difficult to shape because they will be used, stretched, and sometimes fail.

You: "I will try. I promise to try."

He squeezes your hand hard enough that it hurts in a way that proves he's present. Then he releases you, and letting go is like learning how to fold a map you once thought you'd never need to refold.

[Music: All instruments swell into a single, aching chord; then, slowly, the sound thins to a single sustained note]
[Visual: Wide shot of the town receding, headlights threading like memory; the lighthouse a thin sentinel against the dimming sky]

You walk to the municipal shuttle—your name on a list—and for an instant the world narrows to the sound of your own boots on wet pavement and the marine-scent of air pressing against your teeth. You look back once. Elias is a dark shape among other dark shapes, hands shoved into pockets, watching a person he loves bend toward a different front.

Aunt Lila calls your name. She hugs you with the kind of hug that is an instruction manual for survival.

Aunt Lila: (gruff) "Bring me home a good story."

You: (voice small) "I will."

You step into the shuttle. It smells of engine oil and disinfectant; there is a line of faces that look as if their history has been condensed into a single suitcase. The driver closes the door with the mechanical finality of a punctuation mark.

Outside, the rain becomes a curtain sheeted across the bay. You press your forehead to the window and watch your town shrink and then fold into the distance like a page turned.

The scene does not end with dramatic fireworks. There is no sudden reconciliation, no last-minute legal reprieve. Instead there is the slow, cruel, human momentum of relocation: vans headed for temporary housing, municipal notices stapled to community boards, interviews on the news quietly translating names into numbers.

You open the Moleskine. The tide charts inside are smudged with old pencil marks. You begin a new list—contacts, legal language to refine, people to meet in other towns, dates for hearings you will push to create. The list is a map for a different kind of returning.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand writing, the ink blurring at the edge from wet fingers]
[Music: A single piano note, then silence]

You think of Elias, of Aunt Lila, of Noah and the bait shop and the potted geranium at the municipal intake table. Each memory is a small argument for stubbornness. Each is also a wound.

You do not know whether this separation will be the life-raft that keeps something alive or the wedge that splits your future. You only know that the choice was made—by circumstance, by law, by the shallow arithmetic of 'safety'—and you have chosen a response.

You close the notebook and slide it back into the waterproof sleeve. The shuttle pulls away. Through the window, the lighthouse becomes a pale wash of light and then a small, persistent dot. The rain is loud enough now to blur faces into watercolor.
