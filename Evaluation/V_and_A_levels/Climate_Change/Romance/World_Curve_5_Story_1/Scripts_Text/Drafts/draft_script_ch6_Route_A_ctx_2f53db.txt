### DRAFT
# Route: Route A


[Scene: Marisol Bay Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Salt-streaked press vans lined along the quay; strings of solar lanterns glow despite the clouded light]
[Music: Steady, warm strings under a light percussion]
[Sound: Distant camera shutters, low murmurs of a gathered crowd, gulls complaining]

You stand at the edge of the boardwalk with the sea at your back and a braided crowd in front of you. Reporters' breath fogs the air in small white puffs; a boom mic dips like an accusing finger. The footage from the stand—Jules' handheld clip of the night people refused to leave—has gone further than you planned. It has teeth now: national anchors, a thread of investigative reporting, and a brightness that makes everything you and the collective did impossible to ignore.

Your scarf is damp where it brushes your neck. The smell of diesel and wet wood mixes with the sea; the sky presses low, as if even the clouds lean in to listen. Your chest tightens in the familiar way it does before you speak to the town: not fear exactly, but the careful sharpening of someone who has to make an argument not only to be heard, but to be believed.

Jules sidles up, camera balanced on an elbow. "They're running the leaked files as a sidebar," she says, breathless in that way that makes you think she didn't sleep. "Reporter wants a quote now. Live feeds in five."

You can see Luka a few steps away, hands in his pockets, his locket catching a silver flash as he turns. He looks tired and honest and somehow, in the middle of all this bluster, profoundly small—a person you can hold in a storm. You want to go to him and say something thoughtful, something that will make the world steady again, but there are people waiting.

You look at the camera. Your voice gathers itself like tides gathering edge.

You (Amaya Reyes): "We stood our ground because Marisol Bay isn't a line item. It's a neighborhood, a childhood, a livelihood. We want protection—real protection—but it has to mean we can still live here."

Reporter: "So you reject relocation outright?"

You (Amaya Reyes): "We reject being chosen by others to lose what we built. We propose a hybrid—scaled engineering where necessary, but funded and guided by the community, with marsh restoration and shared aquaculture to keep livelihoods intact."

There is a pause long enough to let a wave of cameras swallow her next breath. Applause begins at the back of the crowd, then unwraps and spreads like a slow tide. You feel the warmth of it against your chest, as if each clap is a small patch stitched into a ragged banner.

Jules: "We pushed the files to someone at the Coastline Review. They ran the inconsistencies—old model changes, cost estimates rewritten. It makes Voss look... slippery."

You: "Slippery won't stop tides. But it changes how people see them."

Luka comes closer, breathing salt and a faint oil-scent from his tools. His voice is low. "Whatever happens, we did the right thing getting those records out."

You search his face for the hesitation you've seen before—his reluctance at confrontation—but now there is a steadier line to his mouth.

You: "We did what needed to be done. And it worked."

[Sound: A reporter's shouted question rings sharp—someone in the crowd laughs, relieved]

[Scene Transition: Fade to Black]
[Music: Brief swelling chord, then soft fade]

[Scene: TideLab | Early Evening]
[Visual: Warm amber lamps inside the repurposed shed; patches of damp earth in buckets, tide maps tacked to corrugated metal]
[Sound: The hush of people speaking in low threads; the clink of teacups; the whir of a drone docked on a bench]
[Music: Low harmonic hum—expectant but calm]

You return to TideLab with Jules and Luka trailing behind, bringing word that the national pieces are running and that calls are coming from every direction. Inside the shed the collective packs tighter than the town's footprint on a map—hands quick, voices practical. The bank of mismatched solar batteries flickers like a heartbeat.

An investigative reporter, Marina's contact, sits across from you with a tablet full of annotated PDFs. Her eyes are tired in a way that means she's been rewarded by truth, not by applause.

Marina (offscreen, through message): "They're calling me. Tamsin's on the line. She says she's being swamped."

You watch the reporter tap the screen. "Look at this," she says. "Old project memos show the original environmental impact assessments were red-lined. There's a cluster of edits that favor built infrastructure over ecological measures—exactly where the relocation language intensifies."

Your pulse picks up a little—not panic, not yet—but the kind of forward motion that translates to action. The files jut into a story headlined by real people rather than polished renderings.

Jules: "We have people ready to testify. Mateo's offered his boat for any filming. Abuela Rosa is willing to tell her storm stories on camera."

You feel the weight of each name like an oar in the hand of a rower; together, the town will move. You imagine Corinne watching from her glass pavilion—slick and precise—and the image doesn't seem as immovable as it once did.

Your phone buzzes; Tamsin's name flashes. You lift it with a thumb that is steadier now than it was in the hallways of City Hall.

Tamsin (on phone): "Amaya. I... the pressure's intense. There are calls to my office from the county and the state. Corinne's people pushed hard this morning."

You (Amaya Reyes): "Tamsin, the town doesn't need to be pushed off the map. Listen—there's a version of protection that preserves homes. If Voss scales back and funds community restoration, that protects infrastructure and lives."

Tamsin: (sighing) "You understand what I'm balancing." (beat) "But public opinion is shifting. If Voss keeps insisting on relocation when the models show other options, it's political suicide."

You can hear the relief in her voice before she finishes the sentence. "I'm taking the council off the path of blanket relocation," she says. "I don't have all the authority, but I can withdraw my recommendation. Give me the data points—I need to present alternatives."

You: "We'll compile everything. Now."

There is a collective exhale in the room. The reporter closes her tablet and rises. "Town wants a hybrid. You're giving them voices."

Jules: "Corinne issued a statement—'We will listen.' It looks staged. Her press team is being reactive."

You: "Listening is different from changing. We need commitments."

Luka: "I'll finalize the sensor package for the marsh restoration—reduce cost estimates, show how community aquaculture plugs into livelihoods. If they can see money tied to people, not just port revenue, maybe it's less abstract."

You: "Do it. I'll handle Tamsin and the council materials. Jules, you and Marina work the human stories."

The plan settles into place like a tide line. No fireworks—only the careful, steady lift of many hands.

[Scene Transition: Cut to Community Hall | Night — Council Hearing]
[Visual: The hall packed, benches heavy with people; projector casts a pale map over wet faces]
[Music: Mid-tempo strings with an insistent underbeat—tension welling toward release]
[Sound: Rain steady on the tin roof, punctuated by murmurs and a single distant siren]

You step to the podium with Luka by your side, salt drying on your jacket. The hall smells of wet wool and coffee. Council members sit under the projector's light like small islands of authority. Corinne's team fills the front row opposite, immaculate despite the weather—her steely gray eyes looking barely surprised.

Tamsin is at the council dais, tablet open. Somewhere in the room, Mateo shifts on the bench, the creak of his movement like a metronome.

You (Amaya Reyes): "Councilmembers, thank you. We brought evidence, models, and the voice of our community. We ask you to consider a hybrid solution: a reduced-footprint seawall where necessary, funded mitigation and restoration, and a community-owned aquaculture program that preserves livelihoods."

Corinne Voss stands when it's her turn. Her voice is cool, precise—an engineer used to thinking in angles.

Corinne Voss: "This company offers rapid protection. Relocation recommendations are based on models that prioritize long-term safety and economic viability."

You: "And what of the people who live here now? Lives are not models. Your plan displaces schools, fishers, families. We offered a hybrid. We offered data. We offered to partner on restoration that both protects and regenerates."

Corinne: (a small, controlled smile) "We are open to compromise, but inadequate solutions risk greater harm."

The back-and-forth stretches, careful and sharp. Questions from the council click like stones—technical and legal, moral and civic. Tamsin interjects, precise and tired.

Tamsin: "Our role is to balance immediate safety with long-term viability. Public opinion and this new evidence will weigh heavily."

Corinne's expression tightens for the first time—an almost-human fissure. A projection fails to load; her PR team whispers. The leaked memos appear on the projector due to a technical quirk; the red-lined changes hover behind her. The room leans in.

You notice the slight slump in Corinne's shoulders—she is practiced at command, not at having the floor turned like this. It's not triumph exactly; it's the relief of being met with a boundary she cannot immediately steamroll through.

Corinne Voss: (after a pause) "Given the changed context and the community's concerns, Voss Marine will propose a revised footprint. We will reallocate funding to distributed marsh restoration and offer a community aquaculture pilot with shared ownership structure. Any relocation will be minimized and conditional."

The hall takes a breath as if the words were a floodgate easing. People exchange looks: cautious joy, disbelief braided with relief. Tamsin nods, her face pale but resolved. The council murmurs, questions continue, but the shape of the debate has shifted.

You feel your knees steady under the podium. It's not a full, unreserved victory—nothing about climate work ever is—but it's the kind of hard, human result you planned for: messy, negotiated, and dignified.

Luka squeezes your elbow, a small gravity to his touch. His smile is quiet and unadorned, the lines around his eyes softening.

Luka: "We won a seat at the table."

You (Amaya Reyes): "We won a seat and the chance to build something from it."

Corinne meets your eyes across the room—only once, a measured acknowledgment. There is no theatrical apology, no villain's collapse; only the pragmatic mechanics of concession.

Corinne Voss: "We will sign off on a revised plan with community oversight. TideLab will be consulted on the pilot program."

Jules, from the back, lets out a sound halfway between a laugh and a sob.

[Music: Swell of strings resolving into a warm, open chord]
[Sound: The rain eases; the room sounds fuller, like a held breath released]

After the votes are tallied in a later, procedural session, the hall spills outward in groups—some crying, some laughing, hands finding hands like anchors. You stand on wet planks outside the hall with Luka and Mateo; Abuela Rosa wraps you both in a shawl that smells of lavender and old sea.

Abuela Rosa: (humming under her breath) "La tierra vuelve, mi niña." (The land returns, my girl.)

Mateo claps you on the shoulder in a way that drives the tiredness from your bones for a moment. "You made them listen," he says simply.

You think of the chaos you helped usher—the nights slept on cots, the viral footage that felt like exposure, the leaks and the legal pressure—and you carry a small, sharp guilt for the upheaval. People were scared. People lost income in the days the cameras were in town. Still, the town is here.

You (Amaya Reyes): (thinking) "We did not save everything. We gave ourselves a chance."

Luka tucks his hand into yours, his fingers warm and sure. "I'm not going anywhere," he says, almost in answer to your thought. "I want to stay. To build this properly."

You: "You're turning down the offers?"

Luka: (a small laugh) "Voss called again this morning. I told them no. TideLab is where I belong—if you'll have me."

You both look at TideLab's little shed—lights like a promise in the distance—and the prospect of growing it until it can hold not just data, but jobs and education and a steady, shared income.

[Scene Transition: Marshland Reserve / Restoration Site | Dawn — Weeks Later]
[Visual: Rows of newly planted seedlings; volunteers with gloves and nets; the morning fog knitting soft grays to green]
[Music: Gentle acoustic guitar with birdsong overlay]
[Sound: Spades thudding into wet earth; Abuela Rosa's low hum; the gentle lapping of water]

The community works by rhythm: laugh, plant, press soil. Abuela Rosa hums as she guides a young volunteer's hands into silt. Luka hands out tools, his pockets full of seed packets and sensor tags. Jules records a montage of hands, faces, and mud-splattered boots.

You are elbow-deep in the marsh, the cold a steady companion, and you feel the smallness of the act enormous. Each seedling is an argument with the future.

Mateo stands nearby, holding steady a bundle of stakes. "You got us more than we hoped for," he says, voice wet with the salt the air keeps putting in it. "You made them look at us like we matter."

You (Amaya Reyes): "We did it together."

Abuela Rosa: (smiling) "And the land will remember those hands."

Luka comes close, wiping mud on his knee, and takes your hand in both of his. His locket brushes your knuckles—its weight oddly comforting.

Luka: "TideLab's expansion will hire local technicians, train young people in sensors and wetland maintenance. No one gets squeezed out by profit."

You: (a quiet laugh) "That's the deal."

He leans in, forehead to yours, salt drying on both your cheeks. You think of all the compromises it took to get here—the files leaked, the legal pressure, the public stand—and also of the small things that mattered, like the way Jules filmed the child's voice-over that made people listen.

You (Amaya Reyes): (thinking) "We were messy. We were brave. We preserved this place."

[Music: A bright, uncomplicated piano motif arises—hope that is steady, not euphoric]
[Sound: Children squabbling happily in the distance as a makeshift tidepool becomes a classroom]

You stand back and watch Abuela Rosa press a seedling into the mud and hum the same song she hummed at the hall. In the distance, council members and Voss representatives kneel to help—awkward, but present. The new marsh breathes slowly into being.

You feel a softness replace the old tightness in your chest. The guilt is still there, a small stone you keep in your pocket as a reminder. It will not disappear. But beneath it—heavy and certain—is gratitude.

You (Amaya Reyes): (aloud) "Thank you."

Luka: "For what?"

You: "For staying. For making this more than an argument."

He squeezes your hand. "We're building something people can live in."

Abuela Rosa: (overhearing) "And a land that remembers us for keeping it safe."

[Visual: Wide shot of the marsh, the town, TideLab—small buildings against the taut horizon; the sun breaks through a seam in the clouds]
[Music: Strings and piano blend into a warm, steady chord]

You watch as people plant, laugh, argue, make plans. The town is not perfect; there are repairs to be made, meetings still to hold, lives to rebuild. But the arc of the work bends here toward something gentler—an infrastructure of care as much as concrete.

You breathe in the wet air and let it fill you. Luka's locket rests against your palm, its pressed kelp a small, private relic of where you came from and whom you love.

You (Amaya Reyes): (thinking) "This is not an ending. It's a beginning we can steward."

Abuela Rosa plants the last seedling and hums a final cadence. You stand together in the marsh, boots sunk into the soft future you and your neighbors are making.

[Music: Fade to a single sustained, hopeful chord]
[Sound: The tide murmurs, steady and steady]
