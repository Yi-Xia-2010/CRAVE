[Scene: Marabel Promenade | Late Afternoon — Post-Hearing]
[Visual: Grey sky split by the Beacon's cold halo; the boardwalk slick with recent drizzle. Delivery drones hum in the distance.]
[Music: Low, relentless percussion — a heartbeat doubled with distant horns]
[Sound: Drone rotors, muffled voices, an ambulance siren carried and then swallowed by wind]

You are here because the ink went where the town didn't want it to. You sit on the splintered edge of the promenade, ledger closed against your thigh like a small, useless shield, the brass of your compass warm against your palm. The sound of waves under the boards is thin today — a sibilant, indifferent whisper — but everything else is loud.

Drones slice the air in precise lines, mechanical gulls delivering packages to gated platforms you used to pass at dawn for coffee and gossip. Where once the market hummed in thick, salty conversation, there are now fewer voices and more algorithmic order: pickup windows, QR codes, covenanted entrances with card readers. A vendor stall that sold lemon-salted fish sits shuttered, a handwritten sign leaning in the doorway: "Moved — ask Mara at #12." Mara's number is a name you remember, not a place.

Your chest tightens until breathing tastes like copper. You count the missing faces like teeth. Every absence is a small eviction notice you carry inward as a wound.

Noah should be here. He said he'd come to see how the changes landed. You still have the feeling of his hand in yours at the lab window — the small, ordinary pressure of it — and how it steadied you in a way plans never could. That memory keeps wobbling against what happened: the public exposure, the lawsuits, Lila's withdrawal, and then the arrival of investors with ledger-thicker offers that smelled of profit and legalese. The Beacon's glass did not go dark; its guardians changed.

Your phone vibrates with a message you do not open. You wedge the compass back into your pocket and let the brass press against the fabric, a private weight. Around you, people move differently — their steps measured, eyes flicking up at security cams, then away. You can smell diesel from the supply trucks, the sharp tang of ozone from the Beacon's sensor arrays, and underneath it all the persistent brine you have known since childhood.

You chose — or you didn't choose — but the hum continues. The promenade crowd parts and reforms like tide. A group of older residents gather near the rail, passing around a thermos and the fragile courage that comes from shared loss. One of them taps the ledger with a knuckle and says something in the local dialect that lands like a small apology for time. You nod because that's what people do when grief is practical.

The Beacon is no longer just an idea; it is administration, and administration has grown teeth. Where once community boards posted flyers for seed exchanges, there are now color-printed notices about "managed access" and "resident verification." The words are sharp, clean — sterile as the Beacon's glass.

[Scene: The Beacon Plaza | Early Evening — Outside the Managed-Access Gate]
[Visual: Floodlights wash the plaza in hard white; private security vests contrast with rain-soaked volunteers' jackets. A placard reads: 'Resilience Partners — Community Integration Initiative.']
[Music: Crescendoing strings with brittle electronic interference]
[Sound: Murmured arguments, a PA repeating calming phrases, a protest chant like a distant wave]

You walk the plaza's rim like someone checking wounds. Mayor Rosa stands near the gate, hands clasped white-knuckled, mouthing legalese she doesn't entirely believe in. Lila is not visible. Instead, there are new faces — polished, sleek, investors with umbrellas and eyes that measure every person as a potential metric.

And then you see Noah.

He stands by a temporary table branded with the consortium's logo. He is framed by the Beacon's light, which flattens him into silhouette and policy. His hair is damp at his temples; there's a restraint in him that wasn't there under the lab's softer lights. When he sees you, something in him shifts — relief, shame, an attempt at steadiness that is also a calculation.

Noah Reyes [soft]: "Asha."

Asha Moreno: "You took the meeting."

Noah Reyes [soft]: "They offered me... they asked me to help as technical director. I—" His voice frays. "I thought I could keep oversight from inside. I thought I could stop the worst of it."

Asha Moreno: "You thought you could keep us safe by signing away the town."

Noah Reyes: "I didn't sign anything. We negotiated terms. We got a seat at governance tables. I can argue for community quotas, for subsidized access. I can—"

You interrupt, the words a staccato tide. "Quotas don't help families who had to take buyouts. Quotas don't replace the market stalls that were someone's entire income. Quotas are a spreadsheet. People are not cells in a spreadsheet, Noah."

Noah Reyes swallows, and for a heartbeat he looks like the man you leaned on in quieter storms. Then he breathes out, a sound boxed by obligation.

Noah Reyes [quiet, urgent]: "Because if I am inside, I can fight for oversight that actually has teeth. If I stay, I can make sure they release sensor data publicly, that community seats aren't symbolic. I can't do that from outside."

Asha Moreno: "At what cost?" Your voice scrapes. "You become part of the mechanism that makes it impossible for my neighbor to stay. How is that softening?"

The plaza noise rises behind your argument: someone yelling about covenants, a child crying, the metallic click of a gate closing. Noah's jaw tightens.

Noah Reyes [quiet, urgent]: "So what should I have done? Walked away? Watched private money steamroll us because I'd rather be pure than useful?"

Asha Moreno: "Maybe you could have asked us first."

Noah Reyes: "We tried—" His hands open, a gesture of caught bargains and cramped options. "You know how meetings run. Things change overnight. The lawsuits, the funding gaps after Lila pulled out — the town needed something, Asha. I blackmailed myself into being useful. I don't like the lines that got drawn. But being at the table means I can soften them."

Asha Moreno: "You made a choice to be inside a system that already decided who mattered."

Noah Reyes: "I'm trying to keep people from being erased." He steps closer. "I'm trying to keep you from being the only one who burns for this town."

Asha, your hands are damp. Your fingers find the compass in your pocket and close around it hard enough to bruise. You feel like you're holding a small relic of a past cartography that no longer maps this place.

Noah reaches for your hand. For a split second, you see the warmth you once found in his palm, the history that could have knitted you back together. He is pleading, not with slogans but with that human, dangerous thing: the desire for connection.

Noah Reyes [pleading]: "Stay with me here. Work with me. We can do this together. Please."

Your throat closes. The sound of the Beacon's floodlights hum like an electric tide. Around you, someone shouts, "They sold the promenade!" as if sale is a single act and not the slow accretion of policies. The air tastes of ozone and old bread and a sorrow you cannot bargain with.

Asha Moreno [brittle]: "Working with you here is not the same as being with you." The words crack like thin ice. "I can't watch them turn our neighbors into case studies and call it safety."

Noah Reyes [voice breaking]: "We can make the plaque say our names because we kept people safe. Isn't that—"

Asha Moreno: "Not if the people aren't allowed to see the names anymore."

He doesn't answer. The crowd presses closer, a living pressure. A woman passes with a cardboard box of what looks like a lifetime of photographs and watches you as if she expects you to split in two and give both halves away. You have nothing for her except your hands and the logistical knowledge of marsh restoration that may not save her house now that access is restricted.

[Scene: Floating Market & Raised Gardens | Dusk — Packing Up]
[Visual: Solar lanterns lighting pockets of people; empty planters and tarps flapping where tables used to be. Delivery drones silhouette the sky like hunting stars.]
[Music: High, thin violin note repeated, a pulse that never resolves]
[Sound: The slap of canvas, the creak of pulleys, distant vehicle horns]

You move through the market with a pack on your back. People hand you leftover jars of pickled fruit, a thermos, a child's stuffed fish whose seam has been soldered a hundred times. Their eyes say what they cannot afford to say aloud: Can you fix us? Will you try?

You sit with families on raised platforms as volunteers argue with municipal clerks who read out the covenants with the softness of people reciting phrases from a storybook. A man you taught cordgrass seeding tips to in exchange for coffee uses his last hour to teach you how to braid supports for a floating bed. You bind the ropes until your fingers sting.

The Beacon glows across the water, a lighthouse for a different kind of navigation. It is not evil exactly; it is simply a machine that responds to incentives. The machine repaired the broken seawall, but it also patented water access and rerouted foot traffic for "efficiency."

You feel very tired.

Noah finds you again at the market edge. He stands awkwardly between a pallet of community soil and a newly-installed sensor kiosk. He has a list of compromises he is trying to make into something meaningful, and in the light his eyes are red-rimmed.

Noah Reyes: "They'll let me put through the community tech liaison program. I'll have the authority to fund small restoration pilots. I'll—"

Asha Moreno: "You will also be measuring our losses on their dashboards."

Noah Reyes: "Is that worse than watching them take everything? Asha, please."

You press the compass into your palm. It is cool now, and the frayed cord rubs the same line into your skin as when it was your father's. The helm of a boat is not a map of how a person should live their life, but it is honest in its angles. Something in you wants the truth of that work.

A child brushes past, leaving fingerprints on your sleeve. You laugh, but it is thin. Around you the market folds into a muted geometry of departures.

Noah Reyes [soft]: "I don't want to lose you."

Asha Moreno: "You already are losing me, if you don't see how far the Beacon's safeguards go."

He reaches out again. You let your hand close over his for a moment, not meeting his eyes. There is no melodramatic shout, no cinematic crescendo of reconciliation. There is only the tactile fact of his fingers in yours and the knowledge that you are letting go.

You stand. The air is raw and charged, filling the space between you with something like finality. The tide pushes a small slick of foam against the pilings and then draws away, indifferent.

Noah Reyes: "Asha—"

Asha Moreno: "Noah." The name is not an endearment. It is an address, a geography. "I will teach people to read the tide and the contract. I will go where the Beacon doesn't reach. I will make itinerant shore-training centers. I will keep the ledger honest."

Noah Reyes [pleading]: "Come back if... if it becomes a place you can come home to."

Asha Moreno: "Maybe." The maybe is a stone. It will sink or it will not.

[Scene: Promenade Edge | Night — Leaving]
[Visual: The Beacon's halo diminishes into a distant, insistently white constellation. Your silhouette is small against the open water. Behind you, the prom carries the new pattern of lights and gated access; ahead, a different shore waits in the dark.]
[Music: One long, sustained cello note that unravels into silence]

You walk away from the Beacon's glow. Your compass thunks against your thigh with each step, a metronome counting the distance. The promenade underfoot is the same wood you grew up on and yet it rings with newness — it carries the memory of what used to be and does not promise return.

You do not look back when Noah calls your name once more. You do not turn. Your shoulders are heavy, and your breath makes small clouds in the humid air. Around you the town rearranges itself into a new geometry where safety has a price and some people pay it with houses, others with memories.

You move toward a different shore, toward vans you'll outfit with living-shore kits, toward places where the law still bites and the tide still speaks. You will be itinerant, teaching people to stitch cordgrass, to raise platforms, to read the slow language of marsh and water. It is not everything. It may be too late for some. It will not be the Beacon's model, but it will be honest and salted and human.

Your heart is raw, but it keeps moving. Love is not annihilated here; it is reformed into a ache you will carry like coral: beautiful, sharp, useful as a lesson.

You stop once at the rail and take the compass out. The brass is scratched, warm with the friction of your grip. You press your thumb to it as if to remember an old map. When you tuck it back in, it swings once and settles.

You take the first step toward the ferry landing. Behind you, the Beacon's light bleaches the pier. Ahead, a different dark waits, the promise of work that will be small and immediate and stubborn.

You do not know if the town will keep its heart. You know that you will not be there when the new plaques go up. You know that some people will be able to live in their houses and some won't. You know also — indelibly — that the cost of safety has been paid in names and kitchen tables and neighborly breakfasts that will not resume.

You walk into the night, and the sound of the sea rises to meet you: indifferent, relentless, honest.
