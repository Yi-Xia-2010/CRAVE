### DRAFT
# Route: Additional / Ending (Sad)


[Scene: Promenade | Early Evening]
[Visual: The Beacon's halo glows across the water; puddles mirror its white ring]
[Music: Urgent strings; a staccato electronic pulse underlies the air]
[Sound: Phone buzzes in the background, gulls cut through wind]

You press the compass harder against your throat because it feels like the only honest weight you still own. The cord bites a shallow line in the skin. Behind the glass of the Beacon, words you signed for—phrases you let sit in legal soil because you needed time—are surfacing in the wrong light.

Your phone vibrates again. The screen blooms with a thread: a leaked clause flagged, bolded, quoted—"investor claims under unforeseen exigency." The headline is a cold pulse on the town's sleep. Comments tumble in; someone posts annotated screenshots of the contract. Somewhere, a property group's logo appears, tagging waterfront titles for review. Insurance companies send terse notices: premiums recalculated, rates subject to immediate reassessment.

The world tilts. Not literally—just the map you were holding. You taste metal, panic thin and bright at the back of your tongue.

Noah is at your shoulder before you realize you've looked for him. His hand finds the small of your back, then steadies on your shoulder—warm, solid against the damp of your jacket. The gesture is simple but precise: a human brace against a machine-cold night.

Noah: "They're amplifying the worst line. They'll spin fear into a firewall between neighbors." (He rubs his thumb in a small, deliberate circle on your shoulder.) "We can do two things right now: hold the facts up where everyone can see them, or let rumors do the shaping."

You want to say the thing you usually keep in the ledger—how legalese can become a weapon if left alone—but the storm is already unspooling overhead and your voice is a thread against thunder.

You: "If people panic about titles, they'll sell, or they'll take mortgages they can't afford. They won't wait for audits. They won't wait for lawyers. They will move to survive the present." (You let anger and exhaustion lace the words.) "We have to stop the panic before it eats the town."

Noah: (breath close to yours) "Then we anchor them. Transparency, now. A public thread with the exact clause, line by line, and our—" (he catches himself) "—and our explanation. And a meeting tonight. Mayor Rosa needs to speak."

You nod, but your fingers tighten on the compass until the brass is warm. The Beacon's feed lights up your face with a cold, indifferent white. Even that light feels like it belongs to someone else.

[Scene: Boardwalk & Tidewatch Lab | Minutes Later]
[Visual: The tide is higher than the models showed; test wall sensors flash red]
[Music: Percussive drums, tempo accelerating]
[Sound: Distant sirens, the groan of timbers, a sharp crack as a test wall gives]

The Beacon doesn't argue with physics. Its sensors splutter a live feed: pressure spiking, displacement vectors increasing—numbers curling into urgent red. Someone in the lab shouts. Screens reflect the line of water as it finds the weak seam in the test wall. Concrete shears, and a sheet of cold saltwater lances across the temporary barrier.

A taste of salt fills your mouth just before the air fills with the sound of people shouting. Volunteers are already running—Marta's voice cutting orders over the din, Eli hauling a line of sandbags like an animal with a harness, elders bundled into blankets on an upturned boat.

Marta: (shouting, precise, fierce) "Raised beds now! Move the seedlings! People, get the tarps—make the gardens into shelters. Kids and elders to the center of every plot. Use the pallets as ramps."

Eli: (grunting as he throws another sandbag) "Asha! I can ferry Mrs. Gutierrez and her twins—pray there's room. The current's already back-swirling."

You step into the squeeze of bodies and water-scented air. The night is all hands and feet and wet cotton. The compass swings against your sternum like a pendulum counting down. You bark instructions you've practiced a thousand times in drills, then adapt in real time—redirect volunteers to the most vulnerable seams, prioritize the elder houses on stilts.

You: "Noah, get the public address on the Promenade, now—calm, facts, where to go. Marta, hold the gardens as triage. Eli, take the lighter skiff and start near the old boatyard; I'll mark the elders who have mobility issues."

Noah: (already moving, voice low but steady) "I'll tell them which streets are blocked, who's sheltering where, and that food is coming. Keep your voice calm. Tell them steps, not certainties. Certainty is what we can't offer."

You taste diesel and fear. A wave of water slaps at your boots, sending a spray over your calves. You clamp your jaw and hear the Beacon ping, ping—engineers at Lila's end scrambling incident reports into the night.

Marta rushes up to you, soil-smudged gloves white with grit.

Marta: (breathless) "We can crate the seedlings higher, use the coils of garden hose as floats, push the lighter ones to the plaza. But the greenhouse frames by the west lane—badly anchored. If that goes—"

You: "Anchor those frames now. Eli, use the spare joists as ballast." (You hand Marta a roll of bright orange cord.) "Make the categories—mobility, medical needs, children. We can't triage people like paperwork."

Eli: (eyes on the dark water) "We lost one test mooring earlier—thought we had a margin. The models were wrong."

Your stomach drops the way it does before a wave crests. The models were your language; they were supposed to be reliable. The Beacon recorded everything in excruciating detail: the wall's moment of failure, the millisecond the water breached, the lag in the automated pumps as code caught up to motion.

[Scene: Beacon / Tidewatch Lab Entrance | Simultaneous]
[Visual: Engineers clustered in the lab, tablets blinking; Lila's badge reflected in a screen]
[Music: A single high sustained note, then a rising electronic arpeggio]
[Sound: Message notifications, anxious murmurs, the distant hammer of work]

You catch a glimpse of Lila's name in your feed—an apologetic video message, then raw, real-time: her engineers flooded with incident reports, hands flying across keyboards. Her face is lit from below by her studio lights; the background is a constellation of code and maps.

Lila: (on the message) "Asha—Noah—I'm watching the feed. We're mobilizing field teams. I'm sorry about the clause leak; we are reviewing it internally. Right now the priority is stabilizing the breach. We can get heavy pumps, modular pontoons, emergency materials on site within the hour. Please—let us help."

Her apology is a polished thing: words arranged to fit optics and urgency. There is sincerity there, honest enough to sting, but also the tidy efficiency of a corporation used to handling crises as operations. You hear the engineers in the lab mutter about incident queues and liability matrices.

Noah: (coldly practical) "Pumps and pontoons buy time. But they also buy leverage—if we let their help become the only scaffold, they set the precedent for control."

You: "If the pumps work and people stop losing houses tonight, what didn't we pay? Pride? Autonomy? We'll write it in the ledger later. For now—" (you look at the flood of messages from residents) "—we keep people safe."

Mayor Rosa's voice cuts through on a conference line—she sounds older on the phone, steadier, the mayoral cadence tightened like rope.

Mayor Rosa: "We need a centralized plan. If Lila can get assets here fast, we accept resources but demand chain-of-custody, public audits, and an immediate suspension of any clause invoking investor seizure while emergency measures are active. We will not sign anything else tonight. Asha, Noah, is that doable?"

You hear the tremor under her authority; the strain of keeping a town's ledger from tipping into panic. Your throat tightens.

Noah: "We can demand those terms. Press them publicly now; make the community witnesses to every move. Transparency nails their hands to visibility."

You think of the leak again—the thread already spooling conspiracy-minded commentary. There is a lure to exposing the clause and forcing Lila on the defensive. There is also the water taking house porches, inventing new desperation.

Marta, hands trembling, turns to you and Noah.

Marta: "If we litigate now, will we have docks to keep people on tonight? I can argue contract law tomorrow—but tonight, the greenhouse frames need ballast." (She looks at you, eyes raw.) "What do we pick first?"

You feel the room's pressure focus into a single point: choice. It's a clean, terrible geometry—safety now with external strings, political war that could cut funding, or throw everything into a risky natural restoration that might fail under the storm's teeth.

Eli returns from a run with a soaked blanket and the scent of diesel; his jaw is stubbled, his breath rough.

Eli: (quiet, resigned) "We can try to build the marsh back faster—root more cordgrass, stack coir logs—but it will take hands and it will take time. Time is the enemy tonight."

The Beacon's live feed shows the breach stabilizing into a new pattern—a scar of displaced water, a place the town must now reckon with. The sensors hum a diagnostic lament; every number is a voice counting loss and margin.

You press the compass to your chest. The brass is slick and warm. Your heart is a storm of its own.

Noah's hand slides from your shoulder to grip your palm. His fingers are callused, his grip steady. You can feel his pulse, an anchor beneath the chaos.

Noah: (low, almost a confession) "Whatever we choose—whichever path—this will change the shape of what we call 'ours.' I'm not ready to lose the parts of Marabel that aren't on maps: porch music, the raised gardens, old boat names scratched on hulls. Neither are you."

You: (a whisper that tastes like salt and fear) "I know. And I'm trying to keep both—safety and the soft edges. But sometimes you have to pick which to put first."

The wind tilts the lamppost light; someone rings a bell to call volunteers. The town is a chorus of urgent tasks, each voice a thread pulling in a different moral direction. The leak's thread continues to burn online; the legal phrase—"unforeseen exigency"—sits like a splinter under the town's skin. Insurance notices continue to arrive. Elderly homeowners clutch deeds as if paper could hold back the sea.

Your chest hurts. Each option tastes like sacrifice.

[Music: Crescendo—strings and electronic percussion driving forward; heartbeats sync with the rhythm]
[Sound: The ocean's roar, a chorus of shouted instructions, an ambulance far off]

You look at the list of options forming between you like trapdoors. Each will change who holds the town's future.

[Scene: Promenade | Night Deepening]
[Visual: The Beacon's halo fractures in the rain; the Promenade is ankle-deep in water in places]
[Music: Tense single-note ostinato, then abrupt silence as voices lean in]
[Sound: One long gull cry, then a press of human breath]

You feel Noah's hand as the last steady point before the decision. The town waits—tense, raw, and impossibly vulnerable. Your mind races, calculating risks in a flurry of practical matrices and moral impression. Your compulsion to protect tugs in three directions and the tide pushes from the fourth.

You know you must choose what to prioritize now: immediate physical safety with corporate help; public legal exposure and political fight; or a collective bet on nature that may or may not hold.
