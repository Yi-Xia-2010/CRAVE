### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C — Alt
# Context: Start

[Scene: Elara Marina Boardwalk | Before Dawn]

[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Sound: Slow, distant gulls. A soft drip from the eaves. Quiet footsteps on wet wood.]
[Music: Low, breathing ambient — nothing urgent, just the pulse of morning.]

You wake before the town remembers itself. Your hands find the familiar grooves of your boots — leather softened by salt, laces frayed where they've rubbed the eyelets a thousand times — and you pull them tight with the same careful rhythm you used when you were a child learning to knot rope.

The coral-shaped pendant swings against your collar. It rubs, warm from your skin, a tiny, recycled weight that keeps being beautiful no matter how many storms it has weathered. You don't think about where it came from; you think, instead, of the smallness of keeping something whole by carrying it.

[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: The muffled churn of the sea. A distant door closing.]

You step onto the boardwalk. Wet wood smells like old memory: tar, salt, the iron tang of stormwater. Your field notebook is tucked under your arm, its duct-taped cover puckered with salt streaks. You flip it open, thumb pressed to a page with crude tide markers and a quick line drawing of a channel you were meant to survey. Your handwriting is small and deliberate; the ink blurs at the edges where moisture found it yesterday.

You make notes — times, subtle changes, the way the current has been cheek to jaw with the marsh channels — because writing it down is the only way to cheat time: to pin the sea, for a moment, to paper.

[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]
[Sound: Cloth shifting as he pulls his cap lower.]

Aiden Kuro is there before you reach the end of the pier, a figure you've known long enough to read like tide marks. His knit cap sits low. He squints into the fog as if it were a stubborn neighbor refusing to speak. When he notices you, there's a small, almost private easing of his shoulders, and the guarded set in his amber eyes softens — just a fraction.

Maya Soler: "Morning."
Aiden Kuro: "Morning." (He tilts his head, letting the sound of the word hang between you.) "You walk early even for you."

You laugh, a sound that settles like a stone into still water. "Couldn't sleep."

Aiden Kuro studies your notebook, then the pendant at your throat. He doesn't reach for either; his hands stay planted in his pockets, fingers curled around a piece of rope as if to test its thickness. He asks what you found, slow and practical, like a fisherman asking about the wind.

Aiden Kuro: "How's the marsh looking?"
Maya Soler: "Tighter in the channels than last week. Sediment's shifting more to starboard — not much, but enough. If we don't get roots back in place they'll start eating the banks."

Aiden Kuro nods, as if that is both comforting and worrying at once. His reply comes in pieces, measured.

Aiden Kuro: "Noor mentioned a pilot at Saltmarsh. You think it'll hold?"
Maya Soler: "It can. If we give the reeds a chance to do what they do. Roots bind the silt. It's slow work, but it's honest."

He watches the horizon — or the idea of one through the fog — and you read his mouth the way you'd read a map. His concern isn't abstract. It lands somewhere that hurts his hands: livelihoods, boats, the sound of nets being mended at dawn.

Aiden Kuro: "I don't like the look of Voss showing up this week." (He lets the name sit a moment.) "Promises people money. Promises quick fixes. You remember what happened after that other 'rescue' up north."

You think of ruined docks and empty sheds, of speeches about progress that sounded like hammers over laughter. The memory stings, but it is also precise: the shape of a town after it has been told to move on.

Maya Soler: "I remember." (Your voice is softer than you'd expect.) "That's why Noor wants the living breakwater pilot. She thinks a controlled start — small, monitored — will show the difference."

[Background:"ch1_Start_4"]
[Sound: Gentle vibration.]

Your phone buzzes in your pocket. It's Noor. You thumb it open. Her voice is clipped with warmth and a scientist's steadiness.

Prof. Noor: "Maya — are you at the pier? I wanted to flag that I spoke to the grant committee. They like the outline, but they want a clear monitoring plan. Can you draft something today? Something simple and reassuring."

Maya Soler: "Yes. I'll sketch one now. Keep it simple and measurable. Humidity, turbidity, root cover... what else?"

Prof. Noor: "Faunal return. We need to show life comes back — small things at first. Birds, invertebrates. And photos. Lots of photos. I'll be by the lab mid-morning."

Her words are practical and buoyant; they hold a promise, small and firm, like a stone under water. You feel a steadiness settle into your chest at her certainty.

Maya Soler: "Alright. See you at Tidewatch."
Prof. Noor: "Bring coffee if you can. And Maya — don't carry this alone."

Her last sentence lands differently than the rest, not a scientific parameter but a soft order. You turn the idea over in your mouth like a pebble: don't carry this alone. It's simple and impossible at once.

Aiden Kuro watches the exchange, then the pendant at your throat, then the boardwalk stretching back toward the town. He says nothing for a long beat — the kind of silence that is not empty but thick with thought.

Aiden Kuro: "You've been... carrying it for a long time, haven't you?"
Maya Soler: (You close your notebook, the pages making a small, damp sigh.) "Since the flood. Since my brother. Carrying the weight felt like the only work that made sense."

He doesn't pry. He folds his hands over one another, fingers finding the carved wooden compass he keeps on his person. His voice is low.

Aiden Kuro: "You don't have to fix everything to keep it together. Sometimes keeping things together is steady work — mending nets, planting roots — not a headline."

You look at him. There's a particular kind of honesty in how he says it, one that doesn't try to solve, only to anchor.

Maya Soler: "That's the plan. Slow, steady. Roots, not walls."

Aiden Kuro: "And if Voss brings walls?" (The question is a stone skipped across water; it asks for distance but lands anyway.) "What happens to the people who need the slow work to make a living?"

You feel a small tightening — old, familiar. You could answer with policy or theory, with models and charts, but answers like that feel like folding hands over a wound. Instead you let yourself imagine reed roots binding silt like fingers lacing around the town's ankle, patient and stubborn.

Maya Soler: "Then we show them the evidence. We show the difference. We do the pilot Noor signed off on. We make the town see the future we want."

Aiden Kuro watches you make that pledge, not with theatrical dramatics but as if you're setting a lantern on a windowsill. It will be there to guide, not to blind.

[Background:"ch1_Start_5"]
[Sound: Faint rustle of reed tips, almost inaudible. The sea sighs.]

You let yourself picture the Saltmarsh in slow focus: shallow channels like breath-lines, reed heads tipped in russet, small birds like punctuation marks along the edges. Your imagination arranges the living breakwater in hands and roots rather than concrete and steel.

Your braid swings when you tilt your head. The motion is small but precise; the braid has always been a tether to self, a repetition that steadies mornings like this. You roll your olive-green jacket sleeves up to your elbows, feeling the damp air on your forearms. Salt settles in the seams of your jacket and the tiny folds of your notebook's tape.

No one passes you on the boardwalk. Shop shutters are tied down; the café's warm halo holds a single chair and the promise of later customers. The town is tired but alive — a lung that inhales slowly and exhales resolve.

Maya Soler: "Do you want to walk the marsh with me later? Noor's asking for boots and good questions."

Aiden Kuro considers, then nods. His agreement is measured, like someone deciding whether to haul a net or mend it. There is something in the decision — a small motion toward shared labor — that feels like the opening of a conversation that might, in time, be more than practical.

Aiden Kuro: "I'll be there. Noon. Bring whatever samples you want me to carry."

You exchange a look that says more than the words: you will both be at the edges of this plan, practical and wary, an unspoken promise to take the shorelines into your hands together. It is not dramatic. It is not a vow with fireworks. It is a small, steady thing.

You think of your brother then, and the flood that took him away. Guilt has been a companion you wear like an old coat — familiar, heavy at the shoulders. It has made you tireless in ways that both save and cost you. For a moment you stand very still and let the memory come and go like a net left in the shallows; you don't untangle it, you only let it be part of the tide.

[Background:"ch1_Start_6"]

You write Elias Voss's name last, the letters small and neat. The word carries a different temperature: polished, confident, a man who will show glossy renderings of seawalls and promenades, who will speak of economic lift and immediate protection. You can already hear the applause in a room you haven't yet entered.

You close the notebook. The sea holds its breath for just a beat longer than the streetlights, and then the light shifts — not suddenly, but as if a curtain has been eased aside.

The day feels like a question, as the outline of what must be done and what might be lost sits between you and the horizon. You can feel the story beginning at the waterline: small adjustments, slow commitments, the first threaded steps of a plan that will ask for patience and courage in turns.

[Sound: The soundscape narrows to the quiet of pockets of bird calls and the soft scrape of your boots.]
[Music: The music settles into a single, steady note — calm, expectant.]

You tuck the notebook back under your arm, ease the pendant against your collar with an absent thumb, and start walking toward the town, toward Tidewatch and the conversations that will shape the week.
