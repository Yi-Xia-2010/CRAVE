# Route: Route C (Ending)


[Scene: Main Street | Morning]
[Visual: Wet cobbles, brass compass on a leather cord swinging against patched cargo knee]
[Music: Low, steady hum — gentle piano with distant marine wind]
[Sound: Distant gull call, muffled footfalls on damp wood]

You walk the length of Main Street with the brass compass against your thigh, its small weight a metronome against the rhythm in your chest. The harbor smells of kelp and diesel and the cinnamon warmth spilling from Rosa’s café; steam threads the air into small, honest clouds. Your braid clings damp against your neck. The morning is neither bright nor dark — washed in the gray that has become this town’s normal — and it presses you into a careful, folding quiet.

Rosa’s door opens before you can knock. She has flour on her cheek and her usual shawl like a stubborn flag. Her hands, warm and slightly stained with coffee, close around yours without asking. The press of palm to palm steadies you the way nothing else can.

Rosa: "There you are, niña. You look like you slept with charts in your brain." (half-teasing, half-worried)

You try to smile. "I wished I had," you say, and the words land smaller than you expect.

Rosa: "How is Tomas?" (eyes searching)

You feel the notebook heavier at your hip, every page a small ledger of worry. Tomas — your brother — has been in the orbit of Aurelia's offers, of movers who measure boxes and futures in months. You picture him in his repair shop, hands raw from work and decisions, jaw set like the doorway he often leans against.

You: "He’s... considering options. He’s stubborn in the way that makes me proud and terrified." (you let a laugh out that is more like a breath)

Rosa squeezes your hand. The café’s air is thick with roasted beans and cardamom; the string of fairy lights in the window casts halos on her wrinkled smile.

Rosa: "Tell him for me — whatever he decides, there'll always be coffee here." (beat) "And tell him to stop using my tables for welding, eh?" (a small deflection, and you both chuckle; the laugh lands like a patch)

Rosa: "Good. Now go. Eyes open, Maya." (her humor tries to armor concern)

You leave with the warmth still pressed into your palm. The compass swings once more, then settles. Main Street narrows toward Town Hall: a low brick building whose steps have been smoothed by a hundred meetings, a hundred weeks where people came together to decide how to keep their homes intact.

[Scene: Town Hall — Exterior Steps | Late Morning]
[Visual: Damp stone steps, umbrellas folded, a few clusters of neighbors speaking in low voices]
[Music: Soft, contemplative strings — a slow build]
[Sound: Murmur of conversation, umbrellas clicking shut]

Under Marco's umbrella, he arrives precisely on schedule, the blazer more immaculate than the weather allows. He moves through the crowd with the night-side confidence of someone practiced at small shows of control.

Councilor Marco: "Good morning, everyone. Thank you for coming." (measured, the kind of voice that fills rooms politely)

His greeting is a thin shield. You can see the way some faces relax and others tighten. Marco's vote is the hinge; he knows it, they all know it. You feel Asha's presence like an anchor at your back — quiet in a way that cuts through the room's noise.

Asha: "We should listen to every proposal fully," she says to you as she joins, voice like worn sea glass. "The science needs to be heard, but the people must be seen."

You nod. Her certainty is small and steady; it keeps you tethered beneath the tilt of your worry.

[Scene: Town Hall — Back Room | Midday]
[Visual: Warm with bodies, string-light haze, folding chairs in rows; a projector flickers to life]
[Music: Low, steady ostinato — the tempo inches up]
[Sound: Soft rustle of paper, chairs creaking, a cup set down carefully]

June Park steps in like a clean line drawn across the room: trench coat clasped, briefcase clicked closed. The silver streak at her temple catches the light like a punctuation. Her presence cuts a different sort of certainty—engineered, polished, no room for fog.

June: "Thank you, Councilor Lin, and thank you, San Brisa," she begins, voice even and practiced. "Aurelia's seawall is a proven solution. We have modeled storm resistance, insurance reductions, and timelines that protect your property values immediately."

She clicks and the projector hums. Steel pylons rise on the screen. Diagrams march in tidy rows: expected wave heights, cost-benefit analyses, insurance-savings charts. The language is clinical. 'Protection,' 'mitigation,' 'efficiency.'

You feel your notebook grow heavier in your hands, though it's unchanged; the weight is a thought that sits in your chest. Words slide into you — 'immediate,' 'certainty' — and though you know the numbers, you also know what those pylons look like: a blunt edge between people and ocean, a boundary that keeps some things safe by divorcing others.

[Sound: A low, focused murmur as people take in the slides]
[Visual: Close on audience faces — worry, relief, calculation]

Eli is there, sleeves rolled as if he'd been pulled from a workbench and set in the front row. His paint-speckled fingers are as you remembered, tucking a folding timber ruler into a back pocket on instinct. When June concludes, he stands with the easy gravity you have come to rely on.

Eli: "Clinically tidy, yes," he says, voice warm but not without edge. "But we need to think about the living systems those pylons replace. Living shorelines—marsh restoration, engineered reefs—absorb energy and preserve access. They’re not just ecological ornament; they’re long-term resilience."

June: "Mr. Navarro, with respect, living systems don't provide the same immediate, quantifiable protection for high-value commercial zones. Time matters when banks demand certainty and insurers calculate premiums."

Eli: "And time also matters when you push people out of neighborhoods to save profit centers. We aren't asking to ignore immediate needs. We're proposing phased pilot projects that demonstrate function — we can model for insurers and show staged results."

June: "Pilot projects are costly and uncertain. Aurelia's approach secures the town now."

Eli: "Secures, or shifts risk elsewhere?" (he presses) "We can design prototypes that are quicker and cheaper than you assume. We have skilled people here. We can prove it."

June's mouth thins. The exchange is measured but the subtext is sharp. Around you, murmurs ripple like a distant tide—approval and skepticism braided together.

Tomas stands at the room's threshold like a hinge. He doesn't enter fully; he lets the door frame hold him. His hands are the hands that fixed your mother's porch, the hands that steady the town's small machinery. The decision today is not just policy — it's a ledger of the life he keeps.

You think of Rosa's coffee-stained smile, of Asha's patient insistence, of Eli's easy gestures. The room divides between those who want solidity now and those who wager on a different future. Every proposal reads like a promise and a threat at once.

Councilor Marco clears his throat. He folds his umbrella and, for a moment, smells like wet wool and rain. The room listens.

Councilor Marco: "Both proposals raise valid points. The question facing us is which path protects our town and its people in the short and mid term." (a pause, careful) "I want to hear from the community. Let us proceed with questions."

June nods, composed; Eli leans forward, elbows splayed, ready. Voices that had been murmurs step into the room like the slow climbing of a tide — careful, edged with worry.

You feel your pulse shift from a steady thrum to a quiet, urgent tapping. It is not loud enough to startle anyone; it is only a reminder under the skin. The options in the room are not theoretical anymore. You picture Tomas's shop window, the salt-etched tools, the list of customers pinned beneath a magnet.

Asha touches your sleeve in a wordless gesture that says: remember the data, remember the people.

You prepare to speak — to thread the numbers with the memories, to make the scientific plausible and the human present — and the room leans in.

[Visual: Close on your hand tightening on the notebook; the brass compass swings once, slow]
[Music: Soft, unresolved chord — the tempo holds, building subtly]
[Sound: A collective inhale, like the slow collecting of breath before a long exhale]

The decision is about to be framed; the first public opinion will land like a stone. You stand on the edge of that moment, feeling all the small things that have led here — guilt, hope, a tether of loyalty to a town that has given you everything and asked for too much in return.

You are ready to speak. The room waits.
