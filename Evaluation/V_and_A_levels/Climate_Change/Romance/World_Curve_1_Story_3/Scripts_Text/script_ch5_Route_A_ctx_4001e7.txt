[Scene: Reclaimed Lot Near the Old Pier | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide on the reclaimed lot — a patchwork of sandbags, crabgrass, and newly planted cordgrass; the old pier crouches in the background like a waiting thing]
[Music: Quickening acoustic percussion with warm strings]
[Sound: Distant metallic clang of sheet piles, gulls, the wet slap of tide on wood]

You step off the boardwalk and your boots sink an inch into loamy silt that smells of iron and old seaweed. The air here hangs heavy with salt and the diesel tang from Aurelia's site across the water. Your braid is a loose rope at the nape of your neck, a few strands whipped free by the breeze; they stick to your cheek as you lean over the sensor array.

The field vest pockets rattle with the little lives of a project: Asha's annotated pages folded into thirds, a roll of flagging tape, a fat mechanical pencil, and the retro brass compass that feels absurdly ceremonial in your palm. You taste the copper of urgency; your throat is dry, but the work clears it out like a bell.

[Visual: Close on your hands adjusting the salinity probe; droplets bead and run into the sensor housing]
[Sound: A small electric beep as the probe takes a stable reading]

Eli Navarro crouches nearby, timber ruler across his knee, sketching a cross-section into a damp notepad. His lines are confident, impatient — the kind of drafting that believes in things that can be touched. He hums under his breath, a loose rhythm that matches the tide. When he looks up, sea-glass green in the fading light, his smile is small and sharp: the kind that says we'll improvise until the tide listens.

Eli Navarro [neutral]: "Okay — core idea. Breakwater of natural modules, staggered in three bands, intertidal plugs to trap silt, oyster bags along the lee. We let the seagrass do the heavy lifting and show Marco what a living shore actually looks like."

Maya Reyes: "And the sensors will show it, if the salinity and deposition data line up. If the modules accrete even a few centimeters, that's proof people can see. Not just talk."

Eli Navarro [grinning]: "Also, it looks nice. People respond to pretty things."

You both laugh, but your hands do not stop moving. The humor is a pressure valve; the real sound is the careful clack of stakes as neighbors hammer them into place.

Asha's voice carries from the cluster of elders near the pier like authority softened by time.

Asha: "Measure at high slack and low slack. Don't just rely on one reading. The storm pulse will be our honest broker."

Tomas stands a little behind you — hoodie speckled with paint, arms folded in a posture that could be defiance or just cold. He watches the sensors like someone watching a game, unsure how to play. You catch his eye; he's not sure he wants to get his hands muddy but he's here. That matters more than he knows.

You move through the crowd like a wave — planting, marking, explaining. Someone passes you a tray of newly rooted plugs in coir; their roots glint with mud. A child asks where the plants will go and Eli Navarro, ever the showman, makes a ceremony of it.

Eli Navarro [kneeling to the child]: "Right here. See how the roots are like little fingers? They hold the mud so the water can't sweep it away. Help me—count with me: one, two, three—plant!"

The child plants with solemnity and bursts into giggles when soil smudges her palms. Laughter ripples and the crowd's energy shifts — from anxious to industrious, from watching to making.

You check the probe's display: a micro-fluctuation, expected and honest. The tide is a reliable liar and a terrible friend; it will show you what it means to build with water instead of against it.

[Visual: Tight on Eli Navarro's hands as he ties an oyster bag; his fingers are quick, clay under the nails]
[Sound: The clang across the harbor sounds closer, more insistent]

The clang makes the group flinch. Someone curses under their breath — Aurelia's pile-driving is a metronome of industry and a reminder of power. The construction lights across the water are a row of beacons; their white glare looks like a promise and a threat at once.

Eli Navarro [soft, for Maya Reyes]: "They never stop, do they? Even when the tide's talking."

Maya Reyes: "They build certainty out of force. We build it out of patience."

There is something in that exchange — a flirt of ideology and something warmer. Eli Navarro wipes soil from his palms and offers you the wetter one; you accept, and for a breath your fingers brush. It's a small contact: an alliance that tastes of salt, not confessions. The moment is a steadying touch rather than a collapse.

[Visual: Medium on your face — resolve and worry braided together]
[Music: Strings swells, heartbeat-like percussion quickening]

As the afternoon collapses into a sharper light, more neighbors arrive. Rosa brings a thermos of coffee, her hands warm, the steam fogging your glasses for a second. Someone sets up a crude tripod to take time-lapse photos. You realize you haven't checked your watch in an hour — time has the soft elasticity of work that matters.

You relay instructions, and the crowd divides into teams: planting, measuring, documenting. You resist the old impulse to do everything yourself; you delegate with staccato clarity. It's not self-abdication; it's the way this will scale. People carrying responsibility are what keeps a town from being a pile of good intentions.

Asha comes to your side, her breath smelling of mint and aged books.

Asha: "You're doing well. Let them see the method. Let them touch the instruments. If we win their hands, we win their patience."

Maya Reyes: "And if it fails?"

Asha [sharply]: "Then we'll have learned faster. But I don't think it will fail. The site selection is sound."

Tension ratchets up; your heart matches the rhythm of the pile drivers across the bay. The choice ahead is a pivot point: do you make a visible, rapid build with bodies and goodwill; do you solidify legal muscle first; or do you risk a flash of attention on Aurelia's doorstep that could either force the town's hand or make you look naive?

You can feel the high current of possibility: every shovel, every recorded reading, every neighbor's hand is a vote. The data is a language, but people need to see the grammar: roots, silt, living things holding shore.

Eli Navarro pulls you aside for a moment, sketchbook tucked under his arm. He's close enough that you can smell the lemon soap he uses, a clean, unsentimental scent.

Eli Navarro [earnest]: "If we scale now, we get visible inches, people in photos, Instagram, Marco sees the crowd. We move fast while the council is distracted. But no legal cover — Aurelia could claim trespass."

Maya Reyes: "If we go the council route, we buy time and rights, but Aurelia could bury the ground with concrete before the permits come through."

Eli Navarro [tilting his head, measuring you as much as the options]: "Or we do something loud — put a living module at the toe of their pile field. Media loves a spectacle, and so do angry voters. But that's a gamble. We could lose the town's trust if it goes sideways."

Maya Reyes: "And it could force the conversation, or it could make us look like stunts."

Eli Navarro [quick, earnest]: "Maya, I trust the way you think. I trust the way you carry this town. I want — I want to build this with you, however that looks. I don't want to push you. I just want you to know you're not alone."

The words land with the weight of someone offering scaffolding. Your chest tightens — not with dread but with a warm, electric clarity. The political calculus is cold; Eli Navarro's presence is a human variable that leans toward courage.

You scan the lot: boots planted in mud, small faces, Rosa's shawl fluttering, Tomas now kneeling and tamping down a plug with a concentration that makes his jaw work. The sensors blink their tiny green lives; the camera tripod whirs.

[Sound: The tide lifts, and the silt shifts with it — a deeper, sotto voce rush that underlays everything]
[Music: Crescendo, rhythmic and driving]

You think of the pique in Marco's eyes at the meeting, of June Park's composed confidence across the harbor, of the people who will lose more than asphalt if they make the wrong choice. The decision is not just tactical; it's moral: do you prioritize visible momentum, institutional durability, or disruptive attention?

Your pulse quickens. You have three ways forward, each demanding trust, resources, and consequences. The lot feels like an altar where the future is argued with hands and salt.

You breathe in the brine, the diesel, the garlic from someone's sandwich — all the smells of San Brisa — and you understand that the choice will change the tempo of everything that follows. Strength will be measured tomorrow in centimeters of accretion and the steadiness of neighbors' resolve.
