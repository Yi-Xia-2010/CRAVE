[Scene: Verdant Spire Roooftop Farm | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of rooftop beds glistening with condensation; string lights sag gently over trellises]
[Music: Soft, slow ambient hum with distant low-frequency wave undercurrent]
[Sound: Quiet irrigation drip, far-off breakers, a gull's single call]

You wake with the taste of wet soil at the back of your teeth and the soft bend of the world still asleep beneath condensation. The tablet has been flat against your ribs all night; the battery icon is sleepily generous. You flex your fingers and the cold of the metal clips to your palm the same way the salt in the air clings to the underside of your jacket.

You sit up. The rooftop smells like turned earth and sea—peat-sweet on one breath, metallic ozone on the next. Your feet find the worn planks without looking. Somewhere below, someone drags a crate across a balcony; somewhere beyond the glass of the city, breakers are rehearsing their long, patient roar.

[Visual: Close-up on brass pendant at your collarbone; tide line engraving catches the dawn]
You fingers trace the tide line engraved on the brass pendant at your throat. The mark is a neat, ruled notch—an insistence you learned to read before you learned to persuade. The pendant is cool and steady. It tells you what the numbers won't say out loud: this is not only data. People sleep under the waterline you model.

> Choice B: "Sit up and stand, let the cold make you sharp"
[Outcome A: "You pull the edge of your jacket up to your chin and close your eyes. The rooftop hum keeps pace with your breathing—two slow, even counts—and the idea of the council room is a distant, dim shape."]
[Outcome B: "You push your knees under you and stand. The chill breathes through the jumpsuit and your shoulders unclench. The tablet's glow is a horizon you need to cross."]
[MERGE: You choose—unconsciously—to stand.]

[Sound: Light rustle of canvas; a soft thud as someone sets a crate down]
A shadow moves in the mist. Emil Kwon steps out past the trellis, his shirt darkened with early-morning damp, a crate of seedlings in one arm and a sheaf of paper rolled in the other. The mist has made his hair more tousled than usual, ocean-soft at the edges. He takes in the rooftop with the same small, practical appreciation he gives to anything that could be fixed with hands and steady people.

Emil Kwon: "Morning."
(He smiles without the wide show—it arrives as a small, steady thing.) "You look like you haven't slept."

Maya Reyes: "I ran the model again."
(You keep your voice level; facts are ballast.) "Late convergence on the southern spit. I—" Your mouth finds the word you practice in meetings: 'uncertainty.' It sounds thinner when you say it aloud.

(He sets the crate down and lifts a seed tray, letting the wet green steam a moment.) Emil Kwon: "Got seedlings. Mangrove mixes and some marsh grasses; thought we might fold them into the floating beds this week." (He taps the rolled papers.) "And, uh—made a quick retrofit sketch. Low-tech floatation, modular so folks can build a piece at a time. You said you were going to the promenade later."

There is an easy pause while the irrigation hums and a gull calls. You look at his palm where a corner of the sketch peeks out—hand-drawn lines, neat but impatient. The kind of drawing that wants to be built before it's perfectly drawn.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Take the sketch and examine it now"
> Choice B: "Pocket the sketch and pull up the tablet instead"
[Outcome A: "You take the papers and let the sketch unfurl. Your thumbs trace a thin marker line, counting densities and attachment points like reading a small, honest map. It's clever—simple redundancies where the city over-engineers—and it smells faintly of coffee."]
[Outcome B: "You fold your fingers around the tablet and the screen wakes. Rows of numbers and color bands swim an honest blue. The sketch waits in Emil's hand like a promise."]
[MERGE: The crate of seedlings settles between you; the trellis throws soft, patterned shadows across its soil. You make a decision without announcing it—you can do both, you think, but one thing you know with the quiet weight of habit: your world has always leaned on the way numbers can make the city move.]

Asha’s name flashes across the tablet first in the stubborn little icon that has gotten you out of bed more times than you can count. You tap, and her voice spills from a voicemail like warm tea poured too fast—tart, immediate.

[Sound: Voicemail chime; Asha's voice, warm and urgent in the same breath]
Asha (voicemail): "Maya—you're up. Council wants you to present this afternoon. I left notes. They'll push for 'bold numbers.' Don't cave to the spectacle—make them accountable to the block maps. Call me when you have a minute."

You listen to it twice. The phrase 'bold numbers' sits in your chest with the soft density of fog. Boldness in a council room often means rounding up the certainty; it means a tidy headline and an uneasy community. Asha's voice has the light grit of someone who has watched marshlands be traded for parking lots and still believes you can make them ask for better.

Maya Reyes: (You say aloud, to nowhere and to the rooftop at once.) "They want bold numbers."

(He leans against a post and watches you, as if counting the steadiness of your shoulders.) Emil Kwon: "Bold like spectacle, or bold like… responsible?" He smooths his fingers over a seed tray as if it were a tiny ledger.

Maya Reyes: "That's the problem."
(You run a thumb along the tide-line on your pendant. The metal is warm from the night's sleep against your skin.) "Bold can mean bulldozers and a concrete solution that protects some at the expense of others. Bold can mean taking the easy headline, not the hard conversation."

(His face tightens, but only enough to register worry.) Emil Kwon: "So what do you want, Maya? Quietly right or loudly wrong?" He doesn't demand an answer. He already knows you have both loves within you—the numbers and the neighborhood, the models and the faces—and he knows you because you once stayed up for three nights to untangle a pollutant curve for the old southeast block.

You look down. Guilty faces sit on the back of your eyelids—the low-lying block you couldn't save, the salt-stiff sheets on a night the pumps failed. The memory doesn't scream; it simply sits with the same steady weight as the pendant. Responsibility has a way of feeling like a ledger that can never balance.

[Visual: Mid-shot of rooftop and city beyond; fog softens the hard lines of cranes and council towers]
A text bubbles up—Asha again, terse this time: "Council wants bold numbers." It feels less like a report and more like a verdict.

You flip the tablet. New flood-projection data populates the screen in neat gradients—cobalt, pewter, pale washed green. The bottom line is not a single number but a ledger of possible tides and timelines, each with its own polite uncertainty. The software has given you many versions of 'what if.' You have trained yourself to love what-ifs; they are where compromise breathes.

Maya Reyes: "They want certainty."
(You hear the tilt in your voice; the map is not kinder for your reading.) "And we don't have it. Not in their terms."

Emil Kwon: "Then show them the terms that matter." He slides a seedling free and holds it out to you—small, bellied root and oily leaf. "Show them the beds we can build without waiting for permits. Bring a human scale to the numbers. Let them smell the wet soil."

You want to argue, to draw a line between polices and practice—to tell him that the models shape the money and the money shapes what gets built. But his hands are steady; his enthusiasm is a quiet insistence: build with us, build now. There's an intimacy in him offering a living thing at a time like this. It is not romantic theater—it is practical faith.

Emil Kwon: "We can patch parts of the promenade this week. Diego's got volunteers. People will come if they can see something tangible."

Maya Reyes: (You rub your thumb over the pendant again.) "If I go in with small fixes, the council will ask why the city isn't doing the big thing. They'll point to a single seawall and call it decisive."

Emil Kwon: (He shrugs, the motion casual, but a shadow passes his mouth.) "Maybe so. Maybe they need to see both."

You consider the crate between you: seedlings that will root in marsh and tide; a hand-sketched retrofit promising dignity and agency; a tablet full of plausible futures. The rooftop is an island of warmth and green in a city that is both wet and brittle. Light pools in the gutters of leaves. The hum of irrigation is a metronome for small acts of care.

> Choice B: "Tell Emil you'll present the model as-is, let the numbers speak"
[Outcome A: "You form the words slowly, weighing each one against the map on your tablet. 'I'll lead with a hybrid,' you say. Emil nods, quiet approval in his eyes. The seedling between you seems to lean toward the sun."]
[Outcome B: "You say, 'I'll stick with the model.' It feels antiseptic when it leaves your mouth—clean, uncluttered. Emil exhales a small, private sound that could be disappointment or acceptance. He doesn't press."]
[MERGE: You do not pick either path yet. You hold both in the same hand like different shells from the same shore.]

Your internal clock—used to late nights and early briefings—winds itself into a gentle preparedness. The arousal of the morning stays low; there is no rush, only the patient building of small truths. It feels like a good morning to be careful.

Asha's voicemail pulses in the corner of the tablet one more time: "They'll want you at the council in the afternoon. Be ready for questions about displacement." Her words are a calm bell.

You breathe. The city beyond the string lights goes on rehearsing its own rules: tides, wind, public meetings. The rooftop gives you a thin, necessary perspective—soil, seed, and the people who will plant them.

Maya Reyes: (You fold the tablet and tuck it into your jacket.) "I'll come in," you tell Emil. "But I need your retrofit drawings and Diego's group at the promenade to bring a team. We show them both the map and the hands."

Emil Kwon: "I'll be there."
(He pauses, then adds, quieter.) "And Maya—don't forget to breathe between the numbers." He smiles without theatricality, a small, human punctuation.

You allow yourself one steady inhale. The pendant presses warm against your skin. The seedlings in their crate seem to settle into a collective hush. Clouds remain low; the breakers continue their soft rehearsal. The morning does not accelerate into panic. Instead it gathers itself, precise and patient, toward a single hinge: the council room that waits like a question.

[Visual: Tight focus on your face; the silver streak in your hair bright against the warm rooftop light]
A last glance at the Tideward Promenade planned in your head—stilted stalls, patched tarpaulin, hands rebuilding what the sea tries to unmake. You imagine the seedlings there, green and insistently alive where tides meet human stubbornness.

You step toward the ladder and the stairwell down, the crate still between you two. The rooftop's lights sway in a tiny, wet wind.

Page-turn thought: You have models, seedlings, and Asha's voice. The morning could tilt a conversation into policy or keep it tied to people. Which will you put in front of the council?
