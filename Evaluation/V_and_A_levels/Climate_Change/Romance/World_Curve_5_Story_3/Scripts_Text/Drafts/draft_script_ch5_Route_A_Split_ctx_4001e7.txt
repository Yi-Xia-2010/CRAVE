# Route: Route A


[Scene: Greenline Boardwalk | Morning]
[Visual: Wet planks streaked with mud; rows of salt-tolerant plantings drooped under rain. A cluster of volunteers hunch beneath a patchwork tarp. A portable whiteboard lists "EMERGENCY: PRIORITIES".]
[Sound: Wind pinning the tarp; a steady slap of waves against pilings; canned radio chatter from a volunteer coordinator.]

You arrive with the taste of rain still on your teeth and the compass in your palm a small, inevitable weight. The morning has been a litany: sand ingression at the culvert, one improvised berm collapsed, two family cellars flooded, volunteer crews running on coffee and stitches. The town's energy is spent in short, hot bursts—hands, hands, hands—then the long slow sag of someone realizing they have to do it again.

Lina Park: "We patched the east terrace at dawn. South face scoured out by nine. Tommy says the new drift's shifted two feet since last week."

You: "Two feet. Not two inches. Not two feet more or less—we're seeing systemic movement."

Lina's fingers are raw, a smear of soil ingrained beneath the nails she bites at when she worries. She looks up and meets you like a question that expects a plan.

Lina Park: "We need carpenters. We need schedules. We need water trucks. And—" (she exhales, softer) "We need someone to tell the people who want guarantees what guarantees even mean now."

Tomas Reyes, leaning on a cane made of driftwood patched with duct tape, snorts a laugh that sounds brittle with exhaustion.

Tomas Reyes: "Guarantees? Girl, the sea doesn't write guarantees. It writes notices in sand."

You close the Moleskine to hide the trembling of your hand and feel the compass press against the shell of your palm—your father's voice in the notch of metal. The harbor feels like a patient that keeps reviving and then coughing blood; you are part doctor and part jury.

[Sound: A child crying somewhere behind the boardwalk; someone tries to soothe them with a scraped joke.]

You and Elias stand shoulder-to-shoulder, elbows damp, his sketchbook folded into his jacket. He looks at the volunteers with a slow, patient calculation, then back at you. There is a question in his amber eyes—a gentle expectant question—pregnant with a kind of trust that makes your chest ache.

Elias Rowe: "We can extend the pilot. Get terracing teams and carpenters. Train two faster crews. If we push tonight, we can reduce the load before the next tide."

You: "If we push tonight, people will burn out. We can't afford burnout—the pilot survives if the people survive it."

Elias Rowe: (quietly, urgent) "Then we build redundancy: staggered shifts, rotation, emergency coffers. I can call my circle—some hardware suppliers will discount. We can make it—if you say yes."

Lina Park: (sharp) "If 'make it' means everyone sleeps with a rope on their lap, that's not making anything. That's holding breath."

[Music: Low, urgent percussion under the wind—heartbeat tempo.]

[Scene: Aster's Reach Harbor | Late Morning]
[Visual: The harbor square crowded with concerned faces. A mobile news van blinks a cold LED. A handheld screen on a volunteer's phone shows a polished corporate spokesperson framed against a CGI rendering of a seawall: Serena Voss, impeccable, sapphire cufflinks catching the light like deliberate punctuation.]
[Sound: Camera shutters; a practiced voice from the speaker; murmurs swell like a rising tide.]

Serena Voss (on-screen): (calm, measured) "We can deploy reinforced modular bulkheads within forty-eight hours. Technicians on site. Funding to stabilize critical infrastructure immediately."

You feel the answer in the crowd. Some hands clap like palms on a life raft. Some faces blanch. A mother near the boardwalk presses her child to her chest and mouths a thank you to the screen.

Elias leans in, jaw tight, and whispers so only you get the fizz of his breath.

Elias Rowe: "This is PR. Fast money for fast optics. She'll patch the high-value areas and leave the margins."

You: "And she'll expect results. She'll write the contract terms—chains on the pilot. We lose control."

Serena steps off the media stage as the satellite feed cuts. Up close she is smaller than the holo-image, but the cufflinks are real, and they glint like coins. She inclines her head with the smoothness of someone who has never had to reconcile a ruined roof with a spreadsheet.

Serena Voss: (easy smile) "Maya Calder. I've watched your work. We can protect Aster's Reach. Let us do what we do best."

You: (cold) "Protection isn't the same as partnership. What are the strings, Serena?"

Serena Voss: (tightening) "Standard oversight. Performance metrics. We need decision speed, not group therapy."

Elias snaps in, harder than you expected.

Elias Rowe: "We won't have 'decision speed' if you take the pilot offline to make a spectacle for investors. We need embedded oversight—community liaisons on every team, transparent schedules, data sharing in real time."

Serena Voss: (a flash—compromise rehearsed) "We can formalize oversight. Contracts can include community auditors. We can set public dashboards. But we move fast, or we risk roofs."

There is an exchange—a practiced hunt and a bluff—two corporate phrases overlaid on a raw, human need. You know Serena's game; you know the reasons she plays it. But you also see her thin face, the locket at her throat when she reaches up to tuck hair behind her ear—an old photograph sometimes revealed in the corner of a business suit. There is something human pinned beneath the polished rhetoric, and that makes the moral calculus heavier.

Serena's smile trembles; she nods slowly and orders a legal aide to draft language. Someone behind you cheers; others frown, worried about where the money will come from."]

The cameras pivot. A toddler tugs at Elias's sleeve, asking a question about sand, and for a moment the whole political theater compresses into a child's innocent curiosity. You answer with a small, practical smile because it's easier than answering everything else.

[Music: Abrupt strings cut through the murmur—an anxious, unresolved chord.]

[Scene: Old Breakwater Pier | Noon — Wind-Slashed, Sky Low]
[Visual: Cracked concrete dropping into a restless sea. The tide line paints a jagged necklace around exposed rebar. You sit on the wet pier, knees to your chest, compass heavy in your palm.]
[Sound: Waves gnashing rock; gulls distant and thin; the metallic groan of an aging piling.]

You remember the tide marker your father pointed to when you were seven: a scar of paint on a post, faded but stubborn. He touched it the way some people touch rosaries—an assurance against the sea's restlessness. "We mark the shore, Maya," he said. "The sea keeps its own accounting but we keep ours too."

Now the marking feels like denial. You count the marks in your head and they line up like debt. Each one is a person you couldn't fix in time. Each one is a roof you couldn't guarantee. The compass in your palm becomes an heirloom of guilt.

Seneca—older neighbor, stoic, voice that smells faintly of chimney smoke—pulls up a low pallet and sits beside you. Rain clings to his cap.

Seneca: "You carrying the town on your knuckles again?"

You: "I carried his boat to the water once. I thought carrying people would be the same."

Seneca: "Different weight. Boats are heavy but honest. People are…tied to decisions that don't sit well when they wake up at three a.m."

You look at him, and all the rehearsed arguments unspool into one blunt ache.

Seneca: "You can say 'no' to Serena. You can say 'yes'. But people need roofs tonight. Consider that."

Tomas comes down the breakwater, boots slapping, carrying a patched bucket of rope.

Tomas Reyes: "We don't get to be moral all at once when the sea is cracking the porch. Sometimes you pick the house."

Your chest constricts. It is both literal and metaphorical—the tide line of your ribs pressed together like two maps that no longer align. Elias kneels a few feet away, sketchbook open but empty, eyes on you like someone waiting to be chosen for something bigger than both of you.

Elias Rowe: "Don't do this alone. Talk to me. Let me help you weigh it."

You: "Weigh what, Elias? Pride against roofs? Independence against broken-porch promises? If I choose wrong—if I prioritize community labor and someone dies because the reinforcement failed, I'll carry that. If I accept corporate funds and later find out they withdrew support from whole neighborhoods to meet investor margins—I'll carry that."

Elias Rowe: (soft, urgent) "No one should carry that alone. We need systems that share the burden—procedures, overseers, audits. Not only you. Not only me."

You: "And if the system is corrupted? If 'oversight' is a word on paper and the money buys silence?"

Elias Rowe: (his voice breaking between strategy and fear) "Then we make the oversight public. We name witnesses. We—"

A gull cries and the sound is a knife. The wind seems to press the conversation small, urgent. The town is a ring of faces back-lit by storm-cloud light waiting for an answer that will decide more than a single shoreline.

You remember the old pattern: grief—bury—do. You feel it now as a physical process. The list grows in your head. Repairs. Volunteers. Legalese. Media statements. Coffee for the night crew. Permissions. The old culvert's mouth like an eye demanding whether you'll be brave enough to act in a way that doesn't simply stave off collapse.

Every option slices something. A piece of trust. A strand of independence. A scrap of safety. You feel the town's collective breath pressed into a keelboat of expectation. Your chest tightens like tide lines drawn too close together.

[Music: A percussion snare snaps into a relentless, accelerating tempo. Heartbeat drums in your ears.]

You look at the faces you know. Lina's jaw set, palms mud-raw. Tomas's thin, wry grin that is hanging on by a thread. The mother who clings to her child's damp hair. Serena's cufflinks gleaming in distant sun. Elias waiting like a harbor light.

Decision hangs before you—not a soft choice but a cliff-face.
