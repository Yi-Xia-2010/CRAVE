You stand under the clean, clinical light and feel the compass at your throat like a heart. The Hall smells of ozone and sharp paper—printer toner, the tang of too many hands on one document. Your palms are damp. On the polished table between you and Eloise, a small stack of pages waits: the leaked documents, annotated by your hand and the careful ink of other neighbors. This is private everything; there are no cameras, only faces that matter.

Eloise Varela sits across from you, immaculate and slightly rough at the edges from tonight's pressure. Her AR lens is down; for a moment you see eyes, not projection. Samira stands beside her, sleeves rolled, the municipal vest looking like an uneasy truce. Rosa is there too—her braid a silver steadiness in the white room. Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq is just behind you, shoulder close enough that his breath brushes your neck; his presence is a quiet engine of resolve.

Your throat tightens with the scale of what you are about to ask. This room can translate "safety" into eviction. These papers can make safety accountable. You clamp your hands around the pages like a promise.

Mara Lin: "These are redacted only where they need to protect sources," you say, voice even but carrying. "They show procurement earmarks, timelines that compress displacement, and—most importantly—points where enforceable community oversight can be written in. Not a promise. A mechanism."

Eloise Varela [measured]: "You understand how unprecedented that is. Committees, audits, community audits—you're asking the city to bind its hands."

Mara Lin: "We're asking the city to prove it's working for people, not investors. Binding checks are what keep displacement from being the cost of progress."

Eloise's jaw tightens. She taps a corner of the table, a precise, counterbalanced motion.

Eloise Varela [a beat]: "Binding checks cost time and money. They introduce friction. We're trying to move in the face of a real, accelerating hazard."

Samira [soft, urgent]: "And we're trying to keep people in their homes. There are ways to make the plan durable without strip-mining neighborhoods. We can phase the barrier, tie release of funds to enforceable community oversight boards, mandate on-site retention of families with relocation funds only as last resort."

Rosa [quiet, steady]: "You remember what it is to lose a block. You remember what it is to watch the tide take what we thought permanent. There are methods that let marsh and municipality breathe together. But they must be legally written. They must have teeth."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [leaning in]: "We've prototyped modular anchoring that reduces displacement. We can integrate it into your timelines. We can set trigger points: if a module fails, municipal resources mobilize instantaneously. Not after the fact."

Eloise exhales, and for a flash you see the fork of old idealism and the hard line of now.

Eloise Varela [a beat]: "You want oversight. You want contingency-triggered municipal response. You want the Flats' neighborhoods declared essential and prioritized."

Mara Lin: "Yes. And a clause that prevents bulk sale of ceded land for ten years—a covenant enforced by city ordinance and monitored by a community oversight board with binding veto on developer displacement projects."

Eloise studies the papers, then you. There is calculation, then a small, nearly invisible softening.

Eloise Varela [lower]: "If I agree, I need assurances—fast. If this goes public as a splintered file, the plan collapses under litigation and paralysis."

Mara Lin: "We keep it private until it's codified. Samira can shepherd the first draft through emergency ordinances; Rosa will head the oversight board with community-elected members. Kai will provide market guarantees against speculative purchase of evacuated parcels."

Samira [nodding]: "I can stall public release until legal language is ironclad. I can draft an emergency amendment that ties funds release to the oversight mechanism. It's messy, but workable."

Eloise Varela [long breath]: "I cannot promise you everything. Compromises will be necessary."

Mara Lin: "We don't want a blank cheque. We want protection for people and for the Flats' character. That's non-negotiable."

There is a charged pause where your pulse sounds like a drum in your ears. Outside, a faint cheer rises—someone in the lobby has announced a provisional agreement to an impatient crowd.

Kai orchestrates with an easy grin, someone hands you a paper lantern, and Rosa adjusts a bouquet of salt-sheared reeds.

Your hands tremble—not with fear now but with a high, thrilling energy. The agreement is a living thing. It could be torn at any moment, but right now it's here, stitched by many hands. You can feel the city's heartbeat speeding to match yours.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [soft, excited]: "They gave us the ordinance language, Mara. Samira's wording is sharp. Eloise signed the provisional memo."

Mara Lin [half laugh, half sob]: "Eloise signed it. The same Eloise who fought for timelines. This is... I don't even have words."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [looks at you]: "You do. You've been saying them all along."

He steps closer. The greenhouse hums with the sound of life—fans turning, people calling out measurements, the low metallic sigh of the city settling into something new. His hand finds yours. The contact sends electricity through you that has nothing to do with power lines.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq [hesitant, then steadier]: "We keep saying we'll build something that lets people stay. Tonight it's not just promise; it's law. And—and I want us to be part of the building. Not just the work, but—"

Mara Lin: (interrupting gently) "—the rest of it. The small, dumb, terrifying rest."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' Rafiq: "I was going to say 'the rest of it' in exactly those words."

You both laugh, and it cracks the last thin shell of composure. For a flash you feel like a child with a kite, heart in mouth, trusting the wind.
