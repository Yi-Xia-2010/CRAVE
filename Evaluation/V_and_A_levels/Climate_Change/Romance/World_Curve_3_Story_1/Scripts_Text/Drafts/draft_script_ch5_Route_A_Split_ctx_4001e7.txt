# Route: Additional Route & Route A


[Scene: The Flats | Late afternoon — storm approaching]

[Visual: Low clouds roil over the harbor; volunteer lanterns bob; a raw wind drives salt spray into faces]
[Sound: Distant sirens, the crowd's murmur rising and falling like a rope being pulled taut]
[Music: Tense, staccato percussion under a low, driving bass]

You are wet before the water arrives — not yet from the tide but the rain that laced the afternoon into a steady, hungry seam. The human chain you helped build is a ribbon of bodies and patched tarps and voices tangled in commands and comfort. Someone keeps passing out hot tea in chipped metal mugs; the steam smells of burnt sugar and sea.

Your jacket clings to your back. Your compass — warm from your palm — presses its brass against your sternum like a pulse you can't quiet. You hear Ibe's voice beside you, clipped but steady.

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: "Sandbags here!" (He pulls a sling over his shoulder, pushing mud-slick hands into the work.)

You: "Make sure the lower seam gets an extra layer. Kai — stack tighter near Pier Nine!" (You bark the instruction and taste the salt on your teeth.)

Ibe laughs under his breath, not from humor but to keep his jaw loose. For a moment, the two of you move as a unit: you anchor, he improvises, volunteers fill the gap. Your fingers slip on a wet rope; you curse and twist your grip back so you don't show the tremor.

A hand presses a folded printout into your palm. Someone's phone has gone viral — a leak smuggled into the Planning Hall's press feed. The paper is slick; the headline reads like a knife.

[Visual: Close on the paper, ink smudged by rain]
NARRATION: The investor clause. It is explicit. It names parcels, it names buyouts, it names terms that let private firms relocate people under the guise of "accelerated safety compliance."

A murmur becomes a wave. The crowd's rhythm changes from work to disbelief to anger. An elderly woman nails her fist to a post and cries out. Someone hits the municipal banner with a fist and it flaps like a wounded wing. Rain stabs across faces like needles.

Rosa Alvarez (shouting, voice cracking): "They signed us into ledgers! They wrote our names on paper like we were an expense!"

Samira (arriving panting, eyes wide): "There's corroboration. Legal added a clause—it's...")
Samira's sentence is cut as a volunteer slams a palm on the pump housing and it groans.

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: (low) "We can shut down the permit trucks for a day, but if the pumps go—"

You: "Then we keep the pumps. We keep people out of evacuation lists. We will not let them count us out while the sea counts the city's profit."

The pumps — municipal machines that hum with bureaucratic electricity — are the city's hidden heartbeat. Tonight their beat will mean the difference between knee-deep danger and the water swallowing whole streets.

The surge arrives like a punctuation, not a whisper. The sea, which had been a distant threat, folds its shoulder and leans. You hear a sound not of rain but of a mass moving: boards banging, the clatter of shutters, the sickening suction of water finding new ground.

Visual: The tide breaches the outer sandbag line. A segment of the Flats — a narrow street of low houses and rooftop gardens — feels the sea push its finger in. Water sluices through a gap in the chain, through a crooked doorway where a child's chair floats away. Someone screams a name you don't catch.

Ibe moves faster than you think he can. He yanks at a tarp, shouting over the storm.

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: "We need to bolster Central Row! I've got modules staged at the greenhouse — light frames, buoyant blocks. They can lift a lane if we can get them deployed."

You can see, in your head, Ibe's designs: neat sketches in your notebook margins, straps and pontoons, the quiet math of survival. But you can also see the pumps on the quay, their nacelles blinking with municipal firmware that could be turned into a hammer against these neighborhoods if permits shift hands.

Volunteer (nearby): "Samira—what about the clause? They can move people when the Hall declares unsafe!"

Samira's face is a portrait of panic forced into procedure.

Samira: "I can slow a vote. I can—" (She swallows.) "But not if the pumps are legally commandeered to 'protect infrastructure' while they 'assess' neighborhoods."

You taste the metallic tang of fear. The leak turns the protest into a crucible: anger sharpens into strategy, grief becomes a ledger of losses that could be ordered and distributed. For every moment you hesitate, a household is recalculating stairs versus sandbags.

[Visual: A volunteer pushes a stroller through shallow water; the baby's blanket is soaked through]
[Sound: The pump's engine coughs, a splutter you don't want to hear]

Ibe crouches beside you. His hand finds yours, whether to steady you or steady himself you can't tell.

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: (voice tight) "We can haul the modular units now. We can get them onto Central Row in thirty minutes if we have thirty hands and the market truck."

You: "If they fail, people drown. If we don't try, homes fail. If we try and the Hall uses the failure to evacuate them for 'safety'—"

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: (interrupting) "Then we occupy the pumps. Stop them from sending riot squads into the Flats. Keep the legal process frozen and buy time. Or—" (He breathes.) "Or we get people moved now, safe, and gather the evidence on the clause. There are choices."

You: "They're all terrible."

Ibe's jaw works. The rain makes streaks on his face like he is dissolving into the storm.

You run because there is nothing else to do. The Flats has become kinetic: mud sucking at boots, the smell of overturned earth, the twang of broken fence wire. People shout names, stack salvaged boards against water, haul backpacks drenched with months of memories.

A messenger staggers over, breathless, phone in hand. The leaked clause has a new appendage — an investor memo confirming "strategic relocation incentives" tied to corporate contractors. The memo's language is clinical and cruel; it speaks of "site-readiness" and "mitigating financial risk."

Rosa (low, furious): "They don't want to shore up neighborhoods. They want to make them disappear and call it safety."

You feel a nausea like cold oil. The moral arithmetic collapses into a single, raw fact: policy will be used to remove people. Your hands numb, your chest tight.

Samira grabs your sleeve, voice near-hysteric but trying to be municipal.

Samira: "There are officers en route. If the pumps are seized in the name of public safety, the Hall can order evacuations under statute. I can flag the clause, but only if physical evidence holds the space. Occupation freezes action, but it risks arrests. Deploying Ibe's uplift might save a lane, but it's untested at full scale in a surge. Temporaries save lives but hand the narrative: 'We had to move them.'"

Your mind lists the possible wounds: arrests that break morale, engineering that could fail and take lives, relocation that severs the place you grew up from its people.

Ibe reaches for your chin, a blunt, intimate motion that makes the rain hit harder in your ears.

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: (quiet, pleading) "Mara—" (He uses your given name like a plea.) "We can do it together. You lead the voices. I lead the builds. We make the modules hold. Help me believe it's enough."

You feel a chasm open between two impulses: to hold ground with your body and voice, to protect the identity of the Flats, and to use the tool that might physically keep the street dry even if you can't prove the clause yet. Your chest tightens the way it does before you speak in public: the place where you choose service over your own safety.

Volunteer (nearby): "They're taking kids to the market — small boats, but not everyone fits."

The Harbor Market is a soggy hub now; its raised walkways are the city's temporary sanctuary. You can see lanterns swinging, people huddled under patched canopy, the greenhouse's glass halo in the distance like a promise of equipment and shelter. The rooftop greenhouse's delivery route is clogged by volunteers hauling wet frames.

Your phone buzzes: a video from a neighbor you barely know. The water is in her doorway, wrapping like a gray animal. She is crying, but steady, and she mouths: "Don't give them our street."

Your mouth is dry. You want to pull Ibe into the small pocket between you and make him promise you'll be safe together. Instead you say something larger.

You: "We cannot let them write our deaths into their contracts. We cannot let a clause replace a family."

Ibe's hand tightens, then loosens. Rain slicks his hair to his forehead. There is a look between you that is not a strategy but a raw human question: can you expect him to stay if you ask him to risk everything?

Ibrahim "Ibe" Rafiq: "If you say we occupy, I'll be with you at the pumps. If you say we deploy, I'm yours on the frames. If you say relocate, I'll do the paper and make sure they cannot call it voluntary without the town's signatures. But you have to decide."

Your chest vibrates with the urgency of hundreds of lives. The arousal in your body — the tight, hot readiness — is not fear alone; it's the fight your bones were built for, sharpened by the knowledge that failure will be counted in names.

[Visual: A child's toy boat flips in the rising water, a small white arc under the gray]
[Sound: A loud, metallic bang — the pump's intake shutters being engaged somewhere upriver]

You can see the pumping station across the flat stretch: a squat building with municipal lettering, its lights slicing through rain. Officers form like a shadow to its doors. The modules at the greenhouse glint like fragile hope under the tarp.

This is the moment where strategy becomes action. The crowd looks to you with an odd, terrible frankness — not for a plan that comforts, but for a hand to take and a direction to move.

[Music: Crescendo — strings slicing, percussion pounding; the rhythm of a heart in sprint]

You breathe once, and the world narrows to three possibilities — each a kind of sacrifice.

You can feel the pressure of the decision — not intellectual, not procedural, but visceral. Your throat burns. Somewhere behind you, someone begins to sob, and the sound knits into the rain.

Your last thought before you speak is simple and raw: there are only so many things you can hold. The sea will not wait for ethics debates. The Hall will not delay for grief.
