You feel the word "funders" hit like salt. You taste its metallic edge and the ache that follows: meters and money deciding who stays. Still, this is where science meets policy. You set down your satchel, pull out the small brass spectrometer that never leaves your belt, and open the field notebook whose pages smell faintly of sea.

Marina Reyes: "We can translate long-line performance into three pilot metrics: peak wave energy attenuation per line, sediment accretion per season, and cooperative-maintenance man-hours. If we model those against projected storm frequency, we can show displacement risk reduction."

Elias Kato leans forward, eyes narrowing; he likes numbers that can be cross-checked.

Elias Kato: "And the confidence interval?"

Marina Reyes: "Twenty-four months of seasonal variance gives us an initial confidence window — not perfect, but defensible. With drone LIDAR repeats and community sensors we can tighten it."

Rina taps a tablet, fingers decisive.

Rina: "If you can give us those datasets with a conservative CI, we'll carve a pilot designation. We'll also include labor credits as in-kind municipal matches. But know that the construction permit for the seawall is already scheduled for site prep next week. Council's under pressure from insurer estimates."

The words "site prep next week" land like an order you didn't write. The air in the conference room feels thinner, as if the walls have drawn in.

Marina Reyes: "Labor credits — how would they be quantified?"

Rina: "Hourly equivalence with prevailing municipal labor rates. A tracked ledger. Verified by municipal inspectors."

Something in that sentence clicks wrong. You imagine Old Tom and other elders bending over ledgers like strangers. The ledger turns hands into entries; people into units.

Elias Kato: "It's not ideal, but it's a compromise that gets community labor counted without creating liability exposures."

You sense his restraint — a careful bracing against rhetoric. He would rather the plan be technically unimpeachable than inflamed by emotion. The rawness in you wants to snap back: counting hands on kelp lines is not the same as paying out a wage, and ledger hours can't account for memories held in a family's dock. But you swallow.
