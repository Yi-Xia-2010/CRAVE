You hold one in your hand because someone hands it to you, not because it has your name on it, but because it smells like a consequence you could have measured but somehow couldn't stop.

Elias Kato [turns to you, the warmth gone from his amber eyes]: "For the town at large. For the infrastructure that keeps the school, the clinic, the market. We had to do something. People will be safer."

Elias Kato: "We did the math that had to be done." He says it like both comfort and indictment.

Marina Reyes: "We did. And it wasn't enough."

Elias Kato [after a breath]: "People think they want someone who makes decisions in a crisis. They don't always want the person who made the crisis visible."

The cooperative — the thing you hoped would be both livelihood and defense — will limp, tied to scraps of funding and to a reputation someone has just called into question.

Old Tom's voice comes back in your head: "Maps don't tell you what a house means."

You cannot unring the bell. You cannot unmake the notices, the headlines, the rhetorical frame that turned your facts into a blade. The town's immediate danger has been blunted, but the social fabric has been frayed into threads.

You close your eyes. In the white heat of the decision you had thought the truth would save people. Now the truth has saved some and cost others, and the moral accounting feels bankrupt.

You walk back toward the town. The market is quieter. Some stalls are closed for the season; some volunteers load crates into trucks bound for sites outside town. Kaori "Kai" Matsuda and Mika pack tools, their faces unreadable — not the fury of a moment but the exhausted, hard edge of people who must keep going.

Before you leave the flats you tie the buoy pendant back on. The knot is small, clumsy with numb fingers. You whisper a promise you don't know how to keep: you'll try to mend what you can. You'll try to make the cooperative useful in the margins the municipal plan carved out. You'll try to be present in the places where the need is raw.

But the wind carries your promise away, scattering it across the flats like small scraps of paper.
