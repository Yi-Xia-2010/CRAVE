# Route: Route D (Sad Ending)
# Context: Start

[Scene: Community Greenhouse & Restoration Lab (Boathouse) | Dawn]
[Visual: Show Amaya (you) bent over a long crate of seedlings, morning light slanting through salt-specked panes]
[Sound: Distant gull calls, a slow drip of condensation from the roof, the faint scrape of a boat tied to the dock]
[Music: Soft, tentative strings — an ascending motif]

You breathe in. The air here is thick with wet timber and the mineral tang of seawater that has crept in through the boathouse seams. Salt beads cling to the blades of new spartina like a scatter of glass beads; when you run a sod between your fingers it smells of silt and something older — the smell your father used to call "the mouth of the sea."

Your notebook lies open by the crate: seedlings logged, coordinates, survival rate percentages in a hand that still shakes slightly from the cold. The morning's checklist sits beneath a frayed seashell pendant you touch without thinking, a small steadying ritual. Your thumb finds the chip of brass on the clip as if to check the world is still held together.

You mark the last tray: twenty-one of twenty-four survived the spring tide, a small victory that makes something in your chest unclench. The numbers are tidy and hopeful for once.

[Visual: Show close-up of the notebook: neat columns, smudged ink where your glove slipped]
Narration continues.

The greenhouse hums with life — tiny croaks from a moisture sensor, the soft rustle of paper tags in a draft, the metallic click of an old fan. Light through the glass turns the dirt and tools to amber. You imagine the marshlands beyond the boathouse, the channels braided like a map of breathing things, delicate and necessary.

Footsteps on the boards outside — stiffer, measured; a different rhythm. You look up.

[Visual: Show Lucas at the doorway, AR goggles folded, awkward smile half-formed]
[Sound: The screen door's latch complaining]

Lucas Herrera: "Morning, Amaya."
(He steps in, wiping his palms on his vest out of habit.) "Looks like the spartina's doing better than my optimism this week."

Amaya Saito: "It did its part. We did our part."
(You tap the notebook as though that will convince the plants to keep growing.) "What brings the corporate emissary to our greenhouse before dawn? Did they send you undercover to sample soil?"

Lucas Herrera: "(A quick, half-laugh that doesn't reach his eyes.) Not corporate. Not today. I—"
(He hesitates, choosing words.) "I heard through Elena that Atlantech's formal proposal is going to be in front of the council this afternoon. Bigger than anything we've seen. They want to present engineered defenses — a seawall system, modular storm gates. Scales, armatures. She asked whether you'd speak."

You feel your stomach tighten at the phrase "engineered defenses," a cold order of clinical steel that feels at odds with the damp warmth around you. You picture concrete forms, cranes, the straight clean lines that cut through tidal curves and marsh whispers. You remember your father's storm journals: his cramped notes of tidal heights and the way he drew ribboned marsh channels with the same reverence you have.

Amaya Saito: "A seawall."
(The words are small but gravity-heavy.) "That would... alter the marsh more than any tide has. Elena asked me to—?"

Lucas Herrera: "She asked if you could present the restoration alternative. She wants both sides aired."
(He leans against a worktable, watching the way your expression tightens.) "There's funding attached if we compromise. Lots of it."

Amaya Saito: (You feel the light on the gold flecks of your eyes catch — they widen, and for a moment the seedlings' green is reflected back at you.) "Compromise is easy to sign away the land with. I'm not interested in paper promises. I'm interested in whether the plants survive the next storm."

Lucas Herrera: (He studies you, a mix of engineer and someone who wants to be believed.) "And I don't want to see houses flooded. I don't want your lab swept away. Can't we design the wall to leave room for marshes? Hybrid solutions exist."

Amaya Saito: "Hybrid in theory, Lucas. We both know how specifications read when they get mapped to budgets."
(You close the notebook a little too hard.) "The marsh isn't just amenity. It's infrastructure. It eats the storm's energy. It buys us time. You know that."

Lucas Herrera: (He flinches, then relaxes into pragmatism.) "I do. I know that better than most. I'm not presenting for Atlantech today. I wanted you to hear it from me first. Because I'd rather you hear it from someone who still cares about—"
(He searches for a word that won't sound like a plea.) "—who still cares about the town."

Amaya Saito: (You let a breath out through your nose.) "Why tell me, Lucas? Because you need someone to argue the slow route at the council? Because you expect me to save the marsh while you provide the scaffolding for the wall?"

Lucas Herrera: (Quiet, reluctant) "Because I don't want to be the cause of erosion — of anything. I'm asking you to trust me enough to stand in the room while I speak truthfully. I can't promise the company will listen, but I will be there."

The air between you is the kind that can braid or fray.

You feel the familiar tightness — not anger, not exactly fear: a pressure that curls around your ribs when something worth holding is at risk. You thumb the seashell pendant again as if confirming an old vow. Your father's journal, a spiral-bound thing with salted edges and his handwriting like a shoreline itself, sits open beneath a stack of volunteer sign-up sheets. A blue-ink notation catches your eye: "We plant for tomorrow we will not see." You let that line set into you like a plank.

From the doorway, Mika's boots arrive as a comic punctuation: bright, mismatched rubber boots that squeak on the wet wood. She pauses, looks at the seedlings and then at you, eyes warm with the kind of impatience only a childhood friend can afford.

Mika: "You look like you swallowed a tidal chart and aren't sharing."
(She half-grins, wiping her palms on her knees.) "Got any spare soil? Or are we pretending to be dignified scientists this morning?"

Amaya Saito: (You laugh before you realize you could have avoided it. The sound loosens something.) "We have dirt in spades. Coffee is over there if you earned it by climbing out of bed."

Mika: (She snorts.) "I earned it by saving a fishing net from the reeds. Barely heroic."
(She glances at Lucas.) "Hey, Herrer— what's the suit doing in Amaya's garden?"

Lucas Herrera: "Dropping off bad news wrapped in corporate stationery. Elena's asked Amaya to speak at a council briefing. Atlantech will propose a seawall."

Mika: (Her face hardens a fraction, but she masks it with a joke.) "Great. Concrete: the official solution to every problem since someone invented the word 'budget.'"

Amaya Saito: (You fold the volunteer lists into your notebook. The list of names is longer than it was last week; more hands, more energy.) "Elena wants to hear every voice. That's why I'm going. We need to put people in the room, Mika. We need numbers and stories."

Mika: "Then tell me when to show up and whether to bring the banner with the seagull on it. Also, are we going to ask Dr. Kaito to come? He always has a weathered anecdote that makes the room quieter."

Amaya Saito: (You think of Dr. Kaito — his voice a slow tide of memory, the way he taps a table when he wants to emphasize a point, the algae-smudged sweater he refuses to throw away.) "I'll call him. He'll have something old and useful to say. He always does."

Narration continues.

You take the photograph into your hand, thumb the edge, and listen to the quiet advice of memory. Your father's handwriting loops again in the journal where he describes the contour of a small inlet he loved. You can feel the pull between preserving that contour and building a thing that might protect the town in the short term. Both answers have human faces: families in low-lying streets, your neighbors, the gulls that ride the reed channels.

The greenhouse feels busier now — not by bodies, but by intent. Volunteers' names fill the margins of your notebook. The idea of showing up at the council is no longer an abstract moral stance; it's a plan being sketched in ink.

Lucas Herrera folds his goggles and sets them carefully on a bench. There is a moment where you both look at the device, at the way technology can translate tidal motion into visual models and also reframe marsh as a problem to be engineered away.

Lucas Herrera: "If you're going to the briefing, I'll be there. Not as the face of Atlantech — as a person who wants to make technical things listen to ecological things."
(He offers you a small, earnest smile.) "We can make them listen if we make them care."

Amaya Saito: (You meet his gaze. You can see the strain of someone trying to reconcile two things that look at odds. Hope, small and stubborn, warms you.) "Then come. Help us translate marsh metrics into something the council understands. Not to paper over, but to make them see the living geometry."

Lucas Herrera: "I'll do that. And I'll bring the models, but only if you let me bring one of your restoration plans alongside."

Amaya Saito: (You nod, something like agreement passing between you that isn't surrender.) "Bring the plan. We'll bring the people. We'll bring the stories."

[Sound: A distant church bell, or the harbor horn — the town waking up, schedules aligning]
[Music: Strings swell with an upward turn — hopeful]

You pin the final volunteer list into the notebook, slide the spiral shut, and feel a momentum that is not just in you: it runs through the pages, the seedlings, the names scrawled in different inks. Outside, the town will wake. In here, a fragile coalition is gathering: science, memory, stubborn hands.

Elena's car parks outside with a polite, courteous crunch on the gravel; the sound is a punctuation, not a threat. A figure in a practical blazer and a bright scarf approaches the boathouse door — the mayor, determined and diplomatic, her expression open.

Elena: (warmly) "Amaya. Good morning. I wanted to ask if you'd join the council briefing today. We need the restoration perspective at the table. I know you understand both hearts and data."
(She gestures at the seedlings as if to underline the point.)

Amaya Saito: (You feel a weight and a lift all at once. The invitation is both responsibility and opportunity.) "I'll be there."

Elena: (She smiles — an honest, town-smile.) "Bring those volunteer lists. Bring Dr. Kaito if he can make it. And Amaya..."
(She hesitates a fraction, then adds.) "Bring your father's journal if you think it will help frame the stakes."

Amaya Saito: (You thumb the brass clip. The seashell pendant rests against your sternum like a promise.) "I will."

[Visual: Show a slow pan over the greenhouse: seedlings, pinned lists, hands set to work]
Narration continues.

It is not the end of anything. It is the first clear stroke on a page. The marsh's fragility is still fragile; the seawall proposal still presses at the edges of the town's future. But inside the boathouse a small network is forming: voices ready to be counted, a mentor's stories waiting to be told, a practical engineer willing to listen.

You pin a map patch to your jacket sleeve, one more token of where you'll stand.

Your chest tightens — not with dread, but as if your lungs are making room. You imagine the council boardroom's fluorescent glare, the hum of a projector, the way people's faces will look when they hear the marsh's name spoken plainly. You think of your father's script and Dr. Kaito's slow, solid facts. You think of the volunteers in the lists, of Mika's mismatched boots on the dock steps, of Lucas folding his goggles like a small truce.

Hope, you realize, is also a decision you make in the small gestures: showing up, naming what matters, bringing other people with you.

[Page-turn breath — the greenhouse hums, the docks creak, and the town prepares]
