## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Veridian

---

## World View
Port Veridian is a small, near-future coastal town—one foot in tradition, one foot in experiment—where rising tides and stronger storms have become the daily weather you plan around. The municipal structure is pragmatic and precarious: a mayor who must juggle emergency grants, developers pressing for contracts, community councils where fishermen and restoration volunteers shout over one another, and elders who remember when the harbor was deeper and the seasons steadier. Economies split between family-run fisheries and artisanal trades face uncertain harvests; a new class of eco-designers and visiting scientists offer technical hope, while corporate developers promise fast money and concrete certainty.

Culture blends hands-on repair with low-tech sensors: tide gauges patched into repurposed buoys, patched waxed jackets and waterproof sketch rolls, community microgrids and volunteer tidewatch labs. Religion and ritual are localized—lighthouse blessings, harvest rites at the boardwalk—but civic faith centers on shared stewardship of the shore and stories of those lost at sea. Geography is intimate and visible: a narrow Main Street sloping to docks, reclaimed wetlands tethered to living dikes, the lighthouse like a conscience on the rock, and a development site scarred for progress. Atmosphere: salt-scrubbed, battered, and quietly hopeful—the air always contains salt-and-ozone and the sense that every decision will be felt in the tide line.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maya Ibarra) stand at the town’s fracture: short-term protection versus long-term resilience. The story begins as Port Veridian is battered by a storm season that makes old compromises impossible—boats lost, sensors flood, and the funding clock runs short. My initial choices plunge us deeper into crisis (Fall): arguments with Noah, pressure from Veridian Holdings, and the town’s fragile trust cracking. Choosing a path (with Elliot’s hybrid designs, Noah’s immediate protections, or the developer’s heavy infrastructure) opens a temporary ascent (Rise): I broker compromises, implement quick wins, and find a fragile alliance that restores confidence and daily safety. But the climb reveals structural contradictions and ethical debts. A later catastrophe—an engineered seawall failure, contaminated dredging, or an exposed tidal breach—forces a final plunge (Fall): marshlands are sacrificed, relationships fray, and I confront the consequences of the choices I made in trying to save everyone. Romance threads with Elliot and rivalry with Noah are entwined with policy: emotional compromises mirror ecological trade-offs. The ending varies by choices—some offer a fragile, ethical governance model and complicated love; others yield bitter loss—yet all follow the Fall→Rise→Fall arc: a descent into crisis, a provisional rescue, then an ultimate, costly downfall that leaves hard lessons and a call for continuing stewardship.
