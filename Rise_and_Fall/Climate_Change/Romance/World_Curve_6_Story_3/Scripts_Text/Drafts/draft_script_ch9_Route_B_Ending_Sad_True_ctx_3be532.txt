### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Ending: Sad/True


[Scene: Beacon Lighthouse | Late Afternoon — Several Months After the Seawall Construction]
[Visual: The lighthouse sits like a sentinel above a straight concrete lip — the seawall — its amber lamp pulsing slow. The horizon is flattened by a low, brassy sky. Marsh grasses that once blurred the edge of water are short, browned, and stunted. A pale salt crust gleams in places where the tide has seeped farther inland.]
[Music: Sparse piano, low and resonant; a single cello undercurrent]
[Sound: Wind carrying the distant rasp of machinery; fewer gull calls, a hollow echo where chorus used to be]

You stand on the rocky outcrop beneath the lighthouse and let the wind wash the salt into your hair. The seawall is a clean, unforgiving seam — a promise kept for roofs and jobs, a line that does not bend for tides. It hums with the memory of machines that never slept: the diesel taste beneath the fog, the hollow thrum of engines moving bulk and concrete, the metallic clink of anchors being set into rock.

Your fingers find the sea-glass pendant at your throat because you do not know where else to rest them. It is cool and honest against your skin; it does not answer. You listen instead for what has changed — the way the shore smells sharper now, like steeped brine instead of peat and reed; the way the boardwalks creak with a different weight because there is less life under the water to soften footprints.

Noah comes up the path with rope coiled over his shoulder, boots carrying the darker, permanent smell of work. He moves like someone who has settled an old account and keeps checking the till.

Noah: "You ought to be easy on yourself, Maya." (He leans against the railing, eyes following the seawall.) "We kept people from losing roofs. People are getting paychecks. That's something."

You watch his jaw work. He smells of tar and storm, of coffee brewed too long in a flask. For a long moment you simply watch him, counting the lines the years at sea have carved around his eyes.

You: "You saved a lot, Noah. But we traded a lot, too." (Your voice is quieter than you expect.) "The marsh is shrinking. The sensor logs—juvenile counts are down, salinity's creeping inland. The nets come back lighter."

Noah's face is unreadable for a second — and then the old fierceness reappears, less theatrical now, sharpened by long days. He holds your gaze like a plank, steady.

Noah: "We did what we had to. If the town floods, there are no nets to talk about. You know that." (He spits into the wind, an old ritual of defiance.) "Don't make it sound like I wanted marshes gone."

You: "I know you didn't want that." (You hear your own impatience.) "But you pushed for speed and guarantees. You convinced people who were tired and hungry. Now some of them won’t listen to anything that sounds like 'long-term.'"

Noah's voice loses volume, becomes almost a confession.

Noah: "I watched my father freeze on a boat once. I promised him I wouldn't let his kids drown in his harbor." (He looks away, at the seawall.) "If that costs birds, I can't sleep at night, but I can sleep with my family dry. Maybe you can't forgive me for that."

You: "I'm not trying to punish you." (You reach out before you can second-guess the gesture. Your hand hovers as if offering a bridge.) "I'm trying to understand how we became a place that treats habitat like a ledger."

Noah's jaw loosened, then tightened. He nods once, brief and brittle.

Noah: "Understand it all you want. But when the nets are empty and people are angry, talking won't fill bellies."

Noah doesn't stay long. He turns, the rope swinging, and the harbor takes him back into its rhythm. You remain on the rocks until the lamp blinks again, counting the light as if it were a metronome for your decisions.

[Scene Transition: Port Veridian — Council Hall | Midday — A Public Briefing]
[Visual: The atrium is crowded with folding chairs, posters, and a line of laptops showing declining graphs. Sad banners hang beside celebratory ones. The air smells of burnt coffee and damp coats.]
[Music: Quiet strings; a slow, descending motif]
[Sound: Murmurs, the rustle of paper, a child's intermittent cry]

Mayor Cortez stands at the podium with a stack of printed summaries. Her expression is that of someone who has learned to keep hope thin and practical. Beside her, an Aria Sol-type representative from Veridian Holdings — poised, immaculate — watches the room as though stage-managing the weather.

Mayor Cortez: "We've secured the town's flood insurance thresholds and steady employment over the winter months." (She taps the podium.) "That was not easy."

Aria (Veridian Holdings): "Our phased approach succeeded in stabilizing load-bearing soils and immediate tidal threat." (She smiles, practiced.) "The region watches Port Veridian as a model for scaling protection."

You present the monitoring packets you brought — the repeated charts, the sediment maps, the juvenile fish counts pinned in red. Your voice trembles in the beginning and steadies as you go on. You are both advocate and mourner.

You: "The seawall is doing what it was engineered to do. But the data shows cascading impacts: salinity intrusion inland by measurable margins, nursery habitats losing structure, shorebirds abandoning traditional sites. This is not hypothetical; it's happening now."

Drifts of cold air fall between statements like leaves. A man in the back claps once, slow, for the jobs. A younger woman near the front shakes her head at the red lines on your charts.

Mayor Cortez: (softly) "We balanced lives and livelihoods. I took a lot of calls. I had to choose the option that would keep electricity on and food on tables while we secure longer-term grants."

You: "And what will we do when the longer-term grants fail to materialize?" (The question scrapes across the atrium.) "Who tends the marsh? Who pays for adaptive governance when the immediate money dries up?"

Aria: "Adaptive governance is part of our proposal's second phase. We can negotiate monitoring timelines." (Her tone remains smooth.) "The economic multiplier of secured ports and industries will provide funding."

You: "We have declining juvenile counts now. If the multiplier assumes biological recovery that isn't happening, what then? No one modeled that scenario into the funding schedule."

Mayor Cortez breathes out, the weight of leadership making the air thin.

Mayor Cortez: "We made a trade. We will need to manage the consequences as best we can."

There is no applause for that line. Only the low murmur that follows such truths.

You leave the hall with your charts damp from the drizzle that has started again. The town swallows the briefing like a tide — some parts drawn in, some left behind.

[Scene Transition: Harbor Wharf | Dawn — The Morning Elliot Leaves]
[Visual: Gray dawn, low mist hugging rigs and ropes. Elliot stands under a tarpaulin, a small suitcase at his feet, the roll-up sketch cloth under his arm. His face is pale, sharper than you remember.]
[Music: A solitary violin, keening gently]
[Sound: Small, intimate — a kettle boiling on a distant deck, the muted cry of an early gull]

Elliot: (softly) "They offered me a fellowship to study coastal interventions in diverse contexts. A chance to look at what works, and what didn't, from a distance." (He avoids looking at the seawall directly.) "It’s... an opportunity."

You: "You were the one who argued for a hybrid, for nuance." (You press your thumb against the sea-glass pendant.) "Why leave just when the model needs people here to defend it?"

Elliot: (a small, sad smile) "Because sometimes you have to step away to see whether what you built actually scales without being co-opted." (He meets your eyes.) "And because I keep getting pulled toward projects where the trade-offs are framed in metrics I can't stand by without checking them myself. This fellowship is to learn how to make designs that hold up against people like Aria as well as people like Noah."

You: "So you think you can make it better from a distance."

Elliot: "I don't know. I hope so." (He takes a breath.) "Maya, you taught me how important place-based knowledge is. Leaving won't make me forget that."

Elliot reaches for your hand. The contact is brief and deliberate; a human attempt to tether the future.

Elliot: "We could try—long-distance, collaboration, something." (He swallows.) "But I understand if you can't—if Port Veridian needs you."

You: "It needs me in ways I can't always be. I know that." (Your voice breaks and you cough it away.) "If you go, promise me one thing: don't let their reports sanitize the costs. Don't let the charts tell a prettier story than the shoreline."

Elliot: "I promise." (He releases your hand, turns his suitcase toward the waiting vehicle.) "I'll write. I'll try to make useful trouble."

He gives you a look like someone folding a map he doesn't want to lose. Then he walks away, shoulders square, leaving you with the hollow step of someone leaving a room after a long meeting.

The harbor swallows his figure. The sky opens a little more, like a bruise being exposed to air.

[Scene Montage: The Months That Follow]
[Visual: A montage of small, sharp images — sensor readouts with red ticks, a nets-of-a-fisherman coming back half-full, a black-and-white photo in Rosa's gallery of a marsh stubble, a community board with 'Thanks for the Work' cards pinned next to 'Who Will Tend the Marsh?'. The lighthouse lamp turning on, then off, through a year of weather.]
[Music: Low, elegiac piano with occasional wind chimes]
[Sound: Distant machinery, a single bird call fading into static]

You watch the data accumulate like a ledger that does not add up in anyone's favor. Juvenile fish counts dip season after season. Salinity sensors show salt moving inland in small, relentless increments. Nesting counts drop; species shift where they nest, if they nest at all. A handful of workers get stable contracts. Some fisheries revise quotas downward. A few small businesses that had depended on the ebb of tourists and fish shutter. The town's payrolls are steadier, but the old seasonal rhythms are gone.

Neighbors you used to trade eggs with now avoid your eye at the market, not because you are blamed outright, but because you are a reminder of a decision that changed the way the town breathes. There are quiet conversations that begin with, "We needed something," and end with silence about what was given away.

Noah is elected to a new committee overseeing harbor operations. He keeps his distance from social gatherings; the applause for his leadership is sometimes thin, sometimes exuberant. Pride and isolation live together in his shoulders.

Elliot's postcards come in bursts, full of maps and questions. He writes about new methods and old mistakes; he sends a photograph once of a successful hybrid trial in another town and remarks that context matters in ways that can't be exported. You answer sometimes, with long, careful notes about soil, about people, about the things that numbers do not capture. Your correspondence is an attempt to stitch a network; it is not always enough to mend what was worn.

[Scene: Shoreline Beneath the Seawall | Dusk — Your Departure]
[Visual: You packing a battered satchel on the low cliff path. The seawall lines the horizon, a hard jaw against the water. Low light pools in the slick ruts of the access road. The lighthouse amber begins its rotation.]
[Music: A slow, resigned cello line; a distant soprano wind]
[Sound: The soft clack of a suitcase latch, the whisper of the sea, one close gull call]

You close the satchel and test its weight. Inside are the printed reports you could not let go of, a notebook full of marginalia, the smartwatch with tide logs, a small roll of photographs — Rosa's work that both indicts and comforts. Your sea-glass pendant rests warm against your palm before you loop it over your head.

You walk the path you walked as a child, the one that used to have drier reedbeds to hide in, the one that now has a thin crust of salt where the water crept closer. The town behind you is awake with lights and small sound: the hum of a freezer, the muttered voices from a late shift, the clatter of a café stirring for a night crew. It is ordinary and precious in the same beat.

At the small pier, Noah is there — not an overseer now, but a man who has kept his oath to his family. He does not offer an embrace, but he holds out a pocketknife, a simple truce tied into a leather thong.

Noah: "Keep it." (He manages a small, lopsided smile.) "If you ever come back for a night, cut a rope with it and let me know you made it through."

You: "I'll come back." (You say it because you mean it in the sense of a tide that always remembers its bed.) "Not always in the same way."

Noah: "That's all anyone can promise." (He turns away first, the harbor taking him like a familiar song.)

You step into the waiting vehicle that will take you to the station and then to a regional office where they want you to help design governance for coastal adaptation across a broader span — a place where policies might outlast a single town's impatience. You do it because you believe the work will matter. You do it because staying feels, in some nights now, like consent to costs you cannot carry forever.

You walk a little way and then look back, at the seawall etching the horizon into a hard line. For a moment you imagine the marsh you wanted — a ribbed living dike of willows and woven coir, communities mending with the rhythm of seasons. That image is a small, private thing. It is also a wound.

You: (inward, steady) "I will make what I can of this. I will carry witness."

You touch the pendant one last time, and let it rest. The lighthouse lamp swings once, then twice, pulsing amber into the dusk.

[Visual: Wide shot of the seawall, the lighthouse, and the small figure leaving along the road — the town's lights small and honest behind. The tide moves against a built edge; the sound of it is steady and indifferent to promises.]
[Music: Cello fades to a single sustained note and then silence]
[Sound: The wind, soft; the faint click of the car door closing]

You do not close the chapter on Port Veridian with triumph. There are protections that hold roofs and bellies. There are scars in the mud and loss in the sound of birds that no longer sing where they used to. People survived; ecosystems were compromised. Trust has been fractured into pieces that will take years, maybe generations, to collect.

You carry the burden and the knowledge of what was done and what might be done differently elsewhere. That is the cost and the work that will follow you through the years. It is, in its own way, both failure and truth.

[Visual: Fade to Black]
[Music: Final, low piano chord; a small wind chime fades]
