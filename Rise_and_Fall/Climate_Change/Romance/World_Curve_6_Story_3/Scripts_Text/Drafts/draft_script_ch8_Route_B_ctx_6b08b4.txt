### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Ending: Sad/True


[Scene: Seawall Construction Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A long, reinforced concrete seawall slices the light; cranes punctuate the gray horizon; spray mists across the promenade]
[Sound: Distant clank of metal, the steady hum of heavy equipment, gulls quieter than usual]
[Music: Low, somber strings; a minor piano motif underlines the rhythm of machines]

You stand at the lip of the new seawall, the concrete cold even from a distance, and the town's applause feels like a tide you can't quite ride. Workers in neon vests sling ropes and clap each other on the back; someone whistles an old fishing tune, but the second verse dies where the marsh used to be. Salt and diesel mix in the air, and the smell stitches itself into a bitter knot in your chest.

You touch the edge of your satchel the way you always do when you need to remember why you're here—prints, sensor logs, a hand-sketched section of living dike that now sits, unlawfully or not, outside a mayoral ordinance. You can still imagine boots sinking into mud among roots, the reedbeds soft and warm under your palms. That image is private; it insists on staying with you even as people cheer.

Noah works the line like a captain delivering a speech. His voice is coarse with wind and triumph.

Noah: "We got work. We got contracts. Kids'll eat this winter. You think I don't know what that looks like? I grew up with nets and empty lines. Today—today some of that comes back."

You watch him accept a handshake from a foreman whose son will be on the dredge. Workers cluster around Noah like buoys; his grin is steady, practiced. The world around him looks repaired in the way makeshift fixes can appear repaired—solid from one angle, fragile from another.

You step closer, and the sound of a hydraulic pump fills the space between you.

Maya: "This isn't forever," you say, voice low so your accusation and your plea blur together. "We are trading habitat for a promise. Promises have a way of becoming contracts that never get revisited."

Noah: (laughs, but not unkindly) "You think I don't know that? You think I don't count the cost? I do, every morning I choke on salt that wasn't there before. But the cost of waiting—Maya, that's what killed the last generation's boats. You wanted action. This is action."

He meets your eyes—face wind-carved and sincere. There's no malice here, only pressure, belief, and the narrowness of his solution. You remember working alongside him in better days; you remember the fracture. Now the fracture has a public face.

Maya: "You say that like it's a choice I didn't make. I voted for this to buy time—to buy leverage for restoration trials. I didn't vote for the marsh to be erased."

Noah: "We do what we can with what we have. People are cold. They need certainty."

The exchange coils on itself. Around you, other conversations knot and separate. A foreman claps Noah's shoulder and raises a plastic cup. Someone passes out hot sandwiches. The town feels like a ledger balancing labor against loss.

[Scene: Docks and Wharf | Early Evening]
[Visual: Puddled planks, damp tarps, the glow of portable floodlights; gulls cluster on pilings like punctuation marks]
[Sound: Low conversation, the creak of ropes, the hollow thunk of nets being folded]
[Music: A spare cello line; wind through rigging creates an elegiac counterpoint]

You walk the wet boards where the fishermen used to pull in schools of silvery heads. Now the boxes are fewer; fish counts logged at the lab have been falling for months. The wharf's ritual—nets hauled at dawn with shouts and jokes—has been replaced by measured shifts and careful inventorying. Noah greets everyone like a man passing out lifelines, and people accept them.

Tomas stands at the end of the wharf with his cane, watching the waves as if reading them. He says nothing at first; his silence has the weight of histories.

Tomas: (slow, deliberate) "We don't get the storms we used to, not the same shape. The water moves like a different mind now."

You stand beside him. The familiar lantern at his belt keeps a small, stubborn light. His eyes find yours and hold—long enough to make both of you feel smaller and older.

Maya: "They called it protection. They called it safety. But I can hear the marsh dying in the contraption's groan. I can taste the reedbeds every time a truck rolls in."

Tomas: (grunts) "You did what you had to do. Folks needed bread. But don't let the bread blind you to the rest of the table. These things... they come to mean more than their makers intend."

You think of your father—his absence a hole shaped like an empty chair, like a boat that never returned. The logic that led you here tugs at you: sometimes to keep people afloat you must cut away the things that made life sweet. The math is brutal.

Noah joins you and Tomas, hands tucked into his oilskin. For once, his victory is tempered by a glance at the marshline, and it softens his face.

Noah: "They're calling for more dredging tomorrow. We'll push the channel out another twenty meters. Keep the boats running. It's work. It's more than words."

Maya: "You understand what dredging does to the nursery beds, right? The fry rely on the quiet channels. You know that."

Noah: "And you understand what it does to keep a family in a house. Tell me which face matters more when it's your own child."

The conversation tilts between them—no winner. Each argument carries a ledger of grief and necessity. When you press, his jaw tightens. He looks like someone who has weighed hard choices in the dark and chosen a path that kept certain people alive. He is not a villain; he is a man making a trade none of you wanted.

He says, 'We can't wait for labs to decide if our kids get dinner.' His voice breaks on the last word, and you feel the absoluteness of his need.")]


[Scene: Town Hall Atrium / Community Forum | Night]
[Visual: Folding chairs still half-stacked, stained-glass panels throw muted color on the floor; a taped-up map of the shoreline hangs with pushpins]
[Sound: Low murmurs, an undercurrent of printers and the distant hiss of heat lamps]
[Music: Quiet, pulsing synth; a single dissonant violin note repeats]

The town hall smells of coffee and nervous ozone from late-night printing. Mayor Lianne Cortez's office door is open; through it you can see Aria Sol's polished silhouette against a wall of promotional renderings. The renderings show glassy promenades and tidy marinas—pleasant images with the marsh erased like a smudge.

Mayor Cortez stands with her hands folded. Her suit is impeccable, maritime in motif, and her face carries the diplomacy of someone who has learned to weigh votes like tides.

Mayor Cortez: "Maya. We had to move quickly. The vote passed. The ordinance is in effect. The contracts are signed. There were—complications."

Aria: (smiling, too smoothly) "Complications are only problems if you let them be. Veridian Holdings is committed to the town's security—and to launching development that will mean jobs for years."

You step into the room, the fluorescent light catching the silver of Aria's cuff and the creased map on the table. Your satchel feels heavy. The living-dike sketches are folded inside like a confession.

Maya: "Complications? There was a permit that skipped the review board. There's a fast-track endorsement signed with a contractor list. Someone waived public consultation on the environmental assessment."

Mayor Cortez: (a small, pained intake) "We couldn't afford the delay. There were threats of withdrawal, Maya. The company was clear: without these assurances, the funding vanishes and the armoring contract doesn't get executed. You know what that would have meant."

Aria: "We expedited the process within legal bounds. The permit aligns with municipal risk assessments. Our engineering teams—"

Maya: "Legal bounds aren't the same as ethical ones." Your voice tightens. "We promised participatory oversight. We promised monitoring. Those promises are on paper somewhere between signatures."

Mayor Cortez: (sighs) "I am trying to keep the town together. I have seen petitions, and I've seen families at risk. I chose to secure something tangible. That decision cost us—politically, and personally. But it kept people from being immediate—literally—washed out."

Aria: (leaning in) "Mayor, the model we proposed actually includes phased environmental offsets. We'll fund restoration elsewhere and deploy mitigation tech. This is how modern development works."

Maya: "Offsets are not always offsets," you answer. "They can become excuses to displace responsibility. Marshes can't be moved like accounting entries."

Tomas appears in the doorway, his shadow long on the floor. He places a slow hand on the map.

Tomas: "Words and ledgers. Don't let the ledgers be the story."

The room tightens. There is a stack of stamped paperwork on Aria's table, a glossy permit with embossed seal and a contractor roster. It gleams under the lamp like an accusation.

Rosa, who was photographing outside, arrives with her camera still warm in her hands and a print tucked under her arm. She hands you a photograph of the permit—an image of paperwork that now seems to represent more than signatures: it is the line between what you fought for and what the town will now be.

Rosa: "This is public record, M. They fast-tracked it last week. I got the printed copy from the clerk before they took it down. People should see this."

Your stomach flips. The hollow feeling you have tried to keep in check spreads like cold oil. You think of all the conversations you had where compromise was the necessary path—how you traded for leverage, believing you could hold both worlds. Now leverage looks brittle and compromised in an altogether different way.

Mayor Cortez: "I know how this will look. I knew the optics. But I also know what happened when we hesitated. I couldn't—"

Maya: "You didn't have to fast-track it in secrecy. You didn't have to let a private contractor write the timeline for our ecology. We promised oversight. Where's the audit? Where's the community monitor?"

Aria: "We will schedule audits. We will comply with reporting. The company is eager to be seen as a good partner."

Maya: (under your breath) "Words again."

The conversation circles—each sentence an attempt to shore the town against a storm by pushing some part of it into the deep. You had come here to steer a compromise that kept marsh and men, but watching the permit and the glossy renderings, you feel the compromise telescoped into a narrower shape than you intended.

Elliot arrives quietly, his trench coat damp. He sets his leather portfolio on the table and opens it—sketches, sensor logs, community-sourced data spread under the lamp.

Elliot: (softly) "I can design monitoring protocols—independent sensors, distributed data streams. If we can get guaranteed, public access to datasets, we might be able to hold them to account."

Aria: (cool) "We welcome collaboration. Transparency builds trust."

Elliot: (measured) "Transparency without enforceable legal conditions is theater. We need clauses—hard triggers if fish counts decline, if turbidity spikes. Penalties that return funding to restoration pools."

Mayor Cortez: "Those clauses are negotiable, Elliot. But remember—every negotiation risks funding being pulled. We are balancing on a very thin wire."

Maya: "A thin wire that could snap." Your hands feel cold. "I thought I could hold this wire without falling. Now I don't know if I'm the one pulling it taut until it breaks."

Elliot reaches for your hand—a gesture half-technical, half-personal.

Elliot: (whisper) "We can force the clauses. I have allies who will champion it. But if they pull funding, the armoring stops—and people lose jobs and homes. There is no clean solution here."

You look at Elliot. You see in his face the same hunger for elegant fixes that always defined him—plans that fold into people's lives without crushing them. But tonight his eyes are tired; compromise has worn him thin.

[Scene: Seawall Promenade Overlook | Nightfall]
[Visual: The seawall casts a long shadow; the town lights pierce the fog; a dim reflection trembles on the wet promenade]
[Sound: The sea against concrete, a single gull call, distant radio chatter]
[Music: A low, unresolved chord; a drawn-out cello line that wanes into silence]

You walk the promenade alone after the meetings. The seawall feels monumental and small at once—monumental in the scale of its promise, small because it cannot carry the memory of the mud under your boots. The work lights throw long strips of illumination; machines stand like sleeping beasts. Behind the wall, the estuary is a strange, flattened mirror, reflecting a sky that refuses to clear.

Your clasp tightens on the sea-glass pendant at your throat. It is cool, a tiny island of history. You let your thoughts move through the day like a tide pooling around the rocks of decision: workers' hands, Tomas's slow counsel, the sealed permit, Elliot's clauses, Rosa's camera, Mayor Cortez's exhausted face, Aria's polished smile.

You had thought this choice—this path of armoring and dredging—would be a lever: secure the town now, hold political capital, then spend that capital on restoration. The reality tastes different. The lever has worn teeth. Money and urgency have teeth.

You feel complicit. Your voice carried weight in the vote that made this ordinance real. Your argument was part of the calculus; your want for action—immediate, visible—helped create a policy that may erode the very things you swore to protect.

A child laughs somewhere inland—two syllables of life. A nearby radio broadcasts a notice about work schedules. The sounds are ordinary and necessary, and they make your hollow feel like a luxury you cannot afford.

You look back at the seawall—solid, straight, a line chosen against the sea. You think of the marshes receding, of bird songs growing thinner on the counts, of the markets with fewer fish. You think of the people who will sleep easier tonight and the ecosystems that will lose more tomorrow.

This is the moment to feel what the choice has cost you personally and what it has bought the town. The chord of the day is not resolution; it is a hardening. The harbor functions. People are working. Political power has shifted. But something softer—rituals, the marsh, certain livelihoods—will not return as they were.

You breathe in the salt and the machinery. The air is colder now, edged with diesel and regret. You know what comes next: the hardened shore, lawsuits, audits, monitoring data that will call truth to the surface. You can already hear the quiet steps of work crews and the quieter steps of birds as they move on.

You turn away from the wall with the photographs and the copies for records tucked into your satchel. For a moment you allow yourself to feel the whole of your complicity: the votes, the arguments, the times you told yourself the ends justified the means.

You walk into the downpour of streetlight and fog, and the town closes around you—both shielded and diminished.

You imagine Rosa's photos becoming evidence in a hearing, Elliot's clauses either enforced or overridden, Noah's name on the lips of every grateful worker. You see a shoreline that is harder, safer, and quieter. You feel the fall of this day like a cold tide: it secures some things and erases others. You step into the night with the knowledge that the next morning will bring the first real data on what those trades have cost.]
