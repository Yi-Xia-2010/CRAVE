### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C — Ending: Sad/True
# Context: c4ca42

[Scene: Council Hall / Community Forum | Midday]
[Visual: Wide shot of the atrium filling with people — folding chairs, posters, and a scatter of umbrellas leaning against a wall]
[Music: Low, insistent strings; a slow drum undercurrent]
[Sound: Murmured voices, the scrape of a chair, a distant gull calling]

You step into the atrium carrying the Tidewatch prints stacked against your chest like a small, wet island. The paper rustles; some corners are damp where rain sneaked past your collar. The buzz of the room presses at your ears: people clustering by issue, children tucked under thighs, someone handing a thermos around. Coffee steam fogs in the shaft of light that falls through the high glass.

The mayor's dais has been set up beneath the stained-glass panel that washes the room in warm color. Mayor Lianne Cortez moves with that practiced, political gait — a little smile for each person, a steadying hand when hands reach hers. Tomas sits near the front, his beard wind-softened and his hands folded, like a man rehearsed in waiting.

[Visual: Close-up of the Tidewatch map draped across a folding table; colored pins mark marsh edges, pilot coir barriers, and sensor clusters]
[Sound: Papering flap as you arrange your prints; the whisper of pins being nudged]

Mayor Cortez: "Thank you all for coming. We know this is tense, and we will give everyone time to speak. We will be… orderly."

Her smile has a crack in it you can feel. You set the prints down and smooth them with a palm that smells faintly of salt and wet leather. You are certain of the evidence on these pages — the sediment graphs, the projected inundation lines, the sensor logs — and you are also painfully aware that certainty alone does not build consensus.

Maya Ibarra: "I—" (you begin, throat tight; your voice feels thin against the room) "—I want to show what the living dike option does. Not just the diagrams, but the trade-offs: where it will protect homes, how it restores marsh function, how it phases with fishing seasons."

Elliot Chen stands beside you with the roll-up sketch cloth tucked under his arm. He unfurls a clear composite of your maps with his schematics at the edge, two kinds of faith laid out side by side.

Elliot Chen: "Phasing buys us time. We can pair engineered revetments in the most vulnerable choke points with coir-anchored plantings farther out. It’s adaptive — it learns with the tide."

Noah Rhee's voice cuts from the back of the room like a line through water. He stands now, salt in the seams of his jacket, voice raw with the particular hard edge of someone who measures the world by the work it demands.

Noah Rhee: "Adaptive is a word. My folks need nets in the water this fall, not theories. We can't tide-watch while the bay empties us out. We need armor — seawalls, dredging, contracts for the crews. Fast. Guaranteed."

The room answers. A half-dozen fishermen clap; a woman by the door curses softly. The air shifts from debate to contest.

Aria Sol rises with the polished confidence of an outsider who learned the room long before she learned the town. Her pitch is a practiced tide: clean, inevitable.

Aria Sol: "Veridian Holdings can fund immediate protections across Main Street, the docks, and key fish-processing yards. Our seawall reduces risk now, while investment in logistics guarantees employment. We have capital, we have permits on a quick timeline, and we have partners."

You listen to the renderings she folds into her words — bright, impermeable images without birds or soft margins. They glint the same way her cuff does in the light. The promise they offer is ordinary and arresting: certainty, in exchange for the marsh.

Mayor Cortez tries to steer, but every word she says is a balancing act on a rope thin with politics.

Mayor Cortez: "We must weigh ecological impacts, jobs, and immediate safety. Dr. Kira—"

She falters when Tomas raises a weathered hand. Tomas is not a scientist in suits; he's a keeper of coastline stories, and his voice is slow but full of old tides.

Tomas: "Listen. When my pa told me about how the marsh used to hold storms like a sponge, he wasn't romanticizing. We're not only deciding what's built; we're deciding what remembers Port Veridian."

A chorus now: hands waving, voices layering. The room tightens. You feel it like pressure in your chest — the same way a storm presses a roof.

[Sound: A chair bangs; someone shouts about jobs; a child cries; a camera shutter — Rosa's — clicks quietly, capturing everything]

Rosa (from the side, near the back): "People need to see what they're choosing. Photos, timelines — show them their kids’ school under water if we do nothing, show them the fish that rely on the marsh."

You catch Rosa's look — fierce compassion, the kind that photographs grief to keep it honest. Her camera clicks are punctuation against the heated words.

Aria Sol (leaning forward; measured): "We are not asking you to choose beauty over survival. We are offering a route that keeps livelihoods intact. Living dikes are experimental. What if they fail? Funders won't wait."

Elliot Chen [low, steady]: "They're not untested. They are scaled, and if we phase them, we can adjust. We can protect both the waterfront and the marsh — if we let the process breathe."

Noah Rhee [interrupting]: "Breathing won't help when the dock slips from under our boots. My crew went out last week and came back with nets full of mud and no catch. They need paystubs, Maya, not promises."

The air thickens. Someone near the front hisses: "Promises don't feed kids." Another voice: "And walls don't make a town!"

You want to say more. You want to list sediment accretion curves and community co-management models until every person in the room understands the algebra of tides and time. But words alone are thin: the crowd needs something visceral.

You flip to a chart — a simple overlay showing where living dikes could be staged and where engineered protection would be concentrated. You point: your finger shakes.

Maya Ibarra: "We can do both in places, if we prioritize. If we accept targeted armor where the infrastructure is critical and let the marsh do the rest—"

Noah Rhee's laugh is sudden and hard.

Noah Rhee: "Prioritize? That's what they tell me after my family's house floods. I'm not asking to prioritize anything. I'm asking to survive."

Your stomach tightens. The room tilts between two kinds of fear: the immediate hunger for security, and the slow ache of ecological loss. You hear the word "experiment" used like an insult. You hear "seawall" used like a salve.

Either way, the debate hardens. Someone from the contractors' table — a man with a neon vest and a clipboard — stands to ask blunt questions about cost and timeline. Aria answers crisply; Noah counters with a list of hands-on needs. The dialogue becomes a volley.

Mayor Cortez: "We will assemble a technical committee and a community oversight group. We need data and guarantees. Can we agree on a moratorium on contracts until that group recommends a path?"

A ripple of dissent. Aria's jaw tenses; funds and tempos tangle in the background like lines in a net.

Aria Sol: "A moratorium halts investment. Veridian Holdings may reconsider if the process is not timely."

Noah Rhee [cold]: "Then your 'investment' becomes leverage to force decisions on our terms. I don't trust 'timely' from a company that designs from a rendering."

Elliot Chen [quiet, trying to bridge]: "We can write enforceable milestones. We can bind funding to ecological performance and job guarantees. The metrics exist."

A man near the door — an operator who handles dredging contracts — scoffs.

Operator: "Performance metrics don't pay the crew when the tide's taken their paycheck. You talk metrics; we talk mouths."

The room fragments along fault lines you feel more than see. People pair into camps: immediate protection and jobs, adaptive measures and long-term viability, private money and public oversight. It is not a neat break. It is a series of small fissures that join into one large crack.

You catch a glance from Elliot Chen. It is complex — an expression that could be warmth, plea, or accusation. You don't know which; you only know he is watching you measure the room.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Call for a pause and offer to host a hands-on demonstration at the Living Dike site tomorrow"
> Choice B: "Demand a formal vote today to force a commitment"
[Outcome A: "You suggest a tangible visit: boots in mud, people seeing the pilot with their own eyes. A few nods. Skepticism remains, but some hands go up — curiosity warming like coals."]
[Outcome B: "You press for a vote; the suggestion sparks fury. Noah slams a fist on a table. People shout; the meeting teeters toward chaos. The mayor's hand goes to her papers, unable to steady the room."]
[MERGE: Both paths alter the tenor of the meeting—one grounding debate in shared experience, the other deepening the fracture—but the meeting continues and a compromise is proposed.]
[/INTERACTION]

The pause you call — or the vote you force — changes the tenor. If you offer the demonstration, the argument grows roots in shared soil. If you demand the vote, the fracture deepens into raw edges.

Rosa photographs it all, then walks toward you with a card of images looping on her tiny screen: flooded market stalls, a child's small sneaker half-submerged in brackish water, a line of gulls on a desolate pier. She presses the screen into your hand.

Rosa: "People have to feel it to care. We can show them both the wall renders and the marsh, side-by-side."

You want to believe that seeing will soften hardened convictions. You want to believe people will choose the long view when they see what the short one costs. But the room is full of exhausted people making decisions with hungry bellies and rent unpaid. Hope is a fragile currency.

Voices rise. The mayor bangs her gavel and calls for order, but order is a thin skin stretched over a wound. The council's proposal to form a committee wins tentative support, but the compromise feels like a bandage on a long break.

Noah Rhee (to you, close enough that you smell the salt on his breath): "Don't let them tell you the marsh can put dinner on the table. If you want the marsh, you deal with us. You tell them what sacrifices you'll make."

Maya Ibarra: "It's not about choosing the marsh over people. It's about not trading one survival for another's doom. I want both, Noah."

Noah Rhee's eyes narrow. His face is unreadable for a heartbeat that stretches. He steps back into the tide of the crowd, his shoulders a rigid anchor. You don't know whether he's heard you as compromise or as a slight.

Elliot Chen crosses to your side; his voice is softer now, threaded with a fear he rarely lets surface.

Elliot Chen: "You can't do it alone, Maya. Let them see the pilot. Let the committee write enforceable milestones. Let us bind funding to ecological targets and crew contracts."

You feel the familiar tug: your instinct to carry the whole town's burdens like a price you must pay alone. It is exhaustion wrapped in duty. You think of your father's empty chair and a lifetime of holding.

The forum dissolves into smaller clusters; people spill into the atrium like runoff. Some faces are angry, some relieved, some blank with exhausted resignation. The mayor schedules the technical committee meeting for next week, with a public demonstration at the Living Dike pilot within days. It is a compromise on paper. It is not a peace.

[Visual: The atrium windows show the sea — gray, flat, patient, as if nothing above can hurry it]
[Sound: Quiet rainamble on the glass; the low ringing of post-meeting chatter]

You gather your prints, but now the paper feels insufficient. The Tidewatch map sits heavy on the table you leave. The sea beyond the window is flat and impatient — a horizon that offers neither answer nor reprieve.

Tomas lingers, his voice slow as he hands you a small, worn rope knot — a keepsake from a coastline repair his father once taught him.

Tomas: "Whatever you choose to anchor to, make sure someone remembers why we chose it. Memory keeps the tide honest."

His words drop in your chest like a stone. Memory, like marshland, holds back water a little while longer.

You feel suddenly raw: the weight of the meeting, the taste of salt in your mouth, the weight of expectation upon your shoulders. You could catalog every technical contingency, every political hazard, but it would not change that the room left with its opinions more set than the tide.

You step out into the light-washed hallway with Rosa at your side. She doesn't speak at first; her camera is quiet, but her presence is a steadying hand.

Rosa: "You did your best. You'll make them see, Maya. You always do."

You place a hand at your throat, fingers closing on the sea-glass pendant. It is cool, a small anchor between clavicle and wind. You let the pendant rest against your skin and inhale the brine-laced air.

The day has given you a plan on paper, a committee, and a future meeting. It has also given you a fracture you can feel underfoot. Voices that were once friends have hardened into policy positions. The town's debate has become a load you no longer know how to carry alone without splintering.

[Music: Low, unresolved cellos; a single piano note repeating like a question]
[Visual: Tight on your hand around the pendant; the sea in the window beyond, unreadable]

You want to speak to Elliot, to Noah, to Mayor Cortez — to repair the rifts with words that are both true and tender. You want to refuse to choose between people and place. The room has told you otherwise.

A small, stubborn thought edges in: choices now will anchor Port Veridian — but anchors can both save and drag. You realize the next move will define not just policy, but who you are in this town.

The air seems to still for a second. Rain slides down the window like a slow erasing. You feel the weight of what needs to be decided, a slow, downward pressure that does not relent.

You breathe. You let the pendant rest. The horizon sits, flat and indifferent, as if waiting for you to give it a reason to move.

[Page-Turn Moment: You imagine the living dike in the early light — your boots in mud, Rosa photographing people who once argued pressed side by side, Elliot tracing a plan on soaked cardboard. You imagine Noah, hands blistered and working, reconciling to a version of protection that keeps nets and marsh intact. The image is fragile and laden with debt. The next choice you make will test whether Port Veridian holds as a place of memory, livelihood, or something else entirely. You step forward with the prints in your satchel and the sea at your back, feeling the fall of this day like a tide pulling you toward a decision that cannot wait.]
