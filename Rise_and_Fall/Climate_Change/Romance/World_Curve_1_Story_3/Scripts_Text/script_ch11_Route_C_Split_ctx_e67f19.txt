# Route: Route C (Ending: Happy/Expansive)
# Context: e67f19

[Scene: City Hall — Council Committee Room | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the chamber; tiered seating rings a polished reclaimed-wood dais. Rain frets the skylights above, turning the light into soft, forgiving silver. A row of community posters—hand-lettered—hangs along one wall.]
[Music: Warm strings with a steady, hopeful underpinning; a muted brass countermelody]
[Sound: Soft murmur of arriving people; the distant hush of rain on the courtyard tiles]

You arrive early, as you always do—because schedules in this city are small seams you can mend with punctuality. The room smells like old paper, lemon cleaning oil, and the faint mineral tang of seawater tracked in on coats. Your boots sink a fraction into the carpeted aisle; the sound is muffled, domestic in a place that could be sterile.

You feel Elias before you see him: a small, steady presence at the end of the public benches, his tablet clutched like ballast. He looks up and gives a half-smile that is both apology and encouragement. The tiny loop of the brass compass at his throat glints when he leans forward to whisper.

Elias Harrow [soft]: "The appendix is uploaded to Mateo's folder. I tightened the vulnerability section where Lucia's team raised concerns. I wrote a short preface tying the pilot metrics to budgetary milestones."

Mara Solenne: "Thank you. Whatever happens today, that mapping—numbers and narrative together—keeps this from being just a speech."

Elias watches how the dais reflects in your eyes; for a second he looks pleased and frightened at once. You know what he is weighing: the consequences of public choices on his career, and on the small, fragile thing between you.

[Visual: Councilor Mateo Qu enters, papers in hand, damp at the edges; he nods to you with a practiced, weary kindness.]
Councilor Mateo Qu: "Mara. Elias. The committee's packed. Lucia asked for a word before we begin; she says it's procedural—but you know how she is."

You take a breath that tastes faintly of peat and metal. It's steadier than it felt yesterday. The work has accrued into something tangible: letters folded into a packet, a defensible appendix, testimonies queued and practiced. Each small compliance is a rung on a ladder you are building toward safety.

[Sound: The gavel tap rehearses in your ears—only metaphorical, but you feel it like a heartbeat.]

Rani slips in behind you carrying a thermos and the smell of sawdust and toasted bread.

Rani Cho [grinning]: "You look like you mean business. Did you bring anything dramatic? A chorus? A dog? We could always train the dog to hold the petition."

You both laugh—short, bright—because levity is adhesive at moments like this. Eda, quiet and steady, folds her shawl and offers you a look that holds a thousand small seasons of the marsh.

Eda Nal [soft]: "Speak plain. They like plans. They forget the tide. Make them remember both."

Your chest warms. Hope is not a single bright flare today; it's a layered, practical thing—rising with the tide of small, persistent acts.

[Visual: Lucia Montrose arrives with that crisp cape and an unreadable expression; she stands near the dais, speaking calmly with an aide. Her eyes find yours briefly—complex, like a ledger with a smudge.]
[Music: A gentle string lift; not alarm, but heightening]

Lucia Montrose [approaches; formal]: "Councilor Qu. Mara Solenne, thank you for your packet. I want to be clear: my office supports initiatives that increase survivability. I'm prepared to discuss matching funds for targeted measures."

Your muscles want to tighten, reflexive. Instead you keep your voice steady.

Mara Solenne: "Matching funds for targeted measures sounds useful. Our concern is that narrow measures can become islands—leaving the rest of the city exposed and displacing communities who rely on the very habitats you're asking to protect."

Lucia's smile is precise, almost admiring.

Lucia Montrose: "I understand the concern. My team favors scale where it is necessary—and caution where it is prudent. We are not opposed to living buffers, Mara. We question where to begin."

There's an edge of negotiation in that sentence, but it's not dismissal—it's an opening. You feel that as a small, undeniable victory.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Lead with community testimony"
> Choice B: "Lead with the appendix and figures"
[Outcome A: You decide to open the hearing with Eda's story—an old photograph and a short, unvarnished telling of loss and repair. The room leans forward at the human cadence; eyes soften.]
[Outcome B: You choose to begin with Elias' data—sensors, cost projections, and modeled flood reduction. The committee listens, taut with technical interest.]
[MERGE: The choice is only about tone—both routes are valid and neither derails what must happen. You choose the hybrid, of course, because the work of convincing this council has always been both code and cadence.]

Elias stands when you do and steps forward to the lectern. The lights throw a soft pool across the dais; his tablet screen mirrors graphs in low blue. He begins with the baseline: the appendix you polished together. Numbers hold a gravity of their own, a way to say "we did the math" to people trained to trust calculations.

Elias Harrow [measured]: "The models show that living buffers across three critical corridors reduce peak surge impact on residential zones by an average of twenty-eight percent, and the cost profile—spread across staged implementation—compares favorably to an all-in seawall strategy."

A council aide murmurs. Mateo nods slightly; you can see the consideration forming in his face like a map taking ink.

You follow with Eda's testimony. She does not dramatize. She places a faded photograph on the table—muddy boots, a child's tie-dyed shirt, a marsh receding and returning. Her voice is a thread.

Eda Nal: "We have kept this land because it remembers how to breathe. Walls make stormwater a technical problem. Wetlands make it a living process."

The room responds not with policy but with the softer rhythm of recognition. Someone in the back wipes their eye. Even Lucia's gaze shifts, however subtly—calculation braided with something that could be comprehension.

Rani stands to speak for neighborhood livelihoods: boat repair, rooftop gardens, the little businesses that will survive if they are protected.

Rani Cho [earnest]: "Tell them you can't rebuild community in a slab of concrete. Watch your numbers, but don't forget the hands that will build with the materials you fund."

Momentum gathers—slow as a tide, but gathering. Councilor Mateo moves the hearing along with procedural competence, but you detect an undercurrent: committee members are intrigued by the hybrid evidence-and-economy case you present.

[Sound: A approving murmur. Papers shuffling. Somewhere in the room a child laughs; the sound is like a bell.]

After public testimony, you and Elias step aside. Lucia joins, alone. Her offer of matching funds for targeted measures is still on the table—practical and tempting. Her aide has whispered some fiscal backing might be made public if the committee favors a pilot-first approach.

Elias looks at you, worry in amber.

Elias Harrow [quiet]: "If we aim for city-wide language—the ambitious option—we risk a political split. If it fails, we lose momentum and credibility. If we go narrow, we win something immediate. If we phase, we keep a path open but slow action. I keep thinking of Mateo's political calculus… and of your neighbors."

You think of the sentence you heard last chapter: "If this passes, my children's school won't flood next winter." It is a hinge.

Mara Solenne [soft, to Elias]: "I hear you. I also remember what it cost when we did nothing. That memory pulls me forward."

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Ask Lucia if matching funds could be extended to a phased approach"
> Choice B: "Decline to negotiate the scope with Lucia, keep pressure public"
[Outcome A: Lucia listens; after a careful pause she says it's worth discussing—conditional on measurable milestones. You feel the room lean toward compromise.]
[Outcome B: You refuse to let corporate-sounding offers set the terms. Rani gives you a thumbs-up, but Mateo looks uneasy. The crowd outside will like the firmness, but the fiscal window narrows.]
[MERGE: You choose to probe. Lucia answers with the professional caution you expected, but not with closed doors.]

Lucia Montrose [precise]: "We can structure funding around deliverables. Proof of concept, measured metrics, verified transferability. That's how bureaucracies buy comfort—and how voters can see returns."

Her tone is managerial, but there is recognition in it. You accept the possibility of structuring the campaign to be both ambitious and politically legible. It feels like translation work—turning urgent moral claims into testable, fundable steps.

Councilor Mateo Qu [low]: "Mara, there is appetite for something that can be sold as both responsible stewardship and fiscal prudence. The committee favors immediate wins—pilot zones with funding—unless you can show a strategy that convinces risk-averse members otherwise."

You feel a steadying confidence. This is where realism acts as grace: you have the tools to make choice functional. You also sense the room for courage.

Eda takes your hand, fingers cool and lined.

Eda Nal [soft]: "You have the people, child. Choose how to plant the seed. Choose the soil that will let it grow."

Rani narrows her eyes, practical as ever.

Rani Cho [energetic]: "If you go big and lose, we get nothing. If you go small and win, we get a beachhead. Maybe think about where the bulldozers will start next season."

You think, briefly, of the bulldozers—of machines gnawing at eelgrass and the small mouths of shops with mud on their thresholds. Hope flickers between caution and daring. Rise swells in your chest not as naïveté but as the firm belief that progress is made by steady hands and brave hearts.

You look at Elias. He squeezes your hand—no great gesture, only a promise that whatever you choose he will stand with you through the consequences.

Elias Harrow [soft]: "We can craft the ordinance in three ways, each with trade-offs. I can write the legal language for whichever path you choose. I want you to know—I'll adapt. We'll adapt."

Your internal monologue trains across images: wetland reeds held against a wall of concrete; a corner of the city where pilot marshes thrive and invite expansion; a phased map with measurable steps and a trail of evidence.

There is no single right answer, only the shape of your values and your willingness to endure political storms. For a moment the committee noise recedes and you are in the marsh with Eda and Rani—hands in the mud, the low sun making everything possible.

[Visual: The skylight brightens as the rain thins; light is kind and forgiving for the first time today.]
[Music: Warm strings swell into a hopeful chord]

You step back toward the dais, feeling steadier. This is a crossroads—clearer than a tangle. The room is watching. The city has given you this moment. You breathe, square your shoulders, and prepare to set the ordinance's scope.
