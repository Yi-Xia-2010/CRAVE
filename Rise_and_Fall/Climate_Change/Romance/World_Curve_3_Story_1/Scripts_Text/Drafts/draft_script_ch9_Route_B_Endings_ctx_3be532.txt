### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Endings


[Scene: Rooftop Gardens of La Plaza | Dusk]
[Visual: Wide shot of terraced planters and string lights, rain beads on salvaged solar lanterns]
[Sound: Soft drip from leaves, distant pumps like a measured heartbeat; a gull's cry folded into evening traffic]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings with a gentle rhythmic swell]

You climb the last step to the rooftop and the air opens: hot, sweet with wet earth and citrus, a green breath after the council's fluorescent room. The braid at the nape of your neck has come undone; a few strands plaster to your temple with salt. Your compass pendant swings once and settles against the hollow above your collarbone, ticking a cadence out of time with the pumps below.

You don't know how the vote looked on paper in every clause — you only know what came home with you: signatures folded into ordinances, promises inked in meeting minutes, and the small cruel math of loss, measured in porches and market stalls. The town's future has been bruised and stitched; the seam is visible if you look close enough. The gardens smell like a promise and like rain on metal, and you take both scents in as if inhaling courage.

Jonah is already here, leaning on the low wall where the lanterns throw gold across his patched jacket. His camera hangs quiet against his chest. Up close, the silver streak at his temple is a small comet; he tugs at it without meaning to.

Jonah: "You made everyone listen. That room — it—" (he tries for levity and finds it salted)

You: "They listened, but listening isn't rescue." (Your voice is dry as a torn map.) "Avi steered it through, and Elena's team... they got the funding stamp. Some people will be safe this winter. Some won't."

Jonah's jaw tightens. He looks at the ribbon of the harbor below, where lights tremble on the water.

Jonah: "We bought time. Isn't that— isn't that something? We can plant, enforce the covenants Claire wanted, push for legal teeth. We can hold people together."

You pause, feeling the ledger of obligations settle over your shoulders — the deals you accepted, the compromises you made, the neighborhoods you couldn't keep. The ache is real, but it is not the only thing you feel. Beneath it, like a stubborn shoot through concrete, there is work that can grow.

You: "Time that matters is the kind with roots, not just pylons and promenades. If the law has teeth, we bite back. If it doesn't—" (you let the sentence hang, and Jonah fills the rest)

Jonah: "Then we'll gnaw at the law until it does." (He laughs once, small and sharp.) "You don't have to carry every ledger alone, Mara. I know you think you do — I used to think that, too. But we don't. We have hands."

You: "Hands are what built this place." (You look over the garden — tomato cages bound with old rope, seedlings cramped in salvaged crates, a banner stitched with everyone's names flapping low.) "If the law holds some ground, we plant there. If other ground is bought out, we make sure the stories stay attached to it."

Jonah studies your face, amber eyes trying to map where the stubbornness ends and the forgiveness begins. His expression is complicated, close to a smile and close to a shutter click at once — the kind of face that measures possibility and loss in the same breath.

Jonah: "Abuela Pilar told me to bring you a lemon tart." (He holds up a small, foil-wrapped thing like a peace offering.) "She says you earned it."

You take the tart. It smells like butter and citrus and afternoons when storms were just stories told by the radio. You bite and the crust flakes, bright and impossible. For a second, the weight thins.

You choose to stay. The soil is darker than you remembered, richer with compost and conversation; hands arrive as if scheduled by instinct. Rosa brings a kettle; Mateo carries a crate of bulbs. People press in, murmuring plans and names. Conversations tangle: legal clauses placed beside planting schedules, technical drawings traded for recipes that teach salt-tolerant crops.

Avi's message comes as a text and a pause: the tablet photo of the signed motion, the time-stamp like an exhale. You don't open it right away. Instead you plant a sapling — a stubborn mangrove cousin lowered into a reclaimed trough. Your fingers are damp and the sapling is surprisingly light for the weight of what it must do.

A woman from the market — a neighbor you know only by the laugh that leaks from behind her scarf — touches your shoulder and says, quietly, "Mi hija will have a place." Her eyes are watery, but not without a spark. You aren't sure if she means what will remain or the hope you have lit in her.

Jonah kneels beside you. He wipes his palms on his jeans and studies the sapling.

Jonah: "You know, when we were kids we tried to dam the tide with sand and driftwood. It didn't work. But we learned where the tide wanted to go. We learned how to redirect it, to soften it. Maybe that's what this is."

You: "Maybe." (Your throat tightens around the memory of your father's hands, of the house they used to call home, of the ledger of feelings you carry.) "We redirect. We make room. We make maps that aren't just lines on paper but lines that people can stand on."

Jonah nods and reaches for your hand without hesitating. He doesn't try to fix the ledger for you; he simply places his palm against yours, an agreement not to let go.

You choose to tell him. The names come out in fits and folds — a litany of addresses and memories and the small ceremonial things that mark a life. You expect him to recoil or to supply easy answers. Instead, he breathes each name into the dark, as if offering it a place to live.

Jonah: "We'll put them in the storyboard for the market. We'll make space in the Sunday table. Mateo will carve them into the boards of the coop bench. People will come and say those names out loud."

You swallow. The idea is both small and massive: rituals as preservation, collective remembrance as infrastructure.

Avi joins you a few minutes later, hands still marked with the council's stamp. He stands at the edge of the garden like someone stepping into a prayer. For a moment his officialness hangs between you — a linen suit wrinkled with the world.

Avi: "There are conditions. There are legal teeth, but they'll need watching. Enforcement language, funding tranches, oversight committees. I'll be present. We'll write them to be hard to break."

You: "Watch them like you watch a tide chart — not as numbers but as a community. Enforcement that allows for people is only useful if people are on the ground."

Avi smiles, tired and genuine.

Avi: "That's a clause I'd be proud to defend at any hearing."

You feel the pragmatic scaffolding meet the living scaffolding of the town: a statutory backbone for a network of friendships, rituals, and gardens. The law will not erase memory; it will only shape the terrain where memory can continue. That is not salvation — it is survival, and there is grace in survival.

Elena's public statement has already circulated. Her expression in the video is, as always, composed and unreadable — success measured in slides and timelines. You do not make your peace with her; you make it with the work that follows.

Night deepens. The solar lanterns glow amber and the market lights below start up, one by one, like buoy lights on a sheltered channel. People share small food and smaller news. Laughter threads into the low murmur of planters and policy.

Jonah tucks his camera strap around his wrist and sets the device beside the saplings, an act that is both symbolic and intimate: he chooses the story of the place over an outsider's distance for the night.

Jonah: "We climb together?" (He asks it like a question and an offer.)

You look past him, over the string lights and the broken tiles and the people bending to their tasks. The town is a patchwork, stitched with careful hands. The hole is still there in some places; in others you've hung new fabric. The seam shows, but it holds.

You: "We climb." (Your voice is steady. Hope, battered but real, lifts at the edges.) "Slowly. With anchors."

[Visual: Close-up of your compass as it ticks; the pendulum rocks, catching a sliver of moonlight]
[Sound: The pumps steady into a comforting rhythm]
[Music: Strings swell into a soft, hopeful chord]

You stand at the rooftop and feel the neighborhood breathe with you — cautious, bruised, alive. Trust has thin lines around its eyes now, but it's still a thing that can be healed by work and by ritual and by the everyday scaffolding of people refusing to let one another go.

You and Jonah begin to descend together, hands still linked.
