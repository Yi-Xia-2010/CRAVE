You are here because something broke open last night — a hidden assumption, a spreadsheet, the polite veneer anyone uses to justify erasure. You are here because people raised their voices and because you chose, once, to put names and timestamps where silence had been.

The lagoon smells of old iron and wet loam; when you step closer the mud gives slightly beneath your boots and the cool wet presses through the tired soles. Your compass pendant swings once and stills against the hollow of your throat, a small metronome for courage.

Aunt Pilar's voice is a memory at the back of your ribs — spare, bright as citrus peel. You feel her in the way your hands curl against the cold, in the way you notice everything: the algae's oil sheen, the way sunlight finds the silver streak in Jonah Reyes's hair, the pattern of patched boards in the old pier behind you. You think of your father's hands on a rope, the weight of a storm taught into a palm.

Jonah Reyes moves beside you, quiet at first. He wipes his hands on a strip of sailcloth, salt crusting the seams. There's the faint damp smell of the market on him — citrus, fish, coffee grounds — and beneath that, the easy scent of someone who still believes people can change the plan when the plan fails them.

Jonah Reyes: "You woke early. Couldn't sleep either?"

Mara Solano: "Couldn't stop thinking. The tide keeps its schedules; we don't."

Jonah Reyes [Half-smile]: "When you talk like that you make even the tides sound like people who can be reasoned with."

You both look out over the lagoon. For a long moment neither of you speaks. Your glove brushes the water and a ripple runs outward, small but clear.

Jonah Reyes: "Whatever happened—it didn't end in silence. People came. People listened."

Mara Solano: "They did. And they kept coming."

Jonah Reyes reaches for your hand the way he used to when you were kids, safe and stubbornly present. His fingers find the warmth of your skin; the seagrass ring he made in youth feels like a memory and a promise at once.

Jonah Reyes: "So — what do we do with the next part?"

You imagine the next part as a ladder you might climb together, its rungs slippery with compromise and salt. You imagine it as a horizon you might leave from to join a wider climb. Both futures are heavy with gratitude and cost.

Both are true in the ways that matter most: people are safer because of the choices you made; people look to you and to Jonah Reyes like beacons and like vulnerable things.

You let your mind spin a thread through each possibility, and you test the weight of them by asking aloud what you would bring with you, or what you would leave behind.

Jonah Reyes: "If you go—if they need you elsewhere—what does that look like? Not the press version. The version where we eat breakfast on a roof garden and gripe about the compost."

Mara Solano: (softly) "It looks like more leverage to protect more places. It looks like Claire in more meetings and Aunt Pilar's stories reaching more ears. It looks like… leaving this body of water in better hands than we found it."

Jonah Reyes's hand tightens around yours. He wants to argue logistics; instead he offers the thing he knows best.

Jonah Reyes: "And if you stay? What happens then?"

Mara Solano: "Then we keep building here. We write the ordinance so it can't be bulldozed away. We teach kids how to plant cordgrass and how to read tide lines. We make it so the place remembers how to fight back."

Jonah Reyes: "Both sound like work I want to do with you."

You both laugh, but there are small breaks in the sound where fear could wedge itself in. Fear is practical — legal threats, late-night noises outside the coop, a call with a recorded voice reading lines meant to unnerve. But you have also been buoyed — by community choirs, by surprised council votes, by people you thought had left coming back to roll up sleeves.

Aunt Pilar's face comes to you again: stubborn, amused, proud. You can almost hear her saying, "Mira, mi'ja, choose where your heart will anchor, not where your fear tugs you."

The cradle of the afternoon arrives like a decision made of light. You and Jonah Reyes stand at the angle where river grasses meet broken docks and make small promises to each other: to write, to call, to show up. The world beyond the lagoon will need you in different ways. Your choice will be a shape that offers shelter: sometimes broad and distant, sometimes narrow and immediate.

You walk back along the mud-slick planks toward the town. People are already arriving to plan plantings, to translate data into petitions, to print posters. Voices knit together into something like a soundtrack: the market's bartering rhythm, Claire's clipped scientific cadence, Aunt Pilar's low, steady instructions. You think of the young ones learning to knot coir logs with thumbs clumsy and brave as they are, and you think of the elders who remember storms like old maps.

You pause on the boardwalk. Jonah Reyes looks at you, really looks — not the way the town looks at leaders but the way he looks when he thinks you are still the Mara who once climbed a piling to rescue a stray fishing net.

Jonah Reyes: "Promise me this — whatever you decide, don't let the fear be the loudest voice."

Mara Solano: "I promise."

He leans in and your forehead meets his for a heartbeat, a private pressure that seals and steadies you both.

You have choices that will ripple outward, and you have a home that is made of people more than place. The ladder you climb will be built rung by rung, whether you build it here or take it to cities farther up the map. It will require leaving, staying, bargaining, protesting, sleepless nights, and sunrise restorations. But the horizon is some mixture of made things: wetlands stitched back together, policies that hold land for those who will steward it, and the slow, patient work of teaching a town to read its own future.

Jonah Reyes slips the seagrass ring back onto your finger—whether it was ever off for more than half a breath doesn't matter. Aunt Pilar's face rests in your mind, steady. Claire's hand squeezes your shoulder. Around you, people are already finding purchase in the mud, in the ordinances, in the headlines, in the places where policy and protest meet.

You breathe in salt and possibility. The climb will be steep — and it will be shared.
