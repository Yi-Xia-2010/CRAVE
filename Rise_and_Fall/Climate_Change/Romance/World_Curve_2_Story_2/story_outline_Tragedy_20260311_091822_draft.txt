## Visual Novel Title
Tides We Could Not Hold

---

## World View
A small coastal town, Harbor's Reach, clings to the mouth of Willowmarsh, where mudflats, reed beds, and raised boardwalks mark the border between human habit and the sea’s slow advance. The near-future climate has raised tides and hardened storms; salt curls the edges of paint, gulls wheel around abandoned storefronts, and the hum of diesel from dredgers is a new background chord. Governance is intimate and fractious: a single mayor, a town council, a contested planning board, and community meetings at the Town Hall where petitions and placards exchange harsh light with corporate renderings. Economies mix fish houses and boutique tourism, boatyards and precarious seasonal work; class friction arises when developers promise jobs while environmentalists warn of long-term ruin.

Religion and ritual are subtle—remembrance days for lost boats, small shrines of fishing buoys—and community identity is bound to place. Conflict centers on the proposed Azure Crescent Development, a corporate-engineered sea wall and raised boardwalks promoted by Cassandra Rey to “fix” the shoreline and revive the economy. Mara Voss, our protagonist, returns as a scientist who believes in living shorelines; Eli Calder, a boatmaker and her hesitant love interest, stands between tradition and necessity. The town’s atmosphere is soft watercolor realism—teal seas, driftwood browns, oyster grays—where beauty and anxiety coexist and every choice reshapes who stays, who leaves, and what the shore remembers.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I return home to Willowmarsh determined to build a living shoreline that can protect marshland and sustain fisheries, but the town is split by Cassandra Rey’s Azure Crescent Development, which promises jobs and a hard sea wall. At the Town Hall my choice between negotiation, legal resistance, or localized restoration launches three broad paths. Each decision pulls me deeper into political pressure, interpersonal fracture, and ecological unraveling. I weave with—or against—Eli and the town elders, negotiate with Cass, coordinate with Rina and Mayor Rosa, and reckon with my younger sister Nia and Tomas Calder’s memory of the old shore. The narrative is a steady fall: miscalculations, compromises, and escalating backlash lead to losses—eroded marsh, fragile livelihoods, and strained love. Branches diverge into distinct tragedies: negotiated compromise that still destroys critical habitat and frays trust; legal victories that save marsh but cost community cohesion and love; and a stubborn experimental path that delays economic relief and fractures the relationship with Eli. Every ending accepts an uncompensated loss: ecological, economic, or personal. The emotional arc is a continuous descent—each choice narrows the margin for repair until the town and I must measure what we sacrificed and what remains.
