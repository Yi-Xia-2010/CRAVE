# Route: Route C (Ending C1)


[Scene: Marisora Harbor | Dusk]
[Visual: Wide shot of crescent harbor; golden light pooling over rusted metal]
[Sound: Low gull calls; distant hull creaks; a soft wind pushing small waves against wooden pilings]
[Music: Sparse, melancholic strings]

You step off the shuttle with the tide-watch heavy in your palm. The brass is warm from your skin, the engraving half-softened by salt; it ticks in a way that feels like a small, private insistence. The air tastes the same as the last time you left — sharp with salt and a faint tang of iron — and because it smells familiar, it hurts more. The harbor looks smaller and older than you remember, as if someone has been shaving years off its edges: broken planks mended with scavenged metal, buoy ropes repurposed into handles, paint layers like geological strata. There is a hardness to it now, like the town has learned to keep everything it loves at arm's length.

You run a thumb along the cracked edge of your tablet in your satchel. The crack fans like a river delta across the screen; the device boots with a stutter. Your faded green scarf is looped in the bag, a small, stubborn memory. You roll your sleeves without thinking — habit, armor, ritual. There is a checklist in your head, tidy and impatient: meet Rita, inspect nets, speak to Mayor, present preliminary models. The list steadies you and also makes you smaller.

A weathered skiff bumps the pier as a man hollers directions. Salt flakes off the ropes in mica-like showers. The harbor smells of wet wood and old diesel, of drying fish and someone frying something in an alley. Low clouds gather offshore, a bruised band that eats the sunset. The light here is flatter, the shadows longer; even the gulls seem to wheel with a kind of studied caution.

You scan faces for familiar lines. Hands that once moved like breathing maps of this place are now patched and callused in different ways—sewn leather, improvised ferrules, a waiting-for-help tenseness in the way people look at equipment. You have returned with answers, or so you told yourself on the flight down. The town has kept its charms — solar canopies blink like small promises, string lights dangle over community tables, someone has freshly painted a festival patch onto a captain's cap — but those details feel less like hope and more like a thin varnish over a deep, exhausted need.

[Visual: Close-up on tide-watch; camera lingers on etched numerals]

You inhale. You can feel responsibility sitting at the base of your skull like an ache. You left after the storm years ago because there was nowhere left to stand; you came back because you believed staying might finally do the work that leaving couldn't. The thought is both a vow and a confession.

[Scene: Community Greenhouse & Seed Bank | Dusk]
[Visual: Warm interior glow through recycled-plastic panels; string lights and hand-painted labels]
[Sound: Hum of heaters, soft drip of condensation, distant muffled conversations]
[Music: Low cello notes underscoring tension]

Rita is waiting inside, kneeling by a tray of seedlings, her apron a riot of colors and soil. The greenhouse smells of loam and citrus and the sweetness of things grown with care. Up close, she looks smaller but keyed-in; the braid at her crown has a few silver threads that catch the amber light.

Rita: "Maya! You actually made it." (She stands too fast and brushes a wet hand on her apron, smiling that quick, fierce smile she always gives when she wants to hide worry.)

You: "I did." (Your voice sounds strange in the humid air, as if part of it belongs to the town now.) "How — how have things been?"

Rita: "Same as the headlines say, but worse between the lines." (She gestures at a bulletin board covered in seed packets and handwritten notes.) "Nets failing in pockets, fish moving offshore, and the grant? Delayed again. The regional office sent a letter — red tape, they said. 'Awaiting review.'"

You: "Awaiting review." (You taste the phrase; it tastes like foam. There's so much that will fit into a review that doesn't meet the people who will drown.) "Do we know what they're reviewing? Timelines?"

Rita: (Her laugh is sharp.) "Timelines are a polite way of saying 'we hope to, maybe, if funds permit.' Tomas is trying to keep calm, but—" (She presses her palm to her forehead.) "We can't do much without cash, Maya. Hands and courage don't buy concrete."

You feel the old reflex: solve, calculate, deliver. The models you can run on your tablet bloom in your head — projected outcomes, error bars, mitigation timelines — all instruments for a battle you are not sure you can win alone.

Rita: "Listen, you look... tired." (She studies your rolled sleeves, the soil under your nails implied.) "We need you sharp. The neighborhood meeting's tonight — it's on the board out front. People will be there."

You: "I know." (You close your fingers around the tide-watch inside your jacket. The brass hums against the bone of your palm.) "I wanted to start with a survey of the nets and some gauging data. If the currents have shifted, we can recommend new trap geometries."

Rita: (She puts a hand on yours, grounding, impatient.) "That's science, Maya. It's what you do. But we've also got fishermen who will tell you the water doesn't listen to a model. People need to be part of the plan."

You: "They will be." (You don't mean to snap, but the sentence is edged with the fatigue of trying to be both scientist and daughter of Marisora. You remind yourself to breathe.) "We—I'll work with Rita here. We'll bring data and proposals."

Rita: "And your scarf?" (She grins, the light in her eyes an accusation and a plea.) "If the storm comes, don't do it for the town alone. Do it for yourself."

You imagine folding the town inside your chest like the scarf, smaller but warm. The thought is an aching luxury.

[Scene: Marisora Docks | Dusk]
[Visual: Narrow wooden walkways, boats moored; Elias working on a small pier shelter]
[Sound: Saw rasping, muffled laughter, tide moving rhythmically]
[Music: A single acoustic guitar phrase; hopeful but weary]

Elias is there with a paint-splattered rag slung over his shoulder, barefoot, nails dark with varnish and salt. He looks up as you approach; his grin is quick but the smile doesn't reach tired eyes. He smells of sawdust and the sea — a familiar scent that lingers like a promise.

Elias: "Hey, stranger. Didn't think they'd let you past customs with all that science." (He cocks his head, examining the tide-watch in your hand before he hides his curiosity.)

You: "I snuck it through in a sock." (You offer a half-smile; it falters the way a tide can.) "How's the shelter coming?"

Elias: "Slow. Arlo messed up a cut and then swore at it like it was personal. But it'll hold." (He taps the beam like it owes him money and then shrugs.) "We pulled three nets this morning that were—" (His voice drops.) "—thin as tissue. Had more holes than the fisherman who owned them."

You: "Did you record where?" (Your training surfaces — logistics, coordinates, a hope to make patterns out of loss.)

Elias: "Yeah." (He seems to hesitate, eyeing your rolled sleeves.) "Listen, Maya. We can't always wait for paperwork to tell us how to save a season. We patch, we reroute, we move the lines. Sometimes that's faster."

You: "Sometimes that's also bailing water with a teacup." (You don't mean to sound cutting, but it's the only metaphor that fits.) "I can run better mapping if we have more at-sea sensors."

Elias: (He laughs, soft and affectionate.) "And I'll stitch the sensors into something the sea doesn't mind. Compromise, yeah?"

There's a pause, and the air between you is like taut fabric. You both know how this conversation usually travels — from plans about nets to questions about staying, about whether staying means losing more than you can bear.

Elias: "You look like you'll build us a future if anyone can." (He reaches and taps the tide-watch, respectful.) "Just... don't forget to come back to dinner. Mom's making her stew."

You feel the tug of two energies: a practical partnership and a personal one that hasn't had time to be named. You answer with something small and truthful.

You: "I'll come. If the stew is as stubborn as our town, I'll need the backup."

Elias: (As he walks you back toward the path from the docks, voice low) "Maya, if there’s anything I can do—" (His hand brushes his toolbelt as if offering it, and there's a question in the movement that is both practical and something else.)

You: "Stay in town." (You almost don't say it aloud; it feels too much like a demand. You settle for promising, instead.) "Help us keep what's left. We'll find ways to make it last."

He nods, but his smile is complicated, edged with a tiredness that matches yours.

[Scene: Overlooking Municipal Presence | Dusk]
[Visual: Municipal Hall windows gleaming; a figure in a trench moving between sandbags and maps]
[Sound: Murmur of officials; paper rustling; distant traffic]
[Music: Piano chords, colder than before]

From the pier you can see Dr. Ayla Voss moving like a scale model of efficiency: trench coat precise, satchel arranged, wristband flashing pale data light. She is surrounded by municipal aides, tapping at a tablet, her gaze scanning maps with a clinician's distance.

Ayla Voss: "We need consolidated data. Individual anecdotes don't translate to cost-effective measures." (Her voice is measured; her face reads like a sealed ledger.)

You step closer, the sound of your boots on wet wood loud in the hush. You feel the town's history compressing into this moment — the storm that took roofs, the morning your father came back with water in his boots, the day you left with a backpack of degrees and a head full of equations.

You: "Ayla." (You try for neutral. It comes out edged.) "I'm Maya Reyes. I've mapped La Niña patterns for three years. The community needs something actionable, not just consolidation."

Ayla Voss: (She looks at you for a long beat. The expression is complex. You can't tell whether she sees the scientist, the hometown daughter, or a useful variable.) "Dr. Reyes. Your credentials are on file. We welcome your input — as long as the deliverables align with the proposal timelines."

You: "Timelines?" (The word tastes like an old wound.) "This town isn't a dataset you can slice into quarters. People are living the lag. Funds are a promise that keeps moving out to sea."

Ayla Voss: (She folds her hands, not unkind but not soft.) "I understand the human element. I also deal with budgets and liabilities. We cannot promise more than the budget allows."

You: "Then promise us honesty." (It isn't a challenge purely of competence; it's a plea for truth that doesn't turn the town into a footnote.) "Tell us what you can do and when, so we can plan between now and then."

Ayla's eyes flick to the tide, then back to you. Her face doesn't reveal the calculus on which she's building policy. The reaction is unreadable, and that's dangerous in its own right.

Ayla Voss: "I'll be at tonight's briefing." (Her voice is flat, but the implication hangs like a barometer.) "Come prepared."

You: "We will." (Preparing feels like a small, defiant act.)

[Scene: Neighborhood Bulletin Board | Night]
[Visual: Coffee-stained board with handwritten notices; a single poster reads 'Community Meeting — Tonight 7PM']
[Sound: Night insects, an anxious whistle of wind through alleyways]
[Music: Single low violin note; tension coiled]

You walk the slow route home, the harbor's lights bobbing behind you. The board by the bakery is a collage of months: missing cats, volunteer lists, children's drawings of a future town. A fresh notice is pinned where a festival poster used to be — Mayor Tomas's announcement about a neighborhood meeting. The paper is wrinkled with fingerprints.

You stop. People will gather. Voices will rise. There will be names called, hands raised, and the slow, grinding machine of municipal procedure will begin to meet the raw, urgent needs of a town that remembers how storms carve memory into walls and skin.

You feel the hum of tension like a current underfoot. Your tide-watch ticks, outrunning and underlining the moment. There is so much to say — models, timelines, the cry of fishermen, the promises of officials — and none of it will be tidied in a single night.

You fold your scarf tighter in your bag and run your thumb across the tide-watch one more time. The night is thick, and you walk toward the light from the hall, toward a room that will hold questions you brought and questions you didn't know you would have to answer.

The decision is not yet yours to make, but you can already feel the pull: plead and petition with the town; push for technical models that will probably be argued over; fight for transparency; and, beneath it all, learn how to hold a person and a place at once.

You inhale. Your chest feels full in a way that is both promise and warning. You step forward.
