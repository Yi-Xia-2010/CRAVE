## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Marisora

---

## World View
Marisora is a near-future coastal town squeezed between hard science and old salt rituals. Sea-level rise, warming currents, and storm volatility have reshaped daily life: wooden piers sit on patched pilings, clapboard houses lean into sandbag fortifications, and municipal sensor arrays hum alongside hand-painted “NO SINKING” signs. Political power sits unevenly — Mayor Tomas Nkem's office negotiates tourism contracts and emergency grants while neighborhood organizers like Rita Ortega press for grassroots equity. Regional policy networks, represented by Dr. Ayla Voss and her municipal tech team, push centralized solutions and funding thresholds that can require relocation. At the same time, Elias Jun and builders of the harbor prototype modular defenses and living mangrove projects rooted in community knowledge.

Culture mixes practical coastal thrift — patched denim, sea-salted hair, community greenhouse seed banks — with the urgency of climate triage; religious and ritual life persists as small memorials to lost houses and harvest feasts now repurposed as resilience fundraisers. Geography alternates between exposed breakwater, dense mangrove ribs, and a crescent harbor that frames the town’s identity. Atmosphere: melancholic, tactile, and intimate — salt on everything, the hum of policy on fluorescent lights, the hush of shared grief at dusk. Romance and communal obligation are braided into each decision; personal wants mirror ecological stakes.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I, Maya Reyes, return to Marisora after studying climate science, determined to help my hometown adapt. The story begins with a period of diminishing buffers — failed nets, slumping piers, and funding delays — that pull me into a moral and emotional descent as the town faces increasingly unavoidable choices. My pragmatic empathy clashes with Elias Jun’s improvisational rebuilds and Dr. Ayla Voss’s centralizing plans. A major community split forces me to choose: rally grassroots solutions with Elias; negotiate with Ayla for a hybrid plan; or advocate for an organized regional relocation. Each decision sharpens sacrifice: relationships strain, infrastructure fails, and a severe storm breaks the town’s fragile armor, marking the lowest point.

From that nadir, recovery begins: working with neighbors, repairing trust, and designing adaptive systems that are both technical and tender. Romance informs each path — trust, distance, or rebuilt intimacy — and the ending varies from bitter compromise to moral clarity, tragedy, or communal triumph. Throughout, the emotional arc follows “Man in a Hole”: a fall into crisis where responsibility, guilt, and vulnerability deepen, followed by a rise through collaboration, acceptance, and hard-won solutions. Choices lead to different kinds of recovery rather than simple success/failure dichotomies; every route ends with the town transformed and relationships redefined.
