### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C — Ending
# Context: Start

[Scene: Tide Lab (Converted Research Ferry) | Dawn]

[Music: Soft, hopeful piano with distant gull calls]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Sound: Low hum of instruments, occasional clank of rigging]

You wake before the harbor fully remembers itself. The Tide Lab rocks with a patient, familiar rhythm that matches the motion of your chest. You sit on a stool that has been sanded smooth by a dozen hands, roll the edge of your leather-bound notebook back against your palm, and wind your brass dive watch the way your mother once wound a sewing machine—slow, deliberate, as if the watch could turn tides by insistence alone.

The strip of silver at your temple glints when you tuck a curl behind your ear. You used to think that streak was a fault of the sea—salt taken from an ocean that took your childhood home—but now it reads like a promise carved into bone: you have been where water and memory meet, and you are still standing.

Your fingers smell faintly of algae and graphite. The notebook is heavy with sketches: scalloped marsh edges, stiching lines where cordgrass might root, notes on sediment accretion. The Tide Lab’s task lamps throw warm cones on stacked maps. Outside, the city is a smear of neon and rain—promenade lights blurred in puddles, the distant silhouette of the PolySilo rising like a thumb against the low sky.

You test a data set: salinity gradients for the Drowned District pilot, tidal amplitude over three months, plant survival rates by cohort. The instruments hum. A green readout blinks in a steady, encouraging rhythm.

Narration: You are here to prove something that feels, right now, almost like a stubborn hymn: nature can be engineered to heal itself if people get the chance to steward it. Not because you hate engineering—because you trust the tide more than the ledger.

[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: Ping of incoming message]

Your comms chime—Jonah Reyes. You know the tone of his texts more than most voices: practical warmth, a little clipped, like a photograph developed too fast but still clear. His amber-brown eyes flash in your memory—the soft laugh lines at their corners, the sun-bleached tips of hair that always look like they’ve been brushed by wind. You imagine him somewhere between the waterline and a drafting table, camera hanging at his neck, boots muddy.

You smile despite the salt on your lips.

You tap the message open. It's short: a photo of a modular berm detail; hand-drawn notes in the margin; a question.

You trace the diagram with your finger as if the ink might teach you rhythm. The berm looks like a small promise—woven coir rolls locked by plantings, a removable core that lets the marsh breathe. It doesn’t scream corporate scale. It whispers possible.

You fold the message back into the page and stand. The deck smells of iron and kelp; your boots make a soft scuff against wet steel. Professor Asha Rao is already at the main bench, spectacles fogged at the edges and a stack of archived tidal charts fanned beside her. Her braid gleams like a silver rope. She looks up when you arrive, and the room softens.

Professor Asha: "You're early, Mara Ellis." (She smiles. The name sounds different from how your mother used it—less intimate, more steady.) "You take that as a good sign."

You set the berm sketch beside the others and feel the buoyant thrum of possibility. Professor Asha leans in, fingers brushing a patch of salt. She points at a map.

Professor Asha: "The pilot's numbers are holding. Not miraculous, but consistent. Sediment accretion is trending up and the cordgrass cohorts are showing higher than expected survival."

Mara Ellis: "Higher than expected by how much?"

Professor Asha: "Three percent above projection in the inner channels, five percent where we added the tidal breaks last month. It's the kind of blip that becomes a proof point when you can replicate it."

You bite your tongue, wanting to add every caveat—you've already learned how quickly politicians love a single good number and how easily corporations co-opt a narrative—but the hope blooms anyway, warm and cautious.

[Sound: Soft laughter from the mess as Maya enters]
[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]

Maya tumbles aboard like a sunburst. She smells of espresso and street citrus. She drops a thermos at your feet and slaps a hand on your shoulder—half-sister shove, full-sister affection.

Maya: "You look like you haven't slept. Or you've been up writing love letters to marsh grass."

Mara Ellis: "They're technical notes, thank you very much."

Maya: "Right—technical love letters. Save me a line when you win the council over."

You grin. She sits beside you and pulls a battered radio toward her. Her eyes scan the reports on the tablet; her hands are steady where hers used to make drinks. She has the neighborhood in her chest—every meeting, every flyer, every scraped knee.

Maya: "Old Mate stopped by last night. He said the reeds look like they're breathing again. Said it slowly, like he's not used to saying anything optimistic."

Your chest lifts. Small things anchor you: Mateo's weathered voice, a neighbor catching a seedling. The pilot in the Drowned District is not a headline yet, but it's breathing, and that counts.

Mara Ellis: "Did he bring any more stories? Any new cautions?"

Maya: "Always cautions. He kept saying, 'A little structure to hold them while they learn to stand.' He liked the look of the trial plots."

Mara Ellis: "That's the whole point—we're not building walls, Maya. We're teaching the shore to stand."

Maya: "Then teach it well," she says, but there's warmth under the challenge. "Also—Professor Rao wanted to talk after the meeting."

Professor Asha: "I wanted to ask if you would present the pilot's visuals at the faculty session next week. Not the council—yet. But people who sit in the right rooms. We need a better frame before we step into chambers."

You feel the familiar prickle of nerves and possibility. You imagine a room where your data slides under the eyes of people who can multiply your pilot into policy. The idea tastes like copper and ocean spray.

Mara Ellis: "I can do that. I can show the replication plan, the cost-benefit that favors living shorelines if you include social resilience metrics."

Professor Asha: "Good. I'll link you with Councilor Patel's aide—political threads and scientific threads together. It improves our odds."

You nod. Each small connection is a stitch. The Tide Lab feels more than a lab; it's a loom.

[Scene: Drowned District (Field Visit) | Late Morning]

[Background:"ch1_Start_4"]
[Sound: Slow water lapping, distant creak of a buoy, faint community chatter]
[Music: Soft strings underscoring cautious joy]

You motor toward the Drowned District with two volunteers and a crate of plantings. The tide sits low enough to let you trudge through mud without drowning in it—enough to show the difference between survival and surrender. The pilot plots are carved into the shallows like small, stubborn islands: woven coir rolls, newly planted spartina, tiny stakes marking study sites.

The marsh edge that was thinning a season ago now shows scallops where sediment is catching. Sediment traps you set earlier are fuller; the soil smells richer, less like rot and more like possibility. Kids from the neighborhood are poking a small wooden boat into the shallows; an old man—Old Mate—knocks his knuckles against a piling and grins in a way that pulls at your ribs.

You kneel at a plot and press a blade of cordgrass between your fingers. It's flexible and proud. You imagine the frayed silver streak at your temple as a marker: having something taken doesn't mean you stop making.

Mara Ellis: "This is it. Look at this root structure—it's binding faster than our models expected. If we scale, we can buffer more neighborhoods than we lost."

Volunteer: "Scaling will mean more materials, more volunteers. And more eyes—politicians won't look away once they see it working."

Mara Ellis: "Then we make them look at the right things: the people living here, the savings in displacement costs, the biodiversity gains."

[Outcome A: "You scoop a small core and tuck it into your bag with meticulous care. You need numbers to turn hope into policy."]
[Outcome B: "You crouch and explain how the roots knot the mud together. Her eyes get wide; she presses her tiny hand to the soil, triumphant. The moment recalibrates what 'proof' means to you."]
[MERGE: Continue]

On your way back to the ferry, a neighbor—a woman with a paint-splattered coat—hands you a small jar of pickled clams and a note: 'For the people who give us back our edges.' It’s modest and enormous at the same time. You tuck the jar into your bag with the sediment sample and the berm sketch. Each is a token of faith.

Back on the Tide Lab, Jonah Reyes waits at the rail, slate-gray jacket flaking with salt, camera slung and palms still dusty. He looks like someone who belongs in both the lab and the dock—an engineer who remembers the name of the tide more than the name of a spreadsheet. Up close, the warmth in his eyes is a kind of honest light.

Jonah Reyes: "Hey. You look like you spent the morning convincing the coast to forgive you."

You laugh. He hands over a rolled detail drawing—a modular berm with interchangeable cores and planting slots.

Mara Ellis: "This is promising. The modularity could let maintenance be community-led, too."

Jonah Reyes: "That's the idea. Keep the tech small enough to fix with hand tools and civic will. Speaking of—Professor Asha said your numbers are looking good."

Mara Ellis: "They are. Enough to ask for more attention."

Jonah Reyes: "Good. Because I was talking to a contractor who might redirect some surplus materials if Council gives the nod. Small wins become supply chains if we push hard."

You pause. The mention of contractors could mean anything—old alliances, new compromises. You are wary of quick fixes wrapped in helpful language. But Jonah Reyes’s voice has an earnestness that is hard to mistrust.

Mara Ellis: "If it stays community-run—if we keep oversight—then it's useful. If it becomes a way to displace maintenance costs onto neighbors, then it becomes another wall."

Jonah Reyes: "Agreed. I want solutions that keep families here, not move them somewhere else 'safer' so someone else can build on the waterfront."

He looks at you, and in that look there is a bridge being built out of ordinary attention. The conversation unfolds with a dozen small turns—questions about anchoring methods, debates over maintenance schedules, a sidetrack about which boat in the harbor has the best coffee. There is friction: you worry he sometimes softens his stance to get approvals; he worries you might be uncompromising to the point of losing momentum. The exchanges are not storms; they are negotiations, tides drawing in and out.

Jonah Reyes: "Can I show you a detail? It's small, but it might be what lets the city sign off without wrecking the ecology."

Mara Ellis: "Show me."

He spreads out a sketch. His hands are sure; the diagram is functional and humane, a compromise that doesn't betray your core. You run your finger along the line he designed and the curve fits into your own sketch like a missing puzzle piece.

> Choice A: "Ask Jonah Reyes about his compromises"

[Outcome A: "You push—politely, sharply. He hesitates, then offers a candid line about balancing permits and people. You taste both relief and caution."]
[Outcome B: "You talk details—porosity, vegetative establishment timeframes, community maintenance schedules. The conversation becomes kinetic and hopeful, two minds drafting a shared scaffold."]
[MERGE: Continue]

Jonah Reyes lingers as the afternoon folds into an amber dusk. You stand at the rail and watch the Drowned District shrink into a silhouette, the new plots visible as dark, patient stitches on the water. Your chest feels open and heavy in equal measure—hope tied to responsibility.

Professor Asha approaches with a thermal mug and a look that says she has been moving a chessboard without telling you all the plays.

Professor Asha: "Council isn't a single hurdle, Mara Ellis. It's a hundred small rooms. You'll present to faculty; if that goes well, I'll arrange a briefing. And I've emailed Councilor Patel. Your pilot is beginning to look like a policy lever."

Mara Ellis: "What should I emphasize?"

Professor Asha: "Emphasize replicability, community benefit, and cost curves that show long-term savings when you include social metrics. Bring people—voices from the Drowned District. Data alone doesn't move faces in a room."

Mara Ellis: "I will. I'll bring Old Mate if he'll come."

Professor Asha: (softly) "Bring your conviction. But remember—conviction is tempered by strategy. You have both."

You lean into that. Here, surrounded by humming instruments and warm lamps and the smell of kelp, you feel a small narrative rearranging itself: pilot to program, sketches to scale. The day has given you something rare—a minor success that does not feel pyrrhic. It feels like momentum.

Jonah Reyes stands a step away, hands tucked into his jacket pockets, watching you plot. You can see the way he measures the world—technically, but with room for tenderness. You both know the other major player in the field: Elias Crowe, whose sleek solutions sit on brochures and lobby tables and who favors decisive, centralized fixes. You have not met him in person yet, but his shadow is a ledger in the margins of every permit and proposal you study.

You touch the brass of your watch, feeling the little ridges where it has been worn thin. It is not counting time for you so much as choices—each wind a small insistence that you will take this next step deliberately.

Professor Asha: "Tomorrow we finalize what you'll show. Tonight, rest. Momentum likes sleep."

Mara Ellis: "Tomorrow we take it a room at a time."

You pack your notebook, the berm detail, the sediment sample, and the jar of clams. Jonah Reyes offers to walk you to the ladder. For a moment, the harbor is quiet in a way that feels like blessing rather than absence.

Mara Ellis: "Thanks for today."

Jonah Reyes: (smiling) "Bring your stubbornness. It makes good policy."

You both laugh—an honest sound that pockets itself in the ferry's hull.

[Background:"ch1_Start_5"]
[Music: Piano motif rises into a hopeful chord]

You stand at the rail a moment longer, letting the harbor wash the day into a slow glow. The pilot's numbers sit in your notebook like small truths. The community is stirring with cautious pride. Jonah Reyes's presence is a tangible possibility. Professor Asha is already threading political routes. The next step—the council corridors, the wider rooms where decisions multiply—feels close enough to taste.

You feel the tide of the chapter turn in your favor. Hope is not naive; it's earned, measured, and ready to be argued for.

Page-turn thought: You fold the berm sketch into your pocket, tighten the strap on your boots, and name the next day's work like a vow—present, explain, connect. The city's glass heart might be sterile and sharp, but you intend to warm it, one community-proof at a time.
