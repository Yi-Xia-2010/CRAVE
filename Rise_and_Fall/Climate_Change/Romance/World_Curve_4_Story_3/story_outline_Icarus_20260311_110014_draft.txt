## Visual Novel Title
Tidefall: An Icarus of Salt and Steel

---

## World View
New Maris is a near-future coastal metropolis carved between intact high-rises and neighborhoods learning to live with water. The city is governed by a patchwork of municipal agencies, private contractors, and neighborhood councils; politics revolve around adaptation funding, zoning, and access to insurance. Culture is maritime and pragmatic: fish markets and coffee stalls rub shoulders with municipal planning sessions and lobbyist dinners. Religion and ritual have reemerged in small ways—shoreline commemorations and tide-blessing vigils—while class divides are explicit: fortified districts with private flood defenses, and lower-lying wards relying on community resilience. Geography folds salt flats into reclaimed promenades, rooftop gardens into storm vents; rainfall is both literal and metaphorical, washing promises away. Conflicts center on technocratic, large-scale engineered fixes promoted by corporate actors versus distributed, nature-based strategies driven by local stewards. The atmosphere is tactile and textured—wet wood, algae, neon reflections—where policy hearings are as charged as rooftop apologies. Intimacy forms among the hum of desalination pumps and the smell of sea-wet moss; the central tension—between quick containment and long-term ecological repair—pulls the city toward impossible choices.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I play Mara Ellis, a marine ecologist who wants to prove that nature-based adaptation and community stewardship can scale. Early in the story I reconnect with Jonah Reyes, an engineer rooted in fishing culture who can translate community needs into practical projects. We ride a rising tide of momentum: pilot restorations, persuasive council testimony, and Jonah’s hybrid designs create a wave of public support and political wins. At our apex, the city embraces a high-profile, hybrid adaptation program that seems to validate our approach and binds Jonah and me together professionally and personally. But the success draws Elias Crowe’s corporate might—and a series of rapid decisions, compromises, and unforeseen environmental feedbacks trigger a catastrophic collapse: a storm surge, a critical failure in engineered elements, political backlash, and community displacement. Each route follows the Icarus curve: an accelerated ascent to a celebrated peak followed by a devastating fall. The branches let the player choose what compromises we make—alliances with Jonah, confrontations with Elias, or legal and scientific gambits—so that each rapid rise differs in flavor and each crash has different consequences for relationships, neighborhoods, and the future of New Maris.

* **Visuals:** Rooftop garden moss and succulents, my brass dive watch catching dawn, Jonah's camera leaning against a reclaimed planter (if present), Maya in a neon-yellow coat handing out seedlings.
* **Valence:** Mixed / Hopeful
* **Arousal:** Low-Medium
* **Branch:** Epilogue (Shared)
* **Next:** End

The garden was small and smelled like wet earth and rosemary. No ordinance, no headline, just hands in soil. I pressed seedlings into a planter salvaged from a shipping crate and felt the tiny give of life under my fingers. Jonah stood with me sometimes—his camera tucked in a shoulder strap, his forearms sun-roughened—or he was far away, caught in bureaucratic repair projects. Maya boiled beans and passed cups to neighbors who had weathered the worst. Old Mate told a tide story that had a cruel twist but ended in laughter. The city still bore its scars: seawalls, vacant lots, and memorial placards where houses once stood. We had flown too high and been burned, or we had been right and crushed by forces that hid as policy and profit. Either way, the day-to-day tending of what remained felt sacred. I set my brass watch beside the planter, the etched tide lines catching the light, and I planted one more seed.
