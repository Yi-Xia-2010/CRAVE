### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Ending


[Scene: Tide Lab (Converted Research Ferry) | Morning — Post-Pilot]
[Visual: The ferry's steel deck gleams with spray; laminated photo prints are clipped to a line like talismans. A soft, clinical blue light mixes with the warm glow of task lamps. Jonah's camera sits open, prints fanned across a wet bench.]
[Sound: Camera shutter echoes softly; distant gulls; the low hum of pumps and monitors]
[Music: Bright strings, steady and hopeful]

You stand at the lab bench with your notebook folded into the inner pocket of your jacket, the berm sketch a small, private compass against your ribs. The prints—Jonah's—catch the lab light and throw back a city you've been trying to name: the shore held, the salt-marsh ribs flexing, people on reclaimed walkways, seedlings catching sunlight.

You breathe in. The air smells of metal, coffee grounds, and the faint green of algae—an oddly comforting bouquet that says this place is alive and working.

Jonah leans across the bench; his shoulder brushes yours and it feels like a quiet confirmation. He points at a print where the berm curves gracefully around a flooded inlet, dotted by small clusters of planted sedge.

Jonah Reyes: (low, pleased) "That's the spread the feed picked up. They ran your quote with it—'Dignity in adaptation.' You gave them a line they couldn't ignore."

You: (half-laughing, half-surprised) "Dignity? That sounds like something Maya would pin to a poster."

Jonah Reyes: (grinning) "Maya would pin it and add glitter. But it's true. People respond to that word."

You study the prints again. Momentum is visible in pixels and headlines, but you feel it in subtler places: the steadier cadence of the pumps, the volunteers who show up earlier, the way the seedlings have teeth now.

Professor Asha Rao enters, carrying a stack of printouts, her braid swinging with the motion. Her face is bright with a smile that doesn't erase the scientist's caution behind it.

Professor Asha Rao: "Congratulations are in order. The council's vote this morning—unanimous to pilot expansion citywide. They cited your data and the community testimony. New Maris wants to replicate the model."

You feel your chest rise, a soft, physical lift that the strings in your head mirror. The word 'unanimous' tastes like rare and fragile fruit.

You: "Citywide?"

Professor Asha Rao: (nodding) "Citywide. But listen—there are strings attached. Investors and contractors are circling. That growth will bring changes we didn't model for yet."

You: (watching the prints) "We planned for scale in the code and the sediment maps—"

Professor Asha Rao: (interrupting gently) "—but not for corporate procurement cycles, not for integrated proprietary systems that gate data. The physics scale linearly; the social systems do not. We need to talk through thresholds, maintenance loops, and governance clauses before anyone signs anything."

You feel the warmth of the moment and the cold of the warning braided together. Hope rising; caution tethered to the keel.

[Scene: Tide Lab — Later that morning]
[Visual: A small cluster of community members, volunteers, and a couple of council aides surrounds a rough model where algae mats are pinned to scaled berms. Jonah runs his finger along a print, explaining exposure pathing.]
[Sound: Murmured conversations, the scratch of pens, the soft slosh of water in a measuring cup]
[Music: Piano motif, hopeful and bright]

Maya is there—neon raincoat patched with pins—her face flushed with pride. She plants her palms on the model like grounding herself to it.

Maya: (laughing, almost breathless) "You did it. God, you did it. The Promenade looked like it was glowing on screen."

You: (letting the pride wash through) "It didn't do itself. People showed up."

Maya: "And Jonah's pictures—every feed had one of his prints. He should be celebrated like a festival photographer."

Jonah Reyes: (shrugging) "I only point the lens. The story is yours, Mara."

You: (softly) "We both pointed it."

Jonah's smile is real and low; it warms the space between the two of you like sun through storm-clouds. The conversation ricochets between gratitude and practicalities—maintenance bids, procurement timelines, questions about who will own the monitoring data.

A representative from a maintenance firm leans in, voice polite, almost eager.

Maintenance Rep: "If you scale, you'll want streamlined contracts. Exclusive maintenance guarantees lower costs and ensure consistency. We can propose a package."

You glance at Jonah. He meets your eyes, and something passes: the implicit weighing of present needs against long-term stewardship.

Jonah Reyes: (after a beat) "We should be cautious about exclusivity. It locks out local crews and raises costs in the long term."

Maintenance Rep: (measured) "Contracts can include community subcontracts. It still benefits from a single point of accountability."

You feel the room tilt toward 'single point' language. Your jaw tightens—not from anger, but from the muscle of stubbornness that has built this model.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Ask for a community-subcontract clause now") -> Reaction: "You speak quickly. 'Any maintenance scope must prioritize local crews and open procurement windows.' The rep nods, but the smile is guarded. You feel a small victory—procedural, but meaningful. || Choice B ("Table contract talks and focus on monitoring clauses") -> Reaction: "You say, 'We need to finalize monitoring and governance before procurement.' The room reluctantly agrees. The rep's expression cools; you note the flicker of impatience."]

You notice Jonah's hand tracing the edge of a print, then pausing near where your fingers rest on the bench. The closeness is a steadying gravity.

Jonah Reyes: (quiet) "There's another thing. NovaSeis reached out. They want me to consult on integrated components—smart sensors that talk to their control systems. They're offering funding to make the citywide rollout seamless. But—" (he hesitates) "—they asked me to sign a non-disclosure for their subsidiary arrangements."

Your heart stutters, not out of jealousy but the recognition of a pivot. The phrase "make it seamless" has under it the fine print of ownership.

You: (focusing) "Did you tell them about the monitoring clause? About community access?"

Jonah Reyes: "I pushed it, but their lawyers were... insistent. They framed the NDA as standard IP protection. They say it's to protect proprietary integration work."

Professor Asha Rao: (frowning) "Proprietary integration—if it means locked data streams, that undermines transparency. We built this to keep communities empowered. You have to be careful, Jonah."

Jonah Reyes: (running a hand through his hair) "I know. I'm torn. They can accelerate the rollout and get shelters in place before the next storm season. But I don't want to hand over control."

You feel the scale of the moment. The city's appetite to replicate your work can be the lever that lifts whole neighborhoods—but the lever is in hands that demand leverage.

[Scene: Promenade | Dusk — Celebration Walk]
[Visual: The boardwalk glows with reflected neon; puddles mirror string lights and faces. Volunteers carry small paper cups; Jonah carries his battered camera in one hand, your other hand sometimes brushing his.]
[Sound: Laughter, distant music from a vendor stall, water lapping against pilings]
[Music: Warm, swelling strings with a hopeful choir undercurrent]

You walk the Promenade with Jonah and Maya, the world feeling a size larger and softer. People nod and call out thanks. A council aide walks beside you with a printed press release; the headline reads: "New Maris Model: Council Approves Citywide Pilot."

Council Aide: "Congratulations. We put out the release. They'll hold a press conference on the quay tomorrow. Prepare a short statement—Mayor wants an emphasis on 'partnership' and 'community leadership.'"

Maya: (elbowing you, proud) "Partnership, huh? There's our word."

You: (smiling) "We earned that."

Jonah takes a photo of a salt-marsh tuft reflected in a puddle; the print will likely make the morning feed again. He hands you the camera for a moment. Your fingers brush the cold metal; you feel clumsy and elated at once.

Jonah Reyes: (soft) "Come with me to the quay tomorrow. Stand with me. We'll say it together."

You: (surprised at the gentleness) "I will. I want the community to hear it from both of us."

He presses the back of his hand to yours—a small, deliberate contact. The world narrows to that warmth.

A group of investors pass by, well-dressed against the salt, their voices low and bright.

Investor Lead: "Scaling this model is attractive. We can help underwrite the expansion—especially if we can integrate some of our tech."

You feel the familiar prickle—the scent of money, and with it, the pressure to move fast.

You: (to Jonah, quietly) "How willing are you to compromise to get that speed?"

Jonah Reyes: (looking at you with honest eyes) "I'm willing to make a plan that protects the community. I want speed because people live here. But not if we trade the things that make it worth saving."

The next words are a promise and a pact.

Jonah Reyes: (smiling) "We keep the monitoring open, we lock in local procurement windows, and we don't sign anything that hands data or control away."

You: "Agreed. On paper."

He presses his palm to yours in front of a cluster of flashing phones as a stranger takes a picture. Cameras capture the moment and the press will frame it as partnership.

[Visual: Camera flash; the Promenade lights reflect sharply in puddles]
[Sound: Multiple camera clicks; a cheer from a nearby group]
[Music: A single, sustained chord of warmth]

Professor Asha's voice arrives in your mind—practical and steady—reminding you to thread the governance clauses that will matter when the city's appetite is hungrier.

Professor Asha Rao (memory): "Scale will introduce emergent dynamics we haven't simulated. Insist on staged rollouts with feedback thresholds. Data governance must be public."

You feel pride in what you've done swell until it becomes almost dizzying. Jonah's presence beside you feels like a second current—steady, carrying you forward.

Maya: (jabbing at Jonah) "You two, stop being adorable and sign the governance clauses."

Jonah Reyes: (laughing) "We'll write them. Together."

You: (letting the laughter and warmth settle) "Together."

[Scene: Tide Lab — Late Night, Quiet]
[Visual: The ferry is quieter; monitors display soft green readouts. The city beyond is a smudge of rain and lights. You and Jonah sit across from each other, hands warmed around paper cups.]
[Sound: Gentle beeping of the monitors; distant thunder like a drumroll]
[Music: Soft piano, hopeful with a hint of tension]

The lab's hum is different at night—intimate, full of small, human sounds. You pour over projected timelines: procurement windows, maintenance schedules, staged monitoring milestones. The sterile lines on the spreadsheet are suddenly about people's roofs and boats and the dignity you promised.

Jonah reaches across the table, folding a printed clause into your hand: a draft NDA excerpt—redacted here and there.

Jonah Reyes: (quiet) "They asked me to sign something like this. It's standard for their IP. But the redactions—Mara, they hide who controls the data streams."

You feel a tightening you know well: the stubbornness that refuses to trade core values.

You: "If you sign, even as a consultant, you risk creating a precedent. NovaSeis can argue integration is necessary for reliability."

Jonah Reyes: (looking down) "They also offered to fund community monitoring for the first two years. It could make maintenance feasible."

You: (softly) "And the cost?"

Jonah Reyes: "A lot of leverage. They'd want favored contracts. They want to show a public-private success fast."

Professor Asha's earlier warning returns in sharper color. You imagine the algal mats and the berms, and you picture them behind a glass pane, their data funneled through a corporate sluice.

Professor Asha Rao: (entering, steady) "Mara, Jonah—before anyone signs: staged authority. You require open APIs, community-led dashboards, and external audits every six months. We can draft it with legal. But it must be non-negotiable."

You exhale. The night air sifts through the ferry's open hatch—salt, ozone, a little of the rain that smells like new clean earth. Your stubbornness and Asha's reason conspire into a plan.

You: "We craft the clauses and the staged rollouts. We insist on transparency."

Jonah Reyes: (looking up, resolute) "And if NovaSeis refuses?"

You: (steady) "Then we slow expansion and protect what we have."

Jonah's hand finds yours across the bench. He presses his palm to yours the way he'd done on the Promenade—quiet courage, a promise that isn't loud but is heavier than applause.

[Sound: A camera phone clicks from the dark as a volunteer checks messages. The photo of your joined hands will already be making the rounds.]

You let the rising hope settle into resolve. The city has offered you leverage and attention; your work has become a public thing. That means choices will multiply, and each will carry heat.

You think of the council's press release waiting to be read aloud tomorrow, of Mayor's speech, of volunteers and seedlings and the people who will live or lose depending on how you move.

[Visual: Close-up on Jonah's palm against yours; the ferry's light casts both in a warm halo]
[Music: Strings swell into a bright, determined chord]

Page-turn thought: The city has opened a door. Beyond it are investors, contractors, and NovaSeis's polished offers. You can feel the city's appetite for scale—and the weight of what you must refuse. The choice will define the model and, perhaps, the shape of your relationship with Jonah as you decide what you will and will not trade.
