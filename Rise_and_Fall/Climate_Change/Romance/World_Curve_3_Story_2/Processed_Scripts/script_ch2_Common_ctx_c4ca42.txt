You close your notebook and press your palm to the leather as if the cover could hold the town steady. The friendship bracelet tucked between pages rubs the pad of your thumb until the threads bite. The memory of your brother is a tide you do not trust; it pulls at the same places your hands remember how to steady things that break.

The room smells of coffee gone cold and paper, with a trace of wet rope like a promise that has already been stretched thin. Tide charts and annotated maps cover the walls in a grid of ink and pinholes. Fluorescent light stutters and pools, turning everyone’s faces slightly sallow, making the edges of voices sharper.

At the head of the long table, Mayor Lucia Varela presides with that small brass pin on her lapel. Her jaw is a map of lines you know—every compromise, every vote etched into the set of her mouth. Around her, bureaucrats trade numbers like weather, voices low and professional, as if the right statistic could be a wall strong enough to keep the sea out.

You feel Elias before you see him: the soft click of a tablet on the table, the measured slide of a slate-gray raincoat folding onto a chair. He moves like someone who has practiced this room—calm, efficient, a smile that smooths edges. When you lift your eyes he meets yours, sea-glass blue searching for an opening the way engineers search for tolerances.

Elias Hart: "Amina." (he inclines his head) "Thanks for coming early. I know this room feels longer than it did in my briefing slides."

Amina Reyes: "Longer in ways slides can't measure." Your voice surprises you—flat, controlled and then quick with something that wants to be fiercer. You can feel the room waiting to see whether you'll hold with facts or snap with feeling.

Elias Hart slides the tablet across the table. Renderings bloom: a hybrid sea-wall, clean lines, ramps and retrofitted facades, a shimmering model of stormwater channels. The 3D model under the dome from the field office sits again, smaller and colder here.

Elias Hart: "This is a hybrid proposal. Targeted walls, urban retrofit, raised ingress points—economically feasible, technically sound. We can move fast if we secure emergency funds."

Mayor Lucia Varela: "Fast is attractive when the horizon looks like it might bite tomorrow." (her eyes flick to you and then away; she wears the weight of votes like a shawl)

From the other side of the table, Niko Kaur leans forward, salt-streaked cargo trousers creaking as they shift. The bandana at their neck is an island of color against their workworn silhouette. Their voice comes out low and hard as packed tar.

Niko Kaur: "You mean everywhere we fish? The lanes are not renderings. They're stories, they're nets, they're people. You build that wall and you push the tides somewhere else."

Elias Hart: (patient) "We account for displacement. Models show mitigations—"

Niko Kaur: "Models don't have hands. They don't know how it feels to pull a hull out on a night when the tide decides who keeps a business and who doesn't."

The room tightens. Dr. Kenji Sato clears his throat, paper rustling.

Dr. Sato: (careful) "Long-term scenarios show increasing frequency of high-water events. The window to reduce risk narrows. Adaptive actions now—whether engineered or ecological—change outcomes over decades."

Marina, who has become a sort of pulse for the town's younger generation, presses both palms flat on the table.

Marina: "The school—please, someone thinks about the school. If the lower wing floods, the kids have nowhere to go. We can’t gamble with them."

Your hands are on the notebook. You open to a page full of tide-line observations, marsh plantings, costs of living shoreline interventions—community-driven steps you sketched into feasibility notes. Logic is a tool you trust; memory is a hornet that stings when you let it crowd the facts.

Amina Reyes: "The marsh filters surge, acts like a sponge. Living shorelines can reduce wave energy without devastating habitats. If we cut them out in favor of a monolithic wall, we erase livelihoods that don't show up on a balance sheet."

Elias Hart: (leans back, voice smoothing) "I'm not proposing erasure. I'm proposing options that keep people safe now, while funding allows—"

Amina Reyes: "Now." The word is a stone in your mouth. "Emergency funding that buys 'now' with permanent loss is a bad trade."

Mayor Lucia Varela: (gentle, but firm) "Nobody wants permanent loss. But if opting for slower, grassroots routes risks a catastrophic event in the window we have—"

Niko Kaur: (interrupting) "Then we prepare to get up in the aftermath like we always have. We don't trade the marsh for a promise written in concrete by people who don't eat fish."

Elias Hart: (a flicker of irritation) "You keep framing this like a cultural artifact vs. a technical fix. It's not zero-sum. It's an integrated plan. If we don't act fast, we lose the option to integrate at all."

Dr. Sato: (counting on fingers, quietly) "Fast can mean rushed. Rushed can cause maladaptation. The timeline here—if emergency funds require compliance in months, there is a high risk of unintended consequences."

The air tastes metallic on your tongue, the old tang of anger you've learned not to swallow. Memory folds around the present: boards floating past a neighbor's porch, a child's lost shoelace trailing like a petition. You feel the room split into two currents—one that wants measurable walls and quick metrics, and one that wants the slower, messier work of living systems.

Elias Hart: (quietly, turning back to you) "Stories matter, Amina. They guide design. They should be in the models."

Amina Reyes: (breathe) "They should be the first input, not an afterthought. The models must account for subsistence patterns, for cultural practices. Otherwise they impose a map on a landscape whose people don't fit the lines."

Mayor Lucia Varela: (weighing words) "Kenji, what do the models suggest if we prioritize living shorelines with phased engineering?"

Dr. Sato: (adjusting glasses) "A phased approach reduces peak risk over a longer horizon, but it requires steady funding, local labor coordination, and tolerance for incremental failure."

The room tilts toward practical talk—budgets, timelines—but under everything is the hum of something more dangerous: politics. Someone slides a dossier labeled "Emergency Funds: Conditions" across the table. The papers rustle like a dry sea.

Councillor (off-screen): (flat) "The regional authority will release money on the condition we accept a standardized compliance schedule. No deviations, no delays. They want to see a signed plan in three months."

The sentence lands like a bell. You taste metal and anger, and the air in your lungs sharpens with urgency and the sharp scent of ozone before a storm.

You glance at Elias. His eyes are unreadable—careful, practiced—that same look you've seen before when negotiations reached a hinge. You look at Niko. Their expression is a closed thing; loyalty coiled like a rope ready to snap.

Mayor Lucia Varela: "That's the trade. Commit now and secure funds. Decline and hope for alternative funding while the sea waits. It isn't an abstract choice."

Your notebook lies open between you and the table, a collection of things that cannot be taken as fact by any regional bureau: hand-drawn marsh cross-sections, a list of households dependent on inshore fishing, notes on elder knowledge. None of it is a line item the funding body will honor without translation.

You feel the room constrict, the margins of safety narrowing. The council looks toward you, toward Lucia, toward Elias—toward the person they expect to translate memory into policy.

Your heart thumps in the ribcage like a fist. Your mouth tastes of salt and iron. The sound of the kettle clicking off becomes a single, brittle note that hangs too long.
