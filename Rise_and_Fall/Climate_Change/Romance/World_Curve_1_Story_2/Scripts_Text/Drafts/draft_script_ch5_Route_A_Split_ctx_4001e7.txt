# Route: Route A (Ending A1)


[Scene: Tidehouse | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the Tidehouse's glass panels streaked with salt; inside, maps and jars crowd a long table]
[Music: Gentle, ascending piano with soft strings — hopeful]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the slow splash of water against pilings]

You step back from the window and let the harbor's pale light settle on your shoulders like a promise. The notebook in your jacket is still warm from being held; three words you wrote—Teach. Repeat. Share.—seem less like instructions and more like oaths. The pilot sites hummed yesterday with people who learned a knot, scraped a plank, seeded a box garden; the footage Arin ran through felt honest and small and impossible-not-to-love.

The Tidehouse smells of wet paper, antiseptic from the lab sink, and the algae-sweet of samples drying on racks. Your wristband buzzes with new sensor logs, a soft, insistent reminder that data and memory travel on the same current here.

Mira Solace: "We have momentum. The team is ready to keep going."

Arin Voss (leaning on a crate, camera slung at his hip): "Ready doesn't mean the city's going to pay attention. But it's a start. The reels are clean—real hands, real fixes. People respond to that."

Samir (standing over a tablet, glasses catching the light): "Data helps. We've got the pilot metrics—reduction in localized flooding incidents, maintenance time estimates. If we frame it as cost-saving over ten years, the city will listen."

You study their faces: Arin's earnest impatience, Samir's careful arithmetic. Each argument pulls at different chords in you. The cooperative model sings to the parts of you that remember the Old Pier's ropes and shared meals; municipal adoption would mean infrastructure budgets and legal frameworks; donor funding could buy scale fast, but at what cost to autonomy?

Noah's voice comes in on a video call—he's elbow-deep in a repair, paint smeared on his knuckles—bringing the personal into the room like salt into a wound and a salve at once.

Noah Solace (on screen): "If this scales, my relocation gets pushed back. My bills don't care how we scale. Do what you can, sis."

You feel the tug—brotherly urgency threaded through your chest. The pilots aren't abstraction for Noah; they're the lines of work that keep his shop afloat. You breathe the harbor air, and your decision muscles flex with the gravity of it.

You walk to the large worktable. Tide maps are pinned beside neighborhood photos. You trace a route with a fingertip: Old Pier, a cluster of green roofs, one of the pilot sites. Each dot represents people. Each dot makes the choice you face more than strategic—it's moral.

Samir: "Municipal adoption gives access to resources. Permitting help, enforcement—things we don't have as a grassroots group. But the timeline is political. It's slow."

Arin: "Slow will get you drowned. Donor funds accelerate installs, training. We could triple the number of pilot sites in months."

Mira Solace: "And a cooperative keeps control local. Folks who fix the roofs would own the process. It might be slower, but it's durable."

Samir looks at you with an expression that mixes respect and exasperation.

Samir: "You weigh durability and legitimacy differently than the rest of us. That's your strength. But we also need to be honest: 'durable' could mean 'never reaching enough people'."

Arin (jaw tightening, not unkind): "Sometimes durable feels like sitting while the city bulldozes what you love. Rapid growth means pressure, but people would have shelter sooner."

Mira Solace: (you let the words come slow) "This isn't just about roofs or seawalls. It's about who gets to say 'we adapted' and who gets to profit from it."

The room falls into a thoughtful quiet. The machines hum. Outside, a ferry's horn draws a long, lonely line across the water. Hope feels like a slow gear turning under the weight of other people's livelihoods—big, but moving.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand hovering above paper, the salt-smudged fingertip tracing the edge of an outline]
[Music: Piano lingers, a hopeful swell underneath]

The rest of the morning becomes choreography. Arin insists on a schedule of shoots to make reels that will make people show up. Samir keeps nudging at model assumptions and contingencies. Noah sends an image of a torn sign outside his shop—an eviction notice fluttering on a string—then quickly says something softer about training apprentices to keep the work local.

Noah Solace: "Don't let anyone sell that patch of dock without giving locals first dibs. That's all I'm asking."

Mira Solace: "I won't forget that. We can write access agreements into the cooperative model."

Arin (smiling despite the undercurrent): "Or, we make the reels so moving the city can't ignore them. They have to act."

Samir: "You can make them listen with both numbers and narratives. The trick is aligning both without losing our ethics."

You think about ethics the way you think about a tide chart: a series of predictable highs and dangerous lows, patterns you can map but not control. Scaling needs three things: labor, capital, and governance. Each path trades one thing for another. You're not deciding in a vacuum—you're choosing which value gets priority first.

[Scene Transition: Rooftop Common Garden | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm, soft light; dense vegetation; strings of solar lamps; a rooftop table with tea]
[Music: Light acoustic guitar and soft wind chimes]
[Sound: Bees, distant city hum muffled by plants]

The rooftop feels protected from the city's harder edges, a green room where conversation can be generous. You arrive with your notebook again, this time filled with sketches, numbers, and a sticky note that reads: "Who owns the outcome?"

Arin greets you with a folded reel and an excited grin; Samir carries a thermos and a stack of printouts. The plants rustle under the late sun. Neighborhood kids run along a parapet, their laughter masking the tension.

Arin Voss: "Showtime. I spliced the pilot footage with interviews Etta helped with. Her voice is a spine. People see the faces, they can't pretend the place doesn't matter."

Mira Solace: "Run it."

Arin sets the camera on a small projector. Against a blank wall, faces bloom: a grandmother teaching a child to tie a knot, Noah fixing a hull, volunteers planting sedum. The edits are quick and intimate—no spectacle, just hands and care and weathered smiles.

Samir watches, fingers tapping a rhythm on a printout.

Samir: "The narrative plus metrics—if we frame this as 'community resilience reduces emergency costs by X', we're not pleading. We're being fiscally rational."

Arin (a little fierce): "Sometimes fiscal talk sterilizes the thing it's trying to sell. Don't let them strip the heart out."

Mira Solace: (you feel both arguments like tides meeting) "We need both. We need language that gets us resources and safeguards that keep control local."

Arin reaches across the table and taps your notebook.

Arin Voss: "So what's the move, Mira? You—because you think in the right-sized steps—what do you want the first legal document to look like?"

You consider the rooftop's warm air and the steady rise of confidence in your team. Hope opens like the garden between your hands.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Start with a membership-led cooperative charter—seed with volunteer labor") -> Reaction: "You outline sections for membership, shared labor quotas, dispute resolution. It feels like sewing a communal garment—awkward stitches that will hold tighter with time." || Choice B ("Set a meeting with the municipal resilience office—pitch adoption of the pilot model") -> Reaction: "You begin composing a formal meeting request in your head: data highlights, policy alignments, implementation timeline. The words are strategic; they feel weighty but necessary." ]

Noah leans against a planter, watching the clip on the wall. His jaw is soft when he speaks.

Noah Solace: "If you choose cooperative, make sure tradespeople get training stipends. If you go city, make sure relocations are fund-backed. If donors—get guarantees written."

Samir nods: "All three paths can carry safeguards. The difference is speed and leverage."

Arin: "And moral clarity. I want folks to feel like they own the work, not rent it."

Mira Solace: (you close your eyes for a second, and in that quiet the harbor's currents map themselves into possible futures) The cooperative would preserve the social fabric; the municipal route would scale with legitimacy; philanthropic funding could let you act fast where urgency matters most. Each path promises ascent in its own register. Each requires sacrifice of something you love.

You stand, hands on the railing, and watch the sun make the harbor glitter. People on the dock light lamps; someone begins a small song. Your chest lifts with the same hopeful energy that's threaded through the morning: the pilots worked because people showed up and repaired what they could. Now the question is how to transform those repairs into a living, spreading thing.

Arin looks at you, eager and patient.

Arin Voss: "Mira—your pick will steer the next months. Think of it like planting. What do you want to grow first?"

You open your notebook, feeling the page's texture and the swell of possibility. This is a moment of rising: you are not deciding to give up or settle. You are deciding how best to hold what you've already made.
