You dislike the math of compromise, but you know its architecture. This is the political physics of the harbor now.

Donor Representative: "We can include a community governance board with veto on site disposition and a tranche release schedule tied to third-party audits. We can underwrite a regional partnership to scale the retrofit modules across other neighborhoods."

But the inclusion of reversibility and local quotas moves the needle more than you expected. Those small designs could scale if the money arrives and is tied to the social structures that birthed them.

Arin Voss: "So we sign a memorandum of understanding that binds corporate match to community-defined milestones and creates a protected operating fund for the cooperative programs. Right? This can't be a one-off."

Donor Representative: "Correct. We can structure a permanent funding line—an endowment that supports training and maintenance—with an oversight board composed of residents, municipal appointees, and independent auditors."

Mira Solace [softly]: "We need language that guarantees accessible dispute resolution, and legal protections against eminent-domain-style reclassification."

Liora Kade [measured]: "We can draft that. It will take legal work, but it is feasible if everyone at this table commits."

A small ease moves through your shoulders. Feasible: the harbor's practical word for hope. You let yourself imagine the cooperative workshops running without the frantic scramble you've grown used to. You imagine funding for Noah's shop to expand into sustainable hull retrofits, apprenticeships in Etta's hands, and a regional network that shares blueprints like songs.
