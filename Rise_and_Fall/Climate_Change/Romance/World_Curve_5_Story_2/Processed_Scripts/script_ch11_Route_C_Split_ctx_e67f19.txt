# Route: Route B/C Shared Ending
# Context: e67f19

[Scene: Marshlands / Living Shoreline Site | Dusk]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_1"]
[Sound: Mud-suction, hushed voices, the distant clip of Jonah's laughter]
[Music: Low, tentative strings—minor key; a steady heartbeat of percussion]

You are planting willows with tired hands when the first odd sound reaches you: not the regular slap of waves against the stakes, but a deep, ragged groan underfoot—soil shifting, the world rearranging itself. For half a breath you think it's your imagination; then the reed-line shudders and a low, urgent roar fills the air.

[Sound: A sudden, tearing rush—like fabric ripping underwater]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_2"]

You move before you have a plan. The movement is pure muscle memory: knees bend, boots sink, fingers reach for the closest volunteer. Someone screams a name you do not know. A willow you've planted minutes ago cascades into the mud like a struck hand.

This wasn't supposed to— Your thought collapses. Pilot designs are meant to absorb, to slow, not to fail like a broken hinge.

[Sound: Shouts, a child's cry choked off, the distant metallic ping of a monitoring sensor buckling]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_3"]

Jonah Reyes: "Lena! Over here—help with the netting!"

You push through cold, the salt-smell thick like iron. The mud pulls at your boots. You find Jonah hauling a collapsed section of coir matting, his face set; his shirt is smeared with dark clay. For a second your eyes lock—there's alarm there, sharp and immediate. You both know what failure looks like, the particular sickening quality of an idea falling apart in public.

Jonah Reyes: "We need to check the foundation posts—something undermined the substrate. Asha, what did the sensors say?"

Dr. Asha Khatri [breathing hard]: "Pressure spikes—unexpected influx. There's subsidence under the sill line. The root mats didn't bind as predicted where the tide ran faster than our survey noted."

Jonah lowers his head, jaw tight. You can see the calculation happening: where to shore reinforcements, which volunteers to call back from the waterline. He looks at you as if asking permission to act.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Run to the Dunehouse to warn the occupants"
> Choice B: "Stay and coordinate shore repairs with Jonah and Asha"
[Outcome A: "You sprint toward the dunehouse, lungs burning, and arrive to see water already hissing beneath the boarded windows—photos peeling on the porch step, the smell of old cedar mixing with new brine."]
[Outcome B: "You stay; your voice snaps orders into the chaotic air. Volunteers move like a single organism—you redistribute lanterns, tally equipment, and try to buy time for the failing sections."]
[MERGE: The scene continues as volunteers, Jonah, Asha, and you respond to the immediate crisis.]

You have no warrant to know who is inside the dunehouse—Schrödinger's certainty says don't presume—but the sight of a familiar roofline buckling under water sends a physical lurch through you. Marco's voice answers from the other side of the marsh, raw and immediate.

Marco Maris: "Lena! They're saying Rosie pulled someone out, but the cellar—"

Rosie's calls cut through—organized, furious:

Rosa "Rosie" Alvarez: "Everyone, pull the coir lines! Don't leave the south patch—south patch holds the inlet!"

The south patch does not hold.

Scene collapses into action. Volunteers skitter; someone drops a lantern. You are everywhere and nowhere at once: remembering technique, remembering the town's young faces at training day, remembering the mayor's light, practical voice telling you 'test, measure, publish' and feeling that instruction twist into accusation as neighbors gape at the ruined section.

[Scene: Collapsed Marsh | Night]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_4"]
[Sound: The slow, awful lapping of floodwater; low sobbing; the metallic ping of a Valence drone above, idling]
[Music: Cello drones deepen; a metronomic tick that feels like a judge's gavel]

A drone hums overhead—sleek, corporate, too perfect for this mud. Its camera catches the scene and flashes a little surveillance indicator at the edge of your vision. Iris Valence appears at the boardwalk's lip like a different weather system: precise, dry-cleaned trench over clean boots; a thin light from her device painting the marsh in diagnostic white. Her mouth is a controlled line.

Iris Valence [calm, clipped]: "This is precisely why large-scale, engineered protections should not be partial. We need continuous, tested barriers, not—" (she gestures at the tangle of fallen plantings) "—piecemeal hope."

You watch the volunteers stiffen. Jonah's face tightens; he does not interrupt, but he does not let her tone go unanswered.

Jonah Reyes: "We were testing a hybrid. The idea was to prove that working with the environment could reduce risk and keep people here. Some variables changed fast—"

Iris Valence [cutting]: "Variables that should have been accounted for by robust modeling. The pilot is not a substitute for coherent defense."

The watch in your chest ticks faster. The crowd around you becomes a jury. Accusations are currency tonight.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Call Iris out—publicly demand accountability for her firm's influence"
> Choice B: "Keep the focus on recovery—deflect Iris and organize the immediate work"
[Outcome A: "You step onto a waterlogged stump and raise your voice. The words taste like seawater: you ask her whether Valence's lobbying skewed engineering approvals. The crowd leans in, some cheering, some shocked; Iris's face is unreadable—calm but like a blade."]
[Outcome B: "You lower your voice and redirect. 'Right now we pull people out and tape the sensors,' you tell the volunteers. Iris watches you choose the practical; her expression is complex, not openly pleased but not defeated either."]
[MERGE: Whichever you choose, whispers of blame and fear spread; the volunteers continue rescue and recovery.]

Dr. Asha Khatri [quiet, scientific]: "The marsh compaction failed where we underestimated localized scouring. We didn't model this exact channel. This is on us—on the data we had."

Your gaze slips to a wrecked logbook, pages swollen with brine. Your field notes feel suddenly inadequate, as if someone tore a seam in a dress you thought sturdy.

Marco, face bright with the heat of fresh anger, lashes out in a way that is both protective and dangerous.

Marco Maris: "So whose fault is it? We swapped firm money for pilot support, now my neighbor's house is flooded. Are we guinea pigs because someone wanted a pilot to look good on a grant report?"

The crowd answers with a swell. Rosie pushes through, voice like a bell.

Rosa "Rosie" Alvarez: "Stop. Stop with the finger-pointing. We pull people. We care for them. We'll have a meeting tomorrow. But tonight we pull people."

Mayor: "Rosie is right. First, people. Then we sort blame where it belongs."

But 'where it belongs' splinters into factions overnight. Camps form along easy lines: those furious with Valence; those frightened and wanting immediate, hardened protections; those who insist on doubling down on grassroots solutions; those who say people must be moved.

[Scene: Dunehouse Interior / Abandoned Dunehouse | Night]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_5"]
[Sound: A low wail, the soft clink of a mug being set down]
[Music: A single violin string trembles, unresolved]

You sit on the edge of a sodden mattress, blanket around your shoulders, and let the weight of the night settle. The house smells of salt and cedar and an old, stubborn life. A child presses a soaked photograph into your palm; the edges are soft.

We built this pilot believing scale could contain care. We built it because we wanted to keep homes instead of bulldozing them. And now— and now the marsh failed in public.

Jonah crouches opposite, hands torn and patient, examining the soaked photograph with you, then meeting your eyes. There's so much unsaid—pride, blame, fear. His reaction is complex; you can read determination and quiet grief, but not certainty. He reaches out and rests a hand on your knee in a gesture that is support without solutions.

Jonah Reyes: "We can rebuild, Lena. We have the data. We can shore the south patch and replant stronger mats. We'll do it. We'll show that living shorelines work if we adapt them to what just happened."

You want to believe him. You want the world to bend like that—repairable, honest. But the town's fractures are wider than a single failed patch.

[Scene: Watchhouse Meeting Room | Late Night]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_6"]
[Sound: Mumbled arguments, the occasional raised voice; the hum of fluorescent lights]
[Music: Low, brooding piano—each chord a question mark]

The council convenes because it must. Voices that were once harmonized now clash.

Iris Valence: "We can fund an immediate structural reinforcement. A targeted seawall where it's needed—"

Marco Maris (interrupting): "And displace people? So Valence gets a clean line item and we lose our permits to live where we've always lived? No."

Iris Valence [cool]: "This is about preventing loss of life, Marco. Not everyone can live on the shoreline forever."

Dr. Asha Khatri: "Engineering can save property in the short term. Ecological approaches can restore function in the long term. A hybrid must mean both—rigorous modeling, legal protections, and community oversight. If either element is missing, failure can be catastrophic."

Mayor: "Asha, what you're proposing is heavy. It will need regional coordination—legal frameworks, funding agreements, seats at the table for the town. That won't come free."

Your shoulders ache from the night. The words you will need to say have weight: they'll across budgets, families, and futures.

If we push for strictly local fixes, we keep control but surrender scale and money; if we push for a big institutional framework, we might get funding and protections but give away agency; if we counsel relocation, we save bodies and sacrifice a way of life.

Jonah's eyes find you across the table. He is exhausted, but something steelier runs under it—his stubbornness, his belief in the slow repair. His posture asks you to choose the path that preserves community labor.

Iris watches you with an appraising tilt—her expression unreadable. Whether she sees opportunity, victory, or a wound is hidden; she is the glass-and-steel alternative standing in the doorway.

Dr. Asha Khatri [folding her hands, careful and deliberate]: "Whatever we choose, we need legal teeth. Anti-displacement clauses, binding funding commitments. Without that, promises are paper in the tide."

Marco slams a closed fist onto the table; the sound cracks.

Marco Maris: "And what about people who can't rebuild? My crew—weeklies, dayworkers—if the town picks a path that prioritizes development over jobs, who helps them? If we get a wall and the harbor bar closes, how do we feed our kids?"

Rosa "Rosie" Alvarez: "We need guarantees and jobs, Marco. The co-op can run training, but that won't be enough without serious policy."

The meeting spirals into accusations of betrayal and of cowardice. You feel the town tilting away from consensus into camps, each with its own moral claim.

Leadership isn't about being liked. It's about choosing what you will sacrifice for what you love. But the cost—

[Music: A single chord, unresolved, hangs long]
[Sound: The distant echo of the night's earlier roar—sea meeting now, a reminder]

The room calms only enough for the mayor to speak in a voice that tries on steadiness and fails.

Mayor: "We need a clear plan. Elena—this is your pilot. The council will listen. What do you recommend?"

The eyes in the room turn to you. Some are pleading; some are accusatory; some unreadable. Your chest tightens. You know every option cuts and sews differently through the town's fabric.

I can ask for legal guarantees and a regional coalition, pushing for seats for community members and anti-displacement clauses—politically messy, but with teeth. I can refuse large institutional fixes and double down on grassroots living-shoreline expansion with Jonah, rebuilding trust locally but possibly losing institutional funding. Or I can recommend organized, voluntary relocations for highest-risk families to save lives now while concentrating protections where they will hold—practical but heartbreaking.

Your voice wants to be steady. It wants to be measured and persuasive. Instead it carries the gravel of hope and the bruise of failure.

Elena "Lena" Maris [quiet, firm]: "We need a plan that keeps people safe and keeps our community intact. But I will not lie—the pilot's failure tonight proves we cannot be naive."

You stop. You let the words hang to make room for the truth that follows—because the room is heavy and waiting for something actionable, not platitudes.

[Music: A low, decisive bass note—falling rather than rising]
[Background:"ch11_e67f19_7"]
[Sound: A single gull calling long and thin]

You glance at Jonah—his expression is a tight question. You look at Iris—her face is an unreadable calculus. You see Dr. Asha, patient and tired; Marco, hot with anger; Rosie, steady, already thinking of meals for volunteers.

This is the fulcrum. A choice now will not fix the broken willow or the flooded cellar; it will shape how Brinehaven answers every future tide.

How do I lead after the surge?
