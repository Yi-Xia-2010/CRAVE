The weight of that choice is not light, but it is deliberate.

Elena "Lena" Maris: "We'll make them clearer. And public. No hidden clauses that let the project override local councils."

Iris Valence [softening]: "We can include joint oversight. An independent review board. Environmental contingencies."

You fold the plans open on a card table. Tide charts, replanting schedules, a diagram of the seawall with deliberate gaps marked for marsh channels—your additions.

Elena "Lena" Maris: "If you're putting up a barrier, you design it so the marsh breathes. You fund long-term marsh restoration. You fund job transition programs for fishers and shore workers. And you agree to a community-led oversight seat."

Iris studies the pages, then looks at Asha.

Dr. Asha Khatri: "If the openings are properly sited and we get a committed replanting schedule, marsh vegetation can reestablish along those channels within three years. That will reduce wave energy and improve fish nursery grounds. It won't be instant, but it's real."

Jonah Reyes crosses his arms, watching you as if gauging the horizon in your face.

Jonah Reyes: "You can't mean for them to take the whole thing off the table. They'll want metrics, timelines. We can give them measurable milestones—planting quotas, survival rates."

Elena "Lena" Maris: "Exactly. Measurable, enforceable, and with immediate economic support for folks who'll lose days on the water during construction."

Iris inhales, then nods—an almost clinical acceptance of constraints.

Iris Valence [measured]: "We'll create a transitional fund, managed jointly. We'll commit to a phased build that pauses for ecological reviews at predetermined thresholds. If the marsh recovery falls short, we'll halt heavy construction and pivot."

There's a pause that feels like the pause before a repaired board is nailed back into place—the air between two people testing trust.

Marco arrives mid-conversation, boots dragging muddy slush in a little. His jaw is set; he doesn't hide the suspicion when he looks at Iris, but when he looks at you, there's relief.

Marco Maris: "You really going ahead with this? With her?"

Elena "Lena" Maris [quiet, steady]: "I stood with her in public to get people breathing easier. I'm not handing her the town. We're making sure the town gets a seat at the head table."

Rosie sets down an armful of flyers—one lists the "Community Oversight & Employment Plan" in bold, messy handwriting.

Rosie: "And I'll run trainings for the job fund. We can have replanting apprenticeships, paid. Folks can learn proper wetland nursery techniques. Keep money here."

Iris looks at you, then at Rosie, then meets your eyes again—there's a seam of respect where once there was only rivalry.

Iris Valence: "This partnership will include local hires and training. We will—not just say it, but fund it. My firm will provide start capital and technical support."

You feel the tension unwind like a taut line letting go—still there, but shifted.

[Visual: A swath of marsh where volunteers knee-deep in mud plant clumps of spartina and saltgrass. Nets and seed trays are laid out; small markers dot newly-formed channels in the seawall.]

You are out there, knees pressed into cooling mud, water lapping at your calves. The scent is a green, damp sweetness—peat and salt and something like new paper. Volunteers hand you small bundles; your fingers, deft and familiar, press the root mats into the mud.

Jonah Reyes: "You did good in there. It was brave—standing with Iris."

Elena "Lena" Maris [breathing in]: "It wasn't bravery. It was an accounting of risk. We're buying time in the only way that lasts—by repairing what helps the town survive."

Jonah Reyes [soft]: "I know. It's just—I've been afraid you'd fold. That you'd decide the quicker promise was worth the cost."

Elena "Lena" Maris: "I almost walked a different path. But when it came down to it, I thought of everyone who can't move—you know that. I thought of your seedlings and Marco's face. I decided to try to bend the project into something that helps us."

Jonah smiles, something like relief and pride crossing his features.

Jonah Reyes: "Then let's make sure those seedlings have a town behind them."

You work together for hours. Children from Rosie’s co-op come by and learn to plant. Marco arrives later with a wheelbarrow of stakes and a pair of sawed planks, gruff but smiling as he lugs materials. Asha checks pH and salinity on a tablet, calling out numbers that Jonah and the volunteers log.

Dr. Asha Khatri: "We need to stagger plantings in the high marsh fringe. If we do the upper terrace now, the root mats will trap sediment. That raises the mudline—gives the grasses a fighting chance."

Elena "Lena" Maris: "And the seawall openings?"

Dr. Asha Khatri: "We’ll place them where tidal flushing is strongest and where hydraulics show the least scour. We'll reinforce the channels with living berms—willow fascines and coir logs—so the wall helps, but the marsh keeps breathing."

Iris Valence: "We have learned, and we have listened. This plan represents a compromise that is stronger because of the town's involvement."

Elena "Lena" Maris: "We are protecting lives and livelihoods together. The seawall will be built with openings to maintain tidal exchange, and Valence Urbanworks is funding a community-managed transition fund and training programs. We will publish monthly ecological data and let the oversight board—half community-appointed, half independent—hold us accountable."

There is a ripple through the crowd—scepticism tempered with hope. The words are meeting reality out here, in the faces of people who will live with the outcomes.

Marco steps up when the question period opens. He speaks bluntly about days lost and fears for the future. Iris listens. She answers, not with spin but with a concession that sounds like honesty.

Iris Valence: "We will reimburse certified losses in the construction zone and prioritize hiring from Brinehaven. If we miss targets, there will be immediate remediation funding and a public audit."

A woman near the edge of the crowd cheers—small, brave. Someone else claps; Jonah joins in, his clapping slow and deliberate. Rosie starts a chant—"Reclaim—Repair—Remain"—and one by one others pick it up.
