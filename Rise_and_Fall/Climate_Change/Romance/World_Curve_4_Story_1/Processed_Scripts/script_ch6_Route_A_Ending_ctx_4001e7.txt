You are waist-deep in cold spray, the new berm you helped place at dawn reduced to ragged teeth against the sea. The pilot — the thing you breathed into being, the small promise to the shore — has become a torn page. Salt and rain taste like iron on your lips.

You can't tell if the surge came faster than the forecast or if the pilot's seams simply couldn't hold the new weight. Either way, the result is immediate and terrible: water finds every stitch, and wherever water goes, the marsh tips toward loss. You slide your hand along a ripped mat of cordgrass; clods of mud come away in your palm, black and smelling of rot. Somewhere behind the howl of the storm, somebody screams a name you know.

Niko is where the sound comes from — down by a collapsed shelf of saplings, one leg pinned under a crate. Blood bright against the storm-dull sand. You move before you think. Your jacket is heavy with rain, but you fling yourself across the drift, boots slipping; the rope bracelet at your wrist scrapes a raw line into your palm. You reach him and the world narrows to a sharp, immediate calculus: stop the bleeding, get him warm, get him out.

Tomas Herrera is already shouting into a soaked radio, his words ragged but clear despite the wind. You catch fragments — "triage," "ambulance route," "hold the line" — and then his voice drops when he sees you at Niko's side, and the direction in it becomes something like apology or accusation. It's hard to tell.

Rosa is there too, moving like a lantern in the dark: gloved hands steady as she presses a donated thermal blanket around Niko's shoulders. She smells of damp soil and citrus, and the clammy air around her seems to calm for a second. Her eyes meet yours, and you read the look as if it were a weather gauge — measured, grave, aware of the long aftermath.

By dawn the storm is spent but not forgiving. The pilot is a ragged scar on the beach. Where cordgrass once bent with the tide, there are empty patches and hooked, shredded roots. A line of grey crabs scuttles through a new channel; gulls wheel over stripped salt flats like watchful brass instruments. The damage is precise where it matters: a narrow tidal creek rerouted, a stand of young marsh plants stunted beyond immediate recovery. Little habitats that were just learning how to hold are suddenly not.

Elys Reed appears on the promenade like a planned cutaway shot: hair brushed, rain-damp coat immaculate, microphone handed up to her mouth by an obliging journalist. Her eyes, slate and controlled, scan the scene and find the camera. She speaks with the kind of economy of language that leaves no room for the messy middle.

Elys Reed: "What we saw last night confirms what experts have warned: partial measures will not withstand the next true storm. We need a comprehensive plan, not pilot projects exposed to risk."

Her words fold across the wet crowd and land like a verdict. A cameraman catches Tomas Herrera's face as it goes hard; Mayor Anton slips a hand to his temple, the tired gesture of someone feeling the weight of a tipping scale. You want to step forward, to say — to say that pilots are how you learn, that resilience is iterative, that these losses will teach you where to build stronger — but the microphone is already moving away. A list of talking points is in the pocket of a reporter's coat; Lena's recorder is invisible in a press pack somewhere, and you don't know if it's already being edited into soundbite form.

You approach Tomas Herrera. The conversation between you begins not with accusation but with the small, ragged currency of exhaustion.

Mara Voss: "We patched the berm with what we had. We couldn't wait for the bureaucracy to catch up."

Tomas Herrera: "I know. I argued for staged visibility so we could prove the concept and get funding. That visibility is what was supposed to buy us time."

Mara Voss: "Visibility doesn't keep people from getting hit."

Tomas Herrera: "No — but without it, we don't get the money that pays for the people who would keep this from happening."

You ride that sentence like a wave of sound; it pulls and offers no safe ground. The air between you is thick with the unspoken: the daily trade-offs, the nights spent drafting emails instead of sleeping, the subtle choices that seemed smaller at the table than they turned out to be on the shore.

Tomas Herrera folds his arms, his voice lower now.

Tomas Herrera: "If we keep playing to both sides, we'll please neither. You know that. I tried— I tried to translate your work into something the council could fund."

Mara Voss: "And when the translation strips the work of the thing that makes it live?"

Your voice cracks at "live," and you hate that it does. You're an organizer; you are steady in storms. But grief is a tidal thing, and it finds the seam in anyone who has ever loved a place.

Mayor Anton steps between you, palms up in the small, civic gesture he reserves for heated rooms.

Mayor Anton: "Finger-pointing doesn't help rescue people or plants right now. We need a coherent message, and we need to show support for those injured. We also need an investigation. That will take time."

Elys circles the political drain like a vulture who prefers to call herself an optimist. She approaches you after the cameras, and her smile is a cold coin.

Elys Reed: "Loss is part of a cost-benefit calculation, Ms. Voss. Sometimes the cost of half-measures is that we have to start over — with the right partners."

Her words are not a negotiation; they are a pressure.

You climb the narrow path to the Lantern Lighthouse because you can't bear the press of the promenade and you need a place where wind speaks with authority and you are merely handed its verdict. The stones are slick; your boots leave dim prints. At the top, the sea is less theatrical and more honest: a bruised sheet, steady and indifferent.

Tomas Herrera finds you there at the rim, looking small against the wide, ruined line. He is wet through, coat hung like armor. His expression has the tautness of someone who has been arguing with facts for too long.

Tomas Herrera: "They asked for a short recommendation this afternoon. The council — it's leaning toward Elys' plan."

Mara Voss: "Because a single failure looks like an indictment."

Tomas Herrera: "And because Elys packages certainty. People want a promise you can read in a headline. They don't want the messy sentences."

There is a long silence where both of you listen to the sea. You want to say that this is not over, that pilot or no, the work will continue — but words are old tools and sometimes they chip when used on fresh grief.

Mara Voss: "We built a map of what could live here. We taught people how to read tides. That's not nothing, Tomas."

Tomas Herrera: "I know."

He reaches for your hand. For a moment your fingers entwine and there is a memory of small, steady things: a rooftop table, a shared thermos, a plan sketched in burnt coffee rings. It is tender and bleak.

Tomas Herrera: "I don't want this to break us."

Mara Voss: "Neither do I."

His face folds inward, torn between the public person he is expected to be and the private man who gives you an almost invisible squeeze at the council table. The strain is visible now: the shoulders he doesn't let down, the tiredness at his eyes. There is no manager of this grief; there is only the two of you and the widening gap of decisions that reached into real storms and pulled harm back out.

Tomas Herrera: "If the council goes with Elys, I'll keep pushing safeguards inside whatever process forms. I can do that from the planner's seat."

Mara Voss: "And I'll keep rebuilding where I can — where the community trusts the work. I'll be in the streets."

You look at him — at the earnestness in his expression and the fragile hope — and you understand the shape of what you are both offering: different continuations of the same care. Neither is a betrayal, and both look like incompletion.

Rosa arrives quietly, leaning on a cane that sings of older storms. She regards both of you, and when she speaks it sounds like something older than politics and younger than the hurt.

Rosa Marin: "Love and duty aren't always the same road. Sometimes they run side by side, sometimes one crosses the tide. That doesn't mean one is wrong."

You feel the truth of that in your shoulder blades as if a net has been loosened. There is mourning — for plants, for a volunteer's reckless bravery, for the illusion of a simple fix. But there is also the small, stubborn fact that people will keep showing up: to clean, to record, to plant.
