### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Option B Ending


[Scene: Rooftop Garden at the Old Textile Mill | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of reclaimed shipping containers, raised beds, strings of low-voltage bulbs; the horizon shows cranes and the sea beyond]
[Music: Warm, hopeful acoustic guitar with a steady, rising undercurrent]
[Sound: Distant gulls, muted traffic, soft hiss of drip irrigation]

You climb the narrow metal stair in the scent of wet soil and salt—your boots scuff the paint that volunteers have trod down to a soft gloss. The rooftop is a patchwork of reclaimed wood and rusted metal warmed under late light. A hummed conversation folds into the evening: someone laughs over a joke, a trowel clinks against a table, and the air tastes of citrus from Rosa's potted kumquats.

On the communal table, the rooftop's makeshift map waits like a promise: butcher-paper taped end-to-end, charcoal lines rough with tide marks, little clusters of green where living-edge plantings are scribbled in. Tomas is already there—an elbow on the map, tablet open like a placemat. He looks up when you reach the top of the stairs. His smile is low and steady, the kind that narrows his hazel eyes and softens his planner's posture into something almost conspiratorial.

Tomas: "You made it. You look like you wrestled a greenhouse out of a storm and won."

You set your Moleskine on the table and feel the weight of the invitation under your thumb—its seal like a compass you can follow. You’ve brought seedlings, a stack of handwritten notes, and the tidy stubbornness that has kept this neighborhood upright through two seasons of bad weather.

You: "It felt like better odds than sitting in a rain-slick office. These people out here know the shoreline better than any render on glass."

Tomas: (grinning) "And they tend better. Which is why I think we start with them—the living shoreline as the spine of a phased plan. It’s defensible politically if we can show incremental wins."

You don't say "I told you so." You instead push a pot of spartina toward the center of the map, fingers smelling faintly of peat and salt.

Narration: Your first move is to anchor the argument in something visible, something that won't be abstract in a council chamber full of suits and projection glare. You want roots to be the thing people can point to when they talk about "resilience" after press conferences have faded.

[Visual: Close-up on the map: charcoal tide lines, blocky renderings of proposed living berms, a sticky note that says "community access preserved"]
[Sound: A kite's distant crackle; the occasional clink of a crate being repositioned]

Tomas taps at his tablet, projecting a simplified overlay onto a small portable screen. The layers slide—flood lines, property parcels, community assets—each click a quiet demonstration of how policy and place can talk to each other.

Tomas: "If we frame the proposal as a hybrid—adaptive berms plus incentives for set-back development—we can get Mayor Chi the votes he needs and keep most of the waterfront in community use."

You: "Votes or trade-offs, Tomas. There's a difference between a hybrid and a patchwork that lets Elys rebrand a privatized spit of land as 'public benefit.'"

Tomas pauses. He’s careful; he picks his words like someone threading a needle through thin fabric. You watch him because he's practiced at this middle ground—finding seams that hold.

Tomas: "I know what Elys will say. She'll sell quick jobs and modern infrastructure. But if we translate ecological metrics into something the council reports can show—reduced stormwater load, cost savings over ten years—it's harder to wave away as 'idealism.'"

You: (softly) "It still feels like asking the sea to sign a contract."

He looks up at you. There’s admiration and a touch of worried calculation. The two of you have argued in softer ways before; tonight there's an urgency with sharper teeth.

Tomas: "We don't ask it to sign anything. We show how, when people help the shoreline heal, the sea is less likely to take back what keeps people fed and housed. We make the case with numbers and faces."

Narration: You hear the outline of the strategy—the human stories strung to the modeling. It should work; it has to. But in the back of your throat is the memory of a house gone in an hour, the way water will take everything that doesn't have time to root. Compromise cannot mean surrender.

Rosa arrives with her apron dusted in potting mix, a basket of seedlings on her hip. The greenhouse warmth has followed her up the stairs: humid breath, citrus, and the soft sound of her keys clinking.

Rosa: "Mara, Tomas—good evening. I brought more spartina and the elderberry cuttings you asked for. Niko said he'd be here with the soil amendments."

You: (relieved) "Perfect. Rosa, your folksong about the marsh—could we adapt that for Lena's piece? People remember melody more than metrics."

Rosa: (chuckles) "Oh, child. They remember what keeps them fed. Songs help them remember to care."

Niko swings in with a crate balanced on one hip, cheeks flushed with sunset. He's always moving, always halfway between being awake and animated.

Niko: "Logistics check: we can stage volunteers at the promenade next weekend if we print the handouts tonight. Also—I've got a guy who can fix the banners if we need something that looks a little less... handmade at the briefing."

Tomas: "We don't want to hide the grassroots look. We want it to be the selling point. Authenticity matters. But high-quality photos, yes. Lena, can you run a portrait session?"

Lena, leaning against a trellis, had been photographing the scene: the way evening scraped gold across wet leaves. Her blue bob cuts a crisp silhouette against the sky. She taps her recorder, brows arched.

Lena: "I'll run a piece that opens with a family planting at dawn, then pull in quotes—Rosa, you give the oral history line, Niko you've got the volunteer stats, Mara—you tell them about the house you lost and what a living shoreline would have meant. But I'm going to frame the headline around a practical victory line: 'Local Design Cuts Flood Damage by X%.'"

You: (a hard breath that tastes like resolve) "Yes. Make it sharp. People need to feel they can see the difference in the next storm."

Narration: Around the table a plan starts to knit itself into a three-act pitch: human anchor, technical backbone, immediate wins. Each voice layers onto the others. The rooftop's air feels full—warm, charged, like the calm before a tide change that's decided to be generous.

Tomas slides a pen from behind his ear and sketches a cross-section of the proposed berm: root mass at the base, a meandering boardwalk on the landward side, native plants labeled with Rosa's neat script.

Tomas: "Politically, we can present this as a pilot with an explicit evaluation timeline—eighteen months. If the metrics show reduced erosion and lower municipal costs, Mayor Chi can point to measurable gains and keep Elys from steamrolling the waterfront to private development."

You: "And if the metrics don't move quickly enough? Elys will call it an 'unevaluated experiment' and leverage the uncertainty."

Tomas: "Then we make the evaluation participatory. We set milestones that the community can measure—school classes measuring seedling survival, volunteers recording tide debris. It becomes both data and an empowerment story."

You feel his hand brush the edge of a seed packet as he reaches for another sketch line. The movement is incidental and human, the sort of small contact that contains warmth and a thousand unsaid supports. You catch his eye and don't look away for a beat too long.

Narration: This is the place where your political alignment and your private life braid: he is the planner who believes policy can be humane; you are the organizer who demands accountability. When the two of you pull in the same direction, the force feels like momentum.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Push for community-led evaluation milestones to be written into the pilot") -> Reaction: "You insist the agreement include volunteer-led checkpoints—measurements and reports that place stewardship back into neighbors' hands. Tomas nods, adding a clause to the sketch with a quiet, satisfied smile." || Choice B ("Trust Tomas to frame metrics in a way that appeals to the council") -> Reaction: "You step back and let Tomas elaborate on cost-benefit analysis. He slides figures across the tablet and you feel the outline of a compromise, clean and strategic."]

Rosa lays a hand over the charcoal drawing and speaks in the low, husky tone that tends to still the younger people.

Rosa: "When my mother taught me to replant the marsh grasses, she said the land remembers how to breathe. Our job is to remind it. If you want the council to see its value, make them see the breath."

Niko: "And show them the jobs—the youth team that wants to learn piloting drones for shoreline surveying, the local supplier who can produce biodegradable mesh. It's not just planting; it's an economy."

Lena: "I can run profiles on those jobs. People like something they can imagine. They don't vote for a concept; they vote for a paycheck, a kid's summer job, a name they recognize."

Tomas: (softly) "We can thread that through the financial model. If we estimate short-term employment numbers and longer-term savings, it becomes a narrative that's hard to argue with."

You fold your hands over your Moleskine, feeling the small dent the invitation has made in the cover. It's an invitation to sit at a table you've long kept at the edge of, balancing between being inside and outside the formal processes that shape your home. It is also an invitation to trust—trust that the municipal apparatus can be bent into service rather than bought outright.

You: "We make it a story people can point to at their kitchen tables. Tell the council that this is their proof of concept. But we also write the protections—community land trusts, conservation easements—into the pilot so 'pilot' isn't a synonym for 'temporary.'"

Tomas: "Agreed. I'll draft protections language. It won't be airtight without legal help, but it's a start. We can get a pro bono attorney on board; Lena, do you know anyone who covers legal clinics?"

Lena: "I do. I'll reach out tomorrow; there’s a public-interest attorney who did floodplains work in '31."

Narration: The rooftop hums into a late-golden glow. People bring their hands to work and slow into voices that are a little softer, a little more thoughtful. The plan starts to feel tactile—the weight of seed packets, the rust of a staple gun clutching brochures together, the warmth of hot tea poured from a thermos.

Tomas: (hesitating) "Mara—about how we move with Mayor Chi at the briefing tomorrow. I can lead with the numbers and the pilot. Do you want to open with the human story? Or—"

You cut him off with a small, deliberate laugh. You are not telling him he's wrong so much as pulling attention to rhythm. In the city, the cadence of the pitch matters: what opens, what closes, who takes a breath in the middle.

You: "You open with the pilot and the protections clause. I open with the story. We both close with the community milestones. If we can practice a handoff, it will look seamless."

Tomas: "Seamless or choreographed."

You: "Both. This is choreography with teeth."

There is a pause where everyone feels the scope of what you're shaping—more than a plan, it's a coalition. It is also, quietly, the two of you practicing a public intimacy: the visible cooperation that will be read in press photos, in council minutes, in the slow code of how a relationship translates into professional partnership.

Niko: "Also—small thing. Bring the kids. Have them line the promenade with plant markers. Squeaky shoes sell stories."

Lena: (laughing) "Squeaky shoes. Got it."

There is a moment where Tomas reaches to tap a line on the map and his hand brushes yours as you both reach for the same charcoal stick. Neither of you flinches. It is a trivial contact; in the context of a day that has thrown storms and invitations at you both, it feels like a seal.

Tomas: (quiet) "When we get this in front of the council—if it goes well—I'd like us to push for a regional pilot next season. Not just Solán Bay. If this works here, it could be a model."

You: (feeling the shape of something larger) "Then we show how a community-led pilot can be translated across different shorelines. We need an exportable toolkit, not a one-off. That means templates, training, and a story that other neighborhoods can adopt."

Narration: Your plans unfurl outward—local success as a contagious idea. The rooftop light slants warmer. You feel the rise of something steady and collective: people aligned by work and story, rather than by the polished promises Elys will parade. The excitement sits beside the seriousness of what is at stake; hope is not naive tonight, it is practiced.

Rosa walks to the edge of the rooftop and looks toward the water, hand on the railing. The wind plays with the hem of her apron.

Rosa: "There is a lighthouse keeper's son who remembers the old breakwater. He can tell you where the current pulls worst. Stories like that—they are the bones of your plan. The council will listen to a bone."

You: "Bring him to the promenade next weekend," you say. "Have him tell his story where people can hear it—between the dunes and the cranes."

Rosa smiles, eyes misting—not from sadness but from the sense of work being done. It lights a warmth that reaches you, as if the rooftop itself is breathing easier.

Narration: There is a rhythm to this preparation that steadies you. You are not alone; you have allies who know the language of plants and people and press. Each of them gives you a small thing to carry tomorrow.

Tomas folds the map back into a neat roll and taps a place on it—a patch of waterfront you both know, marked by a battered bench where old couples used to feed gulls. He meets your gaze there.

Tomas: "Whatever happens tomorrow—I want you to know I trust you in the room."

You feel the gratitude like heat on a chilly night. It is a small, human recognition that carries weight. The rooftop seems to hush a little, as if conceding space for something intimate under the municipal light you're both about to step into.

You: "I trust you too. Just—don't let them make 'pilot' an excuse to delay protections."

Tomas: (half-smile) "I won't. And I'll make sure the protections language is visible in the first fifteen slides."

Narration: The two of you pack the map, the seedlings, and the small artifacts of planning—sticky notes, a thermos with the dregs of tea, a few seed packets. People drift down the stairs with crates and laughter; the town is a careful machine of hope moving in small, steady increments.

You pause at the railing, looking down at the promenade where a van with campaign stickers idles like a detail in a larger painting. The cranes in the distance are ink-black against the orange; the sea beyond them shifts from glass to slate.

Inside your pocket, the invitation feels like a coin you can spend. It is both a formal opening and a reminder of what stands to be lost. The rooftop, with its soft lights and living beds, offers a model tonight—a tethering of love, labor, technique, and stubbornness.

Tomas slides a hand into his satchel and pulls out a small printout: a simplified slide that could be a closing image for your joint presentation—the community milestones, the protections clause, and a single line at the bottom: "Living Shoreline Pilot: measurable, accountable, community-led."

Tomas: (softly) "Ready?"

You consider the rooftop—Rosa's hands, Niko's energy, Lena's recorder, Tomas's patient pragmatism—and what you will carry into a fluorescent room full of maps and opinions tomorrow.

Narration: The plan is nearly whole. The team is aligned. The final motion is to take this fragile, living thing into the sterile institutional light and ask it to survive. This is the moment that lets a chapter like yours turn: not a finality, but a hinge.

You inhale, tasting peat and salt and citrus, and you let the warmth of the rooftop—of friends and purpose—fill your lungs. It gives you steadiness.

[Visual: The sun slips behind the cranes; strings of bulbs flicker on, bathing the rooftop in a cozy glow]
[Music: Guitar resolves into a single ascending note that lingers]

Narration: You can feel the page-turn in your bones—the small, brave conviction that a community can make policy when it is given a seat at the table. The municipal meeting tomorrow will test that conviction. It will not be the end of the fight, only another step where careful breathing matters.

You strap your Moleskine into your bag. You and Tomas make your way to the stairwell together, shoulders almost touching in the narrow light. There is a closeness that has become another kind of tool: not a distraction, but reinforcement. You are entering this fight not alone, but with a partnership that can hold both public strategy and private care.

You step down into the stairwell, the rooftop noise falling away behind you like a soft curtain. Your heart is steady; hope is worked, practiced, and shared.

[Page-Turn Moment: You and Tomas exchange one last look at the harbor and the mapped pilot; the stairwell narrows into a fluorescent corridor that will lead you to the Municipal Planning Office. The image of the community milestones slides into your mind—children with plant markers, Rosa handing a cutting to a councilmember, Niko shaking hands with a volunteer who just signed up for the first time. The thought hangs there, bright and fragile, and you realize the next morning will ask you to let the rooftop's warmth enter a room that has been cold to your kind of work. You tighten the strap on your bag, and the invitation's seal presses against your palm like an old promise.]
