[Scene: Municipal Planning Office | Morning]
[Visual: Glass-paneled meeting room. Large tide-model renders pinned under glass; a long table surrounded by chairs. Rain streaks the windows. Tomas Herrera stands beside you, tablet in hand.]
[Music: Gentle ascending strings, hopeful]
[Sound: Low hum of HVAC, faint city rain, paper rustle]

You take a breath that tastes of salt and leftover rooftop soil. The Moleskine—the sealed invitation you tucked away—rests against your ribs under your jacket like a quiet instrument. Coming from the rooftop, the warmth of strings and laughter still sits under your skin. It steadies you now in this cold, fluorescent room.

You set your boots softly on the concrete floor and feel the familiar friction between your urgency and the municipal rhythm. This room runs on schedules and minutes; your life runs on tides and seedlings. Today they cross.

Mayor Anton: "Good morning. Thank you all for coming on short notice. We have limited time before the council session. Let's hear the proposals."

Tomas Herrera offers you a small, encouraging smile—one of those looks that says, We've practiced. We will translate it. You place the Moleskine on the table, open to the folded tide map; someone from the city aides glances, then nods to indicate a projector. The renderings on the wall blink to life: one glossy, vertical, Elys' redevelopment plan; another, your living-shoreline diagrams, soft-green and annotated with community notes.

[Visual: Split-screen of Elys' glossy render vs. Mara's community sketch; damp light pools on wooden table]
[Sound: Projector whir; soft exhale from the room]

You feel Tomas Herrera's presence at your shoulder—practical, patient—and you start to speak.

Mara Voss: "Mayor, members of the planning committee—thank you for making space. We're not proposing a symbolic garden. This is a phased living-shoreline pilot that reduces storm surge impact, restores native marsh, and creates resilient public access—all built with community labor and local stewardship."

(Your voice is steady; your fingers trace a line on the tide map. You can see the models in the renderings respond: lower wave energy here, restored eelgrass banks here. The room leans forward.)

Tomas Herrera: "To add—phased design means we can start with temporary breakwaters and marsh plugs that cost a fraction of a seawall, measure performance over the next two seasons, and scale with assessed benefits."

Mayor Anton: "Two seasons. Cost estimate?"

Tomas Herrera: "A smaller up-front capital request than full redevelopment—targeted grants and in-kind volunteer labor to reduce paid-contractor scope. We also build local workforce capacity."

Elys Reed [smooth, cool]: "Mr. Mayor, if I may. My plan addresses the same problems at a different scale. We propose a waterfront redevelopment that elevates parcels, installs engineered flood defenses, and attracts private investment to fund broad infrastructure. It's faster, comprehensive, and creates jobs."

(Her slide shifts to animated financial tables. The room's air tightens; the pitch is polished—employment numbers, projected tax revenue, slick before-and-after visuals. The argument is persuasive in the way a veneer can be.)

You feel the push between two logics: the granular, living knowledge of roots and sediment, and the clean arithmetic of ledgers and renderings. You lean into the knowledge you carry—Rosa's greenhouse, the kids with their plant markers, the smell of peat and citrus—and decide how to stitch it into the sterile language of the room.

You chose the image—because stories open doors where statistics hold them shut. You tell them about Rosa: how she taught a class of kids to plant spartina after the last surge, and how the plants kept a stretch of promenade from washing away long enough for neighbors to secure their belongings. You gesture at the map; the anecdote becomes a hinge to the technical slide you have rehearsed.

Rosa Marin [soft, firm]: "We are not against investment. We are against losing our hands in the place. These plants learn where the water can be held. They teach you where to walk again."

Her voice is a warm soil—every word weighted with lived seasons. She looks at the mayor, then at Elys, and you register something in Elys' face: a brief, complex unreadability. It could be consideration; it could be recalculation. The Schrödinger weight of politics sits in that expression.

Elys Reed: "Respectfully, Ms. Marin, ecological measures are valuable—but they can be integrated into a larger redevelopment plan that accelerates protective infrastructure. We can guarantee outcomes faster."

Mara Voss: "Guaranteed outcomes often mean winners and losers are decided before the measuring tape comes out. Our pilot measures ecological performance alongside community use. If we can show measurable surge reduction with lower capital, we can convince council and investors to scale what works."

Tomas Herrera: "And by phasing, we reduce fiscal shock. We can bring in municipal logistics—waste-systems, permitting assistance—to speed installation without ceding design control."

[Lena Park watches intently; her fingers hover over a recorder and a small notebook. Her expression is an arrow aimed at the truth of what's said.]

Lena Park: "Quick question—how do we guard against co-optation? When private partners show up, how do community safeguards remain enforceable?"

Tomas Herrera: "Legal covenants in the pilot agreements. Public-access easements. Staged funding triggers."

Mara Voss: "Community stewardship clauses—maintenance plans run by local cooperatives, training programs funded by matched grants. These are not vague promises; they're administrative tools I've written with Niko and Rosa."

Mayor Anton: (leaning forward, contemplative) "You're all presenting compelling visions. The city's budget is strained; deadlines are political. Elys' plan moves capital quickly. Mara, I appreciate the community work—but can it scale fast enough if a storm hits this season?"

(You feel the room tilt—political urgency versus ecological patience. You search for a way to make patience actionable.)

Mara Voss: "We can't build resilience solely on fear of the next storm. But we can start where experiments reduce risk immediately. The first phase of our pilot is deployable within three months—temporary breakwaters, marsh plugs, and neighborhood mobilization teams. That's not just a theoretical slow burn; it's a practical mitigation."

Elys Reed: "Three months still risks exposure. A sturdier, engineered wall—"

Mara Voss [interrupting, but measured]: "A wall pushes the problem down the line. It buys time, yes, but it also cements private fortunes on a false security that ignores habitat. Hybrid solutions exist—engineered armoring paired with living systems. But the difference is who keeps tending them."

(Your words hang. The mayor's fingers drum a slow rhythm. You can almost see the images in his head: political fallout, ballots, emergency sirens.)

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Invite Rosa to stand and offer a brief testimony"
> Choice B: "Keep the focus on policy and step through the phased timeline"
[Outcome A: "Rosa rises. Her voice is small but unwavering; the room calms, and the anecdotal proof becomes harder to dismiss."]
[Outcome B: "You slide into policy detail. The mayor nods at the timeline; eyes in the room track the milestones and deliverables. Momentum builds under administrative logic."]

You ask Rosa—no, you don't ask; you make room. She stands, soil-stained palms folded, and speaks in the way older seasons speak to younger plans. You watch faces soften as community memory becomes evidence.

Rosa Marin: "We have held this land in our hands. Our elders taught us where the reeds gather and where the water forgets its reaching. If we are part of the plan—if our hands can keep tending—then the city keeps what it needs: its people."

A council aide makes a note. Lena's pen moves faster, recording the cadence of practicality and memory that could shape headlines. Elys watches, and for a sliver of a second her tone shifts—recalculation again, a pragmatic tempering of rhetoric.

Elys Reed [cool, then unexpectedly collaborative]: "I respect Ms. Marin. If living systems can be integrated into a larger plan that ensures rapid protection citywide, I'm open to hybridizing. My concern is scale and liability."

Tomas Herrera [quick to grasp the opening]: "Hybridization is exactly the conversation I hoped we'd have. We can pilot living systems in high-priority zones while engineering elevated parcels as necessary—"

Mara Voss [holding up a hand, careful]: "But we need guardrails. If we go hybrid, community stewardship and public-access covenants must be non-negotiable. We can't allow redevelopment to swallow public land with conditional promises."

Mayor Anton: "This has been the crux—how to protect the public interest while moving quickly enough to satisfy investors and to meet immediate hazard timelines."

(There's a long pause. The rain outside eases. The strings in the background swell slightly, hopeful but restrained.)

Mayor Anton: "I need a recommendation from this group: do we—partner directly to fast-track a phased pilot with municipal support; mobilize the community through a referendum; or open full public inquiry into redevelopment narratives and oversight? Each path has tradeoffs."

(The room looks at you. Tomas Herrera’s eyes are patient but expectant. Lena's gaze is sharp, Lena a recorder of truth and a potential amplifier. Elys folds her hands, her expression—complex, unreadable—holding both the possibility of concession and the danger of political pushback. Rosa offers you a small, steadying smile.)

You feel the choice like a tide turning—momentum beneath your feet, community breathing with you. The meeting has done what you hoped: it opened the municipal door wide enough for something living to pass through. The question now is how to step through.

[Visual: Close-up on the Moleskine, mayor's pen hovering over a decisions sheet]
[Music: Strings resolve into a calm, ascending chord—hopeful, decisive]

You gather your breath. Whatever you pick will color the next seasons—how funds move, how stories are told, who gets a seat at the shovel. Tomas Herrera gives your hand a light, almost invisible squeeze at the table edge. That touch says: trust the work you know, and trust the people beside you.
