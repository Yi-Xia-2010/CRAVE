You stand at the edge of the courthouse steps, your Moleskine closed against your chest like a small, stubborn plank. The judge's chambers have been a rhythm for days—inked notices, the hollow rattle of court doors—but today the air tastes metallic and urgent. Every phone glows with models and warnings; a line of officials cluster near a glass case of architectural renderings that feel suddenly fragile.

Tomas Herrera is a few feet away, fingers tapping across his tablet. When he looks up, his hazel eyes are the same as always—careful, trying to calculate the calmest angle. He gives you a small, near-invisible nod. You answer it by leaning your hip against the stone, grounding yourself in something solid.

Mayor Anton speaks with a string of volunteers, then moves toward the group. His voice is low but steadier than you feel.

Mayor Anton: "The injunction buys time."

Your throat tightens. You think of Niko's face creased with worry, Rosa's hands still smelling of damp soil, of the children's paint-scrawled plant markers. You think of the seedlings tucked into your bag because you could not bring yourself to leave them behind.

Elys Reed arrives with a polite, clipped smile that doesn't reach the weather. She carries a slim tablet against her chest, the city crest reflected coldly on its screen.

Elys Reed: "The investors are pausing work."

Mayor Anton uses the word 'we' the way politicians use anchors—smoothing, binding. You want to ask who the 'we' really is. Elys's jaw is set; there's a sheen of calculation behind the curve of her smile that feels like stone warmed in sun—beautiful and suddenly sharp.

Lena slips beside you, recorder tucked into her vest, eyes raw and bright with sleeplessness.

Lena: "I'm streaming this."

The radio crackles. A municipal meteorologist's voice—calibrated, exhausted—reports an unexpected shift: the storm is tracking faster, the tide higher than predicted. You feel something in the room shift like a rope pulled taut.

[Visual: Waves roll in under a bruise-sky. Headlights bob and cut through spray. The promenade is a scattering of silhouettes: human forms against a machine of wind and water.]

[Music: Percussive, sparse—snare brush beating like a heart under duress]

The first wall of water hits with a violence that surprises the stomach. It is cold and loud and it eats the flat light of the promenade. The sandbag lines buckle where concrete fractures hide, and a chunk of the walkway slides into the sea like a page being torn. You smell oil and the sharp, metallic tang of bent rebar.

Someone screams a name you don't recognize. A child's laughter—fear-thin—joins the storm and then is cut off. You feel the surge push against your boots, then your shins, like a living thing trying to rewrite the map. A crane, stranded and unmoored at a distance, groans as it settles with a metal complaint. Over the roar, you hear radios calling out coordinates, names, the urgency of triage.

Tomas Herrera grabs your wrist—hard—then loosens to bring his hand alongside yours, steadying.

Tomas Herrera: "Mara Voss—get Rosa to higher ground."

You nod and run, plastered to the wind, calling Rosa's name like a spell.

You reach the greenhouse path and find it half-swamped, the door hanging on one hinge. Inside, shattered seed trays float in cold water; the smell of softened earth and old wood is strong, like grief. Rosa is there, knees muddy, dragging a battered pot toward the loft. Her face is pinched in a way you have never seen, as if the work itself has been struck down. When she sees you, she doesn't speak at first. Then, with Rosa's hands that have always known how to cradle seedlings back to life, she grips your forearm.

Rosa: "You saved what you could. We'll remember how we tried."

You tell yourself that remembering is a kind of mat that keeps you from falling through. Still, watching water take what you've held for years feels like a physical absence that will not be soothed by planning sessions.

You and a small squad walk the first pass of the beach. Bodies are where people should not be—curled, still. Volunteers cover faces with jackets in a movement that is awkward and necessary. Lena moves through the scene, recording, voice lowered but insistent: the public must see; the truth must be known. Her eyes meet yours once—raw, asking you to carry something that cannot fit in a ledger.

Mayor Anton moves down the line, shaking hands that tremble with the effort to remain institutional and human. He stops beside Elys Reed, who is on the phone and then not on the phone; the expression on Elys's face is, for a moment, unreadable—she is sheltering something private that a camera won't capture. You label that look complex in your head, because you cannot know the private calculus that sits behind a politician's polished stare. The Schrödinger of the moment keeps you from certainty.

Tomas Herrera: "You did everything you could."

You want to tell him that nothing is binary—that saving seedlings and filing injunctions mattered even as roads failed—but the words feel like straw under the weight of what's left.

[Scene: Lantern Lighthouse | Dawn]

[Sound: Low gulls, ropes snapping against masts in the distance, the soft conversational hum of people who are too exhausted to speak loudly]
[Music: Sparse piano in the lower register; a humming undercurrent that keeps time with the sea]

You climb to the lighthouse with Tomas Herrera. Silence follows you both—heavy, necessary. At the top, you look back at the bay: a torn ribbon of neighborhood, parcels of community plots sliced into new, jarring geometries. In the ruin, faces gather in small knots—Rosa crouched nearby, Lena with her recorder, Mayor Anton with lines on his forehead that look like maps.

Tomas Herrera: "We can take the injunction and press for emergency funds."

You think of courtrooms and columns of paper and the slow machinery of policy. It is necessary, perhaps, but it sits alongside a deeper, harsher truth: the law cannot reconstruct the childhood that the sea will never return.

Mara Voss: "You want to fix infrastructure, but some of this—some of this is about how we hold each other when the worst happens. It's about memory, and who gets to decide what we remember."

Tomas Herrera: "I know. I— I don't have the words to make the storm undo itself. But I can file the claims. I can try to make the city bend to the urgency."

Elys Reed: "We will rezone as necessary. We will allocate emergency funds with oversight. There will be accountability."

Mayor Anton: "We do what we can. We set priorities: immediate shelter, restoration of disrupted services, a public accounting of damage. We put the injunction to work on what remains."
