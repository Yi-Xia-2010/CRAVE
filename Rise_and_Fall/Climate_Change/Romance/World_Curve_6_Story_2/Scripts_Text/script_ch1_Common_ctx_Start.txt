# Context: Start

[Scene: Highwater Cove | Dawn]
[Sound: Distant sirens; gulls crying; a low generator hum]
[Music: Sparse piano with low strings — tense, brittle]
You wake to the vibration of a town that will not sleep. Sirens thread through the fog like needles: warning, warning. Salt and diesel sit thick in the air, a tang that settles on your tongue and drags your lungs into work before your eyes are fully open.

You fumble for the brass compass at your throat out of habit—fingers that know its dents better than the maps in your head. It is warm where it presses into your chest beneath your jacket, heavy with the memory of Nan, of promises. Rain has left pavement mirror-slick; the reflectivity turns the streetlights into a dozen small moons that wobble beneath your boots as you step outside.

[Visual: Wet street reflections; Maya's field jacket with patched elbows; the dented compass catching a smear of dawn]
You did not mean to fall asleep. There are notes taped to the inside of the door—Rafi's scrawl, a grocery list, a half-finished diagram for a pump—but the sirens made the choice for you. You draw the scarf over your mouth against the spray and step into the mirrored street. Every footstep sends up a soft squelch; the wet presses into the fabric of your boots like memory into bone.

[Scene: Highwater Cove Harbor | Dawn]
[Sound: Boards creak underfoot; distant shouts; the slap of water against pilings]
[Visual: Wooden piers slick with tide, patched boats rocking; Jonah’s silhouette on the pier]
As you wade toward the harbor—water lapping around the soles of your boots—you see the town's patchwork response already in motion. Neighbors form human chains to haul sandbags; someone bangs a drumbeat of shouted instructions. A generator coughs; its exhaust threads through the air and mixes with rotting kelp and frying salt.

Gracie "Nan" Armitage sits on a low crate near the fishmarket, needle moving through bright quilts the color of stubborn flowers. Her hands are slow and sure, stitching warmth into squares that will line the school gym turned shelter. She looks up as you approach, and for a moment her lined face is a map of all the storms you've both weathered.

Gracie "Nan" Armitage: "Maya. You're up. Sit before the wind takes you whole."

You move closer, the compass tapping once against your sternum. Nan smells of lavender soap and the sea. Her eyes—small, sharp—take you in and hold you as if weighing whether your shoulders will buckle today.

Maya Armitage: "I can't. Tanks at the Institute—"

Gracie "Nan" Armitage [cutting you off gently]: "The tanks will leak if you don't get there, and the quilts won't sew themselves while you debate. You promised the town anything you could do, child, not everything in the world."

Maya Armitage: "I know. I just—"
you swallow the rest
Maya Armitage: "I woke to sirens and—"

Gracie "Nan" Armitage: "—and the town stares at you because it wants you to fix a tide. That isn't on your back alone, love. Not today." (She taps her needle against her knee.) "Bring me one of those sensors and I'll stitch the patrol schedules into the corner. Make the shelter know who's coming and leaving."

Her pragmatism is an anchor and a chisel. You feel gratitude and a bitter, familiar guilt at the same time; old promises ache like a tendon.

[Scene: The Tidal Institute (Maya's Field Lab) | Morning]
[Visual: A repurposed fish-processing warehouse; tanks with algae at the edges; one sensor array sagging]
[Sound: The patter of leaking water; the intermittent ping of a failing sensor; Rafi's voice under the clatter of tools]
[Music: A single electronic tone intermittently warping — the sound of a failing monitoring system]
The warehouse smells of bleach and old seawater. Inside, a fluorescent strip buzzes and casts clinical light over benches choked with notebooks, test vials, and a spiderweb of sensor cables. One of the large glass tanks—home to the salt-marsh flora you have transplanted for testing—has a fine, oily sheen along the lip where pressure breached a seam. Rafi is elbow-deep in a backup pump, face streaked with grease and a smudge of algae on his cheek.

Rafi: "Maya! Look—this seal's blown. We throttled the intake but the differential's spiking. I can rig a bypass, but we need more clamps and—"

Maya Armitage [breath short]: "Read the logs. Are the microclimate spikes local to us or a regional front?"

Rafi [hesitates, rubbing his forehead]: "The bracelet's streaming raw timestamps; it's messy. The overlay looks like a frontal amplification—pressure drop, humidity surge. But the central array's—" (he gestures to the sagging sensor) "—is offline. I can't get a definitive read with that gap."

You fumble the sensor bracelet at your wrist; it vibrates—an almost panicked tremor. The graphs you can see are jagged teeth, microclimate variables spiking in a pattern you've mapped before and named with nothing but clinical shorthand and dread.

Maya Armitage: "We need triage. Prioritize drinking water, then samples for salinity shifts near the nursery beds. And Rafi—get the temporary seals on the tank. If that goes, we lose the restoration batch we've been culturing."

Rafi: "Got it. Also—" (he pulls a damp tablet toward you) "—the city's sent an advisory. Mayor Ortiz called a public meeting—today. The grants committee wants a go/no-go on a development partnership. They want you to speak."

The words fall into the room like ice. Your mouth goes dry. The town needs triage and testimony; the lab needs patches and meters; the mayor needs a vote. Everything asks so much of you at once.

Rafi: "Maya, you look like hell. You need to slow down."

Maya Armitage: "Slow down and watch the tanks fail? No. Help me clamp that seam."

Rafi: "I'll patch. You prep your notes—I'll print a condensed data sheet for the council. But don't—" (he searches your face) "—don't make this the thing that consumes you entirely. You left once for a reason. Don't punish yourself with being the only answer."

Maya Armitage: "Leaving didn't fix anything while I was gone. I came back to do the work that matters."

Rafi: "And you're doing it. But we're a team. Promise me you'll let someone else carry a piece."

You want to argue—about responsibility, about staying awake and being the person people look at in a weathered tent and decide is hope—but Rafi's earnestness grounds you. For a breath you let the three of you be a small network: hands on clamps, fingers on keys, voices mapping priorities.

You choose a route, but you cannot postpone the way both demands pull at you.

[Scene: Harbor Pier | Late Morning]
[Visual: Jonah standing by the rail; his knit cap dark in the pre-noon light; waves slapping pilings]
[Sound: The slap of waves; distant boards being hammered; muted conversations]
[Music: A low acoustic guitar — steady, melancholy]
Jonah's silhouette is still when you reach the pier, hands tucked into the pockets of his waxed canvas vest. He watches a line of younger fishers hauling a spray of nets toward a patch of the harbor where the water now smells faintly of diesel. When he turns, his eyes are the warm hazel you remember from summers spaced with routine and salt.

Jonah Reyes: "Maya." (He says your name like an anchor.) "You look like the tide took half your sleep and all your patience."

Maya Armitage: "Do you have a free hand? The Institute is leaking and Mayor Ortiz wants a meeting."

Jonah Reyes: "I've got plenty of hands—just none that can be in two places." (He watches your face.) "How bad?"

Maya Armitage: "Bad enough to make people talk about leasing the marina to an outside syndicate yesterday. Bad enough that the sensor array—" (your bracelet buzzes again) "—that the sensors are down in places that matter."

Jonah Reyes [brisk]: "We can jury-rig things at the harbor. Nets, patched bladders for floatation. But the bigger problem is politicians with good teeth and bad plans."

Maya Armitage: "They presented glossy pamphlets. Big promises. 'Resilience by investment.' They talk about rapid embankments and economic uplift. Little of it mentions who stays and who goes."

Jonah Reyes [his jaw tightens]: "They sold my uncle's crew on a plan like that ten years ago. They left with more paperwork than boats. I won't let them buy our homes with a render."

Maya Armitage: "Then we testify. We show numbers, we show plans for community-led measures. We push for oversight."

Jonah Reyes: "I will stand behind you. My people will stand behind you. But Maya—" (he hesitates; the guarded set of his mouth softens) "—don't carry the town like a sack of rocks on your back. Let us take some of the weight."

Maya Armitage: "You think I haven't tried? Leaving wasn't cowardice. It was training. But I can feel the old guilt like a bruise—like I failed once already."

Jonah Reyes: "You didn't fail. The ocean is just...unforgiving. But we can be stubborn in better ways. We can be persistent, not alone."

He steps closer until you can smell the coffee he's been carrying in his flask—bitter, warm, an earthy anchor to a man still unwilling to let the sea take everything. His voice layers practical warmth over a grief he won't name. The conversation bends around a past neither of you grant space to name aloud. There is history in the slack of his shoulders and in the careful way he avoids the word 'brother' you both know drifts just under the surface.

[Outcome A: "You close the short gap and let him track the exhaustion on your face. He doesn't say much—he wraps an arm briefly around your shoulders, the motion measuring out a kind solidarity."]
[Outcome B: "You step back and pull your tablet from your pocket, words already forming. The space between you becomes a map: emotional line items on one side, triage on the other."]

Jonah waits for you to choose, patient in that way that has always been his steadiness. The pier creaks beneath all of you, a chorus of small collapses that sounds suspiciously like the town's patience.

Jonah Reyes: "Whatever you pick, I'll cover the field. I'll get my crew to the nursery beds and pull samples. But Maya—" (he studies you) "—when you speak at the council, say the things that hurt. Don't let them dress the hurt in numbers alone. People need to hear the edges."

Maya Armitage: "The edges cut both ways. They'll call it fear-mongering."

Jonah Reyes: "And they'll call you stubborn too. Maybe both are true. But stubbornness is not always a sin."

You laugh once—short, a dry sound. It is easier to talk about stubbornness than to say the thing that sits like a stone in your throat: that you once left the town because you thought science could fix everything, and that promise might have hardened into an assumption that you could fix people as well.

[Scene: Abandoned Promenade (High Tide Line) | Noon]
[Visual: Tide-line graffiti; driftwood sculptures; overcast sky]
[Sound: Muffled town radio; distant construction; kids' laughter somewhere down the market]
[Music: Hollow wind instrument — melancholic]
Mayor Lena Ortiz's voice reaches you before you see her—ordered, municipal, composed. A message blares from a municipal van parked where the promenade starts: a tall woman in a practical coat and a neat ponytail stands beside the open door, speaking into a microphone.

Mayor Lena Ortiz [over PA]: "Residents of Highwater Cove, by order of the council, a public meeting will convene this afternoon at two o'clock in the City Council Chamber. We will hear proposals and decide on immediate funding priorities. Your participation is encouraged."

The crowd mutters. Some cheer the semblance of order; others spit curses at the timing. You feel the leash of time tighten: two hours to patch, to marshal data, to persuade a room whose chairs are always eager to look toward the "efficient" solution.

Maya Armitage [to Jonah]: "Two o'clock. Council chamber. I have ten pages of notes and a voice that trembles when I try to make it personal."

Jonah Reyes: "Make it personal. Make them name each home that will be lost if they don't fund proper buffers. Make them give a face to every number."

Rafi [arriving, breathless, holding printed sheets]: "I condensed the data. Two pages: salinity trends, habitat decline, cost of inaction. I stuffed it between the quilts—Nan will carry it in. We can get you a projector if you need visuals."

Nan approaches, needle tucked away for the moment, a bundle of quilts at her hip and a band of safety pins looped through a sack. Her face is softer in public—older, willing to hold the town's edges.

Gracie "Nan" Armitage: "You'll speak as my granddaughter or you'll speak as the scientist? Doesn't much matter to an old woman—say what will keep the kids in the cove."

Maya Armitage: "I will try to say both. I will try to be honest. I will—"

The bracelet at your wrist buzzes again. You glance down; another spike. You feel the pattern in your bones: pressure, water, people leaning on decisions like a house leans on a single strong post. You know the council room will not be a place of simple answers. You know Evelyn Hart-style proposals—slick, centralized, with a promise that sounds like safety—are already wrapped in binders, waiting.

Mayor Lena Ortiz [steps closer to you, voice low]: "Maya Armitage—I've read your reports. We need data in the meeting, yes, but we also need a plan you can outline quickly. Investors are anxious. I need you to be concise."

Maya Armitage: "I will be concise. But I won't be concise on the human cost."

Mayor Lena Ortiz: "Then make it count. Two o'clock. Council Chamber. Be ready."

Her courtesy carries the thrum of pressure. It is not hostility; it is an administrative narrowing. You nod because words are currency and you must spend them carefully.

Maya Armitage [alone, to yourself]: "I left once to learn how to fight the sea with knowledge. Came back with a bag of tools and a hollow promise to carry everyone. I cannot save everyone alone. But I will not let the town be sold on the idea that a ribbon-cutting solves the tides."

You brace against the wind, feeling the weight of the compass at your throat. The harbor smells of fish and diesel; the town smells of urgency and old fear. Your storm-gray eyes sting with weariness. There is a tightness just behind your ribs—guilt honed into focus.

You pull your scarf up against the spray. You need to organize triage, seal tanks, set up visuals, and write testimony that pierces the council's sterile air. You need to keep Nan's quilts warm, Jonah's crew mobilized, Rafi's data printed, and the town honest about what the shoreline demands.

[Music: The strings swell into a single unresolved chord — a page-turn in sound]
A meeting in the Council Chamber looms like a crest: it could break and drown you, or it could roll and throw you onto a safer shore. You have to choose how to stand when it hits.

You breathe in. The salt and diesel mix tastes like the past and the future at once. The bracelet buzzes one last time—a reminder, a metronome—and you feel the old, commanding ache of responsibility: to act, to speak, to hold together what you can.

You leave the pier with Rafi and Jonah at your back, Nan's quilts over one arm, the compass biting into your jacket. The town moves around you, speaking in small triages and larger fears, a choreography you have led and will lead again.

You approach the Council Chamber as the clock crawls toward two. Doors that have always smelled of coffee and old varnish wait like a threshold. People stream there with signs, with charts, with grief folded under their coats.

You haven't decided how to balance saving tanks and saving testimony, how much of the truth to dress in numbers, how much to leave raw in the council's sterile light. The sirens are quieter now—replaced by a hum of a different urgency: the political kind. You can feel the town's eyes settle on you like rain.

[Music: A single held note, unresolved]
There is a meeting. There are speeches to rehearse, clamps to tighten, quilts to distribute, fishers to mobilize. The choice of what to prioritize will wait in your chest like a stone—but the present demands steps.

You glance at your bracelet once more. The numbers are spiking. The harbor will keep changing even as the council decides whether to buy a plan or buy time.

[Sound: Murmurs; the creak of the chamber doors]
You step forward, and the town steps with you. The decision is not yours alone—but it will need your voice. The room waits.
