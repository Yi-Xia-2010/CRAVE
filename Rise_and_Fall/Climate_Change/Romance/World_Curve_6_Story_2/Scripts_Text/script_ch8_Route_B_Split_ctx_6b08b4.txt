Rafi’s grin is rueful. Rafi: "I know, I know. But the underwriters are asking for 'measurable attenuation within twelve months.' Contractors say modular concrete gets that—less upfront uncertainty."

Evelyn Hart steps forward, voice like polished glass. Evelyn Hart: "We have a responsibility to protect people now," she says. "Jobs are on the line. If we can guarantee coverage, we prevent displacement in the immediate term. The city can backstop this pilot with promise of retrofitting later."

Her eyes find yours—ice-blue, steady. There is no malice; only the precise calculation of someone balancing futures against deadlines. You feel the town’s expectation in the air, like pressure in the lungs.

Marco: "Concrete modules reduce installation time by half, Ms. Armitage. Less risk on schedule. Less cost now."

Sullivan: "We model risk reduction. We need metrics we can verify quickly. Living systems are great, but their returns are distributed—and slower. Investors won't wait forever."

Your stomach drops—not because the clause is unexpected but because no one flagged it for discussion. The words have a cold geometry in your mind: emergency, override, expedited. You picture what 'expedited' looks like when money and timelines tilt the scales.

Evelyn Hart’s jaw tightens for a beat; her outward face does not change. Evelyn Hart: "That clause is standard," she says. "Emergencies require decisive authority. We negotiated the oversight board to prevent abuse."

Rafi's voice is sharp now. Rafi: "Standard for whom? That wording gives the city broad discretion. It could allow administrators to replace living breakwaters with concrete modules under 'emergency'—without community veto."

A murmur goes through the gathered crew. Jonah Reyes pushes himself up and wipes his hands on a rag. He looks at you—briefly—and then to the radio clipped to his vest. Static scratches, and then a voice: "—Net tear, sector three. Jonah, you still there?"

Jonah Reyes: "I'm here. Give me coordinates—I'll head out after I secure this keel."

You watch him sling a canvas bag over his shoulder. The tension in his face is complex—protective, pulled tight by memory and responsibility. He hasn't looked at you like that for long; there’s something unspoken sitting between your hands.

Rafi steps closer. Rafi: "We have to bring this to the oversight board's vote today. If that clause stays—"

Before he can finish, Marco the contractor intercepts. Marco: "We can do a hybrid," Marco says, palms open. "Concrete cores with living elements attached. Same install time, reduced risk. We meet metrics, you keep some ecological function."

You sense the room shifting; leaders and laborers, hope and fear, practical ingenuity and compromise. Your chest tightens—not in despair, but in the focused ache of responsibility.

Rafi stands, the tablet in his hands like a relic. Rafi: "Clause forty-two: 'In the event of an emergency declaration by city administrators, expedited measures may be enacted without full community oversight if such measures are reasonably necessary to preserve life or critical infrastructure.' Mayor Lena folds her hands and looks at you. The room leans in with expectation. You feel the weight of people who look at you not because you control policy, but because you have been the town's voice for data and heart.

You open your mouth, and your mind sorts through options like fisherman sorting nets for snags: enforce fidelity and risk money; accept substitution and buy time; expose clause and force renegotiation, possibly losing the funding thread. Each route will tangle with people’s lives.

Jonah Reyes returns at a pause in the meeting, salt-dark on his sleeves, a small smile ghosting his mouth as he counts the nets—he's fixed them, but the radio is still clamped to his belt. He doesn't bring a solution to the legal tangle. He offers steadiness, the kind that is tactile and warm.

Evelyn Hart moves to you, leaning a degree too close for mere conversation. Evelyn Hart: "Maya, I know you care about ecological integrity," she says. "But I also know you care about people. You built this bridge to the table. Use it. If we can show jobs and reduced displacement, we can buy more time for living systems."

You feel pride—someone recognizing your work—and the tug at the center of your pragmatism. You remember Nan's words again: when to let the harbor teach you to dance.
