### DRAFT
# Route: Route B (Ending)


[Scene: Harborfront Lane | Early Evening]
[Visual: Strings of bare bulbs crisscross the lane; wet cobbles reflect neon from a distant Aquila rig]
[Sound: Murmured conversations swell into chants; a distant engine thumps like a restless heart]
[Music: Low, insistent cello — a falling motif]

You return to the harbor with rain drying on your jacket and the taste of salt in the back of your throat. The boardwalk smells of smoked fish and damp rope; the lights make everyone look a little more courageous and a little more tired. Ivy stands on a milk crate, arms wide, turning a cluster of volunteers into an orchestra of purpose — clipboards, hand-painted signs, a knot of people you recognize and some faces you do not.

Ivy: "Okay—rotate teams, block the east entrance at first light, and don't let them take the pilings without us seeing it. We keep this humane and visible. We keep it nonviolent."

You step closer. The crowd’s attention tilts toward you because you lead with a list of reasons and a way to keep them rooted in facts. You feel the weight of those eyes like a tide pulling at the edges of everything steady inside you.

Ivy: (softening) "Maya, you okay? You look like you carry the weather inside you."

You: "I am fine," you say, and the truth is knotted. "We do this by the hour. We log who comes and goes. Eli—what's the rota?"

Eli: (from the side, voice practical) "Three fishermen per shift on the docks at dawn and dusk. We’ll rotate so no one misses the daywork entirely. I have a list—" (he taps a water-streaked notebook) "—and Rosa's got the kids to hand out flyers at market."

You: "Good. Keep it tight. If Aquila tries to claim emergency access, we need names, timestamps, witnesses."

Eli's laugh is a thin thing. Eli: "Always the scientist. Don't scare everyone off with spreadsheets."

You both grin, but it doesn't clear the sky.

Ivy steps down and meets your eyes; there's friction between her urgency and your desire to be measured.

Ivy: "We can hold this as long as people show. We've got momentum—people want to protect the marsh with their bodies if they have to."

You: "Momentum is fragile. We need legal backup. We need to keep families from burning out."

Ivy: "We have community law volunteers, bring what paperwork you can, but this is us—this is the town saying no."

Conversation forks, then returns. Eli folds his arms like he's weighing the wind. Overhead, the bulbs hum and a pack of gulls cuts through the light, their calls sharp as punctuation.

Eli: "Maya, can you speak tonight at the school? When Rosa hands out the kids' drawings, hearing from you will steady people."

You feel the old knot in your chest. Speaking means naming what might be lost. It means saying your misforecast aloud, letting it hang in the room. It means trusting the town with your doubt.

You: "Yes. I'll go. But Rosa—make sure the kids understand why they're drawing the marsh. We need pictures for the petition, not fear."

Rosa: (from the back, voice bright despite everything) "The kids already know, Maya. They asked if the marsh would remember them. They asked if the oysters would be okay."

You: "Then let them speak, too."

[Scene: School Auditorium | Night]
[Visual: Gym lights halo a packed audience; children’s drawings taped in a procession across the stage]
[Sound: Rustle of paper, a child's insistent whispering, an undercurrent of coughs and shifting bodies]
[Music: Sparse piano; a single note hanging like a held breath]

The auditorium smells of dry gym floor and crayons. The stage is manifold light and the drawings—docks and grasses and small, smiling fish—soften something inside you. Rosa moves through the aisles handing out the drawings like offerings. The room is full: fishermen in weathered jackets, elderly neighbors with municipal concern folded into their hands, a scattering of journalists, and the Mayor sitting with his hands clasped, sympathetic eyes scanning the crowd.

You climb the stage and the light catches your pendant. For a second you are only the outline of a person and the history that brought you here.

You: "We are not protesters first. We are a community asking to be heard. We have proposals—restoration, managed retreat in parts, oyster re-siting. We have data, and we have a history that tells us what the marsh needs."

A man in the front row raises his voice, skeptical: "How can that stop heavy machinery? How can words keep the rigs away?"

You: "They won't, not on their own. That's why we stand where they need to pass. We make it costly — socially, reputationally. We gather every permit they try to push through and point out what it erases. We build a public record."

The crowd murmurs, sometimes agreement, sometimes anxious. The Mayor clears his throat and comes forward, measured.

Mayor Jonah: "Maya, I am trying to hold council between immediate economic relief and long-term safety. I can't promise to block a developer outright—the town needs jobs—but I will listen to any hybrid plan that offers protection without erasing neighborhoods."

You: "Sympathy matters, Mayor, but timing matters more. If Aquila files for emergency access, permits can slide through under pressure."

Mayor Jonah: "I'm asking you for proposals that we can implement in sixty days. Legal realities bind my hands."

The exchange threads tension. Someone in the back shouts, "What about injunctions?" and your phone buzzes—brief, insistent. You check it: an alert that Aquila has filed for an emergency access motion and is seeking expedited hearings. Your stomach drops.

You: "They've filed for emergency access. That accelerates timelines and reduces public comment windows."

Ivy: (a quick grit to her voice) "So we force the timeline back on them. Public visibility, petitions, blockades. All of it."

Rosa hands you a drawing—a child's tidal marsh rendered in violet crayon—and you tuck it into your notebook like a talisman. Your old misforecast is a low hum beneath your ribs; speaking feels like stepping on that wire. You imagine every word you say twisted into justification for someone else's bulldozer.

The night unspools into lists of actions: petition stations, rota sign-ups, a call for legal volunteers. A few well-meaning lawyers offer patchy counsel — a mix of local knowledge and underfunded public defenders. The Mayor's caution sits between applause and a thin, hopeful choir of "Please don't let it come to arrests."

[Scene: Old Pilings | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: Pilings jut like broken teeth from the shallow water; dawn low and thin, sky a bruise]
[Sound: Shouts, shoes on damp timber, a nervous crackle over a police radio]
[Music: Drum roll of distant thunder; percussion building]

You arrive before the cranes. The air is sharp enough to cut; salt on your lips, the wet wood slick beneath your boots. People have come as planned—wives with thermoses, teenagers with scarves, a line of fishermen with hands like weathered planks. Eli stands at the front, jaw set, and when your eyes meet there's a look that means more than a thousand words: steady, unblinking, fearful.

Eli: "We hold the line. We make this costly for them to just roll through."

You: "We keep it nonviolent. If anyone gets arrested, we document every moment. Witnesses, timestamps, phone recordings. No one acts alone."

Ivy: (breathless with adrenaline) "We had to escalate to be seen. This is what being seen looks like."

The police arrive in a neat, institutional tide—boots, badges, the brittle efficiency of uniforms. A younger officer steps forward, voice amplified by a cautionary calm.

Police Officer: "You are ordered to disperse. This is a private construction site and you are blocking lawful access."

A murmur runs through the crowd. Someone laughs, high and brittle. An older man shouts, "Our town is private now?"

Eli folds his arms and leans into the officer, his hazel eyes narrowed. Eli: "This is public shoreline. Our livelihoods depend on it. Where do you stand, officer? With machines or with families?"

The officer's expression is practiced; he repeats the order. People begin to form human chains across the planks—hands linked, voices low and deep. Someone begins to chant, and the chant becomes a rope pulling the whole place taut.

Your chest throbs with the old forecast—what happens if you are wrong again? If your predictions fail and your resistance costs somebody more than bad paperwork? The memory of the one time the tide took part of the neighborhood presses like a bruise.

Then there is the first shove. Not brutal, not cinematic, but heavy in its implication—a hand on a shoulder trying to slide an arm loose. Someone from the back pushes forward, an officer grips an arm, and the crowd screams. For a heartbeat the world is only motion and sound: a child's cry, someone cussing, the slap of a palm against wet wood.

Eli: (shouts) "Stand down! No pushing—document—watch the badge numbers!"

Ivy moves like a contained storm, grabbing the arm of a man about to throw a fist. Ivy: "We don't need to give them an excuse. Hold the line."

But excuses are thin things. An officer reaches for someone's wrist; a neighbor goes to intervene and is pulled to the ground. The first set of handcuffs closes on rough wrists. The sound of metal is obscene in the quiet.

You step forward because your body remembers action better than your mind remembers caution. You put yourself between the officer and the person being cuffed. The crowd parts like water around a rock, faces unreadable—some with fury, some with fear, some already looking for safer ground.

Your throat is dry. The guilt you carry about one misread forecast is a low, insistent drum in your ears; every breath feels like a verdict.

Eli: (voice tight) "Maya—"

You: (whisper, more to yourself than to him) "I can't let them do this alone."

Ivy grabs your sleeve, eyes bright and dangerous. Ivy: "We asked the town to stand. Now the town is standing—and paying."

Someone shouts that Aquila is filing for emergency access, that attorneys will be in by midday with injunctions, that the mayor is being grilled on the phone. The legality of the moment collides with the physicality of it—boots, wood, wrists. Media lights flare distantly like predatory stars.

Your notebook is heavy in your pocket. You think of the children's drawings, of Rosa, of the people cuffed now being led to a police van. The price of making a stand is unfolding in real time; it is not heroic in the way you rehearsed. It is small and sharp and human.

You realize, with a cold clarity, that the town you knew is splitting into factions—those who will keep fighting in the streets, those who will step back to protect what they can, and those who will take Aquila's money and disappear from the docks. The energy that once braided everyone together is fraying at the edges.

Eli reaches out and your hands find his for a moment—tense and private in the public crush.

Eli: (soft, a fissure in his voice) "Whatever happens, don't—don't lose yourself in the mess."

You: (the old guilt a tide under your words) "I won't. I promise."

But promises feel thin as paper when metal snaps and a door slams. The crowd's chant turns into a press of voices, some angry, some grieving. Someone begins to sob openly. The dawn is a pale promise of light that feels far away.

You inhale the salt air and feel your heart hammering like a call bell. Choices that once seemed theoretical have become bodily, immediate. You know there are more costs ahead—arrests, legal battles, fractured relationships—and that the simple act of standing may not be enough to hold everything together.

A media van pulls up, its camera swiveling like an eye hungry for the moment to define this story for everyone else. The officer barks orders again. A rope of voices tightens around the pilings.

You see, across the water, the silhouette of heavy machinery awaiting permission to begin. The grey bulk of it looks like an inevitable winter wave.

Page-turn thought: You can feel the fall accelerating, a slow but inexorable pull; everything you have built to protect the marsh now moves through the narrow opening between principle and consequence, and the next hour will decide who pays—and how much.
