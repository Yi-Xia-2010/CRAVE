## Visual Novel Title
Tide Lines

---

## World View
I grew up with the tide as a calendar: the marsh hummed with oystercatchers, the harbor smelled of smoke and salt, and people marked time by the slack of the water. By 2035 the sea had learned new habits. Lowtide Marsh is a spongy, living boundary between town and ocean—spartina reeds, broken pilings and old oyster racks holding fast to history. Harborfront Lane clings to the water with patched docks and storefronts lit by warm lamps; the Coastal Research Annex sits on pilings with its cool LEDs and tape-measured samples; the crumbling Old Lighthouse watches storms like an old sentinel. Aquila Developments is a different language: mirrored glass, projected maps, and a rooftop model that promises safety in exchange for a remade coastline.

Politics curl around immediate survival and long-term identity. The Mayor negotiates with outside capital and emergency aid; grassroots organizers like Ivy and fishermen led by Eli push back against top-down fixes. Religion here is practical reverence for weather and harvest; culture is craft and recipes passed down with salt on their hands. Class frays where jobs vanish and redevelopment glitter tempts young people. The atmosphere is painterly coastal realism—salt-smoothed textures, neon rescue buoys bleeding into fog, and a town that keeps living while being slowly rewritten.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Rios: scientist, daughter of this coast, guardian of notes and guilt from a past misforecast. As sea levels rise and storms bite deeper, Aquila's glossy redevelopment proposal beams promises of stability and jobs, while proposing to reshape shorelines, elevate structures, and centralize control. Cass Adler, once a collaborator and sometimes lover, now fronts Aquila’s plan—sharp platinum bob, icy blue eyes scanning data, a polished pitch on every screen. Eli Navarro, sun-bleached and tethered to family oyster beds, wants the town to remain in local hands. Ivy and Rosa strain to bind the community together. Mayor Jonah tries to balance livelihoods and funding.

The story is a tragic arc: each decision pushes us deeper into moral compromises and practical losses. I choose a path at a town meeting—negotiate with Cass, resist with grassroots action, or take direct—but risky—restoration. Each branch leads to new stakes: betrayals, legal fights, sabotage in storms, or a public takeover. Splits repeat: whether I trust Cass enough to bend proposals, whether I escalate civil disobedience, whether I leak Aquila's plans. Across all routes, things worsen: trust fractures, a crucial structure is lost, a campaign fails, or I sacrifice personal life for a cause. Endings vary by nuance—community partially preserved at a cost, personal exile to wider activism, or corporate redevelopment that erases ways of life—but all land in loss. The curve is a steady fall: new mistakes, mounting failures, and a final nadir that asks what it means to love a place when the place itself is changed.
