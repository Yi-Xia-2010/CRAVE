# Route: Route B / C and Route C / B


[Scene: City Hall Promenade | Early Evening, Steady Rain]
[Visual: Polished stone steps slick with rain; a few lonely kiosks glow with tidal alerts. Evelyn Harrow stands near the glass railing, hair immaculate though damp, coat collar up against the wind.]
[Music: Warm piano motif, a single note of cautious optimism]
[Sound: Rain tapping in soft, steady percussion; distant gulls]

You step onto the promenade with your jacket clinging to your shoulders, leather journal damp at your side. The petitions are folded inside the journal like quiet evidence—names in ink that smell faintly of salt and late-night photocopy toner. The city lights smear on wet stone; every reflected screen is a promise, every reflection a ledger.

Your pulse hammers warm and bright under the ribs where guilt lives. You taste coffee and metal in the air, and beneath that the brine that has been home to half your life. You breathe slow and let the braid of hope you’ve been nursing loosen into something louder: you came here because the neighborhood could not be ignored.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand on the journal strap; the silver wave ring catches a pale flourish of city light]
[Sound: Footsteps—one measured set approaching; Ilan’s smartwatch soft chime in the distance]

Evelyn looks at you as though she is calibrating a machine: a quick tilt, an internal running of variables. Her eyes are steel-gray and direct; she wears decisiveness like armor and, for a moment, you feel small and essential at once.

Evelyn Harrow: "Ms. Marin. Thank you for coming. I read your submission. The archive—it's thorough."

You can hear surprise in her voice, a thin note that complicates the clean lines of her public persona. It makes your throat soften.

You (Ava Marin): "I came because numbers are only meaningful when someone remembers what they measure. These names—these stories—are the ground people stand on."

Evelyn Harrow: (nods once) "I understand the cultural value. I do. I also understand what insolvency looks like. Bond markets are already pricing the coastal districts as extreme risk. If we don't act at scale, insurers will withdraw, and the city will have fewer levers to protect anyone."

Her words are crisp, almost surgical—risk matrices and timelines like predictable waves. Still, there is no sneer. There is the economy of a pragmatist who has watched failure once and is allergic to repetition.

You feel the old image—your uncle's shop dark and empty—fold into the present and make your chest tighten. But alongside that clench, a current of something else runs: the tiniest ember of possibility. If she is offering a seat at the table, then maybe you can move more than a line item.

[Visual: Ilan arrives, sleeves damp, a small wind of technical optimism about him; sunlight—whenever it dares—catches the micro-solar panels on his vest]
[Sound: A bottle clinks in Ilan's bag; a faint electronic hum of a prototype powering up]

Ilan's presence changes the air; hope takes a different flavor when it's paired with a man who believes utensils can be turned into scaffolds for survival. He smiles that careful smile and for a second you remember why you defended the idea of prototyping in Tideward.

Ilan Cortez: (soft) "Did I get here at a dramatic time, or exactly on cue?"

You (Ava Marin): (half smile) "Both."

Evelyn Harrow: (to Ilan) "Mr. Cortez. Your work is noted. The city is interested in scalable solutions—if you can demonstrate cost and durability at a neighborhood level, you'd have a path to procurement."

Ilan's face warms but there is a shadow when she says "procurement"—as if the word hints at constraints you both know too well.

Ilan Cortez: (measured) "Procurement can be a force for good. But the way it's written matters. If the parameters only accept low-maintenance, high-margin solutions, small-scale tech gets absorbed or phased out."

Evelyn Harrow: "Then make your parameters explicit. Demonstrate the lifecycle, show the maintenance schedule, the community oversight. We will run it against our scenarios."

The exchange lengthens. You press, not because you want to argue, but because you need the contours of her openness.

You (Ava Marin): "And cultural costs? If Tideward is rebuilt as a sanitized model, who remembers the storms, the recipes, the routes we learned as kids?"

Evelyn Harrow: (tilts head) "Cultural preservation is not antithetical to resilience. But it must be quantified. What guarantees do you propose? How will the community sustain these preservations beyond a grant cycle?"

Her question is a gauntlet. It is also an opening, precise as a hinge.

Your mind reels through practicalities—the archive as a legal covenant, easements tied to property deeds, an enforceable community co-op with audits. The technical language sits in your mouth like unfamiliar saltwater, but ideas surface. They are messy; they need lawyers and time and stubborn neighbors who refuse to be simplified.

Evelyn watches you with that same measuring patience. When you press on timelines, she produces them as if from a pocket: a slim tablet that contains heat maps and future projections, all the city rendered into a tidy graph.

Evelyn Harrow: "We have a three-year stabilization window with bond backing. If Tideward can present a feasible plan within that time—costs, maintenance, community governance—we can allocate a pilot tranche. Without that, funding will follow market certainty and not cultural sentiment."

Her words are blunt, but undercut by the fact that she just handed you a timeline: a finite opportunity.

Hope flares, not because everything is solved, but because possibility exists—an actual hinge.

You (Ava Marin): "So—work within your system, or be sidelined."

Evelyn Harrow: (not unkind) "Yes. The city will not wait forever. But I will say this: bring me a model that protects people and memory, and I will not only listen; I will advocate for it before my board."

There it is: an invitation and a challenge that lifts the mood. The rain seems to soften as if even the weather leans toward forward movement.

Ilan reaches out, fingertips brushing the journal's edge—support without pressure. The contact is small, ordinary, and it steadies you.

Ilan Cortez: (quiet) "We can prototype governance with the modular run on the old pier. If we log community labor hours, maintenance incidents, and cultural uses—that's data proof that keeps the narrative intact."

You feel the rise in the room: a momentum that begins with a hopeful plan and moves outward. Your stubbornness—that old muscle that made you keep mapping milk routes—has a purpose that could finally speak in the language of contracts and budgets.

Your internal monologue scrambles through doubts: legal complexities, cynical developers, Evelyn's appetites for neat solutions. Still, the overriding note is hopeful—an ember fanned by possibilities.

Evelyn Harrow: (folds her hands) "I can't promise you perfect preservation. I can promise you resources and a procedural seat if you accept oversight. I can also tell you the city will favor scalable, auditable programs. Decide which part of Tideward you cannot let be subject to market forces, and define that in your proposal."

She offers the gauntlet with a softness you didn't expect.

You (Ava Marin): "And Ilan? Legal constraints—procurement, patents—will any of that block community control?"

Evelyn Harrow: "It can, if we don't write it into the agreement. Public procurement can include community clauses, but they must be explicit. Intellectual property should be licensed in ways that prevent appropriation. Bring me those clauses. Show me how you will enforce them. I will bring your proposal before the executive committee."

Every syllable lifts weight from your shoulders in a strange, promising way. The machine—grinding, efficient, hungry for clean results—has a seam you can slip something meaningful through if you are precise and unrelenting.

Ilan's eyes find yours. There is an earnestness there that steadies your wild hope with practical scaffolding.

Ilan Cortez: "Then let's build the pilot—metrics, maintenance training, a clause that ties cultural artifacts to community trusts. I'll sketch the tech lifecycle. Ava—your archive becomes the cultural ledger."

The three of you linger in a short, electric silence—the sort of pause that holds plans like seeds before sunlight hits them.

You can feel the city breathing—vulnerable but receptive. The rain eases to a mist, and the promenade glows with softened light.

Your voice is steadier than you expected as you speak, proof that the ember is becoming something that can catch.

You (Ava Marin): "If we do this, we do it on our terms. No cosmetic placemaking. No erasure. We build resilience that remembers."

Evelyn Harrow: (a fraction softer) "Then show me your terms. I'll bring this forward. But know what you're asking: I'll ask you to operate in a system that rewards certainty."

A small, bright hope uncoils in your chest—enough to carry you forward, enough to make you believe that memory can be legislated into survival if you do the hard, exacting work.

[Music: Piano swells into a hopeful major chord]
[Visual: The distant city skyline, for once less forbidding than it has been—lit with the idea of after]

You glance at Ilan and then at Evelyn and feel the old guilt turn into resolve. It's not the end of anything; it's the opening of a doorway that will require every stubborn, careful part of you.

You let yourself feel the lift: not false joy, but the honest warmth of possibility.

Now the city offers a real choice—an axis on which Tideward might pivot.
