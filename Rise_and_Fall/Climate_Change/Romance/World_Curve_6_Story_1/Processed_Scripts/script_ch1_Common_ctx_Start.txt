[Scene: Tideward | Morning]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Music: Low, minor-key piano with distant gull calls]
[Sound: Distant generator hum; the soft rattle of loose shutters]

You wake to brine and oil braided into the air—the smell presses against your throat before your eyes are fully open. Your field jacket is still damp from last night’s rooftop repair; the copper tips of your dark green-black hair have flattened against your forehead, a salty curl plastered to your temple. Tideward is half-asleep and half-alert, the way a tide is patient and relentless.

Light from the streetlamps is stalled behind a thin mist; the lamps themselves stutter, little electrical coughs in the fog. The ferry mural on the corner—painted years ago by someone who remembered the old routes—throws a perfect mirror of color on the puddled cobbles. Sea-glass green and rust reflect like a ledger of what’s been submerged and what is still visible.

You sit up and reach for the leather journal on the floor next to your mat. Its cover is the familiar, soft-scraped brown you’ve worn smoother than any map. Fingers trace the twine that keeps it closed, the same habit you had the morning after the shop was taken. Sixteen. The day the storm folded the block like paper and left our tools on a mudline. You remember the smell of diesel and wet wood and the small, honest heat of the repair stove your uncle kept in the back. That loss folded something into you—a duty that sits in your chest heavier than grief: map the high-tide routes, stitch the defenses, carry our stories so they don’t get flattened with the houses.

Outside, folders of half-dried flyers flap on a lamppost—mildew along their edges, glossy renders of elevated high-rises and glass promenades. Evelyn Harrow’s face is everywhere: immaculate, silver bob precise even under rain, steel-gray eyes on a thousand kiosks. The city calls it a plan; your neighbors call it a verdict. Your mouth tastes of salt and the day's first decision.

You sling the jacket over your shoulders. The patches are still damp; when you move, they whisper against each other—canvas rubbing canvas, the tiny mechanical music of people who still fix things by hand.

[Sound: A metal clip snaps; footsteps on wet planks]

You step through the narrow lane where rope fences sag from porch to porch. Someone has strung a line of salt-tolerant scarves like prayer flags—the colors muted, beaten flat by weather and memory. Mira is a rhythm in the street even before you see her: the clink of seed packets in a thermos. She’s on her way to the rooftop garden, hair cropped close, a patchwork skirt swaying.

Mira: "Morning, Ava Marin. Council went long. I passed the community board—half the notices are already soaked through." (She tilts the thermos toward you; the faint clink of seeds inside is steady, purposeful.)

Ava Marin: "They printed too many glossy pictures and not enough language that matters." (You pull the collar up against the damp. The journal sits warm under your arm like a small, stubborn ember.)

Mira: "They make it look like a postcard. Whoever designs those renders hasn't had to pump water out of a basement at two in the morning for a week." (She laughs, but it's a small thing without mirth.) "You okay? You look like you swallowed a storm."

Ava Marin: "Just tired." (Honesty tastes like iron. You remember Tomas at the dock, the way his hands move over a carburetor; you remember the shovel you sharpened last winter.) "I was up on the roof till late. We patched the north span near the herb boxes. Might hold another season if we keep at it."

Mira: "You'll make a whole season out of stubbornness." (She presses a seed packet into your hand. "Keep it," she says. "For the archive." Her face softens, careful.) "Also—" (she leans in conspiratorially) "—there’s talk of a meeting at the pier this afternoon. People want to know what the maps mean."

You want to answer with certainty, because certainty is an action that can be planned and measured. But your chest tightens—guilt as automatic as breathing. The journal presses at your ribs where your shoulder bone meets your heart.

You move toward the dock at the end of the lane where the smell of kerosene sharpens and Tomas is crouched by a small outboard, hands stained with old oil and grease. His canvas jacket is patched with sailcloth; his ponytail is damp; he’s never been one to wear the city's language lightly.

Tomas: "Ava Marin." (He doesn't stand—he rarely does unless a mast or a rusted hinge requires him.) "You sleeping on the roof again? You'll catch your death. Or worse—you'll catch the city giving up our names."

Ava Marin: "There's work to be done." (You kneel beside him and watch his fingers find the stubborn screw. The outboard clicks the way a heart does when it's uncertain.) "How’s she running?"

Tomas: "Like a stubborn old woman," (he grunts, tightening a bolt) "—but she’ll take us out. That’s what matters. You listening about the council? They listed Tideward for relocation, Ava Marin. Not all of it—some chunks—but they named streets by number and painted them beige."

Your laugh is bitter as tannin. "Painted them beige," you repeat, as if the words might rearrange themselves into something kinder. "They render new skylines and forget the people who taught them the shoreline."

Tomas: "They think if they make it prettier, no one will notice what’s gone." (He looks up at you, and there's more in his gaze than mechanical salvage: a question about staying, about what staying costs.) "You gonna go down to City Hall? Or will you stitch a quilt for everyone who’s leaving?"

Ava Marin: "Both." (You say it too quickly, like a promise you mean to keep whether you can or not.) "I’ll map, I’ll stitch the defenses, and I’ll—" (the tasks pour out of you like water, a list that becomes a life) "—I’ll document the stories. I won't let memory be leveled."

Tomas: "Ava Marin." (He stops working for a second, and the dock yawns around you with gulls and diesel. "There's pride in keeping your ground. There's also pride in knowing when the ground's been taken." His fingers return to the bolt. "Don't let pride be what buries you, girl." He says it without accusation; only the care an uncle can have when he has seen too many storms.)

[Sound: Phone buzz — a small, insistent tone like a bee trapped in glass]

Your phone vibrates in the inner pocket—brief, precise. Ilan Cortez. The name alone is a small, warming current in your chest: technical vests and burn marks and the compass on a chain that used to sit on his mother's window sill. You thumb it out. His message blinks on the screen: "Demo at Saltworks today. Modular barrier prototype hold tested at low tide. Come by—think it might help the north span."

You read it once, twice. The prototype has been a conversation between optimism and nervousness for months: marketable but humble tech, modular pieces that promise to be put together by neighbors, not developers. Ilan Cortez's confidence is contagious. But every tech that promises salvation has, in other places, been an invitation to outsiders with different agendas. Evelyn Harrow’s photographers are already selling the dream of towers that make room for investors.

[Outcome A: "Your thumbs type with habitual speed but your eyes water—whether from rain or the idea of letting something new enter our neighborhood. The message sends. You feel, for a breath, less alone."]
[Outcome B: "You save the message and pull your jacket tighter. The reply sits like a possible detour; you tell yourself you need to see the maps and faces at City Hall before you commit. Responsibility doesn't like to be subdivided."]

You cross the short pier that leads to the main path up to the promenade. The city is a study in contrast: Tideward’s patched blinds and rope fences against City Hall’s clean, clinical lines. The Promenade’s glass railings hiss with seawater and rain; municipal kiosks blink tidal alerts in muted blues. Someone has swept the steps clean; someone else has left a damp flyer on the first bench like a threat in polite paper.

[Scene: City Hall Promenade | Midday]
[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Music: Low orchestral strings with a metallic undertow]
[Sound: murmur of distant crowd; the automated voice of an information kiosk]

Mira navigates beside you, thermos clutched against her chest. You both read the same poster stuck to the kiosk—managed retreat, they call it, in soft fonts that want to make the word acceptable.

Mira: "They've got the render for Tideward right here. Look—'strategic consolidation.'" (She squints with a kind of fury that’s almost reverent.) "Strategic for whom, do you suppose? The render doesn't show the herb boxes on the roofs, or the mural."

Ava Marin: "They've redrawn the coast without us in it." (The words fall heavy and precise.) "They’re proposing scaled, efficient models. Numbers and projections. No smell of diesel. No damp quilts on the rail."

Mira: "What’s the meeting like later? Will people be angry or quiet?" (There’s a tremor you recognize in her—fear wrapped in practical curiosity.)

Ava Marin: "Both." (Your voice is low. You think of the shop, of Tomas’ hands, of the way the mural catches light. You think of Evelyn Harrow’s face on the screens—controlled, composed.) "We have to be both. We'll bring facts and stories. We’ll tell them what a street sounds like at three a.m. when the water finds the wrong gutter."

Mira: "Bring the journal." (Her eyes slide to the leather at your hip.) "Bring everything. Stories speak."

You touch the journal reflexively. It is a talisman and a ledger and a small fire in your palm. For a second you imagine reading names aloud in a room that will prefer renderings: the names will sound like contraband and courage.

[Outcome B: "You let the flyer lie. It flaps its resignation in the rain. You breathe in the cold, sharp air—salt and polished stone—and let it collect in your lungs like a promise of work."]

The signs feel like a verdict and like a map at once. The council's announcement hangs over the city like low cloud—compressing, pressing, insisting that someone decide. Your chest tightens in the way it does when you walk into a room and all the people in it are holding the same kind of grief. Guilt folds into duty into resolve until it's hard to separate one from the other.

You think of all the small things you know how to do: mend a seam, thread a pump, reroute a storm drain. You think of the archive you want to build: stories stitched into pages, lists of where the rosebushes once grew, a map of a milkman’s route that no one else cares to save. You promise yourself—not in a public, loud way, but in the private clench of your hands around the journal—that you will not let memory be leveled with the houses.

[Sound: Another vibration—this time a longer tone]
[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]

You can feel the choice lining up like tide marks: go to Saltworks and see whether Ilan Cortez’s prototype is a real tool or an appealing distraction; stay with the neighborhood, stitch defenses, rally voices at City Hall; split your day between both. Each choice makes quiet demands on what kind of person you will be.

You taste salt and decision.

You close the journal, feeling its weight and the map that lives folded inside you—the routes, the faces, the small, vital details that a render could never account for.

Your resolve is not a sudden flame; it is a long, slow ember you have been carrying since you were sixteen.

You move because movement is the only protest you can afford right now.

(Page-turn thought: If the city erases our names from its maps, you will have to make something that remembers them. You already know where to begin.)
