Ava Marin: "They're calling it help, Tomas. They want numbers to make it look reasonable."

Tomas: "Reasonable is how you bury a neighborhood in steps and approval stamps."

You feel the old hurt under your ribs—the memory of the repair shop half-swept away the year the storm came. The festival is bold and lovely, but already it’s a stage where others decide the script.

Ilan Cortez finds you near a table lit by a single lantern. His hands smell faintly of solder and fish smoke; there’s a sheen of adrenaline on his forehead from the demos. He holds a small, hand-sized filter in the cupped curve of his palm like an offering.

Ilan Cortez [defensive then softer]: "I know. I know you’re right. But we can do both—show them the humanity and offer tools that keep people safe. We've seen the filings; the injunction's margin is thinning. We need to press every angle."

Ava Marin: "And if the angle they favor is to make us palatable for tourists? If they want a sanitized Tideward to sell nostalgia while the rest of us are moved?"
