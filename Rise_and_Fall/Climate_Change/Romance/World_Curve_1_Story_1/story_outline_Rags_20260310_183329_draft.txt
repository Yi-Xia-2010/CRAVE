## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
A near-future coastal city (circa 2035) clings to habit and reinvention. Once a small fishing port, it now hosts pilot sustainability projects, rooftop farms, and nascent green-tech firms clustered around an imposing regional authority: the Kade Resilience Lab. The political scene is a friction plane between institutional, rapidly-scalable engineering and neighborhood-rooted adaptation—between seismic proposals for hard infrastructure and the slower, human work of cultural preservation. Climate anxiety is everyday: frequent storms, salt-scarred facades, intermittent power, and contested maps of future flood lines. Class tensions thread through markets and planning meetings: retrofit optimism promises jobs and investment, but risks displacement; nostalgia for the old livelihoods resists top-down solutions. Informal community networks—rooftop gardens, mutual-aid kitchens, music gatherings—are as vital as sensor arrays and modeling dashboards. Religion and formal civic ritual are sparse; what binds people are memory, food, and the daily labor of keeping homes above water. Visually, the city is painted in seafoam, slate, and rust, lit by neon trial-project signs and bioluminescent planters. Atmosphere is tactile and intimate: drizzle, patched roofs, salt on hands, and the quiet hum of machine interventions—an ecosystem of human feeling and engineered urgency.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
In first-person, Maya Calder returns to the harbor she vowed to protect. She balances engineering know-how with community memory and a private hunger for companionship. Romance with Eli — a sun-bleached urban farmer/musician — grows amid seedbeds and late-night rain. Institutional rival Dr. Sienna Kade pushes a fast, centralized seawall plan that could secure large tracts but uproot the harborside. The narrative is a steady "Rise": each obstacle catalyzes gain. The player navigates strategic choices—partnering with Sienna to shape policy, mobilizing the neighborhood to force inclusive plans, or focusing on decentralized adaptation and voluntary relocations. Each path leads to different kinds of success: a hybrid technical-community seawall, a grassroots retrofit that becomes a model, a sacrificial but strategic protection that saves most lives at cultural cost, or a morally honest transformation where new cooperative governance emerges. Throughout, Maya’s romance with Eli deepens into a partnership of shared commitment or becomes transformed into advocacy companionship. The story culminates in a set of distinct successes—each earned, each costly, each rising from the initial vulnerabilities toward stronger community agency and ecological resilience.
