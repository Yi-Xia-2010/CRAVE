Aria Marin: "You did more than promised," you say, fingers brushing the satchel at your hip where the town map still folds into neat, dog-eared edges. "The tenant fund — is it funded?"

Your mouth tastes like salt and something metallic. You remember the rooftop, the three pins on the corkboard, the way you arranged options like a surgeon arranges tools. You remember choosing.

Aria Marin [softening]: "I know. I know the cost. I bargained for constraints because if we didn't put them in, they wouldn't exist at all. If there's no partial victory, there may be none at all."

Kai Solano: "Partial victories are an excuse to write off whole lives." Aria Marin: "And romanticizing waiting until everyone drowns while we tell ourselves we're pure does what, Kai? I won't let people drown on principle. That's not purity — it's negligence."

It is intimate in the way only old arguments can be, frayed edges folding against each other. Onlookers watch: a foreman wipes his hands; a young organizer tucks his clipboard closer.
