### SCRIPT
# Route: Multi-route Final (True Ending)
# Context: 4001e7

[Scene: Civic Rooftop Garden | Morning]
[Visual: Long shot — the rooftop framed against a pewter sky. Torn banners droop from string; a corkboard leans, thumbtacks scattered. A coffee cup lies shattered near seed trays; a glass storefront across the street has a jagged, spiderwebbed hole.]
[Music: Low cello, slow and minor; sparse high strings like breath]
[Sound: Distant gull cry, a far-off siren that dies into the hour, wind threading through lanterns]

You climb the last short flight of stairs and the town opens and shrinks at once beneath you: the boardwalk's splintered boards, the cafe awning flapping like a torn flag, the pale, institutional outline of the Arcology site looming beyond the harbor. The satchel at your shoulder is heavier than it should be — maps inside, reams of notes, a pair of printed flyers with a smear of mud on the corner — and the brass compass at your throat feels impossibly cold.

Your hands still smell faintly of smoke and chalk. You think of the whistle at your lip two nights ago, sharp and ridiculous against the deep roar of so many throats; you think of the drone's red eye tracing your face; you think of how quickly righteous noise can calcify into something else. Your mouth tastes like metal and salt and the after-voice of a crowd. You have to put all of that somewhere.

[Visual: Medium shot — people clustered around the rooftop corkboard; a mesh of damp coats and anxious faces]
[Sound: The soft crinkle of paper, someone rubbing a bruise on their knuckle, a murmur that is almost a new kind of argument]

Kai Solano is there first in sightline — shoulders hunched forward, one palm flat on the corkboard. He slams his fist in a way that makes the whole board shiver; plans, sketches, and a handful of torn flyers flutter. His jaw is set; the amber in his eyes is a live wire.

Kai Solano: "You told them to be patient, Aria. You said 'work with the system.' Did that feel good when the cops were blocking our way at Third and Dune?"

Aria Marin: "It wasn't 'good.' You know that, Kai. You know why we—" (You stop because the word explodes into a dozen justifications you don't have the appetite for.)

Kai Solano: "You know why? Because you were trying to make everyone comfortable. We needed disruption. We needed to break the narrative that says we don't matter."

You can hear the conviction in him — the same conviction that once bolstered your own courage. But anger sharpens it into accusation. The corkboard creaks under his hand; a thumbtack skitters to the decking.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Step between him and the board, hold his arm gently"
> Choice B: "Let him speak and keep your distance"
[Outcome A: You place a palm at the base of his wrist. The heat of him is immediate. He flinches almost involuntarily, then lets out something that might be a laugh or a sob. His shoulders loosen a fraction.]
[Outcome B: You stay back, folding your hands into your jacket. He unloads, and hearing it without interruption pinches at you — part guilt, part a curious, thin relief. There is a rawness in the sound that you don't want to own, but you do.]

Noah Vega stands a little apart, tablet open. The screen throws a pale rectangle of light across his hands; the angle shows contract headers and flowcharts you know too well. His expression is practiced civility — a half-smile that steadies his face like a mask.

Noah Vega: "Aria—" (he begins, voice even) "There are routes to de-escalate that don't end in criminal records and broken windows. I can get our legal team mobilized to speed up the protections. We can fast-track permits; we can set conditions for tenant safeguards. It will take concessions, yes, but we can deliver measurable safety."

Aria Marin: "Concessions like what, Noah? Where's the line — when does 'measurable' become erasure?"

Noah Vega: "I'm proposing a binding schedule. Funds allocated for buyouts will prioritize long-term residents. We can include cultural preservation clauses." (He taps the tablet; a section highlights.) "I can't promise everything, but I can promise speed."

His voice is steady, and the tablet's glow is seductive in the dirty morning light. You can taste the practical logic in it: fast numbers, models, timelines. You can also feel the trade-offs like a cold ledger you might have to sign.

Dr. Linh appears at the edge of the group, her field coat streaked with damp soil. She holds a small plastic vial and an envelope of labeled samples; her brow is pinched in that way scientists wear when results refuse to be kind.

Dr. Linh: "I've run the runoff from the low-lying block by the estuary twice overnight. Salinity spikes are higher than the model predicted after the storm surge. If the Arcology footprint goes ahead without immediate mitigation measures, the soils we'll be working with are going to salt-out in a season."

Aria Marin: "How bad?"

Dr. Linh: "Bad enough that some of the halophyte buffers we planned won't establish. Bad enough that basement dwellings will see accelerated decay. We can adapt, but we need time — and we need to keep certain crews off the lower lots while we stage living shore measures."

Her hands don't shake, but the exactness of the numbers she doesn't need to voice speaks.

[Sound: A quiet gust; a paper plan flaps; someone breathes sharply nearby]
[Music: Cello deepens; a single violin thread like a cracked string]

Elder Tomas sits between you all; his stick is planted like punctuation on the deckboards. He looks like a map of the town: lines scored into his face, a softness at the eyes that never quite leaves him.

Elder Tomas: "This town's lived with tides before tides were 'news,'" he says slowly. "We hold places with our hands and memories. When those places go, a piece of us goes too. Compromise that shadows the memory is not a compromise at all; it's an erasure."

Kai Solano: "Then what? Do we let the developers bulldoze memory for a clean metric?"

Elder Tomas: "No. We don't let our anger make us blunt. We don't let our fear make us small."

Aria Marin: "So we do both, Tomas. We don't let the town be taken, and we don't let it be frozen into a museum."

Elder Tomas: (He taps his stick once.) "Words are warm. Actions freeze or thaw. Choose the thaw that keeps the bones of the town."

Maya moves closer to you, sleeves muddy from yesterday's repairs. She looks at your face as if reading a ledger she already understands.

Maya: "You said you wanted everyone in. Now people think your 'everyone' is the reason we couldn't punch through. They need movement. They need to feel like they matter."

Aria Marin: "I know."

Maya: "You know, but do you feel it? Because I do. I'm tired of being 'inclusive' while watching our neighbors pack boxes."

Her voice is small and blunt at once. You feel the tug of responsibility as both a planner and a sister.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Pull out the timeline from your satchel and point to the milestones"
> Choice B: "Close the satchel and put the map away, speak from your chest"
[Outcome A: You unsnap the satchel, produce the folded timeline. Numbers and dates look infantilized in the wake of bruises and broken glass. Kai snorts but leans in; Noah scans it with a thumb that betrays interest.]
[Outcome B: You close the satchel. The paper rustles like a small animal settling for shelter. You say what you feel, and for a moment it lands raw in the air: a confession more than a plan. The group hears it; the silence that follows says as much as any nod.]

You are halfway between three answers you know will be ugly in different ways.

You think of the picture of the café owner — old Mr. Calder — whose window was broken, of the muralist who lost half a day's work, of the family up on Seabreeze whose lower floor flooded last spring. All of them carry stories you have promised to hold. Your insistence on an open table once felt like the only moral strategy; now it tastes like an abdication of leverage. Your chest tightens with a guilty, familiar pressure: the same pressure that steered zoning meetings years ago, that made you hold the slow path as the righteous one even while tides changed.

Noah Vega steps closer, lowering his voice into a more private register.

Noah Vega: "Aria, if we present a unified front — my firm, the council, and you at the center — we can get emergency protections in place within weeks. You chair the oversight. You ensure tenant prioritization. We—"

Aria Marin: "And then?"

Noah Vega: "Then the town doesn't get swallowed. The trade-off is we build certain structural protections that might alter the shoreline. We can fund cultural mitigation, heritage grants, relocation assistance where necessary."

You can see his hand on the tablet hovering near a clause labeled 'Cultural Preservation Fund'. You imagine signatures, press releases, ribbon-cuttings. You also imagine leveled alleys, a phased demolition schedule that might carve out whole blocks of memory.

Kai Solano cuts in, voice raw.

Kai Solano: "Noah's 'solutions' always come with blueprints that make other people the collateral. You doing the oversight is still you at a table with architects who don't know the names of the people they'll relocate."

Aria Marin: "Kai, oversight matters. If I can get stronger legal teeth into those guarantees, more people stay."

Kai Solano: "Or you get used to the smell of concrete over a lifetime."

He glares at you, fingers pressed like rivets to the corkboard. There is a softness there too, just beneath the ire — a more private grief you and he share from other storms. But his impatience has hardened into a blade pointed at the middle ground you once believed in.

[Sound: A distant, nearly inaudible hum — the generator for a construction site; the town keeps making noise even as you bicker]
[Music: A low, unresolved minor chord; no resolution forthcoming]

You turn the conversation the only way you can: by naming the unavoidable.

Aria Marin: "There are three routes in front of us. None of them are perfect. None of them will make everyone whole. We choose a tactic now, and we live with the shape of that choice."

You feel the gravity of the moment in your limbs. Each option will rearrange loyalties, who loves you, who trusts you, and who will stand awake at night counting loss. There is a taste of iron on your tongue.

Elder Tomas watches you — patient, weathered — before he speaks.

Elder Tomas: "This town's bones are not eternal. They need tending. The question is not whether you can keep them; it's what you are willing to bear to do so."

Silence curls around the rooftop. People look at one another, at the torn banners, at the corkboard, at the street where the broken glass still catches the light. No one really wants to move first.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Say nothing more and let them speak first"
> Choice B: "State clearly you will not be passive — propose a compromise framework"
> Choice C: "Ask for a vote among those present to pick a direction"
[Outcome A: You fold your hands and keep your mouth shut. Voices rise and fall; positions reveal themselves like mapped currents. You learn things you didn't know: alliances and fissures that might change the calculus.]
[Outcome B: You outline a skeletal framework: timelines, safeguards, and community oversight. Faces shift, some softening, some hardening. The air tastes of negotiation and the ache of necessary losses.]
[Outcome C: You ask for a small vote. Hands go up — some trembling. The tally will be imperfect, but it forces the group to bear the responsibility. The act of counting makes decision feel more real and more dangerous.]

You know this next step will be defining. Your chest rains guilt and possibility in equal measure. The satchel at your hip is suddenly unbearably heavy, as if the paper plans and flyers had become anchors.

You take a breath that feels like the last before a dive. You feel your control-surge crack; you feel the old pattern — your need to make decisions that spare harm — skitter against a harsher truth: sometimes the effort to spare everyone ends up sparing no one.

[Music: The cello drops to a single sustained note; the wind seems to hold its breath]
[Visual: Close-up — your hand hovering near the corkboard, over the three colored pins you use to mark options in your plans]

You lay out the choices with a voice that is steady but small.

Aria Marin: "We have to decide now. We can escalate, we can accept an accelerated engineered approach with oversight, or we can force the pilot we've been talking about — living shorelines with a community-managed berm that pauses construction so we can prove it works."

Kai Solano's breath catches. Noah Vega's fingers hover near the tablet's signature field. Dr. Linh's jaw tightens; she is already calculating timelines in her head. Elder Tomas shifts his weight, the scrape of wood against decking telling you he's ready to bless or curse whichever way you turn.

This is the ledger moment: a morning enumerating costs not in figures but in faces.
