### Valence Evaluation (Emotional Tone Progression)

Valence represents the emotional direction of the story. Each chapter is labeled with one of five levels:

* VeryNegative: despair, terror, collapse
* Negative: tension, sadness, discomfort
* Neutral: calm, reflection, transition
* Positive: hope, relief, warmth
* VeryPositive: triumph, catharsis, unconditional resolution

When judging valence, you must evaluate the **shape of the emotional curve across chapters**.

You must check:

* Whether the progression follows the intended emotional arc (for example: Fall–Rise should go downward emotionally first, then recover upward toward a positive ending)
* Whether emotional transitions between adjacent chapters are smooth and logical
* Whether emotional highs and lows occur at appropriate narrative moments (e.g., climax, turning points, resolution)
* Whether the ending valence matches the expected resolution type of the story arc

You must penalize:

* Emotional values that do not match the required arc pattern
* Sudden jumps (e.g., VeryNegative directly to VeryPositive without narrative buildup)
* Flat or repetitive emotional levels across many chapters
* Endings that do not deliver the emotional payoff expected from the emotional arc

A outline should show a **clear, intentional emotional trajectory** that evolves chapter by chapter and supports the narrative events.

---

### Arousal Evaluation (Pacing and Energy Flow)

Arousal represents narrative energy and pacing intensity. Each chapter is labeled with one of five levels:

* VeryLow: stillness, emptiness, silence
* Low: slow, reflective, atmospheric
* Mid: normal activity, dialogue, investigation
* High: urgency, tension, argument
* VeryHigh: chaos, panic, combat, crisis

When judging arousal, you must evaluate how the **energy level rises and falls over time** and whether it aligns with the events of the story.

You must check:

* Whether high-arousal chapters align with moments of conflict, crisis, or climax
* Whether low-arousal chapters are used for reflection, setup, or emotional recovery
* Whether the pacing gradually builds toward major events instead of jumping abruptly
* Whether the overall pacing rhythm avoids monotony

You must penalize:

* Constant high arousal without rest or contrast
* Constant low arousal without escalation
* Misalignment between event intensity and arousal label (e.g., combat scene marked as low arousal)
* Lack of buildup toward climactic chapters

A strong outline will demonstrate a **controlled pacing rhythm**, with rising tension toward key events and proper cooldown periods.

---

### Alignment Between Valence and Arousal

You must also evaluate the **interaction between valence and arousal**.

Check whether:

* High-arousal chapters correspond to emotionally intense states (positive or negative)
* Low-arousal chapters correspond to reflective or neutral emotional states
* Emotional turning points are supported by shifts in pacing
* Climactic chapters show both high arousal and strong emotional valence

Penalize outlines where:

* Emotional tone and pacing contradict each other (e.g., calm emotional tone with chaotic arousal)
* Climaxes lack sufficient arousal buildup
* Emotional resolution occurs during high chaos without transition

A strong story will show **coordinated movement between emotional tone and pacing energy**.

---

### Consistency and Coherence

Across the full outline, you must judge whether:

* The valence curve and arousal curve together form a **coherent storytelling rhythm**
* The emotional and pacing changes are justified by the narrative events described in each chapter
* The story does not contain structural contradictions in tone or pacing

You must prioritize:

* Clear emotional arc
* Controlled pacing flow
* Strong alignment with the intended emotional arc

You must be strict, analytical, and consistent.
