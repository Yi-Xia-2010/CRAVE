## Visual Novel Title
Tideward Ledger

---

## World View
In 2040, the coastal city of Meridian teeters between reclamation and retreat. Sea levels have redrawn its edges: low-lying Tideward clings to stilts and reclaimed boardwalks; new solar piers and modular scaffolds stitch the shoreline into provisional habitability. Municipal power is increasingly centralized, driven by investment-backed development that promises security in exchange for relocation. Grassroots resilience counters with cooperative gardens on rooftops, salvaged boat workshops, oral archives of lost streets, and patchwork defenses sewn from local ingenuity. Politics are transactional and urgent—City Hall Promenade convenes press briefings under cool lighting, while community organizers and tinkerers negotiate survival on wet cobbles. Religion and ritual persist in informal memorials and salt-spray shrines to the sea; class divides are sharp where high-rises rise on land cleared by managed retreat. Tension centers on two models: Evelyn Harrow’s technocratic, centralized managed-retreat policy (formal, swift, costly in cultural memory) versus community-centered, slower adaptation championed by Tideward residents (messy, distributed, rich in heritage). The atmosphere is melancholic but active—salt in the air, murals cataloging the lost, and the constant sound of tide against salvaged pilings. Romance and practical choices entwine as individual attachments collide with civic imperatives.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I, Ava Marin, lead Tideward’s fragile resistance to erasure. The story begins with a collapse—storms and political pressure push the neighborhood toward a legislated clearing. As the city imposes an ultimatum, I fall into despair and guilt, haunted by my repair-shop past and the faces of those who might be forced to leave. I meet Ilan Cortez, an inland engineer with amber eyes and a prototype optimism: modular, affordable adaptations that could keep people here. Our attraction is immediate and complicated: his micro-solar vest and brass compass contrast with my patched olive jacket and salt-streaked field journal. Together and apart we attempt to broker a solution. Early choices plunge me into conflict (Fall), then spur a phase of hope—pilot projects, temporary barriers, and public demonstrations where Ilan’s tech pairs with Tideward’s memory work (Rise). But victories are provisional. Political compromises, legal maneuvers, and the city’s economic calculus unravel our gains. In the final act the community faces displacement, co-optation of technology, or a durable yet altered life—each branch follows the arc of Fall → Rise → Fall: we fall into crisis, we solve something temporarily, and we suffer an ultimate loss whose form depends on my choices and compromises.

### Chapter 1: "Drowned Streetlights" [Fall] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I wake to the smell of brine and oil—the day after the council’s emergency announcement. My field jacket is still damp from last night’s rooftop repair; the copper tips of my dark green-black hair stick to my forehead. Tideward’s lamps flicker, and a mural of a vanished ferry gleams with wet reflections. I move through lanes of rope-fenced porches, hearing Mira’s seeds clinking in a thermos and Uncle Tomas tuning a small outboard by the dock. I think of the shop we lost when I was sixteen and how that loss carved a duty into me: to map high-tide routes, stitch flood defenses, and record our stories in the battered leather journal that I carry like a talisman. At City Hall, mildew-scented flyers advertise managed retreat with glossy renders of high-rises on elevated land; Evelyn Harrow’s silver bob and steel-gray eyes are on every screen, immaculate even under rain. The announcement feels like a verdict: portions of Tideward are slated for relocation. My chest tightens—guilt, responsibility, and a stubborn refusal to be moved. I promise myself I will not let memory be leveled with the houses. The phone buzzes with a message from Ilan about a prototype demonstration. I taste salt and decision.

He speaks quietly; amber eyes alive. Rahim warns that scaling requires coordination and might invite municipal oversight. Evelyn’s plan, promised across crisp renderings, offers relocation with infrastructure investment but displaces the very networks that keep memory alive. My chest tightens with the old guilt and the new pressure; I have to decide the face of Tideward’s resistance. Stay small and grassroots, trusting handmade defense and oral archives? Let Ilan’s tech scale with cautious institutional partnership? Or push for a legal blockade to force a public hearing and buy time? Each path feels like a moral hinge; there is no neutral ground. People laugh when the prototype leaks at first, then cheer when it holds. For the first time in months I sleep without waking from guilt. The city press notices our demonstration, and Rahim drops by with measured praise—he warns us of legal entanglements should the municipality appropriate the design, but also suggests pathways to scale ethically. The community begins to imagine staying. I tuck a strand of my dark green-black hair behind my ear and trace the curling wave ring on my finger. Hope feels perilous and real—like a tide held back by a human hand.

The archives in my leather journal feel at risk, pages fluttering like loose tides. I taste brine and regret.

We salvaged memory but at a cost. The narrative arc has curved—hope rose, but the fall returns with a new gravity. Love, like coastal defense, is maintenance against a tide that does not stop.
