### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Ending: Sad


[Scene: Abandoned Promenade | Late Afternoon — Overcast]
[Visual: Long shot of the promenade; tide-lines like faded graffiti, storefronts with glass facades replaced by polished signage, a private security van idling under salt-streaked lights]
[Music: Distant cello, low and slow]
[Sound: The soft slap of residual waves against revetments; a chain-link gate thudding somewhere down the strip]

You arrived here knowing both roads that could have led you—preserve the nodes, accept staged redevelopment—and you carried the taste of each in your mouth like iron. The particulars of past arguments blur now; whether you argued for locks on buyouts or for clauses in redevelopment, the timetable collapsed the same way: investments arrived faster than your community could bind them, and decisions made with deadlines favored the speed of capital over the slow, stubborn work of repair.

The promenade is the proof. What used to be a mishmash of fish-smoke shops and homecraft stalls is now a display—gleaming terraces, curated tide-line exhibits, an expensive café that uses reclaimed driftwood as décor while a security guard in a windbreaker checks a tablet. The air smells of lemon polish and diesel, not kelp and fry oil. The sea keeps the same indifferent rhythm, but the edges of the town answer differently.

You run a thumb over the compass dent in your jacket without thinking—its brass warm from the sun that breaks through a fringe of cloud for a single weak moment—and watch a family walk past with cardboard boxes stamped by a municipal relocation contractor. They avoid your eyes in the way that people avoid opening a wound in public.

Gracie Nan stands a few steps away, her cardigan pulled tight against the damp. Her braid is thinner; sunlight picks out silver threads. She looks at the waterfront as if reading a book she knows by memory, and her face folds with a history you cannot fix with numbers.

Gracie 'Nan' Armitage: "They'll make museums out of what they couldn't keep," she says quietly, the words a pebble dropped into the water.

You: "Museums don't keep the smell of fish or the way the dock plank creaks under a child's foot." (You keep your voice low; the cove doesn't need performance now.) "They keep curated versions."

Gracie 'Nan' Armitage: (She exhales through her nose) "Curated versions are easier to sell. Easier to forget."

You want to argue—about clauses, trust mechanisms, the sanctity of a communal process—but the private security van rolls by and the guard nods at Nan as if recognizing a species rather than a neighbor. Her mouth tightens. You feel the thing you fought against become an artifact before your eyes.

Jonah appears at the mouth of the promenade—coat open, hair damp from the sea-spray. He moves with that patient gait he always had; his eyes take in the lights, the storefronts, the contractors with coastal-issue clipboards and branded hardhats. He tries a wry smile that doesn't land.

Jonah Reyes: "They're calling it 'revitalization,'" he says, half teasing, half furious. "Like they're rescuing us from ourselves. Everything has to sound palatable."

You: (There's a sharp edge in your chest that answers) "They've always sold rescue as mercy."

Jonah Reyes: (He laughs, a small, bitter sound) "And sometimes rescue shows up with a price tag. Nan—" (He hesitates, watching her braid.) "She still remembers the nets my grandfather mended here. The work isn't a display for their glossy brochures, Maya."

You exchange a look long enough to carry decades. Jonah's hands fidget with the rope bracelet at his wrist, the way he does when he's deciding not to stir old grief into new courage.

Jonah Reyes: "I can teach in the city for a while. There's a program—steady pay, nights on call, something for the boy cousins. They'll think it's sensible. I..." (He stops; his voice catches, then steadies.) "You'll keep the records, right?"

You: (Because this is the knot you don't know how to cut) "I will document. I'll keep the trust documents, the affidavits, the photos—everything. I won't let them sanitize every memory into a footnote."

Jonah Reyes: "Promise me you'll... keep some of it just for us." (He finally says the part that matters; it's small and naked.)

You: (You want to promise more than you can give. You hold out both hands; there is no simple binding.) "I promise I'll try."

Jonah smiles in that way that crumples the sunlight and tells you he's trying to be brave. You watch him walk away toward the bus stop with a case of workshop tools strapped across his back—tools that once mended boats and now will patch a life in another town. The distance already begins to stretch.

[Visual: Jonah's figure receding; the security lights flare as a truck backs into a service lane]
[Sound: The wet slap of his boots grows faint; a gull cries]

[Scene: Reclaimed Marsh Restoration Site | Night — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Low-lying reedbeds, half-submerged check-dams; floodlights from a nearby development site cast hard rectangles across the mud]
[Music: High strings, nervous and thin]
[Sound: Wind beginning to run like a ruler across marsh grass; the first cold rain]

You climb the narrow boardwalk toward the restoration site, notebook heavy in your arms. The restoration plots that once hummed with careful hand-plantings now show gaps—saplings collapsed, coir rolls begun to fray. Where the new commercial wall meets the marsh, there is a seam: a band of geotextile cloth tied in a knot with contractor tape. Someone stapled a "Work In Progress" sign and left.

Rafi's sketchbook is splayed in the vestibule of the field tent; his blue ink diagrams tremble under a plastic sheet. He looks at you without surprise and then with the gravity of someone who has been carrying a forecast no one wanted to read.

Rafi Chen: "They reallocated the seal funds," he says. "Investors wanted the promenade to look safe for ribbon-cutting tomorrow. That money came from the marsh budget." (His mouth tightens; he has the neat hands of someone who builds solutions and is now forced to unbuild a part of them.) "We can do emergency buoyant collars, but—"

You: "But it's temporary." (You don't finish. You both know the math of temporary underfunding and next-storm physics.)

Rafi Chen: "Temporary collapses faster in salt water. We can jury-rig, but if the storm hits hard—" (He drops the sentence like a stone.)

You walk the lines between the planted grasses, feeling the wet through your boots. The air is iodine and metal: salt and the smell of nearby fleet engines. The storm arrives sooner than hoped; rain begins to bead on the exposed pages of your notebook.

Evelyn Hart is there under a canopied truck light, clipboard in hand, her trench coat buttoned up against the rain. Up close, she looks smaller than in council chambers, but her voice carries the same clean efficiency.

Evelyn Hart: "We prioritized the visible infrastructure," she says without apology. "Investors need confidence. The boardwalk is symbolic; if it fails their confidence, we lose all leverage." (Her fingers don't tremble when she taps her pen.) "Ideally, we'd restore both simultaneously. Reality demanded a choice."

You: (You hold something back—rage, grief, a litany of missed meetings—and let your voice be measured) "You chose investors over marshes. The marsh is not symbolic to those who sleep with its tide lines. It is their lifeline."

Evelyn Hart: (She meets your eyes, and a moment of something like guilt flickers—but it is quickly folded away) "We seeded a trust for relocations—legal protections. We didn't put them on the table as performance metrics for a ribbon-cutting. I didn't like that we had to pick. I negotiated what I could."

You: "Did you consider asking for more time? For staged allotments that guaranteed restoration before commercialization?" (You press, because negotiation is what you've lived in and you refuse to let the ethics be smoothed over by procedure.)

Evelyn Hart: (Her jaw works) "I asked. The board placed timelines on deliverables. This region had to show immediate returns to secure larger grants. I argued for the marsh, for the trust—" (She stops. Her eyes flick toward the failing check-dams.) "I did what I could with what I had."

The rain thickens. The marsh reeds whistle in the wind like a chorus of thin voices.

You: (You step closer, voice low) "What you did had consequences."

Evelyn Hart: "Consequences are part of policy." (She says it like a fact: neither apology nor boast.) "If you want to litigate, you'll have to show malfeasance, not just bad outcomes."

You: "This isn't just bad outcomes," you say. "This is people's homes, histories. We will tell this story with names, with dates, with photos. We'll show the sequence."

Evelyn Hart: (She studies you as if weighing whether you're a threat or an ally, then gives a small, formal nod.) "I don't want the town to be a parade for inequitable adaptation, Maya. If that happens, I will answer for it." (There is something like a promise wrapped in the legalese; it is not comfort, but it is acknowledgment.)

Rafi pulls the tarp down over the kit with a practiced motion and looks at you both, tired and raw.

Rafi Chen: "If the storm hits, the marsh won't be the only thing we lose," he says. "Those families we tried to keep in place? The ones moved to the temporary barracks? Their support networks are gone. They'll be the most at risk."

You: (You think of the faces you've catalogued—older neighbors who couldn't relocate fast enough, young parents trying to salvage heirlooms from damp boxes—every entry a small elegy) "We did what we could." The statement tastes like ash. "But what we couldn't do will have names."

[Scene: Promenade | Early Dawn — After the Storm]
[Visual: Dawn light flattened by cloud; driftwood memorials and weathered quilts staked along the boardwalk; in the distance, towers of private development glint; a strip of municipal vans lines the access road]
[Music: Solo piano, sparse and aching]
[Sound: The slow drip of water from eaves; a distant megaphone announcing a cleanup schedule]

The storm came overnight and found the weakest seams. The partially funded marsh bulged and lost sections; the check-dams gave where the current found leverage. One relocation cluster—temporary housing pitched too close to an undermined berm—suffered a partial failure before crews could reinforce it. No lives lost where the managed retreat succeeded, thank the measured plans and a lot of overnight heroics. But loss is measured in more ways than lives.

You walk the promenade at first light and find residents who arrived early, forming a chain to carry quilts and damp boxes to dry platforms. They move like a community excavating its own memory. You step in to help, hands used to work that saves more than it comforts.

Relocated Families: (A voice from a group) "We were told safety would follow soon." (Someone laughs a small, sharp laugh.) "Soon was supposed to be a timeline, not a betrayal."

You: "We tried to make sure 'soon' was enforceable." (You meet their eyes because they demand it.) "If anyone here needs help with affidavits or claims, I'm on the table today. We'll catalog every item, every damage. I will not let these vanish into a ledger."

Someone gives you a tired, grateful look; someone else looks away. The cove is full of compromised loyalties—those who stayed, those who left, those who came back. You have documents to prove misallocation; you have photographs of the failed berms; you have witness statements that will form the spine of the story you refuse to let the developers fold into a glossy brochure.

Gracie Nan sits on an overturned crate near a small driftwood shrine where people have left shells and carved name tags. Her braid is even thinner after the wind and rain. She lifts a hand, and you kneel to take it.

Gracie 'Nan' Armitage: (She whispers) "We made songlines out of work and tide. They've turned the song into a lecture you pay to hear. They took the living off the water and put displays of it behind glass."

You: (A hot surge of anger and grief pushes through) "They took pieces. We kept others. We can't unmake what happened, Nan. But we can name it."

Gracie 'Nan' Armitage: "Names keep people alive. Don't forget how to name, child."

You take a breath that catches the salt in it. You think of Jonah—packing tools into a van and leaving a line of promise stretched thin across a map—and you think of all the neighbors whose livelihoods unravel in slow, bureaucratic motions. Love and logistics no longer fit neatly in the same sentence.

Your phone rings. Jonah's name blinks on the screen: an international operator prefix you weren't prepared for. You answer.

Jonah Reyes: (Over the line; his voice rougher for distance) "Hey," he says simply.

You: "Jonah." (You hear the tar in your own voice.) "How's the job? The first week okay?"

Jonah Reyes: "It's... steady." (A pause.) "My cousin got sick. I took overtime. The pay helps. I feel like I moved into an engine room." (He forces a laugh you don't hear on the other end.) "I miss the smell of the bay."

You: (Your throat tight) "I miss the sound of your hammer on wood." (You try to keep the sentiment bright enough to be breathable.) "I'm here, doing what I can."

Jonah Reyes: "You're doing more than I could stand. I should be there. But—" (His voice breaks and he swallows) "—I can't make them wait. I have mouths to feed, Maya."

You: "I know." (What else is there to say that isn't an accusation or a plea?) "We both chose survival in different forms."

Jonah Reyes: "I don't want to lose us on this." (His words are small but they tremble with meaning.) "Distance is a thief."

You: (You imagine his hands—callused, still—making knots in new rope. You imagine him learning a different city's tides of labor.) "We will be frayed," you admit, truth like a rag. "But if we stop cataloguing, if we stop caring, then the place that made us will be gone in every way that matters."

Jonah Reyes: (Long pause) "Promise me you'll keep your shoulder sometimes for me." (He sounds like a man asking for a dock in a storm.)

You: (You want to promise more than you can guarantee) "I'll do my best. I can't promise I won't be angry. I can't promise you won't go. But I'll keep recording. I'll come when I can."

Jonah Reyes: "That's something." (He laughs, and it is small and sad.) "Take care of Nan for me."

You: "Always."

The call ends with no grand resolution—only the precise, measured ache of two people trying to tether themselves to each other as tides pull them apart. You fold the phone away and pull your scarf up against the damp. The promenade hums with cleanup crews and volunteers; the new storefronts open their doors to staged tours for the curious and disbelieving. The town functions in two registers: the lived cove and the curated cove, each reflecting the other imperfectly.

You sit on the edge of the boardwalk and open your waterproof notebook. Pages are full of lists: names, times, funding flows, photographs zipped into sleeves. You draw a line through a budget table where a marsh allocation was redirected. You sketch Nan's hands, the way they find each other and hold. The notation is not neat; it is a ledger and a prayer.

Grief comes in slow increments. People will rebuild, some in relocated clusters where safety is purchased but community is attenuated. Some will leave. Some traditions will be moved or die. The managed retreat saved lives where it succeeded, and that matters; but the cultural fabric—threads of song, smell, boat-building know-how—was severed in places, and those gaps will not stitch themselves back with money alone.

At dusk you walk the promenade one last time before the official ribbon-cutting—an act that will frame the town in a new narrative. You stand under one of the new lights and watch the polished crowd arrive: city officials, investors, ribbon in gloved hands. Behind them, a cluster of relocated families stands with quilts over their shoulders, the makers of the actual work reduced to witnesses.

You think of the possible endings you once modeled—the happy, the bittersweet, the true—and now see how our choices, no matter how heroic in intent, met a force larger than a single town's planning commission. The result is a bitter, necessary calculus: some survive with dignity; some survive through displacement; some lose not only property but the mnemonic threads that let a place be itself.

You close your notebook and pull Nan's hand into yours. Jonah's voice lives in a small corner of the page, written in his hand in a note you keep folded: "Keep some of it just for us." You put the note into your pocket where it warms against the compass.

[Music: A low chord; a long exhale]
[Visual: Wide of the promenade; ribbon-cutters silhouetted against the new storefronts; volunteers and security standing like two different patrols]
[Sound: Scissors snip; a polite, rehearsed applause; the distant sea, indifferent]

You rise as the ribbon falls and the cameras flash. Someone hands you a program—fancy paper with glossy photos that sweeten the wound. You refuse the offered drink and instead walk the edge where the promenade meets the water. The tide advances with the patience of an indifferent god. You place your palm on the railing and feel the cold that comes from long seasons.

You are here. You will keep documenting. You will keep naming. Some will stay and stitch together a future with graft and grit. Others will leave. Jonah may come back for visits; perhaps you'll meet at the margins, two maps overlaid imperfectly. Love persists—tired, stretched, and not whole—but it is not enough to restore what was lost.

You breathe the salty air and hold the moment in the only way you can: with witness. You make an entry in your notebook that reads, simply: "Shoreline farewells catalogued—names, dates, circumstances. Truth kept."
