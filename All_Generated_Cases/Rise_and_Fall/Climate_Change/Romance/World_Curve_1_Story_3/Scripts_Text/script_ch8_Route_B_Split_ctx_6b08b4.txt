[Scene: Beaconworks Lab | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the lab — long tables cluttered with tidal models, resin-encased plant samples, and a wall of pinned sketches. A large window shows the Promenade cranes in the distance through mist.]
[Music: Warm, rising strings with a soft electronic underpinning]
[Sound: Low hum of servers, the murmur of voices, a kettle clicking off]

You step over a coil of sensor cable and into the thin, bright air of Beaconworks, the smell of ozone and wet peat folding into the lab's usual metallic tang. The microscope lamps cast small islands of gold on the tables; someone has left a tray of strong coffee on the communal bench. Your watch ticks, slow and steady, and for a moment you let the steady sound locate you: here, breathing, present to a thing that's finally moving from paper to place.

Elias Harrow is already here. He stands by the table with his tablet open, shoulders squared in a way that looks practiced — posture for when one carries responsibility heavier than oneself. He looks up when you enter, and his smile is careful, like a sentence you choose because it will keep more doors open than it closes.

Lucia Montrose is here too: polished, mobile, a presence that organizes the room like a compass needle. Rani has her toolbelt slung low, sawdust on her forearms; Eda sits quietly at the edge, palms stained with marsh mud even here. Councilor Mateo Qu thumbs through briefing papers, the edges damp where he leafed them in the field.

You feel the aftertaste of the last meeting — the memorandum lodged with the city, the pilot approved for phases. It hums beneath everything like a charge. Today is about making that approval real: who speaks first, who signs the forms, who takes the photos that will shape the story the city tells itself.

Mara Solenne: "Morning. What's the morning look like?"

Elias Harrow: "Morning. We have a contractor slot next week, the permit drafts are in review, and—" (he glances at Lucia) "—Lucia's team has offered phased funding contingent on a public commitment."

Lucia Montrose: "Conditional, yes. Visibility accelerates buy-in. If people see the pilot working, the council votes faster. We can front media, resources, and fast-track certain supplies."

You feel the room tighten at the word visibility — a bright-faceted thing that can cut or illuminate. Your palm finds the hydroponic locket at your neck without thinking; the sprig inside hasn't wilted yet. You picture the faces of your neighbors: elders who remember when the marshes were broad enough to hide boats, kids who learned to plant reeds on patched pontoons. Visibility could mean recognition and protection. It could also mean headlines that erase nuance.

Rani Cho: (breezy) "Visibility gives momentum. Cameras mean donations, and donations mean people can buy hammers."

Eda Nal: (soft, rooted) "But visibility can take the face of a thing and make it into a billboard. We must be careful what parts of us get magnified."

Elias Harrow shifts, fingers worrying the edge of his tablet as if the device is a talisman against sudden decisions.

Elias Harrow: "There are risks. If the pilot is framed as Beaconworks' brainchild, the community voice could be sidelined in the narrative. I—" (He exhales slowly.) "—I don't want us to disappear into a press kit."

Lucia Montrose meets him across the table like a quietly trained adversary.

Lucia Montrose: "You won't disappear. Beaconworks wants community oversight seats, training programs, credit in the technical reports. But we can move faster with our channels. I'm offering scaling with accountability."

Councilor Mateo Qu leans forward, eyes on both of you.

Councilor Mateo Qu: "Technically, Lucia's offer is the difference between a pilot that stays a pilot and one that becomes policy. The council watches momentum. They vote for what's winning."

Your throat tightens with the weight of histories: the wetland groves taken for expedience, the neighborhoods rebranded and swept away. You have spent years stitching dignity back into people's livelihoods. You also understand political math. This moment wants you to stitch and count at once.

You clear your throat, steadying the tilt in your chest.

Mara Solenne: "We need both speed and voice. Our people need to be visible on their own terms, not as an aesthetic in a corporate release."

Lucia Montrose's expression is measured; there is a flicker, unreadable — neither disdain nor embrace.

Lucia Montrose: "Then propose the terms. If your model proves survivability and reduces scour, I'll recommend it. And if you want co-branding, put it in writing. We can draft language that satisfies both assurance and acknowledgement."

Elias Harrow meets your eye. There is an unspoken ledger between you both — affection written in margins of compromise. He tucks a strand of hair behind his ear and nods, small, supportive. You catch the way he avoids the word "ours" when he should say it, and you know he's weighing public exposure against the professional weight on his shoulders.

The micro-choice reframes the room in microseconds. Everyone reshuffles language, negotiates commas and intent like currency. It feels good, practically speaking — like soldering a weak joint until it holds.

Later that day you go to the Promenade for an on-site walk. The site smells of diesel and salt; the steel of the seawall sections rattles with each gust. A small cluster of neighbors stands behind a safety barrier, wearing Beaconworks-branded vests and your own community's patched seagrass emblems. A camera rig stands ready on a tripod — Lucia's eyes keep flicking to it.

[Scene: The Promenade | Midday]
[Visual: The shoreline is a mixture of churned mud, clamped eelgrass, and stacked modular cells waiting to be slotted. Workers shout over the whine of machinery; gulls circle above like punctuation.]
[Sound: Distant clank of metal, shouted instructions, gull cries; rain is only a whisper at the horizon]
[Music: A steady, optimistic pulse]

Eda Nal kneels at a patch of sand, brushing it with the back of a hand like she is reading a palm.

Eda Nal: "This is where the first rush used to hit and be tamed by roots. These inserts will need time to root, and the people will need time to learn to care for them."

Rani Cho is already taking measurements, her tape snapping out with a comedic efficiency. You walk the line with Elias Harrow by your side. He points out sensor mounts, annotations in his tablet forming a small topography of hope.

Elias Harrow: "If we make the data open, other crews can replicate the inserts. If we keep it tight, we can control the rollout and protect the community from quick exploitation."

You feel that familiar pull between public good and guarded trust. Open data can be a force-multiplier; it can also be a spotlight that drags your neighbors into committees and headlines before they are ready.

Lucia Montrose's voice comes across the machinery noise, cool and practical.

Lucia Montrose: "Open data accelerates adoption. But it also invites critique. I can fund a PR campaign alongside published data. If the pilot looks authoritative, citywide frameworks will follow."

Elias Harrow glances at you, his jaw set. There's a tremor in his voice you haven't heard often.

Elias Harrow: "And our relationship—" (his eyes flick to you, and you feel the public and private blur) "—we'll be visible. If I'm on Beaconworks' roll-out team and people see us together in photos, that changes perceived ownership. I need to know that's okay with you."

Your chest warms at the care beneath his concern. You know the way the city polices alliances, how relationships can be parsed into leverage. There's a risk here — but also possibility. The two of you together could model a new kind of partnership: a scientist and a community organizer knitting technical decisions to lived experience.

Mara Solenne: "I'm okay with being visible. But not as a token. We will set the narrative. We will teach reporters what to look at: community-led stewardship, long-term maintenance, and the people who will live with this work."

Lucia Montrose regards you with that same calibrated attention.

Lucia Montrose: "Then we set the terms now. We pull a communications plan that includes community spokespeople, training for interviews, and shared bylines. Funding is conditional on an agreed framework that both protects and amplifies community voices."

[Outcome B: "You opt for an organic tour. The first few sentences are rough, but elders speak with a force that no press release can match. A small reporter records, breathless with the realness. Elias looks vulnerable but proud."]

The choice you make here is small in logistics but large in tone. You pick a way to show who you are.

That evening, a quieter room in City Hall holds the last conversation before you must decide the pilot's public face. Mateo sits between you and Lucia, his papers now rearranged into a kind of roadmap. The skylight diffuses the citylight into a soft, conciliatory glow.

[Scene: City Hall Council Chamber (small meeting room) | Evening]
[Visual: Low-angle shot through the skylight; the dais looms like a promise. Mateo's briefing papers are damp from the day's fieldwork.]
[Music: A warm, ascending piano motif]
[Sound: Faint distant city noise; a soft rain begins]

Councilor Mateo Qu: "If this pilot demonstrates what it promises, it becomes a template. The narrative you choose will inform not just how people see you, but what the council can justify funding."

Lucia Montrose folds her hands with the economy of someone used to making decisions under pressure.

Lucia Montrose: "I prefer a Beaconworks-led rollout with community oversight for efficiency. But it's not the only path; I can support strong co-branding if you deliver measured milestones on time."

Elias Harrow watches Lucia with a careful calm that hides an internal tally of consequences.

Elias Harrow: "I think a public platform that publishes results and invites civic input would build the most durable momentum. Transparency builds trust."

Rani Cho smirks, leaning back in her chair, nails still stained with ink and resin.

Rani Cho: "Transparent, tidy, or loud — pick your battle. But whatever you pick, make sure people know they're not being used."

Eda Nal reaches for your hand, the skin warm and reassuring.

Eda Nal: "Decide with the next fifty years in mind, not the next headline. The marsh remembers. We will teach it, and it will teach us back."

You look at those faces: Lucia's pragmatic clarity, Elias' quiet reason, Rani's blunt heart, Mateo's political arithmetic, Eda's patient knowledge. You feel a tide lift within you — not victory yet, but a steady upward pull. The pilot is a hinge. Whatever you choose about public framing will tilt the city's perception of your people and the shape of resources that follow.

Your impatience — the guilt that has pushed you into late nights and shouted meetings — whispers that waiting is a betrayal. But your years of organizing have also taught you that legitimacy built with patience endures.

You breathe. The room contracts on the question, and all the arguments make their case. Lucia offers funding paired with visibility. Elias offers the chance to make the project both open and scientifically replicable. Rani and Eda anchor you to the living people and living soil that will carry the pilot forward.

You feel Elias' hand find yours across the table, fingers curling with a trust that steadies rather than commands. You squeeze once, a small, private punctuation mark.

Mara Solenne: "We have to choose how we go to the public. That choice will define the pilot's storytelling, the community's dignity, and how fast we can get protective measures in the water."

Lucia Montrose: "Agreed. Decide quickly. Funding windows and political calendars move fast."

[Music: Swell of hopeful strings, then held at a high note — a moment of potential]

You look at the page in front of you, at the line where names can be printed, where logos can be placed, where the first public sentence about this work will be cast into the city like a net. You know the stakes. You are tired and bright at once. Partnership has multiplied your influence; now it asks you to spend it.
