You try to map the options mentally: visibility versus safety; an injunction that might buy time but depends on procedures; a committee that could buy breathing room but also be a velvet glove for co-optation.

Mayor Jonah Cruz meets you on the boardwalk with the careful stance of a man used to people measuring him. He is precise when he speaks—measured words, the politician's cadence—but his hands fidget with the brass pin of the town seal as if it keeps him tethered.

Mayor Jonah Cruz: "Maya. I can't promise everything. I'm trying to thread a needle between judges and jobs. If we push too far too fast, people could lose the grants they need, the phased funding. But I can call a temporary halt—request a joint committee to review—"

Maya Rios: "A temporary halt?" (You hold the question and the anger in the same breath.) "Temporary has been Aquila's favorite word since they came here. How temporary? Will there be teeth? And who sits on that committee—Aquila's engineers or our fishers?"

Mayor Jonah Cruz: (sighing) "That's why I say limited. Neutral mediators, legal observers, local representation. It's not perfect, but it buys time. Time to gather more evidence, to make the case without putting bodies on the line."

Ivy Okoye: "Time is what Aquila needs to set the narrative. Time lets them reshape the town in a model room and then tell us to accept the miniature. If we let them convene committees, we hand them the clock."

You watch Jonah's expression harden and then go soft—political calculus turning to personal fatigue.

Mayor Jonah Cruz: "Look, Ivy, I'm not naive. But if we can get a legal injunction—if the lawyers can argue undue haste—we can stop the machines from rolling on a judge's order. It won't be glamorous. It won't get cameras overnight, but it could keep the heavy equipment tied up until we have to make the larger case."

Eli Navarro: "Legal stalls take weeks. If the weather hits, it's not paperwork that'll stop a falling pier. And if people are camped on the waterfront when the forecast turns—"

Your chest tightens. You can hear the weather the way you used to—barometric clicks and a certain metallic smell that rises before wind. You know, scientifically, that a storm could erode newly placed fill overnight. You also know how the courthouse's slow gears grind—delays that turn into deadlines that slip.

You walk along the old pilings after the meeting. The place is a ruin and a memory both—where children used to leap into calmer summers, where fishermen mended nets and told stories. Now, it is a place of strategy and reckoning. Salt has eaten the wood, and gulls pick through what is left. The air tastes iron-bright, and your skin prickles with the sense that storm is coming whether you choose to fight it or negotiate with it.

Your mind cycles through the options like a machine testing scenarios. For each, you feel the tug of the town's faces: Rosa's children's drawings up in the schoolhouse, Eli's hands lined with oyster fingernails, Ivy's relentless energy, Jonah's tired diplomacy. The choice you make will not be a clean arrow to a single end; it will be a braid of consequences that pull others with them.

You imagine the night occupation: lamplight bobbing on tarps, cameras flashing, a raw image of community resolve splashed across national screens. You imagine the rain—slick ropes, collapsing tents, the risk of people slipping into freezing water as machines and policemen push to clear the scene. You also imagine the national sympathy, the pressure on Aquila's boardrooms when profit narratives collide with human faces.

You imagine the injunction: court filings, affidavits, lawyers with files and small victories in the form of delayed machinery. It feels safe in a certain bureaucratic way, but you can almost hear the slow drip of time—days becoming weeks, momentum leaking as press cycles move on.

You imagine the committee: a table with polished wood, Aquila's engineers across from your neighbors, the conversation slow and mediated. It could be a place to steer outcomes—if you have enough leverage. Or it could be where persuasive slides and glossy models seduce people into accepting half-measures.

Ivy Okoye: "We can't theatricalize our way out of this if the rain comes. But spectacle gets the eyeballs and moneybags move when cameras are on. We need to choose the pressure point. What do you think, Maya? You'll have to be the one to call it."

Maya Rios: (Your thoughts narrow until the world is a single compass of risk.) "We don't have the luxury of a perfect answer."

Ivy Okoye: "Then pick the best imperfect thing. Make it count."

Eli Navarro: "If people are going to stand in the rain, they need dry escape routes, shelter plans, medics. If it's legal, we need witnesses and fast affidavits. If it's a committee, we need non-negotiable items before we sit. Each path needs work. Each path needs people."

You close your eyes for a moment. The wind lifts the hem of your jacket and tugs at the hair behind your ear. Your mind is a ledger of costs and probabilities: arrests, legal fees, lost trust, lives at risk. The past—the miscalculated forecast that still hums inside you—feels like an old debt demanding payment in caution.

There is no elegant strategy that eliminates risk. There is only choosing which harms you're willing to accept in order to prevent a larger harm.

You look at the three faces around you—Eli's cautious steadiness, Ivy's incandescent drive, Jonah's weary pragmatism—and you feel the weight of their expectations settle over your shoulders like damp cloth.

Your throat tightens. You know what you must decide.
