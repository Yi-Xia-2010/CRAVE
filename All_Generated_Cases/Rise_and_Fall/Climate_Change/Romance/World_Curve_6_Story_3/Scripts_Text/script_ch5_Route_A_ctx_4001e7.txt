# Route: Route A — Ending: Bittersweet/True
# Context: 4001e7

[Scene: Living Dike | Dawn]
[Visual: Mist hovering over a ribbon of disturbed earth and coir rolls; the coir frays where the swell has kissed it. A row of sensor flags droops, some bent; one sensor housing gapes like a missing tooth.]
[Music: Low, mournful cello; a single clarinet note hangs]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a dull, repetitive thunk where piles meet soft mud; the faint hiss of last night's swell receding]
[Smell: Wet peat, brine, and diesel faintly from machines parked on the far bank]

You arrive before most people. Scaling fast was the promise you carried into the night—community labor, a hundred hands, salvaged funds—so the dike would grow before the weather could take more. The promise is still in your satchel; in your head it shivers with the same small flare of hope that got you through the weekend.

Up close, the living dike is ragged where it should be taut. The outer lip has a clean, terrible shear along three meters of the coir barrier. Where the marsh grass once threaded itself into the weave, there are torn clumps, root-balls splayed like open hands. Mud hangs from the roots; tiny amphipods scuttle in the exposed silt. The sensor flags you ordered integrated—strain gauges, salinity monitors meant to tell you when to hold or halt—stare blank, some still in their boxes on a pallet a little inland.

You taste metal in your mouth. The satchel presses at your hip; your fingers go to the sea-glass pendant out of a motion older than thought, a reflex for steadiness.

Maya Ibarra [soft, under your breath]: "This is worse than the photos."

Elliot Chen is already there, his trench coat damp at the collar, hands full of rolled plans. His face is pale, the kind of white that happens when someone who usually fixes things hears the sound of something breaking.

Elliot Chen: "We should have kept the teams smaller, staggered the pilings. The subcontractor—"

You cut him off with a look because words are thermal and will only add frost to the scene. You know how this reads: quick scale, subcontractors on overtime, a promise made to residents who were terrified. You also know the other line: delay is permission for the sea to keep taking.

[Outcome B: "You stay high and dry, pen scratching against your pad, translating what you see into measured words. Keeping distance gives you a narrow comfort—a way to catalog grief instead of swallowing it."]
[MERGE: Continue with main scene]

Noah Rhee arrives on the south path with a rope coil slung over his shoulder and the scent of old salt and smoked fish in his wake. He doesn't bother with pleasantries; the market doesn't let him. His eyes go to the torn lip and then to the pallet of unused sensors. He doesn't need all the technical terms to know what this means: the net returns shrink, the marsh tightens, livelihoods wobble.

Noah Rhee [hard]: "You rushed this. You promised we'd have our nets. Now half the marsh is gone and my crew's hauls are thin."

Maya Ibarra [first, quietly]: "We didn't rush to fail. We rushed because the storms kept getting closer, Noah—"

Noah Rhee: "You rushed because you believed a model. Models don't bring fish back to the boat."

Elliot Chen [interjecting, trying]: "Noah, the pilings—some subcontractor pushed through without waiting for the sensor mesh. That's on me for not being clearer in the contracting. We can—"

Noah Rhee [barks a laugh that turns to something like grief]: "Blame and apology won't fill the coolers, Elliot. They eat what the marsh gives them."

His words land heavy, and you feel your chest hollow. This is not a lecture about engineering; it's a ledger of days taken from people's hands. You understand that ledger too well; your father's empty chair is a line in it. Shame leaks into your throat, warm and personal. You had said scale, and scale had a price.

Rosa arrives with her camera slung and her yellow jacket sodden. She moves like she is absorbing heat by proximity—snapping photos, talking in clipped, bright sentences so the others can hear and her own voice stays steady.

Rosa: "I got shots of the shear. People are already posting. Tomas is down at the docks asking about compensation. The mayor's office called—says they're arranging a press line. Aria from Veridian wants to come in with 'rapid repairs' and a PR piece."

You can see the shape of it: Veridian's floodlights on the sand, concrete pilings ordered up like teeth. You can see the slick photo of you smiling next to a hard wall, the caption reading 'Port Veridian secures future'. You imagine marsh disappearing under concrete and that image tastes like iron.

Tomas arrives, slow and deliberate. He touches the exposed root-balls as if remembering how the old dike used to be—a stitched thing grown over decades.

Tomas [low]: "We planted this coast with hands, not machines. The rush will bruise more than it fixes if we don't look."

Elliot Chen [defensive]: "We planned for integration. We had a timeline. The subcontractor jumped because of a delivery window. I—"

Noah Rhee: "Delivery windows don't talk to fishermen, Elliot. We don't get paid to wait for bureaucracy."

Multi-turn friction curls through the air: you, Elliot, and Noah trying to compress different temporalities into a single narrative where everyone can meet. Your voice is a rope pulled between them.

Maya Ibarra: "We should have—" (your hands find and fold, always folding like a mollusk protecting a pearl) "We should have held tighter on the integration. I thought community labor and oversight would keep things honest. I thought—"

Noah Rhee [cutting]: "You thought. We fished."

He says it so simply the room bends. All your hands do is reach for what you can hold.

> Choice A: "Apologize to Noah openly, accept responsibility"

[Outcome A: "You look him in the face, let the apology out in the shape it takes—no hedging, a weighty 'I'm sorry.' It doesn't fix the nets, but it lowers the barbed wire for conversation to begin."]
[Outcome B: "You begin listing missed checkpoints and how to reinstate sensor checks. Numbers fall into the air. Noah listens, but his jaw is set; data is necessary but insufficient here."]
[MERGE: Continue with main scene]

The market at the docks smells of coffee and fish blood as midday slides in. Word has spread like spilled salt. People cluster in weathered jackets: some angry, some frightened. Conversations curl like smoke. A few fishermen lift thin returns from their cooler—shrimp the size of thumbs, a couple of sad flounder. A child tugs at a parent's sleeve and asks for the old tale of when the sea gave more.

Mayor Cortez arrives with a suit that reads 'practical'—navy wool, tailored with nautical buttons—and a face practiced in compromise. She stands on the edge of the scene and watches, cataloguing optics and potential grants.

Mayor Cortez: "We can get Veridian in for emergency remediation, and the optics will reassure the wider county. They'll fund temporary pay for impacted crews if we sign a rapid-repair clause."

Maya Ibarra [quiet, fraying]: "And the clause?"

Mayor Cortez: "It allows them to contract certain work directly, expedite permits. The county's pressure—"

Before she finishes, Aria Sol's name is already a long shadow. The corporate hand offers immediate relief with an unspoken line: in exchange for speed, oversight loosens. You see the straps of that trade—the steady, slow erosion that starts with 'temporary' and accrues like salt in the seams.

Elliot Chen steps next to you, voice low enough to be only for you.

Elliot Chen: "I can pull together the sensor team—retrofit what they missed. It'll take time. But if they pour concrete and anchor piles without our data, we lose the dike's living edge."

You think about the people standing around, weary and cold. The hunger for security is understandable; you don't fault them for wanting a wall that will keep the sea from their doorstep tonight. You fault yourself for promising a rapid scale without nails of contingency hammered in.

Rosa's camera shutter clicks steadily; she catches a fisherman folding his hands around a plastic crate as if in prayer. Her eyes find yours and stay there, unblinking.

Rosa: "This will go online. People need to see you're trying, Mai."

Her use of your childhood nickname is a hand on your shoulder you didn't know you needed.

Noah Rhee pulls you aside, voice lowering but not softened.

Noah Rhee: "If Veridian steps in, we get pay now. But you said this would be ours. You said the town would do it together. Which is it, Maya? Your town or their ledger?"

There's nothing theatrical about his question. It's a ledger balanced on a finger: trust vs. payment. You feel the scales tip.

You walk back toward the shear, fingers sweeping through the exposed mud where the roots once held. You think of all the times you insisted on 'community labor' because you didn't want to externalize the town's decisions. You wanted people to own the process—because ownership is tie and tie is care. Now ownership looks like blame.

> Choice A: "Call for a community meeting here, now, to decide as a group"
> Choice B: "Ask Mayor Cortez to arrange a closed negotiation with Veridian immediately"
[Outcome A: "You shout across the quay, your voice cracking at first but catching: 'Meeting—five minutes—by the boat ramp.' People shuffle; they come. For a moment, the decision tails into public ears and faces, and you feel the weight distributed, not carried alone."]
[Outcome B: "You step up to the mayor, asking for a quiet negotiation. She nods, fingers already flipping through her tablet. The negotiation will be efficient. It will keep the town out of the messy, messy public center—and it will be faster."]
[MERGE: Continue with main scene]

Elliot watches you as if measuring a tide. Tomas lingers, as if watching older tides in your choices. The sky lifts and lowers; gulls wheel in small, precise circles. Your stomach is a stone.

You imagine headlines: 'Living Dike Falters as South Swell Strikes'—or worse, 'Town Splits Over Repair Deal.' You imagine Aria in a photo op, Mayor Cortez smiling with a cheque, Noah's hands in a net and the marshline bleeding away.

You think of your father and of the small, stubborn way he taught you to mend nets: not to replace the whole, but to braid the broken strands back together. You think of Rosa's camera and of the community breakfasts after a long build, soups shared from a single pot, laughter like repair.

Shame thickens. The decision you made—to scale fast, to lean on people's resolve and hope—has worn a seam. You feel the tug where it might unravel the fabric you tried to stitch.

You step back from the shear and feel the town pulse around you: anger, fear, calculation, a hunger for shelter that might erase the marsh. The air tastes like an incoming tide: charged and inevitable.

You reach for the satchel, for the prints, the notes, the sensor logs that now matter more than ever. Your fingers close around paper and plastic and, beneath them, the small, steady glass of your pendant.

The day is not ended, but the decision that will define the next swing of the town's fate is pressing close like a swell on the horizon—too near to ignore and too complex to resolve in a single breath.
