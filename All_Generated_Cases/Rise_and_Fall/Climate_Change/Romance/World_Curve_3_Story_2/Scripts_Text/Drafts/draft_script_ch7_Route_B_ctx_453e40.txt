### DRAFT
# Route: Ending


[Scene: Niko's Boatyard | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm late-afternoon light through rafter gaps; wood shavings float in a lazy dust-mote ballet]
[Sound: The steady rasp of a plane against oak, a gull calling from far off, a drain of rain starting like a quiet promise]
[Music: Soft, hopeful strings with a low, resonant cello underpinning]

You follow Niko without meaning to watch your feet. The boardroom’s fluorescent chill still sits in the back of your throat, like an oyster shell caught on your tongue. But here — among hulls half-finished and the smell of tar and varnish — the air is an old language you remember how to read.

Niko's hands are the first thing you notice: knuckled, charcoal-streaked, steady as tide-marks. They tap a bench once and gesture toward the space beside them. Their carved wooden locket catches a sliver of sun and throws it like a small promise across the plank floor.

Niko: "Sit. You'll get oil on your new jacket."

You (Amina Reyes): "I haven't got anything new left to ruin." (You let out a breath that tastes like salt and papers.)

Niko watches you for a long beat, the look complex—equal parts mistrust and an old, roughened affection. You know what they mean when they call you "the scientist who talks too much to lawyers." You also know the reason you didn't walk into a council chamber and shout is because someone has to build things that last.

Niko: "So we're actually doing this, then. Not just talking about it."

You (Amina Reyes): "Not just talking." (You flip open your waterproof notebook. The pages whisper under your thumb; tide gradients bloom in blue ink.) "If we start with oyster reefs at the shoals and stagger sediment traps along the flow corridors, we can—" (You trace the lines.) "—slow the surge, trap fine sediments, help the marsh migrate. The channels can be cleared and reshaped so fishing lanes stay viable. It's labor and timing, not permits."

Niko's jaw tenses, a map of someone used to repairing splinters and storms rather than arguing with bylaws. You sense their skepticism like the pressure before rain.

Niko: "Sounds like a sermon. Who's paying for the sermon?"

You (Amina Reyes): "We might not get money from the county. Not at first. We get elbow grease, knowledge, and people's time. We start small — bundles of eelgrass, oyster cages tied off to new pilings, crew rotations. We keep the boats moving."

Niko snorts softly, but the sound is lighter than a dismissal. They slide a well-worn multi-tool across the bench to you with a flat, businesslike motion.

Niko: (softening) "Show me."

You hold the multi-tool for a second like it's a baton, an instrument to marshal a new kind of chorus. Your thumb presses the leather strap of the notebook; the friendship bracelet tucked into the spine rubs a memory against your palm, a small hot ache that steadies you instead of breaking you.

Niko watches your face as if trying to read tide-levels there, and you feel, for the first time since the vote, the slow unknotting of something inside you. It's small — not victory, not even a promise of it — but a spark: work you can touch, people who will come with you, a plan laid on wood and rope rather than on paper.

Niko: "You know none of the kids here grew up thinking about 'sediment traps' as poetry."

You (Amina Reyes): (a dry smile) "Maybe we can make it poetry. Or at least practical metaphors."

Niko: (a corner of their mouth quirks) "You always did try to make everything sound like a lab workshop."

You laugh, a short, surprised sound. The laugh wedges between you like a plank, steadying the small boat of this conversation.

Niko reaches into a crate and tosses you a thin coil of nylon line, salt drying along its edges.

Niko: "Right. We do the cages, we check currents, we train people how to seed oysters without killing their lunch. We'll need lists, shifts, a chain of folks willing to stand in a rain that isn't yet theirs."

You (Amina Reyes): "I'll draw the schedules. I'll map tidal windows and volunteer shifts. I'll—" (You stop when the image rises in your head: your brother hauling sacks of seed oysters into a skiff. It is a quiet, blinding thing you haven't let yourself keep.)

Niko watches that look, the one that folds your gaze inward.

Niko: (quiet) "You okay?"

You (Amina Reyes): (softly) "I am. This—this is how I remember him. Not just the night that took him, but him lifting something heavy because it needed lifting. If we can make that count, then—" (You press your fingers to the notebook. The page catches, the ink smelling faintly of the field office.) "—then it won't be only grief."

Niko's expression shifts—there's a tension you can't read fully, so you label it complex and let it be. They nod once, solid.

Niko: "Then let's make a thing that holds."

[Visual: Montage — hands knotting oyster cages, a dinghy being rigged, close-up of a callus nicked by a plane, a child's small fingers pressing eelgrass into mud]
[Sound: Laughter, small barking instructions, the soft slap of water against a hull]
[Music: Swells into warmer strings; a quickening of hope]

You and Niko set to work. The drafts are rough at first: sketches on the back of plywood, nails hammered with rhythmic patience, lists shouted over the scrape of sanded timber. When you outline the idea of sediment traps, Niko interrupts with a snagged memory of an old family method for directing currents around a keel. The two languages braid — boatwright lore making sense of scientific models, and models giving structure to lore.

Niko: "If we anchor the first reef here, and angle the traps this way, the flow should drop turbidity and let the cordgrass take. You'd want more reef density in spring, right?"

You (Amina Reyes): "Yes. And stagger depth. If we space them with gaps for juvenile fish and eel movement, the fishing lanes won't choke. We can use reclaimed pallets and local stone for the first frames."

Niko: (a laugh, incredulous) "Only you would say 'reclaimed pallets' and 'habitat connectivity' in the same breath."

You (Amina Reyes): "It's called multitasking."

The day runs into evening and you walk the work with Niko: measuring, correcting, planning shifts. Somewhere between the sound of a plane smoothing a plank and Niko's low humming as they carve a sign, you feel the edge of something you haven't felt since leaving school: belonging more complicated than nostalgia, practical and warm and stubborn.

[Scene: Rooftop Community Garden / Evacuation Shelter | Evening]
[Visual: A converted rooftop, rows of raised beds under a pewter sky; students clustered around Marina like a flock gathering]
[Sound: The murmur of young voices, trowels scraping soil, the distant roll of thunder]
[Music: Gentle, uplifted piano; the cello from earlier grounding the chord progression]

Marina appears with a flurry of small bodies and a stack of hand-lettered signs: "SEED DAY," "PLANTS = FUTURE," "MARSH GUARDIANS." Her braid is threaded with shells; she moves with the quick surety of someone who can corral hope into action.

Marina: "You made it! I told them we had the scientist in our corner and they'd stop asking me if 'science' is a person."

You (Amina Reyes): "Only sometimes. Mostly I make graphs that scare people into action."

Marina: (teasing) "Graphs scare me, but you showed up with a toolbox and Niko, and that's half the battle."

You (Amina Reyes): "Niko brought more than a toolbox."

Marina: "No kidding. They brought stories, too. The kids love that."

You hand out seedlings — the cordgrass culms are small and bright in your palm, so fragile and so defiantly green against the dim sky. The kids' hands are quick and earnest; one child names a shoot "Marisol" and you almost cry because the name sounds like a promise.

Multi-turn and earnest, your conversation with Marina tangles policy and practice. She speaks of the classroom — of the kids who asked if planting could be homework — while you map volunteer rotations on the back of a cereal box. There's friction, too: the budget is a whisper that keeps finding its way into sentences.

Marina: "How many cages can we realistically build before the storm season?"

You (Amina Reyes): "A handful to start. Enough that the pilot shows measurable accretion in six months. We document every step — photos, sediment cores, volunteer logs. If we can show community labor gives outcomes, we can crowdsource modest funding and maybe catch a grant geared to 'community resilience.'"

Marina: (hopeful) "And if we can't get the grant?"

You (Amina Reyes): (firm) "We show up anyway. We use what we have. We make the case in pieces that are hard to ignore."

The rooftop feels like a small bastion. String lights hang, already beginning to glow, and you watch a child tuck a seedling into soil with a solemnity way too big for their hands. The sky grows grayer, the thunder nearer. Rain is coming — the sort that marks budgets as flimsy, that turns hand-painted signs into mush. A part of you counts the minutes like a tide.

[Visual: Close-up of your notebook; fingerprints around inked tide gradients; the friendship bracelet tucked in the spine gleams faintly]
[Sound: Rain coming on stronger, first heavy drops spattering the rooftop boards]
[Music: A soft swell, resolving into a hopeful cadence]

You make lists, you measure, you laugh at Niko's jokes about bureaucrats, you teach a child to tie a knot so small it feels sacred. The movement — messy, hand-made, vulnerable — sews itself into the town's fabric through hands and hours. Without institutional funding, every plank and planted cordgrass is an act of fragile courage; and yet, as you stamp names into volunteer rosters, you feel a steady heat behind your ribs: purpose.

You think of your brother again — not in the sharpness of loss, but in the slow insistence of memory that asks you to do something lasting. The plan doesn't erase sorrow, but it gives sorrow an action.

Niko: (quiet, standing at your shoulder) "You look like you're going to start crying."

You (Amina Reyes): (half-laugh) "I might. Or I might start hammering. Either way, it's working."

Niko: (a small, genuine smile) "Good. Keep both in reserve. You'll need them."

The rain turns the wooden walkways slick, and lights inside town blink on like small promises. The clients who poured cold certainty into the boardroom are a distant memory for the night; here, hands and rope and mud argue back.

You gather the community's plans into your notebook. Lists, annotated diagrams, a scrawled schedule for seed-planting days. The rooftop garden hums with the energy of people making things, and for the first time since the vote, you believe that small, steady acts can add up to something that matters.

[Visual: Wide shot of Marisol from the rooftop — patchwork lights, the marsh dark and breathing beyond, rain streaking into the horizon]
[Music: Quiet hopeful chord held into a gentle silence]

Beyond the rooftop, the marsh breathes and waits. The storm-season calendar still looms; the need for broader support remains. But you leave the rooftop with mud on your boots and a list of names penciled into the spine of your notebook. You carry more than plans: you carry faces, promises, and a growing sense that hands can outlast a vote.

You stand at the edge of the rooftop, the town spread below like a stitched map. The rain has washed the air clean enough to see the faintest line where sea meets sky. For now, this is enough — work you can do, people who will turn up, a living shore that might breathe again.

[Visual: Cut to close-up on your hand splaying over the tide-gradient sketch; the pen hovers as if over a new map]
[Sound: The rain recedes to a soft sigh]
[Music: A single rising piano motif; then a resolved, warm chord]

You feel the smallest spark of purpose turning into a steady ember. It will need tending. It will need more hands. It will need time.
