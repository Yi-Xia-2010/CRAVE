[Scene: Rooftop Community Garden & Storm Shelter | Sunset]
[Visual: Golden-hour light drapes over raised beds; salvaged windbreaks flutter. Strings of solar fairy-lights are just waking. The town has gathered in a loose semicircle around the dais; old wood and patched tarps smell faintly of sea and rain. A soft breeze carries the metallic tang of salt—and the low murmur of conversation.]
[Music: Warm acoustic guitar with a steady, hopeful rhythm]

You step up to the dais feeling the give of weathered boards under your boots. Your wire-frame glasses fog a fraction at the edges—small, human proof that you're here, not behind a lab pane. You push them up with a thumb, and the salt air slices the fog away, leaving clarity and the taste of brine at the back of your teeth.

You can see them: Mayor Ana with the practiced neutrality of someone who has said "we'll see" to too many storms; Rosa leaning against a crate, paint-splattered overalls catching the light; Dr. Lian checking a tiny handheld sensor as if it were a talisman; Old Tomas folded into his chair, attentive in that slow, dangerous way elders reserve for things that matter. Kaito stands a little off-center, fingers still faintly rough from rope and coil; when he meets your eyes his smile is quick and tight, part hope and part plea.

Your journal—battered, sea-streaked—rests under your arm. You tighten the braided shell bracelet at your wrist without thinking, letting the familiar weight anchor you. This is the same tightening you did in the greenhouse: not a knot of fear so much as a bracing.

You inhale. The rooftop smells like wet soil, warm wood, and diesel from a distant work-boat. The light paints everyone's faces gold and honest.

Marin Solé: "Thank you all for coming. I—" (you clear your throat; the sound is steady) "—we've been working with Dr. Lian, Rosa, and volunteers from the co-op to test marsh plantings and modular berms in the Boardwalk Floodplain. The pilot shows promise: increased sediment capture, preserved lanes for the small boats, and volunteer brigades that can maintain the structures seasonally."

Applause rolls like a small swell—few hands, but sincere. Your voice carries better than you expected. The plan you sketched in the greenhouse stands here now as words and people and a green patchwork behind you.

Mayor Ana: "You make a good case, Marin. Concise, measurable. The council appreciates the work."

Mayor Ana's voice is weary but fair. She folds her hands, the motion as if sealing a note. There's gratitude there, but you can feel the calculus: funding deadlines, public pressure, and the thin ledger where hope must sometimes be weighed.

Kaito Navarro steps forward, voice rough with seaside practice.

Kaito Navarro: "We all see the ideas, Marin. My only worry—" (he gestures, knuckles showing old rope burns) "—is that any plan that fences the water off will choke the pier. People need reliable access. Kids need the tide pools. We can't trade our season for a promise."

You nod; you expected Kaito's worry and you share it. This project was always meant to be a bridge, not a wall.

Marin Solé: "I hear you. The hybrid design keeps channels open. The berms are modular—there are gaps, maintained by brigades. Boats can pass. We measure access as a primary metric, not an afterthought."

Kaito Navarro: "Primary, not afterthought—good. Keep saying that."

There's a swell of murmurs—people voicing support, raising concerns in the same breath. The reaction warms the room; it feels like compromise in motion, like a net being woven. You feel your chest ease.

Dr. Lian Obasi steps forward with a slate of numbers. Her quick, exact voice knits the story tighter.

Dr. Lian Obasi: "Our sensors recorded a 22 percent increase in sediment deposition over the pilot month where marsh plugs were installed. Turbidity decreased during managed tides. Those are not just good signs—those are measurable trajectories toward resilience."

She scans the crowd, then you. Her eyes are bright, and when she says 'we' you feel the force of collective work behind the data.

Rosa: "And the volunteer rota is already half-organized. I can get the school kids involved for weekend planting. It's not just you, Marin—this town shows up."

Rosa's grin is bright and contagious; it makes your shoulders drop in a way that is almost childish in its relief. Her faith is the kind that makes plans happen.

There is movement at the rooftop edge. The air shifts. Aria Chen arrives with the quiet, efficient authority of polished steel. Her coat catches the light; the TideLine cuff glints like a promise you can bend to your palm.

Aria Chen: "Good evening. I'm glad to see such community engagement." (she lifts her smart slate with a practiced wrist) "TideLine's prototype barriers offer immediate reduction in flooding risk—measurable, rapid, and scalable. We can secure the funding now and implement across vulnerable stretches within six months."

Her words are smooth and bright; there is a crispness to them that smells of clean rooms and glossy reports. Jun Park stands a little behind her, hands fidgeting with a handheld comm, face the color of someone carrying questions he is not yet allowed to voice.

You study Aria's slate—a rendered sea wall, sleek and efficient. The projection is seductive: calm water, a neat line between town and tide. It promises speed. It promises numbers.

Aria Chen: "We don't have to choose nights of compromise. We can have immediate protection and meet regulatory benchmarks. The contract comes with technical oversight and monitored performance metrics."

A ripple of practical nods runs through a corner of the crowd—people who think in budgets and deadlines. You feel the tug. Fast funding can keep roofs over heads through the coming season. It can keep the mayor’s office ceilings dry for months.

Mayor Ana folds the contract's printed cover in her hands like a stone.

Mayor Ana: "TideLine's offer is tempting. It guarantees funding that could shore up homes before the next storm season. But—" (she looks at you, the weight of the city hall ledger on her brow) "—I want this meeting to be honest. Marin, give us your recommendation."

You reach for your notes, but the important parts are already braided into your voice. You remember the greenhouse light, the marsh plugs, the volunteers with mud on their knees. You remember Rosa's insistence that access not be a footnote.

Marin Solé: "My recommendation is a hybrid-first approach. Prioritize the pilot that centers marsh restoration and community-managed modular barriers, and—" (you let that 'and' hang like a bridge) "—design any tech work to be adaptive, community-controlled, and reversible. Funding matters. Speed matters. But so does who gets to shape how we live on our shore."

Your words land. A small cheer rises—soft but sincere. You feel a warmth flood you from the crowd, not just approval but alignment. This is the peak you have been working toward: science, community practice, and the smell of soil making a public argument together.

Aria Chen steps forward, not with malice but with the confident economy of her firm.

Aria Chen: "We can make those things happen at scale, Marin. Community control is possible within our governance models. We have clauses—" (she taps her slate) "—that ensure local oversight and maintenance training. The difference is speed and scope."

Jun Park looks at her and then at the crowd; his fingers stop fidgeting. For a moment you see something human flicker—maybe doubt, maybe the small kindness of someone who knows a work contract can chew up more than it pays for.

Jun Park: "There are ways to tailor the clauses. TideLine is open to editing contract language—" (he glances at Aria) "—within limits. We can discuss."

Kaito Navarro folds his arms, worry layered with a stubborn stripe of hope.

Kaito Navarro: "Limits are the tricky part. Once the line's in the sand, who moves it? The pier is old but it's ours. We don't want a fence that says 'no.'"

Old Tomas chuckles, and it's not mocking—it's the gravelly noise of someone who has seen a hundred fixes come and go.

Old Tomas: "Promises get polished. The sea remembers longer."

The room reacts—some with applause, some with uneasy silence. You sense momentum and also the pull of consequence. This is where plans either braid together or fray.

You step forward and offer a small, concrete image—something people can hold.

Marin Solé: "Imagine a two-year plan: year one, pilot marsh corridors and modular berms in the floodplain with community brigades trained and funded; year two, scale with assessable metrics. If TideLine wants to partner, we make them sign a covenant that ensures access corridors and community governance, with independent audits."

The phrase "independent audits" lands with a particular sound—authority, process, time. People nod. It complicates the timeline but preserves dignity and access.

[MERGE: Both outcomes return to the main discussion.]

Mayor Ana exhales. The sunset has cooled to the long, honest pink that makes faces glow.

Mayor Ana: "This council needs to know the town's voice. I propose a vote—tonight. We can accept TideLine's early fast-funding contract as a fallback, pending conditions, or we can proceed with Marin's hybrid-first pilot relying on local funders and the grant process. A single vote will set the immediate course."

The murmur rises into a hum of urgency. It's not panic—mid-level arousal—just the electrical charge of stakes hitting decision velocity. You feel it, like the moment a tide turns and the rhythm changes.

You look at the faces around you. Rosa's jaw is set—not dogmatic, but ready. Kaito watches you with that pleading steadiness. Dr. Lian's fingers tighten around her notes. Aria's posture is impeccably composed, like a machine calibrated to persuasion. Jun's mouth opens, then closes. The rooftop seems to inhale.

Your chest tugs with very real hope. This is the moment you imagined in the greenhouse when you wrote Bridge. Listen. Protect. The community's shoulders are squared as if they are preparing to lift something together.

You could reach for the microphone and tell everyone to vote no to TideLine's immediate contract, to push the hybrid plan forward. You could advise a conditional agreement, to buy time and resources. You could ask for delay and an independent audit. Each choice is an act of care with different flavors of risk.
