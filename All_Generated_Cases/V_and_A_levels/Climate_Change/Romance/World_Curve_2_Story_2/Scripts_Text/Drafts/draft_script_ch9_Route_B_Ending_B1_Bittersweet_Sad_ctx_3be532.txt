### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Ending B1 (Bittersweet/Sad)


[Scene: New Promenade / Seawall Ribbon-Cutting | Midday]
[Visual: A broad, gleaming seawall of poured concrete and laminated glass rises where the old boardwalk used to spool. Camera drones circle like impatient gulls; a banner flaps—NEW ASTER RESILIENT COAST—brass letters bright against salt-streaked sky.]
[Music: Triumphant brass overdubbed with an urgent, high-tempo string ostinato]
[Sound: Reporters' murmurs, camera shutters, a ceremonial scissor-cut amplified over a speaker]

You feel the signature you pressed into municipal paper as if it's a physical seam under your skin. The ink has dried, but the pressure hasn't released. You are here because you chose motion—because, in a greenhouse lit once by seedlings and rain, you folded your throat around a decision that made things move. The seawall is the proof of that movement: precise joints, a promenade glass enough to see your face in, and a set of concession stands that smell faintly of new varnish and coffee.

Livia stands on the low dais like an island built of posture and planning. Her trench is immaculate; the wave-brooch glints exactly as it should. She speaks into the microphones with the cadence of someone who learned to make complex trade-offs sound like inevitabilities.

Livia Chen: "This wall is for every child who should not have to measure the tide to see if their home will be there tomorrow. It's for jobs, for safety, and for the future of New Aster."

Her voice is warm and practiced; the crowd answers with measured, relieved applause—some genuine, some performative. Cameras eat the moment like small birds swallowing the last of the light. You watch the room filling with photos of a safer town and feel the air thin.

You step closer after the speech, notebook in your hand because habits die slower than towns. The page is blank, and for a beat it is easier to let the page be blank than to name the people who will lose anchorage while the ledger gains columns.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand—a smudge of concrete dust on your thumb; the sea pressing at the glass edge below]
[Sound: Distant honk of a delivery truck, the muffled creak of folding chairs]

Eli finds you at the edge of the crowd—he's moved through the press the way he always moves through things, hands open, carrying the workman's gait like a stubborn stubbornness. His eyes are lidded with tiredness; the rest of his face does the work his eyes try to hide. He has a small canvas duffel at his feet; his jumpsuit is folded inside someone else's laundry basket already, passed along with a muttered joke like contraband.

Eli: (soft) "You heard her. It's—" He stops, searching for the word that won't make him sound small. "It'll be steady work for a lot of people."

You: "Steady work," you repeat, hearing the edges. "And steady displacement."

Eli's jaw tightens. He looks at the seawall, then at the promenade, then at you, as if trying to find which of you is the true shore.

Eli: "I—" (he exhales sharp and fast) "I know what this buys. I know what it costs. I tried to make things that could keep everyone—" His hand gestures, helpless and then emphatic. "It wasn't enough. The grant money, the contracts—it's bigger than what we could do in the Arbor."

You: "So what do we have now? A safer map and fewer people on it."

Eli: "It's not that simple, Mara." (He steps closer, voice pitched low, urgent.) "I can't make something out of nothing. I tried to do right. You know I did."

Your throat feels like it's been packed with gravel. You want to tell him you know, and that that knowledge is a poor substitute for the smell of his paint and the way he figured new bolts out with ragged fingers. You want to tell him you don't know. You say both.

You: "You did what you could, Eli. But the people who could keep living with their boats—some of them are gone. Rafi's boy packed at dawn. Grandma Hira's nephew took the last crate of gear and wept on the pier."

Eli swallows. He laughs once, a short thing that doesn't reach his eyes.

Eli: "I folded my jumpsuit into someone else's hands this morning. Told them they'd have first crack at my tools. Is that—" (he jerks his chin at the promenades and the plaque) "—enough? Is that redemption? Is that even dignity?"

Your hands curl around the notebook until the paper crimps. He looks like he's about to ask you to come with him—to leave with him—but then he chokes on the words and instead sets his duffel down.

Eli: "I'm going to try—somewhere else. Maybe the city. Maybe a co-op. I don't know. I can't—" He stops and his voice goes small, a child voice under the noise. "I can't stay and watch my friends priced out."

You: "Eli—"

He reaches for your hand; you take it because it's the last honest gesture left between you in this crowd of curated progress. His fingers are callused and still smell faintly of salt and engine oil. He squeezes once, like a promise or an apology.

Eli: "I'll write. If I find work that matters, I'll come back and help—we can still—"

You: "We can try." The words come out needing belief as much as they offer it.

[Visual: The crowd recedes; Eli turns and walks through the new plaza as if crossing a border. The promenade's glass reflects him small and thin; the sea beyond glitters like a thing concreted over]

You watch him recede until the crowd and the ceremony reassemble around a different center—the mayor, a ribbon cut into neat arcs—and you feel the scrape of public congratulation like sandpaper along your palms.

[Scene: New Promenade / Alleyways Below Promenade | Afternoon]
[Visual: Where sunlight should be there is instead shadowed under the promenade. New retail units line the walkway; below, the old alleyways are cordoned with 'REZONED' signs. The fishermen's sheds crouch like animals waiting to be told their fate.]
[Sound: Faint radio chatter from construction crews, the hollow echo of a jackhammer further down; a child's distant wail]

The formal applause fades but the consequences are louder. Rent posting notices, digital displays listing 'AVAILABLE UNITS'—prices that read like a taking. Men with clipboards and tight suits walk by with the air of appraisers; their steps are the sort you can hear even when they're polite.

Grandma Hira appears at the edge of the crowd like a memory stepping into a new frame. She smells like lavender and boiled seaweed; her shawl is a map of repairs. Her eyes are bright and terrible.

Grandma Hira: (quiet, not looking at Livia) "They've made the sea polite, child. They put a fence where we used to argue with it. Who will hum at dawn if they sell the sheds and the boats and replace our voices with places to hang lanterns for visitors?"

You: "They said—" (you falter) "—they said safety. And jobs."

Grandma Hira: (turning to you, not unkind) "There's a place for jobs. There's a place for safety. There's also a place for remembering how to whittle a rope and mend a sail. When remembering is priced into an exhibit, it becomes novelty."

Her voice lands like a pebble thrown into your sternum. It skips and bruises.

[Visual: Close-up on your notebook again. You flip to a page with a small pressed piece of seaweed—a memory, now awkward between lines of planning and lists.]
[Sound: A low, almost inaudible hum as the city's new lighting system powers on; the hum feels, for a moment, like judgment]

You walk the new plaza alone later as the crowd thins. Food trucks hum; a band plays cover songs that have lost the teeth of their original anger. The promenade is clean; the rainwater drains into channels that boast engineering lines in the stone. Plaques with bullet points about 'community benefits' are set like apologies in bronze.

A reporter asks you for a quote. You give one that sounds like it belongs to someone who has been schooled in compromise—precise, careful, watchful of legal lines. It reads better on camera than it does in your stomach.

[Scene: Private Tent Behind the Dais | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A small, utilitarian tent with a folding table and bottled water—where politicians and press flit in for private confabs. Livia is there, hands folded, hair smoothed back. Her face is blanking itself into composure when you enter.]
[Music: Minimal piano—each note tight as a held breath]
[Sound: The distant fade of the crowd; the nearer sound of the seawall's pumps running like a lung]

You and Livia are alone for the first time since the applause. The tent smells faintly of coffee and industrial-grade fabric softener. Her eyes are tired in a way you haven't seen; she unfastens one cuff and rubs at it with a thumb.

Livia Chen: (soft, unguarded) "Mara. I—" (she stops, recalibrates) "I know people are hurting. I knew there would be displacement. We proposed relocation assistance. We opened a fund. We—"

You: "You opened funds. You rezoned. You hired management firms. You called the reports 'development metrics'."

Livia: (one corner of her mouth tries to be a smile and fails) "Yes. Because if we did nothing, New Aster would be—what? Sunk? Stripped of infrastructure? The projections were brutal. The models demanded a scale that our piecemeal projects couldn't satisfy. I did the math, Mara. I did what the town needed on paper."

You: "On paper."

Her fingers drum on the table, anxious punctuation.

Livia: "I didn't want people to lose homes. I thought, foolishly, that I could shepherd the process. I thought I could make a plan that kept our heritage and brought funding. I misjudged—some of the market effects, some of the speed. There were people I thought we could protect who slipped through the safety net."

Her voice breaks like a thin thing. The practiced façade cracks and reveals, briefly, the person behind the platform—a person who took a hard, political route and now sees the human scatter.

Livia: "I misjudged the harm. I'm sorry."

You stare at the word. The sound of architected sorrow. The admission lands and fractures you.

You: "Sorry for the models?" you ask, and the bitterness cuts. "Sorry for the policy? Sorry for Rafi's boy sleeping in his truck? Sorry for Eli leaving with no fixed return?"

Livia looks down—humiliation or grief, you're not sure. When she raises her eyes again, there's a terrible clarity there.

Livia: "I will try to do better where I can. I will press for displacement funds to be real and immediate. I'll—" She searches for a thing big enough to balance losses. "I will add covenants. I'll change contracts. Tell me where to sign and I will sign."

You: "You helped start the process that priced them out. You offered legal patches now, after the fact. The seawall isn't a neutral tool—it's a lever."

Livia: (leaning forward) "Tell me how to use the lever without collapsing the market."

Her question is not rhetorical; it's hunger in a public form. You could give her a blueprint. You could name the concessions that would actually make a difference: real subsidies, rent control measures, community land trust covenants. But the very existence of the seawall accelerates capital in ways that contracts can only partially arrest.

You feel rage flare and then drown in a more precise grief—the kind that looks like math and policy but smells like empty sheds and packed boats.

[Visual: The sun descends and the promenade lights blink on, each one a small star in a newly smoothed sky]
[Music: A single high violin note sustains, then warps into a low, aching drone]
[Sound: The distant thud of heavy doors—someone is moving out, somewhere below; the scrape of packing crates]

You stand in the plaza after the event has packed away its banners and speeches. The crowd is gone. The sculpted wave-bench catches the salt light and throws it back in a way that makes everything look like a photograph. You flip your notebook open and write the word: 'CURATOR' across the top of the page. The word looks both right and ridiculous.

You think of every time you'd chosen inclusion over spectacle, of every single, ragged vote to repair rather than replace. You think of the haunted praise for being 'practical' and 'realistic'—how those words can be used like knives to shave memory off a town. The seawall stands; its surface is smooth and unblemished. It keeps the metrics low and the insurers calm. It keeps the bigger picture safe in spreadsheets.

You breathe in salted air, and it tastes like a bargain half-kept.

A message buzzes on your wristband—Eli: "Got to the ferry. Can't talk long. Love you." Short. Clinical. Human.

You fold the notebook closed. It snaps with the small, decisive sound of a life compartmentalized. You have done something that made the town safer in measure and emptier in memory. The cost is an ache that will not be vaccinated by better statistics.

You touch the sea-glass pendant at your throat—a child-thing, a talisman—and for a moment the weight of it is the only honest thing you can find. You forgive Livia in a legal sense, because forgiveness is sometimes a pragmatic tool that lets you keep working. You do not forgive in the soft way that erases loss; that kind of undoing is reserved for storms and stories that we haven't been given.

The promenade hums under your feet like a machine that has been wound and now runs on.

[Visual: Wide shot of New Aster from above—the seawall a hard crescent against the sea; the town rearranged into tidy blocks; a few boats still at a pier, but fewer than before]
[Music: A low, sustained chord resolves into silence]

You close your notebook and head down the new steps toward the alleys that were rezoned. You will do the work you can—covenants, petitions, community funds, midnight meetings—but you carry the knowledge of what was traded. The town is safer in the way numbers can be safe. It is poorer in the only ways numbers cannot count: the names that walked away, the songs that are slower to be sung, the hands that no longer know how to splice rope.

You pause at the old boardwalk's edge—where the sea used to come up into conversation—and you make yourself a promise you cannot fully keep: to try to stitch the missing parts back, even if you know some things cannot be sewn whole again.

[Visual: Close-up on your face in the glass of the seawall—reflected, doubled; for a moment your eyes fill with the exact, bright salt of unshed tears]
[Sound: The faint, stubborn cry of a gull]

You walk away.

[Music: A single piano pulse, then a soft, descending cluster]
[Visual: Fade to Black]

[ENDING]
