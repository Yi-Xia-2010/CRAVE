## Visual Novel Title
Tidebreak Promise

---

## World View
New Aster is a small coastal town in the near future (circa 2038) where salt, wind and politics shape every choice. Rising seas and sharpened storm seasons have turned ordinary storms into town-altering events; the harbor, reclaimed wetlands and a patchwork of resilient infrastructure form the physical skeleton of the place. Economically, the town teeters between legacy fisheries, small craft businesses and promises of outside investment. Politically, a council led by Livia Chen champions centralized, large-scale development that promises jobs and a robust seawall — at the cost of cultural zones and fragile ecosystems. Community leaders, craft workshops, and grassroots scientists push for decentralized, maintainable solutions that keep residents in place. Religion and organized faith are backgrounded, while a strong culture of place-based memory — elders like Grandma Hira and lifelong fishermen like Rafi — inform local ethics.

Geography alternates between ragged stone harbor, ruined boardwalks, and productive communal spaces (the Arbor greenhouse, Tidewatch lighthouse rooftop gardens, and a modest estuary research lab built on pilings). The atmosphere is salt-scented, constantly cloud-streaked, and sometimes brutal when sunbreaks scorch repaired masonry. Visual style is painterly coastal realism: textured cel-shaded characters in weathered, salvaged fashion and neon accents on wearable tech. Conflict centers on trade-offs — short-term economic stability vs. ecological and cultural continuity — and is humanized by romance: choices to protect others or chase individual safety mirror policy decisions.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mara Kestrel) return to New Aster to help design resilient, community-centered measures. Early discovery of troubling data sets the stakes: sea levels and a new storm modeling scenario make the council's proposal look urgent. Livia Chen offers a centralized high-tech seawall and a mixed-use development; Elias "Eli" Rowan and I push for modular, community-maintainable infrastructure. Tensions mount at a public council hearing; my decision there splits the story into three primary routes (A: grassroots resistance, B: align with institutional development, C: brokered compromise). Each route contains further hard choices: tactical activism, political maneuvering, or secret bargains that peel back loyalties. Romance with Eli threads through each route — our closeness becomes a lens for trust and failure. The emotional arc is a tragedy (Fall): as the story progresses, mistakes, betrayals, and the inevitability of climate impacts accumulate, leading most routes to severe loss. Some outcomes are bittersweet, a few end in isolation or ruin. Thematically, the narrative interrogates whether pragmatic sacrifice for the many justifies the cultural erasure of the few, and whether love can withstand the moral compromises climate crises demand.

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I arrive at New Aster Harbor, carrying my battered leather field notebook and sea-glass pendant. The town is both familiar and ragged: patched wooden piers, new concrete plates, the hiss of surf, gulls circling above a paint-splattered boat. I meet Rafi at dawn; his tanned, wiry silhouette and oilskin apron smell of diesel and kelp. Eli is there too, his sandy blond hair windblown, a salvaged drone remote looped on a braided cord. We walk the boardwalk to the Arbor, where seedlings and recycled solar panels catch the thin light. I set up in the Estuary Research Lab, calibrate the solar-charged wristband that hums on my wrist, and run the first tidal model of the season. The numbers are worrying but not yet catastrophic: small increases in return periods, shifting flood lines that nudge into familiar neighborhoods. My chest tightens — the scientist in me catalogues variables while the child who remembers the old ruin feels the weight of memory. I tell Eli I’m home to help: he grins, safety goggles cocked on his head. Hope and urgency sit side by side like tide-wet rope on the pier.
