[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse Lab | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Rain-traced glass panes; rows of plant trays glisten; the city skyline stacks cranes like questions]
[Sound: Steady rain pattering against glass, distant gulls, the soft drip of irrigation tubing]
[Music: Quiet, steady strings — thoughtful, forward-moving]

You push through the greenhouse door and the air folds around you — warm, humid, green. The scent of wet soil and salt is immediate; it fills your throat and somehow steadies your pulse. From below, the city is a smear of cranes and steel; up here, everything is measured in leaves and the slow choreography of water.

Elias looks up from a spread of rolled plans and a laptop glowing under a strand of warm bulbs. He tucks a stray ash-blond strand behind his ear and smiles the way someone who knows you two coffees in can, familiar and careful.

Elias: "You made it. The timing's perfect — the rain'll help show the tidal diagrams in real time."

You set your notebook on the table, its edges damp from your jacket, and for a beat you both look at the paper like conspirators. The skepticism that had lived in your chest since the mayor's meeting — the memory of Camille's polished cuff and patient efficiency — sits there, a cold pebble. Elias notices it without saying the word.

Elias: (softly) "I know what you're thinking. That I came from a different table. I left that because I couldn't make it fit the people I care about."

His fingers trace a line on the plans, revealing a bracelet at his wrist — braided reclaimed fishing line. A small thing. A signal.

Narration:
The plans he lays out are not theatrical. They are stepwise and honest: phased infrastructure that pairs marsh restoration with modular flood-relief units — low-profile berms, living sills, permeable walkways — designed to maintain tidal exchange and preserve habitat rather than seal it away. He taps a sketch that shows channels left open for crabs and eelgrass beds, and then points to a small, stackable module meant to dissipate storm surge without stranding the tide.

Elias: "This pilot here — three months of monitoring, simple sensors, community volunteers on the rotations. Metrics tied to both ecological health and household impact: percent reduction in inundation hours, resident displacement risk, biodiversity indicators. We can make it readable for the mayor and the funders."

You lean in despite yourself. The greenhouse warmth fogs the rim of your glasses. On the laptop, a slide mockup floats: plain language, clear outcomes, a timeline that doesn't pretend overnight miracles.

Maya: "Metrics are fine on paper. Camille will spin them into engineering claims. She'll pick the numbers that justify a wall."

Elias: (nodding) "Then we pick the numbers she can't co-opt. Community-defined outcomes — not just economic ROI. We show people returning to the marsh, not just square meters of reclaimed land. We package the science Rowan's got, but we translate it so it's about fishing mornings, not just biomass percentages."

Narration:
You name Rowan Hale in your head — the slow, deliberate man who has taught you to read a coastline like a history book — and the image steadies the plan. Rowan's tidal charts and long-term species records thread through Elias' engineering language. Amaya's crowd lists and the neighbors' Saturday crews fit into the volunteer monitoring days Elias suggests. Every piece has a fitting place; the plan is not sterile, it is crowded with people who will have to live with it.

Elias: (hesitates; then with a small, earnest cadence) "I don't want to design for you. I want to design with you. Co-design has to be more than a bullet point. It has to be a condition of every grant we accept."

Maya: "Grants come with strings. They ask who signs the contracts, who holds liability. Co-design gets thin real fast once permits need names."

Elias: "Then we insist on the names being both community and municipal. We bring Amaya and Rowan into the application. We write clauses that preserve adaptive decision-making. Mayor Sofía can be the bridge — if we make it politically convincing."

Dialogue:
Maya: "You trust the mayor to be that bridge?"

Elias: (a small, rueful smile) "I know she's tired of making hard choices. But she's also someone who listens when people show up with a plan that doesn't just complain. We can give her a plan that lowers risk and honors place. That's the pitch."

Maya: "And if the pitch fails?"

Elias: (sober, careful) "Then we keep fighting in the mud. But if it gets us legal access to pilot the marsh and funding to pay neighbors for monitoring shifts, it's not a half-measure — it's leverage."

Narration:
You feel a slow rise in the room — not panic, not triumph, but a mobilizing warmth. Mid-level intensity, like breath before a sprint. The practicalities tighten your focus: liability language, matching funds, pilot metrics. Elias writes as he speaks, the pen leaving neat loops across both your notebook and the laptop's digital slides.

Elias: "We make the first slide human. Show a morning at the fishing cove, not an algorithm. Then we lay out the science and the metrics. Then — crucially — we give the mayor a timeline she can sell. You're the one who knows what people will accept. I'm only here to translate."

Maya: (softly) "Translation can whitewash. I won't let that happen."

Elias: "I wouldn't want you to. And I won't do it."

Narration:
The back-and-forth is careful — not the quick sparring of strangers but the steady negotiation of people trying to find a shared language. Elias listens to the sharp edges of your mistrust; he doesn't dull them, he sharpens them into questions the proposal must answer. That steadiness erodes an old reflex in you to retract into isolation. It is still early; your guard is not down. But it is lower.

Elias: (quietly) "I'll stand with the community in any meeting. I'll answer the engineer questions. I'll make sure the technical stuff doesn't get used to paper over the human parts."

You look at him. Up close his slate-blue eyes are steady, the small crow's-feet from squinting into sun and storm softening his expression. There's something in his steadiness that doesn't ask you to choose between purity and practicality. It asks you to choose a method.

Maya: "Standing is more than words in a room. It means showing up when the optics get rough."

Elias: "Then I show up when it gets rough."

Narration:
You both spend the next hour drafting the proposal. Words are pared and repared until the tone is exactly what you wanted: polite, firm, and uncompromising about co-design. You add an appendix listing community-defined success indicators; Elias writes a pilot timeline with check-ins and clauses for renegotiation. You insert Rowan's long-term monitoring recommendations. You tag Amaya for mobilization logistics. Each sentence makes the plan feel less like a hope and more like a tool.

[Visual: The proposal download completes; the file name is practical and unromantic: MARSH-PILOT_FINAL_v2]
[Sound: A soft chime as the file saves]

Elias: "We can bring this to Sofía together tomorrow. Or — if you prefer — we run a community-only review first and adjust."

Maya: (weighing) "Tomorrow the mayor convenes a planning committee. If we miss that window, the funding narrative moves forward without us."

Elias: "Then tomorrow it is. We'll ask for five minutes and make it human."

Narration:
You close your notebook and tuck the pen behind your ear, an old habit when you are thinking too many steps ahead. Outside, the rain eases into a thin, steady mist. The greenhouse smells of wet wood and new growth; the city beyond holds its cranes like questions waiting for answers.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Dusk]
[Visual: Salt-streaked planks gleam; strings of bulbs throw warm pools of light; the harbor exhales mist]
[Sound: Boardwalk boards creak, distant engine hum, gulls calling intermittently]
[Music: Low, steady piano — contemplative, carrying a hint of resolve]

You and Elias walk down the boardwalk toward the neighborhood, the proposal safe in his satchel and the evening folding around the two of you. The air tastes of salt and frying oil from the market; a neighbor patches a tarp under a lamplight. Your steps are measured; the conversation softens into practicalities.

Elias: "If Sofía asks about scale, we say pilot. If she asks about cost, we show payment for local monitoring and avoided displacement costs. If Camille pushes for a single-engineered solution, we show the living answers."

Maya: "If Sofía decides the political risk is too high?"

Elias: (after a pause) "Then we don't stop. We pivot — public demos, volunteer monitoring, press. But we give her a real, responsible choice. Not empty opposition."

Narration:
You feel a mixture you can't name fully — relief that there is a strategy that doesn't turn everything into a wall, and the old fear that compromises will hollow out the things you love. The arousal inside you is steady, a moderate drumbeat: clarity without panic, urgency without heat.

You walk a little way in silence, listening to the tide breathe against pilings. Elias falls into step beside you, his presence unassuming but present.

Elias: (softly) "I mean it when I say I'll stand by you, Maya. In the meeting, in the mud, in the paperwork. I don't have answers for everything, but I won't let your voice be the only one in the room."

Narration:
His words sit in the humid air between you, a small anchor against the tide of decisions. There is no grand promise, no dramatic declaration — only a steady, intentional offer.

The boardwalk lights smear reflections across puddles. Ahead, the outlines of the marsh look like a dark promise. You think of the neighbors who will sit with baited breath at the meeting, of Rowan's charts that show slow recoveries and the improbable tenderness of ecosystems. You think of Camille's tower, too — bright and efficient and built to keep things out.

Maya: (thinking) "Is this the pragmatic path that keeps people here, or the slippery slope that sells the shore in pieces?"

Narration:
You feel the decision pressing at the edges of your thoughts — not yet a cliff to jump from, but a bend in the path where direction matters. Elias' offer rests between the two possibilities like a simple hinge. Your next steps will determine whether the hinge opens toward co-design or toward the uncompromising mud of direct action.
