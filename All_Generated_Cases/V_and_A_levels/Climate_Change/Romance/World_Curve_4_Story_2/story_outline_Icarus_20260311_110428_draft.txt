## Visual Novel Title
Tidal Promises

---

## World View
Harrow Bay is a mid-21st-century coastal city stitched from history and improvisation. Rising seas have reordered daily life: elegant promenades are threaded with raised wooden walkways, downtown marble steps wear sandbag banks, and rooftop gardens crown retrofitted tenements. Political power is split between municipal councils pushed by institutional science and grassroots resilience coalitions that protect neighborhoods. Economy and class trace the waterline: affluent enclaves invest in private modular barriers and dry docks; flood-prone districts—Lower Quay, the boardwalk vendors, longshore carpenters—rely on communal repair, seed-keepers, and barter. Religion and memory survive in small rituals—seed exchanges, repair feasts, and memorial plaques naming lost homes. Geography compresses tension: the bay, the workboat Aster, the resilience hub greenhouse, and the domed City Archive sit within visible storm-lines. The atmosphere is humid and briny, visual detail favors salt crust, peeling paint, algae, and rusted copper accents; cinematic light plays wet surfaces into small miracles. Everyday life balances hope and grief: people hold tightly to relationships, elders keep ecological memory, scientists warn of tipping points, and activists demand equitable governance. Harrow Bay's central moral friction asks whether technology or community should steer adaptation—and whether love can survive the choices shaped by water.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I narrate as Maya Soren, a pragmatic urban ecologist tasked with brokering an equitable adaptation plan for Harrow Bay. The narrative follows an Icarus (Rise-Fall) arc: an early period of rapid hope and partnership culminates in an apparent breakthrough—Elias Voss’s ambitious kelp-restoration and sea-wall prototype, merged into my negotiated hybrid plan, looks like a victory that could protect the city. That success is personal as well as civic: Elias and I draw close; Asha Reed reluctantly recognizes the coalition I formed. But the triumph accelerates risk. Political shortcuts, hurried deployment, and hidden ecological trade-offs precipitate a sharp collapse: fisheries falter, displaced neighborhoods suffer, public trust fractures, and blame concentrates on me. Branching choices throughout force me to weigh romantic loyalties to Elias, moral solidarity with Asha’s coalition, and pragmatic responsibility to the wider city. Multiple endings resolve differently—happy compromise, bittersweet sacrifice, catastrophic failure, or a “true” ambiguous renewal—yet every path follows the same rise to hope and the plunge into consequence. The story insists that love, ethics, and community cannot be separated from climate decisions; each choice colors who we save, what we lose, and how we live with the aftermath.
