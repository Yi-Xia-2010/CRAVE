# Route: Additional / Ending (Sad)


[Scene: The Old Boatyard | Morning]
[Visual: Sunlight thinning through patchwork sails; a half-reclaimed hull sits on blocks, its name sanded away]
[Sound: The rasp of a plane on wood, gulls distant, a radio muttering weather updates]
[Music: Tight strings, brisk tempo]

You still smell the night—wet rope, soot from the makeshift fires, the metallic tang that hangs after a storm. Your fingers find the compass at your throat as if on habit alone. The fraying cord is warm from your skin; the brass face is dulled by salt. The memory of last night—the sirens, the patched losses, the children counting names of lost things—sits behind your breastbone like a stone you keep turning over.

Eli works with a patience that looks like grief made visible. His plane skims the hull; wood curls into a pale snow at his feet. He hums under his breath, a cadence you learned as a child when he mended nets on your father's boat.

Eli Duarte: "You make the ledger look paler than the sea, Asha. Why don't you come rough it up with me for half an hour? It'll keep your head from thrashing in the tide."

Asha Moreno: "If I stop, the plans don't get refiled, and someone has to catalog the sink points before the council asks for them again."

Eli pauses, wipes his forehead with the back of his hand, sawdust tattooing his palm.

Eli Duarte: "Cataloging isn't the only way to keep memory. This—" (he taps the hull) "—this becomes memory if you help carve it. People will come by, touch the names. You don't always have to keep it on paper, you know."

You look at the plank, at the shallow letters Eli is mapping across the stern. The names are not just names; they're coordinates of what you've already failed to protect and the small victories you managed to hold. Your throat tightens. You can feel the town's breath around you: the urgent, ragged inhale of people who refused to let the night take everything.

Eli watches you with an unreadable expression—part gratitude, part question. You neither promise nor refuse; you only choose.

Eli Duarte: (soft) "Either way. We keep what we can."

[Music: A low, single cello line underlines the moment; time feels parceled into tasks]

You leave Eli at the hull and walk barefoot—boot soles wet with sap and salt—toward the raised gardens. The boatyard's smell follows you: varnish, damp wood, the ghost of diesel. Behind you, someone laughs, brittle and relieved. The town is operating like a limb waking up: slow, then suddenly quick.

[Scene: Floating Market & Raised Gardens | Midday]
[Visual: Bright awnings lash in gusts; raised beds bristle with sprouting shoots]
[Sound: Voices, clinking jars, a child's shout, the sifter's soft patter]
[Music: Percussive rhythm picks up; urgency through staccato piano]

Marta runs the seed table like a drill sergeant in a greenhouse. Her hands—small, stained—work the sifter, and people drop seed packets into it like prayers. Around her, neighbors trade stories of what worked in last night's flooding, swapping tips on which root systems held, which planters tipped.

Marta Chen: "Asha, get over here. People will sit through council speeches, but they'll dig for hours. Teach them the difference between spartina and cordgrass while you can make them plant it."

Asha Moreno: "I can teach a dozen people about living shorelines and still miss the filing the mayor wants this afternoon."

Marta slaps your arm in a practiced motion, not unkind.

Marta Chen: "Then do both. Teach them to seed and teach them to argue. Two kinds of resistance."

You grab a handful of mud, cold and smelling of silt, and let it slip through your fingers. It feels like a small mercy that the soil accepts hands. Around you, people are patching pots, knotting new trellises, and repairing frames with an economy of motion earned by necessity.

Noah Reyes arrives with two paper cups of coffee, steam cutting through the damp air. He sets one in your hands without pretending it will fix anything.

Noah Reyes: (half-smiling) "You look like you need something warm between your ribs."

Asha Moreno: (grim) "I need the ledger to be seen. I need Mayor Rosa to see that the sink points form a map we can adapt to, not a clause to be signed away."

Noah reaches for your hand as if to steady more than your balance.

Noah Reyes: "You won't be the only one making them see. We can make the map speak. I'll bring the council the story, you bring the numbers."

You let your fingers rest against his, brief and solid. It is a small civil defense against the weight pressing at your chest—a shared pressure that only increases.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Stay longer at the seed table to teach") -> Reaction: "You step up to the makeshift lectern, voice steadier than you feel. People lean in, palms dirty, skepticism softening into curiosity." || Choice B ("Walk with Noah back to the Tidewatch Lab to prepare the presentation") -> Reaction: "You allow Noah to pull you toward the lab. The steps feel hurried. Prepare together, argue less—make the data hard to ignore."]

Marta watches you go, hands covered in mud, and calls after you with a smile that doesn't quite reach exhaustion.

Marta Chen: "Don't make promises you can't keep."

You don't answer. You are already mapping the next room in your head.

[Scene: Tidewatch Lab | Afternoon]
[Visual: Clinical light, screens showing shifting contours of shoreline; volunteer maps pinned to foam board]
[Sound: Hum of servers, low conversations, the occasional beep of instruments]
[Music: Fast string ostinato; a sense of mounting pressure]

Teaching should be small and contained, but your lecture is an act of defense. You stand before a semicircle of faces—students, residents, a few journalists who smell like wet coats—and explain living shorelines with a clarity that makes your chest ache.

Asha Moreno: "Living shorelines buy time. They trap sediment, they slow wave energy, they give communities a stake in the land's accretion. But they are not a permanent wall. They are a strategy of slowing and rebuilding; they require labor, governance, and—critically—local control."

A hand shoots up: an older man, voice like gravel.

Resident: "And if the storms don't slow? If the sea keeps coming the way it did last week?"

Asha Moreno: "Then we learn where to put people and what to let go of first. We fight to keep things that are worth the cost of fighting for. We don't pretend to hold everything."

You notice Mayor Rosa in the back—her scarf wound tight, eyes rimmed with the same sleeplessness you share. After class, she stays; her gait is deliberate, practiced.

Mayor Rosa Alvarez: "You spoke well. The town listens when you talk like numbers and like memory."

Asha Moreno: (soft) "They listen because it matters to them. They know their houses, their names, their thresholds."

Mayor Rosa Alvarez: "The council needs a framework. If we can pass a phased governance model—resident seats, refugee support, clear oversight—it's a political tool we can use to keep corporate fingers off the tiller."

Before you can answer, your tablet vibrates. Lila's name lights the screen. The message is formatted, cool: an offer summarized in bullet points with an attached non-disclosure. Help scaled, funds immediate, deployment window tightened. Strings not visible in the preview.

You let the phone sink into your palm like a stone. Lila's voice is measured when she walks in—still that practiced cadence of someone used to shaping rooms with presence.

Lila Park: "Asha. Good—you're teaching. Visibility matters. We've drawn a scaled plan; it accelerates protection in the most impacted corridors. We're offering matching resources if the town signs a Memorandum of Agreement."

Asha Moreno: "Matching to what oversight? To whom will the sensors report? Who controls the data?"

Lila Park: (tilted head) "Operational efficiency requires centralization. We can provide transparent dashboards. We can set resident access tiers. But there must be an administrative node to coordinate emergency response. Otherwise, chaos."

Noah steps between you and Lila with the easy intention of a mediator, voice calm but carrying a weight.

Noah Reyes: "We need assurances—not just dashboards. Resident seats with veto power on deployment. Data porting for the lab. No clauses that allow seizure of waterfront plots."

Lila's eyes flick to Noah, then to Mayor Rosa, reading the room with the precision of someone used to closing deals.

Lila Park: "Veto powers are efficient until they shut down operations in a storm night. We must have the ability to act decisively. I can propose resident advisory seats—advisory, not operational. That's how systems stay functional."

Mayor Rosa Alvarez: (quietly) "Advisory won't cut it with the people who lost their homes."

The conversation tightens like a clamp. The lab's air tastes of ozone and coffee and tension. You can feel the town being pulled between two mechanics of survival: local autonomy that risks slower action, and centralized speed that risks dispossession.

You speak because silence would feel like consent.

Asha Moreno: "Speed without sovereignty is a sale. We cannot trade control for protection and call it resilience."

Lila Park: (leaning in, vaulting over the softness) "And we cannot trade lives for the luxury of governance. You saw what happened last night. If we can secure lives quickly, we should."

Your pulse picks up like a current you cannot tame. The room breathes with you—Noah's knuckles white at his side, Mayor Rosa's jaw hard, Eli's shoulders visible at the doorway, Marta's hands folded in the crowd. This is no longer a policy discussion; it is a negotiation that smells of salt and possibility and the possibility of betrayal.

Lila watches you consider like a chess player watching the clock.

Lila Park: (soft) "Whatever your choice, know that time is the one currency we cannot make. Delays cost risk."

Your compass digs into your collarbone. The urgency is tangible: storms are not theoretical; sensors blink, tides rise an inch a week, and people's roofs are patched with faster hands than they deserve. The ache in your chest is not just for the houses but for the people who can't keep choosing between dignity and rooflines.

Noah takes your hand beneath the table—a tiny, steadying anchor.

Noah Reyes: "We'll choose together. Whatever you do, you don't have to carry it alone."

You squeeze his fingers like a vow that is not yet a promise.

[Scene: Marabel Promenade | Evening]
[Visual: Boards still wet from a light drizzle, lampposts beginning to blink on; the Beacon a distant halo]
[Sound: The distant crash of surf, a child's laughter muffled by distance, the scraping of a broom on wood]
[Music: Crescendo of strings and percussion, heartbeat rhythm]

The ledger is open on a bench, pages annotated, margins crowded with alternatives and strikeouts. You annotate again—adaptive housing models, shared resource pools, a matrix tying funding to governance seats and sunset clauses. Each line is an attempt to translate urgency into durable structure.

Eli approaches with the carved panel slung over his shoulder, names etched with a tenderness that steadies your breath. Marta brings a bag of seed packets and a roll of sticky notes; Mayor Rosa carries a manila folder with draft governance language. Lila walks the edge of the promenade with her hands in her coat pockets, offering a smile that doesn't reach her eyes.

Mayor Rosa Alvarez: "This phased governance model could be presented at the next council. It enshrines seats and refugee support and gives us leverage."

Lila Park: "And my firm will provide phased deployment if the town signs the MOA with operational clarity. We can move within weeks."

Marta Chen: (hard) "What you call 'operational clarity' is a leash. We saw the clauses in the pilot. We watched sensors sunset at the wrong moment."

Lila Park: (level) "We will revise them. There are ways to build safeguards."

Your chest feels like a drum that everyone is playing. Each word is a mallet blow. There is no neutral ground: every concession is a cut; every victory is porous.

You look at the names Eli carved—faces remembered in letters—and at the ledger, where you have written "priority: dignity" in ink that is already smudging. Your guilt, which has been a constant ember, begins to soften into something that is both aching and forward-pushing. The calculus of protecting bodies and preserving autonomy is not a tidy equation. It is a list you keep updating as the tides change.

Noah stands close, breath fogging in the cool air. He doesn't offer solutions. He offers steadiness—a plain thing, but necessary.

Noah Reyes: "Whatever you choose, we'll bear the aftermath together. We will be the ones making the calls late at night and fixing what breaks."

Asha Moreno: (quiet, to Noah) "I don't want to be the person who chose wrong for everyone."

Noah Reyes: "Then don't. Choose with us."

The town watches in a dozen small ways: Eli's hands steady on the memorial; Marta's bag of seeds a promise; Mayor Rosa's folder a political tightrope; Lila's firmament of resources a looming choice. The arousal inside you has built from the boatyard's quiet grief to the garden's industrious hope to the lab's sharp negotiations, and now it reaches its height—raw, electric, thrumming under the skin.

You close the ledger and feel the weight like an answered question and an urgent demand at once. The sea will come again. The measures you enshrine now will determine who sleeps under roofs and who must choose exile.

You cup the compass at your throat and look up. Everyone is waiting. Not for a solution—the town knows there is no clean solution—but for the next move that will mark who gets a voice in deciding Marabel's future.

Your mouth is dry. The night air presses as if to squeeze decision out of you.

Page-turning thought: There will be a council. There will be signatures. There will be nights when the sirens go off and people look to the Beacon and to the maps and hope. You have to choose what you will stand for when the ink dries.
