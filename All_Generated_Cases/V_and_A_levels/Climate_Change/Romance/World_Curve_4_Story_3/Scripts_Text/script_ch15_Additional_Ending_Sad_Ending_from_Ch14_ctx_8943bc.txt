The clauses you helped drag into the light became a scalpel and a wedge. Lila Park's firm stepped back, furious and public. Investors moved in as vultures move at low tide—patient, precise, polite. They brought money shaped like fences.

You watch a drone lower a white crate to a paved kiosk where once Marta's bright tomatoes stacked high. There is a scan badge at the kiosk's edge now, a chip-lock humming underneath a plastic awning. A familiar laugh—Mariana's, who once bartered change for seedlings—doesn't reach you. Her stall is a flat sign on a door that reads "LEASED — RESIDENCES UNDER COVENANT." The word hits like ballast dropped into your ribs.

You close your fingers around the brass compass in your pocket. It is heavier than memory. It is a relic of a map that promised routes rather than rights.

Narration:
The Beacon stayed. The sensors hummed. The sensors were not the enemy; the way they were parceled out was. Managed-access boards. Covenanted spaces. The town's buildings were intact but bound by new rules: residency tied to contracts, access to shorelines gated behind fees you and your neighbors could no longer match. Preservation with terms.

You taste metal—anger and a kind of grief that almost tastes like blood. Very quickly the feeling breaks into motion: you move along the boards, past empty benches, past the seafood vendor who now sells only to a subscription list pinging on tablet screens. The Promenade is tidy. It is efficient. It is not home anymore.

Narration:

You teach people to plant living edges, to weave willow stakes, to count species you hope will hold. You organize squatter gardens for those priced out of covenanted lots. You file petitions. You sit in public hearings and speak until your voice is a sanded thing.

People listen. Some help. Others have already left. The Beacon manages to keep roofs over many heads. The rooms are colder now—efficiently heated, monitored, legal. The town survives in a way that a photograph survives after the original is burned. It exists. It is not whole.

You are exhausted and incandescent. The days blur into a ledger of loss and stubborn graft. Very quickly, your life compresses into deployments: a training, then a legal meeting, then a late-night call from someone whose apartment has been reclassified. You are both a rescuer and a witness, and both jobs tear you.

You see Noah sometimes, in meetings, a figure who once stayed up late sanding a shared plan and now signs minutes in tempered ink. He wears the technical director badge like a bandage—functional, visible. He is present. He is compromised. He keeps a corner of what you fought for, but the corners are smaller now, carved by boards and bylaws.

You stand on the Promenade one night and watch his silhouette pass under a Beacon lamp as he enters a building armored with glass and policy. Your chest splits into a soundless thing that is bigger than anger and colder than loss: a steady, aching grief.

Noah Reyes: "I don't want us to lose each other."

Asha Moreno: "Neither do I."

Noah Reyes: "Then don't walk away tonight."

You walk away from the Beacon because staying has become its own apology. You want to help. You will continue to help. But you cannot stay within the architecture that made "help" contingent on price tags and access codes.

Noah stands where he stands—inside rooms with windows that face the sea but are sealed by policy. He sent a text that morning: "I wish things were different. I wish we could have done this without price. I love you." You read it in the light of concessions and feel the sentence as if it were salted.

You answer with nothing for a long time. The town will keep a version of itself with roofs and sensors and tidy plaques. It will be tidy. It will be safe for some. It will be different. The heart of it has been resized to fit the architecture of investment.

Your brass compass rests against your leg like a remnant of an old map. You keep it because it remembers a coast that was full of people and not thirty-second delivery schedules. You keep it because memory is a kind of refusal.

You do not know if your itinerant work will save the town. You do not know if Noah Reyes's presence behind Beacon doors will make a difference that can be measured in lives saved or in lives pushed to the margins. You know only this: your love for him has become another kind of shore—one you can see and name, but which sometimes recedes when the tide of policy rises.
