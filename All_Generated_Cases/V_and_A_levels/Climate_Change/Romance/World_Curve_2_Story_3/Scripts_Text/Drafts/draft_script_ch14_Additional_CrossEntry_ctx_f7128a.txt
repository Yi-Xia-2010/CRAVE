# Route: Additional / Ending


[Scene: Glasshouse Lab | Night]
[Visual: Interior of the reclaimed warehouse; curved translucent panels glow a sickly, greenish amber. Rows of hydroponic trays cast small, damp shadows. Shelves of labeled spores and seed vials line a wall like a fragile archive.]
[Sound: Pumps clicking and humming; a metal crate lid slides; far-off, the sea bangs against pilings — a persistent, urgent percussion.]
[Music: High, insistent strings; sharp percussion undercurrent building.]

You taste iron and salt when you breathe — not metaphorically, literally: the air is a bruise of sea-spray and old metal. Your buoy pendant presses cold against your sternum, a small stubborn weight that becomes a metronome for decisions.

The pumps keep clacking. A humidity haze blurs Kai's neon braid into a streak of coral. Mika's hands flutter, quick as gulls, between a tablet and a coil of rope. Amalia stands with a clipboard, face lit by the lab's green light and a storm-map on her phone. The three of them look at you as if you were the hinge they all hold.

You think of the sacks of spores in your satchel, of the lab's seedbank rows that have held hope through drought and hard winters. You think of the long-line you promised to tie tomorrow, and you think of the meetings you did not win, the council minutes you could not bend. Between the two futures hangs the shape of a simple verb: protect.

Marina (You): "We can't lose the seedbank. Not tonight."

Kai: "We can't look away while the foremen drive piles into the flats. We can't—" (Kai's voice fractures; a laugh and then a cut) "—we can't just put everything in a closet and call it strategy."

Mika: "If the pumps short, the cultures die. If the cultures die, we lose more than racks and spores; we lose the months of assay, the strain lines Mika tracked for two seasons, the protocols—"

Amalia: "They'll say you ran. They'll say you hid the research so no one could use it to help their homes."

Silence cracks like thin glass. The pumps click louder, a machine heartbeat that can't be reasoned into stillness.

You look at the seed vials, the neat labels scrawled in your hand and in your father's jagged script. You feel a squeezing behind your ribs, a pressure that wants to flush into action: haul the crates, anchor the long-line, drag the experimental pens inboard, make people see you were choosing to protect — that retreat is not surrender but insurance.

But Kai's jaw is set like a storm ridge. Kai steps closer, palms raised as if to touch the rows of trays and feel them for damage.

Kai: "Insurance? Marina, people need hands in the mud. They need living examples now. You pull the lab back, they lose hope—and hope keeps people from leaving."

Marina (You): "They'll lose more if the cultures die. If the technicians miss a pump alarm tonight, we lose months. I can't gamble the seedbank on a public show that doesn't change the maps."

Kai: "So you make a calculus while my neighbor's porch is on a list? You make a calculus and call it prudence."

Mika (voice tight): "Kai, yelling won't change the pumps. We have to triage. The field arrays are wet; the lines are failing at the joints. If we can secure the isolates—"

Kai interrupts, sharp and small, the way a wave lurches into a dock: "Triage? You speak like we are patching a clinic. We are patching a home. We—"

Amalia's eyes flash. "And what about trust? They'll look at us and think we've chosen to hide what we know so we can keep a monopoly on help. They'll say we loved the lab more than them."

A crate slams in the back room — Mika's hands, too quick, too rough. The sound ricochets; the glasshouse light trembles as if with every impact the building is being counted.

Your pulse surges. Panic threads your breath. You imagine the lab empty, the seedbank sealed, the town staring at unanchored experiments drifting away like promises. You imagine the opposite: standing in the flats, soaked and swearing and building kelp-lines under a rain that will carve the town's memory.

You have already been choosing. You have been choosing in the quiet: what to risk, what to keep, what future you can allow.

Marina (You): "We have to protect tomorrow's work. If we lose the seedbank tonight, there's no cooperative to rebuild with. There will be no long-line to plant next season. We buy time by preserving what can't be replaced."

Kai laughs once, hard, without humor. "Buy time. Buy for whom? People will be driven from their porches by the time you have enough 'tomorrows.'"

Mika's tablet pings — an alarm flaring red: a pump voltage spike. The sound is tiny and then enormous. Outside, the banging of the sea changes tempo: rapid, a staccato that sounds almost like an answer.

[Sound: A loud metallic clatter from the flats — pilings or machinery; a wind gust that makes the panels shudder.]

Everything ratchets up. Your chest, the room, the town — all of it tightens to a single, snapping point.

Amalia steps forward, voice low, measured and breaking: "Marina. If you pull this back, some people will think you chose the lab over them. They'll be right, in the only way that hurts — it will feel like abandonment."

Marina (You): "And if I don't—if I let the cultures die—what then? We throw away the one asset that lets us rebuild seeds, strains, protocols. We give away the future to a night we couldn't stop."

Kai: "Then do both. Do something meaningful in public and protect the lab. Don't hide."

You swallow. Both feels impossible: doing both would break you, or break the team, or break the town. Your hands find the satchel strap, fingers tightening on worn leather.

Mika: "We can strip non-critical rigs. We can consolidate isolates. We can move the most vulnerable samples to the cold-room and bring auxiliary power up. It's... it's ugly work. It will take hours."

Kai: "And all that time the foremen keep driving bolts—"

Amalia: "And the townspeople keep watching and feeling smaller."

The storm outside answers with a sound like a fist on a door. A siren wails somewhere distant — municipal, perhaps, or someone calling for help. The lab's humidity beads on the inside of the panels. Someone — you don't know who — turns a light off and on, off and on, as if Morse could reorder fate.

The argumentative voices loop, reframe, accelerate. The room becomes a tightening coil where every sentence is a lever pulled toward some irreversible motion.

You feel very small and also enormous; the paradox makes your hands shake.

At the edge of the glasshouse door, the night seems to lean in, patient and hungry. You picture, for a moment, the seedbank as a vault of possibility, the town as a single fragile thread. The arithmetic of loss and preservation lays itself like a map you cannot unsee.

Kai steadies, for a fraction of a second, then: "If you choose the seedbank, don't think we'll stop trying out there. We'll hold people together in other ways. But we won't be quiet about it. People deserve to know."

Amalia's clipboard hits her thigh with a flat sound. "People deserve to not be broken by symbols. They deserve to have you next to them in the mud."

Mika's voice, small and precise: "We can pack the most important isolates in portable dew boxes. We can patch pumps. But it's going to be wet work and cold. And somebody has to sit on the generator swaps; we don't have enough hands to do both in an hour."

Marina (You): "Which means a choice."

The word lands like a tide. The pumps click faster. Your fingers dig into the satchel strap until they hurt.

You imagine the town's faces: Old Tom's hands, raw and callused; Amalia's bright, tired grin; neighbors who will read tomorrow's notices and see lines through their addresses. You imagine everyone's accusing eyes turning to you. The buoy pendant is warm beneath your palm.

Your breath becomes a metronome: in—out—in—out—short, clipped. The room's voices rise to a single unreadable howl under the sea's percussion.

You know what you will do. You can feel the plan—cold, technical, a list that fits into crates and generator schedules—arrange itself inside your skull. You know who will pack, who will patch. You know you will stay with the archives and the cold-rooms; you know to order Mika to bring the dew boxes; you know to tell Kai to take volunteers out as a gesture so the town doesn't feel abandoned.

You also know how the town will feel betrayed.

The choices bloom and close like tidal pools. Whatever you pick will shape how the night unfolds, who resents you, who forgives.

You choose words. You choose order. You choose to stay. Your chest is a drum you can no longer silence.

[Visual: Close-up on your fingers as they flex against the satchel, on the dew on the panels, on Kai's braid catching the lab light like an accusation.]

You step toward the cold-room door — or you are about to step. The storm outside swells into a roar. The metallic banging from the flats becomes a punctuation, a demand: decide.

The pumps click. A panel flashes a caution light. Someone mutters a prayer or a curse; it's impossible to tell which, and the ambiguity feels right.

Your hand lifts to the latch. The buoy pendant bounces under your collarbone — a pendulum marking an instant that will tilt everything.

There is a last, impossible pressure of thought: by saving the seedbank, you save the technical possibility of future restoration. By doing so, you risk the living trust of the town tonight.

You breathe. The room leans into your choice.

[Page-Turn Moment: The lab door yawns open a hair; wind gnaws at the threshold. You realize the night's true work has nothing to do with protocol sheets or grants — it's a ledger of human faith. Your thumb closes on the latch. The decision is not a single motion but a fracture that will be read for months. You make the move inward, and the sound the night makes when you do is a wet, keening thing.]
