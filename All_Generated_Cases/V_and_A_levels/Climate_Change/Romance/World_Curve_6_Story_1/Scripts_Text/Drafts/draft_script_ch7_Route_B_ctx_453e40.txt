### DRAFT
# Route: Route B / Ending: Bittersweet


[Scene: Stormwall Construction Site Office | Night]
[Visual: A cramped plywood trailer lit by a single swinging construction lamp; blueprints pinned with bulldozer bolts, a thermos with dried salt rings on the lip]
[Sound: Wind rattling the trailer’s siding; distant metallic clang as a crane swings]
[Music: Pulsing low strings — urgent, forward-driving]

You push the trailer door open and the smell meets you first: wet cement, diesel, the iron of rain. The air is colder inside, as if the trailer holds its breath and waits for whatever you bring with you. Your field notebook is in the inner pocket of your jacket, its taped corner familiar under your fingers, but you don't pull it out yet. Tonight is for listening; tonight you are meant to measure words as much as numbers.

The steel skeleton of the stormwall looms beyond the trailer's thin window—an articulated silhouette of promises and threats. Its lattice catches the lamp and throws a grid of light across Elias Hart before he rises from a fold-out chair. He is almost absurdly neat against the construction dust: navy pea coat pressed, brass pocket watch catching the lamp's flare. For a heartbeat his smile is a practiced thing, but his eyes—cool, assessing—hold a softness that you didn't expect to find in a city man who returns with contracts in his briefcase.

Elias Hart: "Alea. Thanks for coming on short notice. I know the hour."

You fold your hands to stop them from shaking in the cold. You keep your voice steady because you know how much sway steadiness buys.

You: "You asked to meet privately. I came. I want to hear the plan directly, Elias."

Elias lets the door close behind you with an obliging nod. The lamp swings and the shadow of his profile follows like a second thought.

Elias Hart: "Direct's easier for everyone. Less room for rumor. We're proposing a scaled barrier—only across the most exposed span. Private capital covers the upfront costs; we source local contractors for the work, and we'll fund an ecological offset program that plants and restores the Dune Gardens. Oversight, measurements, adaptive triggers—I want you to help author them. Give us the credibility we lack out here."

You feel something unclench in your chest at the word 'local.' It's a soft, dangerous ease.

You: "You want me to write the metrics. To sign a name to the thresholds that allow you to pour concrete through our coastline."

Elias Hart: (hands steepled) "Not to sign: to structure. You know the science better than anyone here, Alea. I can bring capital. You can guarantee—at least in design—that we don't worsen sediment starvation, that we fund long-term monitoring. That matters."

He watches you as if reading an equation, waiting to see whether you'll plug in the right variable. The lamp paints a thin crescent of gold across his watch; he taps it once, a small, unconscious clockwork gesture that seems to say he understands deadlines in a way you know only too well.

Internal thought: The numbers line up in your head — stress curves, sediment transport, modeled deposition rates. A scaled wall reduces wave energy where it's built. Downcoast, currents could accelerate erosion.

You: "Short-term reduction in overtopping doesn't disappear the long-term trend. A wall in one place can starve another of sediment. Lives are saved now, but other livelihoods might collapse later."

Elias Hart: "I'm not arguing who's right about physics, Alea. I'm arguing about timing. Policy moves slowly; storms don't. We can patch the town a little more time with this. Use that time to implement nature-based solutions properly. I'm offering both. Private funds now, community projects in parallel. If you help structure the oversight, we can make that binding."

There is a cadence to his pitch — urgency in his words that matches the wind beyond the trailer. It warms something dangerous in you: the intoxicating certainty of someone who believes speed can marry rigor.

You: "Binding is the word everyone likes until binding becomes a loophole. Who writes the penalties? Who enforces them if the offsets underperform? Who sanctions contractors if they cut corners? I've seen 'community projects' used as greenwash in places with fuller pockets than ours."

Elias smiles, not dismissive this time, but sincere.

Elias Hart: "Then we write penalties. We define performance metrics. We appoint a mixed oversight board—community, independent scientists, and a third-party auditor. You pick the scientists. You choose the community reps. We put the contract in escrow, with clauses triggered by monitoring data."

The lamp swings again and a gust slides through the trailer; you smell salt on his coat. Your throat tightens at the thought of authority in escrow—power temporarily tamed but still held by hands that can open clauses and invest differently come morning.

You: "You say 'we put it in escrow' like you're comfortable handing over strings. Why? What's in it for you besides legacy and votes?"

Elias Hart: (leaning back, hands open) "I grew up near enough to see towns disappear when decisions are delayed. I'm tired of watching people choose between ruin and hasty fixes. If I can help save Harborstone and avoid the mistakes that hollowed other places, that's enough. The funders want a model that proves concrete and nature-based strategies can coexist. If we pull it off—if we prove it—the model spreads. That's my incentive."

Your thought stumbles, not on the math but on the image he paints: a model exported like a curriculum of hope. For a second you imagine scaling solutions beyond Harborstone and the smallness of your early promises. It feels good and unbearable all at once.

Elias watches your face closely during the pause, as if cataloguing which parts of you he can move. He speaks softer.

Elias Hart: "I want to be clear: giving you authority is not PR theater. I want someone who'll be strict. I'm asking you to be unflinching."

You: "It's flattering to be asked. It's dangerous to be used. Why me, Elias? Why not hire a consultant who won't make headlines?"

Elias Hart: (a short laugh; then candid) "Because your name carries the town's memory. Because you left and came back with both grief and knowledge. That combination scares funders and galvanizes people. It's the only thing that will get both sides—investors and residents—to sign."

He leans forward. The construction lamp throws a sharp line under his chin; the watch glints.

Elias Hart: "I can give you authority, Alea. But with real power comes responsibility—and the burden of decisions that won't satisfy everyone. I won't hide that."

Your throat tightens with an old ache—survivor's guilt refracting into policy choices. You think of a small graveyard of choices and of the family that lost a child in the last flood. Your pen moves at the memory, though you haven't opened the notebook.

You: "If I accept, I won't be a figurehead. I will hold the metrics to the standard science demands. I will require adaptive management, bonds, clear triggers for mitigation. But I need guarantees: long-term funding for monitoring; a legally enforceable restoration schedule; community-led seats with veto power on contractors."

Elias Hart: (nods, almost relieved) "Those are reasonable. Enforceable language can be drafted. Funders line up for certainty. We give them predictability. You give them trust."

The whole trailer seems to compress around that exchange. There is warmth in the idea of partnership, warmth that is almost personal. Elias has been careful with his attentiveness; you've watched him listen in a way that leans toward you, and the sensation is faintly intoxicating. The line between political alliance and something else blurs for half a beat.

Your phone vibrates in your jacket. A single unread message from Dr. Lian Cho: "Data can't be political theater. Keep a hard line. Call before you sign anything." You let it sit and don't read more. Her voice—precise, patient—combines with the memory of Rin's warning like two counterweights in your mind.

Internal thought: Rin's words hiss beneath the practical promises: "We will lose our pier if we sell hope cheaply." Dr. Lian's message steadies you: "Hard line." Elias's offer invites expansion and rescue. Your pen rests over the page like a cursor blinking before you send a message into the world.

Elias Hart: "I don't want secrets between us. If you see clauses you can't live with, you'll tell me. If I step out of line, you tell the board. We'll make recourse easy and public."

You: "Transparency only works when the public can read and judge the terms. How will you keep escrow clauses from being rewritten quietly in closed meetings?"

Elias's jaw tightens for a fraction of a second, and then he smiles with a steadiness that feels rehearsed and genuine all at once.

Elias Hart: "We put an independent trigger mechanism in place. Automatic audits tied to public dashboards. If performance dips, penalties automatically disburse to restoration funds. No rewriting without unanimous approval from the oversight board—and the board will be public."

You: "And if the funders try to lobby the board?"

Elias Hart: (measured) "We include cooling-off periods, conflict disclosures, and binding arbitration clauses. We can make it ugly to corrupt."

He says the last with a sort of clean confidence that almost persuades you to want to believe. Your pulse rises. The trailer feels both too small and like the fulcrum of everything—your hometown, your guilt, a larger policy that could ripple outward.

Elias watches as you read the clause for a moment, then withdraws slightly and places both palms on the table. The movement is unthreatening but resolute.

Elias Hart: "I can wait a short time for you to consult. But the funders expect movement this quarter. We mobilize in months, not years. I need someone who understands both the science and the social machinery. I'm asking you to choose with that in mind."

You think of the family that lost a child and of the countless small choices that add up to safety or to ruin. You think of Rin's kitchen, of seed packets and shouted laments at town meetings. You think of Dr. Lian's steady voice saying 'data' until it becomes a mantra.

Your pen hovers above the notebook. The trailer's lamp buzzes; the skeleton of the stormwall outside stands like a promise and a question. Your heart is a metronome, faster now—your arousal climbing with the stakes, the warmth of possibility, the risk of compromise.

You realize, with a clarity that both thrills and terrifies you, that this moment will define not only Harborstone's shoreline but a part of who you will become here: strategist, guardian, or compromiser. The decision is a pivot and the trailer feels like the fulcrum.

You breathe in the cement-sweet air, the salt on Elias's coat, the faint memory of community kitchen warmth, and you let the options align in your head like lines on a map.
