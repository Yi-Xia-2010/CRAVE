## Visual Novel Title
Harborstone Tides

---

## World View
Harborstone is a small mid-latitude coastal town where the sea is both provider and threat. Weather-worn wooden piers and low-lying blocks of housing give way to tidal wetlands and the tentative Dune Gardens, while a proposed concrete stormwall site carves a wide scar along the shore. Political power sits in uneasy tension between local councilors and outside capital; Councilor Bea Ortega negotiates budgets and optics while Elias Hart—an urbane liaison with access to private investment—pushes decisive engineered fixes. Community life clusters around Tide & Thread, Maya Calder’s cafe and cultural hub. Class lines are subtle but sharp: fisher-artist families, volunteers like Tomas, and commuters who can afford to leave during storms. The region’s economy is a mix of tourism, small-scale fisheries, and an emergent “resilience economy” pitched by developers. Culture blends devotion to place, a pragmatic superstition about weather, and growing organized resistance: seed banks, dune plantings, oyster reef projects, and public meetings. Religion is private, civic ritual public: candle-lit vigils after floods, shared casseroles in hard months. Geography alternates between salt-streaked piers, brittle dunes, and an outcrop with an old lighthouse that’s a place for private reckonings. The atmosphere is tactile — spray, mud, damp rope — and underlaid by an urgent political calculus about whether to move people, harden the shore, or rebuild natural buffers.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I return to Harborstone after years away, carrying technical training and survivor’s guilt. The town faces a crisis: Elias Hart and external investors are pushing an immediate engineered stormwall promising quick protection, jobs, and capital; Rin Sato and the grassroots are mobilizing living shorelines, dune gardens, and managed retreat to protect community and ecology. I (Alea Maren) must choose how to act: join Rin and the community, negotiate with Elias while retaining technical rigor, or accept a rapid pilot that promises data but risks social fracture. The story’s emotional arc is Oedipus (Fall → Rise → Fall): I descend into moral paralysis and blame (fall), find momentary hope and apparent solutions through collaboration and ingenuity (rise), but the choices we make—ethical compromises, political trade-offs, unforeseen geophysical responses—produce a final fall that forces a devastating reckoning. Romance with Rin grows as I let down defenses; moral tension with Elias forces political and personal concessions. Multiple branches show different balances of community resilience, engineered fixes, and personal sacrifice: a fragile victory, hybrid compromises, institutional betrayal resulting in displacement, or a painful choice to leave Harborstone in order to influence larger systems. The endings vary in flavor but all follow the Oedipus curve: a temporary solution that precedes a deeper loss.
