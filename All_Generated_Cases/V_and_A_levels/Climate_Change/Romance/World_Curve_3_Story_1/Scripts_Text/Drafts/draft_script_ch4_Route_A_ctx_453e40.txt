### DRAFT
# Route: Additional Route & Route A


[Scene: Municipal Planning Hall | Late Afternoon — Low, Bronze Light Before Storm]
[Visual: Wide shot of the glass façade reflecting a brassy sky; protesters clustered on the steps, banners wet with spray]
[Sound: Distant projector hum, a rising tide of voices; gulls cut across like ragged punctuation]
[Music: Tense, pulsing percussion under a low synth pad]

You are already on the steps before your voice finds the cadence of a chant. Your hand closes around the compass at your throat without thinking—brass against damp fabric, a private instrument to steady you when everything else shakes.

Rosa stands near the column, shoulders folded into her apron, eyes like coal that refuses to cool. Kai moves between clusters of people, passing off sandwiches and rain blankets with quick, practical tenderness. Volunteers you know by name—Luis, Amina, two teenagers from the Flats with seaweed braided into their hair—press close to you as if proximity could make you less alone.

Eloise appears at the entrance as the chant swells, the projectors painting a thin blue line across her coat like a manifesto she’s already worn. Her smile is the same as before: composed, calibrated, and impossible to read.

Volunteer (shouting): "No homes into developers' pockets! No shield without people!"

You step forward and lift your voice so it can be heard over the crowd and the ocean that always seems to be listening.

You: "This isn't about politics—it's about people! We will not trade our streets for a glossy promenade!"

Eloise (from the hall, cool): "Bring me your proposal. Sit at the table. We can find a path forward together."

You (the words like stone): "Work with us? Or work over us? You said 'together'—but who's on the map, Eloise? Who gets the promise, and who gets the paperwork?"

Eloise: "Those are hard questions. Bring them to the advisory board—safety doesn't come from shouting. It comes from structure."

There is a tightness behind your ribs, a small animal panic. Her words feel like a ledger ledgered in other people's ink. You can see the municipal security collecting behind the glass doors, like a dark tide.

Rosa (soft but firm): "Mara—" (she calls you by the name that makes your shoulders relax for half a beat) "—remember the Flats. Remember the cordgrass. They need time to root. They need human hands, not high walls that forget names."

You: "Then we will give them time. We will not let them be moved like chess pieces."

A murmur passes through the crowd, an ember shared. The officers shift. Metal rattles—bike cops, a chain-locked barrier being wheeled into place.

You don't let the choice stretch into indecision. The decision is not a luxury. Security posture hardens; helmets gleam like threatened gulls.

Kai (arranging volunteers): "We can stretch a human chain—block the side gates and keep them from hauling out construction trucks. Or—" (he glances at you, urgent) "—we can march to the harbor road and make them see the Flats as a place with faces, not a line on a screen."

Ibe appears at your shoulder, sweat already in the hair at his temple though the air is cool. He smells of solder and rain, a sharp, metallic warmth that steadies you.

Ibe: "I've got a crew that can reinforce the reed fences at high tide. But I need eyes and hands there now. If those trucks roll, we lose the ground we just reclaimed."

You: "Then we buy time. We shout. We march. We make the Hall choose between the optics of a barrier and the weight of a crowd that remembers its neighbors."

Ibe (low, quick): "If the weather turns, if the sirens go—Mara, we can't be everywhere. Tell me where to put the pumps. Tell me who gets the high ground."

You: "South channel. Two pump units. Kai takes the north access with Rosa's team. Ibe—call Samira. Tell her to freeze any permits and to stay on the record that the Flats are being actively reinforced by volunteers. Get every pair of boots you can."

Ibe hesitates a breath, the carved talisman at his throat rubbing into his palm like a small, private prayer.

Ibe: "I can do that. But—" (he looks at you, and the question in his eyes is not technical) "—what about you? If things turn, if security pushes—"

You feel the room narrow around that syllable. There are other costs you didn't want to map onto your chest: arrests, bruises, a night in the holding cell where the lights are too bright and the walls smell of bleach and fear. But there is also the Flats—mud like memory, Rosa's hands like a promise.

You (breathe): "If I have to stand between them and the doors, I will. But the Flats stay a priority. Move now."

[Visual: The chain barrier clangs into place; a uniformed line forms like a second shore. Rain begins—first like a fine mist, then a sting. Clouds roll in fast, like someone dragging a blanket across the sun.]
[Sound: A municipal alert tone blooms through phones; a test siren wails and cuts off, leaving a hollow in the air]

Security (through a PA, clipped): "You are ordered to disperse. This is an unlawful assembly. Move to the designated area or you will be removed."

You: "We are not dispersing. We are asking for a seat, for time, for community protections."

Security (stepping forward): "Last warning."

A push forms at the front of the crowd—a physical pressure that translates the tension into a single motion. Someone grabs a banner pole; it bends like a soft thing. Your forearm meets another hand—callused, warm, foreign and familiar—and you are aware of the contact like a live wire.

Volunteer (near you, whisper): "They're bringing in the trucks."

You (voice fraying but sharp): "Block the access now. If they try to load equipment, pull them into the crowd. Make it slow. Make it visible."

The officers step up their formation. One of them—young, helmet askew, eyes uncertain—reaches for a baton and then lets his hand drop. The hesitation is a fissure you can imagine widening into something fatal if not bridged.

Eloise watches from the glass. For a fraction of a second, the projector's blue line glows across her face like a cipher. Then she turns away, as if to hide the look you know you caught: a private doubt she will call weakness later.

Rain shifts to harder droplets, enough to sting when they hit the skin. A gust from the harbor brings the smell of overturned seaweed and diesel. Your hair clings to your cheek. The compass at your throat cools beneath your palm; the brass is suddenly heavy.

Ibe's radio voice is clipped: "They've loaded a crate. Security claims it's 'emergency materials.' It's the wrong trucks."

You: "Stop them."

Ibe (into the radio, louder): "Stop the convoy. Block the lane. Kai—cut off the quay access. Rosa—get the cordgrass teams to anchor the loose sections. Volunteers—link arms and form the human chain. Now."

You move with them, not above them. You plant yourself between the doors and the nearest truck as it tries to edge forward. The driver looks like a man who hasn't slept; his hands jitter on the wheel. He doesn't want this. Maybe he needs the paycheck more than he believes the plan.

Security shoves. A woman falls; her raincoat blooms like a wound. Someone screams. The world stabs into high definition—salt in your mouth, the burn of cold wind, the sharp metallic clank of the barrier—and your vision narrows to the small, precise things you can do for a person right in front of you.

Volunteer (grabbing the fallen woman's shoulder): "Are you alright? Talk to me—what's your name?"

Fallen Woman (breathing hard): "Lina. My—my little one—"

You: "We will keep her place. We will keep her home. Hold on, Lina."

Rosa kneels, hands already working to lift, to stabilize—not a sentiment but a craft. You taste despair like iron; it sits over the back of your tongue. The officers' boots pounded stone. One officer's gloved hand closes around a volunteer's wrist; the volunteer's fingers go white around the banner pole.

Security (a voice now close, clinical): "You are obstructing civic operations."

You (voice small but endless): "And you are obstructing our lives."

Ibe's hand finds your shoulder—brief, grounding. He looks at you like someone measuring whether to cling or cut loose.

Ibe (urgent): "We have to pull people back to staged safety if the sirens come. I can hold the Flats with my crew, but if the storm hits during this, it's going to be chaos."

You: "Then we split—some stay to press, some go to secure the Flats. Call out names. Use the WhatsApp chain; use the whistles. Do not leave the kids."

[Sound: A municipal emergency alert slices through the crowd—a voice both calm and impossible—'Storm Warning: Immediate coastal surge possible within hours. Evacuation advisory issued for low-lying districts.']
[Music: Percussion accelerates; strings cut in like a breath held too long]

The sky rips open in a single long bar of thunder. A gust sends banners snapping into frayed ribbons. The crowd's shouts break into cracks of panic and resolve.

You look at Rosa, at Ibe, at Kai. You look at Eloise's reflection in the tower's glass—and you already know the ledger will not care about names.

You (internal): This is the hour you swore you could keep people safe. This is the hour when plans meet weather and bureaucracies meet bodies. There is no good map for what happens when both fail at once.

You move through the crowd, shouting specific names, reallocating bodies like lifelines.

You (to the crowd): "Teams A, B, and C—south channel now. Kai, hold the north quay. Rosa, with me—medical triage on the steps. Ibe, confirm pump deployment. Volunteers—if you cannot go, you keep the line. No one goes alone."

Ibe (tight smile): "Copy that. Samira is on a hold—she says the legal team is trying to freeze the approvals. She's trying to make it official that the Flats are active. I'm sending photos."

The clash at the gate becomes a physical grammar: arms, banners, the press of chests. An officer pushes back harder and a sound like brittle wood splinters—someone's banner pole snaps into two. The snapped wood falls like an accusation.

A child cries somewhere, and the sound pinches you raw. You move toward it, part leader, part mother, part immediate responder, your hands already checking for breathing, a pulse, a name. But there are too many names tonight.

[Visual: Close on your palm as the compass slips free and falls onto the wet stone—brass catching the light, a tiny, bright thing in a field of gray]
[Sound: Thunder swallowing the city, a howling wind through the columns]

You drop to your knees to pick it up. Fingers clumsy. The compass is slick with rain. As you close your hand around it, someone tugs at your sleeve—an officer, firm, not careless. He is not unkind. He is a cog. He tells you to disperse.

You stand. Your voice is a thread trying to hold a rope that wants to snap.

You: "If you remove us, you remove the witnesses to whatever is unloaded. If you proceed without us, this becomes something the city cannot explain away."

Officer (voice hollow): "I am following orders."

Rosa (without ceremony): "Then follow the order to protect people. Ask your superiors why they are loading equipment while the sea is sending warnings. Ask them if they've ever touched the mud with callused hands."

The officer's jaw tightens. A radio crackles. Someone near the trucks yells that a loader has started to roll. The moment stretches like a taut cable ready to break.

You glance at Ibe—his face is a map with all the right coordinates. He nods, small, like a knife that decides to cut a tether. He steps away to his phone, fingers moving at the speed of someone who knows how to build things when the world is folding.

Ibe (quiet): "Mara. If the surge comes before we can stop them—"

You (whisper, all the weight of your promise in it): "Then we do what we've always done. We hold people. We plant the cordgrass. We make noise so they remember."

A young officer lunges. Hands close on a volunteer. The first scuffle is not theatrical; it is jagged, human, a bruise forming where a promise is broken. The crowd roils, some pushing back, some trying to pull people away from the line of enforcement. Rain sluices down your neck.

Your throat burns—not from pepper or smoke, but from the strain of trying to be the line between two kinds of loss. The incalculable cost of being right flashes before you in a sudden, efficient terror: homes gone, names on lists, Rosa's hands empty of soil.

You lock eyes with Eloise for a second that holds like a verdict. Her face is a portrait of someone who has chosen the plan. The projector's blue line divides the room, but the sky is already fuller than any line can contain.

You: "We will not let the Flats be erased while you clap tariffs on the books."

Eloise (soft): "Then stop the trucks. Prove your model. If you can get your hybrid proposal signed by the community leaders and Samira within twenty-four hours, I will hold the permits."

There is a sharp intake everywhere—hope like a live wire. But then the siren wails again, longer, and the rain pivots like a blade.

You (internal): The offer feels like a promise wrapped in time; twenty-four hours could be a trap or a doorway. You have hours at best before the surge reported on every transmitter becomes something physical and unbending.

You look at the compass in your hand as if it can tell you which betrayal will hurt less.

[Visual: The camera pulls back to show the crowd, the Hall like a hard certainty behind them, the sea an imminent sentence beyond the quay]
[Music: A single, sustained chord that feels like a held breath]

You make a quick choice—split forces, maintain the human chain, send the photos and the legal hold to Samira, and pray the city's bureaucracy can run slow when the storm runs fast.

Your last clear thought before everything compresses into motion is simple and terrible: there are only so many things you can hold.

[Page-Turn Moment: You hear a different sound under the rain now—a low, rising pressure that isn't just weather. It is the sea making its claim. You feel, with the absurd clarity that belonging brings, that some ledger will be written tonight whether you sign it or not. The compass in your hand is warm, and you press it to your palm as if you could fix fate to a finger's span.]
