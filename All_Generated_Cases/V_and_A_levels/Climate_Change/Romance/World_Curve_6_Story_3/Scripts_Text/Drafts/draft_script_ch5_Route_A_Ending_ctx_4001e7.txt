### DRAFT
# Route: Route A (Ending)


[Scene: The Harbor & Living Sea Wall | Morning]
[Visual: Mist lifting over the harbor; the living seawall's reeds glint with beads of salt. Wet planks reflect the pale sky. Volunteers move like a slow tide along the edge.]
[Music: A single high violin note, held; under it, a low industrial thrum grows—heartbeat to engine]
[Sound: Damp footfalls, rope creaks, distant gulls]

You came here with the taste of the council room still on your teeth — the lift of a crowd, the unfinished math of consequence. You can feel that weight in the way your shoulders lock when a volunteer looks to you for the next instruction. This morning the town looks at you as if you are the hinge in a door that must not swing shut.

Elias stands near the dock with a thermos cupped in both hands, leaning on a coil of rope as if it were proof he can stay upright. When he sees you he lifts the thermos the way someone raises a flag. His smile is small, steady; for a second your chest forgets the meeting and remembers how his laugh used to fill empty rooms. Then the tide gauge on the post ticks up and the present returns.

You walk the seawall, palm on the damp stone, reading the instruments the way a priest reads scripture. Gauges blink green; a sensor on a reed mat blinks amber. Your voice moves down the line — not a speech, but a machine of small commands.

You: "Anchor lines, tighten two meters. Reed mats: stagger the seams. Watch the western swallow of wave refraction—set the second buffer line."

Rosa: "Got it. Luca, you and Priya take the east flank. Don't let that spool run free."

Luca: (breathing hard) "Been waiting for this. We got it, Maya."

You're aware of every smell: seaweed sharp as citrus, old tar soft under the planks, coffee smoke from a vendor's cart. The salt on your lips burns like a promise. The crowd murmurs—neighbors, small-business owners, a few reporters with cheap umbrellas. Tamsin's delegation stands apart, umbrellas immaculate, their shoes not yet salted.

[Scene: Docks | Mid-morning]
[Visual: The tide is higher now; wave tips foam against the living wall. Volunteers haul rock bags, their hands red from cold and motion.]
[Music: Rhythmic percussion joins—the tempo quickens, a metronome speeding up]
[Sound: Shouts, the slap of waves, the metallic clink of tools]

For a while the plan sings. Waves meet the planted reeds and break into polite, forgiving shushes. The marsh holds like a palm under the sea's wrist. People clap—quiet, stunned—when a higher swell folds away without swallowing the dock. You think: this is it. This is what we said would happen.

Elias moves beside you, handing over a rolled tarpaulin and a tally. His voice slides easily into the pauses you leave.

Elias: "You seeing that? The refraction at the northern point—it's doing the trick. The models underpredicted the attenuation."

You: (exhaling) "They always underpredict the kindness of a place when people fight for it. Look how they're—"

Elias: "—pulling together. Yeah." He hesitates, then adds, low, "You did good, Maya."

The words catch you off guard and for a heartbeat you think of small, private things — the compass at your throat, a boy who used to run laughing down this very boardwalk. Your throat fills with that strange, grateful ache that comes with being seen right before the world demands more.

Tamsin doesn't clap. She watches with an unreadable face, hands folded over a tablet as if she were holding a verdict. When she speaks later, her voice will be a scalpel. For now she is a sculpture in the rain, perfect and distant.

Rosa pulls you into the center of a half-circle of volunteers, cheeks flushed, hair salted. Her hands are full of rope and stubborn pride.

Rosa: "Look at this. People came. People who said they were done showed up."

You: "They showed up because you knocked door to door. Because Luca and Priya and the fishers spent their nights sewing reed mats instead of sleeping. This is ours."

Elias: (watching the crowd) "Exactly. The story isn't 'we lost nothing'—it's 'we cared enough to try a different thing.'"

Luca: "They'll try to spin it either way. But for now—" He grins, brief and fierce. "For now we have mud on our boots and smiles on our faces."

[Scene: Docks -> Surge | Mid-morning]
[Visual: The sky beyond the horizon bruises. A cold green light gathers under the low clouds; the sea breathes in twice and draws closer.]
[Music: Strings shred into dissonance; percussion becomes a staccato panic]
[Sound: A low roar like a distant train; wind slaps; a single sharp crack of wood]

Then the sea surprises you.

The surge is not a slow, polite rise but a hungry, sudden inhalation of water. The tide gauge's digital face jumps. The second buffer line — the improvised anchors you placed with laughter and prayer — strains in a way the models didn't promise. One anchor shudders. The next slips.

You feel the change before the cry does — a physical thrum in your teeth and in the soles of your boots. The world tilts toward motion. You bark commands so forcefully your voice cracks; the air around them renders them small and thin.

You: "Hold the east line! Rosa, Luca, shove your weight into it! Priya — cut the slack, now!"

Rosa: "On it! Pull! Pull!"

Luca: "I can't get purchase—mud's giving way!"

Hands slip. Mud loosens like old promises. A coil of rope slides off a stone; a reed mat peels like wet paper. Volunteers scream as a chunk of the old boardwalk that was already hollowed by rot gives a wet, guilty groan and collapses into the water with a sound like a dozen doors slamming.

[Visual: A vendor's stall — colorful tarps, jars of preserves, string lights — tilts. Water finds the opening. The smell of caramelizing sugar and salt blends into something sharp and unfair.]
[Sound: Shouts escalate into a chorus; the roar of the water becomes a presence]

You lunge. Mud splashes into your boots, cold and biting. You grab a line and feel it chafe through your gloves, each fiber a threat as much as an anchor. Rosa's knuckles are white, throat raw with a sound that is half prayer. Luca's face is tight, young and furious.

Elias is at your side in a heartbeat, his shoulder pressing into yours as you both take a weight that is too much for one set of hands. He looks at you and for the first time you see the rawness under his steady face—fear braided with the thing you both tried to keep out of daylight.

Elias: (shouting over the surge) "Maya — the vendor! Get the shutters! Two people, now!"

You: "Priya! Help with the shutters! Luca, Rosa — tie that rope to the piling!"

Priya: "I'm trying! The hinges are corroded!"

Someone's voice — a neighbor's — is sobbing somewhere behind you. You taste salt and iron. Your heart bangs against your ribs like someone trying to get out.

Tamsin stands at a distance, flanked by assistants, umbrella useless in the wet. Her mouth is a thin line. She steps forward once, then back; she does not get closer because getting closer means getting dirty, and she came prepared to be clean.

Tamsin: (measured) "We will need an assessment of the losses. Emergency relief will be considered."

Her words are a scalpel that finishes the wound the sea started. They are careful. They are already a script. You do not trust the generosity in them.

[Music: A single brass note, then silence beneath the chaos—an awful, expanding drum]
[Visual: The boardwalk gap yawns; a vendor's stall topples as jars smash, orange jam smearing like sunset gone wrong]

You fight with numbness and muscle. You pull until your arms burn, until the rope bites into your palms and you don't feel anything but the rhythm of effort, the blunt arithmetic of rescue. When the water finally ceases to batter and the pieces settle into a shuddering quiet, the outcome is small and terrible.

A stretch of the old boardwalk is gone; a vendor's shop is flooded and ruined. Someone counts with the look of someone cataloging losses — three wheelbarrows of inventory gone, twenty customers' meals unserved; a child's favorite hat floating in brackish ruin.

Elias meets your eye. His smile is a fissure now — grief passing through it like smoke. He opens his mouth as if to offer some argument against the scale of the damage, a counter-evidence to the losses. He closes it again.

Elias: (soft) "We saved a lot of the harbor. We—"

You: (cutting) "Saved a lot. Not everything."

He doesn't argue. Neither do you, not because you're resigned but because a new, colder clarity has been planted in both of you. The demonstration has been what you hoped and feared: a proof of concept and a spectacle of failure at the same time.

[Scene: The Boardwalk Aftermath | Late Morning]
[Visual: People stand on the ruined edge of the planks, boots caked; some kneel to salvage what they can. The sky is pale; a leaking umbrella flips like a surrendered flag.]
[Music: Sparse piano lines, each note spaced wide, the tempo of exhausted breathing]
[Sound: Murmurs, the drip of water from soaked tarps, the occasional sob]

Conversations circle like scavengers. The crowd that had cheered now debates, and their voices have teeth.

Vendor (angry, shaking): "You promised this would protect us. You promised—"

Rosa: (steady, furious) "We promised a different future. Not a promise of perfection. You heard us, you came, and we tried."

Council aide: (quietly) "This shows scale. It's proof that without a larger wall—"

A ripple of agreement. Your stomach flips. People you don't even know look at the breach and imagine the worst-case scenarios you have lived with at night. In the hush the council members exchange glances like conspirators closing ranks.

Tamsin approaches at last. She steps over a soft, ruined plank with a policy-maker's care and looks at the damage as if it were a dataset she could fold into a report.

Tamsin: (cool) "The demonstration demonstrated two things: community effort and clear vulnerability. The region's funding window opens if we can present this as grounds for a comprehensive, engineered seawall."

You: (voice low) "So the collapse is now your lever."

Tamsin: (a brief, almost apologetic tilt) "I do not celebrate anyone's loss. I celebrate an opportunity to prevent larger loss."

You search her face for any sign of mercy and find instead a careful arithmetic. Her posture doesn't hold the weight of the people whose roofs leak tonight.

Elias: (to Tamsin) "We can develop a hybrid—living defenses with targeted structural reinforcement. There is a middle ground."

Tamsin: (meeting his gaze squarely) "A hybrid is more expensive, more complex, and slower. We need something that secures this town quickly."

Elias: (sharp) "Quickly doesn't mean right. We can't just concrete over livelihoods."

Tamsin: (cooler) "And we can't watch people lose businesses while we debate aesthetics."

The exchange is polite until it is not. Bodies gather around the argument—neighbors hearing words that will become headlines. You stand between them like a seam trying to hold the future from unraveling.

You: (thinking) Your demonstration has given them the proof they wanted. But it has also given them the story they wanted to justify what you feared. The irony tastes like rust. You can see the headlines already: 'Harbor Vulnerable; Experts Call for Large-Scale Intervention.' You wanted stewardship. The sea handed them a rationale for overhaul.

Luca kneels beside the ruined stall, pulling out a soggy photograph of the owner's daughter. He passes it to you without looking up.

Luca: "She came to help. She lost her summer wages."

You take the photo — a sunburned girl smiling with two missing front teeth — and your hands tremble.

Elias reaches for your shoulder. His touch is warm and human among a lot of clinical voices. For a moment there is a possibility — stay and fight this with him, or hold the losses and look for repair in other ways. The choice feels like someone pulling at both sides of your ribcage.

You: (internal) You have rallied your people. The pilot showed tenderness and the limits of tenderness in the face of a hungry sea. The town's story has tilted. The council's narrative will now be spiked with urgency you didn't invite but cannot deny.

Rosa: (quiet, resolute) "We rebuild what we can. We press for mitigation, not erasure."

Tamsin: (a pause; then, quietly) "We will write the emergency package. Expect a meeting with regional officials."

You: (a bitter laugh unspooling) "And the 'we' becomes a line item. Plans on paper are not the same as hands in the mud."

You look at Elias again. Grief has left spoor on both your faces, but also a strange, feral clarity—like a lighthouse that has been dented but still stares down the night. The loss sharpens arguments you never wanted sharpened. It stitches new seams where old beliefs must be reexamined.

You feel the terrible pride of someone who has succeeded in inspiring action but failed to keep the cost from landing on the poorest of your neighbors. The town will now be propelled toward scale — fast money, heavy machines, tidy solutions that cut through complexity. Whether those solutions honor the town or hollow it out will be the next, bitter argument.

[Music: Long, low cello note. Rain begins again, small and steady]
[Visual: Close on your hands — rope burns, salt-crusted — then lift to the horizon. The sea is both threat and teacher: indifferent, enormous, honest.]

You breathe in the salt and the smoke of a vendor's ruined stove and the cold, metallic tang of council politics. You let the compass at your throat click softly against your sternum — the harbor coordinates a small, private map of where you began and what you refuse to lose.

Elias squeezes your hand once, quick and almost apologetic. His eyes are wet. You do not know what his next choice will be. You do not know if the town will choose to hold to its character or to exchange it for immediate safety. You know only that you have rallied people, and that their losses have tightened other people's resolve into a weapon that may not be wielded with care.

You stand on the edge of something: victory and ruin braided together. The air tastes of copper and rain and a future that will demand harder decisions.

[Music: The orchestra thins to a single, sustained dissonant chord that hangs like a question]
