[Scene: Tidehaven Town Hall | Afternoon]
[Visual: Amber lamps cast a warm, forgiving light over wooden benches; maps in teal and ochre pinned to cork boards; a long oak table crawled with papers and a tablet displaying shifting elevation overlays]
[Music: Low, somber piano with distant, steady strings]
[Sound: Murmurs of the council, the scratch of pens, a coffee cup set down with a soft clink]

You sit with the compass pendant tucked against your sternum, its brass cool under your jacket. The chain is a small, steady weight you can palm when words get too loud. The room smells like old wood and stale coffee—an odor that belongs to meetings and promises, the slow scent of municipal life.

Evelyn moves between stacks of petitions and developer memos, her smile a practiced thing that arrives with exhaustion in its seams. She sets a folder in front of you and for a second you read more into the crease of her mouth than the line items on the page.

Evelyn: "Mira, thank you for coming. Sit—please. We have a lot to get through."

You fold your fingers over the pendant. The council chamber feels smaller than the lab did, as if decisions made here will be bound in parchment and memory rather than code. You think of the maps you've stared at in fluorescent light, the teal-and-ochre layers that speak of time and thresholds. They are beautiful and cruel in the same breath.

[Visual: Asha steps to the front, slate-gray coat against the warm lamps; tablet in hand displays a precise overlay of levees and retreat zones]
[Sound: Quiet intake of breath; the hum of the tablet as Asha taps through slides]

Asha: "I appreciate the invitation. The regional models make the choice clear—fast, decisive infrastructure reduces risk across the basin. Flood gates at the harbor mouth, reinforced berms along the low stretches, and designated managed retreat corridors. The math is simple."

Her voice is clipped, practiced; the room feels the sterility in it like a draft. You watch Asha's steel-blue eyes sweep the room—no warmth, just appraisal. She slides a map toward the council's center; lines and numbers fall into place with a competence that leaves little room for sentiment.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "The models show a narrow window for effective intervention if we prioritize hard engineering. That said—" (she pauses, pulling at the sleeve of her coat) "—combined approaches can extend that window, but only if funding and implementation timelines align."

Jonah arrives late. He smells of the harbor—fish oil and diesel, rope and salt—like a person who has the sea under his nails. He drops into a bench with a soft thump, jeans rolled, waders still scuffed. The mood shifts when he speaks; his voice is worn but steady.

Jonah: "Asha's plan takes a lot of land from us—no sugarcoating it. Flood gates and berms? Fine for some places. Here, those structures cut off nets, change currents, and push marshes to the wrong edge. We can do living shorelines. Marsh planting. Breakwaters built with local crews. Keep the fishing access open."

The meeting tilts between two logics—one scalpel-sharp, one callused and patient. Your notebook, half-full of tide-lines and Bento's old stories, sits at your elbow. You didn't come here to tilt a balance you don't fully understand, but the room expects you to weigh their scales.

Evelyn: "Mira, you're the engineer who left and came back. We need someone to shepherd a pilot—someone who can make technical proposals legible to residents and funders."

Her eyes find yours with the gravity of a friend asking a last favor. You can see the political arithmetic in the way her hands fold.

Asha: (without looking away from the tablet) "And with technical leadership we can fast-track the design, apply for regional resilience grants, and set enforceable timelines. Delay means lock-in of risk."

Jonah: "And 'fast-track' becomes 'fast-cut' for the harbor. You can't just draw lines and call it community. People there—" (he gestures toward the audience of residents, their faces tight in the amber light) "—have lives tied to access. You bulldoze access and you bulldoze livelihoods."

Bento clears his throat, the sound an old rope rubbing over a cleat. His voice is a weathered instrument—not loud, but every syllable carries weight.

Bento: "When I was a boy the tides were different. We read the water like a book. Not every problem needs a wall. Not every wall remembers the names of the people it cuts off."

Dr. Lian: "Traditional knowledge matters. It can guide restoration priorities. But we can't ignore the statistical realities—if the storm surge exceeds a threshold, the community's current layout stops being viable."

You feel the sentences coil inside you—equations and stories, models and marled histories, each one a partial truth. You have proposed living shoreline attachments in lab simulations; you've also modeled berm scenarios that reduce peak inundation. Standing in the middle of the room, you know both approaches can be right and wrong in ways that bruise people.

You open your mouth, then close it. Conversation splinters into quieter, sharper threads: whispered concerns from councilors, a developer's clipped note passed behind a hand, murmured names of properties at risk. It is in these small, human noises that the moral geometry of the problem shows itself.

Evelyn: "Mira—what do you think? Can you lead a pilot? The grant window closes in six weeks."

Asha: (leaning forward) "We need someone who will sign technical plans that stand up in arbitration. Deliberation is a luxury we can't afford."

Jonah: (brittle) "And we need someone who will walk the flats at dawn and understand the tide by touch. That's not a checkbox."

Your throat tightens. Every path from here slices some memory, some livelihood. You think of your father's boat—wood and salt and a name you barely say—and that guilty ledger that makes your hands steady and your heart small.

A councilor in the back—an earthy woman with a ring of keys at her belt—leans forward.

Councilor: "People in the flats don't want to be told to leave on a map. They want promises that help them stay or choices that don't feel like exile. Words like 'managed retreat' sound like eviction notices."

Asha: "Managed retreat, when applied judiciously, saves lives and resources. It is not eviction; it is planning."

The word 'planning' lands like a stone in still water. You want to argue for nuance—managed retreat with generous buyouts, relocation assistance, cultural preservation. You want to say the words that make policy humane. But you also worry that nuance is what tanks funding boards and makes legal counsel nervous.

Dr. Lian: "Hybrid solutions can be engineered. We can phase interventions—pilot living shorelines where they have the best chance, hard infrastructure where the risk is intolerable. But that requires a coordinator who understands both community practices and the math."

Bento: "And a place that remembers why it's worth saving. Not everything about Tidehaven fits on a chart."

Jonah looks at you; his amber-brown eyes do not ask for your consent so much as your solidarity. You feel the weight of that expected solidarity like the pull of a current.

You try to imagine the pilot: marsh grasses planted with neighbor hands, a small breakwater built of oyster shell and reclaimed timber, contour lines approved by a funder who needs signatures and certainty. Or else, a scarped berm, lights on mechanical arms, people moved inland with little more than a file of forms.

A hush takes the room. For a moment the only sound is your breathing and a distant gull pitched thin through the town hall's windows.

You rise because the room is waiting in a way that feels like accusation. Your voice is not steady at first.

You: "I—" (you close your eyes briefly, feeling the weight of the compass) "—I can draft a proposal that combines field-led restoration and targeted infrastructure. But I can't promise it will look like any single plan on that table. The path forward has to be defensible in funding terms and legible to the people whose lives we alter."

Asha: (softening, for the first time) "Will you be willing to sign technical conclusions? To stand behind the numbers when councilors and lawyers push back?"

You: "I will stand behind the analysis. But analytics separate from lived experience make poor policy. If we're going to be protective, we need protection that remembers people."

Jonah: "So—are you with us? With the people out on the flats?"

Asha's jaw tightens; she looks like she might argue, but Evelyn lays a hand briefly on her arm, the gesture equal parts plea and pressure.

Evelyn: "We need a plan to present to regional authorities. A hybrid draft might be the bridge. Mira, will you chair the technical committee and work with both teams?"

You feel every eye on you—expectation, calculation, old friendship, and something complex from Jonah that you refuse to name in public. The pendant at your throat grows hot as your pulse matches the tolling of your own internal bell.

Words tumble—agreements, grudges, legal caveats scribbled into margins. The council votes to convene a technical committee. Evelyn's thanks is small and human. Asha offers a clipped, professional nod; Jonah's smile is tight. Bento touches your arm with a fisher's hand and says one thing you can't map onto policy.

Bento: "Remember the song of the harbor. It will tell you when to build and when to let go."

You do not know whether he means the same kind of song as Asha's models, but the image steadies you a little.

[Visual: The meeting disperses; lamplight pools on the table like slow amber sea]
[Sound: Chairs scrape; a distant clock begins to measure out the rest of the afternoon]

You gather your notebook, notes of tide-lines smudging with the oil of fingerprints. The weight of the pendant is familiar, and yet heavier now. The council has given you a role that sits between steel and moss, between policy and mud. It is exactly the kind of compromise that could save the town or break it in ways you can already imagine.

Evelyn: (quiet, between friend and mayor) "I'll stand with you in council. But everyone will be watching what you bring back."

You: "I know. I'll be careful."

Jonah: (under his breath) "Careful won't be enough if careful means standing still."

His words are not a reprimand so much as a warning. You leave the town hall with your coat collar up against an April drizzle that smells faintly of ozone and sea. The bell above the town hall tolls—low and insistent.

[Scene: Tidehaven Boardwalk | Dusk]
[Visual: The boardwalk is slick with rain; the sea is a thin mirror stretching toward a horizon that looks smaller than you remember; lamps pull the fog into gold rings]
[Music: Single low chord sustained, then a sparse piano theme like an unanswered question]
[Sound: Bell tolling; distant gulls; the soft hiss of tide against pilings]

You step alone onto the boardwalk. The council's amber light falls away behind you and the wet air closes in. The sea reflects a thin silver threat that changes with every shifting breath of wind. Your notebook is soaked at the corner where you kept Bento's notes; the ink runs in lines that could be mistakes or new maps.

Your chest feels hollow and full at once—the way grief and obligation can be twin weights.

You look across the harbor at the places you will, perhaps, be asked to reimagine. The thought of shorelines drawn as lines on a map feels suddenly like drawing names in sand.

The bell tolls again, lonelier this time, and the sound threads through the fog. You let the pendant rest heavy against your throat and allow, for a moment, the rawness you have been avoiding—the sense that you must make a choice that will both save and wound.

The town breathes around you, held between plans and promises.
