[Scene: City Hall Glass Atrium (Closed-door Briefing) | Late Night]
[Visual: Pale fluorescent panels, a bank of tide-forecast screens casting cobalt light]
[Music: Low, steady synth — a single minor chord looping like a tide]
[Sound: Quiet radio chatter through a wall, the soft click of a presentation remote]

You walk back into the atrium as if through shallow water — each step slaps small and final against the polished concrete. The council chambers have emptied into a string of after-hours rooms and corridors where decisions unlatch into action: contracts being notarized down the hall, crews getting their manifests, a printer biting through receipts like teeth through paperwork.

Jun stands by a screen, his coat a dark shape against the maps. He is tidy in a way work likes: lines folded into a plan. When he turns toward you there is the memory of childhood tides — of rope climbs and shared pie — braided into the polite tilt of his head. Now those same lines read as policy.

Jun Park: "Good. You're here. We need the liaison to keep things moving without creating panic."

He doesn't sound harsh. He sounds like a captain who knows the ship must alter course even if a few cabins are left behind. Lian Mor watches from the table, her face an unreadable calm that offsets the edge in Jun's measured voice. A council clerk lays a thin folder on the table and slides a tablet toward you; the screen glows with black text and red highlights.

You take the tablet. The numbers are clinical: acreage, projected savings, timelines. Between the columns is a paragraph stamped CONFIDENTIAL — a clause that would let the city accelerate land acquisition under emergency authority, shortening notice periods and streamlining compulsory purchase.

Your stomach tightens. The clause reads like a scalpel: efficient, precise, and indifferent.

Jun Park: (softly) "It allows us to secure critical buffer zones before the next season. The delays we saw last month cost people their homes when the sea took the jetty. We can't repeat that."

Your role is clear on the surface: translate policy into care. But beneath it you feel the ledger of names you've been keeping — your father's boat, Ivy's laugh, Rosa's hands smoothing a quilt gone salt-stiff. This clause turns those names into rows, into assets to be moved.

You open your mouth. Jun tilts his head as if following a current.

Jun Park: "You know how to move through here without inflaming people. Keep me informed. If you need exceptions, take them up with me directly."

There is patience layered over necessity in his tone. He believes in triage by outcomes; you believe in triage by people. The difference is a slow erosion, like rust working from the inside out.

Lian Mor: (folding her hands) "Mara — we appreciate your position. We need a liaison who can reconcile community needs with municipal timelines. The last thing we can afford is ceremony when the water is rising."

Her words are a push and a shield at once. She is not unkind; she is not cruel. She is the mechanism that will make the clause teeth sink quicker.

You press your thumb to the tablet's cold edge and feel the tiny nails of your palm pick up salt from the air.

Theo is elbow-deep in tubing, cursing affectionately at a pump. Elias stands beside a tank, chin propped on his hand, watching a colony of mussels settle their feet.

[Scene: The Basin (Community Floodplain Garden) | Dusk]
[Visual: Mud paths, young mangroves like clustered teeth, puddles reflecting a bruised sky]
[Sound: Distant engines; a child's muffled laughter turning brittle; the squelch of boots through mud]
[Music: Sparse piano, the notes spaced like slow breaths]

By the time you reach the Basin, the air is thick with the scent of wet earth and young growth. Community art — a hanging assemblage of salvaged metal and sea glass — moves in the low breeze. Ivy is there, sleeves rolled, helping a family pack boxes into a cart. Her hair is sun-freckled and furious; she doesn't look up when you approach, but you catch the quiver at the corner of her mouth.

Ivy Kestrel: (without looking) "They can't just 'relocate' Grandma's garden. She buried the first maps there."

Her voice breaks as she pulls a crate closed. You kneel to help and feel the mud give under your boots like a small guilty thing.

Rosa Alvarez appears from the path like a memory made flesh: shawls layered, eyes deep as old water. She watches the crews measure a line with a laser; Lian's hired team stands efficient and small against the green.

Rosa Alvarez: (softly) "They count gardens as square footage. They count childhood as a legal status. You have to decide what you let them count."

You fold your fingers into the fabric of your jacket and feel the moth-worn thread. A crew leader approaches with a tablet and a polite, rehearsed smile. He scrolls through an acquisition list: house numbers, owner names redacted, compensation brackets. One line in bright red: "Immediate acquisition — expedited under Clause 12-B."

You recognize the clause number now as if it has weight. It is small on the tablet but heavy in your chest.

Crew Leader: "We need signatures to verify notice. We can offer temporary housing vouchers and moving assistance."

You look at Ivy helping a child close a shoebox. The child's toy — a small wooden boat — sits on the edge of a crate, its paint flaked like old surf.

You imagine delivering compensation forms to Rosa, to Ivy, to the child. The forms would be signatures and vouchers; they would not be memory, and they would not be the slow, stubborn work of community adaptation that you and Elias have been building.

Jun appears at the edge of the Basin, his figure a cut against the sky. He watches for a heartbeat, expression — you know from experience — complicated. He does not look pleased or ashamed; he looks like someone who has agreed to a hard calculus and now checks that the arithmetic holds.

Jun Park: (quietly) "We move now, not later. The buffers must be clear."

You hear the work boots across mud and the clink of measuring tape. You hear the child's small protest as a box is closed. There are a thousand small sounds that add up to a verdict.

Rosa touches your shoulder and speaks without sharpness.

Rosa Alvarez: "You were once the person who kept the ledger for the harbor. Remember well what you were counting."

You can taste iron — not blood, but the metallic tang of decisions made in the dark. The clause is no longer a line in a briefing. It is an instrument that can quiet the Basin in a night.

You know the choices ahead are not abstract. Each path will creak at different seams: the law, the people, your conscience. You are Jun's liaison; you are also Mara Kestrel — the person who taught kids to patch nets on the Strand. The two roles are a pair of mismatched oars.
