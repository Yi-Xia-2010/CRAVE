[Scene: City Hall Glass Atrium | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the atrium — banners from the hearing ruffle on their stands, tide-forecast screens glow cobalt in the shadowed room.]

You walk out beneath the atrium's angled light feeling every eye like a weathered plank pressed into your back. The council’s decision to open the audit was supposed to be a victory — and, in the blunt arithmetic of politics, it is: auditors arrive, doorways are opened, and every ledger must be proved. But victory tastes metallic and thin when funding freezes and crates destined for breakwaters sit in municipal yards untouched.

Rosa is at your side, shawl wrapped tight against a damp wind bled through the glass. Ivy stands a step behind, paint still on her knuckles, staring at the floor as if the salt in the grout might be a map back to what was lost.

Jun stands at the dais as if he belongs in the light. He looks the same from the waist up: immaculate coat, a scale ruler clipped to his belt like a talisman. In his face there is a quality you have learned to read as "complex" — not quite apology, not quite defiance, only the tight calculus of someone already balancing consequence.

You can feel, in the small muscles at the base of your neck, the way the city is leaning on you. The press smells of coffee and wet paper; the atrium air holds the faint tang of sea and old stone. Your journal is heavy in your pocket, damp at the edges where you pressed your palm through the night. Your compass pendant is warm against your sternum, like an ember.

Jun approaches. He does not hurry. He keeps two steps between you, respectful in distance but not in posture.

Jun Park: "I—I didn't want this to be a spectacle, Mara. Transparency was the only path I could see after the evidence came to light."

You: "Evidence that someone sabotaged the project, and someone else's hands were in the manifests. Not transparency, Jun — you opened doors, yes. But you also opened a season of paralysis. Funding frozen, crews sent home. The living reefs are idle while the tide doesn't stop."

Jun Park: (hands raised slightly) "I know. I know the timing is brutal. But if we hide things now, it becomes worse. Audits hurt the project in the short term, but they protect it in the long run."

You: (quiet, sharp) "Long run. People don't always get to wait for your 'long run.' Ivy's neighbors are scraping the floors of their houses tonight for sand and rope because crews are on hold."

Jun Park: (his voice softer) "I didn't want you or anyone to pay for my politics, Mara. I offered oversight. I offered auditors with community liaisons—"

You: "Oversight isn't relief. Audits are paperwork; paperwork doesn't lift a roof in a storm."

Rosa: (intervening, steady) "Jun, we all know the calculus. We also know the cost of delays. The question is how to balance accountability with urgency. Name the balance, and we will help keep it."

Jun looks at Rosa, then at you. For a moment his amber eyes fold inward, as if searching memory. You cannot tell if he is recalculating policy or conscience.

Elias Navin: "I— I told them this, but they wanted numbers, not feeling. Now that numbers are demanded, we're stuck between a watchdog and a clock."

Elias: (a beat, and then) "That makes it sabotage, not just bad timing. The council has to know what this looks like. The press needs the evidence."

You look at the half-finished concrete teeth pointing into the water and feel your stomach as if someone has poured cold tide into it.

[Scene: Old Beacon Lighthouse | Dusk before the cyclone]
[Visual: The battered lighthouse silhouette against a sky bruising into purple; ropes flail on the breakwater, gulls scattered like loose shrapnel.]
[Sound: The first low thunder of the storm far at sea, wind building into a high, keening note; the Beacon's ruined glass clinks gently.]
[Music: Increasing low brass and a sustained cello]

The city issues evacuation orders. The sirens have the same hollow timbre as church bells gone strange. You stand at the edge of the breakwater, the Old Beacon looms, a cathedral of salt and missed repairs, and you replay Jun's face one more time: steady, unreadable.

Jun arrives, coat flapping, not in a press line but alone. He walks up the rocky path, boots crunching on salt-caked stones. He looks smaller against the stairs and tide-sprayed masonry than he ever did in meetings.

Jun Park: (voice rawer than the last time) "Mara."

You: "This is what happens when you choose spectacle over speed?"

Jun Park: "I chose— I chose what I thought would stop the rot. I didn't want to hide things. Hiding kills trust. But... I didn't know it would slow work so thoroughly. I should have anticipated diversion."

You: (anger pricking like cold rain) "You call it 'rot' and you mean the system, but it's people—our neighbors—who fall through."

Jun Park: (a pause, then quietly) "I can see that now."

Rosa: (arriving behind you, voice an old tide) "Seeing is not the same as repair."

Jun Park: "I'm here to help with evacuation logistics. I'm releasing municipal crews to the worst-hit corridors. I'm asking the military liaison for pumps. I'm opening the planbook and I will accept oversight. I will—"

You: (cutting) "Will you accept blame too? Will you stand and tell the city where the manifests were altered? Will you tell them when you let funding be reallocated?"

Jun Park: (his jaw clenches) "I won't lie about what happened. I will not pretend politics did not compromise decisions. I can not undo what's done, Mara, but—" He stops. For the first time you see a man with his public armor off: his brow furrows, mouth working around words that taste like rust.

Elias stands a few paces back, wrapped in a donated raincoat. His sensor band flashes, then stabilizes. His hands are caked in mud. He looks at you, then at Jun, then at the horizon where the first walls of cloud roll.

Elias Navin: "We need to move. The channels will come alive in fifteen minutes."

You: (softly, to Elias) "Help me with the east route. Theo and the volunteers will take the Basin."

Elias Navin: "Always."

[Scene: Coastline and Evacuation Routes | Night — The Cyclone]
[Visual: A blackout of rain; sheets of water peel across the boardwalk. The partially built barriers, half their weight in the sea, shudder under pounding waves.]
[Sound: A deafening, liquid roar; the crash of water; metal wrenches away from fittings; a child's cry folded into the storm.]
[Music: Full orchestra in dissonant chords; timpani pounding like footsteps]

You fight through the wind and the noise, shouting over it, hands numb and clumsy. The flood doesn't come like a single beast but like an organized betrayal: currents finding paths the engineers did not intend. The half-built groynes, their incomplete faces, act less like shields and more like funnels, channeling the swell into neighborhoods whose streets were not modeled for that energy.

You see it — a wave finding a cul-de-sac, slamming into a row of houses, sweeping doors into the dark. A boat in a small yard flips, the figure inside thrown into a sudden cold pocket of water. People you know — neighbors, a volunteer with Ivy's paint on her overalls — are pulled into the chaos. Sirens are swallowed. Phones show only static.

Elias is at your shoulder, a hand on your elbow, directing people with a voice that is both calm and breaking. His palms are raw. You take a breath that tastes of salt and iron.

Elias Navin: "There — that alley. Move them to higher ground. Use the lane by the Basin."

You: "Theo! Get the harnesses on—now!"

Theo: (shouting, over the wind) "We're pinned! The supply crate's shifted!"

You see a wall of water bearing down on the old Basin; the mangrove stands tremble, some pulled free like teeth. The partially completed breakwater on the far side behaves like an inexperienced referee, redirecting more violence into the neighborhoods you fought to save.

You run. You wade. You lift, carry, haul. You watch one neighbor — an elderly fisherman you remember handing out boiled chestnuts years ago at Strand festivals — get scooped by an undertow and pin a flailing hand toward you. You reach and you don't get him. You hold air instead of a life. The detail of his scarf — the moth-eaten gray scarf — tugs a memory of your father's boat, and the ledger in you closes with a hard, unavoidable calculation.

Everything goes into slow, terrible clarity: moral clarity that demands exposure, and the cost paid in bodies and broken homes.

Hours later, in the fogged light after, you stand at the Old Beacon. The structure that once guided ships is scarred; its lantern room shattered. People cluster in blankets. The ground still smells of brine and something else — char, a trace of diesel, the copper tang of blood.

Rosa moves among the survivors, touching forearms, whispering names. Ivy kneels with hands in mud, sorting through wet papers and toys. Theo counts supplies with mechanical efficiency that breaks into a hiccuping breath now and then. Elias stands beside you, his face wearing the soft erosion of sleepless guilt, his hands stained by rescue.

Jun is there too, coat caked with grit. He does not hold himself like a ruler anymore; he is a man trying to be useful. His eyes are not apologetic exactly — they are smaller, more human.

Jun Park: (to you, voice low) "I opened the audits because I wanted the truth to be known. I didn't— I failed to protect the project from sabotage. I didn't see how politics would stall the work. For that, I'm sorry."

You: (the words come out as gravel) "Sorry doesn't bring them back, Jun. It doesn't fix the houses with one apology."

Jun Park: "I know. I know. I can arrange emergency funding now. I'll take the fall in the council for the missteps that let this happen. I'll work the paperwork to get crews out immediately and I will stand at the docks to watch it done."

You study him. You want to carve anger into the shoreline and keep it there like a name. Instead you feel something raw and strange: that acknowledgment is a kind of currency too, earned only through cost.

Rosa: (nearby, to you) "We can demand more than excuses. We can insist on accountability and immediate repair. Make them open ledger and hands. Refuse the same old bargains that cost the poor the quickest."

You: (the compass warm against your chest) "We will. But some things are already lost."

Elias slides your hand into his. His fingers find yours like a map returning to sea glass. He doesn't speak for a long time. When he does, his voice is small — not the scientist's even tone you know, but something weathered and honest.

Elias Navin: "We knew the risk of exposure. We argued it might be worth it. We didn't think the storm would come before the work was done. I— I'm sorry for what happened. For everything."

You: (the ledger and the salt in you meet) "I asked for truth. I asked for accountability. I didn't ask for this price."

Elias Navin: (squeezing your hand) "We will rebuild. Together. And we'll do it differently. We'll put the people, not the charts, at the center."

You look at him, feeling the frailty of vows made in the shadow of disaster, and the stubborn insistence of them anyway. In the rubble of the Beacon and the scattered mattresses along the Strand, you find a new kind of ledger — names counted, not statistics; hands recorded, not profits.

Rosa stands with you and Elias at the foot of the Beacon. She presses your shoulder, the same way she did all those months ago when she told you to make them count as people.

Rosa: "We made them see. That was necessary. But seeing doesn't absolve the timing. We must make sure the ledger remembers the cost — and that the city pays it back to the people in a way that heals."

You: (pressing your compass) "We will make that ledger. We'll make it public, and we'll tie every cent to houses, to pumps, to community funds. No more hidden reallocations."

Elias Navin: "And we'll rebuild with what we know now. Better foundations, redundancy, and community-led maintenance. We'll design with people in the room, not just the numbers on a screen."

Jun listens. He does not interrupt. He nods, slow, like a man learning to be measured by what he does next.

Jun Park: "I will stand before the council and take responsibility for what my decisions allowed. I will offer the city's resources and my oversight in the work to come. I will not hide behind policy."

His words are not absolution. They are a beginning of something else: a public taking of burden. The cost is still counted in the faces around you, in the empty house on the Strand where a music box used to play.

You close your journal and let the damp spine rest in your palm. The city will rebuild — because it must and because the people will not let it not. The moral victory of exposure is a bitter thing: the truth is out, but truth did not stop the tide. You carry both facts with you now — the clarity and the ache.

You think of Elias's hand, of Rosa's braided hair, of Ivy's paint-streaked knuckles, of the people whose voices you held when councils did not.

There is mourning. There is accountability in motion. There is a plan forming out of grief and stubborn, practical hope. The city will not be the same. Neither will you.
