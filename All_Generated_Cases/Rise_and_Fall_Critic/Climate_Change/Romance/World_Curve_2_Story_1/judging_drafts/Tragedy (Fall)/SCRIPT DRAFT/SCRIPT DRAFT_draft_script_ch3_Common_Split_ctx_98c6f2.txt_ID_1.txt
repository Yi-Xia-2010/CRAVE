[Scene: City Hall Glass Atrium | Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the atrium — sun slanting through salt-streaked glass; banners for the council and tide projections glow on suspended screens]
[Music: Low, uneasy strings; distant, rhythmic percussion like a muted tide]
[Sound: Murmured crowd, the tap of tablet screens, a distant gull cry muffled by glass]

You arrive with the taste of brine still on your tongue and the damp weight of your jacket against your ribs. The projection feed you carried in your pocket earlier is alive in the atrium now: tide forecasts ticking across a column of screens, a scrolling budget line, Jun's measured voice bleeding from a council livestream into the space.

The atrium smells of coffee that has been brewed for arguments and the antiseptic tang of polished concrete. People cluster in loose knots: cooperative members in patched canvas, municipal aides in crisp tech-fabric, a scattering of reporters with lenses that catch the light and make it sharp.

You press your palm to your journal where it rests in your jacket as if you could press the ledger of promises into the walls and slow time. For a second you feel your father's boat beneath your hand, the shudder of it in a storm you've already lived once in memory and are about to live again for everyone. The memory doesn't comfort; it calibrates.

[Visual: Close-up on your face — sea-glass eyes reflecting the scrolling proposal headlines]
[Sound: A single, abrupt chime signals the start of the hearing]

Elias is at your side before you know you've been reached. His sensors flick faint green against the underside of his sleeve, a staccato heartbeat to the room's low hum.

Elias: "You're here early. I— I wanted to make sure you had everything you needed before they call names." (He folds his hands around a water-stained notebook as if it has anchors.)

You: "You look like you're trying to steady yourself with science." (You force a small, dry smile; it feels like lifting a weight incorrectly.)

Elias: "Maybe I am. Or maybe I'm trying to make sure my mistakes are visible so they can't be blamed on chance." (He doesn't look at Jun; he looks at you, and the look is a soft unspooled worry.) "Mara, if this goes sideways—"

You: "If this goes sideways, we make sure people know why we tried. We name the risks and who will answer when things break." (Your voice is steadier than your chest feels.)

Elias: "Naming is not the same as convincing." (He's measuring the distance between conviction and votes, like tide lines.)

You don't get to dwell. Rosa finds you, her braid threaded with salt-bleached reeds that seem to catch the light differently in this room — older, patient, the map of decades written in small lines on her hands.

Rosa: "They'll ask for numbers. They'll ask for precedent. But you know how to tell them about people." (She rests a palm over yours, an old ritual that never felt ceremonial until now.) "Don't let them make the ledger forget the ledger-keepers."

You: "We won't let them. I... I have to speak the truth about risk and care." (The words are heavier than you'd practiced.)

[Sound: A hush as Councilwoman Lian Mor steps to the dais; the carpeting swallows the small scuffle of shoes]
[Visual: Lian Mor framed in the glass light, neutral and unblinking; a screen flashes 'PUBLIC HEARING — ADAPTATION PROGRAMS']

Lian Mor: "Today's docket centers on prioritization of the city's adaptation funds. Each proposal presents trade-offs we must weigh carefully for the sustainability of Corvena Bay." (Her tone is polished to the point of chill.) "We will hear from proponents for living infrastructure, municipal engineering, and hybrid models."

Jun stands just off the dais, the city signage falling behind him like a backdrop he learned to stand in front of. His amber eyes find you — and for a sliver of a moment it's the boy from the Strand, the friend who once climbed the old lighthouse with you to watch a storm pass. Then the moment collapses under the weight of every decision he's already had to make for many.

Jun: "Mara." (He inclines his head.) "I'm glad you're here. The council needs your voice. We need to make a decision that saves the most lives in the shortest amount of time."

You: "And we need to make sure the people whose lives we prioritize are remembered as people, not line-items." (You hear the crowd lean; your sentence lands like a shell on wet concrete.)

Jun: "Practicalities don't stop for sentiment. I believe you know that." (There's no accusation exactly; it's a fact laid between you like a plank.)

Elias: "Practicalities can be designed with community at the center. My team—Theo and I—have prototype data. We can show incremental improvements, reduced wave energy." (He's careful, patient, as if coaxing a chemical reaction.)

Jun: "Prototype data doesn't feed a neighborhood next season when budgets are short." (He turns to the council, the words measured to officials who only count months and votes.)

Rosa: "And engineered walls don't bring back those whose homes we displace. The ledger must count the living." (Her voice is a counterweight.)

Council murmurs thread through the atrium — a weave of fear, calculation, and hopeful pleading. You can feel the momentum like a storm surge — not yet visible, but inevitable.

[Visual: Transition through glass doors into the Council Chambers — quieter, more controlled]
[Sound: The click of a microphone being tested; the low murmur of cameras]

Inside, the room narrows focus: rows of seats for council members, the public observation gallery, a central podium where you will be heard. Screens again show tide maps; another shows a red line labeled 'At-Risk Neighborhoods'. The air is artificially cool, but you can taste salt still. The microphone feels like a small animal in your hand — alive with possibility and danger.

Ivy slips into your line of sight in the gallery, hands smeared with earth from a Basin planting. Her face, sun-freckled and fierce, makes the decision heavier in a new way.

Ivy: "Promise me you won't let them sell someone for us to survive. Promise." (She's seventeen in the way rope still feels in her palms and older in the way she understands trade-offs.)

You: "I promise to fight for them." (You want to add that fighting doesn't always mean winning; you swallow the rest.)

Councilwoman Lian Mor: "We'll hear three minutes for initial statements from each side, then questions." (The structure is clinical, a way to shrink a moral problem into segments.)

A technician hands you a tablet with the clock in the corner counting down. The number looks small and bright and inadequate.

Elias touches your arm — a quick, practical gesture — and his hand trembles just enough that you notice. He looks at you, and for the first time his words break: not anger, not accusation, but fear that data alone won't save people.

Elias: "You have to understand: in the field, we can pilot — small deployments, monitor outcomes. We can be transparent. The living reefs can grow stronger than their critics claim, if we let them. If you back me, I will not hide failures."

You: "And if I back you and the trials fail publicly?" (It's the question both of you are leaning into.)

Elias: "Then I will stand by whatever consequences happen. I am—not in front of the council, but in front of our neighbors. I'll answer." (His jaw tightens. That is more promise than his usual reserve offers.)

Jun intercepts the space between you two with the measured weight of municipal urgency.

Jun: "Mara, the city cannot wait for living systems to mature while developers hold cranes and storms don't wait for experiments. I can open emergency funds now. We save what we can, fast."

You: "Save what we can, but at what cost? Who chooses what to count as 'what we can'?" (You hear the word 'sacrifice' like a pebble in a boot.)

Jun: "I choose based on scale. You have to choose based on people. Both are necessary." (He doesn't say 'and you are wrong', but the implication is a current that pulls at you.)

Rosa, the anchor: "Both is a word that must mean more than compromise on paper. It must mean enforceable promises." (She looks at Jun, then at Lian.)

Lian Mor: "Enforceability depends on monitoring, auditing, and funding sources. The council will need a plan that satisfies both fiscal accountability and social protections."

You look at the clock blinking at the top of the tablet, at Ivy's clenched fists, at Elias's hands folded as if in prayer for an equation, and at Jun's steady, political mask. Each face is a tide-mark. Each choice will rewrite the coastline of lives.

Your chest tightens. You can feel the room contracting into a single hinge: your voice.

You rehearse sentences like lifelines: safety with dignity; evidence with transparency; rapid action with community oversight. None of them land as a full answer. Each phrase that could save on one axis threatens on another.

[Sound: A low, collective breath among the crowd — the prelude to decisions]

You: "This vote isn't for a single method; it's for whose faces line the budget sheets and the timelines." (You hear your own voice in the chamber and it sounds like an instrument you haven't fully tuned.)

Jun leans in, amber eyes softening for a fraction: "I want to preserve the city as a whole. I need your support to do it."

Elias's reply is measured and raw at once: "I want to protect neighborhoods like ours with something that can heal— not just hold back water but rebuild life." (He looks at you with an almost-hidden plea.)

Rosa squeezes your hand under the bench like it's a secret. "Remember: every time you choose to protect some, others will be left to rebuild from ruins. There is grief in the ledger; we cannot let it be anonymous."

You feel the weight of your commitments like a weathered map in your pocket — currents traced in pencil and ink, some lines smudged by rain. The room narrows to two small, pulsing centers: the people who will be safe now, and the people whose safety will depend on trials that may arrive too late.

[Music: Strings swell into a low, descending motif — the 'Fall' signature]

You take a breath. You can hear vague clicks as council members ready their devices; a reporter whispers into a recorder; Ivy breathes in the gallery.

The decision hangs like a weather front on the horizon, enormous and implacable.

You step toward the microphone, fingers brushing its cool edge.

You: (internal) "This is the ledger that will define me to my town. I can stand with Elias and risk being accused of naive experimentation. I can back Jun and secure immediate resources but watch neighborhoods become lines on a map. I can try to negotiate a hybrid and risk delays that storms won't forgive."

You inhale. The microphone waits. Every face in the chamber is a small coast you might either protect or lose.

You close your eyes for a fraction of a second — an almost prayer to wind and wood and those whose roofs you'd climbed as a child. When you open them, the light on Jun's profile, the barely-controlled tension at the edges of Elias's mouth, and the patience threaded through Rosa's shoulders all hit with fresh force.

This is the hinge.

You will have to choose how to cast your public weight at the hearing — allegiance, compliance, or compromise. The words you will speak next will not only be a policy position: they will name what you are prepared to risk and who you are willing to stand beside.
