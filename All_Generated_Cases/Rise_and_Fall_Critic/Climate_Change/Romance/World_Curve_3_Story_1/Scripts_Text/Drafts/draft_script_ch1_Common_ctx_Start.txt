### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Ending


[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Dawn]
[Visual: Polycarbonate panes fogged and streaked with last night's spray; hanging planters glisten with bead-like water. A narrow slit of pale sun pushes through clouds over the harbor, turning wet glass to a soft, muted gold.]
[Music: Low, minor-key piano with distant synthesized wind]
[Sound: Drip of condensed water from guttering; a gull calling far off; the faint creak of a hanging lamp]

You push the door open and steam reaches for you like an old, humid hand—warm and smelling of soil, compost, and the sea. Your jacket drips a little where the collar caught salt on the walk over; the fabric still smells like the promenade after a morning tide. You breathe in: brine, turned earth, and the sharp, sweet sting of thrift-store coffee that someone left by the pot.

The benches are a forest of trays. Seedlings stand in rows, a fragile green on a bed of dark, damp peat. Some lean upright and hopeful; others slump, leaves browned around the edges where water sat too long. A late spring surge did its work—most damage was shallow, but some trays are mottled with mold and the broken stems of plants that won't root again.

You set your tablet on a crate and open the inventory sheet. The screen is battered but obedient, a rectangle of light with your annotations from last week still visible: "salicornia trial — hold" and "rooftop substrate blend — adjust N." You scroll through sea-level projections on a different tab—numbers that trend the way a tide comes in: slow, then steady, then unmistakable.

You touch the braided leather bracelet at your wrist without thinking. It fits the skin like a promise and a chain both. Your thumb finds the bent metal ring you keep threaded there; for a second the pier festival is perfect in your head: lanterns bobbing, children darting between stalls, the band playing a song you can't hum anymore. There was wind then—no storm at first, just a rumor of one—and the pier that held everything splintered like an old bone. You remember the sound of timber tearing and the hush that followed, the way faces folded inward afterward.

You swallow. Steeling yourself is a small, repeated ritual: tighten jaw, inhale, rearrange priorities. Today there is a forum tonight. Tonight you will stand under fluorescent lights and ask neighbors to trust a plan that will demand sacrifice and patience. Charts will be necessary. So will a story.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands—gloved, scraping soil into labeled vials. Tiny tidelines of silt line your nails.]

You work methodically, scooping soil into sample vials, sealing each with a practiced twist. The fan hums above, trying to keep humidity even; condensation runs in rivulets down the polycarbonate like slow rain. Each vial gets a barcode you scan with the tablet, and you annotate pH, salinity, moisture. Salt numbers are higher near the loading door—where the surge pooled—and lower on the benches closest to the heaters.

Etta appears without fanfare, small as a jar of preserved peaches and twice as warm. She moves between rows as if she knows where each plant should be, her hands always finding the soil like a familiar face.

Etta: "Morning, love. You look windblown enough to be proper for the harbor."

You: "I started before the sun. The surge took a few trays hard."

Etta: "Those heirloom lettuces?" (She studies a tray with a frown.) "Oh, my stars. They were thin leather for winter. You bound them up good with seaweed, didn't you?"

You: "I wrapped them, fed them compost tea. The roots are stressed—some will pull through, some won't."

Etta leans close, the scent of rosemary in the hem of her scarf. Her eyes—bright and patient—catch yours.

Etta: "We can't afford to cushion everything, child. Not anymore. But we can choose what to keep. And we keep what people still know the names of."

You: "We keep what matters. That's what the forum's for—asking the town to choose with us."

Etta's smile is small and knowing, then it deepens with something like sorrow.

Etta: "You never were one for small talk. Good. You'll need that spine out there."

There is a softness under her words that steadies you. But Etta is also merciless with practicalities. She steps back, fingers smeared with potting soil.

Etta: "Tom'll be down with his pickup in an hour. He says the shop can spare a few pallets if you need staging for tonight."

A scrape of a heavy boot, then Tom fills the doorway like a doorframe—broad, weathered, and smelling of motor oil and old tobacco.

Tom: "You hauling dirt at dawn now? Least romantic schedule I ever heard."

You: "Seedlings don't care for romance."

Tom: (gruff laugh) "Right. They'll die for indifference, not love."

You: "How's the shop?"

Tom: "Busy as ever. People still need boat patches even when the rest of the town's arguing about promenades and developers. Ravi crawled through here yesterday with that council look—tight-lipped and tighter about the numbers."

You: (quietly) "Ravi's cutting lines?"

Tom: "He didn't say 'cutting' so much as he said 'reallocating.' But he left with a list and the kind of face that says he expects to be blamed either way."

You feel a small, cold stone roll in your stomach. Budgets being "reallocated" is an insurance policy for private favors, sometimes. It is the phrase politicians use when they need room to say yes to contracts that smell of profit.

Tom: "And Marin. Heard they'd been scheduled to talk funding at the Hall. Someone's driving themselves down to argue the town's teeth off, I hear."

You: "Marin's coming?"

Tom: "That's what Etta's boy at the diner said. Stationed at the municipal table in a trench coat, looking like a lawyer and a lighthouse both."

Etta: "If Marin's there, the conversation will go up a notch. They'll try making the numbers do something pretty."

You: "Pretty numbers don't keep seawalls standing."

Tom: "Nor do pretty words stop the storm, but they buy a lot of papers. Keep your guard, kid. People's ears get quiet when their rent's on the line."

You pause while the choice simmers like coffee. Both tasks are small siphons of time that lead to different flows of energy; both are necessary. You pick one and your hands settle into the cadence of it.

Rain begins to whisper outside—first a fine thread against the greenhouse skin. The sky over the harbor has gone from pale gold to the pewter of a bruise.

You record a soil sample, but your mind is drafting the forum in parallel: an image of the promenade as it stands now—benches bolted to rotting boards, the low wall cracked with salt stains—and an image of what a consolidated plan might look like: a satin brochure, private terraces strung along the coast, public access traded for stabilization funds.

Your stomach clenches at a rumor you've heard in the market: a consolidation plan whispered to fold the promenade into a commercial project and hand management to a private developer. The words "public access" and "long-term leases" have been braided together in a way that smells of ledger-books and suitcases.

Etta notices the tightening at your mouth.

Etta: "They told you that at the co-op? Or from rumor?"

You: "Rumor. But it's a rumor with a name attached—talk of consolidating the promenade."

Etta: (face hardens) "If they hand our shore off for a little tidy cash, they'll stitch a scar across what the town rests on."

Tom: "And they'll do it when we're looking at budget numbers and forget to listen to the people who lived here before the ledger."

You: "I have to make tonight count. Data shows what we can do with modular barriers and rooftop gardens—real, do-able stuff. But I also have to talk about what we'll lose if they sign over the promenade. That's not numbers. That's memory."

Tom: "Then tell them the memory. Make 'em see the lanterns and the children. Make 'em hear the band breaking. People remember better than spreadsheets when you hit that note."

Etta reaches out, fingers warm on your shoulder. For a beat she is not the spry elder who tends the cooperative; she is the keeper of an entire town's small, fragile histories.

Etta: "You carry both, Iris. Not one, and not the other."

You close your eyes for a second. The name—your name—lands oddly from her mouth. For a second you are a child on the pier again; then you are the person with a tablet and a timeline of projected sea-levels and a community whose patience has frayed.

The fan above whines; somewhere two blocks over someone slams a dumpster lid, and the rain goes from a whisper to a steady palm against the greenhouse skin. Your jacket darkens along the shoulders where the salt draws out, the fibers taking on a mottled, lived-in pattern.

You taste the coffee again, now gone cold. It sharpens focus, or maybe it's the salt inhaled with every breath. You load the last row of vials into a tray and snap the lid. On your tablet the forum draft sits—half charts, half story. The town will need both kinds of proof.

A thought—small and insistent—slides like a fish in a net through your mind: Marin is at the Hall to negotiate emergency funding. Ravi is tightening lines. If the council approves reallocation in the wrong direction, the promenade could be endangered before your forum even gathers its first occupant.

You stand and run a palm over the fogged glass, watching the harbor blur into watercolor. The pier is a dark spine in the gray light, its broken end disappearing into the same fog that keeps swallowing whatever horizon there is. The sea presses at the town's edges; the town presses back with gardens and concrete and stubborn people.

Your chest tightens again, but this time it's not only the memory of the festival. It's the knowledge that negotiation and compromise will come at a cost—and that some of those costs won't be refundable.

You gather your things: tablet, vials, a sheaf of printed trial graphs, and a small packet of seeds Etta pressed into your palm. You tuck the seeds into your apron like a talisman.

Tom hefts a crate toward the door and pauses.

Tom: "You heading to the Hall after the forum? Or you staying to shepherd the commons?"

You: "Forum first. Then I'll go to the Hall if I need to. Marin's meeting could tip things. I should be there."

Tom: (nods) "Good. Keep your temper—people will try to charm you into silence."

Etta: "And keep your song. If numbers don't make them listen, a remembered sound might."

You close the greenhouse door behind you. The rain has thickened into a steady curtain; each drop writes the town's concerns in a fresh language on your jacket. Outside, the promenade is a washed painting of greasepaint and salt. A couple of early risers—two figures under a shared umbrella—move past, faces drawn as if carrying invisible weights.

[Visual: The camera pulls back to a wide view of the promenade and the shattered pier jutting into the gray sea, a thin line of lanterns somewhere in the memory.]
[Music: Piano shifts lower, a single cello note hanging]

You pause at the railing and allow yourself one steadying inhale. The tablet buzzes in your apron pocket with a message previewed and deliberately unread: "Ravi — budget meeting updates. Marin to appear at Hall today." You do not open it. The choice of when to know is petty; the city's heartbeat is not.

Your thumb finds the braided bracelet again. You press the bent ring until it cools the pad of your finger. Tonight you will stand under lights and try to make a case with both memory and metric, and tomorrow Marin and Ravi will move numbers in rooms you can only watch.

A gull sweeps low, a long, lonely arc. The sea is already whispering.

You turn away from the pier and set your shoulder to the wind. The path to Municipal Hall runs wet and reflective; the rain will beat harder by the time you get there. The moment to step into the council's orbit is arriving like an incoming tide—inevitable and inexorable.

There is no neat solution on your tablet. There is only the work of making people care enough to choose the right thing.

You step off the promenade.

[Page-Turn Moment]
You cross the threshold into rain that feels heavier than it looks—each drop a small accusation. Your notes are a weight in your bag and a promise in your mouth. Ahead, Municipal Hall's glass face waits, and beyond it, a conversation that could reframe the town or let it slip into someone else's ledger. You lift your chin against the weather and finally open the tablet to the message from Ravi, and the screen's glow is the only warm thing between you and the storm.
