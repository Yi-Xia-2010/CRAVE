Mira Kestrel: "Mira Kestrel. I'm a coordinator at Tidelab and I've been running the tidal garden pilot for the last two seasons."

You clear your throat. The data feels like a thing you can hand over and set down — modeled attenuation curves, species survival rates, maintenance schedules — but the room is not just numbers. It's the lives behind the numbers.

Mira Kestrel: "Our pilot shows a consistent reduction in wave energy across the restored fringing marshes — modeled at twenty to thirty percent under the scenarios we simulated — and that translates into decreased erosion for the boardwalk and lower frequency of property-level inundation on the north inlet. More importantly, the work is maintained by community brigades: it creates local labor, preserves in-place livelihoods, and strengthens social networks that respond faster than contractors do in storms."

[Visual: You tap a printed graph; the projector paints pale lines across the Mayor's scarf.]
[Sound: Some applause from the back; a murmur of approval from a few neighbors.]

Jonah Hale: (leaning forward with an even smile) "Those are encouraging numbers, Ms. Kestrel. But models can be optimistic. The consortium's barrier offers a different metric: ninety-plus percent immediate protection for critical infrastructure, funded and guaranteed. Time is also a variable. Can your solution scale fast enough for the projected three-year storm intensification window?"

You feel the old pull in your chest — the pilot you ran the season it failed, when a freak surge undercut a replanted bank and three months of work rotted away. The memory is a cold stone: responsibility, the fear of being the cause of loss. It tightens your throat.

Mira Kestrel: "Scaling is the question we've been living with — not because it's impossible, but because it requires resources and a legal framework that lets community-managed projects expand without being squeezed out. The pilot grew local expertise; with municipal support and a staged funding plan, we can implement living berms and marsh expansions that buy time and create jobs."

Mayor Lin Park: (folding her hands) "Staged funding is a municipal conversation. The city doesn't have unlimited capital, Ms. Kestrel. There are trade-offs. That's why I'm listening."

Cassian's smile is stubborn in the front row — not loud, just a promise that he is present. He catches your eyes and lifts his chin in a small, wry salute. It steadies you.

Ruben rises when his name is called. The microphone picks up the rasp of his voice like weathered rope.

Ruben Ortega: "I've watched these currents for fifty years. The orchard—" (he glances over at Nadia, who nods through watery eyes) "—those trees used to promise winters full of fruit. Now their roots slide on softened banks. I don't want to lose a place where my granddaughter learned to tie her boots. This town remembers when we saved each other."

[Visual: Close-up on Nadia as she steps forward with a folded letter, voice small but clear.]
Nadia: (reading) "Dear Mayor Park, we planted the strawberry bed last spring. If we go, who will take care of the strawberries? — Love, Ms. Keene's class."

The smallness of that letter cracks a smile from someone near the back; it also makes several faces in the councilroom more solemn. Hope is heavy. It carries names.

Jonah Hale: "Mayor, councilors, residents — the consortium's barrier is a known entity. It is engineered to a spec that accounts for median storm escalation. We have contractors, warranties, and financing that doesn't require months of fundraising. Lowry Bay needs certainty."

Mira Kestrel: "Certainty for some—"

Jonah Hale: (interrupting, but not unkindly) "—for those in critical zones, yes. We prioritize lifesaving and infrastructure. I'm not blind to the cultural value of places like the orchard, Ms. Kestrel; the plan includes compensatory public spaces."

The room bristles at 'compensatory.' The word sits like a small, hard stone in mouths that prefer roots and neighborly bargains to calculations.

Cassian leans forward, voice low but carrying.

Cassian Rhys: "Compensation doesn't keep the same streetlights and the old bakery. It doesn't keep the mural on the corner. We can design protections that retain place identity, Jonah. We just need to refuse wholesale displacement."

Jonah Hale: (smile folding briefly) "Then show me the design that does both at municipal scale and municipal budgets. I'm not an enemy, Mr. Rhys. I'm proposing what I think will work quickly."

Mayor Lin Park: "Both sides here are arguing urgency. We need a pathway that doesn't stall recovery or steamroll residents."

Asha's absence is a phantom at your shoulder; you've relied on her calculations before. You imagine the validation packet she promised, tucked into a tablet somewhere. The Schrödinger part of the room — what you may or may not know — settles into a single axis: making the next move.

Mira Kestrel: "Complexity isn't failure. It's resilience. It lets us trade one-size-for-all 'security' for adaptive protection that evolves."
