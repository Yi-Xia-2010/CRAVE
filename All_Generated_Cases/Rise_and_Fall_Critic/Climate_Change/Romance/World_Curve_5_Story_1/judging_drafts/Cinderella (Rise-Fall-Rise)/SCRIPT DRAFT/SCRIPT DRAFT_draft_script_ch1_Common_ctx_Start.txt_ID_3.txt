Narration
The tidal garden is a stitched-together miracle—raised mounds of soil, scattered fiberglass rebar poking like patient ribs, seedlings tied in place with twine. The rebar feels cold and slightly gritty under your gloved palm; the rust is not something to romanticize—it's a structural deadline. Your hands move slowly, deliberately: measure, tap, note. The sensor piles along the berm chirp data into your wristband. The LEDs blink; you match them to entries in your notebook, handwriting tight and functional.

[Sound: Beep of portable sensor; a small metallic ringing]
Ruben: (from the docks, voice low and wind-worn) "You always make schedules like prayers, Mira."
You: "Maybe prayers with clipboards. How are the nets holding up today?"
Ruben: (chuckles) "Still catching more moon than fish. But they're fine. You're checking the south span, right? You'll want to look at the anchor at stake three—I've seen more movement there this week."
You: "Thanks. I prefer hearing that from you than seeing the movement. Your eyes catch what sensors sometimes miss."
Ruben: "Your hands do the same for the plants. After your pilot, I thought you'd hang up the gloves."
You: (your voice softens) "I thought so too. But the plants keep asking to be planted. And the town keeps asking to be saved."
Ruben: (pause; then, quietly) "Save it in a way that keeps people in their stories, Mira. Not all 'saving' needs to be loud."

Narration
Ruben's silhouette rocks against the dock like an old mast—salt-streaked, steady. He smells of tar and linseed; his cane taps a rhythm that matches your pulse. His words land warm and heavy. The failed pilot is an ache in your chest you can't smooth away here; instead you lay a hand on the nearest rebar and steady yourself with the small, mechanical motions you trust: check the weld, mark the corrosion, note the needed batten. The guilt is precise; hope is coaxing.
