[Scene: Mayor's Office | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Rain streaks the glass facade; fluorescent lights inside hum weakly. The mayor's reception desk is a clutter of ringing phones and glossy pamphlets about flood safety.]
[Music: Low, dissonant piano undercut with a pulsing cello — a minor-key, uneasy motif.]
[Sound: A dozen distant voices through glass, a phone abruptly hanging up, a single bassy ambulance siren ebbing and returning.]

You push open the door and the room seems smaller than it should be, packed with people's expectations and their anger. Mayor Lin is on the line again, forehead creased; her hand leaves a damp ring on the desk where the phone rests. The mayor's aide gestures for you to come in — the paper's headline is on everyone's lips before anyone speaks.

Journalist: "CONSORTIUM DOCUMENTS SUGGEST 'ACCEPTABLE DISPLACEMENT' — PILOT DELAYS NECESSARY HOUSING RELOCATION."

You taste the metallic edge of worry as the headline lands in your chest. A truncated model — a version of Jonah's team's projection — has been circulated and the tone in the room changes like weather shifting under a low cloud. People expect quick answers, simple outrages, someone to blame.

Cassian is there, paint still dried in the creases of his hands, a smear on his cuff like a badge. When Jonah enters, his coat is impeccable even in the drizzle; he carries a slim slate, eyes the room the way a surveyor eyes a shoreline.

Cassian (slamming his satchel onto the council table): "Do you realize what you gave them to chew on, Jonah? You send a snippet out and—" (his voice tightens) "—and then you wonder why folks think you're counting houses like tabs."

Jonah (cool, controlled): "That file was an internal draft, Cassian. A working model, not a public release. There are context notes —"

Cassian: "Context notes don't print headlines. Context notes don't show up on the boardwalk this morning with spray-paint and threats."

You inhale. The room smells of wet coats and coffee gone lukewarm. Your tidewatcher pendant presses against your sternum, steady as a pulse.

Mayor Lin (pinching the bridge of her nose): "We need to calm things before they spiral. Call the paper. Release a statement. Get Asha to confirm what actually changed in the model."

Jonah (defensive): "I've already sent clarifications to my team. The parameters were truncated; misleading by themselves. Someone —" (his gaze flicks around, heavy and searching) "—someone mishandled the file."

Cassian (sarcastic, low): "Mishandled. Convenient word."

You step forward because doing nothing feels like accepting the weather. There is a weight to every step you take now: a thousand small consequences waiting for the next push.

You: "Jonah, did your team mark that draft clearly? Were there flags? Because the way this reads to people is—" (you find the words) "—that displacement was modeled as an acceptable outcome, not an edge-case."

Jonah (rubs his jaw): "There were annotations. 'Acceptable displacement' was shorthand in an internal sensitivity test — an economist's language. It was not code. It was not policy. It never left our internal memos with an endorsement attached."

Cassian (bitter): "Semantics. When a teenager gets a flyer that says 'you will be moved,' words don't get footnotes."

Jonah: "I understand the outrage. I do. But the released file is incomplete. If you want to stop this, we need to control the narrative by releasing the full model — let Asha and Mira speak to what the parameters actually mean."

Cassian meets your eyes across the table. Something unreadable moves under his smile; indignation, fear, the ragged shape of trust eroding.

Mayor Lin (urgent): "You two — stay. Mira, Asha — get in here. We need your numbers, your explanation, and a public-facing clarification now."

The private meeting is a claustrophobic pressure chamber. Phones are tamped face-down. Asha's fingers are threaded through a strap; her knuckles go white as she opens her tablet. You reach for her hand without thinking — a small, automatic human thing. She takes it, and it's trembling.

Asha (voice small, tight): "The truncated model removed the sensitivity matrices. The headline picked up a line from a cost-benefit column that was part of a scenario set. It wasn't meant to be read alone."

You: "How easy would it have been, technically, for that snippet to look like a recommendation?"

Asha (exhales, eyes wet at the corners): "Very. An editor who doesn't know the algorithms will see 'acceptable thresholds' and assume human decisions. Our language is technical — it doesn't translate without careful framing. And the memo — one of our engineers used cold language in a private note. It was a lapse. A stupid one. But on paper it reads like a policy."

Jonah (quiet, not his usual poised self): "We should have vetted every memo attached to that package before the transfer. That was on me."

Cassian (hissing): "On you? On all of you? Where was that vetting when poor families were waiting for any sign of certainty?"

Asha (looking straight at you): "Mira — the models indicate that misinterpretation will push people to extremes. If the perception becomes 'the pilot delays safety,' then calls for a major barrier will spike. People want immediate security. The narrative will harden toward hard infrastructure."

You press your thumb into the tidewatcher pendant, focusing on its small weight. Data and feeling are braided now; one misread number can snap into a headline and pull people's hearts like tides.

[Scene: Tidelab | Dusk]
[Visual: Horticultural lamps glow through mist; volunteers drift in and out with muddy boots. The greenhouse windows fog with breath.]
[Music: Sparse piano with a slow, unresolved string line; the motif is anxious and melancholy.]
[Sound: The distant thump of a megaphone; a hammer striking wood somewhere outside; the wet slap of footsteps on planks.]

You come back to the greenhouse thinking work will be a refuge. Instead, you're met with two groups: one building living berms at the south marsh, hands steady with familiarity; the other convening outside with signs, chanting about betrayal and displacement. The volunteers' faces are divided; someone you trained in sensor calibration is now airing grievances into a megaphone.

Nadia (from the doorway, voice small): "Mira, some of the afterschool parents are asking if we're coming to the protest."

You feel the fissure in people: the same hands that plant cordgrass now also lift placards. It smells of wet paint and damp earth, the greenhouse's humid breath mixing with the sharp citrus of spray paint from nearby banners.

Asha finds you among the hydroponic racks. Her lab coat is streaked not with paint but with coffee and raw data printouts. She puts papers into your hands — charts with jagged lines, annotated in a handwriting that looks exhausted.

Asha: "They took the partial runoff of a sensitivity test and presented it as policy. The press didn't know these runs were hypothetical. The memo where my colleague said 'acceptable displacement'— it was about modeling edge cases to stress-test evacuation logistics. It wasn't advocacy. But it's out. And people don't parse nuance under fear."

You (softly): "Could we publish a forensic review? Show the chain? Expose the leak's path? The public needs to see the context."

Asha (shakes her head, desperate): "If we publish everything, the consortium funding could pull overnight. The pilot collapses. We lose the political cover that let Mayor Lin keep the city at the table. Without funding the community programs stop. But if we don't, the town moves toward the idea that only a hard barrier can save them."

You think of Ruben, of his hands coiled like rope, the orchards he taught you to plant. The thought of contractors flattening streets to pour concrete makes your stomach clench.

Cassian (entering, quieter now): "People are scared, Mira. The paper lit a match. Some folks want to block the construction equipment before they even break ground; others think we should be pushing—" (he flinches) "—organizing safe exits, relocation help. There's no single voice anymore."

[Scene: Lowry Bay Boardwalk / Night]
[Visual: The boardwalk is a string of lamps and shadows; banners line the railings. A cluster of people faces a line of municipal trucks, and a few younger protesters spray paint stencils on plywood that will soon be used as barricades.]
[Music: Tension rises — a repetitive, anxious percussion, a distant brass warning.]
[Sound: Sirens nearer now; raised voices; the metallic clank of tools; the rustle of paper leaflets.]

You walk the line where volunteers and activists meet, passing faces you know. Someone has scrawled a slogan in hurried white paint across a folding sign: "OUR HOMES ARE NOT STATS." The scent of wet paint is everywhere, sharp and new.

A man yells from the crowd: "They planned this — they knew the trade-offs. We trusted them!"

From the other side, someone shouts back: "If we don't accept large-scale protection, the sea will take us all! You want our children to drown for idealism?!"

The argument is less about the sea now and more about trust. The leak has turned technical friction into moral fracture. Each accusation hits like a stone, and the tide of indignation threatens to swamp calmer speech.

Cassian catches your sleeve; his breath fogs in the cold air. "They're listening to headlines, Mira. We can march, we can block, we can bring cameras. Or we can step into the mayor's office and shut this down through process. None of it feels enough."

Jonah stands on the municipal steps across from you, the slate in his hand like a shield. He looks tired in a way that has nothing to do with weather. He turns to the assembled cameras, every move measured.

Jonah (into a small recorder): "We will convene a full review of the leaked material. We'll call for transparency. But I also warn against sabotage — damage will only be used to justify expediency."

Cassian: "Warnings feel like threats when people's homes are on the line."

You feel the pull between immediate safety and long-term justice. Your consciousness catalogues outcomes: funding stability vs. political pressure; quick protection vs. consent; exposure vs. collapse. The weight of it presses at your chest until you can taste salt and paint in the same breath.

Asha squeezes your hand so hard her knuckles whiten. Her voice is a whisper, but it splits the night: "We can ask for three paths, Mira. We can try to move people with protections. We can let the market push a barrier. Or we can blow the lid off and risk everything to stop the barrier."

You stand at the edge of a turn in the town's story. Sirens thread past. Paint fumes hang like low cloud. In the air there is the faint sound of someone hammering a plank into place, a work that could be for protection or for blockade.

Everything narrows to one unavoidable question: how do you lead when the town's consensus has cracked and every answer spills consequences in other people's lives?
