# Route: Cross-Route Ending — Sad


[Scene: Saltgarden Research Lab | Morning]
[Visual: Sunlight slanting through glass panes thick with condensation; rows of shallow trays gleam with green growth]
[Sound: Soft drip of water, a rack creaking as kelp shifts; a distant gull’s wail like an unanswered question]
[Music: Low, melancholy strings — a slow descending motif]

You come into the Saltgarden the way you come into other guilty rooms — with your shoulders already braced for what must be decided here. The lab smells like wet slate and fresh kelp, that green-sour tang that tastes of possible things: food, medicine, shelter. Your notebook is folded against your ribs; the coral pendant rests warm at your throat as if it understands the weight of the morning.

Ilias is at the northern bench, sleeves rolled, trench of sunlight cutting across his forearms. His hair is pulled into a loose knot; the tips are lighter where the sun has been drying samples. He looks up when you enter and the faint smile that comes is a small, steadying thing — and then it's shadowed because he knows, as you do, that steadying alone won't hold the seawall of choices.

Ilias Navarro: "You came early," he says. (He sets a clear sleeve of eelgrass on the bench between you, the little cutting trembling with damp.) "Thought you might like to see how the cuttings take in controlled flow."

You let your fingers hover over the sleeve without touching. The green is small and foolishly brave. You remember the town hall's wooden doors, the calibrated smiles, the mayor's 'interim protection' — a phrase that sits sour in your mouth. Here, among wet glass and grow lights, those words feel like a foreign currency.

You (Maya Kestrel): "They look... alive." (You force a small laugh that doesn't reach your eyes.) "Ilias, you said there was something else. You sounded... off at the meeting."

Ilias's mouth presses together for a beat; he rubs a thumb along the edge of a tray as if to steady a thought.

Ilias Navarro: "Seren's team reached out. They offered me access to the regional lab — more equipment, more controlled trials, better data resolution. On paper, it accelerates restoration timelines. They want validation from local fieldwork to claim the engineered wetlands are effective."

You taste bitterness — not just at Seren's name but at the idea that your town's experiments might be folded into someone else's press release. The lab hums around you; a fan stirs the damp air. You think of kelp beds holding the sea, of hands in mud, of the slow work of learning when to bend and when to hold.

You (Maya Kestrel): "Validation — or co-option? Their wetlands are engineered, right? Concrete cells, staged flows. The kind of thing that gets finished fast and looks like safety until you look at what it replaces."

Ilias leans back on his heels, fingers finding the braid of his tide-watch bracelet.

Ilias Navarro: "They're engineered, yes. But the models show boosted recruitment for eelgrass if combined with living beds. They offered me a position to run trials at scale, to see if our methods can be integrated. I didn't— I mean, I haven't said yes. Part of me thinks, if we can prove restoration at scale, we get time. If we get time, we save more than we lose."

You feel the old bruise in your chest shift — a memory of a flashing storm and the brother you couldn't save. 'Time' is a ledger you can barely afford to open. Ilias's optimism sits next to your exhaustion like two fragile lamps under a gale.

You (Maya Kestrel): "And the cost?"

Ilias Navarro: "Cultural concessions. Access rules. They want branding and a fast rollout. They also have political clout. Seren can get permits signed before the next high-tide vote."

He watches you measure the sentence by its impact. The fan clicks; the kelp rustles like low applause.

Ilias Navarro: "I'm torn. I want the science to work. I want people to be safe. I want your plan to be the way forward. But I'm starting to feel the town's needs with a stopwatch."

Silence rises between you and takes the lab with it. You find the coral pendant and rub its carved grooves until they blur.

Ilias responds to each impulse with patient, human answers — technical graphs, a memory of a late-night field transplant, a promise he isn't sure he can keep. The conversation keeps folding, each layer revealing more: shared values, slightly different methods, the same fear humming like machinery underneath.

Ilias Navarro: "Maya, I don't want to be the reason we lose the town's voice. But I also don't want to be the reason we lose the town. What if both are true and we just make whichever loss is smaller?"

You want to snap at him — 'That's not enough' — but instead you press your knuckles to your mouth and let the salt on your skin taste like old maps. There's no easy calculus.

[Visual: The lab's glass doors fog as a rain squall begins outside; a row of condensation beads slide in parallel]
[Sound: Rain thudding on the roof, a far-off horn from a cargo skiff]

You walk the perimeter of the lab, fingers skimming trays. Each tray is an argument: some with seedlings thick and promising, some patchy, stubborn as gum. Methods stack like tomes — your planner's mind cataloguing them into possibilities and threats. Ilias watches the way you catalogue, the way your shoulder tenses with every hypothetical loss.

Ilias Navarro: "Whatever you choose, I want you to know — I don't think the options are pure. I want to help you and the town. I can be in that regional lab without selling out, or I can refuse and we try our thing here. I need you to tell me which 'we' matters."

You want to say 'both,' because that's what your heart wants: science that keeps the town's shape. But the world gives blunt instruments and doesn't listen to hedging. The fan hums; a drip falls with the same slow certainty of tide.

[Scene Transition: You leave the lab with damp hair, salt still clinging to your jacket. The rain has turned the harbor into a moving silver, boats rocking like restless thoughts.]

[Scene: Mangrove Patch / Abandoned Shipyard | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Tangled roots and half-sunken hulks; shafts of slanting sun through moss-heavy air]
[Sound: Mud sucking at boots, the distant clang of a reclaimed hull; birds flitting nervously]
[Music: A single, low cello line that descends gradually, underlining regret]

The mangroves keep their secrets well. Etta's voice carries through the tangles like a weathered bell. She is already there, standing where the old slip used to be, hands full of woven cord and an old net. Her shawl smells of smoke and brine; when she sees you her face softens and then hardens in a way you've learned to read as a directive.

Etta Muir: "You been running between rooms like a stitched-up gull, child. Come here." (She folds you into a hug that smells like cured fish and the sea. When she releases you her hand finds yours and grips.) "Look at this place. Roots plain as bone. This is where we learn to hold."

Her fingers are thin and callused; she presses them into yours with surprising force. The pressure isn't merely physical — it is a summons.

You (Maya Kestrel): "Etta, Ilias told me... Seren's firm is offering labs and permits if we… if he validates their wetlands. Rafi messaged about relocation pilots in Kinmar—"

Etta cuts you off with a small, sour sound, like someone pinching unleavened bread.

Etta Muir: "Kinmar's not us. Aye, they let other folks go when the water took their roofs. But Hearthbend is our song. We don't hand the chorus to someone who doesn't know the verses."

Her eyes pin you, and for a beat you are a child again learning to mend a net by lamplight. All the elder's stories come with a cost: hold the lines, and someone will always ask who you are holding them for.

You (Maya Kestrel): "What if Seren's way keeps more people from losing homes? What if it's quicker?"

Etta's knuckles whiten.

Etta Muir: "Quicker for who? Quicker for the ledger that counts dollars and buildings, maybe. Faster don't mean kinder. Faster can mean forgetting names. I lost a husband in a move. Not to the sea — to a new town where their faces didn't fit on the pier and the nets never smelled right. You want me to name the thing you risk? I'll tell you: a life that's unmoored from itself."

Her voice is low, stoked from years of weather. You feel the truth like a bruise blooming. The mangrove roots around you seem to echo the idea — tangled, holding ground the way people hold memory.

A chirp from your pocket: Rafi's texting. You glance and read: "Leak: Seren's firm sponsoring relocation pilots in Kinmar. Mayor met behind closed doors. Thought you'd want to know. —Raf"

The word 'relocation' blooms like a warning in your mind. It is not new; Seren's model always had relocation as part of the toolbox. But the leak sharpens the picture: steps already being taken elsewhere, momentum that could wedge itself into Hearthbend's choices.

You (Maya Kestrel): "Rafi says they're already running pilots. If they can show results, Ansel might bend. Or worse—everyone might see 'success' and forget that success leaves holes."

Etta's jaw works; she lets out a sound you can't name.

Etta Muir: "You were born here, Maya. You know the taste of our drifts. You know what we asked of the sea — we learned to read her temper. If you let those who don't know our names decide quick, they'll write over us."

You look down at your palms and see the ink stains of maps and the salt grooves of lines you've traced a hundred times. The decision is no longer academic. This is about who gets to keep the ritual of their life.

Etta's hand tightening is a benediction and an indictment.

Etta Muir: "Choose the town's heart over efficiency. If you don't who will? If not you, who will mouth our names when there's a grant letter with a logo to fill the page?"

You hear the weariness in her question like the pull of tide back off a cliff. The mangrove shadows lengthen and your throat closes.

You feel the coral pendant at your throat like a metronome. Each beat is a small tribunal: the faces of fishers, the market stalls, the smell of sea-steamed bread, the names that could be written out of the town ledger if Seren's branding overwhelms local claim. You taste salt and the metallic tang of fear; the past — the brother, the storm, the promise you made in the wake of loss — presses against any thought of compromise.

Ilias's words echo in the air: 'Make whichever loss is smaller.' Etta's demand reverberates like a vow. Rafi's leak is a hammer against the thin glass of political will. The choices fold in on themselves until all you can attend to is the immediate physics of your hands — your fingers drum on your closed notebook; you feel the carved coral warm under your thumb.

You (Maya Kestrel): "This isn't only engineering. It's naming whose losses we accept."

Air tastes iron. The mangroves sway, companions and witnesses. You can feel the town in the press of earth against your boots and the slickness of the hulls: whatever you say will tug at that fabric and either knit it tighter or cut it loose.

Everything narrows down to a single fork in the path, and for the first time today the ledger feels like something you could open and sign, rather than something that signs you.

You take a breath that is not entirely your own and let the decision settle into the shape of your ribs.

[Music: The cello line resolves into a quiet, unresolved chord — the kind that stays with you]
