[Scene: Saltgarden Research Lab | Afternoon]

You set your palm against the damp rim of a tray and feel the pulse of the place — a small, steady heartbeat beneath the glass and algae. The Saltgarden hums with potential: low mechanical breath of pumps, the whisper of volunteers comparing notes, the faintly medicinal sweet of kelp warmed by sun lamps. Your notebook sits in your jacket, but your hands are already wanting to be messy.

Ilias is beside you, leaning over a tray with the same careful intensity you recognize from mornings at the harbor. His ocean-blue eyes crinkle when he looks up; the expression is easy and earnest and unmans you in a way that is both ridiculous and grateful.

Ilias: "Look — if we stagger harvest windows and map micro-currents, the transplants have a much better chance. We can surface the data in a simple map people can read while they're standing on a pier."

You: "Simple. Accessible. Community-operable maps, not something behind a paywall." (You feel the words land like a promise.)

Ilias: (spreading his hands, mud smudged under his fingernails) "Exactly. We train folks in two-hour blocks, set up rain-catch barrels like the ones on the rooftop, and make the protocol a printable pamphlet. If someone can carry it to a fishing shed and understand it, we've done something real."

You follow him through the motions — measuring spacing between plugs, folding new shoots into netting, marking coordinates on a grease-pencil sheet as if you were sketching a small, local future. The tactile rhythm steadies you: the slick slide of eelgrass, the coarse grit of reclaimed timber you press into a low berm trial, the way your gloves smell faintly of brine and soil.

You: (quietly, to yourself) "This could be taught. This could spread."

Ilias watches you with a softness you don't let yourself keep for long. You trade notes on currents and shading and how to anchor frames against bigger surges. His hands move like someone who remembers the sea by touch; you remember how you promised to protect people like him from a tide that would otherwise sweep them away.

Ilias: "If Dr. Hana sees the preliminary survival curve tomorrow, she'll vouch for the method. Her endorsement could make this visible to the mayor without the whole thing being filtered through Skyhub's PR."

You: "Her cautious endorsement means credibility, not acceptance. We still need the town to do this, not just nod and hand over budget lines."

Ilias: (a small smile) "Then we do both. We make it rigorous enough for Hana to sign and accessible enough for Etta to teach."

[Scene: Saltgarden Research Lab — Entrance | Late Afternoon]

Dr. Hana Sato: "I've been watching the survival numbers you sent last week. The spacing variations and the microframe technique — it's promising. You're not just planting hope; you have replicable results."

You feel a small, private lift in your chest. Validation from Hana tastes like clean rain after a long dry spell.

You: "We want to make sure it's not just a lab trick. The plans have to be feasible for someone with two hours and a bucket."

Dr. Hana Sato: (studying your grease-pencil map) "Then the protocol's strength is its pedagogy. Keep the language plain, include troubleshooting notes, and track failure modes. If we document failures as much as successes, Mayor Ansel can see both the limits and the realistic benefits."

You: (nod) "We'll do that. We want Mayor Ansel to act with eyes open, not because someone's polished a pitch."

Dr. Hana Sato: "I'll give a cautious endorsement at the next council briefing. I won't overpromise, but I will vouch for feasibility and the metrics you can use to measure impact."

Ilias: (relieved) "That could cut through some of the noise."

Dr. Hana Sato: (a flicker of wryness) "Noise is part of coastal governance. You make the model loud in the right places."

Her soft confidence sends a ripple through the room. Volunteers trade looks of renewed purpose. Someone begins to sketch training schedules on a whiteboard; another starts labeling seed collections for tomorrow's workshops. Momentum is contagious.

[Scene: Harborfront | Early Evening]
[Visual: The boardwalk is a hive: volunteers weaving low berms from eelgrass and reclaimed timbers, lanterns strung along ropes; puddles mirror the sunset]
[Sound: Laughter, the creak of woven timbers, the distant thud of a bell buoy]
[Music: The hopeful motif swells; rustic percussion keeps a steady, working tempo]

Etta Muir stands at the edge of the work area, hands methodical as she ties bundles. Her face is lined like maps of old tides; when she speaks, people listen because she holds memory in a way official reports never do.

Etta Muir: "We were stewards long before anyone in a suit came with a 'solution.' Keep the roots in the water; don't chisel every shore into a straight line."

Her voice is an anchor and a caution both. Around her, a few elders nod; others — worried and practical — look toward the boardwalk where the High Tide promenade cuts a clean, engineered line across the bay.

You: (feeling the town in the press of footsteps) "We're not erasing tradition, Etta. We're adding tools so those traditions can breathe longer."

Etta Muir: (a slow look) "Tools with hands on them. Not tools that will take the docks from us."

Then the peaceful hum stutters. A sleek figure moves through the crowd — an emissary from Seren's office — glossy jacket, polite smile. Two aides trail with a slim tablet and a folded pamphlet stamped with a corporate seal. They approach Mayor Ansel, who is talking quietly by the lamp post, his tweed collar turned bravely into the wind.

Seren's Emissary: (bright, smooth) "Mayor, we appreciate Hearthbend's initiative. Our funders are willing to match community investments — a 1:1 fund to expand scale quickly. We would, however, ask for designated 'priority zones' so engineered channels and navigation corridors are preserved during scaling."

The word "priority" lands like ice in the air. Some volunteers glance toward the water, where boats bob and children skip stones. The crowd's harmony fractures: safe engineering vs. community stewardship.

Etta Muir: (hardening) "Priority for whom? Priority can become permission to redraw the map without us."

Seren's Emissary: (leaning in) "We don't want to redraw anything in secret. We want to secure commerce and transport so boats don't lose livelihoods in storms. It's an efficiency measure."

Mayor Ansel: (rubbing his temple) "We need funds to act before the next season. If this can accelerate protections, we can't ignore it."

You feel the old bruise of guilt flare: you promised to protect everyone, and the word "everyone" is suddenly full of conflicting angles. The crowd looks to you — not to make the call, necessarily, but to hold the protocol's moral center.

[Ilias places a steady hand on your shoulder; mud under his fingernails, warmth through glove fabric]
Ilias: (quiet) "We can make this open-source. If the model is public, the data can't be locked away behind conditions. People can replicate, audit, and adapt. It scales without strings."

You: (the idea unfurls) "A community-mapping protocol — public, editable, with governance templates — so other towns can adopt it on their terms. We make the method our common property."

Seren's Emissary: (a polite half-smile) "Open-source is admirable, but scaling requires coordination. Our matching funds come with assurances; 'priority zones' simply manage where heavy infrastructure goes so it doesn't interfere with ecological restoration."

Etta Muir: (bitter) "Manage where? You mean tell us which docks to let go."

Mayor Ansel: (looking between you and the emissary) "We need both speed and legitimacy. If Hana backs this and Maya can show the pilot outcomes—"

You: (internal) You can hear both currents pulling: the urgent need for money and the stubborn insistence on local sovereignty. Your chest aches with that old responsibility. You were elected — not by votes — by trust; you carry a ledger of promises in ink and salt.

Ilias squeezes your shoulder, and his suggestion of open-source pivots the mood toward possibility. The idea is elegant: legible protocols, shared governance templates, training modules, printable pamphlets, and a simple web repository that any harbor town could use. It satisfies your planner's mind and your protector's heart in one tidy, bright sketch.

You: (imagining) "A network of small towns, swapping lessons, adapting the model to their coves. Hearthbend could be the first node, not the last. We teach, they teach; we don't outsource our decisions."

Etta Muir: (softening, but still watchful) "If it's truly ours and we can see how it grows, then maybe—"

Mayor Ansel: (weary, hopeful) "That kind of regional credibility could attract non-conditional philanthropic grants. It might leverage more than a single corporate purse."

The tide is turning. Volunteers hum like a choir that learns a new harmony. Your guilt — the old bruise — eases into fuel: this plan is both a shield for the town and a promise you can keep in public. For a moment you imagine Hearthbend leading a regional movement, an open-source wave of living shorelines that spreads without strings.

You breathe in salt and wood smoke and the sharp sweetness of newly cut eelgrass. Hope rises in your chest not as a fragile thing but as a constructive energy. The work ahead will be long, political, and uneven. But right now — the protocol sketches on the table, Hana's cautious nod, Ilias's rough hand on your shoulder — make it feel possible.

You know a crucial decision is coming. The community flanks you, some trusting your judgment, others waiting to be asked. Seren's emissaries hover with their matched funds and their imitation of efficiency. The next step will determine whether the protocol stays communal and open or whether it accepts conditions that might compromise the very things you pledged to protect.

You look down at your notebook, at the map of trial plots and the scribbled heading: "Community Mapping Protocol — public, auditable, teachable." Your pen hovers.
