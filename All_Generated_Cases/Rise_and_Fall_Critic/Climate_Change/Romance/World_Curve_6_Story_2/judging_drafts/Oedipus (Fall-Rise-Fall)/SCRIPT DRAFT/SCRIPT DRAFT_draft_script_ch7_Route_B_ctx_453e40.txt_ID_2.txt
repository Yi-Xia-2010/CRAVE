You step into the Skyhub Pavilion wearing your patched field jacket under a borrowed raincoat. The salt in the air feels thinner here, traded for a faint tang of ozone and polished surfaces. Your notebook is tucked against your ribs like a talisman; the carved coral against your sternum is warm from your grip.

You remember the choice that led you here — the decision to try negotiation instead of refusing outright — and it sits in your throat like both a promise and a warning. Dare you let something this large bend toward the town you love?

[Visual: Close on your hands as you rub the rain from your sleeves; the coral pendant peeks out.]
[Narration]
You breathe in the Pavilion's efficient air and let your feet find their place on the floor. The space is designed to make small things feel inevitable: the long table that funnels attention, the screens showing tidal projections, the model of a raised boardwalk that gleams under a focused lamp. It is all built to translate weather into numbers, loss into ledger lines. It is also, impossibly, correctable.

Seren turns as you approach. Her voice is quiet but sure, the kind of voice that has rehearsed the difficult phrases into something that lands flat and clean.

Seren: "Maya. Thank you for coming. We don't have forever — you know that. I'll be direct: we can expedite the wall and bring forward engineered wetlands if Hearthbend agrees to a phased relocation for the lowest streets. It's the fastest way to reduce immediate risk."

You feel the words like cold water against your ribs. They are efficient; they are decisive. They are also the sort of thing that could erase more than floodplain lines.

Maya Kestrel: "Phased relocation," you repeat. (quietly) "That language moves families from place to place. It names who gets to stay and who has to go. Which streets are you talking about? Who decides precedence?"

Seren: (tilting her head) "We use elevation bands and economic vulnerability as criteria. We can protect critical infrastructure and guarantee compensation. It's messy, but it is fast. You asked for certainty, Maya."

Mayor Ansel: (intervening, fingers tight on his overcoat) "We need something tangible for the people. Funding tied to quick protections would stabilize businesses that can't afford prolonged uncertainty."

Etta Muir sits near the margin of the conference space, a shawl around her shoulders despite the inside warmth. Her face is a map of old tides, and she hasn't smiled much since you stepped in. When she speaks, it is gravel-and-wind honest.

Etta Muir: "If there's a way to keep the mangroves and the old wharf, then maybe—maybe we can bear some moving. But I won't see the harbor paved over in my lifetime."

Seren studies the map when you lay it flat. For a beat, her expression is unreadable — steel for someone who has had to make hard calls, perhaps softened by the ink of your cartography.

Seren: (slow) "Those are precise nodes. If you can bring community buy-in, we can carve out 'cultural sanctuaries' — legal designations that protect certain parcels from relocation or heavy engineering. We can codify community stewardship over those zones."

You would have expected a corporate negotiator to avoid phrases like 'community stewardship' — and yet here she is, offering them. Hope feels like glass: fragile but luminous.

Maya Kestrel: "And co-governance? Not just token councils but actual vetoes on the footprint and the timeline?"

Seren: "Yes. Joint oversight boards with community representation, technical liaisons, and binding clauses for maintenance responsibilities. We want to avoid repeating mistakes. We want buy-in, not resistance."

Ilias stands slightly behind you, sleeves still beaded with mud from the mangrove trial he couldn't let go of earlier. He looks half out of place in this sleek room and half like the bridge between both worlds.

Ilias Navarro: (careful) "Engineered wetlands could accelerate eelgrass recruitment if we seed with nursery-grown stock and shape the substrate. If we pair those efforts with community-managed sanctuaries, we gain both ecological and social resilience."

Maya Kestrel: (to Ilias) "Do the numbers back that up? Or is that hope dressed as math?"

Ilias: (a rueful smile) "It's math with the patience of tides. But yes — models show faster establishment under managed conditions, and if the community manages the sanctuaries, maintenance costs drop. We keep local knowledge central to the process."

Seren: (nods) "We can allocate a technical support stream for those nurseries and seed banks. It'll be in the grant language."

The conversation unfurls in back-and-forths and careful compromises. Seren outlines timelines and milestones; you count them in your head like breaths. Mayor Ansel asks about job protections; Etta presses for the wharf and the mangrove; Ilias interjects with biome-level specifics and the smell of mud in his descriptions. Each time one concern rises, Seren offers a measure to address it. It is pragmatic. It is exhaustive. It is moving, like the slow setting of a tide that reveals a possible shoreline you could walk.

You tuck a curl behind your ear and feel something in your sternum unclench. The plan is a scaffold: wall plus wetlands, with sanctuaries and co-governance written into clauses. It doesn't resolve what will be lost. It narrows the list of unknowns. For the first time in weeks, you can point to pieces of infrastructure and say, "This could hold."

Seren: (softening) "We can fast-track an initial phase to protect critical infrastructure near the harbor, and simultaneously fund community-led nursery programs. I want your name on the oversight board, Maya. We need someone who knows Hearthbend's stories."

You hear the plea beneath her efficiency — not an apology, but a recognition. It makes your chest ache in a way that feels like relief.

Maya Kestrel: (quiet) "Show them living water, then. Let's make the proof as public as possible."

Mayor Ansel: (relief edging his features) "Public proofs, jobs to run them, money for the wall — this might actually hold. If we can show immediate wins, the relocation becomes less of a leap and more of a managed step."
