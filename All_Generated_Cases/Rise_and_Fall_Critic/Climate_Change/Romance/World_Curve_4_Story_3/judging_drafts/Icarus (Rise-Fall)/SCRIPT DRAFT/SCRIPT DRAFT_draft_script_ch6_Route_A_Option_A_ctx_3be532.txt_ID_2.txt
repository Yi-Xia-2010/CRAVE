Raff's voice cuts into your feed: "Watch. Now." He uploads footage—handheld, the camera teeth grinding against a metronome of panic. You watch your terrace, from a distance, as a pulse of water finds a weak throat in the inlet you both postponed studying. The water surges, then pulses again—like a beast tasting something new. A culvert, older than your conditional clauses and installed under a different permit, belches open under pressure. An undercut opens at the terrace's far side.

Raff (on the phone): "It’s tearing at the new plants—look at those roots—Marin, do you see this?"
You: "I'm seeing it. How bad—?"

Jonah: "We shore it—the stakes, the coir. We hold what we can."

[Scene: Floodwall — Night; storm fully arrived]
[Visual: Rain slants hard enough to blur features; a collapsed section of terrace shows muddy, ragged roots. The inlet gapes like a wound. A screen on your satchel shows Raff's live stream—comments cascading like flotsam.]

Ben finds you by the broken edge, umbrella useless against the sideways rain. His tie is damp and dark; his practiced mediating smile is stripped thin.

Ben Rhee: "Investors are freezing staged payments pending audit. They want assurances—hard assurances. Lydia's counsel says centralized fixes are the only way to restore confidence quickly."
You: "If they pull funding now, our maintenance and co-management clauses have no teeth. The terraces won't recover without continuous work."
Ben Rhee: (hesitating) "I know. I'm… I'm between a rock and the council's seat. They need a narrative of certainty."

You turn to the footage again. Raff's upload shows the terrace pulsing in the dark—soil washing like ink. Comments call the pilots 'dangerous experiments.' Someone posts a frame showing a child's toy, washed free, bobbing like a dark white eye.

Lydia appears at the head of the boardwalk as if summoned by the camera; the storm lights catch her pavilion in a harsh, clinical halo. She stands wrapped in a waterproof coat that does not show the rain. Her press team moves with efficient choreography; her expression is composed but not unreadable—complex, steel-lipped. She speaks when Ben brings her close.

Lydia Voss: "The region needs a single plan the public can understand. Contingent measures create confusion. We can deploy a full-scale seawall and guarantee investor timelines."
You: "A wall that keeps people off the water's edge—what does that do to livelihoods? To culture?"
Lydia Voss: (cool) "It buys uninterrupted access to capital and immediate protection from documented surge scenarios. This isn't emotional. It's logistics."

Your mouth tastes like iron. The salt on your boots seems small, like coins that won't buy anything. Jonah stands with you, drenched and furious and quietly determined.

Jonah: "So we throw out everything that was grown from neighborhood hands because somebody's spreadsheet needs neatness?"
Lydia Voss: "You misunderstand. I am offering safety."

The conversation arcs into pressure and counter-pressure, three-way lines of force: Lydia's institutional cadence, Ben's pleading for compromise, your stubborn insistence on co-management. Words tumble, interrupted by feeds and urgent pings.

Elder Mae is there too, leaning on a cane, her shawl wet and heavy. She looks at the torn marsh with a slow, steady gaze. When she speaks, it is not to the cameras; it is to you, to Jonah, to the people huddled close.

Elder Mae: "You did something that kept us out of the worst of storms before. That matters. It is not nothing. But the world can be cruel and sudden. We mark what we lose so we can name what we save."

Her words land with the force of tide. You think of the child's hands in the soil, of Asha's midnight sketches, of months of meetings and slow graft. The sense of ascent—of community singing together—pulls away until only memory is left like salt on the tongue.

You stand at the edge, boots pressed into saturated earth, and the town's conversation folds into a chorus you can no longer steer alone. Jonah's hand finds yours—not to steady you against falling, but to share the cold.
