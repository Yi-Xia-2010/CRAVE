Jonah stands at the edge of the quay with a coil of rope in his hands. When you arrive, he looks up—salt at the temple, grin quick and honest.

Jonah: "You look like you just carried the sea in your satchel."

Marin Solace: "Maybe I did." (You hand him a clip of a graph. He squints, brow furrowing in that way that means he's trying to read an engineer's story in a sketchbook of data.)

There is an easyness about Jonah that is almost reckless—he believes firmly that things will hold if you anchor them right and do the work. You like that about him, and you rely on it.

Marin Solace: "Show me how you tie the anchor. I want to learn the knot you always use."

Jonah: (laughs) "Finally. Took you long enough."

He winds the rope through his hands and explains—half tutorial, half storytelling—how a fisher's knot is a promise. He works with the float; you reach out to steady the frame. Your fingers brush wet rope and the temperature of his wrist; the contact is small but electric in the mundane way of shared labor.

Jonah: "You know, you made a lot of people show up for a thing that used to be boring and cold."

Marin Solace: "People are tired of being asked to accept a decision after the fact. They wanted to help, not be afterthoughts."

Jonah: "Then let them help. Let them be the ones to anchor it."

Marin Solace: (you tie off the knot, your hands learning) "We'll make the work visible. We'll pay them. And we'll take care of it, not just build it and walk away."

Jonah: (eyes warm) "Good. I trust you on that."

[Scene: Greenridge — Late Afternoon]

Marin Solace: "Thank you, Mae. It means—"

Ben Rhee: "I'll draft the council liaison notes. Lydia—" (he gestures subtly toward the bluff) "—has been quieter. She sent a message of cautious congratulations. She called it 'a promising complement' in public."

You hold your face calm; beneath the surface, something like vindication warms you. Not triumph—there's no room for that yet—but the ache of having worked in the dark being eased into daylight.

You and Jonah keep repairing small things together on the quay—anchors, nets, little solutions stitched into the town's fabric. Sometimes you argue about priorities; sometimes you don't speak, and the silence is comfortable and full of work.
