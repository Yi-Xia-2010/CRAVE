[Scene: Experimental Terrace | Morning — Months Later]
[Visual: Sunlight slants across tiered marsh hummocks; small seedlings brace in muddy ribs. People gather beneath a banner that reads "Havenpoint: Growing with the Tide."]
[Music: Warm strings, a bright acoustic guitar picking a simple motif]
[Sound: Murmur of a crowd, distant gull calls, the soft clack of camera shutters]

You stand at the lip of the terrace with the satchel lighter than it was a year ago. The soil smells of tannin and salt; the membrane of the terrace still holds the faint lacquer of the initial contractor's seal. Fingers of sunlight sketch maps on your jacket. Around you, hands lift shears and ribbon, laughter knitting the crowd together in a rhythm you didn't know you'd been missing.

Jonah's laugh reaches you before his face does — full, immediate, the laugh you've learned to read for safety and for mischief. He ducks under the banner with wet sleeves and salt at his cuffs, the rope bracelet dark against his skin. When he sees you, the laugh folds into a smile like a rope coiling into a cleat: familiar, secure.

Jonah: "You did this," he says, breath visible in the cool morning. (He props a hand on the rail, studs of dried mud on his knuckles.) "Not some suit. Us."

You: "We did it," you answer, because the cooperative has names and fingerprints on every bench screw and every sapling. You can feel each of those nights in your arms, in the way your satchel rests against your ribs.

Jonah: "Raff's already got the live—" (He jerks his chin toward a phone, where the frame bobbles with Raff's grin.) "—and Asha's out of control with the confetti. Ben's smiling, which means he thinks he made a good call. Mae is crying, which means it's real."

You laugh, soft and brief. "Those are the metrics I wanted."

Jonah studies you a moment longer, his smile becoming a question. "You look like you could order the tide if you wanted to. Are you... okay?"

You search for the half-answer you usually give: a list of tasks that makes the world right again. But in front of the terrace, with seedlings catching the light like tiny flags, words fall into the places where work and meaning meet.

You: "I'm tired. And proud. Mostly proud."

Jonah takes your hand in a quick, rough squeeze. The contact grounds you; you taste dried kelp and the metallic tang of the morning on his knuckles. For a breath you're carried back to the Saltworks, to late nights with chalk dust on your palms and Asha muttering about root densities. The town hums around you as if smoothing out a bruise.

[Scene: Floodwall Community Garden | Golden Hour]
[Visual: The garden beds behind the concrete curve—mornings of tender green among patch-painted posts. Kids harvest small heads of lettuce while a radio on an open crate plays a folksong that now names the cooperative. The floodwall's graffiti is weather-smoothed, the murals still bright where hands have repainted them.]
[Music: Low, contented cello under the market tune]
[Sound: Soft clatter of tools, someone humming, the distant thrum of an inverter from a row of solar panels]

Ben Rhee approaches with the quiet gait of someone who learned to soothe meetings into motion. He stands beside you, hands in pockets, watching the beds and the people like a man watching a project he finally understands.

Ben Rhee: "Milestones are holding," he says. (There's relief in his voice that doesn't try to be flamboyant.) "Accretion rates are within projection, biodiversity indices surprised even the biologists. You're getting recognition."

You: "It's not just recognition," you say. (You look at the terrace, at the way the roots have hooked into the marsh mud like small anchors.) "It's evidence that doing it with the town keeps more than soil intact."

Ben: "Exactly." He glances down at the satchel at your feet. "Conditional funding buys you time to make those conditions matter."

Your chest eases, the balloon of worry deflating a fraction. In the market the folksong is hummed by a child who doesn't know clauses or contracts but knows who's fed her family. For months the town has leaned into the work and the rewards come in small, honest ways—microgrants for Jonah's dock retrofits, a line item for maintenance wages, a new bench at Elder Mae's garden.

From somewhere near the boardwalk, Raff's voice calls out, publicity bright and breathless. He waves a phone like a talisman. Small screens blink with proof: soil anchors, smiling faces, Jonah's repaired mooring backed up with footage of fish returning to a shallower channel. The hue of optimism is almost warm enough to burn.

[Sound: Distant, a low wind shift. Clouds scrape the sky like hands over a piano.]

The day tilts. The air darkens at the same pace the inverter hum changes, a fraction of a hertz difference your body feels before your instruments do. You taste salt on your lip; the forecast on your phone catches static and the line reads "watch."

You: (Your hands find the rail as the wind increases.) "We postponed the inlet study," you say, more to the curve of the floodwall than to Ben. The sentence is a stone you might have thrown and can't retract.

Ben's face goes taut, the politician's ease cracking to show the bones under. "We scheduled it for Phase Two," he replies. "We had to prioritize—"

From the edge of the terrace a shout cuts through. Raff's video feed, live-streamed, shows a narrow inlet you've been watching with half a plan: a contractor's culvert, old and corroded, buried under a tangle of approvals from last season. Water hurries through it like a throat pulsing. The stream on people's phones lurches, and you're watching the inlet in real time.

You: (Your stomach drops.) "That's the culvert. We flagged maintenance, but the window for contractor access was after the audit."

Before Ben can respond, a sound like a stone dropped down a well: the culvert collapses. Water, black and angry under light, punches through the bank with the force of a fist. The terrace's lee takes the first blow—mud slides from under a planted node, a small hummock shears like bread crust. Seedlings are yanked free, uprooted green hair spinning in a white-water tongue.

[Sound: Loud rush, a collective gasp from the crowd, someone crying out. The live feed shudders as Raff's camera tilts.]

Raff: (On the stream, voice raw over the wind.) "This isn't a test anymore—look—look at the water!"

You run. Your boots find slick wood and mud, a note of panic under the sound of the surf. For a heartbeat everything is the thin, precise sound of your own breathing and the violent ocean.

Jonah slams into your side, both of you lunging toward the lee. He grabs at a bench with one hand, tears at a root with the other, trying to pin it in place. His face is mud-streaked, mouth a hard line.

Jonah: "Hold—hold—keep the net—" (He shouts commands like a captain as the surge tests the line between order and chaos.)

You: "Move that arm—anchor the stake—" (Words become procedures. Your training takes over: where to drive the rebar, how to lace coir logs against the current.)

For a wild minute, hands are everywhere, work over hope, but the surge is smarter than the two of you can be. It finds the soft seam you postponed. The terrace dips where the culvert washed out, and a panel of planted marsh slides, the little engineered tides unstitched.

[Visual: Slow-motion close-up of roots ripping, mud ribboning into churning water; a seedling floats like a green coin away.]
[Music: Violins screeching high, then a low, sinking synth as everything tilts]
[Sound: Phones pinging with alerts; a single, sustained alarm in the distance]

You taste iron, real and shocking, a clean metallic edge on your tongue. Not metaphorical—iron and salt and the sharp copper of fear. For a second you can't remember if you are inhaling or if the world has inhaled you.

[Scene: Floodwall — After the Surge | Night]
[Visual: Floodlights throw harsh angles across torn marsh and slick concrete. The terrace is a mess of plastic mesh, scattered plants, and a gouged seam where the water found purchase. People move like ghosts under lamps—tired, wet, too awake.]
[Music: Distant low piano, minor key; a wind that sounds like a chorus of disappointed things]
[Sound: Generators throb, a distant siren, the crinkle of tarps]

The footage is searing in its clarity. Raff's stream shows the exact moment the culvert collapsed; it shows your hands in the mud, Jonah's jaw clenched, Mae's shawl dragging at the edge of a muddy puddle as she keeps someone from falling. Within hours the clip goes viral beyond the town's circuits: abroad, a feed of green engineering being unmade by a single negligent collapse.

Investors flinch. Emails land in your inbox like pebbles: scheduled payments suspended pending audit; due diligence requests that read like interrogations. Lydia Voss issues a statement on the pavilion's slate and it slides across everyone's feeds in a clinical blue:

Lydia Voss (broadcast): "While our hearts are with Havenpoint's people, the recent events underscore the risks of unproven pilots. Immediate regional coordination and centralized structural measures are required to secure the bay."

Her words are not untrue, but they're not the whole truth. They arrive in a tone that has no room for small places; it is a tone that demands a lever to be pulled, a contract to be signed, a wall to be poured.

You reply—first into the void of your inbox, then into a livestream interview that feels like standing on a precipice. Your voice is steadier than you feel.

You: "We wove this plan with community trust as its backbone. Yes—we have gaps. We asked for time to study those gaps. We asked to be allowed to fix them with the same careful, measured work that got us here."

Lydia (via the pavilion feed): "Measured work has its place. But in the face of escalating surges, there's a responsibility to act at scale."

Ben tries to negotiate the space between you. He is tired, his suit damp at the collar.

Ben Rhee: "Can we—" (He looks at you, then at Lydia's feed.) "—agree to an independent audit? Set immediate emergency maintenance funds while we conduct a rapid assessment? Keep co-management oversight in place?"

Lydia: (Her face on a screen, eyes cool.) "We can proceed, but with a centralized command to manage immediate threats. Local participation can be advisory."

The crowd around the floodwall listens. Some nod in relief at the promise of speed; some mouth curses at the implication that "advisory" will be the limit of their voice. The town's folksong dies out like a candle suffocated—no one knows what to sing.

You feel the weight of the satchel again, but it's metaphorical now: signatures you once held like ballast, now thin pages in a sea that smells of iron.

[Scene: Market — Next Morning]
[Visual: The market stalls are patched with tarps; a hand-lettered sign reads "REPAIR FUND" next to a battered jar spilling coins. The banner from yesterday hangs askew, its letters dirt-smudged but legible.]
[Music: Sparse piano, single notes echoing]
[Sound: Conversation, low and urgent; the clink of a calculator, a child's distant sob]

Elder Mae finds you by the bench, shawl wrapped tight. Her braid hangs like an anchor rope across her shoulder. She puts a papery hand on your arm, the skin like crusted leather, warm and steady.

Elder Mae: "The tide takes and the tide gives," she says, as if reciting a proverb and offering neither comfort nor complaint. (She turns her face to you, unafraid.) "You built with the town, child. That is a rare thing. You carry the memory of the doing."

You: "They say we're experimental." Your voice cracks and you taste iron again.

Elder Mae: "They will call much of what matters 'experimental' until something big enough falls into the ocean."

Her bluntness is a kind of mercy. You want to weep for the terrace, for your satchel, for the seedlings—small, stubborn optimism that now floats in brown water. You also want to press your forehead to the Saltworks' cool glass and never speak to another meeting agenda again.

Jonah appears in the doorway of a stall, rubber soles caked with last night's brown. He looks at you with the same mixture of worry and steadiness that kept him on the boats through storm seasons. He sits beside you with no fanfare.

Jonah: "They froze payments," he says. "One of the retrofits I started—there's no microgrant to finish the pilings. I can patch it for a bit, but it's not... it's not safe for the long haul."

You look at him. In his eyes there is a tectonic shift: anger at the world and at how quickly support can turn to spectacle. He swallows, a small, ocean-borne movement.

Jonah: "I'm not saying leave. I just—" (He traps the thought.) "This is one of those moments where the town will change whether we like it or not."

You: "If we let them bulldoze the governance out of co-management, then we lose more than moorings." (The words are an echo of all the nights you drafted clauses and argued for oversight.)

Jonah: "I know. But we also have people who need work, and walls make jobs fast."

You: (You imagine the concrete pouring: a perfect, brutish curve that keeps water out but locks people out of their heritage.) Your throat tightens. "The worst thing would be to let fear make us give up everything we fought to protect."

Jonah's jaw moves. "Then we don't give up. We adapt again. But you—" He reaches for your hand, thumb rubbing along the back. "You promised the town you wouldn't carry it alone."

You look at the satchel at your feet: lighter in weight, heavier in consequence. The town's chorus has tapered to a few voices now—some supportive, some hysterical, some hungry for quick fixes. Lydia's narrative is climbing the regional platforms; her proposals come with contractors and schedules and the blunt promise of speed. Investors want measurable returns and clearly defined risks; the culvert collapse recalibrates both.

You: (You think of the seedlings.) "We have to be honest about what we couldn't do fast enough. We have to show our data. We have to press for a hybrid: immediate emergency fixes with community oversight, plus a committed timeline to finish the inlet studies and repairs."

Jonah: "And if they take the timeline and never return the oversight?"

You meet his eyes. "Then we keep building elsewhere. We keep teaching other towns how to anchor with roots instead of walls. We don't let one failure rewrite our method."

Jonah looks at you like a man taking stock of a storm-damaged pier. "Then we'll be damned if we let them take the whole harbor."

Elder Mae squeezes your shoulder. "Hold to what you can. There's honor in steady work even when the world seems to prefer the loud solution."

[Music: A single cello note, mournful, then a tentative rising phrase]
[Visual: Close-up on your hands — worn, a smudge of mud under the fingernails, the chipped lighthouse pin catching a strip of light]

You stand at the middle of a town that has both just celebrated and just been blamed. People whisper in the market about liability, about who signed what and when. The applause at the ribbon-cutting has not vanished entirely—it has been tempered by the sound of water and the needs of paperwork. You feel your peak, the months of cohesion and small, shining victories, crack under the pressure of an event no model fully captured.

Your resolve is not gone, but it is altered. The image you carried of scaling carefully now bears a stain. You taste iron and salt and the bitter tang of public trust eroding like the bank under the culvert. You feel anger, grief, exhaustion, and an odd, small ember of stubborn clarity: the thing you made is not wholly destroyed.

Raff's footage circulates in a hundred forms—some edited to shame, some to plead, some to rally. Lydia's press machine gains momentum. Investors call for audits and hold back payments. Ben tries to thread the needle; Elder Mae says prayers into the wind; Jonah prepares to shore up what he can by hand.

You: (You think of the children who learned the folksong this week, of the benches someone painted with a crooked heart, of Mae's braid and Asha's mud-streaked overalls.) "We didn't lose everything," you say aloud, and you are not sure whose ears you intend to comfort.

Jonah: (He pulls you close, the tide of his breath steady.) "We didn't."

The town is bruised but breathing. There will be audits and hearings and possibly a hybrid plan negotiated under pressure. There may be homes that need managed retreat and docks that will need to be rethought. There will be stories that get written about failure and bravery in the same paragraph.

You close your eyes and imagine the Saltworks window, the chalkboard crowded with hydrographs. You remember Elder Mae threading the final knot into a marker post, the children clapping, the sound of that single sustaining violin note that once promised an ascent.

The peak has broken beneath you, and you taste smoke at the edge of what was bright. The flame hasn't gone out, but its light is smaller, more intimate. It will take a different kind of tending now—less spectacle, more care; less triumph, more repair.

You breathe in, the salt and iron of the town and its seaworn hope, and breathe out a decision that feels like both an ending and a beginning: you'll stay. You'll keep the cooperative's bones intact, even if it means fighting in courtrooms and council chambers instead of ribbon-cuttings. You'll fight for oversight, for wages, for the gardens, for Jonah's mooring—and for the quiet, necessary work that doesn't look like a victory when cameras are nearby.

Elder Mae squeezes your hand and says, simply, "We will remember how you did it."

Your satchel is lighter, your feet raw, but your body still knows how to plant a root where the current wants to take one. The market buzz is softer. The folksong has a new verse: not a proclamation of triumph but a line about standing in wind and beginning again.

[Music: A low, resigned chord that resolves into a quiet, persistent plucked motif]
[Sound: The sea, patient and inevitable]

You do not know how many will stay, or how many will leave when contracts come with hard edges. You do not know whether Lydia will be able to marshal a centralized solution that keeps the ecology intact. You know this: you will keep showing up, and you will keep building where you can.
