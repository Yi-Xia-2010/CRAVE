[Scene: Old Shipyard Ruins | Dusk]
[Visual: Rusting cranes silhouette against a pewter sky; puddled oil reflects streaks of cold light]
[Sound: Wind through skeletal rigging, distant thump of a pile-driver, the low murmur of gathered townspeople]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings — a hopeful swell under the industrial noise]

You stand on the edge of what used to be a working dock, the bones of shipbuilding around you softened by salt and time. The air tastes of iron and old rope; a gull keels against the wind and cries like someone trying to remember a name. Light from portable lamps pools in oily puddles, catching the edges of concrete freshly poured into forms — a line of new defense carving the shoreline.

Jonah is three yards away, his braid-rope bracelet coiled in his fist like a small talisman. He speaks to a foreman whose high-visibility vest is the same color as the emergency lights; both their breaths make pale ghosts in the cold. You can hear the cadence of Jonah’s voice — equal parts impatience and entreaty — and you feel the familiar tug: the town and the sea pulling at different ends of a rope you live on.

Jonah: "We need a gap for the skiff. You can't cut off access and call it protection. Folks depend on those slips."

Foreman: (gesturing toward the plans) "Design spec says controlled access points. Too many openings compromise integrity. Safety first—"

Jonah: (cuts in) "Safety's how you live out there, not how you lock it away. You tell me which boat owner we tell when they can't feed their kids."

You tighten your fingers around the rolled map in your satchel. It creaks; the damp paper smells faintly of seaweed and printer ink. Practical instincts — the ones you trained in labs and late-night co-ops — prick at the edges of your thoughts. Top-down fixes read beautifully in slides: crisp cross-sections, calibrated tolerances, timelines with guaranteed milestones. But slides don't show the way the rope burns a palm or the small, stubborn rituals of a town that still measures time by tides.

[Scene: Floodwall (Construction Stretch) | Early Evening]
[Visual: The curving wall runs like a grey spine; community murals stop mid-stroke where paint cans sit]
[Sound: Concrete being poured, soft laughter from workers clustered near heaters, the rhythmic clunk of scaffolding]
[Music: A lilting piano motif layered over the strings, gentle and determined]

Down the line, people clutching hot drinks gather and watch. There's a heat to their faces that isn't just from the lamps. You see relief written in small gestures — hands rubbing at wet sleeves, a woman leaning her forehead against the cold and letting out a long breath. Fear still hangs under their eyes, but for the first time in many weeks it loosens its grip enough for sleep.

Ben Rhee stands nearby, phone tucked to his ear, his suit collar bright with sea spray. When the vote came through — faster, sharper than you expected — the town tilted toward the thing that promises immediate safety. You're not naïve; you know how persuasion looks in council chambers. But you also know the sound of people stopping to sleep when alarms have dwindled to distant, manageable hums.

Ben Rhee: (off the phone, to you) "Folks needed a visible line of defense. It calms people. It buys us time — even if it's not the perfect time-bought solution."

You nod, feeling the weight of his pragmatism against your own ideals. It's not a betrayal; it's triage. You can feel the cooperative's prototypes sitting like a small litany of quiet conviction in your satchel, and you translate the moment into pragmatic language: lives and livelihoods have been spared from the night's worst imaginings. That counts.

[Scene: Lydia's Command Center (Temporary Pavilion) | Night]
[Visual: A sleek pavilion glowing like a clinical beacon on the bluff; inside, cyan and coral displays map surge scenarios]
[Sound: The soft click of Lydia's tablet, hushed conversations, distant waves steadying into rhythm]
[Music: Subtle electronic pulses underscored by strings — authoritative, brimming with possibility]

Inside, the air is warm and dry, a contrast to the salt-slick outside. The pavilion's interior smells faintly of ozone and coffee. Lydia stands near a bay of screens; her posture is all precise lines and calibrated calm. She watches you with the expression of someone who has already calculated the variables that led to this meeting.

Lydia Voss: (measured) "Marin — thank you for coming. There's an opportunity to ensure this wall isn't just concrete. Integrating living features on the lee side can reduce ecological impact and preserve some access. We need someone who understands both engineering and the lived needs of fishermen."

You feel Ben at your shoulder, an anchor. Jonah lingers at the doorway, sleeves rolled, his hands smelling of oil. The offer hangs between spoken promise and the thin metallic scent of the pavilion.

You inhale. Your mind lists the contingencies: influence from inside can shape design, but being inside also narrows your field of movement. The cooperative's independence — the thing you built with Asha and the neighborhood — feels like a compass. Lydia's offer is a hand on that compass.

You: (careful) "What scope are you imagining for that liaison role? How much decision-making authority, and who signs off on the living features?"

Lydia Voss: (tilting her head, approachable) "A technical advisory seat on the implementation team. You'd review plans, suggest locations for marsh inserts and scour along the lee, and have veto power on ecological concerns tied to permits. We're moving fast; we want guidance that keeps community trust."

You let that settle. It is precise. It carries the hum of bureaucracy wrapped in the soft sugar of inclusion.

Jonah: (low, vulnerable) "And if 'guidance' gets trimmed for speed? If the contract says 'we did consult,' but the features are tokenized? Are you sure the board won't box you in?"

Lydia Voss: (not backing) "That's why we want you on record. Influence from within and public documentation makes tokenization visible. People will see you there. Your voice matters."

Ben Rhee: (soft smile) "We need legitimacy and speed. Marin, this could be the lever to pull the design toward a hybrid outcome later."

Your chest tightens with the sense of a lever in your hands. You imagine drafting letters, arguing with contractors, walking the lee line at dawn with watches and boots. You also imagine the cooperative back at the Saltworks, empty chairs at your table if you step too far into the pavilion's light.

Lydia reaches into a leather sleeve and produces a slim card with the project's crest and the words: 'Technical Liaison — Implementation Phase'. The card is small enough to be a talisman. You turn it over in your hand, feeling the texture of possibility: a seat at the table, access to plans, a route to soften the wall's bluntness.

You think of Asha's late-night models of marsh berms, of Elder Mae's hands pressing seeds into soil at the floodwall's lee, of Raff's viral videos that made people care about a single reed. You imagine those elements, small and stubborn, threaded into the concrete's shadow. The image is quietly beautiful.

Jonah watches you, and in his amber eyes there is trust folded with fear. He steps closer and lays his hand briefly on your shoulder — a small, grounding contact. It says more than any argument: remember who you are when you choose.

You: (to yourself, low) "Pragmatic. Safety first."

The words are half reassurance, half bargaining with the part of you that prefers not to trade trust for expedience.

[Music: The strings rise into a hopeful, sustained note]

You stand at the edge of decision without having to decide yet: an invitation held in the glowing palm of the pavilion, a role that could anchor living features to a plan that might otherwise sweep them aside. Outside, the floodwall's curve catches the lamps like a promise; inside, the pavilion hums with the clean certainty of engineers and funders motivated by results.

For a moment, the town breathes as if released from a held lung. It is an uncomplicated, honest hush — not the end of struggle, but the blessed easing of immediate pressure. You allow yourself that small exhale. Hope is not naïve here; it is tactical. It is repair, stitch by stitch.

You imagine what it would be like to step inside Lydia's light and keep your hands dirty at the same time — to be both advocate and interlocutor, to make small, durable things happen. The thought thrills and terrifies in equal measure.

You look out the pavilion's glass at the sea and the wall and the town that leans into both. You tell yourself once more: this is pragmatic. Safety first, then restoration. For now, the town sleeps easier.

A thought presses at the edge of your mind — a practical to-do, a moral ledger, an image of the Saltworks table with empty chairs. You tuck it away like a note to return to. The next step waits, and it will require that you choose a posture: to enter the command center's logic hub as an insider or to stand just outside and keep the cooperative's flame burning on the shore.

You take another breath and set the card on your palm as if weighing it.
