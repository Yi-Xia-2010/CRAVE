Jonah: "Don't make this all on yourself. Some of this—you can't carry alone."

The garden is a place where the town's contradictions gather. People come to tend and to talk; they come to lay offerings and to read notices stapled to the bollards. Here gratitude and grief braid together like vines on a trellis.

You step over to her. The town has given her small winnings; she has delivered quick visual certainty. But the speed of it—and the kind of certainty—has a cost you now feel in the thin, gathered lines on your neighbors' faces.

Marin Solace (You): "Lydia. We need to talk about the narrowness of your 'efficiency' metrics."

Lydia: (cool) "I know you've made some tactical moves, Marin. This winter's work helped stabilize certain livelihoods. That's an outcome we can all appreciate."

You: "Appreciation isn't the same as accountability. The materials we used weren't designed for the hydrology we deferred monitoring on. Now the tidal exchange near the older channels isn't what it used to be. That affects eelgrass beds and nursery habitat."

[Scene: Raised Beds / Your Flat | Night]

You sit at the small kitchen table with the mail spread like a map of conflicting truths: thank-you notes penned in looping script; a council letter promising 'regulatory review'; and cold, stamped eviction notices with dates that feel like final tides. Jonah enters quietly, cleaning salt from his boots, carrying the simple weakness of a man who has spent his life making and fixing. Jonah: "You checked the market this morning, right? Stalls were full. They sent a photo to Raff's channel—people bought fresh mackerel and kelp preserves. They asked about you."

You let a tired smile come. The immediate proof of survival warms like soup.

You: "They did. That was...good to see."

Jonah hesitates, a weathered expression softening into something like confession.

Jonah: "I worry about the channels by Old Shipyard. The water is different at low tide. The nets don't hang the same. My cousin said the juveniles hang out in a new patch now—different flow. Maybe it's temporary. Maybe it'll sort. Maybe not."

You think of the hydrology models Asha has sketched, of the deferred monitoring you authorized, and of the months of silence that will be required to know if those 'maybes' will hurt the bay.

You: "We fast-patched; we didn't get the long counts. We bought time at the cost of certainty."

Jonah reaches across and covers your hand. His fingers are warm, an island of steadiness. In bed, that warmth is the same, the simplest human remedy for a complicated wound. Tonight, though, the two of you are awake to the unsettled edges.

Jonah: "Do you regret how it turned out?"

You: (honest) "Sometimes. I regret what I had to let go of to keep other things. But I don't regret that we kept people fed and the market alive. We kept hands in work."

Jonah: "Then we keep doing the harder part of that—watching, being loud when we need to be, making sure the next thing is careful."

You rest your forehead against his for a long moment. Outside, a slow wind moves through the raised beds, shaking loose a few leaves like soft applause. There is a quiet pact between you: to love and to bear the burden of imperfect choices together.

You remember Elder Mae's line from months ago—"Do the thing that makes people remember kindly"—and you feel the weight of it settle into your bones. You will teach, record, and raise new beds. You will codify oral archives and stitch rituals into the patchworked landscape. It is not a victory in any grand sense. It is not whole. But it is a way forward.

Jonah: "Whatever comes, we'll keep tending."

You: "Yes. We'll keep tending."

He squeezes your hand, and for a breath the tide eases.
