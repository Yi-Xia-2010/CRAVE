# Route: Route B → Option B / Route C → Option C


[Scene: Old Shipyard Ruins | Dusk]
[Visual: Rusting cranes silhouette against a pewter sky; puddles reflect strip-lights of worklamps]
[Sound: Distant mechanical groan of winches, gulls arguing, the low chatter of crews]
[Music: Quiet, ascending strings with a warm undertone — hopeful, breathing]

You came here because the vote closed like a hinge and the town swung with it. Lydia's plan moved faster than the tide—contract ink drying as the emergency lights blinked on—and tonight the ruins are a construction site instead of a cemetery. Cranes stand like dark sentinels. Concrete trucks empty their guts in slow, satisfactory rhythms. For the first time in weeks, the edges of sleep feel possible again for people who used to wake to storm sirens.

Jonah stands near a half-submerged slip, the braided rope bracelet from his wrist wrapped around his fingers. He looks smaller against the hulks of old hulls and bigger than he ever has to you—shaped by the work he does, by the people he keeps. He is arguing with a foreman; their voices bite and rise, then soften into technical bargaining. You can tell it's not just about access. It's about whether the sea will still be theirs to read.

Jonah: "You cut the ramp at that angle and half the skiffs won't fit. We lose morning runs and the school program—do you want that?"
Foreman (rough, practical): "Design tolerances don't account for sentimental schedules. If we guarantee every slip, we push timelines and cost. Lydia's mandate is a fast line—priority is flood exclusion."

You watch Jonah move between measured fire and a humor that's trying to defuse the weight.

Jonah: (lowering his voice, but not conceding) "People eat off those boats. They teach the kids. If you block us out for efficiency, you break more than docks."

Foreman: (rubbing his temple) "I get that. But if the water keeps coming, everyone loses everything. You know that."

Their exchange hangs there—friction and an unspoken mutual fear. You could step in; you could let them settle it. Both feel like work you meant to do, both feel like part of the price of the vote.

You choose—somewhere in that small choice you feel your old habit: you lean toward tending the place where systems meet people. But even standing still you are part of the negotiation; your presence nods into the room.

Your coat smells like damp canvas and the bittersweet tang of seaweed left drying on racks. The wind presses salt into your hair; the sky is a wide pewter lid under which small lights—headlamps, string-bulbs, the glow of cellphone screens—begin their patient pulse. There is work, and the work is filling the silence that fear carved.

[Scene Transition: Walk along the shoreline, past skeletal docks; footsteps on broken planks echo into the next scene]

[Scene: The Floodwall & Community Garden | Early Night]
[Visual: Newly poured concrete curves; community murals pause half-painted; raised beds crowd the lee]
[Sound: Water lapping a measured beat against cofferdams; distant hammering; low voices of neighbors]
[Music: Warm piano notes under a steady string line — calm forward motion]

You step onto the floodwall path where people have gathered with thermoses and patched jackets. The wall itself is a strange hybrid of brutalist slab and conciliatory timber; someone has already stenciled a slogan in bright paint: SOMETHING TO COME BACK TO. Gardeners kneel among chard and carrots, hands up to their elbows in soil—defiant life behind cold concrete.

Ben Rhee appears with the practiced smile of someone who has told hard news many times. He has a salt-dark smudge at his cuff and the tired kindness of an official who knows the arithmetic of fear.

Ben Rhee: "I know this felt sudden. Council moved because momentum matters in a crisis. But people needed a visible promise. They got that tonight."
You: "A visible promise is different from a durable one. The wall buys time—I'll give you that. It doesn't buy the community's trust overnight."
Ben Rhee: (sighing) "Trust is rebuilt in parts. You've mobilized people, Marin. Your cooperative filled a vacuum. If you accept a seat at the table Lydia's setting, you can shape how that wall breathes—where living plugs could go at the lee, where access is preserved."
You: (quietly) "And if I don't? If I try to keep distance, we risk being ignored as an inconvenient alternative."
Ben Rhee: (nods) "You do not need to decide tonight. I asked Lydia to offer a technical liaison role to you—she insisted. I thought... you'd at least want to hear it here, among the beds."

His words land like a catalyst. You picture marsh grasses rooted against a concrete lip, salt-tolerant reeds cushioning surge energy—living systems as patches that might soften a hard line. Your practical instincts bristle at that slide into governable optics, but something steadier answers: the immediate alarms quieted. People slept. That is not nothing.

The garden smells of wet earth and chamomile; a neighbor offers a thermos of something sweet and bitter. Relief hums through people like a shared current—some tired, some cautiously buoyant. For a town that has been living on the edge, a concrete edge that keeps one thing out can mean something as simple as a baby's full night of sleep. Small human mercies have gravity.

[Scene Transition: From the floodwall lights, your path turns inland toward the bluff. The glow of a pavilion sits against the dark like a clinical lighthouse.]

[Scene: Lydia's Command Center (Temporary Pavilion) | Night]
[Visual: Glass and matte steel glowing cyan; digital displays map surge scenarios in cool coral and blue]
[Sound: Low hum of inverters, the soft whir of servers, muted conversation through glass]
[Music: A single piano motif builds into a hopeful chord, warm strings underscoring possibility]

The pavilion is all clean lines and calibrated calm. Inside, screens animate projected surges and cost timelines. The lighting feels intentional—assuring, meant to steady nerves. Lydia's presence matches the place: polished, precise, but now with a less standoffish tilt as if tonight's mandate loosened something like armor.

Lydia: (stepping forward) "Marin. You did important work getting people organized. The vote was quick, but it was the town speaking in the face of danger. This is about preserving lives now."
You: "I'm not naïve, Lydia. Rapid protection buys breathing room, yes—but it's blunt. If we don't plan the lee, we risk ecological damage and harm the very livelihoods we're trying to save."
Lydia: (nodding) "I know your perspective. That's why I want you involved. We need someone who can translate community detail into specifications I can work into contractor bids. I'm offering you a technical liaison post. You'd advise on living feature integration and, crucially, on access points for fishers."
You: (voice held steady) "That sounds like influence. It also sounds like being co-opted. My cooperative exists because top-down fixes left gaps. How will your team make space for adaptive management?"
Lydia: (a small, almost tired smile) "We have clauses for adaptive contracts. We'll require monitoring and incremental upgrades. If your cooperative can show proven performance—benchmarks, maintenance plans—we can tie those into payouts. This isn't just optics. I want the wall to function with the town, not against it."

She gestures at a screen: a render where a slotted timber ledge allows small skiffs; a recessed marsh bed in the lee; a maintenance stair cut through at precise intervals. The render is tidy in a way reality rarely is, but you can start to see the bones of a hybrid solution. The offer tastes like possibility. It tastes like compromise. It makes your chest feel oddly full.

Jonah slips into the pavilion doorway, damp hem of his trousers catching the light. He has been arguing all day; there's a fleck of concrete on his sleeve. His expression toward you is complex—relief braided with suspicion, affection braided with the same practical stubbornness that made him argue at the shipyard.

Jonah: (low) "You're really considering this?"
You: (meeting his eyes) "They're offering me a place at the table. I haven't accepted. I wanted you to know—because it changes things."
Jonah: (runs a thumb along the braid bracelet in his hand) "It changes things for me too. If you go in, you might make it better. Or they might swallow you up with contracts and stamped approvals."
You: "I know. I also know leaving it to only engineers in suits won't keep the nets in the water. Being inside lets me push for access points, for living edges that preserve tidal flows. It's not clean. But the town needs nights without sirens."
Jonah: (after a breath) "Then don't take it for me or because it's the safe route. Take it to make the thing better—if it will be better."

Lydia watches this exchange with an unreadable tilt—a strategy formulating, a calculation aligning. Her hands fold with that economy of someone used to making decisions by weighing outcomes publicly. You can feel the current: a role you take could shift the project's margin toward the living systems you argued for. It also could tether your cooperative to a timetable that prizes completion above iteration.

Lydia: "We need practitioners, Marin. People who will do the hard work of turning policy into functioning places. Will you at least come in tomorrow, go over the specifications, and tell us where the living inserts can actually survive our construction timeline?"
You: (the thought arrives like a tide) "I'll review the specs. I'll raise the questions of maintenance, of who keeps the marshes alive after contractors leave. I won't promise you unconditional endorsement."
Lydia: "Nor would I expect it. I want honest oversight—someone who will say 'this won't work' as much as 'this will.' That candor is how we avoid mistakes."

Her words are a rare currency: an invitation with room for dissent. You feel the tug of both sides—your cooperative's independence and the immediate safety the wall promises. For the first time since the vote, the tension doesn't feel like a choice between only loss or only gain. It feels like a bridge you might help stitch.

You walk to the pavilion's glass, palms pressed to the cool surface. Outside, along the newly poured concrete, worklights stitch a line. Small figures move like deliberate thoughts. The sea beyond is a dark, patient plane. The lights crawling along the wall look like a town's nervous heartbeat, steadying.

You imagine, not with naïve certainty but with careful hope, marsh grasses threaded into recesses, a maintenance rota signed by neighbors and contractors, skiff ramps preserved in ledger—small victories stacked into a form that keeps both water and culture at bay. For a moment, the town breathes, and the breath feels like the right kind of breathing: alert, not panicked.

You consider the trade-offs—the cooperative's independence, the integrity of living systems, the immediacy of shelter—but tonight the air is mostly light. People will sleep without bailing. Children will wake and maybe, maybe, still learn to read tides from sterns. That is a rising note you allow yourself to hear.

Your phone buzzes softly: a message from Raff, a clipped video of someone painting a mural's last stroke before the rain. The pavilion's glow feels less ominous and more like a tool that can be wielded for good, if handled carefully. You have a role placed in your hands, tangible and risky and necessary.

You have time to think, to ask questions, to set conditions. You have not decided.

You breathe in the metallic-sweet air of the pavilion and feel an odd steadiness settle in your chest. This is where plans become things. This is where you might fold community care into concrete geometry.

A page-turn waits at the edge of your thoughts—an appointment tomorrow, specs to read, people who trust you and people who doubt you. You straighten your jacket, feeling the small weight of the satchel at your hip: drafted notes, a list of maintenance priorities, a half-eaten granola bar. It all feels less like baggage and more like tools.

[Music: The strings resolve into a warm, rising chord; a single violin lingers]

You step away from the glass, the sea a dark promise beyond the lights. The decision isn't made, but a pathway forward is illuminated: a place at the table, the chance to press for living features, and the immediate quiet the wall will buy the town. Tonight, for the first time in a long season, hope feels practical.

You turn toward the pavilion's interior where Lydia's team waits with plans and coffee and cameras. The next step will be inside, where technical language will decide what gets built and how. You can almost hear the hum of machines translating intention into structure.
