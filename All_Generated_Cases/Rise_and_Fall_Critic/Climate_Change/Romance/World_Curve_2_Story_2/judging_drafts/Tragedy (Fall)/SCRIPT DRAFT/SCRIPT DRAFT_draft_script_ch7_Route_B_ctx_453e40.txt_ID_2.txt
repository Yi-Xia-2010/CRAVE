[Scene: Kaito's Field Lab / Boathouse | Late Afternoon — Rain]
[Visual: The boathouse door yawns open. Fluorescent task lamps slice through rain-streaked shutters, catching motes of salt and soldered wire. Glass jars labeled with tide samples catch the light in a tired, jewel-like row.]
[Sound: Rain smacking the corrugated roof, the distant slap of tide against pilings, a coffee kettle sighing on a hotplate.]

You push the warped door open with the heel of your palm. The smell that hits you — a clean, metallic tang threaded with wet kelp and fading solder — is exactly the kind of comfort that should have no right to settle warm and close in your chest. It tugs at something practical and tender at once: the memory of hands that could coax life from broken things, and the memory of a brother's laugh before the storm took him away.

Kaito looks up from a bench cluttered with microcontrollers and twisted copper. His ash-brown hair is flattened by the wind; a few stray strands hang over his forehead. Sea-green eyes look up and hold on you like someone checking that a fragile experiment hasn't shattered.

Kaito: "You're late," (he says, half-grinning, but his voice loses the joke in the reverb of the room) "which is to say — right on time to pretend you haven't been thinking about sensors all evening."

You can't help a small, dry laugh that tastes like rain. The edge of your dive watch has fogged; when you rub it, a fine bead of water blurs the metal.

You: "I was watching the promenade," you say. "Thinking about whether banners look like hope or promises."

Kaito's smile softens. He sets down a spool of wire and gestures toward a weathered schematic taped to the wall: a map of the reclaimed marsh with tiny nodes pinned along submerged channels.

Kaito: "We can start with the low-impact berms by Tomas's old inlet," (he says) "mangrove plugs here, staggered sensors to record salinity, pore pressure, wave overtopping. If the data shows success, we get the proof we need without handing the town to a corporation."

His optimism is a small flame, steady against the wet. It comforts — and it unnerves you. You can almost hear the counter argument: LuxCorp's slick renderings, their promise of immediate protection, of lives saved with a single contract. The community that trusts quick certainty is not the same as the one that trudges into muddy water at dawn planting roots by hand.

Dr. Noor steps forward, tablet in hand, the blue glow reflecting on her glasses. Her datasets are lean and efficient, the kind of language that could sway a mayor or win a grant panel when translated into charts.

Dr. Noor: "We ran the hydrological models again," (she says) "and the variance looks promising if we maintain planting windows and low-disturbance construction. Scaling is non-linear — doubling manpower reduces error, but not without community labor input."

She taps the tablet. Colored bands advance across a screen — projected salinity fluctuations, predicted sediment accretion if berms are placed in a particular arrangement.

You study the projections, and your chest tightens. You know what the models mean in practice: damp mornings on your knees, volunteers stiff from cold, Tomas's hands cracking into callous. You know what Lux's plan means in practice too: cranes and concrete, a quick payoff for investors, a hard line across the shore that would save some lives and erase some livelihoods.

Tomas is at the doorway, leaning on a cane more from habit than necessity. Rain gathers in his beard. He has a thermos tucked into his jacket; when he walks in, the room smells briefly of brewed coffee and fish oil.

Tomas: "I've seen storms enough to know concrete holds," (he says, voice gravelly) "but I've seen concrete turn men feral when it takes their coves. If the fishing mouths get cut off, what do we have to feed the town?"

Kaito meets Tomas's look and doesn't try to brush it away.

Kaito: "We're not trying to stop engineering," he says earnestly. "We're trying to show there are other options — ones that keep the ecological function so livelihoods can persist. The sensors tell us when to reinforce, when to retreat, when to plant more. It's responsive, not just reactive."

There are multiple silences that answer him. Each is a tide pulling different things away.

You: "And the money?" The question is brittle. It always is. "Who pays for months of volunteer labor? Small grants don't buy storm insurance."

Kaito's face tightens for a fraction of a second — the admission that idealism needs wiring to reality — then he reaches into a crate and produces a thin envelope.

Kaito: "We have a seed grant," he says. "Small. Enough to get the pilot in the water and buy materials. Dr. Noor's network can help with validation for a larger application. But it's not a sea wall, Maya. Not in a hundred lifetimes."

You fold the envelope and feel its weight like a coin that will not pay the town's fear.

Dr. Noor folds her hands, watching you both.

Dr. Noor: "There's also reputational risk," (she says softly) "and political pressure. If we pilot and fail — if a storm overwhelms our adaptive measures before they've matured — it becomes ammunition for LuxCorp. The data would be used against community-led approaches."

You taste the metallic tang of possibility. Noor's point is not hypothetical in the way you learned to fear hypotheticals; it's the cold arithmetic of grant panels and headlines.

Kaito: "Then we pilot carefully," (he says) "we pick the sheltered coves Tomas suggested, run sensors for a season, show incremental success. We present the mayor with numbers and conditions. We don't need a spectacle — we need credibility."

You can see the logic. You can also hear the town's own rhythm — people wanting action now, not seasons of patience. Your chest aches with the knowledge that patience costs lives; that haste costs culture.

You walk to the open shutter and look out toward the reclaimed marsh. The path they plan to use is half-submerged: knee-deep water where salt grasses sway, the wooden walkway bowed and patched. Little orange flags mark the sensor nodes, like a constellation hung over a wounded skin.

You: "If we do this," you say, "can we keep it Harborfall's? Not just 'ours' but truly ours — volunteer-driven, locally managed? Or will we end up doing the legwork while someone else sells the story?"

Kaito: "That is the point," (he replies, voice low) "the governance design is as important as the berm. Community-managed stewardship, open-data sensors, rotating leadership. We build a model that is replicable but tethered: community rights, local hiring, shared IP."

Tomas snorts softly, almost a laugh and almost not.

Tomas: "Talk is fine. But talk doesn't plant a mangrove. People do. Do we have hands for this?"

You feel the familiar coil of guilt tighten — the reflexive wanting to shoulder more than you can carry. It's the same knot that sat in your throat the night you couldn't save him. It warps your confidence: if you champion this publicly and it falters, you'll be the person who promised a shore and delivered mud. If you keep it quiet and build proof, you risk the town being sold in the meantime. If you take it to Mayor Elena as a compromise, you might anchor the pilot with official backing — or you might let politicians trade it for votes.

Dr. Noor leans forward. "Mayor Elena is due to meet with Lux's council next week," she says. "If we present numbers sooner, we have leverage. If we wait, Lux will have the floor. The timing is brittle."

Kaito: "We can be ready with data in two months," (he says) "but the storm season starts in six. Two months to make a case; four months to prove it won't fail catastrophically."

You let the numbers sit where they will. They feel less like measures and more like counting breaths between waves.

You: "And the people?" you ask. "Are our volunteers rested? Are the families ready for the work this winter? Or will we burn goodwill until there is nothing left to spend?"

Noor: "We can stagger shifts," she offers. "We can pay stipends from the seed grant to anchor critical labor. It's not much, but it's something."

Kaito adds, quietly, "I'll handle outreach for training. Tomas can bring fishermen crews. Hana can --"

You picture Hana in her apron, hands quick, sending soup and schedules to muddy volunteers. You imagine the diner a locus of warmth and logistics; small, human scaffolding for a larger plan. The image is lovely and terrifying in equal measure.

A hollow tap on the wooden floor makes all three of you look toward the doorway. A municipal courier drops a folded note — stamped with the mayor's seal. The paper is damp at the edges where rain seeped through the outer envelope.

You break the seal. The note is short. Mayor Elena requests an urgent briefing tomorrow morning. LuxCorp will present their proposal at noon; she asks you to come early to discuss "community options."

Your throat goes dry. The room seems to tilt, the warm lamplight bending around the hard edges of the choice like a bruise.

Kaito: "That's sooner than I expected," (he murmurs) "but it's an opportunity."

Tomas's jaw tightens. "Or a trap," he says. "Elena's trying to hold us in the middle. Playing both sides."

You close your hand around the wet paper until the fibers crease and give. In your waterproof notebook, you scribble three words: Pilot / Mayor / Quiet. They sit like anchors that could pull different currents.

You think of how Lux makes things look inevitable — renderings of a clean, raised boulevard and a company logo stamped into every pier. You think of how nature-based work is messy, distributed, and painfully slow. You think of Kaito's gentle insistence that models can be humane and that data can be an ally. You think of Tomas's hands and Noor's tablet and Hana's soup-stained aprons.

Everything you feel — duty, fear, love — folds into the small, terrible calculus you have to perform now.

You stand at a line where three roads converge: champion the pilot publicly, take it to Mayor Elena as a compromise, or keep working quietly to build proof before you ever step into the town square. Each option bends the future of Harborfall in a different direction.

You breathe. The rain on the roof keeps time with the old watch on your wrist, the glass of your jars glittering like small, patient witnesses.

You must decide.
