[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Dawn]
[Visual: Fog lifting off the water; damp pamphlets stacked on a crate, brine beading on edges]
[Sound: Gull calls, distant engine rumble, a soft clatter as someone ties a crate down]
[Music: Low, mournful piano with an undercurrent of distant drums]

You stand beneath a threadbare banner—BLACK TYPE on recycled paper: "OUR HARBOR, OUR VOTE." The paper is slick with morning mist. Your waterproof notebook is in your coat pocket, weighted with names, scrawled phrases, and a pen that never seems to dry. The dive watch your brother left you presses into your ribs through the fabric; it sits there like an accusation and a promise.

You breathe in. Salt and diesel. The smell sharpens the memory of storm-night wind and a siren you still can't forgive the world for not silencing.

You recall the greenhouse room where the decision was framed—hands stained with soil and faces folded with fear and hope. The referendum was meant to be a valve: a way to return control to people who know the town's rhythms. It was not fast. It was not neat. It would be messy and it would matter. You promised yourself that, at least, you would try.

You set the crate of flyers down and fold your scarf tighter against the wind.

[Scene: Hana's Diner | Mid-morning]
[Visual: Warm, cramped interior; sunlight through steamed windows; paper chains and mismatched chairs]
[Sound: Clatter of plates, low chatter, coffee steam hiss]
[Music: Muted acoustic guitar — hopeful but strained]

Hana: "Good. Tomas already called three fishermen. Said he'll bring the old lanterns for atmosphere." (she laughs, then grows serious) "You're sure about this? No going back to Lux?" 

You study the way she fills orders with the same hands that stitch aprons and mend nets. She believes in doing the small things until they add up. You want to tell her that the referendum is both the small things and the only thing that might stop a slick contract from swallowing the cove whole.

You: "I'm sure. People should decide. Not corporate press releases." (you sip the coffee; it tastes like another town's morning)
Hana: "Then we'll make our case. Soup and signatures." (she claps you on the shoulder, too hard—comfort disguised as momentum)

Hana leans across the counter and drops a stack of freshly photocopied argument sheets. One reads: "Community Protection Plan: Mangroves, Berms, Local Jobs." Another reads: "Why Outside Contracts Risk Our Shoreline." They smell like toner and resolve.

Tomas arrives, cheeks windburned, a slow kind of urgency in his step. He plants himself in the nearest booth, the leather creaking.

Tomas: "Maya—" (he uses the name like an old rope, familiar and taut) "You're stirring a hornet nest." 
You: "They're already buzzing, Tomas." (you feel the thread of impatience that sometimes sits beneath your careful words)
Tomas: "Listen. I don't like Lux any more than you do—" (he says the name carefully, as if it could cut) "—but we can't throw everything we have against a thing and hope it bends. People need protection. They'll vote with their fear." 

You let the weight of what he says settle. He is the town's memory of safe distances: how close to fish, where the shoals bend, how storms once came but didn't stay. He believes in tradition because he has kept it alive with his hands.

You: "We have to make fear into something they can say yes to. Not just 'no'." (you tap the notebook; the pages flutter with signatures already)
Tomas: "Aye. But don't parley with the devil by pretending victory's guaranteed." (he looks at you, then at Hana) "This referendum—it's a battle that's wearing us thin."

[Scene: Harborfall — Canvassing Route | Noon]
[Visual: Market stalls; salt-worn posters; a large digital board on a municipal pole flickering with a LuxCorp ad]
[Sound: Murmurs, a child's laugh, the persistent looped voice of a corporate narrator from the ad]
[Music: Low strings tugging at the edges, a cold undertone entering the room]

You walk the square with a clipboard, offering pamphlets to people who take them with fingers salted by work. Your voice runs on the cadence of rehearsed truth—short, clear, and repeatable. Tomas follows in the distance, passing out tea in thermoses like an anchor.

A municipal digital board flips images: smiling families behind a gleaming blue sea wall; LuxCorp's tagline: SAFE HARBORS, SUSTAINABLE FUTURES. The narrator's voice is warm and professional, and it makes your volunteer's hand tremble when they try to speak over it.

Volunteer: (muttering) "They've got money for that screen. They can buy half the town if they want."

You: "Then we make people see beyond the view." (you hand a leaflet to an old woman who smells of fish and lavender) "Ask them what they want the sea to look like. Ask them what jobs they want to keep." 

The old woman takes the leaflet, studies it with a slow, practiced skepticism, then looks up.

Old Woman: "My house's porch is where I sit and watch the boys come in. If there's a wall that takes that porch—what am I sitting on?" (her voice is small but contains decades of question)

You falter for a moment—the practical pitch of flood control clashes with the tiny human geographies the wall would erase. The watch in your pocket buzzes against your ribs, a small, useless timekeeper. Kaito appears then, carrying a bag of sensors and a tired, careful smile.

Kaito: "We should record that—where people mark their porch-lines. Data helps when you make an argument about alternatives." (he speaks softly, more to you than the passing crowd)
You: (a flicker of irritation) "Data doesn't comfort someone whose porch is swallowed." (you keep your voice low; you don't want to lose the room to statistics)
Kaito: "I know. But it gives Mayor Elena leverage. It shows loss in numbers, not just sentiment. The city council listens to costs, not to poetry." (his eyes search yours; brief, earnest)

You: "And numbers won't stop the billboard." (you glance at the Lux ad as it slides into glossy images of "success")
Kaito: "They can help the petition look less like nostalgia and more like a plan, though." (he reaches out and tucks a sensor pamphlet into your clipboard; it's a small, practical act)

The conversation stretches between you: ideal and instrument, heart and tool. Neither of you lets the other wholly own the room.

You and Kaito split the route. You take what people hand you—stories of lost nets, a child's bedroom with salt lines along the wall, nights that smell of tar. Kaito takes numbers, long columns of data. At a corner, a group of teenagers in LuxCorp shirts hand out glossy brochures with calm, practiced smiles. One of them nails your gaze with a question that smells of rehearsed doubt.

Teen: "Isn't it risky to say no to a proven system? What if—"

You almost answer with the truth (what if we lose everything trying to keep what we love?) but instead give a measured line about democratic choice and local solutions.

You: "Risk is why we need to measure every option. We shouldn't hand the town over because a company offers a quick promise." (you feel the old ache for certainty tighten) "We deserve both safety and a voice."

[Scene: Harborfall Mayor's Office / Pop-up Meeting | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Sparse municipal room, chairs in a circle; a poster board with "Fair Debate Guidelines"]
[Sound: Chalk on board, muffled traffic, a ringtone cut short]
[Music: Dissonant piano chords, slow and pulling down]

Mayor Elena arrives with a stack of municipal memos and exhaustion in the hollows under her eyes.

Mayor Elena: "I've tried to ask Lux to slow their ad buys and give us a fair forum." (she presses her hands together) "They agreed to the forum, but they won't pull the billboards. Their PR says it's 'community outreach'." (she exhales as if the breath is expensive)
You: "Can we get public financing for the debate? A neutral moderator?" (you ask, listing logistics because lists help)
Mayor Elena: "We can try, but my budget's tight. The county's already funneling disaster funds to match Lux's bid—if the investors smell compromise they might pull out." (her voice is small; political pressure smells like a coming tide)

Kaito: "We could crowd-source the streaming. Local tech, open-sourced moderation." (he brightens, offering a fix)
Mayor Elena: (half-smiles) "That would help. But it won't stop the ad in the meantime. The town's idea of 'choice' is being shaped by a continuous, polished message of safety." 

The room hums with the sense of being padded into a shape not of your making. You see the county's money as a hinge—if it swings one way, it shuts the other out.

Tomas: "People will be frightened," he says slowly. "And frightened people hand over keys to strangers." (his tone is not accusation but warning)

You: "Then we show them what they would keep if they choose this path." (you push a map across the table, pointing at porches, gardens, the mangrove estuary—spots of local life that could be lost) "We turn fear into stakes, not slogans."

Mayor Elena: "I'll push for fairness. But be clear—this referendum is a gamble. If we win, the town decides. If we lose, investors will treat that as a mandate." (she stares at you as if measuring your spine)

Kaito reaches across and places a hand on the map, right over the estuary.

Kaito: "Either way, we need to be ready. If we lose, there needs to be contingencies to protect people long enough for other options to breathe." (he looks at you, worry like weather)

You: "We will make contingencies." (you say it to calm him, to anchor the room)

[Scene: Market Square — Evening Rally | Dusk]
[Visual: Handmade signs, lanterns bobbing, LuxCorp vans parked at the edge with ambient lighting]
[Sound: Cheers turned thin by the distant hum of a LuxCorp generator, a recorded slogan drifting on the wind]
[Music: Slow cello, underpinned by the low thrum of anxious breath]

The evening is raw and alive: volunteers singing old sea shanties to get attention, Tomas with his lanterns leading a small procession, Hana passing out bowls of soup. For a moment you feel the town as it was—wounded, singing, stubborn.

A LuxCorp representative steps onto a small platform with the practiced charm of someone who has rehearsed every pause. She smiles—steel-gray eyes scanning the crowd.

Lux Representative: "We all want Harborfall to be safe. Our plan offers protection, jobs, and a tested model." (her voice is warm, a balm)
You: "What about the coves they'll have to sacrifice?" (you step forward; the microphone feels like a torch)
Lux Representative: (a trained flicker of pity) "We aim to minimize displacement. Models are imperfect—" (she chooses the word) "—but we must act before the next major storm." 

The crowd murmurs; some nod, some scowl. A teenager in a Lux shirt yells that the referendum is selfish. A woman near the back clutches her child and says she can't risk another night like the last one.

Kaito appears beside you, voice low.

Kaito: "They're good at framing urgency as inevitability. We need a counter-frame—something that says 'we are acting, too'." 
You: "I'm aware." (your chest tightens; the watch grows heavier) "But we also can't be sure that a counter-frame will compete with their money." 

Later, as the crowd dissipates, someone in the back—an investor-looking man with an expensive parka that smells faintly of ozone—leans in and hands you a glossy card.

Investor: "You've got spirit. But I'd watch your tone if I were you. The town needs to know you can lead without scaring out the voters." (he smiles like a shark folding into a conversation)

You look at him. The offer is ambiguous: warning or threat. You fold the card into your fist, feeling the paper's gloss.

[Music: A single long violin note hangs, unresolved]

You walk back toward Hana's diner with Kaito and Tomas. The town is quieter than it was this morning. Flyers dampen in puddles. The neon on LuxCorp's distant plaza stabs at the horizon like an accusation.

Kaito: (softly) "We're losing pace. Their ad buys don't sleep." 
You: "Then we don't either." (you say it with the brittle confidence of someone trying to stitch a net while the tide is coming in)

Tomas: "Remember why we do this." (he grips your shoulder, his hands smelling of brine and tea) "Not for us alone—so the next ones know the harbor's shape by heart." 

You close your hand around your brother's dive watch. It is warm from your skin. The band has a fainted dent from where he used to beat time back into a boat's engine. It is both talisman and weight.

[Sound: The clock over the counter ticks; distant automated LuxCorp ads loop faintly outside]
[Music: Sparse piano, descending motifs—somber and unresolved]

Hana: "You did good today." (the compliment is small, but heavy)
You: "I don't know if 'good' is enough." (you rub the bridge of your nose; exhaustion has softened your edges)

Mayor Elena: "Maya, I can't promise fairness in reach. The county's saying Lux's contract looks appealing for regional funds. Mayor Elena: "I know. I also know people are tired. Keep the canvassing through the weekend. Focus on porches and kids' rooms—things a seawall can't replace without cost imagery." (she pauses) "I'll try to secure the forum. But Maya—be careful. This could backfire."

The line goes quiet. You curl your fingers around the warm mug and feel the hum of the town's thousands of small, exhausted choices moving like a slow, sad tide.

Kaito: "We can run community monitors—get independent readings that show how nature-based restoration could work while we vote." (he tries to set up a plan on the napkin)
You: "That's the plan, yes. But I'm watching that watch in my pocket." (you touch the dive watch where it rests against your ribs) "Time isn't our friend."

Kaito: (after a beat) "Then we make time do its job—let it show what resilience looks like over months, not corporate quarters." (his fingers brush yours briefly over the napkin; for a second the city's weather drops into the space between you)

You pull back. The room fills with the many small silences the town keeps.

Hana: "Get some sleep. Even soldiers need a pause." (she hands you a folded blanket)

You look out the fogged window at the distant lights of LuxCorp, blinking like a promise you can't accept right now. Your mind walks the shoreline in your head: porches, gardens, the mangroves with their kneeling roots. You remember Tomas's hands and your brother's laughter like a bell cracked and mended. You think of the referendum as a lever that could lift those things—or as a saw that could cut them away.

Everything feels raw. Your principled choice sits like a bruise: necessary, painful, and uncertain.
