Tomas leans over the map spread on the workbench, his fingers tracing lines long older than the town's newest seawalls.

Tomas: "That curve there—old Fisher's Cove—used to hold nets like teeth. The mud's deeper now, but the pocket's still there. Plant a row here and another staggered there; the roots will snag silt and slow the water."

You nod, but the nod is a thing you do for other people—proof that they can carry on with their plan because you believe in it. Inside, you name each seedling as if that gives it purpose: mangrove propagule, saltwort shoot, nurse grasses. The clipboard feels steady in your hand, the stamped lists your compass.

You: "If we stagger them, the flow slows earlier. If the sensor array picks that up, Noor can model it into the town's plan. It buys time for berms to catch up."

Tomas: (a slow smile) "You're thinking in rescue lines, Maya. Good. But don't forget—these trees remember how to be the sea's enemy in their own way. They don't surrender quick."

You meet his eyes—his face a map of seasons and storms—and for a moment, the ache behind your ribs eases into a practical problem to solve.

[Scene: Mangrove Estuary | Afternoon]

You carry a tray out across the walkways. The sun is a flat coin over the water, and each step makes the boards moan. Kaito waits by his sensor pack, sleeves rolled, fingers deft as he calibrates the pendant that will track salinity and root movement.

Kaito: "If I angle these just right, we'll get baseline shifts within a week. That'll tell us whether the staggered planting is pulling sediments the way we hope."

You: "A week feels like too long when the tide climbs three inches every year, but we planted on science, not prayer. Show me how they read the first spike."

Kaito explains the graphs, but his voice tightens when he mentions the funding constraints and the time it takes for roots to anchor. He offers you a small, private smile—soft, hopeful, worried.

Kaito: "You did all the talking for the vote, Maya. You gave the town a reason to believe. The sensors will tell the truth—good or harsh. We can adapt."

You: "We can adapt only if we survive the next three storms. That's the calculus I wake up to."

Kaito reaches out, brushing a smear of mud on your wrist—an accidental intimacy that lands between practical and tender.

Kaito: (quiet) "Whatever happens, we'll read it together."

People sing as they plant—an old work-song Hana taught them last summer. It threads through the estuary, a human counterpoint to the tide's steady logic. Children stamp out clumps of mud; Tomas shows a teenager how to hold a propagule without bruising the tender root.

Tomas: "Slow hands. Respect the root. They'll do their work if you don't hurry them."

The planting goes on like this: small, stubborn joys measured in inches. The town moves as an organism—neighbors arriving with scuffed boots and thermoses, the mayor sending an approving nod from the haul road, elders cupping their chins and remembering when those coves were full of cod. You feel the referendum's victory not in a headline but in these hands, in these low, soft laughs.

[Scene: Promenade / Market Square | Evening]

You speak at the square that night. Your voice is steadier than you feel. You speak for the seedlings, for the people who made time and borrowed boots, for the smell that has come back to the docks. Hana stands at the edge of the crowd, hands folded, eyes bright.

You: "We won't be saved by a single wall. We'll be saved by a thousand hands. These roots bind the earth; they buy us time to do the harder work of being a community that chooses its future."

Hana: (afterwards, pressing a bowl of soup into your hands) "You gave them a picture of home. That matters."

You: "It has to matter. I can't—" You stop. The watch on your wrist ticks, inherited metal and demand. You press your thumb against it until the tick becomes a thing you can feel.

Hana: "Hey. You did the thing your brother would have wanted. You gave people agency. That counts."

You want to argue (what if agency isn't food? What if time runs out?), but the evening's warmth and the chorus of voices singing as they leave the square hold that objection in check—or at least, defer it.

Kaito stays to run the first overnight diagnostics. He meets you by the door, the sensor pendant blinking a slow, trusting rhythm against his chest.

Kaito: "Early data looks promising. A change in flow near the staggered rows—we're seeing slight sediment accrual."

You: (relief, thin) "Slight. It's a start."

Kaito: (hesitates) "There are satellite blips showing an incoming atmospheric pulse. It's not on the scale of the 'thirties storms, but—"

You: "But it could redline what we've just started." The sentence lodges like a stone.

Kaito: "We'll keep sensors on. We'll reroute volunteers to the eastern quay at first light."

You run your thumb over the face of your brother's watch. It keeps time with a stubborn, unflinching beat. You think of the ocean, patient and terrible.

[Scene: Eastern Quay | Dawn]

The surge hits faster than you let yourself measure. The water lurches up, a sudden, hungry hand across the quay. Seedlings—no thicker than a child's wrist—are snatched from the mud before their roots can claim purchase. Planted lines unravel like a pulled seam.

A family from the eastern quay—two parents and an old woman who'd pointed you to a particular cove last month—stand with sodden sleeves as their storeroom roof peels like a tin hat in a gale. Tomas is there, shouting directions, the old man's voice thin with fury and something like pain.

Tomas: "Get the boxes! Pull the nets! Move the kids inland—now!"

You run. The mud grabs at your boots. Water brushes the hem of your jacket and climbs. You see the storeroom's door twist and then rip off, crates tumbling into the brine with a sound like a body dropping.

Mother: (wailing) "Our fish for winter—it's gone! How—how could this happen?"

You: (that practiced leadership cracking) "Tomas, we're taking the kids—Hana's diner is open—get them sheltered, I'll—"

Kaito arrives, sensors bobbing, eyes wide and raw. He reaches for a crate half-submerged.

Kaito: "We—this shouldn't have—" His sentence breaks on the cold air.

You scoop at the mud, hands slick, nails grooved with silt. The seedlings you planted less than a season ago float like wounded things. You try to hold one, but the husk slips through your fingers.

You: (to Kaito, voice low) "Did the readings show the surge? Did we—"

Kaito: (shaking his head) "It climbed faster than the models. A pressure anomaly—unexpected. We didn't have lead time."

Your knuckles whiten on the watch as you twist it until the metal leaves a crescent on your skin. The town's moral clarity—its steadfast refusal to hand its future to an outside promise—feels suddenly like a choice made in a different weather. LuxCorp's machines hum closer, a background of efficiency that grinds at the scene like a second tide.

Water takes the feel of everything—temperature, pressure, the salt that stings the inside of your nose. You plunge into the mud not because you think it will save all of it but because ritual requires motion. Each dive is a small absolution and a confession: you cannot stop everything.

You surface sputtering, a mangrove shoot clutched in your palm like a child's wrist. It is limp. You press it against your chest like a promise. The sound of LuxCorp's machines, a steady, unrelenting thrum, carries over the quay like a sentence pronounced.
