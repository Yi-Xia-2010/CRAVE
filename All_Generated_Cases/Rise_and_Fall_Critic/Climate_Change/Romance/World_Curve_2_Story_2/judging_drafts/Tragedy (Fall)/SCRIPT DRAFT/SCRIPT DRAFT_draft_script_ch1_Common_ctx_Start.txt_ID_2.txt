[Visual: Fog clings to the railings; salt streaks the wood. LuxCorp banners, clinical teal, blur at the edge of the plaza.]
[Sound: Distant gull calls, the soft creak of tide-slick boards, the distant thrum of an outboard engine waking]

You wake before the town does — not because sleep deserts you but because ritual calls. The air tastes of iron and cold salt; it presses against your face through the collar of your jacket. Fingers you almost don't trust move on their own.

You check the dive watch on your wrist. The metal is warm from your skin, the glass scratched in the places your brother used to joke made it look "well-traveled." His name is flat in your chest like an unhealed place. You don't speak it; you let the watch speak back in the single, dependable tick. Each notch is an old promise: don't let it happen again.

You twist your hair, feeling the teal streak tucked into the braid at your temple — a small, private rebellion against a sea that wants uniformity. Your hand lingers on it, a brief contact that steadies your head. You cinch the rope belt of your cargo trousers until the loop is familiar under your palm. Little rituals make a map through the day.

You breathe in: salt, rusted iron, simmering broth from Hana's still-dark diner. The horizon is a bruise-blue line that refuses to brighten. Retrofit lamps blink their tired rhythm along the promenade; someone has affixed extra sheathing to the posts where the last surge gnawed at the wood. Farther on, LuxCorp's banners hang crisp and clinical — teal like the screens of their models — the promise of certainty against the fog. The color feels wrong here, like a bandage over a living wound.

You run the list in your head with the careful efficiency of someone who learned to measure danger in checkboxes. Sandbag inspections first, then seed runs to the rooftop greenhouse, a call to Dr. Noor about salinity readings, and a line of names in your waterproof notebook that will not leave you alone. You thumb the edge of the notebook through your glove, feeling the ridged indent where "Tomas — low tide check" is written. Each name is a life that looks back at you.

Kaito's voice comes across that inventory like a soft calibration: calm, analytical, hopeful. It is memory and possibility at once. He talks sensors the way other people talk weather — precise numbers, code for care. You imagine little blinking things along the shoreline, patient and unbiased, keeping watch so the town doesn't have to always be so tense. The thought eases you, then tightens; hope offers comfort, but this season feels different. The ache under your ribs is a reminder: it isn't just the sea you measure. It's the weight of what happens if you misread it.

You move along the promenade. The boards are slick; your boots find purchase. By habit your hand rubs the stamped edges of the clipboard hanging from your shoulder — names, times, little boxes drawn for checkmarks — an object that feels heavier now than it did last year.

Hana is already in the door of the diner, a warm shape against the cool morning. The lamp above the counter spills honey light out into the mist, and through the window you see steam rising in gentle columns. The bell over the door rings when you push it — a small, bright sound in the hush.

Hana: "You're early. You look like you wrestled a tide to get here."

You: "I lost the match," you say, and her laugh is a small relief.

Hana: (shoving a chipped mug toward you) "Coffee. No questions. Well, maybe two. Did you sleep at all?"

You: "An hour here, two there. I checked the berm sensors again before bed. Saw some drift on the southern channel." You fold the details into the sentence plain as weather reports, because if you don't it will clatter in the back of your skull all day.

Hana: (softening) "You always make the ocean sound like a patient that's waiting for you to get it right." She pulls the scarf from around her neck and offers it; it's stained with flour and paint. "You want this? It'll taste like the diner, which is to say it'll remind you to eat."

You tell her you will take a cup, and the exchange becomes more than caffeine. Hana watches you with a practiced, fond concern that knows the difference between composure and carrying too much.

Hana: "Lux banners up at the plaza look sharp this morning," she says, nodding toward the window. "Mayor's gonna be meeting with a rep today, rumor is."

You feel that small internal shift — a ripple of something like preparedness and something like fear. Reports and banners mean money, means decisions that land on a town like an incoming tide. You resist the urge to snap at the rumors; it's easier to be useful than to be right.

You: "I'll stop by the greenhouse on the way back. We need seedlings moved to higher trays." You let the practical plan displace the larger dread. Plans are clean; dread is messy and contagious.

Hana: "Bring soup back later," she says, already ladling, already offsetting the fatigue with motion. "And Maya?"

You glance up. Her eyes are bright, watching you with the clarity of someone who makes houses from soup. "Don't forget to breathe."

You allow yourself a small, rueful smile and tuck the mug into your satchel as a ballast.

You step back out into the wash of cold and find the pier where Tomas stands, his hands folded against his waxed jacket. He smells of weather and tar, a presence both stubborn and patient.

Tomas: (gruff, but not unkind) "Morning. The sea's keeping certain secrets tonight. We need to check the outer markers yourself soon."

You: "I was headed to the berms first. Then greenhouse." Your voice is practical. You want to trade words for tasks; it feels safer.

Tomas: (narrowing his eyes) "You've that look. Like a net that won't close. You keep it up too long and you'll snare yourself."

You: "I'm not the only one watching. Tomas." Saying his name grounds you — a tether. "We're moving seedlings higher. I'm setting teams for sandbag rechecks. I'm meeting the greenhouse crew."

Tomas: "Hmph. Good. Keep the crews steady. And Maya—" He searches your face. "Don't carry it all on your own. The sea doesn't remember who held the rope last."

You want to tell him that you know — that the watch on your wrist is an entire conversation with grief — but the words stick. Instead you nod.

Tomas: "If you need the boat, you come to me. The old hull's got more life in it than it looks."

You: "I'll keep that in mind." You mean it, but also know how those offers will complicate planning. A boat is a tool, but it draws attention — and attention is a scarce currency this season.

You take a breath that tastes like metal and decision. The pier creaks underfoot; sea-spray mists your face. There is a continuity here that will not be swayed by banners or meetings: tides rise and fall, people prepare or fail to prepare, and you stand somewhere in the middle trying to keep the ledger balanced.

You flip through your waterproof notebook when you find a sliver of bench to rest on. Names and times are inked in small, careful handwriting — a geography of concern. Your thumb finds a scrawl you haven't allowed yourself to read properly: the loose, looping line that might have been a phone number or a prayer.

Kaito's voice is calmer in memory today — the sort of even tone that offers schematics and hope. "If the sensor arrays get placed along that last promontory, we'll have a real-time read on the shoaling," he would say, and the phrase "real-time read" is as tender as a promise right now. You picture his hands patched with ink, the camera strap across his chest, the little pendant blinking on his neck. You imagine him walking the greenhouse with you, talking about thresholds and tolerances, naming the things you're trying to keep alive.

You stand, closing the notebook with a snap that sounds louder than it should in the soft morning. The ache in your ribs tightens and becomes a bell: not an alarm, not exactly, but a call. All of the lists and sensors and soup won't mean anything if you hesitate when the tide moves faster than expected.

You move toward the rooftops where the greenhouse waits — a warm, glassed refuge perched on the berm. You'll go there to organize the seedlings, to give people work that matters and hands that can steady others. The greenhouse is both shelter and symbol: life raised above the waterline, fragile and deliberate.

As you walk, Harborfall unwraps itself in slow details. Laundry lines sag, a child's kite caught in a shrub like a signal. The teal banners at LuxCorp's plaza pulse in the half-light; corporate voices will start their conference calls and slide out models and diagrams that make everything sound inevitable. You can almost hear the velvet in Lux's pitch: "efficiency," "scale," "security." Words that mean safety to some and erasure to others.

Your chest tightens again. Memories are not only images; they're weather patterns. They make you read the horizon like a ledger and not a sea.

You pause at the base of the municipal stepped berms and look up at the greenhouse — glass panes beaded with salt, the promise of warmth inside. Around its foundation, the town's patched solar panels glint dully. You think about Kaito's sensors, about Tomas' offer of a boat, about Hana's soup and her steady, ridiculous faith in you. You think about the watch against your skin and the way its tick is both consolation and accusation.

A gull cries, high and ragged. The lamps along the promenade blink again, one and then another, like the town taking inventory of its light.

You take one last breath of cold air, feeling the weight of names in your bag and the ache in your ribs as if it were an extra pack to shoulder. You tuck your notebook back into the satchel, fingers lingering on its edge as if on a pulse.

For a moment you let the doubt come without language — a dull pool forming beneath the day. You remind yourself why you do this: the brother's absence is a promise reshaped into purpose. But purpose does not eliminate fear. It sharpens it.

When you start up the stair toward the greenhouse, every step is deliberate. The town's early light throws long shadows. Behind the neat geometry of LuxCorp's banners and the mayor's imminent meetings, the real work waits: hands in soil, rerouted drains, honest tradeoffs. The day will ask decisions of you. You are steady and small and not, perhaps, enough — and that might be the beginning of everything.

You reach the greenhouse door and pause with your hand on the handle. Heat and the smell of damp earth and compost press against the glass like a promise. Inside, people will be waiting — hands stained with soil, faces briefed on a hundred tiny contingencies. The meeting you have been steering toward all morning is near. It is not the mayor's conference room or LuxCorp's neat plaza; it is a place where roots meet hands, where decisions are measured in seedlings and salt tolerance.

You close your fingers around the handle, feeling the tendon near your thumb pulse with a dull, persistent ache. It could be nothing. It could be everything.

A gull slices across the bruise-blue horizon one last time. The fog parts in a thread of lighter gray, revealing the sharp teal of the banners beyond. You can see Tomas on the pier, a dark shape against the early light. Hana's lamp glows behind you, humming the town's warmth into the fog. Kaito's tentative optimism is a small, resistant flame behind your eyes.

You breathe and step forward.
