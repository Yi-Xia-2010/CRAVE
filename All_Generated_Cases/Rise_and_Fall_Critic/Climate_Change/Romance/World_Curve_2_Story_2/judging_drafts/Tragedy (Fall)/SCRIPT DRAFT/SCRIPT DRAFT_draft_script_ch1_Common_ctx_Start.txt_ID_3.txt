[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Dawn]
[Music: Low, melancholy piano with distant percussion—like rain on metal]
[Visual: Wet wooden planks, salt-darkened rope, LuxCorp banners half-visible through fog]
[Sound: Distant gull cries, the slow creak of mooring lines, soft patter of residual drizzle]

You wake before the sun remembers it should. The promenade smells of salt and rust—metal and sea braided into the town's breath—and the horizon is a flat, bruise-blue line that makes your chest tighten like the first cold of winter.

You feel for the dive watch on your wrist as if the motion itself is an anchor. It is heavier than you expect; heritance and loss worn in brass and a cracked crystal. You twist your hair with the other hand until the teal streak at your temple clicks against your teeth—an impatient, private fidget—and then you cinch the rope belt of your cargo trousers, knotting it twice out of habit. The wool of your scarf smells faintly of the rooftop greenhouse—damp soil and basil—and for a second you imagine sunlight in a place that will get it later.

[Visual: Close-up of Maya's battered dive watch; tide-line groove on its face]
Narration
You run through the morning inventory the way you always do: sandbag inspections at the eastern inlet, the seed runs to the rooftop greenhouse before the volunteer shift, a round of calls—check Tomas, check the south berm where the old pilings creak. Your waterproof notebook lies against your ribs like a small, stubborn heart; names are written in a looping hand that knows every street and every door. You flip it open with a thumbnail. The paper is water-stiff. The list is patient and unrelenting.

[Sound: A soft vibration—someone calling, or memory]
Narration
Kaito's voice lingers at the edges of your thoughts: calm, analytical. "Sensors in the berm will tell us whether the saturation is critical before we send people," you imagine him saying, the words more a promise than a plan. He taught you to trust numbers in the quiet way someone teaches you how to read the stars. The promise eases some of the ache—makes the problem feel like something you can map, name, and perhaps fix. But the ache in your rib is sharper today, a physical reminder that this season will ask more than it did last year.

[Visual: LuxCorp banners, clinical teal, a bit too bright against the fog]
Narration
From across the small plaza, LuxCorp's banners cut the fog with a clinical teal. Even from here they look almost sterile—neat corners, the promise of control—like someone drew a straight line over the town and called it a horizon. The retrofit lamps along the promenade blink in sequences: safety, standby, attention. They are municipal, municipal because the town insisted, practical because lives are at stake.

[Sound: Door bell, faint clatter from a kitchen]
[Scene: Passing Hana's Diner | Continuous]
[Visual: Warm lamplight bleeding from a steamed-up window; a handpainted sign reads "Hana's - Open Early"]
Narration
Hana's diner is already casting the smell of broth into the dawn; a steam-wreathed warmth against the chill. You pass the window, and Hana looks up from a ladle, flour dusting the apron at her neck like a quiet constellation.

Hana: "Mornin', Maya! You're killing me—up before the sun as usual. Coffee?" (she smiles, hands wiping faster than they need to)

You: "Please." (your laugh is half-automatic; the muscles around your eyes loosen)

Hana: "Sandwich later? I made extra for the volunteers—thought you might like one." (she crosses to the counter, speaks low) "And—there's talk. Lux's team left the plaza early with a mapping drone last night. Tomas saw them."

You: "Mapping drone." (you fold your hands around the cup she presses into them; it's hot, bitter, human) "Did they tell the Mayor?"

Hana: (shrugging) "They told someone. They always tell someone. You okay with them buzzing the inlet?"

You: "Only if it's a data-sharing thing. I want their scans, not their promises." (your voice is even, but tired)

Hana: (tilts her head) "You look like you pulled the tide around your neck in your sleep."

You: "Feels like it." (you finger the watch; the brass is a familiar weight) "This season's... different."

Hana: "Different's got teeth." (she says it matter-of-factly, then softer) "If you need folks, I've got souppots and ears. Tomas came by early asking about the pilings too. He said the net had a new tear."

You: "I'll see him at the pier." (you pause, watching steam fog the glass) "Thanks, Han. Save a sandwich if you can."

Hana: (grins, then falters) "Always. And—" (she lowers her voice further) "You looked at Lux's banner yesterday. You okay letting them in?"

You: (a beat) "We have to listen," you say. The words are sharper than you intend. "But the town decides. Not banners."

Hana: (nods, understanding more than she lets on) "Good. 'Cause the town needs you steady today."

Narration
Her steady, bright presence is a small buoy. You leave the warmth to the fog and the salt and the sound of the boards beneath your boots.

[Scene: Pier | Moments Later]
[Visual: Tomas leaning against the railing, waxed jacket beaded with spray; the sky behind him is a muted watercolor]
[Sound: Old wood groaning, a gull landing with a ragged call]

Tomas: (gruff, soft at the edges) "Maya. Early. 'Bout time someone beat the tide this morning."

You: "Thought I'd get the east inlet done while the water's low." (you approach, boots thumping on wet wood)

Tomas: (eyes flick to your watch) "That's your brother's, isn't it? Still runs faithful." (he pats the watch in a slow, practiced gesture) "Time keeps to its own business."

You: "It keeps me on schedule." (there's a tilt to your mouth that doesn't reach your eyes) "How's the net?"

Tomas: "Tore near the southeast marker. Salt eats knots like it's gossip. I can mend if you need—got the hands, not the time for long pulls." (he chuckles, then there's a small, sharp silence) "You sleep any?"

You: "Not much." (you say too quickly) "Plans, lists."

Tomas: (nods slowly) "Lists are good. But listen to the water too. It tells you where to walk."

You: "It tells me where to walk, then asks me to hold the line." (you stare out at the horizon, where the sea looks patient and indifferent)

Tomas: "Holdin' lines don't mean you hold them alone." (his voice is simple and certain) "You're not the only one with shoulders."

You: (the words are small and light in your chest, but they mean something) "I know." (you try for warmth, and almost manage it) "Thanks, Tom."

Tomas: "And—" (he hesitates) "There are tax boats coming by to survey the outer—" (he shrugs) "City folks. Keep your eyes on the drone paths. If they start takin' samples without askin', bring 'em to the Mayor."

You: "Will do." (you tuck the notebook under your arm; the paper's edges are wet with town weather)

Tomas: (a faint smile) "And Maya—don't let the watch tell you more than it should. He wouldn't want you to be the tide."

You: (the ache flares; you remember hands pulling your brother's body from a storm-sunk hull) "I won't." (you lie to him as you do to yourself, half-protective, half-hopeful)

Narration
The sea keeps moving, ignorant of the promises written on paper and the banners hung by men in suits. The LuxCorp banners across the plaza blink clinical teal, patient as a scalpel. Your list flutters in the same rhythm as your breath.

You check the notebook again. Names. Places. People who fold into the town's map like stitches. You count the seeds of the rooftop greenhouse in your head—enough to start new rows, not enough to save everything if the berm gives. The town looks smaller today, not in size but in the kind of vulnerability that makes every decision dense and sharp.

Narration
You move through Harborfall as if walking through a ledger: each building, each familiar face a line item demanding attention. The LuxCorp banners blink, municipal lamps click in sequence, and the air tastes of rain about to begin. Your brother's watch ticks soft and relentless. You breathe against the ache in your rib and the knowledge that the choices in the coming days won't be small.

[Visual: Shot of the promenade stretching toward the town, rooftop greenhouse rooflines visible in the midground]
Narration
You stop at the base of the street that climbs to the rooftop greenhouse. The town holds its sleep in pockets—the diner, a single light in a fisherman's window, the glint of a sign. Above, the greenhouse glass fogs like a promise. You trace a finger along the notebook's margin, where a name blurs under a water spot. Names are not just ink; they are people who will stand or fall on what you do.

The ache in your ribs is a steady, physical clock. You remember fingers that didn't come back from a hull, the taste of salt mixed with diesel and metal, the hollow of a house after loss. Those memories sharpen your planning like a blade. They make you vigilant. They make you hardened in ways that sometimes push people away.

You tuck the notebook closed, the list clasped between your palm and your heart. The day will ask decisions of you, and you will answer as you have always tried: with steadiness, with work, with the stubborn hope that procedure can prevent catastrophe.

[Music: Piano pulls back; wind rises softly]
Narration
You take one last look at the promenade—the lamp posts, the LuxCorp banners that promise certainty, the distant line of the sea—and step upward. The town's roofs rise beneath the greenhouse like a path of small defenses. Your feet find the stairs you climbed a hundred times; the town's smell shifts to the warmer, muddier scent of soil and compost as you go.

You do not know yet what the morning will demand beyond the lists and inspections. You only know the sense of forward momentum—the way the tide always turns, whether you are ready or not.

A breath, a tightness, a moment before the work begins.

[Visual: Close on your hand resting on the greenhouse door handle; condensation leaves a salt-streaked print]
Narration
You push the door open and step toward the warmth and the people waiting inside.
