## Visual Novel Title
Tidekeepers

---

## World View
A near-future subtropical archipelago clings to changing seas: a town of reclaimed marsh, shuttered hotels repurposed as labs, and a patchwork economy of fishers, artisans, engineers, and organizers. Technology is practical and local—solar textiles woven into tarps, low-cost sensors, tiny autonomous survey boats—and coexists uneasily with market pressures and municipal budgets. Politics is small-scale but intense: a mayor must balance safety, tax revenue, and the town’s heritage; engineers promise measurable defenses; community organizers demand stewardship of shared coastal commons. Religion takes loose form in ritualized tides and barnacle festivals; culture prizes oral memory, craft, and mutual aid. Class divides run along access to land and capital: longtime fishers and artisans risk displacement, while a few technical contractors profit from large contracts. Geography is fluid—muddy mangrove flats and low cliffs offset by narrow piers and a rooftop greenhouse that is the town’s intimate agora. Atmosphere alternates between lullaby calm after storms and an electrical bruise before one, always scented with salt, diesel, and fried fish. The central conflict is ethical and intimate: can you harden the shore with engineering and save livelihoods fast, or restore ecosystems and ask some places to retreat? Love and labor are interwoven with that question.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Aria Navarro) return home to defend the town I love. I believe in a locally led regenerative shoreline that marries science to lived knowledge; two people pull me in different directions. Mateo, the engineer with oil-stained pants and a copper compass, offers decisive, deployable structures to buy time. Sora, the organizer with cobalt streaks and hand-painted anoraks, demands communal mangrove restoration and cultural sovereignty. The narrative follows a Cinderella curve: I rise—winning a pilot and brokered support—then a sudden, brutal fall (a storm and ethical rupture) forces hard reckonings; finally, through compromise, sacrifice, and leadership, a renewed, hybrid approach gives the town a durable future. Branches let me choose alignment—Mateo’s fast infrastructure, Sora’s grassroots reclamation, a hybrid coalition, or a more solitary path of sacrifice—while still driving toward recovery. Each route tests my guilt-driven sense of responsibility, my capacity to love without abdicating duty, and the town’s ability to bind technical fixes to cultural stewardship. Romance develops differently across routes but is never separated from policy: the choice of partner shapes what “home” will cost and mean.

The morning after the retrofit, the harbor is quieter in a good way. The modular blocks no longer sit like blunt teeth; mangrove saplings thread through housings, roots finding purchase where ballast used to sit alone. Jun hands me a sensor readout with a grin that crinkles grease on their fingers; the sediment holds better than our worst-case models predicted. Mateo comes up the pier, his reflective jacket traded for an old denim shirt, the curl of hair damp. He looks at the hybrid line we built and, with the sort of soft decisiveness that once drew me, takes my hand.
