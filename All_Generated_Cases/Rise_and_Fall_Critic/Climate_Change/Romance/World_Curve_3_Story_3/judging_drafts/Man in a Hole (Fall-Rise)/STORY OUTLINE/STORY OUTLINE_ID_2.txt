## Visual Novel Title
Saltbridge Tides

---

## World View
Saltbridge is a near-future coastal town perched between two temporal textures: the slow, salt-burn of its old fishing neighborhood and the slick promise of climate-adaptation projects. Politically, power flows from a city hall that balances votes, developers, and short-term relief funding; Mayor Lillian Cho navigates that tension, favoring visible infrastructure while blunted by budget politics. Socially, neighborhoods remain tightly networked — cafés, Sunday markets, and fishing crews trade favors and memories — but class lines harden where new, gated adaptation projects meet battered boardwalks. Religion plays no central role; instead, civic ritual and memory (memorials for lives lost to surges) act as secular sacraments. Geography is intimate: a low-lying shoreline, boardwalks bowed by salt, dunes scarred with sandbags, and a reclaimed rooftop garden that feels like a sanctuary against the tide. Conflicts pivot on climate policy inertia and competing visions of justice: grassroots resilience versus corporate-style engineering. The sea is both literal antagonist and moral mirror, rising in scenes of fog and sheen, forcing citizens to choose between sentimental preservation, pragmatic containment, or radical relocation. Atmosphere: melancholic hope — rain-glossed lamplight, salt textures, fogged horizons, and watercolor skies that could either drown or baptize.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Serrano, a community resilience coordinator rooted in Saltbridge. The narrative follows a Man-in-a-Hole arc: a fall into crisis when a new storm threat, policy delays, and a fractured funding decision push Saltbridge toward displacement. At the nadir I must choose a path: ally with Elias’s grassroots urgency, negotiate pragmatic infrastructure with Noah’s firm, or lead a risky independent community action. Each path forces different sacrifices and reveals different facets of love and commitment: romance with Elias channels collective mobilization, romance with Noah complicates moral compromises within systems, and a solo-led route tests the limits of communal sacrifice. Across each branch I confront loss — damaged public space, personal guilt, or community fragmentation — and then climb toward recovery through coalition-building, science-led strategy (with Dr. Hana Park), or hard-won policy negotiation. Endings vary (Community Triumph, Compromise and Renewal, Corporate Win, Catalytic Sacrifice) but all follow the Fall→Rise emotional curve, emphasizing how personal intimacy and political choices interlock when the sea demands a response.

Police question activists; fines are levied. The public narrative shoves us into villainy and weakens our moral standing. I watch as families that relied on our credibility stop trusting us, and the sea, indifferent to our gestures, keeps its steady rise. I am arrested again, and this time the consequences ripple into long-term constraints on our ability to access grants. The fall leaves a bitter aftertaste: righteous anger without a durable plan can become a self-inflicted wound.
