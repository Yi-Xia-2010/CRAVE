[Scene: High Tide Laboratory | Dusk]
[Visual: Sodium lamps throw pools of bruised orange across wet steel. Instrument panels blink in pale blues; a projection map shivers over a table like a captured tide. Rain spatters on deck, making the lights puddle and run.]
[Sound: The soft clank of metal, low fan hums, an occasional distant horn. Somewhere below, water licks at a hull.]
[Music: Low, dirge-like cello with sparse electronic pulses]

Narration:
You come in on the smell first — salt and oil and paper that has been handled too many times. Your jacket beads with fine spray; the compass at your throat is cold against the damp. The choice you made in the dunes carries like a bruise under your ribs: not "fight the developers" and not "negotiate with steel," but a third way you have sketched and resketched in margins until the edges are raw.

Dr. Hana Park stands over the central table, sleeves rolled, a tablet in one hand and a ream of field printouts in the other. She pins a translucent layer of data onto the projection and steps back as if holding a rope taut between two spindles of light. The lines she spreads look like a city rewoven from tide-lines and thresholds.

Dr. Hana: "You don't look surprised," she says, but her voice is tempered — practical grief, the kind that keeps a scientist awake and no longer surprised by what the sea will do.

You: "I am surprised at how clear it feels. Or how terrifyingly practical." (You wrap your thumb around the dent in your compass.) "There are people who can't climb stilts, who can't afford to move. This—" (you gesture at the map) "—is the least I can imagine doing."

Dr. Hana: "Least and least-worst are different metrics." (She taps a node on the projection; a row of rooftops glow.) "Phased relocation. Rooftop Sanctuary relays. Living shorelines where we can build them. Buyouts where we must. Staged evacuations tied to tide models. It's all feasible on paper. Politically explosive in practice."

You lean in. The projection smells like ozone and decisions you haven't yet lived. You want every number to mean a neighbor kept safe; want every projection to feel like a promise, not a ledger of loss.

You: "Talk to me about the ethics. At what point does preserving a block as 'historic' become a death sentence? At what point does saving the town become abandoning it?"

Dr. Hana: (She breathes out, a slow, exhaled sadness.) "Managed retreat is a blunt instrument prettified in committees. Ethically, it asks who gets priority. Practically, it asks what we do when funding, time, and will don't align. We can design a staged evacuation to minimize risks — staggered relocations, free housing in Rooftop Sanctuaries, floating caches for supplies — but the sacrifice will sting. Someone loses a home. Someone loses a place they cannot rebuild. That's the world this plan asks us to accept."

You: "Then we make the sacrifice as fair as we can." (You press your palm flat to the projection, as if feeling the town's pulse through blue light.) "No secret sales, no backroom buyouts. Community oversight. Open ledgers."

Dr. Hana studies your face, the lab's light catching the gold flecks in your eyes. For a second her expression is unreadable and, in that unreadable line, you find both permission and caution.

Dr. Hana: "If you go public like that, you will be lionized and vilified in the same breath. People call that leadership; sometimes leadership means accepting the loneliness that comes with choices others cannot — or will not — make."

Narration:
You imagine the map again: rooftops strung like lifeboats, strings of solar fabric and composter bins; the converted mall a sanctuary garden buzzing with bees. Each relay point is a promise and a potential target. You feel the weight of the rope Hana holds up between science and ethics, and you know you will have to anchor it to people.

[Scene: Rooftop Sanctuary (Reclaimed Mall Garden) | Night — A few hours later]
[Visual: Dew on raised beds, strings of solar fabric draped like sails, soft lamplight over soil. The city's bones are visible below; steam rises from vents like distant chimneys.]
[Sound: Bees are quieting; the murmur of a small group arranging crates and map pins; the occasional scrape of a trowel]
[Music: Sparse piano, low and unresolved]

Narration:
Rosa meets you under the canopy of herbs, face warm and open despite the damp. Finn lingers on the edge, shoulders hunched against a wind that smells of salt and wet plastic. Elias comes last, wind-blown and wide-eyed, like every idea is a megaphone waiting to be raised.

Rosa: "If we do this, the café can be a logistics point. We have room for supplies and a back area for paperwork." (She taps a pin into the map.) "People will come to me first. I can hear them."

You: "I need you to be our face in the mornings. Keep a list for me — names, vulnerabilities, whether they can climb, any chronic meds."

Rosa: (She nods.) "And you'll tell folks why we're asking for buyouts? Make them understand it's not abandonment."

You: "I'll do my best." (Your voice cracks at the edge; you swallow.) "I need people who can trust me with the worst news."

Elias: (A breathy laugh that doesn't reach his eyes.) "You built a plan and then hid it in the lab like a promise too fragile for sunlight. I admire the courage, Maya. But you're not meant to carry this alone. Isolation eats plans the same way rust eats steel."

You: "So what's your answer?" (You look at him, trying to read whether he's offering to throw in with direct action, or to stand at your side in coordination.)

Elias: (He crosses his arms, impulsive, sincere.) "I can organize volunteers for the relays. We can teach people to run the floating caches and form evacuation drills. But I worry — if you're seen as the architect of 'managed retreat,' you become the lightning rod. I don't want the fight to consume you."

You: "Then don't let it."

Elias: (He smiles, complicated.) "Don't tell me what to do, Maya Serrano. Tell me what line needs crossing, and I'll stand there with you."

Narration:
Elias's warmth is a current you both want and fear. It promises momentum but also a spotlight. You feel the pull — to be buoyed by his energy or to be washed out by it if everything turns sour.

[Scene: Edge of a Construction Site | Night — Brief Visit]
[Visual: Floodlights swivel over skeletal frames. A Kestrel Marine crane stands idle, its chrome cab reflecting the lab's orange light. A temporary fence is plastered with developer signage.]
[Sound: The soft thud of a generator, clanks distant and muffled; murmured voices, a radio chatter you can't quite make out]
[Music: Low percussion, the sound of a heartbeat]

Narration:
You go to the edge because you must see what you're up against. No barricades yet — just machines grown like weeds. Noah Kestrel is there, sleeves buttoned, hands in pockets. He looks as if someone tightened him into a suit and forgot to take the measures out of his posture.

Noah Kestrel: (He gestures at the crane and the blueprints someone has tacked to a plywood wall.) "The tide models are conservative. We can stage the fill work so it doesn't precipitate immediate failure. Your plan is technically sound in parts, Maya, but you're leaving social dynamics as a variable."

You: "That's the variable that decides whose doors stay closed and whose are pried open by insurance."

Noah: (He studies you, voice quiet.) "If you formalize the buyout schedule and coordinate evacuation windows, you can reduce risk. Modular floating platforms with anchoring redundancy could buy us more time in some neighborhoods. I can sketch reinforcement designs that avoid mass relocation for certain blocks."

You: (A flicker of hope, sharp and sudden.) "You'd help with that?"

Noah: (He looks away for a beat, as if measuring what to give.) "I can offer a technical consult. Limited. Without corporate cover, my firm will withdraw. I have constraints."

Narration:
He reaches into his coat and slides across a small, precise schematic — not a corporate marketing piece, just a technical note on modular anchoring. You accept it like a relic.

Noah: (Softly) "I am not campaigning for you, Maya. I'm offering a calculation. Use it or don't. Just — be careful. Politics and engineering are married monsters; both will try to decide what sacrifices are acceptable."

You: "I know."

Noah: (He withdraws then, like a tide pulling away.) "I'll check on the models. Don't expect more public support."

Narration:
Noah's involvement is a fissure of gray light — practical, small, and quietly given. It helps. It does not insulate you from the coming storm.
