You give them your name because your name is the hinge between being a citizen and being a statistic. For a frantic moment you think you'll share a cell with him, that you'll grip a palm between bars and count windows together. [Scene: Holding Pen — Night]
[Visual: A corrugated metal cell with a single bulb; the city beyond is a smear of sodium orange and rain]

They let you keep the Moleskine. It is weighted in your lap, a small and stubborn thing. You open to a blank page and the handwriting looks like someone else's. Each line you write is a calculation — what we did well, what we did wrong, the moments hope dissolved into panic. You catalog failures with the kind of clinical cruelty reserved for after-action reports: no marshals on the perimeter, insufficient medics at the barricade, a plan that trusted courage more than geometry.

Narration: The neighbor's face rises unbidden — the one whose porchlight went out the night the storm took them. You promised to do everything. That promise now tastes like sand and old salt. You had said "we," but the weight of we has stretched into something you almost can't shoulder.

A man in the next stall coughs. He speaks to you across the thin metal.

Other Arrestee: "You lot made waves. Folks will remember that."

Maya Serrano: "Will they remember why we were on that pier, though? Or only the glass?"

Other Arrestee: "Depends who writes it. Depends who shows up when the money starts talking."

Conversation folds into the dark: practicalities, rumors, fragments of outrage. The holding cell is a one-act play of small violences and shared cigarettes pressed into the drizzle of the night.

A guard unlocks the outer gate briefly and someone hands you a plastic cup. A radio mutters numbers. The world outside continues on its slippery axis — people move, decisions are made — and you are a small island of incandescent thought.

You turn a page and sketch a map of the pier in the Moleskine, the lines jagged, the plank names turned into shorthand for losses: MED TENT, CAM SMASH, KETTLE ENTRY. Ink smudges where your fingers have been damp.

There is an ache to his plea that knocks the breath from you, a rawness that removes the consolations of rhetoric. He is defiant across the legal line, and that defiance carries a tenderness that startles you with its fragility.

You find yourself naming the failures in the notebook with a fierce, almost tender honesty. You write: logistics failed. Medic plan under-resourced. Communication lines clogged by adrenaline. The cup of plastic slips in your hand. The thought of Rosa's empty morning, the cost to the people you love, settles into you like cold stone.

You think of the inked bracelet — a cold circle that links you to a moment you'll never quite leave. It is humbling in a way that cuts; you had believed moral clarity might inoculate you against consequence. It did not. The clarity is sharp, purified by failure, but your capacity to act is diminished: volunteers bruised, the café shuttered, Finn in Mercy's emergency, some neighbors shaken enough to stay home next time.

Elias presses his forehead to the fence, close enough that you can feel the heat through the metal.

Elias Novak: (soft, hoarse) "When this is over, we rebuild. Rooftops, gardens. We patch what we can and hold the rest together — together."

Maya Serrano: (you repeat it, not because it fixes anything, but because it makes a future sound like a promise) "Rooftops."

He nods. There is a stubbornness in that nod that is both a plan and a benediction. But night in the holding cell is long and full of small reckonings; the bottom presses in and you are small, remembering a porchlight gone out and a neighbor that no one could pull from the waves.

You close the Moleskine and feel the dented compass cool at your throat. The red thread chafes.
