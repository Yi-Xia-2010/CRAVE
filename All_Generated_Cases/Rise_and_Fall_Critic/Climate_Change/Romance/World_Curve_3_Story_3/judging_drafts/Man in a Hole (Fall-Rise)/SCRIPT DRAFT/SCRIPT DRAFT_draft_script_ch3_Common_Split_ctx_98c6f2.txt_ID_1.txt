[Scene: The Dunes and Emergency Barricade | Late Afternoon — High Tide Approaching]
[Visual: Wide shot — wind-creased sandbags lined like teeth along a frayed dune; hand-painted signs ripple; a flat, trembling horizon]
[Music: Distant, low cello; under it, the steady thump of a heartbeat tempo]
[Sound: Gull cries, fabric snapping, the soft grind of sand under boots]

Narration: You come down the path from the boardwalk with your jacket collar up against the salt wind. The air tastes of metal and old rain; every inhale scratches at the back of your tongue. The sand grits between your teeth when a gust throws it across your face. Your hands smell faintly of coffee and ink.

Narration: The barricade looks smaller close up and impossibly large when you picture the sea pushing past it. Sandbags—some patched, some bursting at the seams—are arranged in a ragged line, hand-lettered slogans flapping like admonitions: NOT WITHOUT US; HOMES NOT MALLS. Someone's protest banner has a smudge where a crying child must've pressed fingers to paint.

Narration: You walk the line like someone checking stitches on a wound. Each bag is a promise; each promise is a question you cannot answer without cutting something else away.

[Visual: Close-up — your fingers find the red thread tied at your wrist; the dented compass swings slightly against your chest]
Narration: The thread rubs your skin raw under the windbreaker. It is an old knot that meant something fierce once. You twist it between forefinger and thumb the way you always do when you are trying not to cry. The compass is cold; its metal feels like every neighbor's face turned inward.

Narration (internal): You did not imagine this crossroads when you first knotted that thread. You imagined small fixes, polite petitions, a braced town that learned to bend without breaking. You did not imagine choices that carve neighborhoods into winners and losers. You did not imagine being the hinge.

[Sound: A distant footfall, the click of a camera strap]
[Visual: A figure approaches along the beach path; sandy hem and a loose flannel—Elias]

Elias: "Maya." (He stops a few paces away, hands unclenched, camera hanging at his side.) "I thought I'd find you here."

Narration: You register him like light. He smells like wet wool and the faint citrus of something he carries in his pockets for other people. His expression is a layered thing—open pleading folded over wary caution; it's complicated in a way that could be warmth or weapon.

You: "You shouldn't be out here alone either." (Your voice is flat. You know what you said aloud will be softer than what you mean.)

Elias: (a small, brittle laugh) "Neither should you. But that's the point, right? People need to know—what we built with our hands, they can take. If we make noise, maybe the Mayor has to choose between a photo op and us."

You: "Or we make noise and they point to permits and hand us forms while they bulldoze." (You let the words out like a warning and a plea both.) "We made a proposal. I told the hall I'd put together a community plan."

Elias: "I heard." (He shifts; his gaze flicks to the barricade, then back to you.) "And? Will the plan burn bright enough to spark people into action? I can rally them. We can do marches, occupy the staging areas, build tide gardens en masse—make it impossible for them to act like the town is silent."

You: (internal) His certainty is a kind of light you can both trust and be burned by. Part of you wants to step into it and be rearranged.

You: "Noise doesn't equal policy." (You say it because you've argued that with councils and with yourself.) "But it makes the room smaller. It makes politicians look human-sized for a second."

Elias: "And what about looking human-sized in the court of public opinion?" (He leans in, earnest.) "You know what shame can do here. People rally around people—around causes they feel in their chests. If we show up, show real hurt, the Mayor can't sit on both hands."

You: "Show up for whom?" (The question is softer than you expect, because for every banner there is a living street with elderly neighbors who will be scared by arrests, kids who will be dragged into nights they don't understand.)

Elias: "For the people who won't be allowed into the new sea-wall when it's done for profit." (His voice tightens.) "For the Rosa's and Finns who stitch the town together."

Elias: "I know you worry about escalation. That's valid. But strategy without pressure gets reduced to press releases."

Narration: He offers you his plan, but in his eyes it is not just a plan; it is a future he wants you in. How that looks is ambiguous—comfort or demand—and you feel the pull both ways.

Elias: (after a moment) "Whatever you pick, I want you with us." (There is no accusation here—only an offering that smells faintly of idealism and fear.)

You: "And if 'with us' means risking arrests and burned bridges?" (Your words cut to the core.) "If it means people losing little more than dignity and maybe more besides?"

Elias: "Then we accept that. Sometimes dignity is the only thing that keeps people from being erased." (He says it as if it should be obvious, and yet it is not a simple truth.)

Narration: The conversation pulls taut between you two, a rope with no neat knot. He is both hope and a blade.

[Sound: The distant whine of a vehicle; a door closing softly]
[Visual: Another figure arrives at the far end of the barricade—Noah Kestrel, navy coat, tablet clutched to his chest; his approach is measured]

Noah: "Maya." (He speaks with the economy of someone used to making lists in his head.) "I didn't expect to find you here. Thought you'd be at the Hall, or Rosa's."

Narration: Noah's presence is a different weight: clean, metallic, precise—like the tablet he holds. His face is unreadable in the wind, like a machine learning a new variable.

You: "You didn't expect..." (You stop yourself. Expectations are knives when layered with consequences.)

Noah: "I came to check the line." (He gestures to the barricade with a professional distance.) "I have a draft from Kestrel Marine. It's an accelerated mitigation contract—sea-walls, prioritized funding for critical blocks, emergency buyouts for the highest-risk properties."

You: "Buyouts." (The word tastes like ash. Eviction dressed in fiscal language.) "Which blocks? Who chooses the criteria?"

Noah: "The firm proposes socioeconomic weighting to minimize displacement, a maintenance clause that keeps long-term costs down, and an expedited permitting schedule. It isn't perfect." (He admits it, rare and small.) "But it gets hard infrastructure in place before the next predicted surge."

You: "At the cost of zoning changes and corporate management." (Your voice is flat. You can see the glossy renderings in your head—the town in glass, a shrink-wrapped old district.)

Noah: "Concessions are the mechanics of compromise." (He says it like a diagnostic.) "I don't like the idea of displacement any more than you do. But denying large-scale engineering when we can fund it now guarantees more people out on the dunes later."

You: (internal) He speaks in probabilities and contingencies. Comforting, if you want to hide behind numbers; cold, if you listen for what those numbers cost in human lives.

Noah: "If you would engage in talks—sit across the table, bring community voices into the model—we might be able to structure buyout terms that include relocation support, job transition money, and community-led maintenance oversight."

You: "Those are words until the legal language buries them." (You exhale a laugh that is too short.) "How do I sell that to people who have watched letters and promises wash away?"

Noah: (his expression softens, briefly unreadable) "You build the accountability into the contract. You insist on an independent oversight board. You get clauses that prevent rapid sell-off of parcels to private developers."

Noah: "I can negotiate pathways in the system. I know how the ledger bends."

Narration: His offer is a bridge—solid, engineered—but it cuts through neighborhoods to make room for passage. Bridges sometimes require demolition.

Narration: The wind threads your hair into your eyes. A gull plucks at a ribbon; the horizon seems to flex.

Narration (internal): Two people stand before you with two kinds of answers. One pulls at urgency, the righteousness of being seen and heard and refusing a fate by sheer visible refusal. The other promises the machinery of protection—quiet contracts, steel, and scheduled maintenance—but it asks for redrawn maps and hearts reallocated.

[Sound: A crackle—your phone vibrates; you glance down to see Finn's name, but the wind threatens to snatch the screen from your fingers]
[Visual: Your thumb hovers over the call; the dunes ripple; the barricade looks like a backbone]

You: (you let it go to voicemail; you are not ready for small voices over the cliff of decision.)

Narration: You imagine Finn walking these dunes with you, or staying inside with the windows taped, or out fishing in a boat with more holes than pride. You hear his laugh memory like a buoy bell—bright and too short.

Narration (internal): Finn's steadiness is the private ledger you keep. You think of the little victories you owe him: rent paid, a roof above his head, a town that still remembers how to fix nets and neighbor's doors. Every path you imagine has a debtor list with his name written on it.

[Visual: The sky thickens; the light goes a degree colder]
[Music: Cello deepens; a slow dissonant piano blooms]

Dr. Hana Park: (sudden, through a short-range call your phone accepts automatically; her voice is clear and undramatic) "Maya. I hope I didn't interrupt."

Narration: Hana's tone is a scalpel wrapped in linen—clinical, warm, and blunt where it must be.

Dr. Hana Park: "I've looked at all three options. From the data—" (she pauses) "—the community relocation plan is what I'd call 'strategically traumatic.' It works at scale and saves lives, but it's designed to break. It's meant to be a hard reset. I can't pretend the psychological cost isn't immense."

You: "You used my notebook like a sacrament in your meeting?" (You let a sharp edge into the question, because you feel unmoored.)

Dr. Hana Park: (a tiny, unreadable sound) "I used the ideas. I used the community mapping you circulated. I owe it to you to tell you that whatever you choose will cost someone something."

Dr. Hana Park: "I'm not telling you which harm to pick. I'm telling you the ledger. Choose with the understanding that a ledger with blank spaces is also a choice."

Narration: Her words are both absolution and indictment. You can hear the field instruments in the background where she stands—their low beeps like a distant tide charting rises.

You: (internal) Hana has the data to name outcomes; she does not have the moral authority to choose for you.

You: "Thanks." (The word feels insufficient and necessary; it's a contract signed in the dark.)

Narration: The three of them stand as three possible futures: Elias with his loud, bleeding heart; Noah with his polished clauses; and the cold, surgical plan Hana described that you could keep to yourself and make real alone. Each one crosses your spine in a different way.

Narration (internal): You feel the red thread at your wrist sing tighter, as if it is being pulled toward a seam. Part of you wants to follow the sound of chanting and barricades; part wants to sit across a table and rewrite the ledger; part wants to prepare the people you love for the hardest goodbye you'll ever make.

[Visual: Close-up — the compass hangs still; seagull cry thins to a single note]
[Music: The cello reaches a somber peak, then drops to a single unresolved chord]

Narration: The tide is coming in. You can see the water line creep on the edge of the horizon, patient and indifferent. Around you, the barricade breathes—fabric, rope, protest paint. The town holds its breath with you.

Narration (internal): This is not a question of who is right. It is the terrible work of choosing which rights you can feasibly protect, and which will be predictions folded into the future like maps. You do not want to be the one to tell neighbors they must leave. You do not want to watch the Mayor sign a glossy plan that erases histories. You do not want to see people hauled off by the police because you called for a march that turned ugly.

Narration: You are exhausted in the specific ways exhaustion can be moral: you are tired of thinking of solutions that cleave people in halves to save the rest. You are tired of being the hinge.

[Visual: The three figures form a ragged triangle around you—Elias, Noah, and the echo of Hana's words—each a different angle on shelter]
[Sound: Wind rising; a distant siren muffled by salt air]

Narration: There is no diagram that will make this simple. Whatever you choose will resonate outwards, shifting friendships, budgets, and maybe love.

[Music: Cut to a single, low sustained note—resignation and pressure]
[Visual: Tight on your face, the red thread glows like a wound]

Narration: You close your eyes and imagine three doors opening onto three different Saltbridges. Each one is incomplete, each one includes losses you can already see.

Narration: You open your eyes. The dune smells of salt and diesel and possibility.

Narration (internal): You feel the decision in your bones. The town waits, not because it knows you will save everything, but because someone must choose direction. The thread pulls. The tide waits only for no one.
