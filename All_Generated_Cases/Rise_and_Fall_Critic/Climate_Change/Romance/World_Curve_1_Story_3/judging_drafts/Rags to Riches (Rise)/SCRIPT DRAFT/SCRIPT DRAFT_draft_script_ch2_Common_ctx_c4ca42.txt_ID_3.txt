You step into Tideworks with the potted cutting tucked against your hip and your notebook warm where it hangs at your belt. The air smells of wet earth and algae, cut by the sharp tang of coffee. Tools clink in the alcove where Teo left them overnight; a ragged patchwork of diagrams and algae samples hangs like an improvised shrine to work that refuses to be quiet.

Dr. Jun is already bent over the main console, fingers moving with the calm of someone who can read the city the way others read weather. When you enter, the older man straightens slowly, eyes warm behind round glasses.

Dr. Jun: "You're on time, Asha. The morning tide flipped the east sensor again—nothing catastrophic, but it tells a story."
You: "Good. Stories are what we turn into plans." (You set the cutting on a bench and read the console. Numbers pulse in soft amber: salinity, pump duty cycles, microstrain across the living wall segments.)

The data scrolls like a tide line. Your stomach tightens at the spikes Jun points out — brief salinity shifts near the lower boardwalk, pump cycles twinned with last night's heavier swell. But beneath the concern there's progress: smoother baselines where community plantings buffer flow, and a cluster of microgrids that kept the demo section running through the midnight surge.

Dr. Jun: "See here. The living-section prototype held through the surge longer than predicted. Your amendments to the root lattice gave it more give."
You: "We let it breathe instead of bracing it into a wall. It moved with the water." (You trace the graph with your finger, imagining pattering roots and the way they could slow a surge just enough.)

Jun watches you, then smiles, small and private, like someone who has watched a pupil become a peer.

Dr. Jun: "You always had a gift for making the technical humane. Hana's presentation will be numbers, but you—"
You: (You hesitate.) "—I bring the stories that make numbers stick to people's ribs." (You feel steadier saying it aloud. It helps.)

The kettle whistles soft against the hum of the servers. Before Jun can answer, the wall panel flickers and a holo-prism blooms in the center of the room: cool, efficient, and unmistakably corporate. The projected Aegis crest refracts across a sample tray. Hana's figure stabilizes within it — precise, composed, eyes like steel glass, her voice carrying through the speakers with the calm clarity of someone who has rehearsed every word.

[Sound: Soft digital chime announcing a secure feed]

Hana (Holo): "Good morning, Dr. Park. Asha." (There's a pause, measured. Her eyes flick to the prototypes, then back to you. In the projection, she folds her hands.) "I wanted to walk you through the modular pump array. Each node can operate independently; they swap power and reroute in real time."

You feel the old rhythm of lab collaboration pull at you — the same cadence that used to carry late-night experiments and softer conversations. Hana's proposal is elegant; you can see the merits at once. At the same time, something in the shape of her mouth is unreadable: not cold, but something refined by other priorities.

You: "Show me the stress points." (You keep your voice even. You want to know every place the plan assumes more than you can give.)

Hana (Holo): "Node three underestimates biofouling in prolonged brackish surges. We need a maintenance schedule that includes automated flushing cycles. It adds cost, but it stabilizes duty cycles." (Her projection ticks through load charts with clinical precision.) "With targeted corporate funding to seed the microgrid, we can place these where they stop the worst failures."

Jun leans forward, fingers steepled.

Dr. Jun: "Automated flushing helps, but it depends on long-term oversight. Who maintains those nodes when the funding cycles end?"
Hana (Holo): (A slight, unreadable tightening) "We design for transferability. Local teams can be trained. The tech is modular by necessity."

Elias arrives at that moment — boots slapping on the raised walkway as he ducks in with a bag full of flyers and a grin that always seems larger than his actual smugness. His presence shifts the room's air; the hum of the pumps feels like it matches his energy.

[Visual: Elias steps into the lab, sunlight combing through his hair, his jacket scoring the room with patched color]
[Sound: The thud of posters against his bag, his laughter like a bright bell]

Elias: "Couldn't let you have all the fun without me." (He drops the bag, eyes going to the holo, then to you.) "Modular pumps from Aegis? Fancy. How'd they pitch it?"

You: (You measure your words. Elias has a history of distrust for anything bearing the corporate crest; his face skews skeptical.) "They're pitching redundancy and transferability. Jun's right — maintenance is the pivot."

Elias: "Pivot? That's corporate-speak for 'our money, your labor'." (His tone is sharp but not unforgiving.) "Asha, you know I'm in for community-run long-game. Tech only lasts if the people who live with it own it."

Hana (Holo): "Ownership doesn't preclude support. My team can design custom transfer protocols so training and knowledge are embedded."
Elias: (He folds his arms.) "Embedded how, exactly? In manuals that get archived? In service contracts? You know our memory is in our hands and our gardens."

Hana (Holo): (A small flicker — the first crack of impatience you can detect.) "We can build the training into the device's operation — guided modules, diagnostics that are easy to interpret. It lowers the barrier."

The conversation thickens, but instead of cleaving, it starts to braid. Each of you brings a strand: Hana's precision, Elias's insistence on autonomy, Jun's tempered curiosity, and your sense of where the neighborhood will actually live through this. No one is speaking past the other; everyone is pushing at the same problem from different angles.

You: "If we accept modular pumps as part of a hybrid strategy, we need guarantees — budget lines for maintenance, training sessions led by community members, and a staged transfer. Start with pilot nodes in places community crews already service." (You can hear the steadiness in your own voice; it feels like building.)

Hana (Holo): "A staged transfer is reasonable." (Her smile is small, the sort worn by someone who keeps many files open in her head.) "If Aegis funds the pilot and we document the transfer, we can scale transparency."

Elias: "And we hold the training sessions at the Skyward Market. People bring food, kids run around — it's how things stick." (He nudges a flyer toward the table, activism already becoming something tactile.) "We make it ours from the start."

Jun: "Combine the data with ethnographic notes. Numbers tell one story; daily practices tell another. If evidence and culture travel together, the system runs on both."

The lab fills with a hopeful, productive noise: the soft clack of sticky notes, the whir of a prototype pump, voices layering into a plan. The tension that could have been fracture instead becomes fuel. You can feel the rising cadence of something bigger than any of you: coalition forming around a shared, sensible compromise.

Hana (Holo): (Her expression shifts, more personal than the holo should allow.) "Asha, I know this isn't just engineering for you. I — we can make this work with your community values baked in. It'll take oversight, patience, and the right legal language."
You: (You pause. There is a history between you both — arguments in a cramped lab, long nights of testing, a friendship strained by choices. Now, negotiations thread through the old familiar.) "Patience is something we cultivate. Let's make sure the legal language doesn't talk over the gardeners."

Elias steps closer to you, lowering his voice in a way that lets the three of you keep the conversation intimate even under the holo's stare.

Elias: "Whatever we present to Mayor Ortega needs to be clear: community control, measurable safety improvements, and a budget that includes handover. We don't promise more than we can keep."
You: "We promise truths we can back with data and people. Jun's logs will help. Hana's hardware can help. And Elias..." (You let his name hang between you, the years folding into the present.) "—you and the community's willingness to show up will be what makes this real."

Jun nods, fetching a tablet and lining up the morning's best graphs to export into the briefing packet. He hands the device to you with a gentle, almost conspiratorial look.

Dr. Jun: "I'll annotate the data, add uncertainty bounds, and the ethnographic notes. If we frame this as a pilot for a community-led hybrid model, the mayor can either fund it or we can take the evidence public." (He shrugs, the scientist's way of acknowledging risk and hope at once.)

You feel the mixture of fear and possibility settle into something like resolve. Outside, the market's muffled laughter and hammering continue — a living soundtrack to the plan in gestation. You imagine the mayor's hearing as a table you need to set: evidence, allies, and a narrative that holds both heart and rigor.

You tuck the tablet under your arm, gather the prototype notes and the potted cutting — small reminders of what is at stake — and feel Elias's steady presence at your side. The lab lights warm; the holo dims as Hana's feed indicates she must sign off to catch a meeting at Aegis Tower.

Hana (Holo): "I'll forward the schematics and a proposal template. Let's reconvene after your community session." (A beat.) "And Asha—" (the holo hesitates, human at the edge) "—bring me the problems you don't want to compromise on. We'll set them as hard constraints."

You: "Good. We'll test them in the field first." (You mean it.)
