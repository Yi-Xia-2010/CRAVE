You taste salt before you can name it — not on your tongue but on the air, in the damp breath of the bay that has been calling you home for years. Wind tugs the hem of your recycled-sailcloth windbreaker; the single unruly curl at your forehead flaps against your brow. Low tide exposes the harbor's stitched seams: oyster shells like teeth, puddles that mirror the sky, and the slow, methodical work of people who refuse to be erased.

Your fingers find the brass compass at your throat without thinking. The metal is cool where your palm meets it. Someone once told you it was for finding directions. Someone else used it to remind you that directions change, but true bearings stay the same. You keep it because you keep promises, and because you have a terrible weakness for relics that make guilt feel navigable.

You dig the edge of your weatherproof notebook out from the inside of your jacket. The spine is taped, pages swollen with damp. Diagrams press through the cover: berm profiles, truncated seawall cross-sections, little sketches of garden swales you swore would stitch neighborhoods back together. Each page is a kind of apology you leave on paper, ink smeared by the same rain that once took half your block.

[Visual: Close-up of notebook — tide-line sketches blurred, notes in the margins]
[Sound: A child's laughter, a bell from a bicycle]

"Hey, stranger." Samir's voice cuts across the market noise like a familiar chord. He is sun-kissed, dreadlocks tied back, and he is standing behind a stall of salted fish and jars of bright, pickled things. He waves with a hand that smells faintly of seaweed and diesel.

Samir: "You finally answered your hometown's call. We were starting to think the university swallowed you whole."

You: "The university let me out for a field day." You keep your tone light; it's a habit, a way to anchor the conversation before the tide pulls it under.

Samir: "Good. I brought something." He scoops a glass jar with fingers skilled from jar-lids and hands. The lid leaves a ring of cold on your palm. "Pickled sea beans. Old Mrs. Leroux says they'll restore your salt balance or your stubbornness, whichever needs it more."

You take the jar. The vinegar smell lifts ghost memories — kitchen tables, hurried evacuations, the smell of iron and wet wood. Samir watches you like he can read the lines etched into the back of your jaw.

Samir: (softening) "You look... tired. But you look like you haven't given up."

You: "I didn't come back to give up." The words land sharper than you expect. "I came back to fix what I can."

Samir: "Fixing's a team job. You remember that, yeah? We still need you here, not just on paper."

You swallow. You want to tell him everything — about the equations that keep circling your sleep, about the guilt that tastes like rust — but you fold it into something smaller, practical-sounding.

You: "I'll start with a neighborhood survey. Soil tests, hydrology transects. We'll map weak points. We'll pilot low-cost berms and swales that can be built by volunteers. I'm—" Your voice tightens. "—I'm not promising miracles. I'm promising plans."

Samir studies you for a long beat, then nods like a man relieved to have his map again.

You move down the promenade, fingers trailing along frayed banners that flap like wounded flags. Their faded paint is the neighborhood's palimpsest — hand-painted slogans from the old storm relief, tacked posters for community meetings long overdue, a child's drawing of a house on stilts. The market's colors are warm, but the light doesn't lie; everything is washed thinner now.

[Sound: Murmured conversations, a dog barking in the distance, rain drums on a tarpaulin somewhere farther in]
[Visual: Wide shot of the market — residents gathered in small knots, faces lined and familiar]

A knot of neighbors parts as you approach. Their faces are anchors that pull at you: a woman whose house still lists on its foundation, a man who changes outboard motors for a living and has that same stubborn set to his mouth, a teenager with a bandaged arm whose laughter starts like a defense. They fall silent when they see you, and the silence is heavy with things left unsaid.

Woman (Maris Block resident): "You left us." The accusation is simple, stripped of ceremony. Her hands are folded over a woven bag as if to keep herself from pointing.

You: "I left to learn how to stop it from happening again." You fold your shoulders inward like a shield. "I came back because the tools we need exist — we just need to put them in the right hands."

Man (Boat mechanic): "Tools? The city says tools; the city says it will 'assess' — that's what knocked down my brother's front door last season. Assessments take time. Time is water."

You: "Then we make the time count." You let each sentence be exact, clipped into something that smells of planning and sweat. "If we map the vulnerable parcels, we can prioritize quick wins: reinforced berms where they'll buy us space, community gardens in higher patches, modular breakwaters as pilot projects."

Woman: "Words. You've been full of words."

You can feel their distrust like a current. It runs under every conciliatory phrase you offer. It is not aimed at you alone; it is aimed at every person who promised and categorized and delayed. You know the faces you see — you see your younger self in them — and that makes their skepticism hurt like an old bruise.

You: "I will put together a budget and a volunteer roster by Friday. I'll coordinate with Samir on outreach. I'll schedule a soil survey this week." You lay plans out as if laying stones across a river. You are careful to be measurable; hope without a plan has always been the kind that leaves people floating.

A child runs between your legs and skids on the slick cobbles, scattering a tray of oysters. A vendor mutters a curse and helps right the tray. Life keeps doing its clumsy, stubborn work around the edges of catastrophe.

You look out past the market, past the stringed lanterns and the low tide, to where the horizon is a bruise of cloud. The harbor light is both forgiving and unforgiving; it softens faces but reveals the ragged edges of everything it bathes. You feel the city's tilt under your feet — not just a physical slope, but the lopsided balance of money, policy, and memory.

Your notebook is heavy against your ribs. You think of berms that become gardens, seawalls that become social spaces, and of decisions that, once made, determine who stays and who leaves. The scale of choices ahead feels like a map of impossible lines: which blocks to defend, which to let retreat, how to route scarce funding and still call it justice.

You close your eyes and let the wind be a ledger. You are small against the horizon and stubborn as the seaweed clinging to the promenade stones. Guilt pushes at the corners of your optimism; it has hands and it wants to help — or to break you.

[Visual: The sky darkens slightly; a line of storm cloud fingers the lighthouse in the distance]
[Sound: A low wind, the distant rattle of construction]

You promise practical things in small, precise sentences because sentiment without scaffolding is a dangerous tide. You want to fix everything; you want to carry the guilt away in a new project that will be both prosthetic and apology. But on the promenade, surrounded by the people who watched the water come and the market that keeps coming back together, you realize the size of the decisions you will have to make is larger than the width of any notebook.

You inhale, the briny air filling your lungs, and for a moment you imagine the map unfurling: neighborhoods redlined with risk, pilot projects blooming green, and a city that can hold memory and motion at once. The image is fragile and dazzling, and then the wind strips it away, leaving only the harder thing — the work.

Everything here insists on a choice, but not the bright cinematic kind. These are the small, administrative kind, the ones that compound: which streets first, whose roofs, what language in the funding application that might warm or alienate a skeptical crowd. You feel the horizon pull at you, demanding more than good intentions.

You check the compass pendant again because you are looking for a bearing that feels like a promise, not just direction. The needle quivers and settles. It does not point you to certainty. It points you to the next step.

[Visual: Close-up of Mara's hand on the compass; the harbor stretches out, gray and patient]
[Music: Piano holds a single, unresolved chord]

You step away from the market, the jar of sea beans light in your pocket, the notebook damp and full of possible futures. The harbor does not forgive easily. Neither do people. But neither do you.
