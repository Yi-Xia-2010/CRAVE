[Scene: Maris Institute — Planning Atrium | Early Evening — storm increasing]
[Visual: The atrium's glass walls beaded with rain; large flood-model displays pulse cyan and magenta]

You follow the channel you chose and arrive with rain in the cuffs of your jacket. Monitors map the pilot site live — camera feeds tilted with wind, sensors popping erratic readings. An engineer from the operations team, sleeves rolled and voice clipped, briefs the group: a combination of predicted surge and sensor noise suggests the safest course is to switch to contingency—immediate shut-offs at certain tideways and temporary closures of the pilot's movable barriers.

Elias is already there, palms flat on a table scattered with printouts. He looks at you — not accusing, just practical. That look is always louder than any accusation; you know it well.

Elias: "We take the closures now. The models say—"
You: "If we close everything, boats and tideway vendors lose their livelihoods tonight. Their income runs on those passages."
Elias: (interrupting) "And if the pumps fail because we kept them open, everyone loses more than a night's work. The infrastructure fails and we can't get it back before the next tide."
You: "This isn't just infrastructure to them. It's food. It's rent money. There are people who sleep with packages of netting under their beds."
Elias: (softening) "I know. I grew up near a system like this. I am trying to balance risk."

The room tightens. The engineers offer probabilities and fail-safes in quantified sentences. You listen, counting the margins where lived experience will be erased by a neat line on a graph.

Professor Anika folds her hands in a small, deliberate gesture that smells faintly of tea and old paper.

Professor Anika: "A misstep now makes the hybrid vulnerable politically. If we order closures and people think we abandoned livelihoods, headlines will feed that line and opponents will use it to delegitimize the pilot."
You: "So what do you want me to do — choose which lives are expendable for the model?"
Professor Anika: (quiet) "I want you to choose the least bad path and make it visible. Transparency matters more than perfection."

The intercom crackles. Down at the pilot site, a prerecorded feed captures a figure moving through mud and rain — Nova. She wears the kind of emblem that reads like defiance even from a grainy camera. She's not shouting into a lit megaphone; instead, she's standing with a small group, rolling out a banner across the edge of a platform and handing out envelopes. The crowd that gathers looks to be a mix: relatives of tideway users, movement volunteers, curious locals.

You can see the protest through the grain. Nova's actions are deliberate and lean, not explosive. The cameras frame her as a provocateur. The editors at lunchtime will frame it as a fracture. Whatever your past with Nova — whether trust hardened or trust cracked — you cannot guess it now. Her face over the feed is complex: a public call shaped by private loss.

You and Elias trade a look that isn't quite forgiveness.

Elias: (low) "If we don't close the tideways, the barrier actuators may be damaged. That is a technical truth."
You: "If we close them, we break people's night income and feed a narrative that we're imposing engineering over people. That is political truth."

He pauses. You can tell he's weighing blame as if it's a physical commodity to be distributed.

Elias: "We won't get everything right. We have to pick the thing that makes the least long-term damage."
You: "Long-term damage for who? For those who can wait, or for those who can't?"

He opens his mouth and closes it. There's a ferocity behind his composure now. He isn't angry at you; he's angry at the situation and the calculus of loss it forces on people. You are both circling the same shore from opposite sides.

An anchor's voice already threads the frame with insinuation.

Samir arrives at the atrium soaked through and holding a folded envelope like something salvaged from a shifting tide. He hands it to you with a look that is older than his face.

Samir: "She had both hands on the rail, didn't want to come inside the shelter. Said 'my place will be here when I get back.'"
You: (taking the envelope) "Who?"
Samir: "Mrs. Ora. Wrote it this morning. Says she won't move for a night or a week — won't be a refugee two times. The handwriting is... small, but steady."
You: "Read it to me."

You unfold the paper. Mrs. Ora's letter is a careful, crooked script: a refusal to be evacuated unless her cat is guaranteed space; a list of the single things she owns that matter; a line about 'don't let them take my work from me while they build something that forgets us.' Your throat tightens around the shape of her words.

Samir: "She wants us to know. Wants us to fight in a way that doesn't leave her behind."
You: "How do we balance safety with dignity? That's what keeps looping."

Professor Anika crouches beside you, her breath fogging in the atrium chill.

Professor Anika: "If we make decisions that the public reads as choosing infrastructure over people, the hybrid dies in the press. If we refuse to act, we risk physical failure. There is no clean path."

Nova's voice comes over a recorded clip — not hostile, but hard as flint.

Nova (on feed): "We will not let fear become an excuse to erase people's livelihoods. If closures happen, they must be accompanied by direct compensation and real guarantees."
You: "That's what I keep saying."
Nova (on feed): "Then show it."

Her tone is a gauntlet thrown across a wet site. It's simple and terrible. Whether she means to fracture the coalition or to save it by demanding accountability, you cannot tell. The public sees a banner and a split; the coalition sees a crack. To some, your hybrid was already an uneasy marriage. Tonight, it looks like a divorce negotiation.

You try to broker words. Elias suggests legal indemnities and a formal compensation schedule, but the legal team warns such promises will take time to finalize and may not stand if funding vanishes.

You: "We can draft a temporary emergency compensation — small, immediate — and promise a fuller fund contingent on confirmation of support."
Elias: "Temporarily promising money we don't have opens us to ruin if the Mayor pulls funding."

The air tastes of salt and diesel and something metallic — the city's money.

You feel tired in a way that is not physical. Your optimism, the stubborn cord that has held you together, frays.

Elias looks exhausted in the half-light. He runs a hand through his hair and pins a sheet of models against a screen, as if forcing the city to look at what the cut will do will make it less true.

You: "Why would she cave now?"
Elias: "Because the optics tonight are bad. A protest, people refusing to close, headlines about outsourcing. She's risk-averse in the worst way — she kills the thing she thinks is divisive to save her seat."

You feel a dozen pairs of eyes — the coalition's, the vendors', Nova's complex stare on the feeds — all folded into one blame-shaped gaze. Elias looks at you, then away, unreadable for a moment.

The coalition meetings that once sounded like scaffolding now feel like falling scaffolds.

Samir kneels in the doorway, eyes raw but steady.

Samir: "They want names. They want someone to blame so they can move on."

You close your hand around the compass at your throat, its brass warm from your skin. Your stubborn optimism is not gone; it's cracked. You are tired of playing mediator between absolutes, tired of translating people's losses into budget lines. You fear—not just for the project's survival, but for the relationships you've been juggling like fragile instruments. You think of Mrs. Ora and her steady refusal; you think of Elias's quiet, careful anger; you think of Nova's fierce magnetism. Love, in this city, is a political decision as much as a private one.

The monitors blink, indifferent. The rain outside is steady now, a sheet that erases detail.

You can feel the next move folding into your palms like a letter you haven't yet opened.
