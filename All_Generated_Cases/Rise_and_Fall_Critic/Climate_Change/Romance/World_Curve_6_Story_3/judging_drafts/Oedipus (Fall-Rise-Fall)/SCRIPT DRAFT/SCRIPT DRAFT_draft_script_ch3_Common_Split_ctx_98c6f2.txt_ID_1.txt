[Scene: City Council Chamber | Midday]
[Visual: Wide shot of the chamber—tiered seating, the long mahogany dais, a projector screen humming slightly in the dim light]
[Music: Warm strings underscoring rising resolve]
[Sound: Distant murmur of the audience like surf; the soft click of a remote advancing slides]

You feel the wood under your palms as you step up to the podium. It is cooler than you expected—polished and formal and somehow less forgiving than the wet, honest wood of the community tables back home. The air smells faintly of lemon polish and recycled AC. Your notebook is tucked away; your voice will have to hold the images you've been carrying.

Across the dais, Cass Green's sea‑glass brooch glints when she moves. Her smile is practiced—public office has made it efficient—but when her fingers flex around the papers you can see the small, private calculation behind it. Arman sits composed in his navy suit; the waveform cufflinks catch a strip of light and scatter it like a promise. The projector washes his profile in cool blue.

In the audience Elena leans forward, paint on her knuckles; Tomas's hands are folded, skin like driftwood. Faces from East Strand ripple through the seating—neighbors, kelp‑garden volunteers, the folks who would be forced to unpack their lives if the wrong plan wins. Their presence steadies the line in your chest. Hope tastes like the salt under your tongue.

[Visual: Close-up on the slideshow. The first slide is a watercolor cross-section of the living seawall—mats of oyster, kelp lines, flexible berms—hand-sketched notes in your handwriting.]
[Sound: A soft, electronic pulse as Noah's sensor blinks on the scoreboard slide]

You press the remote. Noah's prototype blinks on the screen—three green lights, patient and bright. It is a small, defiant heartbeat. You think of the nights in the lab measuring microcurrents, the compromises to straps and adhesives, the way Noah said, half‑joking, "If it fails, at least we built something beautiful to fail with." Now it pulses like a promise.

You inhale, and the room tightens around that one breath. Your voice shakes only once.

Mara Evans: "East Strand is not a line on a map. It's Elena's storefront—her sign flaking where the rain hits it—and Tomas's knowledge of this bay that goes back longer than our zoning code. It's children's feet learning the edge of the world. The living seawall is designed to buy time, to hold soil, to let people keep their businesses and their history."

There is a hush. The technical slides follow—tide series, modeled attenuation curves from Priya's overlays, kelp biomass growth potential, a phased rollout plan. You speak to both heart and spreadsheet because the room will ask for both.

Councilmember Alvarez: "Your attenuation numbers assume community maintenance. What assurances do we have that the labor and stewardship won't collapse when funding dries up?"

You see the question like a tide pulling back. It would reveal the rocks if you let it.

Mara Evans: "We've proposed a co‑op model—small stipends for stewards, microgrants for local businesses to provide space for nurseries, and an open-source maintenance protocol trained in partnership with the Resilience Lab. It's not magic. It's work. But it's work the neighborhood is already doing, and the program scales with the space it protects."

Councilmember Alvarez: "Who underwrites that initial period? Two years between pilot and city‑scale policy is a long horizon."

You look to the audience. Tomas nods once—an old, slow motion of approval. Elena's jaw is set. You feel, sharply, the ledger that the municipal language keeps: timelines, deliverables, measurable outcomes.

Arman rises when it's his turn. He moves with the ease of someone handled to a polished script.

Arman Kade: "Protecting our economy protects homes. We don't have the luxury of slow experiments when storm models show acceleration. My proposal is a rapid hard barrier—fast deployment, measurable sea-line protection, investor-backed guarantees. Delay risks lives and commerce."

You watch his hands: precise, measured. He knows the metrics that make councilors sleep at night. He knows the power of a schedule and the sound of reassuring numbers. His rebuttal is not cruel; it is efficient, and efficiency comforts people who have mortgages and funds to lose.

Mara Evans: "Hard infrastructure can work, but at what cost? A sealed wall severs the shoreline from those who live on it—permanent exclusion in the name of protection. There are ways to hybridize—placekeeping measures, guaranteed public access clauses—but those must be designed now, not negotiated after displacement happens."

Arman Kade: "Hybridization is a nice word until it becomes an excuse for indecision. Investors won't pay for ambiguity. We need clarity on a schedule."

The mayor leans forward. This is the moment when policy becomes a human arithmetic problem.

Cassandra 'Cass' Green: "Ms. Evans, Mr. Kade—both of your proposals come from legitimate fear and legitimate care. My responsibility is to protect the city as a whole. If the council is to consider a community pilot, I need metrics: timeline, risk thresholds, guaranteed backstops if the pilot shows vulnerabilities. Can your living seawall demonstrate measurable protection within the emergency horizons we have?"

You feel the question like a tide-stone dropped into your chest. It calls for a number, a date, a certainty that the living system—beautiful, repairable, communal—cannot promise in the same language as a contractor's schedule.

Noah stands in the aisle, fingers tightening around the strap of his vest, then loosening. He steps forward and taps the remote. The sensor slide scrolls to a live readout—wave attenuation from the micro‑prototypes in the kelp beds, a line bending downward in the right way.

Noah Ríos: "We can produce measured attenuation within a pilot area's three-month test window. It won't be the whole East Strand, but it'll prove the principle. We can show trend lines city staff can validate. It's fast, and it's replicable."

Your heart catches. Noah's voice is calm—pragmatic and generous at once. In his eyes there is that tired, eager look: the man who will run the work if you ask.

Mara Evans: "A pilot that proves the efficacy of living systems in quantified terms—that changes the conversation. It also centers the people who will maintain it. It doesn't erase the community's right to the shoreline."

Council murmurs. The language of immediate success—the low-tide arithmetic of deliverables—hangs in the room like fog. Yet in the fog there are beacons: Noah's live data, Tomas's steady hands, Elena's sign worn from wind.

You answer a final series of pointed questions from the dais—funding mechanics, maintenance chains, legal guarantees—and with each answer you try to bridge the warm, messy truth of care to the council's need for hard assurances. You can see some council members softening; others press for strict timelines.

The room votes later that afternoon to table an immediate endorsement—an outcome that locks the calendar in place and forces a binary decision in the weeks to come. Not a defeat. Not a victory. A concentrated pressure where action will have to be chosen.

You step down from the podium and the chamber feels larger, not emptier—filled with possibility and obligations. Arman offers you a polite nod; it is not an olive branch, but it is not a challenge either. Cass places a hand on the papers and meets your eyes with the kind of look that says negotiations are already beginning.

Elena squeezes your hand when the crowd parts. Tomas taps your shoulder and, without words, gives you something like permission to keep going. Noah falls into step beside you; his shoulder is a quiet anchor.

Your mind sharpens into the fork you have suspected since you began drawing those first cross-sections: a choice that will define who gets to stay and who must leave, who gets to keep the shoreline under their feet and who must exchange it for the promise of safety elsewhere.

You feel the rise—the sense that there are paths forward that are not predetermined, that human design and technical validation can be braided together if you choose to thread them. The room's applause after the tangle of questions did not belong to you alone; it was a small, collective vote for keeping the conversation alive.

You stand at that fork. Each road is honest in its own way.
