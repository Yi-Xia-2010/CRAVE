Noah: (his thumb tracing a line on the schematic) "The terraced mats get reduced. Less soil mass, fewer planted pockets near the base. You: "Less living." The words taste of salt and apology. "So—people stay, at least for now."

Noah looks up, searching your face for the steadiness he thinks he sees but worries he can't always count on.

Noah: "We reduce immediate erosion rates. Cass gets the breathing room she needs at City Council. We can show numbers, show a pilot that works before the next storm season. That—"

You cut him off because your throat tightens. "That was the ask. The ask was to keep people here. Not to make a version of it that only looks like home."

Noah: (softly) "I know. I know. I'm trying to make both things—proof we can scale, and proof that the community isn't erased."

You hold onto the edge of the workbench until your knuckles ache. The lab hums around you; pumps in the back ripple like muted applause. You think of Elena's face when she read the building notice—worry that was edged with a quick, hard grin she uses when she wants to look brave. You think of Tomas's hands, moving with the patience of someone who has mended more than nets.

You: "So we sell some of the experiment for guarantees."

Noah: (looking at you like you might break) "We did what we had to, Mara."

An ugly little sound—half-laugh, half-anger—escapes you. It isn't judgment; it is the friction of grief rubbing against necessity.

[Scene: Community Space | Midday — Pilot Presentation]

You: "This first phase is designed to stop immediate erosion, to protect ground-level access along the main promenade and to demonstrate structural durability for the council. Tomas: (looking at his hands) "Enough keeps people breathing. It's not whole. But enough is something to pass on."

[Scene: East Strand Pier | Night — After the Vote]
[Visual: Empty pier. A single light on a buoy keeps a pool of yellow steady. The water is black and constant, reflecting the lamplight like an attentive eye.]
[Sound: The steady breathing of the bay; distant traffic, the creak of timbers]
[Music: Distant piano, slow and small]

You walk out with the hydrophone slung in its canvas bag. The pilot has the money. Cass can breathe. Elena still paints her signs. Tomas is counting on you to keep teaching. The list of small, human victories is long enough to make your chest ache and short enough to hurt.

You slip the hydrophone out and set it to the surface. The recorder lights a faint red. You listen—not for numbers, this time, but for the sound the bay makes when it knows decisions have been made in its name.

The sound is low and patient: a raw chord under everything, like a held breath turned into water. You press your forehead to the cold metal of the buoy and close your eyes. The sound wraps around old losses and new promises.

A message pings on your phone—a short line from Noah.

Noah (text): I'm boarding a flight tomorrow. Donor meeting in the morning. They want the sensor kit demo with the pilot in a month. Could mean other cities too. Can we talk?

You rub the pendant at your throat and type with fingers that feel like someone else's.

You (text): Meet me at the pier. One hour.

He is already there when you look up. His breath fogs in the night; his jacket smells like the inside of the lab and a faint, clean trace of aviation fuel. He does not try to fix the bay's sound. He stands beside you and listens.

Noah: "They like the numbers. They like the sensor story. They want to scale. They can fund the next phase if the pilot proves the sensors decouple the insurance risk."

Noah: "Yes. I can go. I can push for retrofit funding if the tech makes the case. But I'd be lying if I said I wasn't… terrified, not about failing the tech, but about us. About what it means to split the work."

Noah: (a small, hard laugh that is almost a sob) "You have East Strand to anchor. I… would not be the person I am if I didn't try to make the tech undeniable. If I can sell this in three other cities, donors will have to fund retrofit pockets. It makes the living design more possible politically."

You: "And if they don't?"

Noah: "Then this pilot—this compromise—was only a delay. It'll buy breath, but not the future you want."

You both look at the bounded pool of light on the water. The city feels slightly larger and smaller all at once.

Noah: "We could be in different places for a while. I—" He hesitates, and you hear the human calculus behind his words. "I don't know how to make it not feel like abandoning."

You: "Then we'll keep making promises that are true as far as they go."

You both stand there with the bay's patient chord humming beneath your feet. The pilot will begin. The grants will arrive. Cass will have time, and Elena will keep painting signs, and Tomas will teach the kids how to read the currents. And yet the terraces you loved are smaller on paper than in your memory; the living things you wanted woven into concrete are postponed to a later time that, while hope-filled, is not certain.
