[Scene: Harrow Hall | Evening]
[Visual: Exterior shot — Harrow Hall's stone steps slick with rain; people in yellow slickers and wool coats form a line under the porch light.]
[Sound: Rain tapping, low murmur of congregated voices, a distant gull]
[Music: Warm, anticipatory chord — an upward swell, promising but measured]

You step into the foyer and the smell hits you first: a salt-and-wood mix that is almost the town's perfume, warmed by the breath of bodies packed for the meeting. The hall beyond opens into a rectangle of oak benches and high windows; lamplight pools along the council table, catching dust motes like tiny, patient stars. Conversation threads and knots through the room — a neighbor asking for details, a child shushing, the shuffle of paper.

You feel the braided cord at your wrist once, a small, steady anchor. Your compass is warm in your jacket pocket. You move to the side table where Leyna waits with her tablet, graphs arranged like soldiers ready for the field. Ivo is nearby, sleeves rolled, a coffee cup clenched between two callused fingers. Maya sits a few rows in, hands folded, watching.

[Visual: Close-up on your notebook tucked into your jacket; the single line you wrote before — "Seed — Measure — Teach — Protect." — is a quiet backbeat in your head]
[Sound: The council bell rings twice; the room settles]

Leyna: "We have fifteen minutes before the mayor opens. The slides are tight — seagrass coverage projections, expected attenuation of wave energy, the pilot timeline. I can cut the modeling by one slide if you want a simpler lead."

You: "Keep the model. If they ask for numbers, we give them numbers. But let's make room for the people in the room — fact with a face, not just charts."

Leyna: (softly, tapping the tablet) "I can make the last slide a clear comparison. It will show trade-offs visually; it's honest."

Ivo: (grinning half-true) "And then I'll make the masks and sandbags look heroic. 'Cause sometimes you need a shovel and a smile to remind people we can do this now."

You laugh, too quick, the sound swallowed by a cough from the back. Hope is steady; it is not naive. The energy in the hall does not feel like fear so much as readiness — a community gathered with patience and pressure both.

[Visual: The mayor's coat-tail appears at the dais; Celine Harrow, composed in a navy coat, glides into the seat of power. Her presence shifts the room like a hand on a steering wheel — everything tilts toward consequence.]
[Music: A gentle uplift — strings underline a hopeful possibility]
[Sound: Clearance of throat, a soft rustle of papers]

Celine Harrow: (measured, persuasive) "Good evening. We stand at a crossroad — funding is available if we can commit to an expedited plan. The municipality can secure capital for structural defenses and emergency shelters within months, with oversight from a centralized task force."

Her voice is smooth as oil, and the room responds in polite, loaded silences. There is a sheen to her certainty that gleams like a promise. You watch people's faces: relief, skepticism, hunger. Celine's solution smells of rapid security — a bridge between the immediate and the institutional.

You: (quietly) "What are the strings, exactly?"

Celine Harrow: (affable, the hand of a diplomat) "Standard procurement and long-term maintenance clauses. We'll work with vetted contractors. There will be benchmarks and audits. Saltwick will be protected quickly."

Ivo: (bristling but controlled) "Vetted contractors. Meaning outside crews, outside priorities. How do you ensure local labor? How do you keep community control when the check clears?"

Celine Harrow: (a soft, precise smile) "Mr. Calder, we will prioritize local hires where possible. We cannot promise the pace without professional teams. We can, however, set quotas and oversight."

Leyna: (leaning forward) "If we're talking about hard structures, we must acknowledge ecological cost. Seawalls can increase erosion downstream and degrade habitat. The models show trade-offs — immediate reduction of inundation in certain sectors can translate to greater vulnerability later."

The murmurs tilt between the words: some nod at Leyna, others glance at Celine, counting every syllable.

You feel your chest tighten with the weight of the town's future. There is the melody of possible salvation: fast funding, quick shelters, brave crews at the beach. There is the counterpoint: the slow unspooling work of restoring root systems, seagrass beds that absorb wave energy and cradle juvenile fish, the patient building of resilience from the bottom up.

Maya: (softly, a public voice that carries more than its volume) "My shop took three months to rebuild after the last wind. If there is shelter money and we don't get it, other people will lose everything before the seagrass can do its thing. I don't have patience for 'later' when my rent is due."

You: (to Maya, low) "I hear you. I'm thinking about how we protect both the present and the future."

Maya: (eyes steady) "Then let's make the future pay attention to the present."

[Visual: The projector flickers to life; Leyna's graphs bloom across the screen — teal gradients, projected timelines, confidence intervals bracketed like cautious hands]
[Sound: The hum of the projector, an intake of breath across the room]

Leyna: "If you look here — and I will keep this concise — the pilot restoration in the Glass Marsh yields a 25–30% reduction in wave energy in five years with sustained planting and maintained living breaks. The variance tightens as monitoring improves. But it requires sustained community labor and initial investment smaller than a full municipal seawall, distributed differently."

Celine Harrow: (interjecting smoothly) "That's compelling. But five years of exposure is not a guarantee when a storm can take everything in a season. We can shield people now and plan restoration alongside. My offer is not mutually exclusive — it's a way to secure the town's immediate survival."

Ivo: "Plans sound good on paper, Mayor. But 'alongside' often becomes 'beneath' once contractors have an invoice to meet. I've seen 'alongside' become a footnote."

Celine Harrow: (tilting her head) "Mr. Calder, you distrust institutions. I trust you distrust what you have seen because you protect people. I am asking you to trust me to deliver, not to betray your people."

You watch the exchange like a tide — ebb and push and the foamy line between. Celine's words are smooth, meant to cleave doubts. Ivo's retort lands with the weight of lived experience; he argues not from theory but from nights hauling.

Your internal monologue settles into an old habit: name the risk, name the promise, then make it small enough to hold. You think of the greenhouse's humidity and seed trays, of Ronan's grin when a net held its first bed of seedlings, of Oba's slow, patient hands showing where eelgrass used to cling.

A woman from the back stands and asks about maintenance budgets; a fisherman asks if the plan will touch his moorings. Questions come not as academic probes but as pruning shears, trying to shape a plan to fit real lives.

You: (rising to speak, letting your voice carry) "This isn't just about walls or plants. It's about who we are as a town — the way we choose to protect each other and the natural systems that keep us fed and rooted. If we choose only speed, we risk handing our shorelines to a strategy that might protect houses but hollow out the ecology that provides for future seasons. If we choose only patience, we risk people losing homes between the planting seasons."

Celine Harrow: (nods, attentive) "Then perhaps the compromise is a hybrid: municipal funds for prioritized shelters and localized living infrastructure projects — funded, but co-managed. With benchmarks to ensure local labor and ecological safeguards."

Leyna: "Those safeguards need teeth. Monitoring, adaptive management, independent audits. Transparency."

Ivo: (quiet, the tremor of emotion underlining his words) "And real boots on the sand now. Not just promises. People need visible protection. They need to know someone's doing the lifting tonight."

Your eyes find Ivo's. He is close enough for you to see a speck of salt in his eyebrow, a bright thread of the sea in his smile. His expression is complex — the kind of look that belongs to someone who has been both a rescuer and a dresser of wounds. You cannot say what that complexity is born of; it reads like loyalty and impatience, like the smell of a wet dock after a storm. You leave the shape of it un-interpreted; it fits more than one history.

The council room leans forward. The mayor gestures as if about to close the floor for a decision. There is a strange, intimate hush — the kind that comes before a crest. You feel all of Saltwick's small, threaded needs in the heat of that silence: roofs, seedlings, futures, paychecks, children with salt in their hair.

Your chest stills. Decisions in the room will not be yours alone, but your name, your science, your presence have become a hinge. The town has looked to you to translate what the sea is doing into choices people can live with. You remember the greenhouse light, the map of pilot sites, Ronan's eager hands, Oba's measured nod. The promise you wrote in ink — "Seed — Measure — Teach — Protect" — is not only a plan. It is a method of asking the town to believe in slow repair alongside immediate safety.
