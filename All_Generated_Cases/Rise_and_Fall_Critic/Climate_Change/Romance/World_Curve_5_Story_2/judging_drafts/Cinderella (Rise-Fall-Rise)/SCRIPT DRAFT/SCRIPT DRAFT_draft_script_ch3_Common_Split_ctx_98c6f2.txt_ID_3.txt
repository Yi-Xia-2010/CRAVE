You stand behind the plywood model, fingers braced against the edge of the table. The room smells of ozone and wet coats, with a faint undernote of coffee and the salt from the boardwalk still clinging to your jacket. Your notebook is in your inside pocket; Jun's printouts are on the table beside the model, graphs folded into neat columns.

You think of the market earlier—hands pressing seed packets into other hands—and of the scar along your palm where you've edited this speech a hundred times. The opening line you underlined that morning presses in like a promise: "We braided the shore; let's braid it better." It is small and intimate for a room that expects big gestures. That is the point.

You lift your head. The room focuses with you.

Aria Solen: "Good morning. Thank you for coming. Today I want to show a path that protects our people and restores the living edge that has kept us fed and safe for generations—phased, community-led living shorelines that work with tides, not against them."

You set the first scale model plank into place. Light catches on the varnished wood; a ripple of attention follows your hands.

Jun Park: "If I can—I'll run through the projections before we move to community impacts. These are the median outcomes based on thirty years of tidal data and localized sediment models." (He taps the laptop; a chart blooms on the projection.) "With a phased approach, initial reductions in surge height are modest—about ten percent in the first five years—but compound as reed beds and reefs establish. Long-term, we reduce extreme surge exposure by up to thirty percent while increasing coastal biomass and jobs in co-op-managed aquaculture."

Investor 1 (hesitant): "Thirty percent is impressive, but the timeline… investors and families need assurances sooner. What about the immediate risk of a major storm in year two or three?"

You feel the question like a splinter. Immediate security is the argument Caden rides in on; you've heard it all before. You do not let the splinter become a splinter in speech.

Aria Solen: "We phase the work to prioritize the most vulnerable nodes first: school, ferry access, and low berm neighborhoods. That means targeted action in year one where it matters most, coupled with short-term, low-impact measures to buffer risk while natural systems recover."

Caden Holt (calm, measured): "Phasing sounds sensible until the sea takes what it wants. My people have modeled reinforced barriers and elevated architecture—seawalls where needed, housing towers on stilts where communities are concentrated. You trade time for risk. I can secure capital now; delays cost lives."

There is a soft shift in the room: investors lean forward. Some co-op members exchange anxious looks. Lina's jaw tightens; her folder rustles. You can hear Marta's breath, a held thing.

Marta Quin (plain, loud enough to carry): "Trade time for whose lives, Caden? You've got money on speed. We got people on the ground. Which will stay when the winds come back?"

Caden's smile does not reach his eyes. He is practiced at reducing voices like a pressure valve.

Caden Holt: "I am not insensitive to local attachment. I'm offering scale. We can safeguard the entire shoreline in a way that guarantees insurability and investment returns. That matters for public services."

Jun Park (interjecting, urgent): "But the model for hard infrastructure shows downstream erosion increases in adjacent neighborhoods unless protein—" (He corrects himself mid-sentence.) "—unless buffer ecosystems are integrated. Seawalls without living systems create reflexive losses. We have the data."

There is a clustered moment of quiet as Jun's slide scrolls: a side-by-side simulation. On the left, a coastline with seawalls: waves compress, sediment scours away, adjacent flats wash out. On the right, your phased living-shoreline: sediment accretes, marsh expands, livelihoods persist. The piano motif lifts as the images linger.

Old Man Rafi (voice like sand and tide): "When I was a boy, we built with the tide—watching, learning. The sea remembers those who listen. You cannot clap a wall at it and expect it to learn. It will take from us what's soft and leave us concrete."

A murmur of assent ripples through the rows of older residents. The investors do not move. You hold Rafi's gaze for a breath; his expression is complex—pride and warning braided together.

Aria Solen: "This isn't an either/or of heart and ledger. Jun's figures show that if we invest in the right places now, we reduce public spending on emergency response and displacement later. We can structure the funding to cover immediate stabilization—temporary berms, elevated walkways—while we scale habitat restoration that pays back in biomass, fisheries, and flood reduction."

Caden: "And who will guarantee those paybacks? When the first budget cycle comes up, how do you protect the money from political shifts?"

Lina Voss: "That's exactly the governance question. If we design legally binding milestones for disbursement—if we tie performance to tranche releases—then investors and the public get teeth."

Murmurs nod again. The piano motif rises in confidence.

Lina Voss (soft, but decisive): "We've heard plans, models, and memories.
