[Scene: Greenwave Community Garden | Morning]
[Visual: Diffused morning light through glass rain shelters; terraced planters glisten with droplets.]
[Sound: Soft drip of water from gutters, distant gulls, a low generator hum at the edge of the terraces.]
[Music: Sparse piano with a low cello underpinning — steady, somber.]

You arrive on the terrace with salt in your hair and the compass at your throat heavier than usual. The jacket at your shoulders smells of salt and wet rope; dirt has worked itself into the cuff where you rolled the sleeve up to tie seedlings into floating planters. The field notebook at your hip is warm from yesterday's page-turns.

The air is saturated with damp soil and an iron tang that reminds you of old nails and the boathouse roof you couldn't keep. You feel the memory like a pressure behind your ribs — not a sharp pain, more a pulled muscle that tightens when you move. There is work to do before you can nurse anything else.

Ivy's laugh cuts across the terraces like a bright hand. She is a splash of neon vest and braids, kneeling with gloved hands sunk into a tray of baby eelgrass. When she looks up, her face is open and immediate, the kind of look that tells you to forget your worries or at least share them.

Ivy Morales: "You're late complaining, Sato. Or just late. Which is it?"

You: "Late. Complaining will come after the second cup of bad coffee."

Ivy: (grins) "Good. Because I've got a whole crate of bad coffee and a stack of ambivalent planters for you. The kelp keeps wriggling like it's already plotting its escape route."

You reach for a thin blue tether and loop it around a seedling's crown. The cord is rough between your fingers; your hands remember the shape of small things — nails, nets, the hinge of the boathouse door. You tie a neat half-hitch and the planter settles in the cradle of your knees. It should be a small, precise motion. But your thumb trembles when you turn a page of pressed leaves in your notebook; the tremor is private and refuses to be domesticated.

[Visual: Close-up of your hands tying a tether; the brass compass pendant just visible against your throat.]
[Sound: A tile of gutterwater hits the plastic of a buoy; somewhere a metal tool clinks.]

The gravel path scrapes and a light raincoat unfurls like a small sail: Ariana arrives, hair catching a stray shaft of the sun through the shelter. Her ice-blue eyes find the map-spread on your knees and light up as if you've handed her a secret.

Ariana Voss: (breathless, excited) "You sketched the scaffold lines. Oh—this modular kelp scaffold could take the nursery load and become an active barrier if the buoys are lined in staggered arrays. Have you considered a segmented drift-break to encourage bed formation?"

You: "I considered your enthusiasm contagious, yes."

Ariana: (laughs) "That's a hazard of being around me."

She crouches opposite you, fingers moving over the stained paper with a confidence you both admire and occasionally bristle at. The maps in your notebook smell faintly of ink and pressed saltweed. You riff through scale ratios and community labor estimates, trading shorthand and margin notes until the blue of the plan looks almost like another seedling, fragile and full of potential.

Ariana: "If we make each module modular — pardon the redundancy — the town can assemble and repair with local teams. Greenwave could train crews, create employment, and demonstrate transferability. We could prototype a short arc before the next high tide."

You: (quietly, almost to yourself) "We have to make sure it's not just a pretty prototype for cameras. It has to be durable for the people who'll sleep within earshot of the breakers."

Ariana's face clouds, immediately attentive.

Ariana: "Community-run, community-led. That's the plan. We'll embed training, and—"

You: "And we need Tomas and the older fishers to sign off. The trust isn't there if the boathouse doesn't get listed as a protected site in the pilot. If we call it a model without their buy-in, it's theater."

Ariana: (a beat; softer) "You always think about the people, Marin. I like that about you. But you worry like it's a tide you can't read."

You want to tell her the truth — that sometimes the worry is less about reading tides and more about being the tide-breaker who couldn't keep a roof over a family's head. That the memory of the boathouse roof, how it peeled back in a hurry of wind like an old scar reopening, sits in the hollow of your chest and makes your hands clench around rope. Instead, you tuck it under a practical sentence.

You: "We sequence the pilot with town workshops. We document everything. We schedule labor shifts around people's incomes. We take Lina with us when we do community outreach."

Ariana: (tilting her head) "Lina will write a good piece. The visuals alone—"

Your phone vibrates against your thigh. The screen is brief and direct: Lina — new message. You see the preview before you open it: "Tideguard press release online. Cassian quote. You need to see." The line is jagged, a small alarm you didn't ask for.

Ariana leans forward, earnest. The momentum of her plans is almost physical — a pressure that both steadies and threatens to bury the ache. She's impatient in the way that makes things happen; you admire it and resent the way it can make you feel small.

Ivy: (teasing, aware) "If anyone can make a crowd of volunteers look like a streamlined workforce, it's you two. But don't let me be the only one who keeps you from collapsing at tea."

A low chuckle moves through the garden but your chest stays tight. The boathouse image reasserts itself: lamp-gold inside, jars of spare nails, a roof that learned how to leave without asking. Tomas' voice from yesterday — 'Make sure it's not just the people with loud voices...' — hangs in the air like a brine-scented mist.

[Sound: A far-off rumble, not yet rain — more the felt bass of a storm somewhere offshore.]
[Music: Cello draws a lower line; the piano grows sparse.]

Lina's text arrives again, this time a headline clipped into your notifications. You thumb the message open and read it out loud because the sound of someone else saying the words makes them less like a threat and more like a fact you can measure.

You: (reading) "Tideguard issues statement—'Comprehensive Harbor Stabilization Plan will commence following community review,' Cassian Rhee quoted about 'urgent decisive action.'"

Ariana's fingers snap as she brings her tablet up, swiping to pull the press release. The corporate language is immaculate and cold; it frames action as inevitability and paints trade-offs as efficiencies.

Ariana: (harder than before) "They're already framing it like it's a done deal. If the narrative fixes the outcome, we lose leverage. We need to move faster on the pilot or on a communications counterpoint."

You: "Faster won't fix the social fragility. We accelerate without trust, and the pilot becomes a banner for what Tideguard calls 'community collaboration.' The verbs change the meaning."

Ariana: (sighs, then softer) "I know. I know. But sitting still while they script the story isn't sitting still either. We can be proactive about transparency."

Ivy: "Lina's probably already on it. She'll want interviews. Maybe we arrange a hands-on demo and invite Tomas and a couple of fishers."

You imagine the cameras, the words that Lina might choose: 'grassroots partnership' or 'local partners endorse Tideguard funding.' The safety of your town becomes a headline possibility. The more polished Cassian's firm looks, the more holes appear in the edges of your plans.

Elder Tomas' shadow crosses the path, slow and sure. He rests his carved walking stick against a planter and surveys the young green with a weathered scrutiny that knows how to read both sea scars and people's intentions.

Elder Tomas Hale: "Good morning. The kelp looks healthy. Remember, when you show something new, show how it keeps our people where they are. Don't let talk about 'efficiency' mean moving them out."

You: "We won't, Tomas. We're building with them, not for them."

Tomas: (a small smile, complicated) "That's what keeps a town, child. Not the strongest seawall but the weakest hand you help hold."

The low rumble grows more insistent. The glass of the rain shelter murmurs as a breeze finds it. On the terrace, every step you take sounds a little heavier. The plan in your notebook seems both luminous and fragile — a line of ink that might be redrawn by outside hands.

You close the notebook and run a thumb over the edge of a pressed leaf. It is brittle and smells faintly of the salt from the page. For half a second you imagine the boathouse's lamp, the jars of nails, the map pinned with the place names of people who would be affected by whatever comes next. The image steadies you and then pierces again.

Ariana folds the tablet shut with a soft snap. She rises, boots leaving wet prints on the planks.

Ariana: (quietly) "Whatever happens, we'll be ready with something honest. We can show how a living seawall is different from steel. We can show it takes work and people. Will you stand with that message in the Hall?"

You: (longing and caution braided together) "Yes. But also—let me make sure the people who taught me to read these tides are not written out of the story. If the Hall becomes a place where decisions are already made, then our work will be footnote."

Ariana: (reaching out, briefly) "Then we'll make sure your footnote reads like a chapter. Together."

Her optimism is a small bright thing in your palm. You want to lean into it; you want to tell her that leaning is all you have left. But the world feels heavy, as if each forward movement requires force against some unseen current.

[Visual: The sky beyond the glass edges darkens; a strip of storm-blue gathers on the horizon.]
[Sound: Distant thunder sharpening; the rain shelter ticks as a first drop falls.]

You tuck the notebook into the inside pocket of your jacket, the pressed leaves rustling like fragile promises. Ivy shoulders a crate toward the loading ramp; Tomas settles his stick under one arm and starts down the path back toward the boardwalk. Ariana checks the tablet once more and slides a slim camera into its holster.

For a moment the garden grows quieter, the usual chorus of tools and chatter replaced by the small, electric hum of an approaching choice you cannot yet see. You feel the tide of attention shifting — toward the Hall, toward a meeting where words will be weighed and power will test your capacity to hold people at the center.

You take a breath that is half salt, half resolve. The compass pendant rests against your sternum, scuffed and honest.

You stand, shoulder the crate, and walk toward the garden gate. Behind you, the smell of damp earth and copper clings to your clothes. Ahead, the town waits: the boardwalk's worn planks, the Town Hall's windows like a quiet mouth, the rumor of press frames forming in the fickle light.

A single, insistent roll of thunder answers the step you take.

[Visual: Tight shot on your face as you look toward the town, then cut to the path leading away from the garden.]
[Music: The cello holds a low, unresolved note; piano hovers like a question.]

You close your eyes for a half-beat and imagine the meeting at the Hall — voices, arguments, the smell of old varnish, the way a room full of people can be larger and lonelier at once. You feel the meeting like a door you are about to open.

You step through the garden gate.
