[Scene: Greenwave Community Garden & Nursery | Morning — Launch Day]
[Visual: Wide shot of terraced planters stacked like green waves; bright orange buoys bob at the edges; magenta flowers puncture the greenery.]
[Sound: Low conversation, the scrape of boots on wet decking, gulls punctuating the air; distant generator hum.]
[Music: A brittle acoustic motif undercuts a tentative, hopeful synth — notes that won’t quite settle.]

You chose this week. You chose Ariana’s living-seawall pilot when the mayor’s question hung in the air and the options smelled like salt and compromise. Now the terraces smell of damp earth and kelp, and the choice sits in your chest like a small, patient stone.

Hands in your pockets, you walk the terraces — your boots leave dark ovals on slick wood, jacket shoulders still flecked with yesterday’s algae from the nursery tests. Volunteers thread between planters, passing buckets and laughter like a relay. Ivy waves you over with both arms, a streak of green safety vest and sun-freckled grin.

Ivy Morales: "Look at this crowd. We scraped names, sure, but people actually came. Lina's here to take photos. Tomas promised to bring the old boat shingles for the nursery float anchors." (she tips her head toward the nearest planter) "And—" (she leans in conspiratorially) "—Ariana’s been running circles around the skeptics already."

Ariana Voss stands at the center, tablet in hand, a halo of damp hair and bright yellow boots. Her voice is exact and warm, two things that seem to settle unsettled people. The tablet screen blinks charts and a small, animated simulation of wave deflection around a tangle of kelp and engineered coir.

Ariana Voss: "If we orient the floats this way, the current shear drops by—" (a flick of her finger, numbers bloom) "—about twelve percent. Not enough to stop every tide, but enough to give nascent plants a chance. We pair that with seeded nurseries and community tending. It's incremental, but it compounds."

You crouch beside the first planter, fingers brushing wet soil and the rubbery stems of young kelp seedlings. The smell is immediate — brine, peat, the undernotes of something alive and stubborn. Your hand comes away damp and slightly sanded.

Narration:
You remember the harbor after the last storm: lopsided moorings, a boathouse half-swallowed by an indifferent tide, your father's hands shaking as he spoke about the maps he kept. You promised yourself then that you would try something different. Not louder. Not grander. Better. This pilot is that promise, or at least the attempt to keep it.

Ivy returns with Tomas trailing behind, his walking stick rhythmically tapping a repaired plank. Tomas's face is a map of creases and memory; he regards the nursery like one regards an old friend who has been allowed to keep a new outfit.

Elder Tomas Hale: "When I was a boy, we built walls out of rock and won't for a storm. We didn't understand erosion like we do now." (he chuckles softly) "These plants... they might be a wall. They might be a promise. But remember: plants take their own time."

You nod because you do remember. You remember the lurch of losing a season, the months of rebuilding, the smell of tar and wet wood. And you feel the pressure that has started up behind the promise: Tideguard's deadline for matching funds, Cassian's polished updates that make the town look like a schematic rather than a place.

Lina Park approaches, camera slung, eyes bright behind glass. She kneels to capture a cluster of kids stomping in the mud, glee on their faces like a small reclamation.

Lina Park: "This will run—" (she checks a note on her tablet) "—human. We need it human. No corporate gloss. Show the kids, show Tomas, show that it's the town doing the saving."

Ariana smiles at Lina, then at you. There's that flame of optimism that drew you in months ago; it softens when it reaches you, a private warmth for the person who chose to stand behind her plan.

Ariana Voss: "We have enough volunteer labor to get the initial array in before the next tide. The lab samples show promising growth rates in controlled tests. But we need to show performance in the wild. We need data from these first four weeks."

You tell her, aloud and with the stubborn steadiness that has become yours:

Marin Sato: "We’ll do it. We'll get the arrays in. We'll tend them. We'll document every growth curve and every failure."

Ariana's hand finds the tablet again, tapping an e-mail draft that reads like a hope: “Request: Tideguard match—pilot scale demonstration.” Her eyes lift to you, and for a breath the terraces quiet.

Ariana Voss: "If we can show three consecutive tidal cycles with positive retention, Tideguard's board is more likely to release the second-phase funding." (she exhales) "They talk timeline. The window's closing, Marin."

You feel the word 'window' like a literal pressure at the throat. It translates into numbers on a tablet, into Cassian's steady voice in your head when he describes necessary speed. You also feel Tomas’s warning: plants have their own tempo. The two things are at odds within you — urgency and patience — and the sea doesn't care which wins.

[Scene: Floating Planters | Midday — Work Montage]
[Visual: Close cuts — hands knotting rope, small seedlings pressed into soil, buoy fenders bobbing; a child's smiling mouth with mud between teeth.]
[Sound: Laughter and instruction layered with the steady slap of waves; camera clicks from Lina’s film.]
[Music: Rhythmic percussive loop that builds slowly then frays at the edges.]

For a week, Havenport seems to pretend at another past: busy, together, noisy with purpose. You move from planter to planter, anchoring, measuring, calling out adjustments. The fishermen who had frowned at the idea begin to ask practical questions. One of them — weathered, fingers like old rope — taps a buoy and squints at the tablet.

Fisher: "You say these kelp ropes break current? How long till they hold up to the northeaster?"

Ariana crouches and points to a diagram she’s sketched in the damp wood of a planter bench.

Ariana Voss: "The fibers we use are treated for salt fatigue. With regular maintenance and rotation, they should last two seasons before needing major refit. But function is collective — it's more than just materials, it's tending."

Fisher: (grunting) "Tending's easy to say when you’ve got time. These boats don't stop for tending."

Ivy: "That's why it's a community thing. If we all commit, then tending becomes part of the days — like nets, like lines. We don't leave it to contractors."

Conversation folds and refolds. You interrupt into the corner of a technical disagreement, offering practical ways to schedule checks and recruit younger hands.

Marin Sato: "We’ll map tending shifts. We’ll train—people will be paid stipends for the first rotation out of the pilot's fund. Not promises — actual hours. Real value."

The fishermen look at each other, then at the seedlings. One nods slowly. Not wholehearted; not yet. Complex. Unreadable. But a small step.

Narration:
Even amid the sweetness — children with mud-splattered knees, the smell of fryer oil from a food stand someone set up for volunteers — there’s a coil of worry beneath your ribs. Tideguard’s clock ticks louder each time you look at the tablet. The pilot needs measurable success fast, and the method you believe in is patient.

A message pings on Ariana's tablet; she reads it and her smile falters for an instant. She covers the screen with a hand as if to hide the number. You know, without looking, there’s a margin shrink.

[Scene: Coastal Research Hub | Night — Lab Lights & Quiet]
[Visual: Blue-lit interior, rows of wet boots, a bank of monitors casting clinical light; Ariana and you alone at a table scattered with sample jars.]
[Sound: Low whir of incubators, distant rain against the hub's reinforced windows.]
[Music: Sparse piano with long, slow intervals; a single cello holds a low note.]

Night narrows the world to the two of you and the data. You watch Ariana scroll through growth curves, eyes rimmed in tiredness that doesn’t quell the spark of hope. The lab's light makes her hair look almost silver where the platinum bob meets the blue streak.

Ariana Voss: "Growth rates are within expected variance. In controlled tanks, a six percent retention by the second tidal pulse is typical. In open water we can expect higher attrition, but if the attachment rates hold we'll have a statistically valid case."

Marin Sato: "But that's the thing — 'expected variance.' In the real harbor there are storm surges and fishing traffic and long tides. We can't control for every variable."

Ariana: (soft laugh) "No. We can't. But we can reduce unknowns. We can learn faster if we deploy more arrays."

Her thumb sweeps a small photograph across the tablet and presses the camera into your palm — a raw, blurred capture of a child's upturned face, mud-splattered and ecstatic. She offers it like a sacrament.

Ariana Voss: "Hold it. Keep it. This is why we do the boring, tedious work. To make this face possible."

Your fingers close around the camera, a weight both physical and symbolic. For a heartbeat you forget the deadline, the deadlines and Tideguard and the rumor of Cassian's slick plans. You remember only the child's grin and the dirt under its nails. Your thumb rubs the cold metal.

You look at Ariana — close enough to count the freckles at the bridge of her nose — and the world tilts, briefly, toward something private and luminous.

Marin Sato: "We can do it. I believe that."

Ariana: "I do too." (her voice small) "But I don't want us to rush into something that hurts the people who'll have to live with it. I want scale, but the right scale."

You both laugh, too short, because the logic of funding compresses time. You slide the camera back to her, your touch lingering at the hinge of intimacy and strategy.

Marin Sato: "We prove it, and Tideguard will have to take notice."

Ariana: "Or we push too fast, and we give them reasons to say we failed. Or we make decisions that favor quick metrics over local realities."

The words land heavy. The lab's hum seems suddenly louder, a reminder that systems keep running whether people catch breath or not.

[Scene: Greenwave Terraces — One Week Later | Early Morning]
[Visual: The planters lined in a more complex geometry; some have spidery repairs; buoy lines show wear.]
[Sound: Wind higher now; gulls fewer; volunteers move with quieter concentration.]
[Music: Low strings, a slow descending motif.]

You walk the terraces before dawn. Some of the seedlings are doing better than expected; others show brown tips where a late swell kissed them. The data looks promising in places and ragged in others. The pilot has demonstrable wins—small, localized—paired with weaknesses that strain your confidence.

A message from Tideguard flashes on the hub’s shared channel: a reminder that the funding window closes in eight days. Eight days. The number is a small weapon.

Ariana meets you at the walkway, coat zipped, breath clouding in the cold. Her expression is an intricate map of resolve and worry.

Ariana Voss: "They want scale proofs for phase two. Their board meeting is in nine days; legal needs the sampling reports a week before that. If we want the match we need to present numbers that can't be dislodged."

Marin Sato: "And 'numbers that can't be dislodged' are—?"

Ariana: "Replication across exposure levels. Three sites, within this harbor, showing positive retention across two tidal cycles with minimal maintenance hours."

She looks at you as if the weight of the harbor has condensed into your shoulders. She doesn't ask you to say yes. She says it like a fact that requires action.

Ariana Voss: "We can push more arrays this week. We can bring in extra engineering rigs and automate monitoring so we can handle more nodes."

You hear the undertone: faster deployment. The undertone that makes your stomach hollow, because faster means shortcuts, because more rigs means a heavier footprint on the shoreline, because automation means less hands-on tending by the community you promised to center.

Marin Sato: "Faster gives us data sooner, but it also increases disturbance and risk. We could damage the very habitats we're trying to protect if we don't stage it carefully."

Ariana: (voice tight) "I know. I've been sitting on an algorithm that can prioritize non-invasive anchor points. But it's not field-tested at scale. We have to accept a degree of experimental risk to convince them."

You both fall quiet. The wind takes words and rearranges them.

Narration:
You thought choosing Ariana's path would feel like carving a clear line — heart and science aligned against Tideguard. Instead it has become a taut wire between two cliffs: the need to prove the method before the funding evaporates, and the knowledge that rushing can break trust. Your own flaw — the habit of throwing yourself forward, believing sheer will can compensate for time — presses at you. You remember the boathouse and the months of repair that followed the last failed quick fix. You can tell the same pattern is waiting if you lean too hard on speed.

Cassian's name surfaces like a slick wave in your mind — not a villain's face here, only a reminder that there are other options, smoother, more decisive, and with capital to back them. He would approve quick builds and engineered barriers; he would call your caution a delay. The idea of him smiling over a schematic that sidelines neighborhood voices makes your hands clench.

Ivy interrupts your reverie, pulling up some preliminary monitoring charts on her phone.

Ivy Morales: "Look—some of the automated sensors are misreporting depth after heavy swings. If we escalate deployment now, we're going to have data gaps unless we bring extra tech support."

Marin Sato: "Can we patch? Rotate sensors?"

Ivy: "Yes, but it draws the volunteers away from tending, and—" (she bites her lip) "—we don't have unlimited hands."

You stare at the chart. The peaks and troughs on the screen look like a heartbeat. The funding timeline stares back—implacable, a demand rather than a plea.

[Visual: Close-up of your compass pendant resting against the fabric of your jacket, brass dulled, the needle jittering faintly in the wind.]
[Sound: The ocean beyond the terrace, patient and unhurried; a single metronomic drip from a faulty buoy line.]

Narration:
You have a choice that tastes like metal and salt. Move fast to scale and risk damaging trust and habitat — but maybe secure funding that can expand community control. Move slow and risk Tideguard turning elsewhere, perhaps to Cassian's plan, which might fortify the town but at a cost to customs and access. Either way, people you love will be affected. Either way, some future will be closed.

Ariana stands a step away, the tablet glowing with a small, relentless clock: days left until the board meeting. Her gaze finds yours, raw and searching.

Ariana Voss: "I thought with you beside me, we could make both things true. Maybe that's naïve."

Marin Sato: "Maybe. Or maybe it's the only kind of naive that can make people stay."

Her laugh is very small, and there is no real answer that fits the scale of the harbor’s needs.

You look at the seedlings, at the muddy footprints trailing off the terrace, at the stretch of the town beyond — rooftops patched with solar, the distant Tideguard pavilion like a polished tooth. The sea keeps its level, indifferent to vows and spreadsheets.

[Music: A final low chord lingers, unresolved.]

Narration (internal):
This is the hinge you felt earlier, the breath caught in your chest since the mayor posed the question. There is no clean outcome. There is only the pressure, and the knowledge that whatever you choose will be a kind of breaking.

You reach for the tablet; your thumb hovers over the quick-deploy command Ariana has prepared. The buoy ropes smell of sea and green promise. The compass at your throat is cool.

[Sound: Screen click; wind; a gull's distant cry.]

You have to decide—how much of the town's future will you accelerate to prove a point, and how much will you protect by waiting for slower, surer growth?
