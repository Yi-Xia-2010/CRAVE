## Visual Novel Title
Salt & Tides

---

## World View
Havenport is a near-future coastal town in 2040 where salt-streaked tradition collides with engineered resilience. Sea levels have risen steadily; storms arrive with new ferocity and the town stitches itself together with tarps, sandbags, and modular tech. Politically, governance is mixed: a town council rooted in fishing families and community collectives like Greenwave contend with regional development firms backed by investors pushing rapid infrastructure. Culture is a blend of old maritime rituals—boat blessings, communal net-mending—and emergent climate labor: kelp nurseries, living seawalls, solar arrays clinging to the cliffs. Religion and ritual persist as seasonal rites more than doctrine: Elder Tomas speaks for memory, the boathouse is a secular shrine. Class tensions simmer: small fishers fear displacement, technicians and NGO staff promise scalable adaptation. Geography is compact—boardwalks chewing into reclaimed terraces, a low harbor, a wind-raked research hub, Tideguard Pavilion perching like an outsider. Atmosphere: melancholic resilience; painterly visuals (muted coastal palette, golden-hour glints, storm-blue nights) give everything a weathered intimacy. Conflicts center on compromise: preserve tradition or accept disruptive protection. Romance grows inside these trade-offs, where choices about love are also choices about who gets to keep the shoreline.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Marin Sato, raised on the smell of brine and rope, back to steward Havenport after storms wrecked my family boathouse. I lead community-led restoration, while Ariana Voss brings ambitious ecological engineering, and Cassian Rhee represents corporate-backed rapid fortification. The story is a tragedy: each choice pushes us deeper into losses—broken trust, cultural erasure, and personal sacrifice. The narrative arc is a continuous Fall: my attempts to balance love and duty escalate conflicts between neighbors, scientists, and developers; mistakes accumulate; small failures magnify into irreversible damage. Branching choices at key moments steer whether I lean into confrontation, negotiation, or personal withdrawal, and each branch ends in a different form of tragic outcome—outright displacement, compromised culture, the collapse of a relationship, or a moral compromise that saves infrastructure but haunts conscience. Through painterly scenes on the boardwalk, the Greenwave terraces, the research hub, Tideguard Pavilion, and the old boathouse, I watch the coastline—and my relationships—erode. Romance with Ariana blooms but is strained by impatience and ethical trade-offs; Cassian's competence forces hard bargains. No ending is comfortable: each is a lesson that resilience costs what we hold dear.
