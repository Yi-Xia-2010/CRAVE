Someone tugs you away as a cable snaps; the arc of oily water throws you sideways and into the mud. You taste iron. For a heartbeat you are not sure if you're more afraid of losing the boats or of watching people you love vanish beneath the swell.

Cassian Rhee does not stand before a camera to take ownership; he speaks with lawyers in a video conference you are not in. You feel the pattern of corporate retreat: when the scale tips from PR-friendly triumph to legal jeopardy, fingers pull back.

You answer. You don't have all the answers. She nods, and you both look out over the harbor where the morning fog curls low. The town will take years to heal; some will leave; some will stay. The pilot collapsed, headlines turned, and Tideguard's shiny promise receded into legalese and a pause. Your compass hangs cold and heavy against your chest, the metal scuffed by hands that have done both care and harm.

You find, in the final hush, a terrible clarity: good intentions are not protection. Community consent cannot be assumed after the fact. Technology without humility can engineer new forms of danger. The loss is precise and personal, and you carry it inward.

You will keep working. You will organize the repairs, help families patch hulls and moorings, and press for real oversight. You will face the questions and the anger, and you will answer as honestly as you can. But the warmth you felt on the morning of the rollout — the gleam of a possible future — is dulled. There is a deeper, colder resolve now: to do the slow, painful work of earning back trust.
