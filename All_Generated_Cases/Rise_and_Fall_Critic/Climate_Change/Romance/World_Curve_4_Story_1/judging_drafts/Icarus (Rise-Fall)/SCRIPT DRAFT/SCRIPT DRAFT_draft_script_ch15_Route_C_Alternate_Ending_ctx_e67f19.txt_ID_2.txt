[Scene: Skyfarm Terraces | Dusk]
[Visual: Terrace rows step down toward the softened skyline; drip lines glisten with collected runoff. A network of thin cables gleams where solar fabric meets automated tilters.]
[Music: Low, persistent hum — mechanical, almost lullaby; a single cello threads through]
[Sound: Distant TideGrid stabilizer pulses as a steady heartbeat; gulls argue over a scrap of light]

You stand barefoot on the warm wooden plank of the terrace, the ribbon in your hair damp with the day's salt. The transitional framework you helped stitch together has been in place long enough that its name is ordinary in mouths now: the Custodial Accord. For a while it sounded like justice — community oversight committees, phased automation, a promise of restitution and reinvestment written into public ledgers.

At first, everything feels like a compromise that might hold. The automated stabilizers lower the highest surge; narrow alleys that used to flood now drink the tide in measured sips. The Skyfarm's drip lines were rerouted to the shelters by the consortium's grant; seedlings find new beds. Children run across raised planks without the old wariness. You remember the way the agreement read, the clauses that glittered with legal care, the signatures that felt like a pact made by hands you could see.

Elias comes up the row behind you, hands tucked into his jacket like someone trying to keep a small, growing thing from being bruised. He moves quieter than he used to; his optimism has folded into a careful competence. When he speaks, it is factual, even when the facts bruise.

Elias Kade: "They finished the last verification cycle this morning. Nineteen modules green. Latency's down by twelve percent over baseline."

You: "Twelve percent buys time. It doesn't buy what we were promised."

Elias looks at your hands, those habitual callouses and the locket warm at your throat, and for a fraction of a second his face is all raw, like the underside of a shell.

Elias Kade: (soft) "We kept the roofs from tearing off in the last storm. People slept through the surge for the first time in years."

You feel both the weight of that gratitude and a colder ledger balancing in the margins of your mind. You know how profits and promises travel on different currents.

You: "We traded a lot for that sleep."

Elias's mouth tightens. He gestures to a modular planter, adjusting its tilt with a small gloved hand.

Elias Kade: "We didn't trade. We negotiated. You asked for oversight. Custodians got a seat on the board. You got—" (he looks for the right word) "—a process."

You want to say that a process is not the same as repair. You want to list the things taken in the name of expediency: the promise of local fund control diluted by line items, the artisans' workshop deemed 'nonessential' during procurement audits. Instead you fold your hands around the thermos he offers and let the tea steam fog between you.

[Scene: Old Dock District | Morning — Weeks Later]
[Visual: Crates labeled 'ARCHIVE' and 'DIGITAL TRANSFER' line a cleared slipway. Mesh tarps ripple where once tangoed bright banners. A crane swings, indifferent and precise.]
[Sound: The scrape of metal on metal; the low chatter of artisans; the tinny calibration ping of scanners]
[Music: Sparse piano, notes spaced like footsteps]

You walk the Old Dock District with Old Man Toma at your side. Where once there were riotous mosaics catching the sun and crowd-song, there are now technicians mapping tiles into sterile pixels. The mosaics are being digitized — cataloged, scanned, exalted into accessible online exhibitions — and then removed. They tell you it's saving the designs; Toma calls it removal dressed up as translation.

Old Man Toma: (voice like dried linen) "They call it preservation. They call it care. Back when I was a boy, 'preserve' meant you kept it in the thing — not in someone else's light."

You crouch to touch a bare patch of wall where a wave of tiny tiles used to curl. Salt has eaten the grout; a spider web clings to the exposed cement. Your fingers leave a wet print that looks indecent and human on the cool stone.

You: "We negotiated a clause for local custodianship. We wrote terms, Toma. We had signatures."

Old Man Toma watches a young artisan remove the final mosaic tile with a soft gloved hand. The artisan's face is open and apologetic, like someone taking apart a kite that was meant to fly forever.

Old Man Toma: "Signatures on paper? That don't warm a room." (He laughs once, a short spatter.) "The ledger's neat. The town's ledger is neat. But what's neat don't tell the stories — they put those in a box the city don't live in."

The artisans gather; they do not glare at you, though some glances are hard as barnacles. You had thought your presence would steady them. You had thought your name, your history, could tug wakefulness into the accord's bones.

You: "If the mosaics must come down, can we at least keep the last panel in the community hall? Let people see the work, touch the stitches?"

An artisan — a woman with salt-bleached braids — meets your eyes.

Artisan: "We've kept one. For now. They'll archive the rest, but the hall panel's fine. The consortium's funders asked for 'centralized storage' for fragile pieces. They said it's safer."

You feel the argument forming like an old tide: safety or memory. It seems petty and colossal at once.

You leave the dock with the echo of machines and the scraped edge of something that used to be whole. The community hall's single panel is a consolation, a museum piece kept alive by hands that remember how to name the old streets when they used to be more than thumbnails on a screen.

[Scene: Canal Twelve — Night]
[Visual: Lanterns hang low; the market marshals itself between repaired stalls. Water hisses gently against new pilings. Reflections in the canal fold the city into doubled, half-remembered images.]
[Music: A single violin, reedy and patient, layered with a recorded loop of city sounds — a marketplace's heartbeat]
[Sound: Clink of a thermos lid; a distant announcement from a consortium tower about scheduled upgrades]

You and Elias find the same bench as you once did, where the light is just soft enough to hide every small bruise. The thermos is between you; the ritual persists like a bandage.

Elias Kade: (quiet) "They approved the three-year upgrade plan this week. They want to standardize module interfaces. It makes integration cleaner."

You: "Integration often means central control. It makes the platform more efficient — and more closed."

He opens his hands, palms up, as if displaying all the moving parts of a problem he cannot dismantle with goodwill.

Elias Kade: "I believed the TideGrid could be modular — open-sourced, community-run. I still believe that. But money answers faster. The consortium answers faster."

You: "They answered by buying the faster answer."

His jaw tightens. He has grown thinner in the face; something in him has learned to chew policy instead of feeling it.

Elias Kade: (after a long pause) "We saved the terraces. We kept the Skyfarms alive. There were people who would have drowned without the stabilizers."

You want to press the ledger of human consequence into his hands — the artisans who lost their markets, the dock's mosaics that now live as pixels in a curated light. Instead you find yourself reciting names, as if naming could keep them from being curated away.

You: "Do you remember Toma's map? He used to point to a corner in the dock and say, 'This is where the sea learned to speak to us.' Now they're teaching it to talk to servers."

Elias closes his eyes for a moment, the streetlight sketching pale lines across his lashes.

Elias Kade: "I thought keeping the network open would keep that voice. I thought code could carry the warmth."

You: "Code remembers shapes. It doesn't smell of wet rope. It doesn't taste of the lemon rind someone used to keep their hands steady while they sewed."

The silence that follows is not empty — it is full of all the noises you cannot fix with tools. Your relationship, once a braided thing of argument and tenderness, has become a series of small exchanges: a thermos of tea left at the workbench, a late-night repair session where your heads bend over a tangle of wires, the way he smiles without reaching for you anymore.

Elias Kade: (softly, as if making a confession to the tide) "I don't want to be the person who builds a dam and forgets the songs that used to be sung on its banks."

You: "I don't want to be the person who keeps the songs in a file and calls it honoring."

He opens his mouth like he might say something that would stitch the two sounds back together, but a passing announcement about a funding reallocation blurs the moment into policy again.

Elias Kade: "They're reallocating maintenance credits to next-gen modules. It's—"

You hold up a hand. You have learned, painfully, that arguments can become blueprints for disappointment. There are things you cannot make Mr. Right with graphs.

You: "I will keep saying Toma's map, Elias. I'll read it in markets and council rooms and to anyone who will listen. They can digitize the mosaics; they can fund a thousand upgrades. They cannot make you, or me, forget the smell of varnish on a morning when the sun came like a promise."

He folds his hands around his knees like a child. He wants to reach and cannot find the route.

Elias Kade: "And if they keep taking? If the ledger grows and the human ledger doesn't balance?"

You: "Then we keep making a different ledger. One without shareholders in the margins."

It feels hardly like a victory. You both know the odds. The city will stay afloat; infrastructure will hum and reports will glint with green check marks. People sleep through the worst of the tides. Yet the artifacts of daily life — the patched jackets, the hand-painted signs, the mosaics that held a community's practical jokes and grief — have been shifted into curated showcases. They are preserved with the tenderness of a museum piece, removed from the daily scrape of living.

[Scene: Canal Twelve — Late Night]
[Visual: Lamplight pools; the canal is a black glass ribbon. The Skyfarm's silhouette holds the horizon like an exhalation.]
[Music: Low, slow piano; a distant, watery electronic hum]

You sit on the promenade's edge, locket warm against your chest, and you speak.

You: "Here, in the corner where the pelican used to nest, Old Man Toma once left his cane leaning against a post for a week because he trusted us to notice."

A passerby — an artisan you know only by her braid — pauses. She leans on the railing, as if bearing witness is a physical act.

You continue, because telling is the thing you can still do that keeps an ache honest: "There was a small shop. He who mended nets lived there. He taught the kids to knot names into the hemp. They called it the knot-shop. It smelled of fish and lemon and tar and the laugh of someone who'd found a way to be alive after losing his roof."

You read the map aloud until the night folds it into people's memories. Some listen and nod. Some record you on their devices, the irony half-bitter and full of necessary contradiction. You do not pretend the recordings are the same as the hands-and-voice of Toma. You simply refuse to let the story be only a curated file in the consortium's glowing archive.

The ledger is clean. Budgets balance. A quarterly report congratulates the city on its reduced flood mortality and efficient allocation of maintenance credits. The spreadsheets show columns of saved structures, of refurbished Skyfarm plots, of modules upgraded. They glow like a kind of triumph.

But the human ledger — the one that contains who was asked to leave a workspace, who had to pack their tools and sign away a claim to a mural — that ledger does not fit on a spreadsheet. It lodges in throats, in the silence at meals, in small acts of mutiny: an unapproved seed planted in a median strip, a child's chalk drawing kept through a cleaning sweep, a song hummed in a stairwell.

Old Man Toma finds you the next morning at the market. He has a folded piece of paper and a small, ridiculous grin.

Old Man Toma: "You kept talking, eh? People came. You got a few to sign up to patrol the docks. Young ones, not the ones who think preserving is just a screen."

You fold the paper open. It is a list — names, times, small commitments written in different hands. It is not much, but it is a thing that might matter.

You: "They'll be watched. They'll be reported."

Old Man Toma: (shrugging) "Let them report. Papers are thin. People are not. We'll keep the stones warm."

You feel, for a moment, something fragile and defiant push up through the tide of fatigue. It is not a victory yet. It may never be one measured by the consortium's metrics. But it is not nothing.

Elias crosses the market, hands empty except for an old wrench he tucks into his jacket. He stands beside you without being asked. You used to think belonging required a line of sight and mutual agreement. Now it just means someone showing up.

Elias Kade: (quiet) "I didn't come to argue. I came to help pass the kettle."

You accept the gesture. It is small; it is exactly what the two of you can manage without breaking.

You speak Old Man Toma's map one last time for the evening crowd: not a preservation statement, not a pledge, but names and senses, the way a place tastes and smells and aches in the memory.

You: "We saved the city's body," you say, and the words rest like a stone on water. "But we lost pieces of its heart."

There is no ceremonial trumpet, no final reconciliation. There is the market's ordinary chorus, the creak of a stall's rope, the distant pulse of stabilizers keeping the ocean at bay. You close your palm around the warm thermos Elias hands you, and you feel the weight of small things that mean everything.

[Visual: The camera pulls back slowly; the terraces and the docks are lit by many small lights, some electric, some human. The city breathes on, steady and imperfect.]
[Music: A slow, minor chord — acceptance wrapped in ache]
[Sound: TideGrid hum as a distant comfort; a child's laugh carried on the wind]

You speak aloud, because a ledger can be neat and the human ledger messy and still both true. You keep saying the names. You fill rooms with them. You teach a new generation to tie the same knots. You keep the map alive.

The ledger's numbers remain tidy. The city does not drown. Some of its laughter moves into curated exhibits, some of its warmth is carried in hands that refuse to let things be only pixels. You live in the in-between: a city put back together with circuitry and memory, with agreements that held and promises that frayed.

You do not find consolation in the tidy pages. Your consolation is smaller, salt-worn and stubborn: the way someone remembers a joke you once told, the way a tile's shadow still falls across a hall, the way hands pass a thermos without asking permission.

The final accounting the consortium favors is a clean sentiment: stabilizers function, upgrades proceed, maintenance metrics improve. The human ledger — the breath before a song, the warmth of a mended jacket, the map read aloud — is uneven and unfinished, and you hold it carefully anyway.
