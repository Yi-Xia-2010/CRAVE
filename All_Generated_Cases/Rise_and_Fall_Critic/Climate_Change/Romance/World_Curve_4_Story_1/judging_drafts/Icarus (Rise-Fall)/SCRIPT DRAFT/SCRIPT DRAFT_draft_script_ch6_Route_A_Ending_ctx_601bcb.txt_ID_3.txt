[Scene: Old Dock District | Dawn — Aftermath]
[Visual: Collapsed piers and splintered beams jut like broken ribs into brown water. A mechanical trench yawns where the tide once lapped gently at warehouses; mud and bright orange buoy fragments cling to torn netting. [Music: Low, hollow cello; distant metallic groan]

You step onto the breached boardwalk and the wood flexes beneath you — a sick, familiar give that carries memory more than sound. Salt in the air tastes of iron. Your boots leave sullen prints in the puddles that reflect a sky bruised with storm-cloud. Where laughter once threaded the terrace paths, there is only a sleek, machine-made scar cutting the dock open like a verdict.

You know the name of the mechanism that made the cut: TideGrid nodes, synchronized as a web across the channel. You also know what it feels like to be woven into a web that tightens until something snaps. But you do not speak in technical absolutes here; you speak in shards — a child's tomato grafting bench overturned, a neighbor's satellite photo of a harvest gone slick with mud, Old Man Toma's cane sunk into clay.

You move slowly. Every damp plank has a memory; your fingers brush one and leave a trace as if trying to press something back into being.

Neighbor (shouting from a collapsed stall): "You said it would save us!"

[Visual: A small group gathers on a raised walkway patched with tarps and rope. Faces are salt-creased; a child's soaked stuffed fish clings to a frayed net.]
[Music: Cellos thicken into low chords; wind through torn canvas]

You keep your hands collected at your sides because there is a weight in every clenched fist aimed your way. When they look at you, the look is not simple anger — it is wreckage dressed in accusation, fatigue, and a need for an answer you cannot hand over like a toolbox.

Neighbor (stepping closer): "We trusted you. We trusted them. What do we have now?"

You: "I believed it could buy time. I still believe that. I—" Your voice catches like seaweed on a cleat. "I thought we could steer this. I didn't expect—" You stop because your words fracture against a wall of grief that doesn't want justification.

Neighbor: "Not expect? Houses fell. The Skyfarm— the children's terrace is gone. How do you not expect that?"

The neighbor's question is not a single strike; it is a slow erosion. You search for something steadier than defense and find instead the raw, precise truth: you acted from hope and perhaps hubris. Hope is a muscle you have trained; hubris is a current that looks like helpful force until it becomes a cutter.

[Scene: Breached Boardwalk — Later]
[Visual: Masts and a half-collapsed sign sway; Elias arrives at a run, water beading off his jacket. He is sodden, hair plastered to his temple; the frayed blue ribbon you once tied at your throat is missing from its usual knot.]
[Music: The cello drops into single, pained notes; a distant electronic beep from Elias's wrist-tablet warbles and then goes silent]

He stops a few paces away, breathing hard. For a moment you only watch each other like two figures reflected in different puddles: distorted, familiar, and hard to reconcile. When he speaks, his voice is small and ragged.

Elias: "Maia."

You: "You found me."

Elias: (hands trembling) "I was in the console for— I didn't leave. I checked overlays, re-ran simulations. We didn't model that— that cascade. I—" He swallows, eyes fracturing. "I'm sorry."

You feel a wash of emotion: the salt-sweetness of relief to see him, the coldness of betrayal like a sudden tide pulling at ankles. There is history in the ache between apology and absolution.

You: "Sorry isn't a credential. People lost terraces, Elias. Children lost a place to learn. Toma— his stoop is a memory now."

Elias: "We built a system to prevent that exact thing. We tried to protect them. If the system failed— if it made things worse— I need to know why. I need—" He searches for data like a tether. "I need to fix it."

You: "Fix it for who? For the city hall data sheet? For Rin's press releases? Or for the people who stood in those terraces with hands in the dirt?"

Elias's jaw tightens; the scientific habit reflexively seeks the problem's parameters, but the personal habit wants defense.

Elias: "This isn't politics for me. I thought— (pause) I thought if I solved the engineering, it would stop being about blame."

You: "Engineering cannot hold memory. It doesn't count the old songs or the way Toma ties his rope knot. We gave a system a map of our coasts but not of our lives."

Elias: "We could have included community oversight—" His voice falters as if those words are both a concession and an accusation. "But the node sync was supposed to prevent exactly this kind of loss."

You: "Supposed to."

There is silence between you, thick as wet fabric. The world around you organizes itself into small efforts: neighbors ferrying pots, Ibe already hauling a splintered bench onto a patched platform. The city stabilizes in the material sense — contractors and Rin's teams run cordons and weld girders — but when people fix structure and leave ruin in memory, what trusts remain are not metal.

You: "Auditable doesn't mean accountable in the morning when a terrace is gone."

Elias: (voice thin) "Then what would you have done, Maia? Let the tide take what it wanted while we argued in the dark?"

You: "I would have built with the people most likely to be there when the tide came. Different tools, yes. But not at the cost of their roofs."

Elias's eyes go distant. For the first time you see the tiredness settle into him like sediment. You both understand the truth you co-created: intention is not exculpation.

[Scene: Flooded Promenade — Afternoon]
[Visual: Old Man Toma sits on a low wrap-around bench, a map unfolded across his knees; his hands hover above lines as if touching memory. Around him are the patched pots and a small makeshift shrine of broken garden tools.]
[Music: Sparse piano, the rhythm of someone counting losses]
[Sound: Quiet conversation, a far-off announcer voice trying to make sense]

Old Man Toma (soft, voice like wind through netting): "We misread the tide."

You: The words are short, but they cut cleaner than any accusation. You move to the bench and sit beside him, the map between you two. You lift it gently because maps are fragile things; they mark not only geography but the rituals embedded in that space.

Old Man Toma: "Not just the sea... the way we think we can hold it with metal and law alone. You came with good hands, child. That was not the problem."

You: "Then what was?"

Old Man Toma: (folding a corner of the map) "We forgot the slow work. We folded ourselves into quick fixes. The tide answers where force and care meet. You taught us to think of saving lives — but saving lives isn't only numbers and turbines."

You sit with that, the copper locket at your throat suddenly heavy as a coin. You press your thumb to its rim and feel the tiny photograph inside — a sliver of coastline that used to be more than an image: a whole world in paper. It feels smaller now, almost an artifact of a time you couldn't keep. The metal is cool against your skin, a small, private anchor.

You stand, the map pressed back into Toma's lap. Around you, the city hums with repair; Rin's teams bolt plates over exposed nodes; contractors raise temporary supports; people who can leave take offers of shelter. Material stability returns a brittle shell, but the interior — people's trust, relationships, the intimate knowledge of how to read a storm — is carved by the event like a shoreline after a strong tide.

You know, without needing to be told, that the balance has shifted. The city will not be the same. The alliance you publicly forged with Elias and privately believed in has fractures you cannot simply weld. You will gather what remains: pots, benches, voices. You will count what you can save and name what cannot be reassembled.

Elias (approaching, quieter now): "They patched the nodes. Rin's people are saying stabilization is effective. It's— it's running."

You: "Running doesn't mean right."

Elias: (looks away) "Maybe not yet. But Maia, we can do better."

You: "Can we? Does 'better' include the small hands that graft seeds on terraces? Does 'better' include who gets to decide when a node is turned on?"

Elias's jaw works. He opens his mouth, hesitates, closes it. There are words he wants — defense, plea, explanation — but none seem adequate.

Elias: (finally) "I didn't mean for—"

You: "I know." The word is both balm and blade. "But meaning isn't enough. We need systems that reckon with meaning. We need governance, not just good work."

Elias: "Governance — (bitter laugh) — you and Rin would make a coalition out of oversight."

You: "I would make a table where those you seek to save have seats. Not Rin's boardroom with its legal filters."

Elias: (his voice small, almost private) "And you'll lead that table without the tools we have?"

You: "I will do the work. Maybe you'll help. Maybe you won't. Either way, I will be there."

There is no reconciliation in the line. There is only an understanding settling like sediment: the personal and the political have diverged, and where one goes, the other cannot always follow.

[Visual: People hauling small planters onto raised platforms; a group sings a low song as they tie knots. The sky is a muted wash; a thin line of golden light wraps the horizon like a bandage.]

You walk the repaired paths with your hands full of seed packets and a small packet of map fragments. You distribute them silently: to a mother, to Ibe, to a child who looks at you like you might still be a source of certainty. Each transfer is a small pledge — not to erase what happened, but to respond.

Old Man Toma watches you and, when you pass, lays a palm briefly on your shoulder. There's no ceremony. There's only a small, human contact that says he has seen you and will continue to watch.

You think about the locket pressed against your ribs and how absurdly small the photograph inside looks now. You laugh once, a sound like a readout error, and then you fold the locket closed and tuck it beneath your shirt. That photograph is not the whole of what you lost or must preserve. It is a reminder of why you began.

You watch the city as people rebuild not because institutions demanded it but because hands do. The TideGrid runs; machines hum; Rin's legal teams parade certainty in slick statements. The material world steadies, and yet the social fabric bears grooves that may take generations to fill. Trust, once gouged, grows slowly — root by stubborn root.

You have learned something in the way only a bad tide can teach: good intentions can precipitate disaster if they are not paired with listening, accountability, and an insistence on the small, everyday structures that hold life. Your grief is not a question only of blame; it is a teacher that will insist you change how you measure 'saving'.
