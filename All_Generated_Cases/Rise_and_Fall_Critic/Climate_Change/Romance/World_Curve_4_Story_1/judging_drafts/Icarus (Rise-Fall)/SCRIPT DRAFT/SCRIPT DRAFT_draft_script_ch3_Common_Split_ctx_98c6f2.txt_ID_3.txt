Elias: "Agreed — diagnostics are essential. But we optimize for deployment and iterated updates. We start where risk is highest."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' N'Diaye: "My cousin's porch sank last winter. We braced it with planks and hope. A machine deciding when our water rises? That's a story written by people who don't know the grain of our floors. You can't patch a life with an algorithm."

Old Man Toma: "We lost the dock where my sister made lacquered boxes. We kept the boxes by keeping the stories. Who keeps the stories if someone else decides the tide?"

Elias's modular vision slides through those numbers and shows smiling rooftops humming again. Ibe's carpentry hands and Toma's stories push you back toward the stubborn labor you recognize.

The mayor's deputy clears her throat. The air picks up a little; the sun throws angled light across the seedlings so that they look like small halos.

Mayor's Deputy: "Council must decide tonight. The governor needs a statement. Do we commit to immediate TideGrid deployment, or do we propose alternative measures? We need a single voice."

You know the familiarity of these moments — a single voice shaping a thousand small lives. You can feel the terrace lean toward you, an improbable center you did not ask for but cannot refuse.
