[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of boardwalk and raised beds; morning mist lifting off tidal pools]
[Music: Soft ambient guitar with distant gulls]
[Sound: Wooden planks creak under slow footsteps; faint drip of water from a tarp]

Mara Lin steps onto the boardwalk with her boots half-full of last night's mud and two fingers of salt still on her lips from the walk down the tide line. The Commons wakes in small, deliberate motions: a spray of brine on her hands when she reaches for the railing, the warm sour of compost when she kneels to check a bedridden seed tray, the way the mist makes everything a little softer around the edges. This is the work she knows by touch before she names it—the pull of reed roots, the patience of salt-tolerant seedlings, the careful tilt of a reclaimed channel so water lingers where it must.

The reclaimed plots glint in the light. Someone has nailed new signage—fresh paint over an older slogan—and a string of paper lanterns still hangs from driftwood poles, drying stubbornly even as the sun comes up. Beyond the marsh grass the sea is a sheet of slate; the tide is going out and leaves a mirror of smeared clouds in the shallow pools. Mara Lin breathes in. It smells like all the things that make Solhaven feel like home: kelp, wet wood, rain waiting somewhere up the coast.

A familiar voice breaks the quiet like a practiced oar stroke.

Tommy: "Mara. You see those mussel lines? Held through the night. Lord knows that's something."

Mara Lin looks up to find Tommy leaning against the post where the boardwalk bends, a thermos in one hand, the other hooked on his beanie. His face reads the weather the way she reads a soil sample—old map lines and worn roads. He blinks against the light and grins, the grin more tired than celebratory.

Mara Lin: "Good. That'll help with the nets next week. Any word from the northern fleet?"

Tommy: "Quiet. Better quiet than not, right? Though—" Tommy [shifting, cautious]: "Council's got papers in the trailer outside the Promenade. Word is a presentation later. Pretty suit boys showing maps."

Mara Lin feels the word 'presentation' the way she feels a nail through wood: small and invasive. She steadies her hands on the railing.

Mara Lin [internal]: "The Promenade plans—Celeste's banners, hard lines, straight concrete—have been the shadow in every conversation these last months. You can map the tension across your own palms: living shorelines versus a hardened edge, community plots versus a paved promenade. Both promises sound like stability. Both ask us to trade something away."

Tommy [soft]: "Rae's got a flyer. Says there's a meeting tonight. She wants you there."

Before Mara Lin can answer, a flicker of movement: Rae emerges from between two raised beds, paint on her fingers and a stencil rolled under her arm. She smells of aerosol and tea. She sprawls on a nearby bench as if the bench has always belonged to her.

Rae [lifts a hand; it's smeared in teal]: "Morning, Mara. You're gonna love what I did with the protest placards. Or hate them. Depends on your mood." Rae [teal-smeared hand lift]: "Also, who do you want me to flip to the front page? 'Grassroots' or 'Grassroots—and stubborn'?"

Mara Lin: "Both. Keep the nuance."

Rae's laugh is quick and usable; it tucks a corner of the edge off the morning's tension.

Rae: "Also—Tommy says someone's at the tide line. Bringin' a tablet and polite shoes." She winks. "Looks like corporate diplomacy, but he sent me a selfie with a light windbreaker and an expression that says 'I have a plan.'"

Mara Lin glances toward the tide line. A figure stands where the wet sand meets the boardwalk shadow—slim, methodical movements that betray an engineering gait more than a fisherman’s. He holds a tablet against his chest, like a small shield. The air folds itself into that familiar space of strangers with proposals: courteous distance, measured optimism.

[Visual: Tight on Elias Navarro as he walks up the damp path; light catches the edge of his jacket]
[Sound: Soft, rhythmic tapping of his shoes on wet wood]

Elias Navarro [polite, slightly tentative]: "Good morning. I'm Elias—Elias Navarro." (He offers a hand, polite, slightly tentative.) "I work with the Coastal Cooperative. We—" He pauses, scanning the boardwalk, taking in wooden signs and seed trays the way someone reads a room. His blue-gray eyes hold a quiet that isn't blank; it's attentive, like a notebook learning to trust your handwriting. "I didn't mean to interrupt."

Mara Lin feels the small heat of her palm as she takes his hand. His grip is steady, neither too firm nor slack. There's something honest in his pause—an engineer learning how much to say and when to listen.

Mara Lin: "You didn't. We're usually better at interrupting ourselves." Mara Lin [nods toward the beds].

Mara Lin: "Mara Lin. We run the salt beds and the marsh channels."

Elias Navarro: "I've read about Saltwren. Dr. Bhatt mentioned your planting protocol. She—" He stops again, as if measuring how public praise lands. "She said you ground things in the tide. Thought it worth seeing with my own eyes."

Rae (elbowing Mara Lin): "See? Not all visitors have power suits. Some have gadgets and glyphs, too." She grins at Elias Navarro like she's already allies with both of them.

Tommy: "So. Come to show us a map? Or come to sell us something?"

Elias Navarro [dry half-smile]: "Neither. I brought a set of proposals—engineering overlays, if you will. But I'm here to listen. Really." (He gestures to his tablet.) "We want to design something that actually works for people here. I figured starting with you—Saltwren—makes sense."

Mara Lin feels the tilt of that sentence. It could be earnest. It could be the beginning of negotiation theater. She keeps her face even; it's what she does—let the work show her instead of promises.

Mara Lin [internal]: "He says 'listen' and you hear 'implement.' You know engineers who mean 'do' and organizers who mean 'stay.' You want to know which he is before you let any plans cross the boardwalk."

Elias Navarro: "If there's time, I'd love to walk the tide line with you. I can show what we've modeled and get immediate feedback." (He catches Mara Lin's neutral look.) "Or—if you'd rather wait until the public forum tonight, that's understandable. I know community spaces are—" His voice smooths, careful not to push.

The morning breathes around that offer. The birds pick at the spilled seeds, the lanterns above sway like watching eyes, and the low tide leaves the marsh to its quiet maintenance. The choice in front of Mara Lin is small in gesture and large in implication: an early, private conversation that could tilt trust or an insistence on public process that honors the Commons' communal life.

Her pulse doesn't race. It steadies. This is part of the careful work: decisions made by hands that have felt the tide shift.
