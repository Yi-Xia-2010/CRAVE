# Route: Additional Route / Ending


[Scene: Temporary Pier & Saltwren Commons Overlook | Night — Storm Peak]
[Visual: A low, rotting launch tied to a battered pier; rain hammers across the boards in streaks; a single dinghy strains at its moorings]
[Sound: Sirens wail distant and near; wind tears at tarpaulins; a child’s cry fragments through the wall of rain]
[Music: Full-orchestra, wind-pressed crescendos; percussion like distant, impatient drums]

You are standing with your thumb on the cap of a radio and a pen hovering over a log sheet that suddenly feels like a verdict. The world around you is a body of water in motion — everything that isn’t already wet is being pulled toward wetness. Salt and rust and the metallic tang of someone else’s fear make the air taste like old coins.

You can feel the previous chapters of your life compressing into this small hinge: the Commons’ wooden boardwalks under your boots, Rae’s stencil on the wall, Tommy’s rope-smell hands, the clinic’s faded blue cross. Memory and map and obligation press into you from every side.

Elias stands a pace behind you, shoulders cut against the rain. His coat is plastered to him, a dark silhouette with the pale glow of his tablet dim under the stormlight. He does not look away from you; he looks at you in a way that is impossible to parse into allegiance or accusation — complex, tired, and very human.

Elias Navarro: "Mara. Command needs authorization on my mark. We can launch the triage run now, or—" (He swallows against the wind.) "—we delay and risk roads becoming impassable in twenty minutes."

You hear Amina’s voice closer, clipped with clinical calm that is almost animal in its focus.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "The models—given observed surge velocity and the compromised berms—predict floodplain influx in eighteen to twenty-three minutes. If the clinic gets hit first, triage capacity drops by fifty percent in our network. The statistical imperative favors moving the clinic." (She steadies a rain-coated tablet with soiled fingers.) "This is about lives, Mara."

Rae’s voice cuts across, raw and immediate.

Rae Carter: "Aunt Rosa’s house sits below the old seawall. There’s no time for models when you’re hauling someone out of water, Mara. You want numbers—we have human bodies right here."

Tommy steps forward, and his face reads like a map of seasons. He does not raise his voice, but the very smallness of it presses.

Tommy: "We can’t let the clinic vanish, but we can’t take the launch if the pier’s going to rip. Someone has to watch the evac path. Someone has to keep the kids from running back for pets. Who do we leave behind when the engines start?"

You inhale. The rain finds the crack in your collar and threads down your spine. Your heart is not merely quickening; it is a percussion section. The urge to fix splits into a dozen hands — to run the numbers, to shout orders, to pick someone up and hold them. Each impulse feels both necessary and insufficient.

[Visual: Close on a red lifejacket, floating and tied to the pier with frayed twine; its color is a punctuation against slate water]
[Sound: Aboard the dinghy, water slaps like fingers against its hull; chains groan]

You look at Elias again. His face shifts. Not a simple expression. There is apology, there is urgency, there is the professional distance he has always used to keep choices legible. It is unreadable in the particular way of people who have been both ally and opposing counsel.

Elias Navarro: "If you authorize, I take lead. We head for the clinic, stabilize, return for neighborhood runs. If you don’t—" (He struggles for the next word; the wind eats it.) "—then we enact contingency B. Triage distributed across the Commons and Old Mill rooftop. It’s riskier, but it keeps teams dispersed."

Celeste’s voice comes from the periphery — sharp, engineered to cut fog and sympathy alike.

Celeste Park: "If you delay, the investor teams get nervous. If they pull funding mid-storm because the plan looks unstable, the promenade gets fast-tracked in a way none of you want. This is the precise moment accountability must look like decisive action, Mara."

Her coat is immaculate, unweathered by salt. She is not cold so much as polished, like a blade that has been oiled. Her words are not just policy; they are a promise with teeth.

Your chest tightens until speech becomes a thin wire. You try to listen to everyone at once and discover you can only hear them in shards: Amina’s models, Rae’s fear, Tommy’s practical grief, Celeste’s strategies. Elias’s hands are steady at his sides, and that steadiness is both a comfort and a means of sharpening the choice.

You could let the radio mute out everything and make for the boat yourself. You could authorize and surrender the Commons to others. You could call for a pause and watch the sea take its own answer. Each path seems to bear a price that will flare and never fully close.

You let the choice hang for only a moment longer than necessary. The storm does not grant patience.

Amina presses a hand to the laminated schematic she’s been holding, raindrops beading on it like tiny, imprisoning bowls.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "You know the math, Mara. The ethical calculus of triage—if the clinic survives, more people live in aggregate. If it doesn’t, the network buckles. I am telling you the utilitarian baseline: preserve the clinic."

Rae steps closer until the rain hits both your faces and makes two small, shared maps of wetness.

Rae Carter: "We own the Commons. We fought for this soil. That matters. They aren’t just tiles in an investor deck. If we keep everything centralized because someone’s spreadsheet says it’s optimal, we lose the part of this town that remembers people by name."

Tommy: "There’s no spreadsheet for Nora’s arthritis, Rae. There’s no model for the neighbor who can’t make it up a ladder. You want to talk ethics—let’s talk about the weight of what we can carry."

Celeste: "And if the town collapses because funding evaporates, who does it help that we kept a patch of mud? Economic viability preserves lives too, Mara. Someone has to see the forest of consequence and act." (She meets your gaze, a deliberate, flat challenge.)

Your mind races. Images flash — your sibling’s face in memory, a waterline on a neighbor’s door, the greenhouses you coaxed from rubble. The decisions you make here will be attached to those images forever, like names inked on something that will not wash away.

Elias steps closer now, bridging the gap between policy and person in a single motion.

Elias Navarro: "I will not sugarcoat it. If you authorize the lead call, you will make people’s lives safer in the immediate sweep. You will also make us liable to choices that come later. If you withhold, you solidify local control but you may be arguing with a storm that doesn’t care about moral clarity. I need you to pick, Mara."

His hand is inches from the radio in your fist. You can feel the warmth of him through the rain. It does something in your chest that is not simply politics.

The wind roars as if answering. Somewhere farther down the beach a structure groans and collapses into the tide — a long, streaming sound that punctures the air and makes every conversation around you thinner, more urgent.

Celeste’s voice becomes unexpectedly low.

Celeste Park: "We are deciding the town’s future in a storm, Mara. That is the kind of decisive moment investors look for. Decide now. Build trust by acting."

Amina’s voice is a scalpel.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Decisions made under duress are still decisions. Ethically, you must choose the one that preserves the most capacity to save lives now. That is the binding moral constraint."

Rae’s hands are shaking.

Rae Carter: "Ethics won't stop the water. People will. People with blankets, people with boats—people who know the street names. Do you trust us, Mara? Or do you trust a plan?"

Tommy: "Trust. Plans. Wind. It’s all the same when the boards start to lift. You have to put down one thing and lift another. Which is it going to be?"

You feel the arousal peak like an animal at the lip of a cliff — not contemplative but frantic, a pressure that demands release. Your breath is thin; each inhale tastes like metal and the small, clean burn of adrenaline. You can hear your pulse thumping in the hollow of the radio and you know, with a deep, physical certainty, that the moment you move your hand will set consequences in motion that cannot be rewound.

Elias watches you, the lines around his eyes accentuated by rain and fatigue. His fingers twitch toward a tablet, toward a map, toward a life saved or lost by minutes.

Elias Navarro: "We need the authorization, Mara. I will take responsibility for the execution. Tell me to go."

You lift your hand. The pen in your other hand rides the sheet like a compass needle.

[Visual: Rain lashes directly; a flash of lightning momentarily brightens every face; for an instant you can see the entire town in a frozen photograph of motion]
[Sound: A high, keening frequency — the siren converging with something metal tearing loose; it crescendos into a single, overwhelming note]
[Music: All instruments converge in a raw, crashing chord]

You feel the decision as a physical thing — a lever crossed with a heartbreak — and your skin crawls with the knowledge that anything you choose will be an answer that echoes.

Your thumb tightens on the radio switch.
