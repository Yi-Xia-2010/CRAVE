[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Dawn — hours after the storm]

[Visual: Boardwalks splintered; raised beds overturned like opened hands; mist and diesel smoke cling to marsh grass]
[Sound: Distant sirens, the low groan of water receding, someone hammering a makeshift stretcher]
[Music: Tense, high-tempo strings; percussion like a distant storm]

You wake to the taste of iron and salt. The Commons, the place you planted for futures, is a map of sudden losses: trellises snapped, seedlings buried in silt, a banner shredded into confetti. Mud forms a slow, greedy tide across the path. The wind still carries the electric stench of upended transformers.

Rae is on her knees by a collapsed hoop-house, sleeves rolled, fingers raw where glass bit her palm. Tommy yells orders in a voice that's half-command, half-anger, directing volunteers who move like a swarm fighting an undertow. Dr. Amina stands under the bent arch of a greenhouse frame, clipboard clutched but her eyes on the distance where the promenade used to be a line of lights — now a chaos of steel and foam.

You push past a board that gives underfoot and find Elias near the marsh edge, his jacket streaked with mud and salt. His wristband flashes a jittery thread of telemetry; his notebook is smeared with rain. He looks at you, then at the ruins, then at a picture taped inside his notebook — maybe a study, maybe a neighbor; the expression on his face is complicated and unreadable.

Elias: "We lost the northern berm. The skiff teams said the current took the east marker—" (his voice breaks; he tries to swallow it back) "—and the pumps failed at the docks."

You: The words land like cold water in your chest. You can feel every decision you've ever made — the meetings, the concessions — collecting weight as if to crush you. "How many—" your question stops; there's no clean number.

Elias: "We've accounted for most of the teams, but there are pockets we can't reach yet. The promenade—" (he swallows) "—they've reported structural collapse near the old pier. We're pulling what we can."

Tommy: (arriving with a volunteer) "We've got people trapped in the lower row houses on Keel Street. One family's roof carried off. We need more hands at the skiff launch."

Rae: "And press is starting to circle. They're—" (she looks at you, eyes alight with a raw, hungry fear) "—they want names and blame."

You: The Commons smells of compost, diesel, and someone crying. You can feel the adrenaline hammering under your ribs like a trapped bird. There is no time for ritual grief. There is only movement.

[Scene: Promenade | Mid-morning]

[Visual: Concrete slabs buckled and slick; the corporate banners half-torn, slogan flapping like a guilty flag]
[Sound: Distant shouting; a generator's relentless hum; a child's wail cut off by waves]
[Music: Sharp staccato strings; a relentless heartbeat rhythm under the mix]

When you reach the promenade, the scale of collapse hits like an incoming tide. The floodwall—where engineers and investors promised safety—lies in violent stutter: rebar like ribcages exposed, the shell split open. A length of promenade has sheared away; houses at the edge are swallowed to their foundations. The shoreline is a graveyard of furniture and signage.

Celeste stands under a half-standing marquee, immaculate despite the mud that kisses the hem of her coat. Her hair is the exact line it always is. She looks at the damage with the same clinical clarity you have seen at council hearings. When she sees you, her face unfurls into a steady mask—too composed to be comfort, too deliberate to be accidental.

Celeste: (hands folded) "We're cataloging structural failures now. There will be a full technical assessment. It's awful, Mara. Truly—" (her voice is precise; there's an earnestness there that doesn't reach her eyes)

You: Her words are like glass slid across a table. They reflect light, but they don't give warmth. You taste the heat of a finger pressed to a burn. "Awful doesn't fix the families on Keel Street," you say. Your voice is small compared to the ocean, and it shakes with a fury that is a kind of prayer.

Celeste: "No, but it helps begin the work of ensuring it doesn't happen again. If we—" (she is interrupted by a shout from the cluster of volunteers)

Volunteer: "They found someone—down by the pier. We need a medic."

Elias is at your shoulder before you can decide if his presence is refuge or another set of obligations. His jaw is tight; his hands are the hands of someone who wants to organize grief into tasks.

Elias: "If we can get the temporary bracing into place, we can shore the adjacent walkways. It buys us time for rescue."

You: (breathe) "Buy time. For who?"

Elias: "For people we can still save."

Celeste watches the exchange. Her mouth tightens into a line that might be regret. Her reaction is complex—public empathy folded into a calculus you cannot read. The crowd shifts, and a woman pushes forward, cradling a child's soaked teddy.

Woman: "Please! My husband—he's under the decking—"

Tommy moves like a living rope, threading through the chaos, and slaps his palm once against Celeste's policy binder.

Tommy: "We need more than binders. We need hands and skiffs." (his voice is raw) "You promised—"

Celeste: (measured) "I promised assessments, funding, and an expedited rebuild schedule. But first—"

You: The sentence dies as another roar breaks behind you. A bulkhead gives with a sound like a closing throat. People scatter. Aboard the nearest skiff, someone clutches a limp body. The world tilts like a painting ripped from a wall.

[Scene: Various Neighborhoods & Reconstruction Sites | Afternoon — the town stitches itself in jagged seams]

[Visual: Makeshift triage tents, shivering families huddled under plastic sheeting, a string of candles on a broken stoop]
[Sound: Low drone of helicopters; the barked orders of relief coordinators; the sustained keening of a chorus of sirens]
[Music: Violin tremolo rising into an almost unbearable pitch; brass clashing intermittently]

Bodies of work pile up—literal and figurative. Volunteers move like constellations around pinpoints of disaster. You hand out spare blankets, point people to compasses when their phones are dead, cradle a boy who will not stop repeating his sister's name.

Dr. Amina is methodical under the tent, voice steady as a scalpel. She lays out triage lines, counts supplies with a precision that sounds like ritual.

Dr. Amina: "We hold triage here. People with breathing difficulty here. Hypothermia here. We consolidate water and ration the generators."

Rae flings herself between the crowd and the press, camera forgotten, paint-smeared hands clapping to get attention.

Rae: (bitter) "They'll make it about policy and dollars again. This is faces, Mara. These are people you know."

You: You think of the signatures on petitions, the late-night council debates. They seem small against the wet weight of a child's soaked stuffed fox. You feel rage—hot, immediate, dangerous—and grief—an acid that eats the edges off everything.

A man approaches with a small wooden box. Inside, wrapped in a plastic bag, are photographs and a soggy wedding band. He places it in your hands like an offering and says nothing. Your fingers close on the metal; it is warm from someone else's skin. You feel the past—every plan, every hope—become a physical ache.

Elias returns, breathless, arguing with a coordinator over radio frequencies. His voice is taut.

Elias: "We need coordinated lift from county. If the county doesn't prioritize our grid, people die overnight. We can't—" (he cuts himself off, eyes searching yours) "—Mara, can you push for the priority call? You know the allocation protocols."

You: There it is again: the hinge. You have authority here, moral and practical. You feel the memory of the marker's ink blot, the marker in your hand, the moment you spoke orders. Every decision you made funnels into this second-by-second urgency.

Tommy: (who's been quiet, his face a map of salt-rimmed anger) "They keep saying 'we'll rearrange resources'—we're not chess pieces, Mara. People are drowning in the gaps."

Celeste glides past the tent with a clipboard and a medic, her lips moving in a whisper that could be apology or negotiation. You catch fragments: "rebuild... insurance... expedited permits..." Her mouth shapes words meant to stitch futures out of broken things, but you see the raw edges where promises meet money and land.

Elias: "I've drafted a bridge plan that integrates living shoreline buffers—it's a hybrid. It won't be instant, but—"

You: "But people are sleeping in gymnasiums tonight." Your voice is thin with exhaustion and something sharper than that. "A bridge plan helps in five years and doesn't answer the lanterns on porches that won't light tomorrow."

Elias: He takes a breath so deep his shoulders lift. "I know. I'm trying to hold both. I'm trying to keep people safe now and keep them safe later." For a fraction of a second, vulnerability flares across his face and then shutters closed: his professional mask slaps back into place.

Dr. Amina: (softly) "Mara, Elias, finger on the present. We triage this: tonight, and the long view next. You're both necessary." Her eyes are steady. "But tonight—now—we need the unified call for mutual aid."

Your chest is an animal trapped in a cage. You can imagine the maps, the meeting minutes, the lawyers. You can also imagine a child losing a name tonight. The two images are a pair of knives.

Rae: "People are starting to talk about suing; people are whispering 'who signed what' and it's like they're trying to find someone to hit." Her voice cracks. "I don't know how to stop it."

Tommy: (bitter laugh) "You could start by telling the truth."

Celeste: (appearing at the tent edge once more) "Telling the truth doesn't fix rubble. It doesn't rebuild a foundation. It doesn't pay relocation costs."

You: The words are granite. You want to tear them down. You want to take her binder and set it alight. But you know fury alone will not mend what the tide took. You also know that silence is a rotten thing that breeds the kind of quiet that eats towns whole.

[Scene: Impromptu Memorial & Council Tent | Late Afternoon — the town in a single breath]

[Visual: A circle of candles, a string of shoes, a plywood board with names tacked to it; over it all, a sky bruised into early evening]
[Sound: The murmur of conversation falling into a hush; the clink of a tin cup passing; distant thunder like a held warning]
[Music: All instruments taut; a single cello line rising into an almost unbearable pitch]

They press you to the center. Someone hands you a damp sheet of paper — a list of names, a litany. You realize with a physical jolt that some names you know, some you don't. A child's name is folded into the line. You feel your throat tighten like a trap closing.

Elias stands beside you, not behind you, but near enough that you can read the tension in his hands. His face is... complicated. Not ally, not enemy. An angle of code and a human being folded into the same line. You don't know what that will mean in the days to come. You only know that now, at this bleeding edge, his presence is a small raft.

Rae climbs the platform, voice shaking but fierce.

Rae: "They're going to ask for someone to lead the rebuilding effort. For names to be called. For someone to say what comes next and why."

Crowd voice: "Who will trust the promises now?"

Tommy: "Who will be held to account?"

Celeste stands at the edge, cataloguing faces. Her counting eyes find yours and, for the first time since the morning, there is a line of something close to sorrow around her mouth. Her expression remains complex — unreadable enough to be dangerous.

Dr. Amina: (quietly, to you) "You can call for mutual aid. You can demand immediate emergency housing. You can demand auditing and oversight. Any of those will change the way rebuilding looks." Her hands flatten on your shoulders with the steadiness of a clinician. "But only you can say it in a way this crowd will hear."

You: The radio is in your ear; your hands still smell of salt. Memory folds back to the marker's ink, to the radio button you pressed, to the promises and compromises you made. Each one is a stone on a path. Each one has a weight.

Elias: (low) "Mara—"

You turn to him. There is a thousand-voice storm inside his look that matches your own. He opens his mouth, then closes it. The space between words is a place where so many choices and regrets live. The crowd waits as if sound were shelter.

Your memory offers you three futures like knives glinting in the low light: a compromise that might stitch the town back together around new walls; a fight that might save the Commons but cost people dearly; a surrender to development that smooths short-term pain but steals the town's long memory. You can hear Celeste's measured promises and Tommy's raw demands. You can see Dr. Amina's steady calculations and Rae's desperate, burning truth.

Your fingers find the microphone. It is cold, humming with borrowed power. You put your mouth to it and feel the world flatten to the shape of the next sentence.

For a single heartbeat everything hangs: the crowd, the sea, the faces of those you've failed and those you might yet save. The strings of the day snap taut, and the pitch of the moment rises until it is nearly beyond the capacity of sound to hold.

You inhale.

You are about to speak.
