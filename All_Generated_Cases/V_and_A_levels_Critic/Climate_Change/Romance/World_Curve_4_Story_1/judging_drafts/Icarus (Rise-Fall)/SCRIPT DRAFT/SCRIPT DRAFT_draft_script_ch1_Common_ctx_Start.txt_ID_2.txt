You step onto the boardwalk with the careful rhythm of someone who has walked these planks a thousand times. The wood smells of salt and old rain; a fine film of algae makes the edges slick beneath your boots. The wind pulls at the loose hem of your jacket and flings a spray of cool, briny air against your face.

Your hands—slightly rough from yesterday’s work—move by habit, checking tool straps, smoothing soil from a seed tray in your satchel. Around you the Saltwren Commons breathes in small, ordinary ways: a kettle clinks in the greenhouse, a child’s kite tangles briefly in the reeds before an adult plucks it free, and condensation fogs a mason jar used for lunch.

You keep the map of the place in your head: the herb beds by the old buoy, the oyster racks in the shallow pools, the narrow tide line where you taught school kids to spot crabs. The Commons is a ledger of people as much as plants—names stitched into compost bins and driftwood benches. It is also the thing you wake for. Not drama, not protest speeches—this daily tending.

Tomas is there, bent over a spool of line near the east bench, a thermos by his knee. He glances up, sun-creased smile folding in. His hands are full of the same patient economy that runs through small towns—mend what’s torn, patch what’s broken.

Tommy: "Mara. You’d think the tide’d give us a bit of a break this week, but no—haven’t seen this much silt in ages."
Mara Lin: "It shifts faster every season. That’s the trouble—nothing settles the way it used to." (You shrug, hauling a soil tray up onto the boardwalk.) "How’s the south run? Nets holding up?"
Tommy: "Aye. For now. But I got three folks at the harbor talkin' 'bout layoffs if that promenade goes through. Celeste’s banners are up again."

He says her name with a pragmatic flatness—no venom, just a fact. The banners have been in the news, on noticeboards, threaded into community conversations like a persistent tide.

Tommy: "You hear anything new? Rae was buzzing about a meeting at Town Hall tonight."
Mara Lin: "I heard the same. I’ve been trying to keep tonight clear." (You find yourself smoothing your hands across the clay of a raised bed—an old, grounding gesture.) "We should be ready to speak about the buffer plantings, the marsh profiles. The engineers will present their models."

Tommy chuckles, a dry sound that belies the worry in his eyes.
Tommy: "Models don’t feed the kids next month. But… we’ll make our case. You do it better than anyone."

There’s comfort—thin, steady—in his confidence. It anchors you, but it doesn’t answer the quiet question that’s been under everything lately: what if compromise looks different than you expect?

Rae arrives in a burst of teal hair and paint-splattered knees, carrying a roll of stenciled fabric. She walks the Commons like a high-wattage bulb—always a project, always pressurized with urgency.

Rae Carter: "Morning, Mara! Look at this—'Remember the Marsh.' We’re mounting it on the east fence. People stop. They stare. It works."
Mara Lin: "Good—visuals hit harder than numbers sometimes." (You take the roll, fingers skimming over the stencil.) "Are you set for Town Hall? You and the youth table?"
Rae Carter: "Got flyers, got chants, got a playlist that’ll haunt their PowerPoints." (She grins, then grows serious.) "Also—FYI—there’s a rep from the cooperative in town. Elias Navarro. Came with Dr. Bhatt’s group, apparently. He’s supposed to be on site today."

You register the name—Elias Navarro—before the face. You’ve read the cooperative briefings; you’ve met people like him in forums and field visits. The name lands like a neutral stone in water: it ripples expectation without bruit.

Rae Carter: "He was asking to see the Commons. Said he wanted to understand 'community needs' before the proposal goes public. Classic line, but… maybe he actually listens? Maybe he doesn't. Either way, he’s headed this way."

Your heart doesn’t jump. You have an economy of responses to outsiders: invite them in, set boundaries, ask for evidence, and make sure the town is centered. That approach has kept projects honest and people safe. Today, the tide and the light and the smell of compost keep the moment steady—no dramatic swell, just the slow gathering before a meeting.

The small choice flattens some of the morning’s static, and when you look up again, there is a man standing at the outer boardwalk near the tide line. He is not intrusive; he carries a field notebook and a lean posture tuned to observation. His clothes are tidy, sleeves rolled, a tablet tucked under his arm. Rainlight catches at the rim of his glasses.

Elias Navarro: "Morning. I’m Elias. Sorry to interrupt. I was told this is where I could—" (He gestures out at the marsh in a way that is both careful and open.) "—see the work you all do. I don’t want to step on anything."

You measure him the same way you measure new soil—by touch and by light. There’s nothing in him that is overtly corporate-slick; he has the patient tone of someone used to walking people through slides and diagrams. He looks like he wants to be useful.

Mara Lin: "Useful is a good start." (Your voice is even. You keep the satchel strap tight. You watch his hands—clear, steady gestures.) "We don’t have long. The Commons is a tangle of commitments and people’s livelihoods."
Elias Navarro: "I understand. I’m here because our cooperative is trying to learn how to work with towns before anything formal is proposed. I wanted to see the buffers, talk about tidal flows—if that’s okay. I can also—if you prefer—make time at Town Hall tonight. I know these things are...public."

He says public with a small, careful emphasis. His eyes flick toward the greenhouse, to Tommy and Rae, reading the room—an engineer reading a landscape.

Mara Lin: "Private conversations can move faster. Public ones make sure the whole room is heard." (You fold the options in your head like a map—a private path or the main street.) "What does your cooperative want from Solhaven, exactly?"

Elias pauses, and for a heartbeat you watch him consider the clarity of his answer. He could reel off models and funding streams, pitch diagrams and timelines. Instead, he chooses a simpler honesty.

Elias Navarro: "We want to design systems that last. That includes infrastructure and nature-based approaches—and a real seat for people who have lived here through changes. I’m not here to tell you what to accept. I’m here to listen, and to share what we can do."

Tommy bristles, then relaxes. Rae folds her arms, eyes bright with suspicion and hope in equal measure.

Tommy: "That sounds fine on paper. But you know developers say a lot of things on paper."
Elias Navarro: "I know. And I know trust is built in messy ways—site visits, revisions, returning when things go wrong. I don’t expect to earn it by saying the right things."

There’s a slow, human cadence to his words. He is careful not to promise what he cannot deliver. That restraint reads as competence; competence reads as a potential partner—or a smoother opponent.

Rae Carter: "Town Hall tonight is going to be loud. We’ll be there." (She points at you.) "Mara’s likely to lead the Commons’ statement. You should listen to that first, if you really want to understand."

Elias gives you a look that is neither solicitous nor defensive—just curious, as if you were a data point he wanted to comprehend beyond numbers.

Elias Navarro: "I’d like that. I can wait until the forum. Or—if you prefer—if you have time now, I can walk the tide line with you. I want to see the marsh from your point of view."

Your throat is oddly dry with the small weight of a decision that feels larger in feeling than in consequence. It’s not a storm; it’s not one of those high-conflict council nights. It’s the quiet imperative that lives at the hinge of everyday choices: prioritize the intimate conversation where you might shape a proposal directly, or hold to the public process that shows the town you stand with them.

You consider the boardwalk—Tommy’s steady presence, Rae’s defiant energy, the kids who will be at Town Hall to sing a song about the marsh. You consider Elias’ steady, patient gaze and the way he framed listening as work.

Your internal checklist flips through logistics and principles: fairness, effectiveness, visibility, personal bandwidth. There is no obvious, moral 'right'—only priorities and how you want to embody them.

Rae Carter: "Whatever you pick, make sure it’s on purpose. Don’t let them set the pace for you."
Tommy: "Aye. Speak your mind, Mara. But don’t get boxed into anything you can’t walk back from."

You breathe. The Commons hums low around you, its small, faithful noises like a community heartbeat. The tide whispers; the seed trays glint. The choice sits simple and exact in front of you.
