[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Morning]
[Visual: Close on your phone screen; a headline blurs into a dozen unread notifications]
[Sound: Persistent notification pings, distant gulls, a low rumble of a generator]

Your phone wakes you before the sun does. The lock screen is a mosaic of banners and avatar icons you half-know: reporters, community lists, a forwarded thread from Rae with a single line—"They ran it." Your thumb swipes; the headline pushes into your chest.

Contract leak: Solhaven development plans exposed; civic uproar as clauses reveal relocation terms and gag provisions.

You taste metal. The air is damp with last night's rain and the compost heap behind the Commons exhales something sweet and sour. You should feel vindicated—information is out, shadow in daylight—but the first wave is a sharpening ache: what did you expect? Vindication has an abrasive edge when it's not only the town that gets splintered by truth.

Rae is already at the boardwalk when you reach the Commons, hair wind-swept, camera slung like a bandolier. She meets you with two breaths worth of fury and no questions.

Rae Carter: "You see this? They put our names into the narrative like we're petty saboteurs. They spun relocation as charity—do they really think people will forget the orders behind it?"

You: "They're calling for oversight, not erasure." (Your voice sounds smaller than you feel.) "What's the mood?"

Rae scoffs, fingers going to a spray can still warm from last night's stencil run. Her energy is a coiled wire.

Rae Carter: "Mood? Explosive. The forums are lit, the fishing channels are calling us sellouts, and there's already a national piece queued. They painted it as corporate philanthropy. We need to push back before the spin becomes law."

You look toward the Promenade horizon; the chain-link on Celeste's site catches a gull's wing-shadow and breaks it into jagged light. You did this because you couldn't bear the clauses that tied people's movements to developer timelines, because silence felt like consent. Now the world is louder than you imagined.

[Scene: Town Hall (Council Chamber) | Afternoon]
[Visual: The parquet floor reflects a thousand camera flashes like stuttering rain; folding chairs press with bodies and noise]
[Sound: Murmur rinsing into shouted fragments; the snap of microphones being tested]
[Music: High strings, brittle and taut]

The council chamber is a kettle ready to sing. People who have loved Solhaven and people whose livelihoods hinge on the Promenade press shoulder-to-shoulder. The press line snakes like a second audience: lenses, tripods, a portable light that smells faintly of burnt plastic. The air is recycled and taut with the scent of coffee and wet coats.

Dr. Amina Bhatt is set up with a projector and sediment maps; her hands are steady but faster than usual. She meets your eyes with a look that's both apology and instruction.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "There are legal consequences to leaks—we understand that. But there are also scientific responsibilities. The data must be contextualized. Please—when you speak, make sure the focus is on the impacts, not the headlines."

You: "I won't let them boil this into a personality contest." (You haven't decided if that's determination or deflection.)

Celeste arrives in a neutral trench and a smile that can be weatherproof. Her arrival quiets something in the room—the scent of her perfume is clinical, lily and citrus that masks the salt. She steps onto the dais like someone entering an auditorium built for her.

Celeste Park: (measured) "Transparency is essential, and I'm open to oversight. But I will not allow misinformation to jeopardize crucial safety measures."

Her lawyer, a flat-voiced man with a tie the color of stormwater, slides a copy of an injunction draft across the table—an aggressive shield that reads like insurance. Murmurs ripple. Someone shouts that the town deserves to know who benefits.

Elias appears at the back, notebook clutched. He moves toward you and Dr. Bhatt with an urgency that makes his shoulders look narrower than usual.

Elias Navarro: "Mara—" (he starts, then stops, as if the word is too heavy with accusation and hope) "We can frame this. We can push for an independent review, mediation, some legal oversight that stops construction but doesn't leave people in the lurch."

You hear the tremor under his calm. He wants to fix the hinge, to engineer a measured emergency. But the public is already a furnace; cooled metal bends under that heat.

You: "Independent review needs teeth. Binding commitments. No gag clauses. Not after this." (Your hands won't stop moving—mapping invisible boundaries on your knee.)

Elias: "I know." (He exhales, then goes softer.) "I know you do. Help me keep it from collapsing into... politics."

His fingers brush the edge of a pamphlet. It's an almost-touch meant to anchor something between you. You take it; your skin stays buzzing long after.

[Scene: Town Hall (Council Chamber) | Early Evening]
[Visual: The dais under cold lights; a projector flickers between slides that show clauses in magnified text]
[Sound: The room coalesces into a single loud organism—breathing, whispering, ready to roar]
[Music: Full orchestra—chaotic strings, brass stabbing like questions]

The hearing begins. Celeste's counsel launches into a legalistic defense, each sentence a sealed stone. They argue harm—delays, lost funding—and paint the leak as malicious interference. A lawyer from a national rights group stands to argue for public interest; his voice resonates with precedent, with injunctions that have toppled corporations.

Dr. Bhatt takes the stand, her voice firm as she unspools sediment cores and projected timelines. She explains the ecological data in plain English, then stumbles when asked to opine on policy.

Council Member: "Are you recommending halting construction until further review?"

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "From an ecological standpoint, precaution is warranted. The modeling suggests that without adaptive measures integrated from the start, certain neighborhoods will face increased risk within a decade."

From the audience, a man shouts that models don't feed mouths. A woman weeps softly. The room fractures into competing human truths.

Celeste, poised on the edge of legal combative and public charm, leans forward.

Celeste Park: "We are willing to enter oversight. But we cannot be held hostage by sensational leaks that destabilize investor trust. Our job is to deliver safety and work with the town."

You want to ask—what about binding protections for residents? What about enforceable relocation plans that aren't tied to displacement? But the world has become not your living room conversation but a courtroom of cameras, a ledger where nuance is a liability.

Elias rises, and his testimony is quiet but edged.

Elias Navarro: "If construction pauses without guarantees in place, people who need immediate protection could be left exposed. We need a moratorium with clear legal guardrails—an independent review board, emergency funding release if displacement is necessary, and legal protection for peaceful protest."

He lays out the scaffolding like an engineer: steps, oversight, contingency. His words are practical, but the crowd hears them as compromise—a bridge or a betrayal, depending on which side of the room they sit.

Rae interrupts—impatience as moral clarity.

Rae Carter: "We've sat at tables too long. The town needs real, enforceable power over decisions that affect where they live!"

Her voice is a blade; it cuts the room into two. Cameras swiveled, someone yells "Order!" The mayor bangs a gavel and it sounds like a starting pistol.

[Sound: A distant siren crescendos; the murmurs become a swarm]
[Music: Dissonant chord held—the highest, most uncomfortable pitch]

Then: a lawyer for Celeste stands and drops a phrase like a detonator—"unlawful dissemination." The room tilts; reporters sharpen like knives. A national outlet flashes the phrase across its ticker: "Alleged Leak Could Warrant Lawsuit." The phrase spreads, seeding dread.

You feel a weight in your hands—your coat pocket, the place where once you'd kept notes. There's a sudden paranoid flutter: who has the other copies? Who will be prosecuted? Who will become a scapegoat?

Elias looks at you. His eyes are a map of unspoken consequences.

Elias Navarro: "We need to prepare for litigation. Dr. Bhatt, will you support an evidentiary review? Rae, can you document chain of custody? Mara—"

He stops, because what he wants to ask is how much you're willing to put on the line.

Your chest tightens into a focal point. The room's noise funnels through that seam in you and sharpens. You remember the sibling again, and the smell of wet rope and raw wood after the storm. The memory is a cold pin that steadies and unmakes you in equal measure.

You can feel the pressure focusing like weather into a single front. Papers shuffle like distant thunder. Outside, someone begins to chant and it swells and then collapses into the room through the open window.

No one can say what will happen next with absolute certainty. The legal machinery can freeze progress. It can also fracture trust, tether money to lawsuits, and pull resources away from immediate protections. The national cameras will cast whatever outcome into a larger narrative that may not care about the particular faces behind the headlines.

Celeste's counsel slides a motion for injunctive relief across the table—an attempt to pause construction and file suit for damages attributed to the leak. A protracted, expensive deadlock looms.

Dr. Bhatt looks at you again, a softness at the corners of her eyes that you have rarely seen.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Whatever happens, anchor the town to process—not to personalities. Evidence, documentation, due process. We have to make sure the science and the people are both heard."

You think: process can be armor, but sometimes armor is slow. You think: truth can starve politics of pretense, but it can also leave your neighbors vulnerable while the courts argue.

Elias folds his hands together in a small, almost prayerful tension.

Elias Navarro: "If the council votes tonight... it will determine whether the site is halted, overseen, or cleared for work under new constraints. The next steps are legal, technical, and intensely public. We need to brace."

His voice goes thin with the weight of "intensely public."

You look at the dais; cameras close like eyes preparing to blink. The mayor taps the gavel once—soft, and then again—this time with all the gravity of a tide turning.

The hearing is the hinge. The leak has opened a wound that will be tended in courtrooms, in national editorials, in alleys between neighbors. You can feel the weather coming—literal and legal.
