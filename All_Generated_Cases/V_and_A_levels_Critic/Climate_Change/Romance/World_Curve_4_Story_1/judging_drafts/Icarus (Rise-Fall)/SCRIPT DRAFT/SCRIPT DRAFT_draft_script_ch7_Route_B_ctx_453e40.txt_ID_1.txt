You chose to meet Celeste privately. The choice that felt reasonable in the stairwell — a private negotiation instead of a public confrontation — now presses against your sternum in real time. The Town Hall conference room is colder than you expected; the coffee carafe at the end of the table steams in a chipped mug. Paper rustles when you shift.

Celeste Park sits at the far end of the table with the precision of someone who has scheduled and rescheduled outcomes. Her trench coat is folded across the back of the chair, heel of one boot tapping a metronome you can feel even before she speaks. A slim black portfolio lies open in front of her. The logo on her lapel catches the light and throws a small, clinical glint across the table.

Mara Lin: "You told me this would be candid."

Celeste Park: (smiles, not warm but not unkind) "Candid is efficient. You brought a good reputation for rigor, Mara. That makes you useful." (She taps a printed set of pages toward you; the paper clicks against the table.) "We're offering a full-scale promenade, built to modern flood standards, plus a relocation fund for those most at risk. We cap commercial taxes for ten years; we charter community oversight panels to advise on plantings and market space."

Your fingers hover over the edge of the packet. The print smells faintly of ink and new leather. The words on the page are designed to land like anchors.

You let the facts line up in your head — money, infrastructure, relocation aid. The town can use them. The saltwren beds, the families on low terraces, the school by the inlet — they all tilt in the wind of this one offer.

Celeste Park: (leaning forward) "In return, the cooperative asks for reasonable limits on demonstrations in construction zones, and engineering latitude to expedite approved designs. Fast-tracking reduces risk overall. We can also bring Elias on board as a technical liaison. He has the expertise — he can help bridge your local knowledge with our timeline."

The room tilts. Celeste's mention of Elias lands with an electric, complicated charge. You can hear the rain louder for a moment, as if the world is holding its breath with you.

[Sound: The soft squeak of a chair as Elias steps into frame; his coat mutters as he removes it.]
[Visual: Elias stands in the doorway, tablet clutched to his chest; his face is half in shadow, half in lamplight.]

Elias Navarro: "You didn't tell me this would be a private offer." (His voice is steady, but there's a tightness at the edges. He sets the tablet on the table and opens it — a schematic glows, neutral and categorized.)

Mara Lin: "You knew we were meeting Celeste."

Elias Navarro: (measured) "You said you wanted to try negotiation before the hearing. I came because it felt—" (he searches for the right phrase) "—strategic to keep technical people in the room."

Celeste Park: (to Elias) "We thought you might like to lead the pilot phase. Visibility, influence, and oversight. A joint appointment — technical director for the promenade's retrofit phase. Compensation will be commensurate; the cooperative wants local leadership invested in the outcome."

Elias looks at the portfolio, then at you. His expression holds something close — a flicker of pride, perhaps, and also calculation. The look is complex; the kind that could mean gladness, alarm, or a reluctance he hasn't decided how to name.

You feel a sharp warmth behind your ribs — not anger, not wholly relief, but a complicated hope. An offer that folds Elias into the plan could build bridges between your mandates. It could also tie him to the cooperative's timeline in ways that matter far beyond his résumé.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (arriving with a small stack of field notes, her voice steady and anchored) "I recommend caution. The engineering latitude clause is broad. Without binding legal language, 'oversight' can become advisory, and 'relocation' definitions vary wildly. We need enforceable milestones, environmental covenants, and independent auditing."

Her presence steadies you; Amina's timbre is the sort that turns anxiety into checklists and mitigations. She has the authority of sediment cores and late-night lab runs; she looks to Elias, then to you.

Elias Navarro: (soft) "I can draft a phased engagement plan. Binding checkpoints, transparent sensors, community veto points coded into the contract." (He speaks quickly now, energized.) "We can deploy tide sensors, tie funding release to ecological benchmarks, and define relocation eligibility with the social services office. I don't want to be a figurehead."

Celeste Park: (tilts her head) "We considered that. But legal constraints slow deployment. We can include milestones, yes, but we won't accept unilateral veto authority — that risks gridlock."

Her words sharpen. The music lifts: a staccato sequence that matches the quickening in your pulse.

You sense the stakes loud and immediate. The promenade could save livelihoods and shore up the tax base; it could also erase wetlands if decisions are ceded in the wrong hands. Relocation funds could mean shelter for the most vulnerable — or they could become bureaucratic tokens if the definition is loose. The tension is not abstract; it has names and addresses and faces, and it presses against the map tucked in your satchel.

Celeste Park: (her voice cool, persuasive) "We won't let this languish on principle. We're offering speed and certainty. Limits on protests in construction zones are standard in every city I've worked with. It's not suppression — it's safety. Give us a workable path."

Your throat tightens. Safety framed as restriction. You see the Commons in your mind: boardwalks under construction, seedlings wrestled into place, Rae crouched with a paintbrush, Tommy hauling nets. The image makes you resent the idea that vibrancy must be quieted to be legitimized.

You push a breath out through teeth you didn't know were clenched and realize you are, despite the pressure, quietly energized. There is a route here, messy and compromised, but it has shape. That feels like progress.
