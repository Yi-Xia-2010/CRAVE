[Scene: Conference Room | Morning]
[Visual: Long table, legal folders stacked on one end; rain-slick windows showing a pewter sea]
[Music: Pummeling percussion under a rising string ostinato — urgent, insistent]
[Sound: Paper shuffling, distant construction horns, a phone ping]

You walk into the room with the taste of salt on your tongue and the taste of the last rooftop conversation still in your throat. The scaled deal sits on the table like a small, bright island — pages clipped, clauses underlined, a negotiated shoreline between what you wanted and what Celeste could, and would, promise.

Celeste Park is already there. She is all measured light and tailored calm, the perfect outline of certainty. Elias stands beside you, fingers curled on his tablet as if it were a lifeline. Dr. Amina Bhatt carries two hardbound reports and the weight of a thousand measured words in her shoulders.

The air is tight and electric. Reporters will want quotes. Contractors will want signatures. The cooperative's funding window is a clock with its hands moving faster than you would like.

Mara (you): "So the co-managed wetland cells are explicit in section two?" Your voice is sharper than you intend; the word 'explicit' tastes like armor.

Celeste: "Yes. Co-management language is included — operational oversight, shared maintenance schedule, and a capital reserve for ecological monitoring." (Her pen taps the margin.) "We provide the upfront underwrite, the cooperative retains governance in the initial five-year term with periodic audits."

Elias: "And the pilot expansion? The hybrid layout that integrates living cells with the promenade's structural base — you backed that in the memo."

Celeste: (nods, measured) "We did. Provided we can ensure liability protections, and that certain thresholds of structural integrity are met."

Amina sets a thin binder down, the edge of paper catching the light. "Those thresholds are what worries me," she says. "At the promenade edges where hardscape meets living cell, we see differential scour and potential for undercutting. If the design isn't adjusted — if we don't arrest localized erosion — the cells could fail under high tide loads."

You feel the room tilt — not from accusation, but from the sudden focusing of all the practicals. The deal is both a victory and a doorway that opens onto a list of new problems.

Elias: "Can we add performance-based contingencies? A monitoring-triggered retrofit clause? If Amina's sensors report scour of X centimeters over Y weeks, funding reallocates to reinforcement work."

Celeste: (a thin smile) "We can negotiate triggers, but they can't be open-ended. Investors require predictability. If liability activation is too frequent, we lose our underwriting capacity."

You inhale. The trade-off sharpens like a blade: safety vs. the funding that will let your wetlands exist beyond a pilot sketch. Your pulse is a drumline in your ears.

Celeste watches both of you as if cataloguing options. Her steel-gray eyes are sharp but not angry; she knows how to sell confidence. You know how to hold it to the light.

Celeste: "I want this built. I want it to show as a replicable model. But scale without structure is fantasy. We fund, we require deliverables. The cooperative gets governance levers for ecological care — not for halting the project on a whim."

You: "Not on a whim. On demonstrable risk."

Elias: "We can formalize what 'demonstrable' means." (He meets your eyes, then Celeste's.) "Amina's sensors, a staged reinforcement schedule — data triggers that both protect the cells and protect your underwriting."

Amina interjects, brisk and merciless with precision. "We have a two-week window before the contractor's mobilization plan locks in channel fills. If we run additional stress tests now, we'll need to change sequencing. That risks the funding window Celeste mentioned."

Your hands curl into fists for a heartbeat. The choice is no longer theoretical: decisions today push boots and cranes and poured concrete into the place you've been cultivating with your own hands.

Celeste: "I'm willing to accept a revision to the monitoring clause. But I need a firm timeline: either you sign within seventy-two hours with the amended schedule and phased reinforcements, or the investors will reallocate. There are other towns."

Elias: (softly) "We don't have seventy-two hours to undetectably test every interface, Amina's right. But we might have enough to prioritize the highest-risk edges."

You taste iron. Hope hums under the tension — a feverish, relentless energy. This scaled deal is real. It is imperfect. It is possibly the first thing that will actually stop the slide of funding away from Solhaven.

[Scene: Promenade | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Survey flags flogging on a flat, exposed shore; construction trailers in the background; mud and rebar shimmer]
[Sound: The high whine of survey equipment; gulls cutting the air; distant, rolling thunder]
[Smell: Wet concrete, seaweed, diesel; touch: cold spray on your forearm]

You step onto the promenade with Amina and Elias trailing in a tight, urgent line. The promenade is a scar that promises healing; rusted rebar and rebar stumps are teeth in the shoreline. Workers talk in clipped bursts that match your mounting heartbeat.

Amina kneels at the edge and probes with a steel rod. "See this layer?" she says. "It's peat over coarse sand — capable of holding plant roots, yes, but highly vulnerable to a scouring current that will develop at the hard-soft interface."

Elias crouches to look, fingers finding a crack where concrete meets wet earth. "If we add edge aprons and stepwise revetments keyed into engineered root balls," he says, "we reduce shear stress. It buys time for marsh establishment."

A contractor approaches, boots clanking. "We were given the go-ahead for phase one base pours next week," he says. "Paperwork's in and permits look green. Unless—"

You glance at the horizon. A storm is threading itself into the sky; the clouds are gathering faster than they should. Your chest is a drum you cannot silence.

Amina: "Permits won't protect young cells from immediate hydrodynamic stress. If the contractor proceeds without reinforcement, the first surge could undermine the cells before seedlings even take root."

Celeste's voice comes on your phone, crisp. "We've agreed on a revised schedule. Sign, and we lock support. Delay, and I'm afraid the window closes. This is the last call, Mara."

You feel the landscape compress around you: the promenade, the funding, your people's livelihoods. The very air seems to urge faster decisions.

Elias: (urgent, to you) "We can draft a narrowed retrofit plan right now — a triage of edges to reinforce pre-pour. It won't be perfect, but it's better than waiting for total redesign."

Amina: "Triage requires materials and additional day rates. That cuts into Celeste's fixed budget and the investor's model. We circle back to the same tradeoff."

The wind picks up and tastes of metal. The very idea of postponement feels like mayhem for funding; the idea of signing without fixes feels like mayhem for habitat. Your throat is raw.

[Scene: Old Mill Rooftop | Late Afternoon — Golden Hour cracking into stormlight]
[Visual: The rooftop's salvaged tiles, hanging planters rocking; salt spray high, a thin line of sun still slicing the horizon]
[Music: Strings frenetically building, horns like alarms; tempo quickens to near breathless]
[Sound: Wind keening, the distant rumble of a generator, urgent murmurs]
[Touch: Rope-ladder creak beneath your palm; a cold metal railing against your palm]

You climb the rope-ladder to the rooftop like a ritual, like an ascent toward a single point that will decide a dozen lives. The town lies stretched below, small and stubborn. The promenade will cut a new line along that shore, and you can already see — in your mind's eye — where hard concrete will meet soft marsh, and where your plants must hold.

Elias follows without ceremony. He does not look unreadable here; he looks raw and honest and exhausted. His hand finds yours for a moment, quick and warm.

Elias: (low) "This is the fork. If we push now for immediate fixes, we stall. If we delay disclosure, we risk trust. If we accept compromise, we keep momentum but accept imperfect defenses."

Celeste arrives, unexpectedly unguarded. She stands apart, the model of composure frayed only by the urgency in her lines. "You have leverage," she says. "We didn't drive you to this table because it's easy." (She looks at you, the city, the sea.) "We did it because this town matters."

Amina, bundled against the wind, lays out her printouts on a mason jar table. "I've run worst-case scenarios and faster-erosion projections given the proposed sequencing. The edges flagged as 'moderate risk' could fail under a single major surge if we don't implement reinforcement within weeks."

Your heartbeat is a high, bright metronome. The roof creaks. Below you, people are making plans that will not wait for perfect alignment.

You: "We can accept funding and the co-management—and then do the technical work—if we guarantee prioritized reinforcement in the earliest phase." Your voice is something you shape carefully. "But we need enforceable language."

Celeste: (sharp) "Enforceable language that allows you to veto contractor replacements on five-day notice is not acceptable to investors."

Elias: "It doesn't have to be a veto. A neutral third-party engineer empowered to pause mobilization for safety triggers is workable."

Celeste's jaw tightens. "That expands cost risk. I can offer an escrow specifically for emergent reinforcement, and a rotating oversight panel, but not a unilateral pause."

Amina: "Escrow with rapid-access funds would do the immediate triage. But only if the triggers and the response time are both explicit. The engineering resources must be identified and committed before mobilization."

You taste salt and adrenaline and something like vertigo. The negotiations have been practical, the deal has been forged, and yet a soft, dangerous seam runs through the plan.

Elias meets your eyes. The world narrows to the two of you and the small, bright point where community and structure must meet.

Elias: (voice thick) "We can fight for the clause that keeps funding and gives you the teeth you need, Mara. Or we can let it go and pour energy into grassroots resilience alongside construction. Or—"

Celeste: "Or we move forward now, accept the phased safeguards I just offered, and ensure that the wetland cells exist at all."

Amina: "Or we delay signature, run focused tests and retrofits, risk losing the underwriting. The science is clear about timelines."

Your lungs burn. The roof tilts with wind. The promenade below hums like a throat. There is a pressure — legal, hydrological, emotional — that has been building and now wants a decision. Not because you want to choose, but because the situation demands it.

You imagine the seedlings in the cells, roots still thin and searching. You imagine the contractors, the investors, the cooperative meetings. You imagine the town at large: fishermen, market stalls, the elderly who sleep with windows cracked against ocean air. Every choice pulls at different people you love.

The arousal in the scene spikes; every voice overlaps and crisscrosses; thunder folds into the conversation.

Celeste: (softening) "Mara — choose the path that keeps the wetland alive."

Elias: "Choose the path that keeps us all standing after the next surge."

Amina: "Choose the path that keeps the system stable."

You close your eyes for one breath. The decision is not just technical. It is moral, personal, explosive.
