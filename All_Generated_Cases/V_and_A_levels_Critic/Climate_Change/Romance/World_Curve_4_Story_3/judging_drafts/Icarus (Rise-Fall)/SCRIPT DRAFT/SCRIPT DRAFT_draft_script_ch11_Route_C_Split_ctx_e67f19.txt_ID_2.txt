Your throat tightens. You had wanted this money to be an enabler — scaffolding for the salt marsh buffers, for the modular platforms, for people to keep living where they've always lived. Instead you feel the scaffolding becoming a cage with a gleaming latch someone else controls.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Early Evening]
[Visual: The amber light tilts low; puddles mirror broken string lights. A breeze lifts a torn banner and flutters it like a ragged flag.]

Your phone vibrates again. Elias. You answer because you know you should have called him sooner. The line clicks alive with static and something that used to be easier — an intimacy worn raw by the salt air.

Elias: (voice tight) "Maya. Did they—did you sign anything today?"
You: "Not yet. I was at the lab, Elias. There are still terms to—"
Elias: (cuts in) "Maya, they already started raising panels. The foundation money clears today whether we like the strings or not. I can see the favor-trading. They put our co-op on the vendor list like it's a consolation prize."

He swallows. On your end of the line you can hear him breathe: the same quick, brittle intake you remember from long nights mapping the fishing routes when the storm forecasts were wrong. The denim jacket he always leans into seems smaller in your memory; now his voice makes it smaller still, as if the sea has taken scale from everything you held.

You: "I know. There are clauses that hand over zoning influence if certain benchmarks aren't hit. Subcontracting for local groups, yes — but then there are carve-outs for commercial expansion. They use our language about resilience to legitimize development."

Elias: (angry, then pleading) "How could they twist it? How did this even happen without a public vote? Maya, we rallied people to trust process. We asked folks to stand up and map what matters. Now they're mapping us into a plan that sells what we stood for."

You: (exhale) "I thought giving them a pilot would buy us leverage, not hand it away. I—" (Your sentence fractures.) "I couldn't get them to commit to community oversight in binding terms. When I pressed, they smiled and said 'we can be flexible in implementation.'"

Elias: (bitter laugh) "Flexibility for them is a door. For us, it's a trap. You told me you wouldn't be soft with this."

You feel the accusation like an electric current. Part of you wants to flinch, to explain the spreadsheets and the pressure and the way the philanthropic rep framed timelines as humanitarian. Part of you remembers a younger you — steady, uncompromising — and how the town once called you that with warmth. Now the epithet feels like an accusation.

Elias: (voice breaking) "If you hand them the keys, don't be surprised if they change the lock."

You both fall silent, listening to the harbor's low roar. He shouldered your ideals for a while; now he sounds like someone watching the scaffolding bend.

You: "I'm not ready to hand them anything irrevocable. But we're out of time if we dawdle. The crew needs payroll. People are counting on these fixtures."

Elias: "Then don't dawdle for money. Move for people."

The line goes soft; his despair rides the call to you like surf.

[Scene: Rooftop Garden & Salt Marsh Restoration Site | Twilight]
[Visual: Bees hum in the last light; rain barrels reflect a bruise-gray sky. The rooftop's salvaged decking smells like wet wood and something hopeful.]

Iris stands by a row of newly planted cordgrass, her hands clasped in bright rubber gloves. Her face reads like salted leather: every fine line speaks of weather and work and stubborn survival. When she sees you, she doesn't smile.

Iris: "They pushed through permits last night. Not just a whisper—actual approvals filed under 'emergency measures.' Someone signed on behalf of the planning board. Who signs that for an entire district without a hearing?"

You: "Who? Do we know who?" (You power down the initial flare of panic and ask for facts. Facts keep the room from spinning.)

Iris: (jaw set) "Not publicly. Samir's footage shows aides in eateries. There's a pattern. They moved faster than outreach. Faster than consent." (She looks at your hands, then at the plants.) You: "We can subpoena records. We can call for an emergency council session. We can bring Professor Kim in to speak to the science of it—"

Iris: (cuts you off) "When you say 'we,' Maya, whose 'we' do you mean? The people on paper, or the people who live in the houses with their names on the mailboxes? Because the 'we' on the contracts is a different 'we.'"

Her words land hard. You want to list out models and leverage and fiscal timelines. Instead you think of Mrs. Calderon's tiny bodega on Second and how the new parcels would make that lot prime for redevelopment. You think of Samir's camera shots of kids fishing off the low pier. Each frame is a ledger of someone else's life.

You: (quiet) "Iris, if we push back now, it could stall construction. People lose paychecks. If we don't, this spins into something the town can't steer."

Iris: "So you choose who to save by choosing how to lose them. Figures, maps, payrolls—none of it tells you who wakes up in a house in five years."

Her eyes are not unreadable; they are an anchor. You can feel the tide of your conviction fraying.
