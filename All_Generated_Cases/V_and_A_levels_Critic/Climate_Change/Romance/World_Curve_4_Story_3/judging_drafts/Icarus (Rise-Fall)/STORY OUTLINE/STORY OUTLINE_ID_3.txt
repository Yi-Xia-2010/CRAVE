## Visual Novel Title
Harborwell Rising

---

## World View
Harborwell is a mid-sized coastal town in 2035 where the salt and sea shape everything: livelihoods, memory, and politics. Once a modest fishing and tourism hub, Harborwell now contends with accelerated sea-level rise, harsher storms, and the collapse of traditional fisheries. The town sits at the hinge between two visions: community-led adaptation that emphasizes marsh restoration, modular platforms, and distributed aid; and large-scale redevelopment—sea walls, commercialized zones, and corporate-backed jobs—that promises immediate economic relief but risks long-term fragility.

Politics are local and intimate: Mayor Ana Delgado navigates competing constituencies, Maren Voss channels pragmatic, top-down governance backed by investors, and grassroots organizers like Elias Hart push for a co-operative path. Science is a visible actor—Professor Julian Kim’s research sits in glass labs on dune ridges—and lived knowledge holds weight, embodied by fishers such as Iris Tanaka. Class tensions are visible in thrifted storefronts, weathered piers, and houses clipped by insurance lines.

Culturally, Harborwell is salt-tinged and nostalgic, with a civic art of repairing rather than replacing. Religious and civic rituals revolve around the sea—small memorials of lost homes, festivals on the boardwalk, and rooftop gardens seeded for resilience. Geography is compact: Tidewatch Harbor, the Boardwalk, an exposed Abandoned Sea Wall, a Glasshouse Research Lab, City Hall, and a rooftop marsh restoration site form the stage for choices that ask: whom do we save, and at what cost?

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I tell this story as Maya Reyes: a coastal resilience planner returned home after a past compromise that left a wound in the community. The narrative follows an Icarus arc—rapid ascent to a political and personal triumph, followed by a catastrophic fall. In the common route I ignite a surge of hope: community plans, collaborative pilots with Elias, and scientific validation from Professor Kim culminate in a visible victory—a pilot salt-marsh restoration and modular housing trial that attracts attention. That peak, however, creates pressure: investors, Maren’s pragmatic counterproposal, and the town's urgent need for jobs converge. Branching choices force me to balance love and principle: leverage Elias’s grassroots momentum, ally with Maren for quick funding, or refuse shortcuts that will harm vulnerable residents. Each path accelerates toward a distinct crash—whether systemic (Maren’s redevelopment passes), political (a costly compromise fractures trust), or personal (romance sacrificed for principle). Endings vary from community-led success (a fragile happiness), to bittersweet compromise, to bitter loss and exile, to a morally honest but uncertain “true” outcome. Every route keeps Harborwell’s fate tied to intimate decisions; romance humanizes policy trade-offs, and every visual—wet boards, fog-diffused sunsets, a braid snagged by wind—anchors the stakes.
