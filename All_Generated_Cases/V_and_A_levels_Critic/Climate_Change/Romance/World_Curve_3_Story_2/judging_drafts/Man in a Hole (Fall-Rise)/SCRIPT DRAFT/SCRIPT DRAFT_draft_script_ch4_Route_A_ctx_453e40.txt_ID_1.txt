You arrive with the memory of the meeting still warm in your throat — the doorway, the rain, the way faces read your hesitation like a ledger. The decision you made at the hall pulled you here, whether by promise or by default; Kaito's workshop is where plans become muscle and salt-scarred hands, where theory meets callus.

The air inside tastes of sawdust and salt; a small lamp throws a warm island of amber against the blue morning. Tools hang like constellations. On the central table, a scale model of the marsh — cardboard, seaweed, bits of wire — looks suddenly fragile under your palm.

You set your satchel down more carefully than you mean to. The shell at your throat catches the lamp's glow and feels heavy, a small, private ballast.

Kaito looks up from a length of driftwood he's smoothing. His apron is flecked with paint; his fingers are raw at the knuckles. He smiles in a way that is at once mischievous and exhausted.

Kaito Sakamura: "You're early. Or late, depending on how you count the tide."

You want to answer with something deft. Instead you breathe and let the smell of coffee and resin steady you.

You: "I couldn't sleep. Too many drafts in my head."

Kaito: (A soft, knowing chuckle) "Good. We need drafts. Bad drafts are still drafts. Come—look."

[Visual: Close-up on Kaito pushing aside a stack of sketches; pinned to the board is a hybrid design: berms run through planted marsh, small floating platforms, narrow concrete footings labeled 'targeted engineering'. Sticky notes crowd the margins.]

Kaito reaches for a pencil and draws a line with decisive, practiced strokes. His hand moves as if it's been translating ideas into structure for years — it has. When he sets the pencil down, his amber eyes meet yours.

Kaito: "We can start small. Test the berms here, add living roots — Maya's nursery can seed them. If we do it right the structures will take pressure off the shore without walling the sea out."

You trace the line with your finger without thinking. You can feel the pressure behind the suggestion: a whole town's back turned to you, a pulse of expectation that tightens your chest.

You: "And if it's not enough? If a storm comes bigger than we predict?"

Kaito: (His jaw tightens; he doesn't look away) "Then we fix. We learn. We don't hand the map to people who plan to draw new lines on top of where our nets and songs are."

The conversation is easy but not simple. Between your technical training and Kaito's craft lies an entire catalogue of trade-offs — some mathematical, some moral. Voices from outside the workshop shift; the town is arriving.

[Visual: Door swings wider; Maya slips in, camera swinging by her hip, eyes bright and impatient. Rin follows with thumbtacked fliers, breath puffing in the cool morning.]

Maya Chen: "We can run a seed nursery tonight. I stole extra trays from the school. We're ready to mobilize kids, neighbors, anyone with hands."

Rin Okubo: (Dropping fliers on the table) "I posted the invites. Luka put out word at the Hall. People want something they can touch."

Luka Sorensen arrives last, leaning on a cane, the lines of his face deep as ravines. He folds himself into a stool as if he's been carrying its weight for years.

Luka Sorensen: "Touch is important. So is remembering what's been lost. But remember — it's not about making a pretty fix. We need work that lasts through hunger, through brine, through people who can't leave."

His voice carries a quiet, inevitable weight that makes the room tighten. You feel the town's history pressing at you — each decision you make will land on someone else.

[Sound: A horn in the harbor — a low, distant signal. The strings swell.]

From the doorway, two figures appear: Ishaan Rao, crisp and composed, followed by Elena Moro, coat lacquered against the salt. They move like a tide of a different kind: smooth, persuasive, channeling capital and institutional certainty.

Elena Moro: (Scanning the model with a cool, appreciative gaze) "This is thoughtful. You all should be commended for the rapid prototyping. But prototypes are not policy. If Saltline expects to survive the next decade, we need structures that are proven at scale."

Ishaan Rao: (Polite, neutral) "We have a limited technical grant on offer. Targeted retrofits, prioritized buildings — schools, the clinic, the food hub. We can move quickly. Marin, you'll know what's needed."

You feel the word "grant" like a cold lever placed against the heart of the workshop. There is opportunity here — resources that could make immediate difference — but also a weight that could tilt the balance away from the community's shape.

Kaito's smile thins.

Kaito Sakamura: "How limited?"

Ishaan: (Smooth) "Sufficient to retrofit critical structures. Not a full barrier. No displacements required if we coordinate."

Elena: (Eyes on you, flat) "We can stage the funding to accelerate resilience. You'll maintain oversight, Marin. We want to keep this local."

Her tone is the softest kind of pressure: framed as partnership, delivered like a pact with fine print.

You: (Internal) The past is a shadow that never leaves your shoulders. You remember proposals and meetings that felt like bridges but became walls; you remember an advocacy that displaced marsh without meaning to. The taste of guilt is metallic — it sharpens your focus.

You: "What strings?"

Ishaan: "Standard reporting, measurable outcomes. We expect quarterly metrics — efficiency, reduction in flood damage. Elena will oversee coordination."

Kaito: (A low exhale) "And control?"

Elena's smile is precise.

Elena Moro: "Coordination, not control. We need accountability."

The room shivers. Accountability translates to deadlines, to data, to the kind of timeline that could hollow out the slower work of community trust. Luka looks at you with eyes that ask a question without words.

Luka Sorensen: "Sometimes 'accountability' is what you call waiting for something to fail so you can prove you were right."

Maya jumps in, voice high and urgent.

Maya Chen: "We can't become a checkbox for someone else's spreadsheet. This work is different. It's lived experience, it's songs, it's kids learning to plant. You can't measure that with a meter."

Ishaan: (Firm) "We measure what matters if you want funding. Lives protected, buildings salvaged. Marin, you have the technical background to make this happen."

You feel the room's pressure concentrating on you like a beam. The arousal ramps — voices sharpen, tools clatter as hands gesture, rain starts against the boathouse roof like a staccato heartbeat.

You: (Internal) You can see three futures unfurling in the fulcrum of your next move: elbow alongside Kaito in mud and root, take agency funding and keep control in name at the cost of needing to show metrics, or step back and let Kaito steer the cooperative from the front while you provide what little oversight you can. Every path looks necessary and precarious.

Kaito moves closer, his voice low.

Kaito Sakamura: "Marin, you don't have to carry this alone. But people are looking. They need someone who'll stand in the gap with them."

You: "I know. I'm—" Your words fracture. You feel the old reflex to over-explain, to bake technical diagrams into apologies.

Rin: (Quick, practical) "We need a plan and a meeting schedule. If we wait until funding paperwork is filed, winter will come and the seedlings will drown."

Elena: (Lean, controlled) "We can pilot a targeted retrofit immediately. Use the grant to shore up the clinic tonight and provide materials for the workshop. A joint statement would reassure insurance underwriters and investors."

Maya: "That's playing to people who don't know this place. Marin, are you going to let them define what 'reassure' looks like?"

Elena: (Turning to Kaito) "Your workshop's value is obvious, but scale matters. If we retrofit just a few buildings, we can demonstrate a model and attract further resources."

Kaito Sakamura: "I can build infrastructure to be beautiful and useful. I just don't want to watch the town become a case study while we lose what makes Saltline liveable."

Elena: (A long, cold look) "And what makes Saltline liveable are the people who stay when systems fail. We propose a framework that reduces failure."

You: (Internal; rising panic) The cycle tightens: every argument circles back to the same axis — who decides what 'reducing failure' looks like. The stakes are not abstract. They are your neighbors' homes, Luka's stories, Maya's future. Your lungs feel too small.

Rin nudges a tray of sketches toward you.

Rin Okubo: "People have already started planning — school groups, fisher families, the floating farm. They want to put hands to work, not just sit at a template."

Luka: (Quietly) "And hands mean time. Time is a currency that isn't always counted on a ledger."

Ishaan: "We can honor that time through matching support. But we need someone who can bridge the work to measurable outcomes. Marin, the agency trusts your expertise."

Kaito: (Soft, urgent) "Don't let trust be a leash. If you accept this, make sure 'local control' means local authority, not local optics."

You can almost taste the metallic edge of accusation between those last words. Elena's jaw drops for a microsecond; Ishaan's polite posture flickers.

Elena: "We are not here to leash. We are here to facilitate."

Kaito: "Facilitation looks like this," he says, tapping the model where living roots interlace with a slim concrete footing. "Not like a wall someone else decides to call a 'truth'."

The workshop hums with arguments and small actions. Someone hammers a nail with a measured rhythm; someone else laughs nervously at a joke about tide charts. Your pulse is a drumbeat under everything.

Your internal tempo accelerates. Images crowd your mind: the marsh beds failing in slow, lethal silt; the clinic's generator shorting out; Luka's hands in soil that will be reclaimed by salt. The negative weight presses inward — guilt, fear, the sense of a town hung in the balance.

Elena steps back, folding her arms as if closing a case file.

Elena Moro: "We can do this with structure. Marin, lead the technical plan for the retrofit; Kaito, you continue your workshop prototyping; we fund both — conditional on deliverables."

Kaito: (A pause — long and telling) "Conditional."

Maya: (Biting) "Conditional means you get to reassess when it suits you and not when the tide does."

Ishaan: "Conditions are standard. They ensure efficacy."

Luka: "And what you call efficacy sometimes becomes a tool to justify what you planned all along."

Silence falls like a hood. The room's arousal aligns on this hinge — the point where theory and craft, grant and grit, might either join or split.

You feel the center of gravity pulling you toward action, because that's what you always do — you act to fix things — but the memory of past fixes that harmed the marsh sits like a seam of cold under your ribs. You can feel the town watching.

If you accept the grant, you get resources and the capacity to fortify critical structures—fast, but with strings. If you step back, you preserve another person's public trust at the cost of looking like you retreated.

Your heartbeat ticks faster. The strings in the music rise to a sharp, insistent chord.
