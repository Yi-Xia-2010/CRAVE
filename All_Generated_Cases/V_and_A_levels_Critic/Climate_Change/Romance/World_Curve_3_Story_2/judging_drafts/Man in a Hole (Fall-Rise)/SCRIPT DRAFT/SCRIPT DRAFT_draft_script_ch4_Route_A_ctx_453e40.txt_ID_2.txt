[Scene: Kaito's Workshop | Late Morning — overcast, sea wind rattling the windows]
[Visual: Interior filled with reclaimed timber, hanging plans, and a row of small wind turbines half-assembled on a bench]
[Sound: Electric sander whines in the distance; gulls cry faintly; a kettle hisses on a small stove]
[Music: Tense, driving strings under a pulsing ambient beat]

You push the door open and the smell hits you first: warm pine, salty dampness, the oily tang of varnish. The workshop feels like a lung — full of breath and effort — and there is no place for pretense here. The decision you made at the Sunken Promenade still sits under your ribs like a stone, heavy and necessary. You chose Kaito. You chose the long, practical grit of this room over the polished certainty Elena promises. There's relief, and a new current of dread.

Kaito looks up from a half-carved beam, sawdust freckling his forearms. He blinks, sunlight catching the pale burn scar on his thumb, then smiles the slow, steady smile that has kept this place from falling into being only memory. He doesn't move to wrap you in certainty. He offers work — and trust — without theatrical words.

Kaito: "You made the right choice coming here. We were already sketching how to lift the nursery beds off the worst tide lines. Want to see the mock-up?"

You nod before you realize you spoke. Your voice is hoarse from the night; the sea seems to have scraped your throat raw.

You: "Show me."

He passes you a damp, salt-streaked model — cardboard, wire, a patch of marsh grass tied with twine. Your fingers find the edge of the model as if to anchor yourself. When you touch it, you taste iron and brine — not just the sea, but the small, metallic tang of pressure.

Kaito studies your face, then leans his forearms on the bench. He has been building solutions with his hands since he was small. When he talks about the town, his sentences are short and full of images: ropes, songs, dinners shared on patched-up tables. He speaks like someone who believes repair can be love made visible.

Kaito: "If we raise the frames, sink pilings with recycled plastic sleeves, and plant native cordgrass in stepped berms, the beds hold longer. Sensors you tune could tell us when to shift planks and where to add compost. We can teach folks in a weekend."

You find yourself listing constraints aloud — tide windows, monitoring bandwidth, potential scour beneath pilings. Each technical point is a way to steer your anxiety into something you can manage.

You: "We can build redundancy into the pilings. But the hydrology's changing faster than the models predict. I'll need to run controlled salinity trials during the next full tide."

Kaito: (half-laugh, tension in it) "Perfect. When do we start? Tomorrow? Tonight?"

Your chest tightens. The urgency in his voice carries hope — and an accusation. You remember the displaced marsh that haunts the papers you keep folded in your bag. You remember Luka's quiet warning about making the sea hate you. You remember the faces that will read your next speech as either betrayal or salvation.

You: (quiet) "We start by listening. To Luka. To Maya. To everyone who keeps these tides from turning us into a footnote."

Kaito's brows knit. He reaches to squeeze your hand — a brief contact, oily with varnish — and then pulls back as if testing the distance you both allow. There's something intimate in the way he respects that recoil: he read the same margins you do.

[Scene: Cooperative Workshop — Community Space | Midday — overcast light through salted glass]
[Visual: Long tables pushed together, sketches pinned to every available surface; jars of tea steam; a projector on a low crate flickers with a rough schematic]
[Sound: Murmurs, the clank of mugs, a child's laughter cut by a briefer silence when someone raises a hand]
[Music: Percussive strings, rising motif]

You walk in like someone who has just signed a contract with the weather: responsibility binds you. Maya is there first, camera slung at her hip, eyes wide and impatient as always. Rin folds handbills with practiced speed and offers you one without comment. Luka sits at the head of the table, worn fingers tracing an old map as if feeling the contour lines through his skin.

Maya: "We don't have time to wait for perfect models. The school garden flooded last week. Kids dragged soggy books home. If we can pilot a marsh bed and show immediate benefit, people will come."

You: "I know. The pilot's not just about the plants. It's about showing care through work. But care means not repeating mistakes."

Rin: (soft) "They'll listen to people who get their hands dirty. But they're afraid. If we ask them to volunteer, we have to make sure they come home dry."

Luka: (gravelly) "History remembers those who plant along the tide, and those who try to stop it with stone. There's wisdom in both. We must keep both voices in the measure."

You can feel the room tilt as different needs try to occupy the same center. People talk over one another — excitement, fear, old resentments. Your lips taste of sand.

Ishaan appears then, stepping through the doorway with a sheen you can read as institutional: a neat suit with the salt-stain pattern of a man who spends time negotiating near the sea but not in it. He holds a slim folder. Elena is close behind, coat buttoned against the chill, eyes like a scanner.

Ishaan: (efficient) "Marin. Kaito. We have a limited technical grant. It could fund sensors, retrofits for key public buildings, and seed materials for the pilot. The agency asks only that stewardship metrics be transparent and reported."

Elena: (calm, near-soft) "We can scale this if you let us. We can provide engineers, materials, expedited permits. The barrier project is still an option, but this—this cooperative funding—lets you keep local control under our oversight."

Your stomach drops. The words are precise, the offer balanced like a scale already weighed. You hear the undertow: 'under our oversight.' Elena's smile does not reach her eyes — they are complex: calculated and, in their own way, frightened. She has told the same story before — efficiency, scale, the promise that structure will spare grief. But you know what centralized solutions have removed from other places: songs, market stalls, the odd corners of maritime memory.

Maya: (sharp) "So it's a carrot. Or a leash. Which one is it?"

Ishaan: (placating) "No leash. Reporting, yes. Accountability, yes. Full partnership."

Kaito: (hands clenched around a mug) "We didn't invite a bureaucracy to remake us. We invited neighbors."

Elena: "You invited a neighbor who can help you not drown. I tell you this because the next storm will be worse. We can stand between ruin and continuity."

Everything in your chest accelerates. You think of the marsh you helped displace years ago — the one that taught you how much harm good intentions can do when divorced from local knowledge. You think of the faces of the volunteers, the scent of cordgrass, Luka's map. The grant will buy sensors, faster models — but at what expense to autonomy?

You: (to Elena, measured) "What does 'transparent reporting' mean exactly? Who determines metrics? Who has veto over site selection?"

Elena: (cool) "Common metrics: survivorship of plantings, reduction in flood incidents, maintenance logs. As for veto power — it's collaborative. The agency funds, the community stewards. We ask only to ensure standards are met."

Rin: (bitter) "Standards that will be used to justify removing what you decide 'doesn't meet the metrics'?"

Elena's expression tightens a fraction. For a breath, her composure flickers into something like regret before she masks it again. You note 'Complex' in the way her jaw sets. You cannot be sure whether she is harder or simply scared.

[Scene: Kaito's Workshop | Late Afternoon — rain starts in earnest, drumming on the roof]
[Visual: Workbench strewn with sketches, a damp jacket hangs on a peg; outside, the sea is a moving gray mass]
[Sound: Rain intensifies; a distant siren wails once and then quiets]
[Music: Rising, urgent strings; tempo accelerates]

After the community crowds thin you and Kaito are left among the scattered plans, the crumbs of tea, the small dangers of tools. The workshop is quieter, but the world outside has ramped up: wind teeth at the windows, the smell of storm in the air. Your sensor wristband is warm against your skin; its tiny LED flickers like a nervous pulse.

Kaito picks up a pencil and then sets it down. There is an intensity in him now like a tide turning. He leans close when he speaks, his voice low.

Kaito: "You could accept the grant. It would make the pilot easier. Sensors, materials — less strain on volunteers. We could do more. But…"

He doesn't finish. The 'but' hangs as a question with teeth. You think of what accepting would look like in public: your name attached to a partnership with Elena, your hands in the signatures that make compromises real. You think of the rumors that would start — 'Marin sold out' or 'She finally realized the town needed experts.' Both of those will bruise different people you love.

You: "If we take money, we take scrutiny. We'll have to show them we can meet their metrics. They might demand alterations that change the pilot's integrity."

Kaito: (soft) "Or they might help us scale what works so it doesn't stay a single island. Think—school roofs retrofitted, emergency buoyancy funded. People sleep safer."

You find your hands behind your back, fingers worrying the seam of your jacket. The workshop is full of small testimonies: a child's carved boat, posters for community lessons, a coffee ring that will never wash out. Your inner monologue cycles faster now — a line of consequences stretching into weathered futures.

You: (honest) "I keep replaying the marsh I lost. I can't promise I won't repeat that harm if I move too fast with the wrong partners."

Kaito reaches across the table and lays his hand over yours; this time you do not pull away. His thumb rubs the side of your hand in a small, messy rhythm. The gesture is practical and tender. You can feel the calluses, smell sawdust and the lingering tea. It steadies you. For a moment, the panic subsides and you can think with the clarity of a person who is loved and therefore allowed to be afraid.

Kaito: "Then we do it together. With the town. With checks. We'll write the metrics. We'll decide closures. We'll not let 'efficiency' write over memory. And if they overreach, we'll push back."

Before you can answer, Luka knocks at the window, three quick taps. He stands in the rain, coat pulled high, eyes bright as if the storm has sharpened him. He lifts a hand and points to a tide gauge blinking on the cliffside. The reading is higher than last week. He mouths something you catch: 'Now.'

The urgency returns, triple-struck. The room spins with implications. You look at the folder Elena left on the table earlier — contracts and glossy diagrams — and then at Kaito's hand holding yours. Your heartbeat is loud; your breath is ragged; the temp in the room feels like a countdown.

Maya's camera buzzes with a message. Rin has already started folding more handbills. People outside will ask, will wait for you to define what the town should be. Your decision is not only about engineering or craft. It is about who you are in the face of an accelerating sea.

You can feel the high arousal now like an electrical charge: every option pulls at some part of your identity — scientist, neighbor, lover, exile. The negative valence constricts the air: dread, fear of compromise, the sharp ache of likely loss.

Kaito looks at you as if asking whether you will lead the way or let the tide decide.

You inhale, a long, deep intake that tries to purchase time.
