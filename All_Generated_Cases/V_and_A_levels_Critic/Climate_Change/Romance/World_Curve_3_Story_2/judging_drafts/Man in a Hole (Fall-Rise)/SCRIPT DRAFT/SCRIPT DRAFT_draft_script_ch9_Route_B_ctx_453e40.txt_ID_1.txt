Kaito Sakamura: (quiet, taut) "Elena, 'minimal long-term footprint' is a phrase. The marshbeds aren't just infrastructure. People have their lives tied to those flats. You cut them out and you erode something else that won't show up on your budget sheets."

Elena Moro: "We don't have the luxury of idealism right now. The storms are arriving sooner than models predicted. Elena Moro: "The condition is straightforward. The room leans in as if gravity moved closer to Elena's words. You feel a heat flush at the base of your skull; everything narrows.

Luka Sorensen: (hoarse, slow) "Control isn't a small word. We remember when outsiders drew lines on maps and called them progress. The sea remembers differently. It mutters at those who think they can command it."

Elena Moro: (softening — tactical) "Luka, I'm not an 'outsider' in the way that matters. I grew up with my city underwater. I know loss. We are trying to stave off the same fate for Saltline. Maya presses her hands together, nails leaving crescent marks.

Maya Chen: "Temporary becomes permanent. Temporary is how you take a community's rights one shift at a time. How does Saltline trust that 'after' will actually come?"

Elena Moro: "By delivering measurable outcomes. By keeping governance transparent and by returning oversight once safety thresholds are met. We will establish community liaisons — local representatives embedded in the operations team."

Ishaan adds, almost as an aside: "Elena's proposal includes a community fund for relocation subsidies, training programs, and a reserves endowment for the floating farm. It's not simply extraction."

You hear the pragmatism in that, but your gut scrambles for all the things grants don't measure: the taste of clams roasted in a shared fire, Luka's stories told on a particular bench at low tide, the songs Maya learned as a child at the harbor's edge. The thought of those things being boxed into "reserves" feels like embalming.

Marin Alvarez: "And my role?"

You say the words aloud because if you don't, the room will try to answer for you. Your voice is steadier than you feel.

Elena Moro: "You would be the public technical lead for the pilot. We will attach your name to the project, ensure your firm — Tidewise — gets a seat at the management table. In exchange, operational control for the adjacent commons moves under the project's governance for the duration."

The trade is explicit now. Your endorsement buys Saltline immediate defenses. It also requires you to be the face that asks neighbors to cede aspects of their daily life for the promise of safety. You can feel the stakes as if someone had set pliers around your sternum.

Kaito's hand lifts, fingers trembling.

Kaito Sakamura: (low, urgent) "You expect me to stand by while you broker away my neighbors' rights? Marin, you know how much is built from people's hands here — the floating farm, the repurposed barges, the boat-repair co-op. These are lives, not line items."

Elena Moro: (flat) "Then be part of the management team. Your workshop could be the primary contractor for the reclaimed docks. We want local buy-in. We are offering integration, not displacement."

"You" — the protagonist — feel the room close like a tide pooling around you. Elena's offer is a tidal force disguised as a handshake: urgency, resources, speed. Kaito hears potential marginalization in the subtext. Maya smells betrayal. Luka hears history.

Your mind races, counting consequences like a ledger: immediate protection versus cultural erosion; guaranteed mobilization versus the risk of bureaucratic permanence; a funded safety net versus the moral cost of telling a community to let go.

Your environmental sensor wristband vibrates faintly — a reminder in your pocket of the last modeled storm surge. You glance down and the soft green LED seems to blink in rhythm with everyone's pulse in the room.

The meeting edges toward you like a wave. Elena folds her hands. Ishaan watches you expectantly. Kaito's voice is a taut wire.

Kaito Sakamura: (quietly pleading) "If you sign on, I want guarantees — binding language, community veto clauses, joint monitoring boards. Not just 'liaisons' and 'resumes after demonstrable stability.'"

Elena Moro: (smiles without warmth) "We can write strong language. We can promise oversight. But the window for deployment narrows with each storm. Legalese doesn't stop water. Action does."

You feel every sentence as pressure. You want to hold both things: the safety of a wall and the survival of a way of life. You can feel your shoulders do that animal roll your mother used to do when she carried wet laundry — bracing for weight you aren't sure you can take alone.
