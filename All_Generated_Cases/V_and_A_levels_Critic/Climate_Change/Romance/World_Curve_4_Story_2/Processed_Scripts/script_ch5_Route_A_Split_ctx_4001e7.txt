[Scene: Ferryworks Greenhouse | Morning]
[Background:"ch5_4001e7_1"]
[Sound: The hiss of a welding torch, the soft thud of booted footsteps on the steel deck, a low hum of generators]
[Music: Fast, buoyant strings—hope in motion]

You walk in on the noise of a place already deciding what it wants to be. The Ferryworks smells of wet soil and hot metal: rich loam under a layer of solder fumes, the clean bite of salt on the air. Your notebook is folded into the inside pocket of your jacket, thumbed margins pressed soft by every meeting and scrawl. The small spark from Chapter Four still lives under your ribs — that ember of agreement with Calder Voss, the pact you made with hazard and people both.

Ori Navarro is at the aft bench, shoulders bent into a winged planter half-sculpted from a salvaged hull plate. His topknot is tight, cheeks flecked with paint; the welding goggles sit on his head like a crown. He taps the edge of the metal with the flat of his palm and grins when he sees you.

Ori Navarro: "Look at it — doesn't it want to fly?" (he laughs, sparks catching in the laugh lines at his eyes)

You let yourself smile because the thing is beautiful: curved, practical, and stubborn — the exact thing you love about his projects. Around him, Silas Roe moves like a practiced shadow, quick hands binding algae filters to a frame that will breathe with the tide. Old Mara sits on a milk crate in the corner, her voice a low hum you didn't realize you needed until it steadied you.

Old Mara: (softly) "Tide remembers. Tide forgives. We will learn to move with her."

[Background:"ch5_4001e7_2"]
[Sound: The recorder light on Jun's collar blinks; a kettle on a smaller bench sings]

You move like someone mid-countdown, assigning volunteers, double-checking the battery matrix under a row of seedlings, sketching the final sensor array with the quick economy of someone who's learned to be exact where there's no margin for waste.

Maya Lin appears with a rolled set of legal drafts and a thermos. Her eyes are bright, ledger-face on, already speaking in short, efficient bursts.

Maya Lin: "Volunteer shifts up two hours. Ferryback will need extra hands for the heavier blocks. Also —" (she taps a page) "— Jun says he has something for you."

Jun Park steps forward, reluctant smile softened by the warm light. He hands you a slim packet whose top sheet has been redacted: thick black bars, the legal printer's silence where meaning should be. You take it with fingertips that are damp from the garden.

Jun Park: "I scrubbed it for the meeting, but there are lines they didn't want the room to see yet. I thought you should read it before any cameras or speeches."

You peel back the legalese. The prototype is singing to you from the benches — the mangrove cuttings are rooting, tiny pale hairs like new constellations; the sensors on the outer planter ping, numbers flattening into a calmer line. Volunteers laugh on the deck when a test pump hissed and then steadied; a child squeals when water runs clear through a filter. That success pulls your chest forward, a rising tide of something like triumph.

[Music: A higher, quicker motif—anticipation twined with momentum]

But the redacted addendum catches at the edges of the moment. You read the scanned passage over and over — the letters remain, but clauses have been blacked out like tide marks. One line you can still see mentions "relocation clause" and "safety thresholds" with the neat cruelty of corporate phrasing. The more you read, the more the chord of joy in your chest hardens into something brittle and small.

Your hope refuses to break. Instead it tightens into a knot you recognize as focus.

Ori Navarro notices the change before you speak. He sets down his tool and comes close, wiping his hands on a rag with the reflexive intimacy of someone who builds things to soothe the world.

Ori Navarro: "You look like you swallowed a storm." (he jabs a finger at you, trying to be light; his voice has an edge)

Ava Maren: "Jun handed me an addendum. It mentions a relocation clause."

Jun Park: (quick, pragmatic) "It's redacted in parts. The thresholds are intentionally vague — they can be set after the pilot starts. It's better to be cautious about how that could be used."

Maya Lin: "Then we tighten the language now. No ambiguity. Tenure guarantees — clear triggers, community oversight. We don't sign until it's explicit."

Ori Navarro reacts, both hands out, as if to hold the air between legalese and living things.

Ori Navarro: "Or we push forward with the demo and make it irrefutable. Let the pilot be proof — public eyes, photos, people touching the wall. Once it's built, they can't displace what already works."

Your chest is a drumbeat: the Ferryworks hums with activity; the prototype hums with living roots; Calder Voss's cameras—eyes from the corporate side—are angled to show smiles and strong hands. The pilot's public optics are gold. Your mind runs a hundred small calculations at once: resources arriving, pressure easing, but a clause like that could be flipped into a means of eviction later. There is no easy arithmetic that makes all harm vanish.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Encourage Ori to build the annex now — make the pilot undeniable"

[Outcome A: You nod, voice quick. "Okay. Make it unignorable." Ori's grin goes wide; he slaps the metal like it's a promise. The team rallies, energy spooling outward as more hands come to lift and weld. For a heartbeat the Ferryworks is all motion and incandescent possibility.]
[Outcome B: You fold the packet closed with a deliberate snap. "We can't build what we can't stay by," you say. Maya breathes out, relief and new work rewriting across her jaw. [Background:"ch5_4001e7_3"]
[Sound: Heated discussion, the clanging of a makeshift mallet, the distant steady squawk of a camera drone]

You watch Ori's face in both possibilities. When he hears you consider legal caution, something tightens behind his smile. He runs a hand through his hair, jaw working. The vulnerability there — the wish to show and to be seen — is as honest as any admission he hasn't given out loud.

Ori Navarro: (low) "If we spend weeks arguing lines, we might lose the window to show them it works. People need to feel it to fight for it."

Ava Maren: "And if we sign without protections, we might give them the right to move us under the guise of those very 'safety thresholds.'"

Silas Roe, overhearing, pipes up from where he's testing a seal on a filter.

Silas Roe: "We can scale enough tonight to make a splash. But if law takes people away later — that's the real leak."

Old Mara hums, the sound like tide on a shell, and then — unexpectedly — she speaks with a bite that slices through the noise.

Old Mara: "Build and bind. Make the wall so stubborn the water will take it before it un-takes you. Also, make the law hold like stone."

The chorus of wants crowds into the ferry's warm air: beauty and speed, law and stubborn proof. The Ferryworks becomes a small, living negotiation — hands making things, mouths making claims, trust braided with caution.

Jun Park steps closer, hand on the packet again.

Jun Park: "There's a middle. We push the demo fast for optics, but we demand an escrow of protections — a clause written into the pilot terms that freezes any relocation action until a joint oversight board reviews actual outcomes with live data. It won't be airtight, but it's leverage."

Maya Lin's pen scratches; she doesn't look up, not yet.

Maya Lin: "Feasibility or theater? We need ironclad tenure in the paperwork. Otherwise, we hand them an exit strategy in a nice bow."

Ori Navarro's hands are clenched; a few flecks of solder decorate his knuckles.

Ori Navarro: "Or I build a little annex — autonomous, not tied to Voss systems. We show people what a community-driven module looks like, outside their control. Let them touch autonomy."

Your heart races. VeryHigh. Each suggestion throws up a different future. The tide inside your chest climbs: the prototype's success, the volunteer laughter, Calder Voss's cameras — all of it spins outward with potential and peril.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Confront Calder Voss's representatives now — demand clause removal in person"
> Choice B: "Call Maya and Jun into a closed room to redline the clause before any more public exposure"
[Outcome A: You grab the packet, your voice a tether. "Bring them here," you say. The announcement ripples through the Ferryworks; volunteers straighten. Calder Voss's team looks surprised but alert. It's an escalation; it's public; it forces a face-to-face that could cut the clause or fracture the deal.]
[Outcome B: You map a quick path to the small meeting room, fingers already reaching for a pen. Maya's relief is immediate; Jun nods into his recorder and then switches it off. The Ferryworks breathes in, contracting into a focused, legal heart where words will be made to hold people.]

[Music: Rapid strings spike; then a drumbeat steadying into a march]
[Sound: A sudden gust through the open hatch; a tarpaulin flaps like an answered bell]

The prototype performs in the meantime: you watch the sensors report salinity stabilizing at the new bio-porous interface; mangrove shoots curl into place and hold; the community planters bloom small, stubborn greens against the deck's gray. A small crowd gathers at the rail — neighbors leaning close, their faces lit and real on Calder Voss's streaming feed. The cameras angle for warmth. It is, in spite of everything, a vividly hopeful scene: people touching living walls and smiling, hands that are not afraid in the moment.

That living proof pulses inside you like sunlight through a storm-cloud. For the first time since you came back to Saltway, the possibility that the neighborhood could stay — that your people's hands and designs could be enough — settles as a real shape beneath your ribs.

But the addendum is a hard stone under that light. You reread the phrase you can see: "relocation clause." Your throat goes dry. Trust, once given to Calder Voss's team, feels like a wager.

Maya Lin folds her papers, face fierce with the tiredness of a woman who has argued for years over things people take for granted. Ori Navarro looks at you like there's an entire future balanced on the edge of your pen.

Ori Navarro: (soft, urgent) "What do you want, Ava? What do you think we should do?"

You could answer in ten different ways. You could sign for speed, trusting to proof and public feeling; you could refuse until the language is ironclad, guarding tenure like the most precious seed; or you could delay and keep building, refusing corporate scaffolds and letting the prototype grow under your hands.

The Ferryworks hums louder — a ship making its own weather. The volunteers wait; Calder Voss's cameras wait; Jun's recorder light waits like a small, impartial sun. Everything presses against the single hinge: your choice.

[Music: Crescendo—everything lifts]

You stand before the assembled group. People look to you for the next breath. Maya's pen is poised. Ori's hands are stained and ready. Jun offers you the packet again, the redaction a dark island in the page sea. Old Mara's beads click like a small, steady tide-watch.

You inhale salt and possibility and all the weight of a neighborhood that has put its future into your hands. The arousal that has built all morning — the quickening, the charged air, the sympathetic hum of bodies ready to move — gathers into one sharp, blazing focus.

This is the moment the pilot either becomes a public triumph that keeps people in place or a tidy demonstration that paradoxically gives permission to take them away.

The choice you make will be a blueprint for more than the seawall. It will shape trust: between you and Ori Navarro, between Saltway and Calder Voss, between beauty and legal permanence. Your fingers flex around the packet as if it were the rudder of a ship.
