[Scene: Glass Pier Negotiation Room | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A glass-walled conference room jutting from the pier; rain-streaked skyline and cranes loom beyond. Inside, a long table scarred with coffee rings and tide maps. Calder stands by the window; his coat catches the light.]
[Music: Rising strings with a hopeful, urgent tempo]
[Sound: Distant hammering from the construction site; gulls call; the faint ocean hiss through the seals]

You step into the room carrying your field notebook like a small, stubborn lantern. The paper smells faintly of salt and old coffee; the pages are thumbed where you mapped tenure lines and sketched living-root profiles. Your hands brush the edge of the table. The sound of it—dull wood against cloth—anchors you to a decision you have already begun to make.

Calder turns. The overcoat he wears holds a clean, ozone-scraped scent; when he moves, there is the quick metallic glint of something at his throat, the ghost of a photograph pressed inside a locket. His pale gray eyes travel your notebook with the casual, practiced interest of a man used to evaluating risk on sight.

Calder Voss: "Ava Maren. Thank you for coming. I read your brief. Your maps are thorough." (He gestures to the chair across from him.) "We can move with speed on a pilot if we align on terms."

There is a softness in his voice that almost undoes the shape of the room—precision dressed in small civility. You keep your pen uncapped between your fingers, a tiny, steady point.

Maya pulls a small stack of papers from her bag and shoves them into your hands: a draft, a list of contingencies. Old Mara sits with her shawl folded around her like a history you can't misplace; she watches you with salt-carved lines that do not leer or judge—only remember.

Maya Lin: "We need names, we need termination clauses, and we need assurances about tenancy. Don't let them sweep those away with a glossy timeline." (She taps the margin of the contract.) "If they want optics, make them earn the groundwork."

You glance toward the construction beyond the glass. On the work platform a few meters away—part of the Ferryworks hull and the tethered barges—Ori's shoulders are bent over a small sensor array. His patchwork coat flaps as he fits reclaimed circuit fragments into place; his welding goggles sit crooked on his head like a crown. When his amber eyes find you, a private current runs through you: the old shy gravity of shared work, of calloused fingers touching the same wood.

Ori Navarro: (calling, light) "Ava—if they try to standardize the mounts, don't let them pick the bolt pattern. We need our tolerances."

He grins, then goes back to soldering. It's a small, intimate defiance. For a moment the room softens to the sound of Ori's tool—faint, human, stubborn.

You clear your throat and begin to speak; the negotiation is both an arithmetic and a language. Your voice is pragmatic, steady—the voice that translates tide charts into tenure rights.

You: "Calder, the living-module integration is promising. It would buy us fabrication resources and permits. But read Clause Seven—'exclusive maintenance rights'—that binds Saltway's designs to VossArc's proprietary access. We need co-ownership of the package and a revocation clause with community oversight if tenure is threatened."

Calder's fingers fold on the table. He tilts his head, as if considering a physics problem you haven't yet stated aloud.

Calder Voss: "We propose a maintenance agreement because the floodgates require consistent calibration. The city's insurance standards demand single-point accountability. Multiple stewards increase failure vectors. It's not about control—it's about responsibility."

His tone is disarmingly reasonable. Around the table, several hands soften; the word "insurance" carries a weight like a life-jacket. Maya's jaw tightens, then relaxes to ledger-mode—she's weighing public perception, not yet trust.

Maya Lin: "Responsibility anchored to what? Your stockholders? We need community oversight that is codified. Responsibility without residency is just a euphemism for displacement."

Old Mara's voice is a low sound from the shawl—two words that make the room lean.

Old Mara: "We remember when 'safety' meant someone else moving us out."

The thin sting of history pinches. You flip the notebook open. The tide-chart ink bleeds in the corner; your annotations crowd the margins like small guards. You make notes in your head faster than your pen can keep up: codify oversight, set transparent maintenance logs, ensure local hiring quotas for the maintenance team, require open-source readouts for sensor data, embed an immediate rescind clause triggered by tenancy losses above a threshold.

Calder watches the movement of your hand over the page. There is a brief moment where his practiced persuasion flickers—no bluster, only the calm of a man used to reshaping a problem until it fits a template.

Calder Voss: "Those are reasonable amendments, Ms. Maren. We can draft co-ownership terms—technical stewardship balanced with community co-governance. VossArc benefits from local knowledge. We want partners, not patrons."

His voice is careful silk over steel. There’s an unmistakable offer in it: scale, resources, and the political capital to get permits approved. The seduction is not loud; it is the kind that lands like warm light on bruised hands.

The room's rhythm accelerates. People exchange looks—some hopeful, some skeptical. Jun's absence is a gap you notice; his recorder would have been a blunt light. Still, you are nudged forward by the collective breath of your neighbors.

You: (softly, then firmer) "If we accept, we accept public oversight, transparent logs, and, crucial—control over housing allocation in the affected parcels. Those three things are non-negotiable."

Calder nods. He reaches into his coat with the casual motion of someone removing a glove. The locket—thin, titanium, a faded storm photograph curled inside—catches the light. For a second you see the private weather of his past: a photograph like a small, dangerous harbor. It humanizes him without making him simple.

Calder Voss: "Agreed in principle. Draft language, signatories, oversight board with community majority—these are logistics. Speed will be on our side if we move now."

Your pulse picks up. There is a high, clean urgency in the room: the kind that feels like an incoming tide—fast, inevitable, and full of possibility.

The choices clang and settle like tools. You feel the high arousal as a tangible pressure, a hinge between careful optimism and the risk of giving away leverage. Ori's glance is a warm anchor; Maya's hand presses once against your wrist—an old-school pact.

Ori steps closer, wiping solder from his fingers with the back of his hand. He speaks to you, not to Calder.

Ori Navarro: "If we make their tech talk to ours, we can keep the living wall's adaptive logic in our loop. They'll provide actuation, we provide the ecological intelligence. We keep the brain distributed."

You look at the sensor array Ori is coaxing—the small things he builds feel alive, little weather-educated brains made from reclaimed nets and spare microcontrollers. Your chest tightens with the clarity of it: integration, not subsumption.

Calder folds his hands.

Calder Voss: "We are amenable to a hybrid architecture—isolated control layers, with an API gate for community modules. We prefer a single certification point for safety audits, but we'll include community validators."

The phrase "community validators" sounds engineered to appease, but it will do work if you make it stick. You can already see the amendments you will push: living-module ownership, enforceable hiring language, a clause that places occupancy protections ahead of 'system optimization.'

Your breath shortens—this is the high-point of momentum; the air tastes like metal and caffeine and clean salt. There is a charge in the room that feels very close to triumph. You imagine the Tide Garden with sturdier terraces, more plant beds, children's boots crossing logs without fear. You imagine Saltway still here, not as a sepia exhibit but as a living, arguing neighborhood.

Maya leans forward.

Maya Lin: "We want an emergency rescind clause—if more than X percent of households are displaced in Y months, this pilot is paused and legally reviewed. No vague promises."

A murmur follows. Calder's expression does not harden; he counts the public-relations and legal costs in his head like someone reading a spreadsheet. For the first time in this meeting his posture acknowledges negotiation rather than salesmanship.

Calder Voss: "We can include an independent review board with binding arbitration language. Not ideal for either side, perhaps, but functional."

You taste the axis of compromise. It feels like walking a plank between two safe harbors. Your internal monologue is a rapid checklist—what to accept now, what to reserve for binding language, which gambits will buy you leverage at the municipal meeting.

You: "We will need an absolute commitment to open maintenance training and priority employment for Saltway residents for every phase. And access to the codebase after ninety days—no proprietary lock forever."

Calder: (a small, conceding smile) "Ninety days is aggressive, but for this pilot—yes. We can set a ninety-day escrow threshold for code transparency. Employment quotas will be tied to the phased delivery schedule."

Your chest is simultaneously heavy with responsibility and light with a fierce, forward momentum. The arousal climbs. This is not mere negotiation; this is the original promise of what you and Ori and your neighbors have been trying to make real: a living infrastructure that holds people as it holds water.

You flip to a blank page and write, in the margin: "Pilot accepted—conditional. Draft amendments: Clause 7 redraft; rescind triggers; hiring quotas; ninety-day code escrow; community validators with legal teeth."

Your handwriting looks small and determined. The room waits for your next syllable as if it were a tide mark.

Ori steps up, voice low and urgent in a way that is not a joke.

Ori Navarro: "If this goes through, Saltway needs—no, we build—an open toolkit so other neighborhoods can replicate without VossArc sucking up the IP. I can write the adapters. Jun can blow the whistle if they try to bury it."

Maya laughs softly, a sound of relief.

Maya Lin: "Don't forget me—we'll be on payroll for the outreach."

Old Mara's hand finds yours across the table with surprising strength. Her palm is rough, like driftwood sanded by storms. It grounds you in a history that is not rhetorical.

Old Mara: "You make them a promise they can keep."

The weight of the room condenses into a bright, clear resolve. You can feel the arousal cresting—every heart in that glass room is aligned to a single point: do this thing, but do it in a way that keeps the neighborhood whole.

There is no dramatic flourish; the decision is practical and luminous. You inhale, tasting salt and the coppered tang of possibility.

You: (decisive) "We will pursue the pilot—with conditions formalized now, and legal review on immediate standby. We want co-ownership of the module designs, binding rescind triggers, prioritized hiring, and ninety-day code escrow. We will work the integration with Ori's team. We sign this if those amendments are in."

Calder nods, slow and exact. He reaches for a pen and slides a clean copy of the contract toward you. The glass beyond you blooms with the reflected motion of cranes; the afternoon tilts toward dusk like a held breath turning into action.

You feel the gravity of the moment—how many small clauses will matter, how many signatures will change people's addresses and access. But beneath the calculation is a deeper current: a very positive certainty that this moment could scale what you have fought for, not erase it.

Your pen hovers over the signature line. Your fingertips tingle with the energy of a community leaning forward together. You think of flooded mornings and patched rooftops; you think of Ori's laugh and Maya's ledger-sure stare and Old Mara's unblinking memory.

You let the pen settle. The decision is made, careful and fierce, a pledge wrapped in conditions.

[Music: One bright chord sustains and then softens into patient strings]

You close your notebook for a single, measured second and then open it again, already drafting the first of the amendments you will demand be written into the pilot's DNA.

[Visual: The camera lingers on the small, indigo inked list at the edge of the page. The city beyond the glass is a promise half-built.]

You are ready to walk the plank between autonomy and scale. You are ready to hold the line.
