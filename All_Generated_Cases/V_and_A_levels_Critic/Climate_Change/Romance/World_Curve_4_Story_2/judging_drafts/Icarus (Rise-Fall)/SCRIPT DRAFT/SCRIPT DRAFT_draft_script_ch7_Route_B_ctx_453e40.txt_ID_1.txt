The hall hums like a throat cleared for speaking. You can feel it in the boards under your feet: the small tremor of hundreds deciding, together, to be visible. Jun's recorder hangs at his collar, its little red light reflecting like a second, fainter heartbeat. Maya moves like a compass—directing, folding lists into routes, whispering names that must be carried to the front. Old Mara sits by the door, each movement measured as tide changes.

Your hands are full. A megaphone—borrowed and patched with tape—tucks under one arm; a banner with hand-lettered demands is rolled against your knee. You taste the metal tang of the meg, a little sour under the sweetness of coffee and brine. People press close around you: neighbors with water-stiffened clothes, kids with salt-crusted hair, a young man with wide eyes clutching a painted placard.

Old Mara catches your eye. Up close, her face is a map of storms survived. She reaches forward without fuss and pins a small fishing-hook bead—threaded through a loop of twine—to your lapel. It presses cool against the fabric.

Old Mara: "For steady hands and remembering the shoreline. You carry much, child."

You: "I will. I will not—"

Your voice catches; you let the sentence rest between you and the room. Being pinned by history feels like a benediction and a burden at once. Maya intercepts with a grin that is all business.

Maya Lin: "Routes double-checked. Jun, op-ed copy in my bag. Silas is on speakers—he'll keep the rhythm. Ori, you okay with handing out the planters past the pier?"

Ori's laugh is a short, bright thing. He appears at your shoulder, sleeves smeared green with algae and the day’s compost. His palms are full of tiny reclaimed planters—ceramic shells, each with a sprig of hardy saltwort.

Ori Navarro: "If handing out micro-habitats is a crime, then quick—book me. But no, I'm keeping it small. We want faces in the crowd, not empty headlines."

You: "Keep them for the kids," you say, thumb brushing one planter. "Make people touch the solution."

Ori meets your gaze for a moment, amber to sea-glass. There is warmth there, a pressure both steadying and complicated—everything you want and everything that scares you when you think about promises.

Jun steps forward, the recorder already near his mouth.

Jun: "I'll run live notes. If PR tries to mask their clauses, we have a timestamped record. We go loud, we go public, we go precise."

Maya: "We march to the Glass Pier. We make them see the people who will be pushed. We don't let them spin it into a photo-op. Remember—tenure, community-managed adaptation, no forced relocations."

You inhale. The hall feels small and full and ready to spill into the salt air. High energy flickers through everyone—an electrical charge of shared purpose. Your survivor's guilt softens into something else: mobilized care.

[Visual: The crowd file out; banners unfurl into the evening; lanterns sway over the boardwalk]
[Sound: Shoes on damp wood; a chant rising—low at first, then building]

You step out into the cooling damp. The boardwalk smells of wet timber, seaweed, and the coppery sweetness of last night’s rain. Children thread through ankles, shrieking with the kind of laughter that makes the world feel foldable. Neighbors form ranks not because a uniform binds them but because the boardwalk itself asks for a steady line.

As you move, Ori hands a planter to a small girl holding a tape-wrapped puppet. She cradles it as if it were the truth itself.

Ori Navarro: (softly, to you) "They look at these and remember—this place grows things. It's harder to walk away from a living thing."

You: "That's the point." You say it quietly, a vow sent into the air like a feather. "If we make it visible, the council can't pretend it's abstract."

Ori: (fishing for safer ground) "We'll be seen. You'll do your speech. It'll be sharp, Ava—sorry, you—"

You: (teasing, but firm) "You can call me Ava here. Out there I prefer 'loudspeaker'."

He grins, but there is a flicker of something else—fear, maybe, or the itch to solder and flee. He presses his palm against yours for a second, an anchor.

The march swells. People chant your core line until it becomes a tide: "Tenure now! Saltway stays!" The Glass Pier rises in the distance—a sliver of clinical glass and harsh lights, cranes like neutral giants. The closer you get, the more the air tastes like tension: antiseptic from corporate vans, the citrus tang of PR breath mints, and the electric ozone of cameras.

At the pier’s edge, the crowd converges on a staging area where VossCorp’s temporary structures slump like a polite fort. Work lamps bloom against the grey; a neat row of suits stands near a branded tent. A woman in a crisp jacket clips a two-way to her collar—PR ready. Beside her, a man with a legal pad thumbs through a stack of pages like a dealer with marked cards.

Maya leans in, voice low and hard.

Maya Lin: "Stick to the facts. Names, dates, clauses. If they try to give us fluff, Jun—pin them down."

Jun: "Already on it. I'm streaming to two outlets and clipping every mention of 'dialogue' and 'pilot.' If they phrase it like a handout, we call them."

You climb onto a ladder someone propped up as a platform. The meg hangs heavy in your hand—the physical thing you will use to shape a public moment. Around you, banners wave and faces tilt up. The light catches wet eyelashes and salt-streaked skin. This is what people will see if they turn the channel: not a sanitized press kit, but a living neighborhood present and loud.

You: (into the meg) "Neighbors, friends—Harborlight—listen."

Your voice cracks at the start, and everything waits for you to find solid ground. You steady yourself on the ladder rung and draw in a long, precise breath. You speak not only to the crowd but into the microphones, into the wide lenses, into Jun's recorder whose red light now seems like a tiny star.

You: "We are not a project to be polished and placed behind glass. We are people with tenure, histories, names, names like the ones in the businesses Calder’s plans would displace. We are proposing a future that keeps our hands on the shoreline we built. We will not be relocated in the name of someone else's certainty."

The chant swells in answer, urgent and full-throated. People shout specific names—of landlords and of family-owned shops—until the crowd itself becomes a roll call. Children chant with fierce mimicry. A woman near the front cries out, and the sound cuts clean into the evening air.

From the branded tent, a staged commotion: a camera lifts, and a smooth voice—well-practiced for crisis—steps forward.

VossCorp Spokesperson (Female): "We recognize the community's concerns and we are committed to an open dialogue. We invite representatives to a meeting tomorrow—"

Murmurs ripple through the crowd. The words "open" and "dialogue" come out like soft ropes trying to bind a current. You can taste the containment in them—lipstick on a leash.

You: (into the meg, sharper now) "Dialogue without guarantees is a leash. We need names, we need legally binding tenure clauses, we need the right to manage adaptation, not eviction."

The spokesperson smiles with practiced warmth, lens-friendly. She turns her head slightly as if to consult an unseen script.

VossCorp Spokesperson: "We have legal teams standing by to ensure safety and compliance. Our priority is resilience for the city."

A mutter runs through the crowd—"Safety" couched in a vocabulary that has been used before to sweep people aside. You see the legal man step forward, the pad of papers suddenly very visible like a blade. He does not need to speak to make the threat felt.

Jun's recorder ticks as he steps up close, voice low but urgent.

Jun: "If you speak of resilience, we'll publish the clauses you're keeping quiet. Transparency is not an insult—it's a duty. Tell us: what happens to tenure? Who writes the relocation waivers?"

The spokesperson's smile never reaches her eyes. For a second the moment tilts; you imagine Calder's pale gray gaze like a distant lighthouse—cool, assessing, unmoved by crowds. The PR machinery is precise and fast; the legal machinery is patient and slower, but its presence is heavier.

Then, as if an answer were being read from the same prepared script that had been passed through polished hands, the spokesperson lifts a small placard with a single, glossy line.

VossCorp Spokesperson: "We propose a moderated dialogue. For tonight, we encourage peaceful discussion. We request the crowd disperse to allow our safety teams to—"

Her voice is honey and varnish. Behind her, a second figure steps forward—one of the younger PR people, phone in hand, already sending a curated image: a smiling spokesperson, a few neighbors close enough to frame the narrative, and a caption promising 'partnership.'

Maya hisses under her breath.

Maya Lin: "They're already practicing the narrative. They want the optics, not the accountability."

Ori squeezes your hand at your side. His thumb is warm and strong; he smells of soil and solder.

Ori Navarro: (quietly) "Don't let them package this into a photo. Don't let your words be chopped into a headline they can own."

You look out over the crowd—faces shining with effort, voices raised until they burn the cold air. The moment tastes of galvanizing hope; your chest is an anvil struck by a hammer of purpose. This is what you wanted when you chose to say no to negotiation at the table alone: a people made visible and heard.

Yet the presence of legal pads and polished smiles is a sharp counterpoint. Public pressure has the power to force transparency, but you know, with the quiet knowledge that is both strategic and personal, that it also invites escalation. Enforcement can arrive as quietly as a clipboard and as loudly as a riot shield.

You tighten your grip on the meg. The crowd looks toward you, expectation heavy as wet cloth. The PR camera clicks. Jun's recorder blinks, capturing time like a ledger.

You can feel the arousal—excitement, fear, righteous anger—cresting like a wave. The hall's benediction—Old Mara's bead—warms at your chest. In the air, people's chants fold into one focused line: tenure now; community stays.

The corporate tent opens its flap like a mouth preparing a sentence of power. Legal counsel steps closer, the pad of pages visible, pens poised. The spokesperson inclines her head and speaks again, the voice polished for headlines and for settlement talks.

VossCorp Spokesperson: "We are ready to discuss, in a moderated forum, the path forward."

You chew the word "moderated" like a pebble. It wants to reshape the moment into something smaller, sterner, and easier to photograph.

You: (into the meg, voice low and steady) "We will not be moderated out of our homes. If you want a forum, we set the terms. Tenure—written, public, enforceable—or you do not begin."

Silence bites for a fraction of a second. Then the chant rises anew, louder and rawer than before.

From the branded tent, the legal man straightens, and two plainclothes officers at the perimeter shift their weight. A new kind of machinery hums into life—official, procedural, and observational.

The air thrums. You feel it in the little bead at your lapel, in the wet boards under your feet, in the tightened hands and the smell of wet wool and seaweed. This is the turning point: public pressure has been raised into a visible, urgent force. The corporate machine has responded—smile, offer of dialogue, the mechanical presence of law.

For an instant, everything holds—a tableau of purpose and counterforce. Your breath fogs the air.

You want to keep speaking. You want to make every clause a lantern. You want to stay loud until the tide answers.
