[Scene: Glass Pier Negotiating Room | Late Afternoon — Storm on the Horizon]
[Visual: The negotiating room is a glass-paneled box at the pier’s edge; reflected sky folds into the bay. Outside, a work platform juts over the water where salvaged solar sails and cranes move like slow metronomes.]
[Music: Quickening strings with a warm brass uplift]
[Sound: The harbor’s constant wash, a distant hammer, a gull cutting against wind]

The entrance tastes like ozone and cold metal. You pull the collar of your jacket higher against the thin spray that finds its way through the pier’s seams, and the room’s air—conditioned, curated—settles around you like a different weather.

Calder Voss is already there, the picture of composure: suit cut to an architect’s line, overcoat hanging from his shoulders in that casual, expensive way that reads as intention rather than ease. When he changes his position the room seems to make room for him. The locket at his throat—a thin titanium oval—shifts under his shirt; you catch, briefly, the ghost of a storm photograph between metal and skin. The scent is faint, like rain on hot stone.

He looks at your notebook the way people look at lit maps: curiosity measured against a map of possibilities. His pale gray eyes travel over tide charts, tenure maps, the inked margins where you’ve written “community right of refusal” and “mara clause.” He does not frown. He does not smile. He catalogues.

Calder: "Ms. Maren. Thank you for coming. You have… thorough notes." (He tilts his head.) "This—" (he gestures to your notebook) "—is exactly the kind of granular knowledge we need if this pilot is to mean anything."

You feel a heat at the back of your neck that is not just from the sun striking glass. Thankfulness, yes. But more: the pressure of every hand in the Saltway that will count on what happens next.

Maya slides a thin stack of documents across the table—coffee rings, a fold where a thumb has worn the paper thin. The title on the top page is printed in VossCorp’s precise font: VOSSARC – PILOT PHASE AGREEMENT. The word "pilot" sits between two worlds: it is both opportunity and leash.

Maya: (low, urgent) "Read the rescission and tenure bits first. Don’t let the 'phase' language trick us."

Old Mara sits with her hands in her lap, beads like little reefs catching light. Her face is carved with the years the water has taken and the years the water has given. She doesn’t speak. Her silence weighs but does not admonish; it holds.

On the work platform outside the glass, Ori is up to his elbows in a tangle of reclaimed circuit fragments and polymer tubing. His patchwork coat snaps in the wind. He’s smiling—half at the sensors, half at something ridiculous that he tried to make communicate with a buoy—and when his warm amber eyes find you through the glass, something steadies in your chest. There is a shy gravity in the way he lifts a hand. A small, private thing, but it gives you courage.

Ori: (calling softly when you catch his eye) "Signal looks clean. The array's talking. If we pair it with your tide charts, we can get predictive alerts that don't cost the community an arm."

You breathe once, and the inhale fills the room. You are both anchor and interpreter—physics and people, tide lines and tenure. Calder’s words are already beginning their careful seduction.

Calder: "Our offer is straightforward. Funding for a living seawall module—built to your specifications—with VossArc floodgate integration to test load reduction under live conditions. All materials supplied. Permit fast-tracked through our legal channels. A public report on the outcomes, co-branded. Phase one deploys within three months."

His cadence is even, the kind that implies both certainty and timeline. You hear the numbers as a promise: material security, logistics solved, a permit that would otherwise take months. You also hear the small print, the things that slip between dates and deliverables.

You push the lid of your notebook back and lay it open where he can see the annotations: "DATA OWNERSHIP? COMMUNITY TENURE GUARANTEE? MAINTENANCE SCHEDULE?" Your pen hovers like a fulcrum. Your voice is steady because you have rehearsed steadiness until it’s a reflex.

You (aloud): "We appreciate—this is significant. But I need clarity on three things before we go further: one, the tenure guarantees for residents during and after pilot; two, data ownership and how sensor data will be used; and three—"

Calder: (interrupting, gently) "—maintenance is on us for phase one. Data will be anonymized and shared with the community. And tenure, Ms. Maren, is the hardest but most important piece. We can include a clause that guarantees residency for households participating in the pilot."

Maya’s jaw tightens. Her ledger-face—trained to read the promises of developers—goes into work mode.

Maya: "Language. 'Guarantee' in your world often becomes 'strongly encouraged' at the signature line."

Calder: (smiles, a practiced softening) "I understand the skepticism. Title companies and permits can be labyrinths. We'll write it as a legally binding protective covenant—folks involved are offered a right of first refusal on any relocation proposals tied to this site for a stipulated period."

Your mind runs the scenarios as if through a tide chart: what does a protective covenant look like when a corporation owns the infrastructure that defines access? What does right-of-first-refusal feel like for someone who has lived their whole life on a shoreline property that could become commodified overnight? The pen in your hand finds the margins, and you scribble “time-limited? how enforced?” in shorthand that means more than words: it means watch the legalese.

Calder’s offer hangs between you—a bridge: materials, engineering, the city’s permit machinery moving in your favor. There is an almost visible movement outside as a crane bobs, as if the harbor itself leans in.

You translate the clause language aloud while your internal monologue runs like a sensor array, catching small, fast blips of risk.

You (aloud): "So a co-branded pilot, VossArc supplies floodgate integration, VossCorp maintains phase one but community trains on maintenance, data anonymized and co-owned—"

Calder: "Co-owned, yes. We want the reports to be transparent; we want to learn from this—together. If it works at Saltway, we scale. If it doesn’t, we iterate."

Ori’s voice comes through the glass; up close he sounds like quick wires twisting into a neat braid of resolve and doubt.

Ori: (louder, so everyone can hear) "And the sensors. We build them so the community owns the firmware. We'll make sure there's an open repo, burned to a physical ledger if that's what it takes."

Calder (inclining his head toward Ori): "I like that. Local stewardship of firmware. That’s the kind of distributed model that scales without losing its soul."

Emotion surges under your skin in a way that is unexpected and bright. Calder, in his gray, can make "scale" sound like a love letter. Ori, in his grease and grin, can make "stewardship" sound like a promise. The room is full of people ready to translate those words into action—and the adrenaline of possibility thrums in your chest.

Maya slides a page marked "RESCISSION & EXIT" forward, the line items underlined in black.

Maya: "What does a 'phase-out' look like if VossCorp chooses to repurpose the pier? We need a kill-switch that protects resident tenure and prevents unilateral corporate takeover of infrastructure once integration happens."

Calder’s expression tightens, not hostile but finessed into negotiation mode. He is not averse to compromise; he is averse to anything that threatens the integrity of his timetable or the reputational promise he is building with investors. You can feel the negotiation beginning to braid itself into terms, each with leverage.

Calder: (finally) "We can agree to an independent review board—representatives from Saltway, community engineers, and a mutually agreed third-party auditor. No venture decision will proceed without the board’s approval during the pilot window."

Your mind lists the components you need to secure: written covenant language, an enforceable kill-switch, an independent board with veto power during the pilot, hardware ownership clauses, and a maintenance training plan. Each one is a small boulder that must be placed just so.

Your internal monologue picks up speed. You list points in the margin and color-code them—legal, technical, social—like tidelines shifting.

The room hums; Maya exhales as if she’s been given a rope to hold. || Choice B ("Accept a balanced board with an independent third-party tie-breaker") -> Reaction: "You choose balance to avoid gridlock. Calder smiles, relieved; he agrees readily, but Old Mara’s silent face remains unreadable."]

Dialogue lengthens, expands—Calder and you volley terms; Maya interjects clauses, Ori proposes technical specifications; Old Mara judges through a silence that rings louder than any argument.

Calder: "If we set a ninety-day review after deployment with clear metrics—erosion reduction, load tolerance, resident satisfaction—then we can measure the pilot's success transparently."

You: "And the consequence of failing those metrics?"

Calder: "Either remediation at our cost or decommissioning and restoration at our cost. We put that in contract."

The words land like ballast. You feel the tether between promise and obligation tighten and, with it, a huge release: there are now concrete options to hold them to. The very specter that had seemed to haunt every offer—profit overriding people—has a shape you can counter.

Ori looks at you through the glass. There is longing there, yes, but more: a shared hunger to see something you made together exist outside of sketches and midnight builds.

Ori: (quiet) "If we get this, we can prove community design is more than sentimental. We can show durability, adaptability…we can seed other neighborhoods."

Maya’s hands fold around a pen as if preparing to sign and not at all ready to sign. Her mouth works.

Maya: "I don’t like being sold on 'scalability' as a reason to move people. But I like that this funds our Wallace Project and the youth apprenticeship."

You catch the quick glance from Calder—an exchange that contains strategy and genuine, if pragmatic, alignment.

Calder: "This pilot is time-sensitive. The city's new shoreline ordinance will be finalized in eight weeks; approvals must be underway or we risk losing the permit window."

The room tightens like a drum. Outside, the sky bruises and a stronger wind threads the pier. There is urgency now—mechanical deadlines matched to weather windows—and you feel the high arousal of everything you care about stacked against a calendar.

Your hand moves of its own accord; margin notes fill into sentences that could be amendments: "Protective covenant — 5 yrs minimum; community majority on review board; audit by independent hydrologist; firmware IP escrow; maintenance training schedule; decommission & restoration clause explicit."

You are aware of the way your voice sounds when you speak the next line—decisive but soft, the tone of someone who will carry both the victory and its cost.

You (aloud): "We can do this—if the language is iron, if the board has teeth, and if the community retains control of the core sensors and maintenance protocols."

Calder’s answer is immediate: "Then we put that language in. I'll have my legal team draft the covenant tonight, and we table it for review by your people tomorrow. We can begin fabrication within weeks."

A hot, almost dizzying rush of relief and possibility doubles your pulse. This is the moment a thousand small acts have been steering toward. You can feel the potential like a spring uncoiling.

Old Mara finally speaks, her voice low as a tide pulling back.

Old Mara: "Make them write it where the sea can hear. Promise with ink and with witnesses."

You look at each face around the table—Maya’s ledger-face softening into something like trust, Ori’s amber eyes steady with the practical shine of someone who will make the promises work with elbow grease, Calder’s pale eyes clear and focused, and a room of people who have gone from the brink of displacement to the possibility of defense.

Your chest is loud with heartbeat. You are acutely conscious of the cost of optimism; you have learned to measure its weight. But right now, you also feel something else: the furious, buoyant hope that comes from seeing a plan that can actually be built.

Internally, you thread the final thought through your margin notes: "Pilot pursued—conditional on legal redraft, enforceable board, firmware escrow, decommission clause."

You let the words hover between the surface of possibility and the ledger of action. There is still paperwork, signatures, and nights of redrafting ahead. There will be bargaining and close reading and the need to hold everyone's feet to the letter of this fragile promise. But the slope you have chosen is forward and not away.

You breathe in, letting the harbor air—salt, motor oil, and the metallic tang of storm—fill you. Your fingers close around the edge of the table like a hand anchoring you to this decision. Your mouth opens to give the community the next move, to accept the terms in principle and send things into motion.

The room leans with you.
