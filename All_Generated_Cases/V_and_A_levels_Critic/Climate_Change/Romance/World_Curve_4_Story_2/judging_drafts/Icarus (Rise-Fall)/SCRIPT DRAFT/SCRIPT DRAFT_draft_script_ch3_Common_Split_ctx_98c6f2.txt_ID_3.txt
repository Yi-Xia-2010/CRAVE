[Scene: Glass Pier | Evening]
[Visual: Cold work-lamp pools slicing through damp air; cranes loom beyond in silhouette]
[Music: Low, hopeful piano with a steady rhythmic pulse]
[Sound: Distant surf, the tick of settling metal, a murmur of voices]

The benches creak under the weight of people who have come to listen. Salt-stiff coats and patchwork scarves press close; the air tastes of salt and machine oil and a hundred held breaths. You stand with your notebook at your hip, the cover warm where Ori tapped his charm earlier, the pages pressed flat beneath your palm like something waiting to be written.

Across the open span of the pier a flood of sharp LED light has been strung between scaffolding—clinical and bright against the soft, leaning shapes of the neighborhood. At the far edge where the glass pier meets the work-stands, Calder Voss appears, precise as a crafted hinge: suit tailored to the seam of his shoulder, an overcoat buttoned with geometric patience. His eyes are that pale gray you always feel measure the room—cool, assessing. When he lifts his hand, the signet ring catches and throws a thin, white flare across a plywood easel that holds his presentation.

You notice the small things because you have to: the way his shoes don't leave salt on the boards, the practiced calm in his voice when he begins to speak. It is disarming. It is also a tool.

Calder Voss: "Thank you for coming. I know time and trust are currencies you cannot easily spare." (He lets that sit, then gestures to the easel.) "VossArc is not just engineering. It's neighborhood preservation through design—floodgates that close on schedule, courtyards raised and protected, guaranteed tenure for those who move into our pilot units."

He runs images across a portable screen: children playing under sheltered rooflines; a micro-park that glitters safe and dry; a schematic of a pier folding into a crystalline tower that tucks the rising sea away. The photos are lit so softly they look like memory—warm light on smiling faces. The room's temperature shifts; curiosity folds its hands with hope.

You feel Jun's elbow against you before the transcript is in your hand—damp at the corner from the diesel air outside. He passes it with a whisper.

Jun: (low, urgent) "Read the fine print. Look for relocation clauses and the reversion terms if 'market conditions' change."

You flip the pages with fingers that know tide charts and grant forms. There it is: careful language buried in paragraphs that promise 'guarantee' with one breath and 'contingent' with the next. You can taste metal at the back of your tongue—an anticipatory tang, like the minute before a storm breaks.

Across from you, Old Mara sits with her hands clasped around a necklace of fishing hooks. She watches Calder the way the sea watches a boat—quiet, holding memory like ballast. Her mouth is a thin line; her eyes unreadable but not unfeeling.

Maya is closer to the front. She leans forward until her braids nearly touch the edge of the plywood stage.

Maya Lin: "Promise checks the paperwork, Ava. Don't be sweet to a suit. Get the clauses on record."

Calder answers with the kind of practiced warmth that could melt a hesitant crowd. He shifts his posture and his smile, and it is carefully calibrated to sound humane.

Calder Voss: "We anticipate where the sea will be. Our model keeps families intact—safeguards for tenure, monetary support, relocation assistance if necessary. We propose co-creating the pilot with community leaders."

There is a light in his voice that sells inevitability as kindness. You can hear it— the exact pitch that makes people bend toward the idea of certainty. It's tempting. It is not the whole story.

Ori stands behind you, sleeves rolled, hands stained with enamel and salt. When Calder mentions co-creation his face softens in a way you read as both hope and the slightest hesitation. He leans forward enough to be heard above the murmurs.

Ori Navarro: (soft, compelling) "Show them what we can build. Give them a place to touch—models, silhouettes, a living wall that won't feel like a mall."

There's a warmth to his voice that tangles with the steady piano under the lights. He is always making an argument out of practice—if people can picture a life, they will fight for it. You look at him, at the smear of paint on his knuckle, and something inside you relaxes. He is on your side in the simplest, most practical way.

Calder's smile doesn't falter; he adapts and folds that suggestion into his rhetoric.

Calder Voss: "We have renderings. If a living wall is part of a co-managed pilot, we're open to integrating local design with our engineering."

Jun's whisper comes again, this time sharper.

Jun: (near-hiss) "Open to integrating until the contracts say otherwise. Watch the arbitration clauses. 'Co-managed' can be spun."

You put the transcript down, the paper breathing with the damp, and the room leans into the space between what is said and what is written. The Pause is its own argument.

There is a small, human rhythm in the hall: a cough, a baby’s muffled protesting from the back, someone tapping a ruler on a bench. Your notebook feels heavier now; the scrawl on the current page is an unfinished sentence about guarantees. You trace that sentence with your thumb like it might become a promise by touch.

A neighbor stands to ask a question—Mr. Estevez, who runs the bait shack by the lower dock. He speaks with the bluntness of someone whose life is ledger and tide.

Mr. Estevez: "You say 'guarantee'—what guarantee? If we move into glass, into a tower, who owns that glass? Who says we can come back if the market turns?"

Calder meets him without condescension, and you—already threaded into the conversation by the community's expectation that you will translate—step forward to bridge.

You: "Guaranties mean more than words. Tenure means land, legal protections, and enforcement without costly relocation fees. How will VossArc secure that in practice?"

Calder Voss: (patient) "We will set up a trust, legally binding covenants. Funding is secured through investors committed to long-term stewardship, not short-term flips." (He pauses.) "We can bring the language to the table with counsel. We want your input on the covenants."

There is a beat after that line—an almost tangible inhalation in the room. The offer is framed as partnership; it hangs with a sweetness that eases a knot in your chest. Hope blooms where restraint once was.

Maya, however, shows the other half of the map.

Maya Lin: "Partnership is good if equal. VossCorp must allow community arbitration, independent oversight, and—we'll say it—no clause that enables forced relocation without express consent and legal remedy."

Calder nods like a man used to being pried open one hinge at a time.

Calder Voss: "Those are reasonable starting points. We will include independent oversight in the pilot. We intend for this to be replicable—if it works here, it will work elsewhere."

Jun slips closer and murmurs in your ear while Calder flips the next slide—an image of a courtyard with kids and elder care seating, an idyllic montage.

Jun: (confidential) "If this is true, we can leverage it. If it's theater, we expose them. The story can be the lever."

Your internal ledger ticks options into place: transparency through Jun's reporting, a negotiated pilot to secure resources, or building silent proof with Ori's models and prototypes. Each is a different risk to the same heart—Saltway's ability to remain itself.

The room's energy folds around these small decisions. People look to you with the weariness and hope of those who need someone to stitch a direction. They want a leader who balances stubbornness with openness. They want you to be right.

Calder's tone is intentionally human—he tells a short story about his own childhood: a photo in his locket, a shoreline with a small house and a storm that took it. He says this so the mathematics of engineering takes on the texture of memory.

Calder Voss: "I lost my home once. I built to make sure other people would not have to. We can be meticulous and humane."

Later, when the presentation slides dim, Jun leans in with the damp transcript open between your hands and taps a clause.

Jun: (pointing) "Look—'reversion to developer control' after five years if 'maintenance thresholds' are not met. They define those thresholds. We either negotiate iron-clad language or make this public and force them to keep the terms."

You meet Ori's amber eyes across the crowd. He hasn't stopped looking at you, the question folded into his thumb tapping the edge of his palm: How far will you press? How much do you trust a hand extended in light?

Ori's voice this time is barely more than breath, and it goes straight to the place in you that learns by building.

Ori Navarro: "If we show them—if we build a living stretch right here, put our own scaffolds around their talk—people will understand the difference. They won't be swayed by renderings alone."

His suggestion is not adversarial. It is craft. It is an invitation: proof before promise. You can hear the relief in it—the prospect of an answer that lets you keep both hands in the work and still be true to the community.

Old Mara's fingers twist the hooks; she finally speaks. Her voice is dry but steady, like driftwood rubbed smooth.

Old Mara: "Words are strong in wind. Laws, stronger. But remember this—people who feel seen fight. People who feel sold leave. Make them see us."

You catch yourself drafting the possible paths in your head: negotiate and bind Voss into iron; escalate with Jun and force the public eye; or keep the flame small and real with Ori, then show not tell. Each path bristles with consequence, but each also holds a shard of hope.

Your field notebook rests heavy against your hip; the scrawl at the top of the page is suddenly all that matters. You write a single line: KEEP THEM IN PLACE. Then you look up, and the room is waiting. The arousal — the steady, midline beat of the decision — pulls at you until you feel every eye in the pier like a tide.

You breathe in the smell of wet wood and the faint copper of the work lights. You think of the children in Calder's slides, the elders on your benches, the faces of those who built the tide garden with nothing but hands and a stubborn idea. You think of Ori's hands around reclaimed metal, Jun's recorder ready to catch a misstep, Mara's unadorned truth.

The choice is not only political—it is private. It determines the shape of your work and the shape of your life with Ori. It will ask you to balance hard edges with the soft, persistent thing that keeps the neighborhood here.
