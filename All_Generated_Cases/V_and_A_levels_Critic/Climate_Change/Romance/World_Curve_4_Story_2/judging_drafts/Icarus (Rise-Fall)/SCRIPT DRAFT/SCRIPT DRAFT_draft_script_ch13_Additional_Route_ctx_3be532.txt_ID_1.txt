[Scene: Glass Pier | Dusk]
[Visual: The Glass Pier stretched against a bruised sky; scaffolding and cranes silhouette like ribs. A mosaic mural of seaglass and circuitry unfurls across the pier's pavilion, backlit by warm LED filaments. Small speakers are woven like beads into the installation.]
[Music: A tense, swelling string motif undercut by distant percussion]
[Sound: Cameras clicking, a low murmur of the crowd, a child's recorded laughter threading through the speakers]

You counted the seconds with the tilt of your chest, felt the weight of the signal you had given Ori like a line thrown over surf. The panels ignite in slow bloom—seaglass and copper catching light, reclaimed circuits pulsing with soft, human rhythms. Voices leak out from the speakers: neighbors telling tiny, ordinary histories—Old Mara humming a fishing tune, a kid bragging about the first crab he caught off the boardwalk, a woman reading the name of the street where she was born.

For a breath you feel something like victory. The crowd exhales with you. Phones lift. A child's voice, high and proud, says "This is home." That sentence lands like a bell.

[Visual: Close-up on the mural—fragments of circuit paths glow into shapes that mimic waves. Children gather at the edge, their faces washed in warm light.]
[Sound: The murmur swells into applause and then, almost instantly, the other sounds rush in—industry, analysis, a quiet suit-radio voice you can’t place]

You step closer. The tactile weave of the mural reaches out to your fingers: soldered seam, smoothed seaglass, fishing twine kissed with salt. You can feel the sweat at your hairline, the salt crusting your collar. Around you, people smile and press their hands against the installation as if to check whether stories are solid.

Ori stands two paces away—grime at his knuckles, a grin that refuses to settle into anything steady. He watches the cameras catch him like a net. When your eyes meet, he tucks a loose curl behind his ear with a motion half-playful, half-frightened.

Ori Navarro: "We made them listen." (he says it like a joke and like an accusation)

You: "We made them look." (your voice is thin; the words do their small work and stop)

Ori's smile falters. He glances at the cranes and then at the corporate banner that glides across a drone-screen above the pier: VossCorp—VossArc: Resilience by Design. The banner bleeds authoritative blue into the warm light of your work.

[Sound: A faint, corporate-voiced commentary filters across a PA—polished, calm, legally measured]

The praise arrives—fast, viral, performative. Clips of laughing children and glinting glass populate feeds. A lifestyle columnist calls it "a moving exercise in community design." An influencer frames it as "the cutest coastal activism you'll see today." You hear the adjectives like stones dropping into a deep, hollow well.

And then a different sound: a measured voice on a live feed, the sort that can dress displacement in linens and call it charity.

[Visual: A wide-angle shot of the pier. A mobile screen mounted on a VossCorp service boom scrolls Calder Voss' face—calmer than tidewater, coiled.]
[Sound: Calder's public address, broadcast through local media]
Calder Voss: "The compassion you've shown is…inspiring. But sentiment alone cannot withstand increasing storm events. Efficient, engineered solutions—scaled, managed—are the only way to guarantee safety for all citizens. We must be pragmatic."

You feel the floor tilt. His cadence is the same thing you've heard at council meetings—wrap the hard trade-offs in soft words. Efficiency. Safety. Guarantee. He speaks of storms as inevitabilities to be contained, not systems to be negotiated with. He doesn't say the word "relocation" aloud, but his smile sharpens around it.

You: "That's not the point, Calder. This—" (you gesture to the mural, to the people, to the moist air itself) "—this is our story. Tenure is not a marketing line."

Calder's broadcast folds into a Q&A, the reporters asking like teeth. He answers each question with the precise calorie-count of policy-speak. There is no rupture in his gentleness; his words are a slow hydraulic force.

[Music: Strings rise into a frantic counterpoint; percussion quickens into a march]
[Sound: A rustle—paper; a muted clack of a keypad]

You notice a man in a neat coat by the crane's console—he's not on camera. He leans in to another suited figure, passing a thin stack of paper. The words on the top sheet are small, bureaucratic: "Authorization—enhanced relocation protocol." Your skin prickles.

Ori stays still beside you, eyebrows knotted. He watches the men with a child's furious clarity.

Ori Navarro: "They were never going to let the story stay the story, were they?" (his hands are talking faster than his mouth)

You: "They reframed the frame. They make people feel safe by imagining you fragile." (the words land raw)

Ori's face tightens. He looks at the mural, then at the cameras, then back at you. He reaches for your hand and doesn't say the things you are both dying to say—about leases and legal clauses and memos signed at midnight.

Ori Navarro: "I thought if we softened the image—made them human—they'd see the politics. I thought they'd have to reckon." (he swallows hard) "I didn't expect…this."

You: "You expected less law and more heart to do the work of law." (it's not a question)

Ori's jaw moves. He is the maker who wants to prove the world with beauty; you are the organizer who wants proof stamped, enforceable, filed. The difference between your choices lays between you like a plank ready to snap.

Heat floods your face. The mural keeps talking—soft human voices floating like buoyant debris—but authority is assembling its machinery. Security teams, previously polite, find a new firmness. They uncoil tape reels and begin to cordon the edges of the pier. Volunteers blink, then fold planters into crates with movements that look like giving up.

A woman you know—Rima, who runs a noodle stand on the lower walk—comes up to you eyes wet, hands trembling as she folds a string of seedlings into a box. "They said it's for our safety," she says, as if tasting the cud of a lie. "They said they'd help move things."

You watch her, the motion numb. Your lungs squeeze. The air tastes metallic.

A security officer presses a small card between his gloved fingers and offers it like an apology.

Security Officer: "We have authorization. For temporary measures, ma'am. We'll help relocate possessions if you comply."

You: "Temporary." (the word cracks) "For how long?"

Security Officer: "Until the VossArc plan is enacted." (he read the phrase from his palm)

You could set the speakers alight with your voice. You could try to pull every camera to your side and make the word "tenure" trend until it chokes on policy. You could place your body between the planters and the tape and risk being carried away.

Your heart beats so hard it is a drum in your ears. The arousal avalanche arrives—panic and fury braided into one fierce rope.

Ori draws closer, his palm flat against the mural's seams, feeling for the stability of something made with his hands. He draws his own charm necklace from his pocket and presses a small, polished seaglass shard into your palm. The glass is cool; it trembles with the faint electric hum from the circuits.

Ori Navarro: "Keep it." (his voice is low, brittle) "For when the boardwalk forgets our names."

You look at him, at the fine lines around his eyes, at the way he has stained his coat with a hundred projects that were meant to be gifts. You think of every night you stayed up writing grant requests and meeting minutes, negotiating safe terms. You think of every time you let someone else carry the visible grief because you had to keep the meeting moving.

You: "Did you ever think—" (the rest of the sentence splinters) "—that beauty alone could stop a corporation?"

Ori's thumb rubs the glass, a small, secret ritual. He laughs without humor.

Ori Navarro: "No. But I thought it could open a door. Maybe someone would step through."

You hold his gaze like a ledger. Somewhere in that look is accusation, and in yours is the naming of a failure you cannot afford but must feel.

[Sound: A new kind of engine—a municipal service barge idling near the pier; a horn that cuts like a blade]
[Music: The strings shred into sharp, percussive stabs; the background hum becomes a rising siren]

A municipal representative appears at the pierhead, a woman with a clipboard wearing the city's crest. She surveys the scene with an expression that means paperwork will be done, people will be moved, and the cameras will trace the choreography of help.

Municipal Rep: "We will coordinate with VossCorp for temporary relocation assistance. We'll prioritize elders and families with children." (she offers it like balm)

You: "Priority?" (the syllable tastes like a ration)

Municipal Rep: "Prioritization minimizes risk. It ensures safety."

You can feel the air compress. People who had laughed central to your mural now bundle up their memories into boxes that are waterproof and neutral. Old Mara sits on the bench, hands folded like a small reef, eyes far away.

You walk the line between planters and tape, counting names like beads. Each name is a ledger of promises you cannot yet collect on. Cameras pan in slow, hungry motion. The mural lights keep singing.

A journalist pushes through with a handheld cam and asks Calder's team rhetorical questions—his footage will be cut into a tidy package: humanized community plus inevitable solution. The frames blur.

You: "This was supposed to be a tool." (you say it to Ori and to the people arranging the tape, and to the world that only looks long enough to take a photo) "Not…a postcard."

Ori Navarro: "We made a postcard." (he looks down at the seaglass in your palm) "But not a permit. Not a guarantee."

Silence drops like a net. Around you, the pier wheezes with the small mechanisms of relocation. The mural's stories keep going—Old Mara's tune now threaded under the rustle of packing paper—and you realize the image you created has already been folded into someone else's argument.

Calder Voss (via broadcast): "We will partner to protect residents. Relocation will be managed, and relocation aid is available to those affected."

He has put kindness in the sentence like lacquer. Somewhere between lacquer and steel there is a decision. The memo at the crane console is signed. The tape tightens.

You step back and feel the pier tilt, not with the sea but with the pressure of a narrative that has been captured and repurposed. The mural that had been a protest of agency has become, in others' hands, an exhibit of vulnerability.

Ori takes both of your hands in his—rough palms, trembling fingers—and for a moment the world narrows to the heat between you.

Ori Navarro: "I'm sorry." (the apology is small and precise)

You: "I'm tired." (it is maybe the truest sentence you have spoken in months)

He presses his forehead to yours in a motion that is more intimate than words, and you let the grief come like gravity. The crowd continues to watch, and cameras continue to harvest sentiment.

[Visual: The mural's lights dim as portable floodlights from VossCorp focus elsewhere. Security tape flutters. Planters boxed. The children's voices on the speakers loop once more, distant now.]
[Music: A low, sustained chord; the strings thin to a single, cold note]

You fold the seaglass charm into your palm and feel its edges. It carries the weight of what you tried and the futility of what followed. There is no dramatic confrontation—no single person to blame, only a system that can wear tenderness as cover.

You turn away from the mural. The pier smells like wet wood, solder, and old promises. Ori's hand slips from yours as he steps back to help a neighbor lift a crate. He looks at you, and there is love in the way his eyes insist on staying, and there is a question there that neither of you can yet answer: will staying mean losing each other to the work, or will the work become the home you share?

You walk along the edge as security teams finish taping off sections and municipal vans glide in. The tide creeps higher against pilings already softened by the year's rains. People fold up planters as if folding up summer. Cameras record the tenderness and the defeat in the same take.

You stop at the edge of the boarded walkway. The mural behind you hums the last of its stories. A gull calls, thin as a fingernail, and the cranes stand like patient teeth against the darkening sky.

You place your hand over your heart and let the salt and the sound fill you. The city will take this moment—its light and its humanity—and reframe it into the language it knows best: an object lesson, a press release, a step in a plan that will, in the end, remove people from place.

You close your fingers around the shard until it stops trembling. The weight is small and absolute.
