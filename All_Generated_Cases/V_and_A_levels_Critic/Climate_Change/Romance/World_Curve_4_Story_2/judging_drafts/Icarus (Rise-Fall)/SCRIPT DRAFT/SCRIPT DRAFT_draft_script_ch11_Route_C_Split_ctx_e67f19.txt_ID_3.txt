[Scene: Ferryworks Greenhouse | Night]
[Visual: Warm lamplight spilling from portholes; the Ferryworks hull glitters with mosaic tiles. Living walls curve like quiet ribs, wires braided with sea-glass catching the light.]
[Music: Fast, bright strings under a driving percussive pulse]
[Sound: A steady hum from the prototype; laughter and low, excited murmurs; phones shuttering]

You hold the notebook against your ribs as if it were a talisman. The little green LED you lit a breath ago pulses like a heartbeat—clean, insistent. The hum that rises from the junction box is no longer a private thing between you and Ori; it is a current the room drinks. You can feel it through the soles of your boots, through the boards beneath your fingers, through the salt in the air.

Mangrove roots curl into sculpted bio-concrete like hands finding purchase. Tiny sensors blink in offset rhythms—moisture, salinity, structural flex—each light a promise written in green. Ori's living wall is a thing of impossible tenderness: woven reclaimed circuitry that glints like mollusk shells, pockets of soil threaded into decorative lattices where seedlings jostle for sun. Children press small palms to the glass and giggle when the micro-sprayers puff a fine mist; an elder from the Quarter rubs her thumb over a root and closes her eyes as if remembering a safer tide.

You stand a moment and simply take them in—their faces, the way the lamplight carves angles from the damp wood, the smell of wet soil and solder. This is what you wanted when you stared at tide charts and price sheets and built lists of who had to be saved first. It's not polished. It smells like work. It smells like life.

Ori slips beside you, boots whispering on the metal grate. Up close you see the tiny flecks of algae caught in his hair and the way his amber eyes are lit by reflected green LEDs. He grins—a crooked, luminous thing that makes the rest of the room seem softer.

Ori Navarro: "They look at it like it's a miracle, huh?"
You: "They look at it like something they can keep. That's better than a miracle."
Ori Navarro: (laughs) "You're the worst at chanting slogans and the best at making miracles sound boring. Good mix."

Ori reaches over, tapping a sensor. A pulse of warm air moves through the greenhouse as a pump cycles up. The sound is mechanical and intimate—like a living thing rearranging the air to breathe.

Neighbor Woman: "Can I—can I put a bay on that wall?"
Silas (brightly): "Make room. I'll plant the seeds myself; I got the cuttings."
You: "Yes. Yes, please. We'll mark it with your name."

The requests come in a tide: names, hands, quick stories of damp basements and lost cabinets, tiny triumphs retold as if they were epics. You answer each with the patient clarity you've learned to hold in meetings and at kitchen tables. Each yes tightens a knot of relief in your chest.

Jun arrives in a smear of rain-damp coat and electric energy, a recorder swinging from a neck strap and eyes already hungry. He moves through the crowd like a comet, making notes with one hand and taking in faces with the other. When he looks at the living wall his expression cracks open into something like awe.

Jun Park: (under his breath) "This is the headline we wanted."
You: (sharp, smiling) "Keep your head on your shoulders, Jun. We don't need headlines; we need houses."
Jun Park: (soft) "Sometimes headlines are doors. Sometimes they slam them open."

He hands you his tablet. A soft cascade of notifications rolls across the screen—photos, shared posts, a fav from someone you don't know, a tag from an influencer who once worked on coastal design. Someone in the crowd had taken a wide-angle shot of the greenhouse, Ori's circuits shining like a constellation, and it had found a new audience in minutes.

Jun Park: "They posted. Someone with a decent follower list called it 'the most hopeful thing on the internet tonight.' People are already asking where this is."
You: (breath in) "Hope gets people to look. Does it get people to act?"
Jun Park: "It gets them to care long enough for action to become possible."

For a heartbeat, the room is a microphone; a thousand small lights bloom as phones lift. The hum in your chest syncs to their shutters. The living wall doesn't feel like proof anymore—it feels like an argument you can hold up and let the city see.

Ori's hand finds the small of your back. It's an anchor and a fuse at once.

Ori Navarro: (quiet) "If they like it, maybe they'll want it everywhere. That's the point, right?"
You: "Yes. But 'everywhere' can mean different things. We have to decide who 'everywhere' is for."

Silas sidles up, cheeks flushed with pride. He holds out a seed packet with clay-smudged fingers like an offering.

Silas: "This one's for Old Mara. She said mangrove and memory are the same thing."
You: "Then let's put Old Mara's name on the first plot. We'll make a marker."

The crowd folds around you like a living map. There is laughter and elbow-jostles and the quick, soft authority of people reclaiming space. That warmth is a fuel you did not know you needed so badly—now you feel urgent, propelled.

Then Jun's voice drops. He draws you close, eyes electric.

Jun Park: "Listen. I don't want to be the bearer of a damp blanket—"
You: "Say it."
Jun Park: "VossCorp's legal team. They flagged the post. It looks like they'll try to file an 'emergency assessment' claim to justify an inspection. If they get a judge to sign—"
You: "They could seize it?"
Jun Park: "They could order a shutdown, cite 'unvetted structural risks.' Calder's people move fast when optics turn. They'll dress it up like public safety."

For a wild second your chest stutters—not with fear, but with the exact opposite: adrenaline that tastes like a bright clean thing. The room's noise sharpens into focus. People continue to laugh and touch the plants, unaware. Your hands tighten on the notebook. This is the moment you trained all your anxieties and discipline toward: the danger isn't the point; the point is what you do with it.

Ori's jaw sets. He forces a smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Ori Navarro: "They can file all the paperwork in the world. They won't stop a seed. They won't stop Old Mara's memory."
You: "They could slow us. They could make people afraid to be seen here."
Ori Navarro: (softly) "Then let's make them afraid of being on the wrong side of this."

You feel the energy in the room spike: a thousand small resolves, hands palm-pressing to bare boards, a boy—maybe ten—standing on a crate and shouting the name of a street you know like the back of your hand. The percussive strings swell; you can taste salt and metal and possibility.

Jun leans in, voice low and fast, every sentence a rope thrown.

Jun Park: "We have options. We can push the designs public—open-source everything and force the city to react. We can make a careful exposé that ties VossCorp’s playbook to this moment. Or we keep it local, shield people we can, and iterate offline. Each path has a risk and a prize."
You: "If we go public, Calder will come swinging. If we go to Jun with an exposé, it becomes a dam-breaking story but takes time. If we stay—"
Jun Park: "You protect the Quarter tonight. You limit the fallout. But you might lose the broader leverage."

Ori puts a hand on your wrist—firm, immediate—then removes it as if to give you space. His fingers leave a map of heat across your skin.

Ori Navarro: "Whatever you decide, I'm with you. I built what I could; you turned it into a place people can hold. I'm not stepping away from that."
You: "I don't want us to be the reason people lose their homes because we were reckless."
Ori Navarro: (quiet, almost a challenge) "And I don't want us to squander the chance to show the world a better way because we're afraid of another letterhead."

The talk stretches. Voices knot and unknot. You breathe in and out until the pulses slow, but the underlying tempo climbs. You are at the crest of a wave that might lift the city or break on the rocks; your palms are full of possibility, your ears full of the community's small sounds—lids clinking, murmured plans, a child's small violin practiced badly but with enormous heart.

You think of Mara's fingers, the ledger pages of tenure marks, the patchwork of lives that would fall through a bureaucratic sieve. You think of Ori's resigned jokes about corporate powerpoint slides and the way he once walked away from a lab because something inside him unclenched. You think of Jun's recorder, always hungry for the story that can tilt power.

The greenhouse thrums. Outside, rain starts—first a scatter of bright beads that freckle the portholes, then a steady silver. The weather presses in like an audience leaning forward. Your heartbeat matches the pulsing LED; your inhalations are small, hot.

You can feel the future like a line drawn in salt on the floor. It divides into three paths that all feel right under certain lights: one throws the design into the world and dares the city to follow; another turns the exposure into an instrument—careful, evidentiary, potentially catalytic; the last knits protection and refinement tighter, keeping the Quarter safe tonight while the rest of the city waits.

Your hands, the same ones that closed the breaker, hover over the notebook. The decision is a bright, unavoidable thing that sits between you and the people who look to you like hope.
