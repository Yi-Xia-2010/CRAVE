[Visual: Cold work-lamps scatter hard light across wet steel; cranes beyond the pier cut jagged silhouettes against a bruised sky]
[Sound: Voices rise and fall like a tide; the soft scrape of benches as people shift; a distant, steady thrum of pumps]

The hall on the pier smells of salt, wet fabric, and the low metallic tang of equipment left drying. Benches creak under bodies that have learned to sit still when a thing that could change their lives is being spoken about. You feel the familiar pressure behind your ribs — a responsibility that has a weight you carry as if it were a wet jacket. Tonight that jacket fits tight.

[Visual: Camera finds you near the front, notebook closed at your hip; Old Mara sits two rows ahead, hands curled around her fishing-hook beads]
[Music: Low, hopeful piano; steady, unobtrusive]

Calder Voss steps into the pool of light like a practiced wave. His suit eats the lamp-glare; the signet ring at his knuckle flashes, an exact, human punctuation. He is not theatrical. He is precise, and that precision has its own persuasion.

Calder Voss: "Good evening. Saltway — thank you for inviting us to speak directly with the community we intend to serve." (He inclines his head just enough to be sincere.) "VossArc is not a replacement. It is a promise: floodgates engineered to a new standard, housing with guaranteed tenure for those who relocate, public courtyards elevated above the worst tides. We are here to offer security."

You listen to the cadence of it. The words are crafted to land like lifebelts. Every sentence is mapped to a fear the city knows: loss of home, the terror of tides that do not care. Images unfurl on the screen behind him — children in clean, sunlit courtyards; a model of a crystalline tower folding into a pier like a carefully solved puzzle.

Jun moves beside you and slides a damp transcript across your palm. The paper smells of rain and the printer ink smeared at the edges; someone has been running copies in a hurry.

Jun: (low) "They're polished, Ava. But read the caveats — conditional tenure, relocation managed by a third party, clauses that let them re-evaluate after ten years. There's leverage in the language."

You open the transcript with fingers that still know the rightness of repair. The legal text feels colder than the lights. Your throat tightens, but the room is quiet enough that Calder's next sentence drifts to you as if meant.

Calder Voss: "We are not here to displace for profit. We are here to provide a scalable blueprint. Think of this as a pilot that can protect the children of Harborlight for generations."

A murmur runs through the benches — curiosity, hope, a thread of skepticism. Old Mara's eyes, layered with a history of storms, don't move much; she watches like she always does, the way someone watches a thing they've seen before and knows how it ends if not tended with care.

Maya leans forward, voice a low cut through the hum.

Maya Lin: "Promises are fine. Paperwork is finer. We don't sign faith — we need proof of protections and a clear path to community management."

Calder smiles, restrained, and produces, from a slender folder, flow charts and projected timelines. He speaks of inspectors, bonded contractors, and a public trust.

Calder Voss: "We are open to co-management models. Partnering with the community reduces risk and builds local capacity. We can pilot together."

You taste salt and something metallic at the back of your tongue — the sound of a possibility that is half shelter, half trap. The room tilts in a way that makes you want to stand and speak and map every 'if' into a 'then'. Instead you find your hands on your notebook, the scrawl of tide charts and small, hopeful sketches pressing under your fingers.

Ori is near the side, the warm shoulder of him a familiar shape in a crowd. He catches your eye and lowers his voice, playful but steady.

Ori Navarro: (soft) "Show them what we can build. Let them touch it. If they can see a living wall that breathes, they imagine staying."

His suggestion is not an argument against Calder so much as an alternate language: craft and touch over sleek projection. You can see how both might be true — and how both might unravel in different ways.

Calder walks the room with his voice, not a performer but a planner.

Calder Voss: "We can offer guaranteed tenure for relocated residents, plus investment in public infrastructure that will be open to everyone. We want residency guarantees written into the trust."

The phrase lands with a different weight now. Guaranteed tenure: a word you have built campaigns around. Guaranteed tenure that means relocation — that is the axis of the trade. Eyes turn to you, not because you crave the light, but because you are the person left breathing in the space where roots and policy meet.

You respond, because silence is a shape too often filled by others.

Ava Maren: (You) "Guaranteed tenure sounds like safety. But relocation isn't tenure. It severs place. We need protections that let people remain in Saltway wherever possible. Will VossArc fund community-run modules, or only the centralized structure?"

Calder meets your gaze with the same pale gray measuring that the city has taught you to read as both apathy and calculation.

Calder Voss: "We can allocate funds to community-managed pilots. The design is modular. Where feasible, community-led infrastructure will be incorporated. We cannot promise every single footing remains exactly where it is — adaptation requires trade-offs."

There's a beat long enough to be a small storm. You sense the room dividing gently: those who hear 'funds and partners' and those who hear 'trade-offs.' In that beat, Ori comes a fraction closer; his hand brushes yours, a small punctuation that steadies.

Maya cuts in, faster now, edging toward the impatient flame she often is.

Maya Lin: "Promise checks the paperwork, Ava. Don't let poetic assurances replace a lawyered clause. If we entertain a pilot, we make their lawyers answer to ours first."

Jun, restless, presses a suggestion into your palm — not another transcript but an angle.

Jun: "We can time a release. We expose weak clauses in daylight; we force them to back up the guarantees with legal teeth. But that makes the fight public, fast."

Ori taps his welding goggles against his satchel, thinking in the hands-on way that makes him both irresistible and maddening.

Ori Navarro: "Or we build something people can see and touch first. Let them fall in love with staying here because staying is beautiful. It'll take time. It keeps us autonomous."

Your chest does the familiar tug of being pulled in two directions: the pragmatic urgency that wants contracts and lawyers and a timeline; and the tactile patience that believes solidity comes from making, from showing. It's the same split that runs through the city — the engineered certainty and the stubborn, slow craft of living systems.

You look to Old Mara. Her hands, knotted and familiar with the way fishhooks bite, rest in her lap. Her face is unreadable, as it must be when a life has been lived in storms.

Old Mara: (soft, gravelled) "Listen to the promises but remember what promises cost. Keep your hands where you can work."

Her plainness is a small beacon. You breathe a little easier; hope nudges you — the kind of hope that isn't naïve but insistently forward-facing.

The building momentum feels like breath held and then let out with intention. Voices circle the idea like gulls around a baited line: Calder's measured architecture, Maya's legal insistence, Jun's appetite for exposure, Ori's plea to prototype with beauty. Each path promises safety in different currencies — tenure, transparency, autonomy — and each asks for a price.

You flip the notebook open, finding the scrawl at the top of the page — a messy list, a small map of what matters: tenure, community control, livability. Your pen hovers. This is one of those thin moments when a decision sets the next tide.

People look at you not because you are grand but because you have been willing to do the small, exhausting work of holding systems together. You feel the room's trust like a buoyant weight.

Maya's words land with urgency: "If we don't get legal safeguards on day one, we hand them the language to evict us later."

Jun's voice is quick and certain: "And if we make it public while they have the optics, they cave or they double down. Transparency can be leverage."

Ori's voice, quieter, cracks soft with his maker's faith: "Or we build something beautiful and undeniable. Give people another reason to stay."

You, who have spent nights sketching living walls and mornings explaining tidal gradients to wary neighbors, understand that all three are true. They are not mutually exclusive in principle — but resources and time bend toward one direction at a time.

The room hushes. This is the moment the meeting has been folding toward: a decision about posture — collaboration, confrontation, or quiet proof. Your pulse is steady but rising, like a tide shifting with purpose. The arousal of the room is mid — engaged, expectant, not frantic.

You set your pen to the paper and write one word: CHOICE.

You look up and speak, the words both private and public.

Ava Maren: (You) "We have three paths to keep Saltway here. We can negotiate a hybrid pilot — take resources and demand co-management and legal safeguards. We can refuse and escalate: force transparency with Jun's reporting and a public campaign. Or we can prototype quietly with Ori, build the proof we need and delay publicizing until it can't be ignored."

Silence answers, full of potential rather than threat. People lean in, as if they too are trying the weight of each option in their hands.
