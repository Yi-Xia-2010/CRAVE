The storm doesn't come like a rounded thing; it comes snapped and particular. Rain slants like wire. An upstream reservoir—someone's engineering decision miles inland—opens a sluice, and the current that meets the harbor isn't what you expected. It rakes along the piers with a violence the models had not foretold.

At first the pilot prototypes sing under the stress—modules flex, anchors take. You can almost read their proudness; you have half a dozen people under your voice, shouting adjustments, knots fastened by salt-stiff fingers. For a moment there is a fragile, furious joy: models you built with neighbors are holding water back. Kids in the Tide Garden lean in and cheer into the roar.

Then a shear line opens where no one imagined the stress would concentrate: between a reclaimed concrete module and the corner of a legacy levee. A seam you had noted in a sketch—an uncertainty you had meant to test under calmer conditions—gives, and a section of a living wall tears away with a sound like wet canvas being peeled off bone.

The failure is small, then it is news. Water finds the breach and makes it hungry. It moves like a sentence being rewritten: where protection existed, there is now a hole. Down the line, another prototype shudders. A family outboard of the break scrambles crates into a rowboat; their children cling to a coil of rope like a lifeline.

A wave of clarity, quick and hot, crashes through you: this is exactly what you had feared. Not that a pilot would fail—you always design for failure—but that the failure would be seized as proof that small things cannot bear the world.

You feel the ground tilt under your feet: a moral fulcrum shifting. The designs you made to be weapons against displacement are now being reinterpreted as architected excuses.

The aftermath is a stratified thing. Some neighborhoods—small, strong clusters where pilots held—hold fast and begin to rebuild with your designs stitched into their edges. There are photos on your phone of children planting again, of a small terrace that bent but did not break. Those images feel like balm and like shards.

You stand in the rain that comes after the storm, the kind that feels like the world trying to scrub its hands clean. You taste metal and old promises. The city has rearranged itself around an answer that is half truth and half pretext.

Ori finds you on the pier. His coat is streaked with salt; his hair is plastered to his forehead. You both know the truth of choices that stack into consequence: speed versus rigor, openness versus controlled scaling. The lists of justifications are long and they do not comfort.
