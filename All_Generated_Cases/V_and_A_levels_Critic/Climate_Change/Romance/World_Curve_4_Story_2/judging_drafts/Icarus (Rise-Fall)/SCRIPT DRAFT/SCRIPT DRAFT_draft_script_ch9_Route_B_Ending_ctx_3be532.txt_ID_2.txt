[Scene: Glass Pier | Late Afternoon — Cloudbank Gathering]
[Visual: The pier stretched like a limb into the harbor; VossCorp banners flash cold light from the glass tower beyond. Lines of hired security in dark vests mirror the angle of the cranes. Cameras on tripods tilt and refocus. Boards slick with earlier spray.]
[Sound: A low, metallic hum of generators; distant gull calls swallowed by the hiss of an incoming wind. Footsteps slap on wet planks. A megaphone breathes as someone checks batteries.]

You stand with the megaphone cupped to your mouth because the crowd needs a place to hold its fury and its fear. Your voice feels like a thin rope in your throat — not fragile, but stretched. Around you, the pier is a chorus of neighbors, reporters, and the polished silence of people paid to watch.

You've rehearsed the lines, the facts, the cadence. You did not rehearse how it would feel to have cameras look for conflict rather than truth.

[Music: Percussion cuts in—fast, urgent]
[Visual: Close-up on your hand gripping the megaphone; the texture of the rubber seam under your fingers, the salt grit under your nails.]
[Sound: A single tweet-tone from a nearby phone: someone has already started tweeting the scene.]

You lift the megaphone. Your voice splits the air.

You (through megaphone): "We have documents. We have testimony. You cannot call this relocation anything but what it is—displacement dressed up as safety. Hear us—"

Security shifts like a dark wave. A line takes form, crossing the pier between you and the glass pier's entrance. Their faces are trained into neutral masks. A police captain's rainproof collar flips as he steps forward, clipboard in hand.

Security Lead: "Clear the pier. For safety—"

You cut in, the megaphone hot against your lips.

You: "Safety is not a notice taped to a door during a storm. Safety is tenure. Safety is listening to the people who live here."

A flash of cameras. A voice from the VossCorp dais, recorded and polished, drifts down on speakers: "We offer transitional housing and comprehensive relocation support. These measures are in the public interest."

Jun is at your elbow, recorder exposed, his jaw working as he checks levels. He leans close enough for you to feel the wet of his sleeve.

Jun: "Ava—(low) the legal team is feeding live feeds to investors. They're framing the crowd as obstruction."

You swallow.

You: "Then we show them what obstruction really looks like—people packing what they can carry while men with badges hold the line. We do not let them erase faces."

The wind answers like an instrument gone loose. Clouds roll vomit-dark over the skyline; a sudden, cold pressure sits in your sinuses. Phones blink. The tide, which has been watching politely, decides to move.

[Sound: The first hard slap of rain—no longer a drizzle but a slash. Boards begin to hiss.]
[Visual: Lightning arranges itself along the horizon, a white notecard folding the skyline in half.]

Someone yells that a feed is trending. You don't look at it. You're looking at the faces in the crowd: Silas's youth, Old Mara's shawl clinging to her shoulders like seaweed, neighbors with taped boxes by their feet and a reluctant dignity. You see Ori, half-running, arms full of a prototype planter—lightframes and plastic-wrapped root mats—trying to haul it to higher ground.

You call to him. Your voice threads through the rain.

You: "Ori! Get back—don't risk—"

Ori: (yelling over the wind) "They'll get crushed if I leave them! These are people's gardens—if they go, people go with them!"

He grins, but it's a broken thing in the downpour. His topknot beads with rain; his overalls cling to him. His amber eyes catch yours for the briefest fraction of a second: an urgent, helpless plea that is equal parts defiance and apology.

You feel a rope of responsibility between you two — taut and impossible to cut.

[Music: Strings fray into a serrated vibrato; tempo accelerates]
[Sound: A whistle blows — police order — then drowned by the shudder of water against the pilings.]

Police: "Clear the pier! This is an evacuation order!"

The words have an authority that moves like oil across the crowd. They hand out laminated forms and plastic seals. Enforcement teams — not just officers but men in corporate-branded vests — move with legal-looking packets clipped to their belts. They carry notices stamped "IMMINENT HAZARD."

You catch the way the paperwork echoes the memos you and Jun exposed: the thresholds, the criteria, the language that could have been written on VossCorp stationery and appears, almost symptomatically, on the laminate now being draped on people's doors.

You: (low, to Jun) "They're using our evidence as their authority."

Jun: "They've weaponized the leak. They'll say disclosure caused disorder. Cameras eat that line."

You want to shout that the memos were meant to show intent, not give them instruction to act. But the wind folds your words. Instead you move down the pier, pressing into the chaos, because someone has to be the one who keeps speaking human names into a system that prefers numbers.

[Sound: A child's whimper somewhere near the stacked boxes. Wooden slats creak. A metallic clang — someone dropped a crate.]
[Visual: Close-up on Old Mara: rain plastering her shawl, beads clinking thin and tired. She looks at you; her eyes are a map of storms survived and memories catalogued.]

Old Mara: (calling out) "Ava! They won't—They're reading from Voss's scripts. They think paper will turn folks into ghosts!"

You hesitate. You want to argue. You want to tell her that you believed exposure would make them stop. Instead you step closer, hand hovering at the shawl as if you might tuck it more securely.

You: "We thought the city would hear. We thought transparency would—"

Old Mara: (interrupting, sharp as a hooked line) "Thoughts are not anchors, girl. There are hands on the ropes now."

Her words land harder than the rain. For a second the storm is not the worst thing happening.

The cameras find the most photogenic misery. A live anchor's voice overlays the scene: "Protests erupt as severe weather threatens the area. Authorities enforce emergency relocation—" The phrasing is clinical, as if every notice were a first-aid kit assembled in good faith.

And then it arrives: the sea, not subtle now, but an advancing, physical argument.

[Sound: A slap of water louder than any drum; the pier trembles. Boards shudder and spray; small objects roll in the rush.]
[Visual: A wave surges up in a white, sudden toe. It climbs the pilings and sprouts foam like a chewed cloth.]

People scream. Someone's camera tips, and a live feed slashes across a million screens: neighbors clinging to railings, security lines bracing, police calling orders that ricochet uselessly into the wind.

From the VossCorp dais, Calder's figure seems small and composed—his voice a measured line. He steps forward under the corporate lights and addresses the press with all the calm of damage control.

Calder: (over speaker, clinical) "We are cooperating with municipal safety protocols to ensure minimal harm. These measures—though regrettable—are necessary."

He does not look wet. He does not look like he belongs in the water. There is a photograph in his locket, a storm and a small hand curled — you know this because you have seen the image elsewhere. In that photograph a man made a vow against chaos; now he interprets his own vow as a mandate.

You: (brittle) "Your 'cooperation' reads like conquest. You're using a storm to rewrite residency."

Calder: (smiling thinly) "We are reducing risk. Sometimes that requires difficult decisions."

The word 'difficult' blooms like a bruise across your chest.

[Sound: The crackle of radios; a hand-held speaker coughs static. A city van revs and leaves a wake of spray.]
[Visual: Enforcement teams move toward the Saltway's lower alleys; officers tape laminated notices to threshold posts, to doorframes, to the paddled windows of little homes. The paper flaps like a flag in storm wind.]

At the edge of the crowd you see families standing on their porches with plastic-wrapped boxes. A woman rocks a toddler with one forearm while she holds a box of photographs in the other. A man stares at the notice with a kind of stunned disbelief, as if the paper might peel away and reveal a different past.

Jun moves through the crowd, his recorder held high. He whispers into it not to capture quotes but the cadence of loss as it takes shape.

Jun: (into recorder) "They issue notices—'imminent hazard'—and people move like it's a parade no one wanted to join. Old Mara's shawl soaked through. The Voss memo language matches the forms plastered to doors. If this is a file, it is now an eviction."

You hear the quietness of his voice in a swell. The recorder becomes an accusation in his hands.

Ori, soaked through, braves the deeper boards to jam an aluminum planter under a low railing, wedging it against the tide. He is scrabbling and swearing; a stranger helps him push. For a moment—so small it feels obscene—he and you lock eyes again across the chaos. He mouths something that could be "I'm sorry" or "I'm trying" or "stay safe." Rain blurs meaning.

You move toward him. There is a hardness building behind your ribs, an unwanted suffocation. You want to fix the unfixable.

You: "We made a spectacle to force accountability. We made truths public. We did not plan for this—this immediate, armed use of 'safety' to empty doors."

Ori: (breathing) "They were waiting. They had their lawyers. They had a storm window. They needed a narrative—"

You: "We gave them one."

He looks away. For half a second the maker in him goes still; the improviser who believes in beautiful resistance sees his craft being swallowed by a policy that can be stapled to wood and shoved through a letterbox.

[Sound: A child's cry sharpens into panic; a dog barks and is quickly quieted by someone hugging it close.]
[Visual: A moving van, corporate-contrasted in sterile blue, eases down a raised ramp. Men with gloves nod at officers and begin cataloguing belongings with catalogue-voice efficiency.]

One of the enforcement officers steps onto a flooded walkway and approaches an elder's home. He peels a notice from his clipboard and presses it to the door with an almost reverent formality. Old Mara stands by, water beading along the shawl's folds. Her jaw is a knotted rope.

Enforcement Officer: "Madam, this notice entitles you to temporary lodging and services at designated centers."

Old Mara: (spitting rain) "Do you think my house is a thing to be sold to the wet? Do you think my bones fit their 'temporary centers'?"

The officer looks at his form, then at her, then away. His hesitation is not refusal. It is the private moment anyone who participates in this system knows: the unspoken choice to comply with the script.

You move—because if you do nothing, you will be the person who let the language do the moving.

You: "We will document every notice. We will record each transfer. We will not let them hide this behind 'safety.'"

Jun: (sharp) "The feeds are already eating them. Investors call it decisive action. The narrative is shifting."

You feel your breath raw in your chest. The effort to keep voices alive has become the very lever they use to push people out. Your mouth tastes of salt and iron, not from the sea.

[Music: All instruments converge — a harsh, collapsing chord interspersed with the staccato beat of rain on metal]
[Sound: An alarm on a boat; the engine of a small launch increases its timbre to cut through the storm.]

You stand on the pier and watch pieces of your plan unravel into the same system you hoped to upend. The images that will travel—news anchors speaking of "disorderly protests" in the middle of an emergency, feeds trimmed to emphasize confrontation, blurred shots of people moving boxes like evidence of voluntary departure—already form in your mind as somber captions.

Someone near you says, "They'll say the protesters caused the disorder. They'll blame you." The accusation is not aimed, but it lands.

Ori lunges past, a planter sloshing, and nearly loses his footing. You grab his sleeve. For a breath your hands meet—clumsy, soaked—and something like fatigue folds into the space between you.

Ori: (low) "We can still fight this. We can file injunctions, get Jun to push the legal angle—"

You: "How many notices until the injunctions are moot? How many people can move while courts write opinions?"

His jaw works. He is suddenly older than he has ever been here.

Ori: "Then we rebuild. We find those who were relocated and plant again. We'll make proof of the harm and find a way—"

You: (voice small) "Some doors are closed."

He looks at you without letting you finish. There is a gathering storm in his face that matches the overhead clouds.

[Sound: The recorder in Jun's hand clicks as he stops it, then restarts to capture a different cadence — the grief-speak of a community being fragmented.]
[Visual: Close-up on Jun's face: an expression that reads as both principle and regret.]

Jun: (into recorder) "They're calling this 'necessary.' Families are being shipped to temporary housing. VossCorp's statement calls the measure 'mitigative and immediate.' We have people who say they had two hours to pack. We have Old Mara standing with a notice that quotes her own history back at her as a reason to leave."

You: (to Jun) "Get every door. Get every seal. We need time-stamped evidence that matches the memos."

Jun: "I will. But the feeds are already making the frames they prefer."

Your hands are numb now, but not from the cold. They are numb from doing all the right things and watching the city rearrange right over them.

[Visual: A montage of images: a child clutching a stuffed fish as someone tapes a notice to a neighbor's door; a volunteer handing out trash bags full of clothes; a VossCorp drone hovering like a silent judge; Old Mara sitting on a crate and letting the rain run down her cheeks like threads.]

You walk the soaked alleys afterward because you cannot stay on the pier where lights and cameras and Calder's measured voice make a theater out of catastrophe. Saltway is a border now — some houses still hold, some already tented by municipal coverings, others emptied, their thresholds scarred with adhesive and promises.

You reach a house where a family has stacked crates on the porch, their small television still playing a loop of anchor commentary. The father watches it with the same stunned patience of someone who has had his life made into a talk show segment.

Father: (to you, voice thin) "They said relocation would be temporary. They gave us numbers for a hotline. I tried it. It goes to a menu. I waited on hold for an hour and then they disconnected."

You sit on a damp crate. Your jacket is wet through; the scent of wet wool and old wood mixes with something metallic — the smell of rules being enforced.

You: "I'm sorry. I thought—"

Father: "We thought. We thought you could turn a light on. We thought the city would see it. Now we're on those buses."

You close your eyes, because if you open them you will find names, faces, the geometry of lives folded into cardboard boxes. Guilty knowledge pools in your stomach like ink.

[Music: A single, high violin note sustains and then breaks into silence]
[Visual: Ori approaches, holding a small, tarp-wrapped planter. He sets it down in front of you like an offering.]

Ori: "It was the only one I could get clear. I—" (he exhales) "I moved what I could. I shouldn't have tried to do it alone."

You look at him, at the mud on his boots, at the way his fingers tremble. This is the place where the romance of resistance meets the hard fact of outcome.

You: (soft) "You tried."

He smiles, but it does not reach his eyes.

Ori: "Was it enough?"

You both know the answer. You can feel the shape of failure in the way the boarded windows reflect the stormlight: fractured, ambiguous, not total, but devastating.

Old Mara sits a few steps away with her shawl curled about her like a small, wet reef. She meets your gaze and there is, for a breath, no sorrow in her face—only a ledger of what has been lost and what cannot be recalculated.

Old Mara: (low, steady) "There's a tide for everything, child. This one took much. It does not take the memory of how to build."

You rest your forehead against the planter Ori rescued. Soil smell rises in a stubborn counterpoint to the salt. It is small — almost obscene to think that a little root ball could be an answer. But Mara's words sit in you like a seed you cannot refuse.

You: (quiet) "Then we remember how."

[Visual: The rain eases, not as an apology but as exhaustion. The lights of the city blur as if through a wet pane.]
[Sound: Distant engines. The rustle of tarp. The soft, steady breathing of people who are just trying, in the dark, to keep going.]

You stand. You gather what you can—names, times, Jun's recorder, a soaked notebook. There will be reports to file and faces to find and legal rooms to meet in that smell of disinfectant. There will be courtrooms and smear campaigns and the slow pressure of policy. There will also be empty houses and children who will sleep on unaccustomed mattresses with the wrong kind of light.

You look at Ori. He looks back, and for a moment the world contracts to the two of you and this small, hard plant between your palms.

Ori: "We will keep building. We will make proof out of what's left."

You: (voice steadier than you feel) "We will keep telling the names. We'll be loud about who was moved. We'll make sure the record is not only their version."

He reaches for your hand and you allow it. It is cold and sure. There is grief threaded through the touch, and something like a vow that is not naive but necessary.

You step away because there are still doors to inspect, people to document, and a neighborhood that will need someone stubborn enough to keep looking after the pieces. The night closes like a fist. You carry the rain-scented air with you.

[Music: A low, mournful chord underpins the final visuals]
[Visual: Slow pan out: Saltway's low roofs, the glass pier's cold light, the cranes like sleeping predators in the distance; threads of small lamps marking where people still gather.]

You record Jun's last whisper for the night into your own battered field notebook: the list of names, the time of the notices, the coordinates where families were moved. You write because the act of writing still feels like resistance.

You: (final, to yourself) "We lost ground today. We lost faith in a quick fix. But we know what happened."

A pause like a held breath. The city will argue about this morning for months. The feeds will clip and reclip. Investors will profit from temporary certainty. Calder will roll his speeches into another press cycle and the VossArc will not stop because a single pier floods.

You fold your notebook closed, the cover soft with water, and you tuck the small rescued planter under your coat. It is heavy with damp soil and grief and improbable hope.
