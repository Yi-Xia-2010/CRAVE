[Scene: Flooded Boardwalk Market | Midday — Low, bruised sunlight after a rain]
[Visual: Neon lifevests bob along the waterline; hand-painted signs flap — “HOMES NOT BARRIERS” in uneven block letters]
[Sound: Voices layer over lapping water and the sizzle from a vendor’s fry station; a distant siren hums like a held note]
[Music: Driving, percussive rhythm — urgent but buoyant]

You step off the raised walkway and into the crowd. The wood under your boots is slick; the edge of the boardwalk is a ribbon frayed by tide and time. Salt beads in the air and clings to the back of your throat. Sami is already there, a bright slash of neon lifevest and a jaw set like an order of tide lines. He catches your eye and grins—green, sun-brown teeth—then turns and mouths something to the crowd; hands rise, kayaks dip like answering hands.

You taste the same mix of copper and motor oil that's always tasted like this place: memory and work. The borrowed camera is heavier than you expect. Its strap digs into your shoulder as you raise it to your face and find your voice somewhere beneath the static of fifty people. You don’t want to perform; you want to translate—take the small, private stories of the block and hand them out like oars.

You clear your throat and begin narrating into the lens, each sentence measured so the old woman by the stall with the patched awning can hear it if she leans close. Your solder-scarred multi-tool catches the thin iris-streak light and glints once; a nervous, honest spark.

You: "This stretch—this is where my brother taught me to tie a good knot. It's where Ana's laundry got waterlogged the first month the tides learned to be mean. We haven't forgotten names, or what we owe this place." (you pause, listening to someone call out a neighbor's story; you repeat it into the camera.)

Sami steps beside you, close enough that the scent of the sea-weed on his jacket blends into your breath. "Keep it tight, Maya," he says, voice low but intense. "We make words simple and heavy. Let them feel the weight."

You nod. Your hand goes to the solar pendant at your collar, the flashlight that has lit more midnight repairs than you can count. The pendant is warm from sunlight; it steadies you.

[Visual: Close on Dr. Noor at a picnic table made into an emergency desk; she slides a slim folio across to a volunteer with a tablet]
[Sound: The click of a send button — small, decisive]

Dr. Noor’s fingers move easily over tide logs and annotated storm models. You watch her submit the motion: an emergency filing that folds the neighborhood’s stories into scientific demand. Her voice is quiet and exact when she speaks to you.

Dr. Noor: "I've timestamped the recent sensor runs, linked them to the property reports. This forces a council discussion under municipal code — they have to respond within forty-eight hours if it's properly filed." (she taps the screen, then looks up) "We can hold them to procedure."

You: "Forty-eight hours is a long time if a storm comes first."

Dr. Noor: (she lets out a small, dry breath) "Then we make sure the city acts like the clock is already ticking. We make the court of public opinion louder than the false calm of bureaucracy."

Across the market, Elliot is working the perimeter. He has a measuring device strapped to a battered tripod and his drafting glasses are smudged—one arm crooked on his head. He keeps his back to the crowd most of the time, but when you catch his eye he gives a small, wordless nod: I'm here, I see the gap, I'll fit a brace if you tell me where.

You weave through the crowd with a clarity that’s almost mechanical: hand over a stack of laminated legal-observer cards to a volunteer, show where to secure the kayaks, whisper the cadence for the chants so mothers with toddlers won't be lost in the swell. The work is old muscle memory—assignments, logistics, the tiny rituals that make a protest hold together like coral.

A woman in a salt-streaked parka grabs your sleeve mid-sentence. Her fingers quiver.

Neighbor: "They keep saying 'relocation package' like it’s a favor. My whole life is in that little unit under the dock. Where would we go?"

You: (you meet her eyes; you feel the instinct to hand her a fix, a timeline, a promise) "We won’t let them buy out our memories. Not without a fight. We map the risks, we map the solutions—together." (you straighten your voice, adding) "If you come to the kayaks we’ll walk the high routes with you. I'll sit with you through whatever the council tries to rush."

She nods, too quickly, relief and fear braided. You notice the tremor in her grip and you press her hand to ground it, a small, practical anchor.

You feel the old reflex: do everything yourself. It flickers like a high current under the surface. You want to write the legal brief, run diagnostics on the sensor array, and literally lift the wet planks to fix the boardwalk. But the rally needs you to be a fulcrum, not a lone lever.

Sami yells into a megaphone now, his voice ricocheting off corrugated roofs. "We are not commodities to be moved. We are neighbors. We demand an emergency hearing—now!" His words land like stones. The chant begins as a ripple and then becomes something wider, rough and warm, and you ride it with the camera to record the flow.

Elliot approaches from the side, breath visible in a cold puff despite the damp heat. He doesn't step into the front line. He keeps his distance the way a good engineer keeps a safe angle. "I've got tide-model prints if they want to see what happens when the barrier wall redirects current into lower streets," he says quietly, voice threaded with concern. "But this—" he gestures at the crowd, his hands small and earnest — "this needs to be their voice. I'll back you."

You: "Bring the models to the front after Dr. Noor files. Let the facts stand behind the anger—let them carry the truth without the gloss."

Elliot's smile is soft and something like gratitude. "Whatever you need."

The first of Luca's envoys arrives like a piece of shine dropped into the wet market: polished waterproof brochures, a man in a tan suit with a water-resistant sheen. He hands a pamphlet to a young vendor whose stall is half submerged. The glossy renderings are tempting—clean lines, lifted homes, "immediate relocation options — compensation available."

Envoy: (slick, rehearsed) "We can offer immediate safety and guaranteed compensation. There’s no shame in choosing stability for your family."

Neighbor two, a teenager with sea-salted dreadlocks, snatches the pamphlet open and studies a promised apartment block rendered in sunlit concrete. He looks from the image to the algae-stained pilings and back again. "They make it sound like a vacation," he says, voice flat. "But my aunt sleeps with salt in her hair."

Sami sees the exchange and pushes forward, face close to the envoy’s. "They'll pave over what we keep. You don't get your aunt back with a glossy brochure."

The envoy spreads his hands. "We're offering a choice. Safety is a kind act."

You step between them, your chest a cord of controlled heat. "Safety and place are not the same thing," you say, and the crowd rings the phrase back like a bell. "We can build safety here, with us. We can do it without turning us into a model home."

The envoy's eyes flick up; for a beat his smooth mask falters. You don't know what he thinks of you personally—whether he sees stubbornness or honor. His reaction is unreadable. He folds his pamphlet away and talks to his colleague, low and urgent.

There is a bright, raw electricity in the air now. Someone has set a camera on a crate and the live feed begins to thread through social channels—the market becomes a node and the feed pulses outward. Messages spike. A city reporter ducks under an umbrella to get closer and asks you a question: "Are you asking the city to stop, or to restructure its plan?"

You: "Both. Stop the demolition schedule until a community-led assessment happens. Restructure so funds prioritize living infrastructure, not displacement. We can design resilience that keeps the people who live here in place."

The reporter repeats the phrase and you see the words travel—into phones, into the inside of municipal buildings, into the hands that count votes.

From across the water, a municipal liaison video-calls in: Irene Voss's council office is a rectangle of steel light. For a moment you see her—impeccable coat, steely eyes—on the reporter’s screen. She watches the livestream with a measured face. Then the screen flicks: a staffer types, then looks up. Back to the reporter, who clears his throat and says, "City hall acknowledges the demonstration and—" his voice lifts with a tremor of officialness — "—has scheduled an emergency agenda for the council to address the petition within two days."

The crowd's sound crashes into a higher pitch, giddy and terrified, like surf hitting a newly-built jetty. You feel the surge in your chest, buoyant with a dangerous, hopeful force.

Sami grabs your shoulder, hard. "You pulled them," he says, fierce and delighted. "You made them see us."

You: (your laugh is short and wet with salt) "We all did. It’s not over, Sami. This is the lever—now we have to know how hard we pull."

Elliot, at the edge of the crowd, closes his eyes for a breath. When he opens them there is a look in them you almost misread as equanimity, but it's worry, sharp and human. "So we keep them talking until the hearing. We prep testimony, floodwatch teams, legal observers. We make the record unignorable."

Dr. Noor folds her hands, exhausted but precise. "And I’ll tighten the models, timestamp everything. Make no mistake: the filing puts them on schedule. The schedule puts pressure on the budget. Pressure bends policy."

You look over the market—the kayaks lined like patient animals, the neon vests bobbing, the volunteers with clipboards. A child runs by with a handmade sign that reads, "My Home Is Here." You feel the familiar tug to take control, to split yourself into every role at once: organizer, engineer, empath, anchor. Instead you breathe in and exhale to the cadence of the crowd. This is how you learned to be part of things that mattered—by giving voice and then giving space for other voices to shout back.

You move to the mic again and speak into the camera. The words are simple, but the timing is right and the feed is hungry.

You: "We are not a problem to be solved for someone else’s legacy. We are the keepers of this shoreline. We ask to be part of the solution now, not erased later."

[Visual: The market explodes into chant; hands push forward; rain begins to fall again in thin, urgent lines]
[Sound: Chants, cameras, a rising drumbeat of kayaks slipping into water]
[Music: Swelling, racing — all cylinders firing]

At the edge of your hearing something else clicks: the municipal liaison's tablet lights up with a confirmation. The emergency hearing is scheduled. The page that you and Sami have worked so hard to set into motion has been opened.

You feel the arc of something you started curving toward consequence—a hinge beginning to move. You are dizzy with the rush: the good kind, the sharp kind that promises risk but offers a path.

You could push now, demand immediate barricades, a city-funded pilot, births of living seawalls tomorrow. Or you could hold the line—gather testimony, build a facted narrative, let the council be crowded by people with names. You hear the crowd waiting; you feel your heart matching their tempo.
