You arrive with your sleeves damp and smelling of peat, the fabric of your field jacket darkened at the cuffs. The garden reads like an argument between color and weather: bright cordgrass and newly tilled brown ridges push up against the coastal gray sky. Children's muddy footprints make a trail through the beds — small interruptions of joy in the mapped seriousness of adaptation — and they snag at the place behind your ribs that remembers a thinner pair of shoes.

Sora moves through the volunteers like a warm current, hair falling in glossy waves over one shoulder as they gesture and laugh and correct a planting angle with a breathless kind of insistence. Their jacket is splattered with paint and mud; their fingers have a streak of peat beneath the nails. When they talk, people lean in. You feel that pull in your chest — the reflex toward someone who turns organizing into a kind of magnetism.

Sora Watanabe: (directing a pair of teenagers) "Firm roots, not too deep — we want them to anchor, not drown. Angle the stems so the flow will find the channels."

You move into the work without theatrics. There is comfort in the repetition of tasks: testing the slope of a berm with your palm, pressing a cordgrass plug into damp soil, watching the way the channel channels the water where you planned it on paper. Volunteers match your tempo; their faces are tentative and fierce at once. You teach them how to map wave overtops with simple stakes and notepads — a system you made because precision can stand in for panic — and they watch your weathered jacket with an almost-feral respect, like a uniform of someone who has spent too many hours with water on their face.

Volunteer (young man, breathless): "How deep do we mark the stakes when the tide's high?"

You: "Mark to the highest overtops from the last charted event. If you're unsure, mark higher — we want conservative data, not false confidence. And note wind direction when you record it."

Volunteer: "Got it. What if the tide comes early?"

You: (soft) "Tell the team lead. We have evacuation keels on the boardwalk. Safety first — always."

You and Sora cross paths as you knot a protective reed bundle. Your hands brush; paint on their sleeve wipes across the cuff of your jacket. For a moment the world compresses to this small smear and the warmth of another's palm near yours. Their dark almond eyes lift to yours, searching — not performative, but asking for the honesty you keep behind a practiced reserve.

Sora Watanabe: (quiet, between breaths) "You're here. I figured you'd be."

You: "Someone has to teach mapping that doesn't put people at risk. Plus—" (you gesture at the seedlings) "—we need hands that know how to read a tide, not just clap at a rally."

Sora: (a small smile) "Practical and romantic. Dangerous combo."

The two of you fall into easy instruction. You correct someone's bearing, Sora makes a joke that loosens a volunteer's shoulders, and the planting moves forward like a conversation where every shovel is a sentence. The intimacy that blooms here is the kind that grows from shared work: no grand pronouncements, only the steady joining of effort.

Dr. Noor: "We had to rerun a section after the last sensor drop. Some of the propagation zones shift inland faster than our first run suggested. That changes where we prioritize plugs."

You: (looking at the map) "How much inland?"

Dr. Noor: (slowly) "Enough that some of the berms we designed for stage-two may need raising sooner. But the wetland channels reduce surge energy more than anticipated. It's uneven, but the net benefit is there — if we can move the community volunteers into monitoring loops more quickly."

Maya arrives then, cheeks wind-bitten, carrying a chipped enamel mug steaming with tea. She sets it in your hand as if an offering and sits on the crate like she never left childhood.

Maya Cruz: "Drink. And don't be a hero; you'll be cold later if you don't."

You: (accepting) "Thanks. I forgot how good this tastes with salt in the air."

Maya: (eyeing Dr. Noor's maps) "You tell them, Noor. Don't let them bury the stories in charts."

Dr. Noor: (soft, almost conspiratorial) "Numbers are stories too, if someone reads them out loud. But I know what you mean."

Volunteers chatter in the spaces between tasks — stories, complaints, small victories. A girl with a freckled nose asks if the cordgrass will hold fast through a storm; you kneel and show her the root mat you'd prepared earlier, fingers moving as your explanations thread through technique and caution.

You: "Plant roots interlock. They slow the water enough that the berm can absorb the rest. It's not magic. It's work."

Girl (wide-eyed): "Will it save the pier?"

You: (the answer a map of probabilities and a hard-won hope) "It will help. But we need maintenance, and we need to keep listening to the water."

As night pulls its shawl across the garden, the volunteers string lanterns that glow like small assurances. Each light casts the reed-channeling work in a new, forgiving palette; the garden softens. You stand back, hands streaked with peat, and feel the satisfying friction of a day's labor: soreness in your shoulders, a mint of sweat at the nape of your neck, the steady lift of something that is neither triumph nor despair but movement.

Sora lingers near the greenhouse, their pendant — polished shell and reclaimed glass — catching the amber light and throwing tiny shards across their palm. They turn toward you with the kind of look that doesn't make demands so much as open a door.

Sora Watanabe: (quiet, almost vulnerable) "Would you stay for a tide watch? Late. Just the two of us, and the sensors. I want to show you the new cross-channel reads; I want to—" (they stop, searching for a word that won't sound like sentimentality) "—I want to keep seeing how the water answers us."

You feel the air tilt, not with panic but with the gentle lift of something that could become more if tended. Your retrofit watch ticks the rhythm of a tide line; it shows a steady, modest rise. Volunteers tidy tools; Maya hands you another mug for the road. The town's night breathes around you, equal parts salt and ember.

A late breeze slides in from the channel, carrying salt and the faint metallic tang of old nails. You follow Sora toward the boardwalk, the light from the lanterns punctuating the way. The world narrows to the rhythm of your steps and the quiet swell of water at the edge of the garden.
