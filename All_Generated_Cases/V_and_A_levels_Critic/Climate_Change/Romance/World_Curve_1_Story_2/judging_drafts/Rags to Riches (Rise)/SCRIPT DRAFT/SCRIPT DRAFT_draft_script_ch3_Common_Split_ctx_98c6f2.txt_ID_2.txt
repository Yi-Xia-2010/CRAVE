[Scene: Mariselle Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot — the boardwalk crowded with people; banners fluttering; Lina at a podium on the municipal plaza steps; Sora on a raised crate with a megaphone; Ephraim's temporary fence and corporate banners forming a harsh vertical line down the promenade]
[Music: Low steady rhythm with a faint urgency — a coastal percussion underlining voices]
[Sound: Waves whispering against bulkhead, distant camera shutters, a drumbeat of footsteps, the crack of a megaphone]

You stand with salt-caked soles on the planks, the wood slightly springy where the tide has begun to compromise its nails. The air tastes of brine and old tar paint; gulls wheel and call like punctuation marks. Your Moleskine is folded in your jacket pocket where your hand can find it without thinking. The old ache behind your ribs—memory as much as grief—tugs when the crowd swells and the voices overlap.

Across from you, Lina's voice cuts the noisy tide of protest with a polished calm that is almost a physical thing: smooth, practiced, the kind of cadence that has been auditioned for cameras. "We must invest smartly, and now," she says. "Engineering paired with oversight will protect Mariselle for generations." Flashbulbs bloom; the municipal seal behind her catches the light and throws it back in cold bars.

Sora's shout is more ragged, immediate. A sea of handmade banners frames them—painted reeds, a child's drawing of a house with a lifebuoy around it. "People first!" the crowd answers. The megaphone distorts Sora's earnestness into a gravelly insistence. You can see Sora's jaw tighten and relax in rhythm to their points; theater for survival, but sincere.

Ephraim stands beside his fence, a lean smile that shows teeth like coins. He gestures to models under a plastic tent—slick renderings of terraces and towers—and his spokesperson keys a line of talking points into a tablet. "Private investment speeds resilience," he says, voice smooth, and eyes that are all appraisal.

Maya is near your elbow, wool cap low, hands stained with mud from the wetland co-op. She leans in, speaking under the noise. "They're all good at the podium," she says, half-sarcastic, half-worried. "It's the bits after the ribbon-cutting that cost us the shoreline." Her breath fogs in front of her face, warm and human against the salted wind.

[Visual: Camera closes on your hands — calluses on knuckles, a faint scar along the left palm — then pans up to your hazel eyes narrowing]
Narration: You are the axis between them: an engineer with plans, a neighbor with memory. The split is not theoretical; it's under your feet. You feel the old roles peel open—method against heart, model against livelihood—and your chest adjusts to hold both.

[Scene: Tidewatch Institute Tent (adjacent) | Continuous]
[Visual: A canvas tent marked "Tidewatch" with laminated maps tacked inside; Dr. Noor standing under the errant light of a work lamp]
[Sound: The murmur of the crowd muffled by canvas; the faint click of Dr. Noor's pen]

Dr. Noor draws you aside with a steely gentleness that has always been her way. She folds the maps so only you can see the lines she wants you to see, the faint pencil corrections like small scars.

Dr. Noor: "Ari — I ran the numbers again, with the new sediment transport data from the winter surge." (She hesitates as though the pause might steady what follows.) "There's a catch we didn't model clearly before. Certain types of rigid levee — if placed without adjacent living shorelines — will increase erosive stresses on older neighborhoods. It’s a nonlinear response. We can protect infrastructure, yes, but only if we pair engineering with ecological buffers."

You hold the corner of the map between your fingers. The letters swim a little in the tent light; the technical language translates immediately into people: the block where your childhood bicycle was chained, the low row of houses with salt-splotched shutters.

You: "Explain the mechanism. How big is the effect? What's the uncertainty range?"
Dr. Noor: (She taps a line of numbers and then looks up at you.) "Magnitude is moderate but very targeted. It concentrates wave energy at nodes where cultural streets curve. If that energy isn't absorbed — living roots, marsh accretion — the cultural fabric is what erodes first. The model's confidence is fair, but the policy implication is binary: pair it properly, or you save concrete at the expense of people's homes."

You: (Your voice is steadier than you feel.) "So a levee-only plan looks safer on paper, but could damage neighborhoods we can't replace with numbers."
Dr. Noor: "Yes. And politics—especially slow funding cycles—favor the visible, the immediate. Someone will sell safety with a ribbon. Someone must insist the safety is truly safe."

She doesn't press further. The tent's fabric sags with the low wind, and the noise of the plaza leaks back in: chanting, applause, a reporter's clipped question.

[Scene: Boardwalk Edge (between plaza and protest) | Continuous]
[Visual: Sora steps down from their crate and threads toward you through the crowd; the megaphone hangs from one wrist]
[Sound: A sudden hush in a small radius around you two as Sora approaches; the squeak of wet sneakers on wood]

Sora finds you with practiced ease, their face bright, hair thrown back on one side, the other side framing their expression in shadow. For a moment, the megaphone is silent. Sora presses a flyer into your hand like a confession or a challenge — you can’t tell which.

Sora: (quiet now, unamplified) "People's Emergency Plan — Build With Us." (They smile, a small crease at the corner of their eye. The flyer is plain: bold letters, a list of skills needed, a schedule for marsh plantings and evacuation drills.) "We move with our hands. We don't wait."

You trace the flyer with a thumb, the paper rough where rain has dampened it. Your training tells you the flyer simplifies complexity—logistics, liability, materials—but your memory remembers a child carried from a flooded street and the awful, human cost of waiting for the 'right' money to arrive.

You: "It’s operationally thin. Who's taking liability? How do you coordinate skilled labor? What about long-term maintenance?"
Sora: (leans closer, conspiratorial and tired and fierce all at once) "We do it together. We train each other. We share tools. We don't sign away our say to a distant funder who ghosts us after the ribbon-cutting. We keep the knowledge here."

Sora's fingers brush yours as they take back the flyer; the press of contact is normal and charged and quickly gone. Their eyes flick to the tent where Dr. Noor had been, then to the podium where Lina speaks in stereo with municipal cameras. "I need you on the line, Ari," Sora says softly. "Not because I want you to pick a side for me, but because your voice—your plans—make people listen."

Sora: (a beat) "Will you come to the wetland dig next Saturday? Even a day? Help us lay the first bundles?"
You: (the answer sits on the edge of your tongue—technical, cautious, or wholehearted) "I can help plan the layout. I can't promise the funds." 
Sora: (a flash of frustration, then softness) "Then be there to plan it. We make it harder to ignore if you help us do it the right way."

[Scene: Municipal Plaza Side Alley | Moments Later]
[Visual: Lina steps away from the podium, abandoning the theatrical architecture to speak with you on a smaller, quieter stairwell shaded by a banner]
[Sound: The hum of the crowd is a distant ocean now; Lina's voice is low, calibrated]

Lina: (beyond public formality, almost candid) "Ari. I want you to lead the municipal pilot. Official backing, resources, monitoring. We can move decisively if you accept oversight protocols. You know the spreadsheets, the standards. You'd have me to push funding through."

You look at her, sizing the offer the way you size a piece of timber for load-bearing: what it can take, what it will transfer stress onto.

You: "Oversight in whose hands? Community boards? Third-party auditors?"
Lina: (slight smile) "A set committee. A steering group. You'd be the technical lead, but with mandated reporting and external audits. Transparency — and consequence if goals aren't met."

You: "And how do you see living shorelines fitting with your levee schemes?"
Lina: (tilts her head, softly) "If Dr. Noor is right, we include them. But only with measurable performance markers. We will not gamble with infrastructure without the metrics."

Her voice is soft but firm. The offer smells of practicality: resources that can be translated into bolts and concrete and payroll. It also smells of strings — audit schedules, committee meetings, the slow, bureaucratic bindings that can strangle fast community momentum.

Across the plaza, Ephraim's smile opens again when he catches sight of the private conversation. He moves closer as if drawn by scent. He speaks without ceremony.

Ephraim: "Ari, if you sway this town and bring certainty, think of the scale—investment that brings jobs, that lifts entire blocks up out of the hazard zone. We can partner on rapid deployment."
You: (you feel the gallery of his models in the tent like a pressure) "Rapid deployment that requires buyouts of property?"
Ephraim: (that slit of teeth) "Strategic relocations, yes. But also new opportunities. We handle the financing; you handle the integrity."

You: (the words "handle the integrity" feel like a test) "And the cultural impacts? The streets that hold our past?"
Ephraim: (smiles, deflects) "Culture evolves. We make space for its future."

Maya returns, breath heavy with a dozen errands and the smell of wet reeds, and drops a hand on your shoulder as if to steady the balance. "Be careful," she says simply. "They will dress the same promises for different markets. Choose the one that keeps people here, not the one that says they'll move people for 'efficiency.'"

Narration: The voices overlap—technical caveat, earnest grassroots, polished municipal promise, slick private assurance. None of it is clean. You can see the vectors of consequence: funds, autonomy, displacement, solidarity. Each promise changes the map slightly, as if drawing new lines on the tide-smeared boardwalk.

Your hazel eyes narrow; the scar near your temple tenses with the weight of memory. The town's breath is a long, steady exhale of expectation. The choice sits in front of you—no grand revelation, just a knot of practicalities and loyalties, the familiar geometry of damnation and salvation that planners learn to speak around.

You take a breath. The mid-level hum in the air swells, not into panic but into the loud clarity of something finally needing to be named.
