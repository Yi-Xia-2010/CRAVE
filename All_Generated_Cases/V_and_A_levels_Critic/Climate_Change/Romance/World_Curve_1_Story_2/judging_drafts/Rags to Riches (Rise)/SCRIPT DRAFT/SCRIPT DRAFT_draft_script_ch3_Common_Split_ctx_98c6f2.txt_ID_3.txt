You step out from the Tidewatch tent into the theatre of the waterfront. The press has already parked itself between the lamp posts and the rail: microphones like obedient metal weeds, lights that paint faces a little too white, camera lenses that eat the horizon. The town is arranged into competing currents.

To your left, under a temporary podium draped with the municipal seal, Lina speaks with the polished economy of a practiced negotiator. Her tenor is measured; she phrases rescue as a project you can sign your name to. Photographers lean in, looking for a face that will make the funding line read humane.

Straight ahead, where salt has mixed into the mud at the promenade’s edge, Sora stands on a crate—megaphone in hand, banners tied to lampposts—her voice flaring like a bright bird. Volunteers move like choreographed waves: handing out fliers, fixing banners, folding up tarps. Sora's rhetoric is fire, but you see gentleness threaded under the gestures when she checks on someone with a bruised hand.

To your right, a cordon of glossy fencing and a neat line of suited aides mark Ephraim Blake's temporary booth. His smile cuts a white line; the men in shirts with his logo hand out glossy renderings of 'revitalized waterfronts'. The renderings have very little moss.

Maya is near the reclamation table, hands muddy, counterbalanced by a grin that's part apology, part local lore. She nudges a box of nursery reeds toward you as if you might accept or return it.

You feel your chest where last night’s decision is still a raw map: the words you might speak in council, the choice to shape policy into places where people live. The pressure that sits there is familiar—an engineer's focus, a neighbor's ache. You breathe and catalog: wind, salt clinging to collar, the metallic taste of camera-light electricity, Sora’s laughter across the crowd, Lina’s calm measured tones.

A hand on your sleeve. Dr. Noor's voice is softer than the megaphone; she draws you into the lee of a weathered kiosk that smells of wet paper and solder.

Dr. Noor: "I needed to tell you first—away from the cameras."

You follow her gaze to the horizon, then back to the maps tacked to a portable board. The paper is streaked with salt, the lines of contour faint where thumbprints have rubbed them.

Dr. Noor: (low) "The model we ran—there's a correction. It doesn't change the need for barriers, but it changes where and how. Certain straight-edge levee profiles, the ones Ephraim's consultants keep touting, can increase wave reflection. That reflection concentrates erosive energy downcurrent—through the old harbor row. If you only build hard, you accelerate the very loss we're trying to prevent."

You feel the phrase before you can parse it: accelerate the loss. Technical language lands like cooled metal. You want to ask for numbers—run the equations in your head—then feel the social vector: neighborhoods, murals, small shops with names you carry like talismans.

You: "So—paired strategies? Living shorelines with roughness elements, marsh buffers? We need to couple them in time."

Dr. Noor: "Exactly. The living elements dampen the energy, hold sediment. But they need both design patience and community stewardship. They don't snap into place like a levee slab. And they'll be easier to fund if there's a municipal partner—someone to sign the contracts. Someone the public recognizes as trustworthy."

You search her face for hidden pressure—she's been your mentor; her eyes are a map of field seasons and tired optimism.

Dr. Noor: (after a beat) "I didn't want to drop this where it would be a weapon. But it is a lever. You can use it to force a reassessment of the hard-only plans. Or—if mishandled—it becomes a bargaining chip for those who'd trade neighborhoods for quick, visible wins."

You: "If I say it in public, it could stall funding. If I don't, engineering gets simplified and culture erodes. There's no simple arithmetic here."

Dr. Noor: "There rarely is, Ari. You're the one who understands both sides. That's why they listen when you speak. Use that."

Her hand trembles the smallest fraction when she folds the last map back into its sleeve—nothing theatrical, only the movement of someone who has spent too long in storms.

The crowd pulls you forward again. Sora's voice cuts across Lina's cadence; the megaphone rasps with a new chant. In that surge, Sora drops from the crate and navigates through a knot of volunteers toward you. When she reaches you, the theatricality is tucked into the set of her shoulders; the tenderness remains, a soft current under the activism.

Sora: (quiet, handing you a folded flyer) "Read this—now. Or later. But don't let them decide without us."

You take it. The paper is slightly damp from a volunteer's hand; the slogan—People's Emergency Plan—has been lettered by hand. Her thumb brushes yours in the exchange, a practiced, private thing that steadies more effectively than anyone else's words.

You: (quiet) "You want me to join this—openly?"

Sora: "I want you to lead it with us. You know the risks better than anyone. And you make things people can live with—literally and technically. We need your voice in the open, not just in margins."

Sora's eyes search yours, not theatrical now but precise, waiting for the level of your commitment. The megaphone buzzes again in the background, but the world contracts to the thickness of paper in your hand.

You hesitate. Your internal ledger opens: community trust versus institutional reach; quick, visible defenses versus slower, maintenance-heavy living systems; your sibling's absence as a tally that must always be accounted for.

Sora: (softer) "If you stand with us now, it's not just strategy. It's people making things with their hands. It's families that will know when a berm was planted by a neighbor, not a camera."

You: "And if I speak for you, Sora—then what about funding? What about oversight? What if they weaponize our pilot and take our agency?"

Sora: (frustration flaring, then tamped) "Then we keep fighting. Then we make the public see who's actually doing the work. I'm not saying it's safe. I am saying it's right."

Before you can answer further, a measured voice approaches—Lina. Her public cadence has smoothed into a private softness that keeps the journalist's whites out of sight. She stops an arm's length away, hands unclasped.

Lina: (quiet) "Ari, may I have a word? Publicly, I said what had to be said. Privately—if you would consider leading a municipal pilot, we can move funds faster than ordinary channels. A signed pilot under your direction. Oversight, yes—but that ensures transparency. We can structure community seats on the advisory board. We want this to be respected."

The offer is an engineering dream: resources, official access, the ability to commission long-term monitoring. The tone, however, carries the thin scent of conditions. Oversight, in municipal speech, often translates into committees and procurement rules that bend the shape of action.

You: "What kind of oversight? Who appoints the seats? What clauses aim at emergency timelines versus cultural preservation?"

Lina: (patient, precise) "We can write community representation into the charter. Independent auditors. A public dashboard. We will not—publicly—force a hard-only mandate if the science supports combined solutions. I want someone who can bridge the field and the council. You would have authority to commission living units alongside engineered works."

There is a pause where you measure whether 'authority' means autonomy or the appearance of it while budgets and contracts remain immutable.

You: "And if the regional funders—Ephraim's backers—press their own design? Do we have veto power? Will the pilot be protected from private developers' claims?"

Lina: (a microfrown) "Contracts can be drafted to protect the pilot. That requires negotiation. It requires signatures at levels I can reach. But signing into the municipal framework invites legal scrutiny and recourse that grassroots projects often lack."

You sense the bargain being laid out in plain terms: trade some procedural space for protective scaffolding. It offers reach, but it bends process into a shape you may not love.

The crowd density increases. Reporters sift into clusters. Ephraim appears beside his glossy fencing like an afterimage of capital: smile too bright, tie slightly damp from humidity.

Ephraim: (smooth) "Ari! Always good to see you at community events. Progress needs partners. My backers are willing to sign an anchor investment. Big, fast works. Think of what a coherent public image would do for Mariselle."

Maya, who has been loading volunteers with native plugs, steps forward with a scowl that looks like a folded flag.

Maya: "Coherent image isn't culture, Ephraim. Your 'fast' tends to wash down the small shops and bail out big returns elsewhere."

Ephraim: (light, almost apologetic) "We don't want to erase history. We want to preserve value. Efficiently."

You: "Your renderings don't show the old harbor murals still standing. They show a polished walkway."

Ephraim: (smile narrowing) "Renderings are visions. They can include whatever we agree on. Blame sounds better in a press release than in court."

You feel the old knot tighten where memory and numbers meet—you can run models on erosion, but you can't run a model for the weight of a neighbor's grief. Legal pressure and glossy promises hang in the air like a challenge.

Lina watches the exchange, unreadable; Sora's jaw is set. Dr. Noor stands a step back, face pinched in that way people have when trying not to tally every possible bad outcome aloud.

The ambient noise—megaphone, camera shutter, gull—threads into coherence. The decision sits in front of you like a crossing tide: join Sora's people-powered refusal, accept Lina's conditional pilot and its compromises, lead a campaign to unmask and block Ephraim's private development, or refuse the binary by proposing a hybrid process that pulls everyone to the table and risks pleasing none.

You feel the scar at your ribs—an old ache that flares with the memory of loss—and it focuses you. This is the fulcrum. Every conversation you've had, every sleepless night with contours of maps, has funnelled into this noisy, salt-scented intersection.
