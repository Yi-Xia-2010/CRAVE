[Scene: Harborfront Boardwalk | Twilight — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Wet planks gleam under stuttering streetlights; a ring of police cruisers throws red and blue into the harbor mist. Jonah's billboard renderings loom like promises above the crowd.]
[Sound: Distant thunder, the low thrum of construction machinery, the thin ringing of a bell from the council house.]
[Music: Taut strings with a rapid heartbeat percussion; tempo rising]

You step forward with the megaphone cupped to your lips. The voice that comes out is yours but larger—rawer—more exposed than you meant it to be. It cuts through the chant and the spray and lands against wet faces.

You can feel the seed vial at your throat swing like a tiny metronome. Its glass catches the strobe of police lights and fractures it into a hundred little alarms. You taste salt, adrenaline, a metallic tang that could be fear or resolve; you cannot tell which is louder.

Kira moves like a conductor. She has already dispatched people with high-visibility vests to document lines, to stand as legal observers. She barks names and locations; her voice is tinny and urgent and somehow keeps you grounded.

Kira: "Legal, on the north pier! Emma, keep the lane clear—document everything! Eyes on the contractors!"

You: "Mateo—hold the east flank. Don’t let them push through the sandbags."

Mateo's hands are steady when they meet yours; his skin is the smell of old rope and diesel. He nods once, wordless, and locks his arm into the living line. Around you, neighbors compound into a human barricade—shoulders to shoulders, sleeves wet, breath fogging in the cold.

A plain-clothed officer pushes through the line with a headset and a clipboard. Mayor Serena stands just behind him, her civic sash damp, eyes strained into a smile that doesn't reach the lines of her face. She is a hinge between the metal and the people.

Mayor Serena: (low, forced calm) "We called for restraint. No one wants arrests tonight. Please—let's keep this peaceful. We can talk."

You hear the plea and the calculation the same breath. Talks. Compromises. The word tastes of sand.

Jonah steps forward with practiced cadence, palms open like a man offering a bridge while his boots are planted on someone else's pavement. His voice is warm—too warm.

Jonah Hale: "This isn't about erasing memories. It's about protecting lives. Every day of delay increases risk. We can build a stronger harbor if we move with the data."

Kira spits a laugh that is half despair, half defiance.

Kira: "Your 'data' is a bulldozer in disguise. We have names, Jonah. We have gardens. We have people who grew up in houses you've never seen."

Jonah: (smile tightening) "And I don't discount that history—I'm proposing a solution that saves neighborhoods on a scale sentimentalism cannot."

The crowd's murmur folds into an angry undertow. You lock eyes with Elias. He stands between a contractor's hard hat and the neighbors, sleeves rolled, face washed in cold light. His hands hover by a tablet he hasn't looked at for minutes.

Elias: (quiet, earnest) "Ayla—if we don't accept some of the engineered measures, we might lose everything to the next surge. I'm trying to keep us from losing people."

You: "People is the definition of everything, Elias. Who counts in your models? Names matter."

Elias swallows. He meets your gaze and there's pleading there—technical and human braided together—but the glare of the floodlights flattens something in him. His next words are careful; they clink like stones in a pouch.

Elias: "I know. I'm not asking you to erase the gardens. I'm asking for trade-offs that reduce mortality. We can calibrate—"

You cut him off without meaning to. Your voice snaps like a wet rope.

You: "Calibration doesn't replace a home, Elias. Not for everyone."

Tension coalesces into motion. A contractor—broad-shouldered, face set like flint—eyes a narrow gap and pushes forward with a pry bar to dislodge the sandbag barrier. Someone from the line grabs the bar. There is a heave. The contractor staggers. Someone shoves back.

The shove happens in a breath. The contractor slips on the slick planks, arms windmilling, and falls hard against the railing. A phone camera already has him in frame. A chorus of gasps, then a thousand little shutter clicks as footage begins its inevitable river.

The contractor scrambles up, face red with indignation and pain. He points at the person who shoved him; a police officer moves like a shadow and detains them in a practiced, mechanical way. The crowd surges protective and furious; hands reach, voices crescendo into a scream.

Sound explodes—sirens that had been a distant thread flare into immediate wails. Someone screams your name in the surge; Kira is on the line with a megaphone, voice thinner and higher. Officers begin to form arrest lines. Rubber boots slap wet wood. The chaos is bright and slicing.

[Visual: Phones illuminate faces. The contractor's fall is already being shared: a looped clip with an outraged commentary, a caption that calls the crowd violent, another that frames the contractors as threatened workers. The narrative fractures into competing feeds.]

Mayor Serena's voice cuts through with a tightness that betrays panic and calculation.

Mayor Serena: (loud enough to be heard over the shouting) "If this continues, the council will convene an emergency vote to authorize full measures for public safety—Jonah's plan in full. I can't—" (she closes her eyes for a heartbeat) "—I can't allow mass arrests tonight. Not if I can offer another path."

Silence presses like a hand. The storm's first big breath rolls over the harbor, a low roar that drowns the tip of sound, and in that swell the offer lands.

Mayor Serena: "Disperse the blockade, and I'll call a limited moratorium on site activity for thirty days. We'll form a mediated review committee with seats for community representatives. No arrests tonight. We keep the cameras clear."

The words are both reprieve and razor. Your chest tightens as if the moratorium were a gauze on a deeper wound—one that might staunch blood or seal something vital shut.

Jonah leans forward, his voice a silvery thread threading through the crowd.

Jonah Hale: "Thirty days is a responsible pause. It allows us to calibrate and act decisively. I will commit to the committee's schedule. This is a pragmatic step forward."

Kira spits on the boardwalk—an old gesture, small and sharp.

Kira: "A moratorium with Jonah steering the table is a leash. He controls the agenda, the data, the timeline. We disperse and he keeps the power."

Elias steps between you and the mayor again, but this time the crowd tightens like a muscle. His hands are empty. He looks at you with an exhausted kind of hope.

Elias: "Ayla, this could save people tonight. It could get us a seat at the table. If you take the moratorium, we can push for binding language from inside."

You feel every option like a rung on a ladder that goes straight down or into the sea. The police form lines, the contractor's clip blooms across feeds with a needle-sharp headline: 'Protest Turns Violent.' The council bell rings in the distance, a slow hammer against air.

Your pulse is a drumbeat against a hull; everything feels exposed under flashing lights.

You think of Mateo's hands, the way he plants seeds in soil that keeps trying to wash away. You think of Kira's megaphone voice, bright and brittle with righteous anger. You think of Elias's careful, tired face and the models he worships like scripture. You think of the children who play on the boardwalk when the water is low, of the woman who grows herbs in the windowbox two streets over.

Mayor Serena's gaze finds you, hard and pleading.

Mayor Serena: (sharp) "This is the last safe option to avoid arrests tonight. Commit to the moratorium, and you'll have guaranteed seats on the review committee. Refuse, and I cannot guarantee the council will not authorize emergency measures."

The halo of lights compresses. The rain begins in earnest—fierce pinpricks on skin and fabric, washing everything toward a decision. You can feel the city's attention like a lens focused on your chest.

Your throat closes. Words tumble up and refuse to form. The seed vial at your collar clinks against the metal of your zipper like a small accusation.

Everything narrows to a single axis: disperse and walk into a mediated bargain that might protect some but could entrench others; hold the line and risk arrests, criminal charges, a narrative that might break public sympathy and tilt the council to Jonah's favor; or split the movement—pull some into negotiation while others stay and keep pressure—an option that might buy both leverage and distrust in equal measure.

You taste iron and rain and the cold burn of people watching.

You look at the faces closest to you: Kira’s defiant jaw; Mateo’s quiet, unblinking resolve; Elias’s pleading eyes, complex and unreadable in the floodlight. The public feeds pulse. The council bell repeats. The officers' hands hover like a question mark.

This is the moment. The world is waiting for the tiny sound that will become the city's story.
