You press the napkin into your palm the way you pressed it yesterday—an anchor of ink and compromise. The pen trembles at the edge of the covenant like a small animal waiting to leap. The greenhouse humidity—warm, close—pulls behind your ribs.

Jonah Hale sits across from you, the polished pen already moving as if it owns the clauses. His amber-brown eyes are unreadable; there is a practiced patience there, the kind that can take a long view and bend people to it. He taps the part about "emergency windows"—a clause that sounds sensible in a boardroom and dangerous in a neighborhood that remembers whose house got left behind last flood.

Jonah Hale: "Emergency provisions are standard. They permit rapid response to imminent hazard. We include them and move forward. The people get protections now; they get the wall and the restoration funds."

You: "Emergency provisions are also the easiest place to slide privatization under the door. 'Emergency' is a word that, once written, can be widened like a sluice."

Jonah Hale: (smiling, even) "You're a careful negotiator, Ayla. We all are. That clause doesn't undercut the land trusts. You're protected—legally. Restoration grants, oversight committee, timelines. The legalese is there."

Mayor Serena Okoye: "We built in oversight language. Community counsel seats. An independent auditor. No unilateral action without a judicial finding of immediate threat."

Elias leans forward, voice low but firm—the technical comfort of a man who can show you a model and say, 'see?'

Elias Rook: "Legally, this is as tight as we can make it without halting the project entirely. The delay risk is real: every month without some action increases exposure. We can litigate later if something bad happens, but right now—this buys time, space, funding."

You feel the room narrow around that syllogism: delay increases exposure. Protections now reduce immediate harm. Buy time to fight later. Your chest tightens in a way you recognize—it's not fear exactly but the drag of an impossible arithmetic that asks you to weight neighbors' roofs against legal clauses.

Kira Tseng: (voice bright with something like hurt) "So we sign? We hand them half the harbor and call it 'co-management'? Again?"

You: "Kira—"

Kira Tseng: "No. 'No' means no. This is exactly what they do. They make you choose between survival and solidarity."

Elias puts up a hand, trying to bridge, but Kira cuts him off.

Kira Tseng: "You can talk about models and timetables, but models don't taste like tomatoes and don't remember whom the water took."

Elias Rook: "Kira—I'm not choosing 'models' over memory. I'm trying to buy time so the memory has something to come back to."

Kira's jaw tightens. She pushes her binoculars and megaphone away as if they burn. The motion is small and final.

Kira Tseng: "If you sign, Ayla, don't expect the neighborhood to forgive it. Not without blood. Not without proof that the promise means anything."

You: "Kira—I'm trying to get you the protections. The greenhouse is in there. The land trust—"

Kira Tseng: (cutting you off) "Legal language is a promise written by people who profit on erasure. I will not be complicit."

Then she rises. The wooden chair scrapes a high, strident note. She strides out, raincoat flapping—her departure a raw, sudden absence. The door bangs. Conversation fragments into aftershocks.

Mayor Serena Okoye: (softly) "Kira's right to feel betrayed. This process is ugly. But the timeline—if we don't act now, the federal money reshuffles and the worst outcomes accelerate."

Mateo sits quietly, hands folded over each other on the table like an old knot of rope. He speaks only when the room quiets enough to hear the baritone of memory.

Mateo Alvarez: "When the tide first came up past the dock, it took the chicken coop and my daughter's old boots. We rebuilt. But they took land to rebuild the shipping yard, and none of us were at the table. Contracts smell like the city after a storm—like promises left to rust. Be careful, niña."

Jonah's pen scratches. He looks like a man measuring odds.

Jonah Hale: "This is not about greed. This is about securing infrastructure to keep people alive. We are not monoliths of profit here. There are literal lives on the line."

You can hear the room constrict. You've walked these negotiations before, where the grammar of 'safety' and 'progress' swallow smaller nouns like 'garden' and 'belonging'. Elias glances at you; there's pleading in his eyes—compassion folded into calculation.

Elias Rook: "If you hesitate, we may lose the scaffold of legal protections. If we sign, we keep oversight and community seats. If the worst of Jonah's people try to privatize, the law will stand between them and the land."

You lift the pen. The tip hovers. Paper whispers under the motion of hands. The muscles in your forearm tense like a rope pulled taut.

Phones bloom open around the table. Notifications cascade like a rain of small alarms. Voices rise. Chairs scrape. The negotiated calm—tense and fragile—fractures into noise.

Jonah Hale: (face narrowing, as if someone pulled the light away from him and something inside gathers) "That's doctored. That's an internal estimate—out of context."

Elias stares at the screen, then at Jonah, then at you—the scientist's face contorting between disbelief and the cold algebra of plausibility.

Elias Rook: "If that's real—if that's real—it changes everything."

Mayor Serena Okoye: (voice clipped) "We need to verify. We will call for an immediate audit. But this—this will be the headline. The council will move the vote forward on an accelerated timeline to appear decisive."

A hand slaps the table; someone shouts. Kira, who had already left, stands in the doorway now—backlit by rain and evening—her eyes red and certain. She didn't stay away. Her presence is a cutting blade.

Kira Tseng: (to the whole room) "You pushed a signature—what did you think would happen? Silence? Complicity?"

Jonah Hale: (cool, but the edges of his voice are gone) "We will investigate. We will hold to our terms. The clause stands."

You feel something that has the shape of being cleft in two: the legal protections on the table that might preserve green roofs and the greenhouse—versus the revelation that those protections could be a paper shield for privatization if the timeline is moved and the 'emergency' windows invoked.

The room vibrates with a hundred small energies: reporters already dialing, feeds sharing the grainy video, a council aide whispering that the vote timeline is being accelerated to head off chaos, Elias asking you, without quite saying it, what you want. Jonah's eyes are fixed on the napkin, then the pen, then the door.

Mateo exhales. "This is how they move—fast and tidy. They make you sign before you can read a single word."

Your chest tightens. You can taste metal. The pen is heavier now than it was a minute ago.
