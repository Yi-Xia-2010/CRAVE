[Scene: Offshore Project Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Sleek shipping containers arranged like a temporary village; scaffolding silhouettes against the bay; an AR schematic pulses on a freestanding display]
[Music: Tense BGM — low synth under fast strings]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the thump of a generator, murmured conversation]
You step off the metal gangway with your palms still carrying the grit of the marsh. The LED glare is harsh after the soft light of the wetlands; it throws shadows into the hollows of the containers and makes every face look more practiced, more tired. Elias waits beneath an awning, jacket neat despite the wind. He opens a leather folio as if presenting terms of a treaty, not a plan, the pages catching the light like held breath.

Elias Wren: "Maia. Thank you for coming. We wanted you to see the staging firsthand before tonight's meeting."

You: (You want to say that staging is theatre until the sea tells otherwise; instead you keep your hands where they won't leave mud smudges on the paper.) "Show me."

[Visual: Close-up on the folio — timelines, glossy renderings of a curved breakwater, arrays of turbines on the horizon; small print labeled 'impact assessment' catches your eye]
[Sound: The AR pin on Elias's lapel emits a soft chime as a coastal schematic blooms in midair]
Elias Wren: (calm, measured) "This is accelerated deployment. Phased installation—breakwater first, then platform integration. We can start piling in six months. Funding is committed. Jobs for the harbor. Significant reduction in modeled flood risk."

You feel the word 'significant' like a temperature reading you cannot fully trust. It promises relief and takes away something in the same breath.

You: "And fish migration? You have models that look at larval drift, spawning windows?"

Elias Wren: (a small, controlled smile) "We do. Our consultants ran biophysical models across seasonal cycles. We can share datasets and incorporate mitigation—thermal refugia, directional flow corridors."

You: (He offers data; you want community knowledge.) "Shared datasets is not the same as community oversight. Who signs off on the mitigation during construction? Who monitors after?"

Elias leans forward, the confidence of someone accustomed to signatures and timelines. His eyes, precise and cool, hold yours a beat longer than necessary.

Elias Wren: "We can set up an independent monitoring board. Outside auditors. Transparency—"

You: (You hear 'outside' and the word feels like a wall for the town.) "Independent as in contracted by you, with limited reporting timelines? Or independent as in community representation with veto power on sensitive habitat areas?"

Elias's expression flickers—just for an instant—before smoothing.

Elias Wren: "Contracts can be written to include stakeholder seats. But operational delays reduce site resilience for everyone. There is a cost to adding veto clauses."

[Visual: Mayor Rosa appears behind Elias, watching, a stack of permit documents in her hands. Her gaze travels from the folio to you, then to the screens showing economic forecasts in neat columns.]
Mayor Rosa Santiago: (soft, urgent) "Maia, these funds could rebuild our seawalls quicker than incremental grants. The council has to consider livelihoods—fast deployment means fishermen back on the water sooner."

You: (Mayor Rosa's voice is a ledger of responsibility. You can feel the electorate behind every pause she takes.) "I understand the urgency. I also understand what gets lost when 'fast' becomes shorthand for 'decide for us.'"

[Sound: A muted roar — voices carrying from the harbor below. Through an open flap you can see Jonah standing on the dock, jacket heavy with salt, jaw set as if weighing an anchor of memory and fear.]
Jonah Kade: (calling up) "You telling them that, Maia? Or telling us it's a done deal?"

You: (Jonah's tone is a line pulled taut between you and him. He stands where the boards meet the water, the harbor breathing around him.) "I'm not telling anyone anything yet. I'm asking questions."

Jonah Kade: (bitter, restrained) "Questions take time. Time is a storm."

You: (You feel the friction of those words like sand between your teeth. Jonah's skepticism is not theatrical; it’s history pulled close—family nets, seasons mapped on calluses. You want to reassure him and also to not promise what you haven't earned.)

You: (Dana's accusation lands like a pebble in your gut and ricochets into every corner of your resolve. Her anger is familiar—she has held you accountable before. You did not come to be an object of blame, but you also did not come to rubber-stamp a plan.)

You step between the murmurs and the folio, feeling the crowd's eyes as small weights. Tomas is at your elbow, cane tapping the wood twice; his face is set like old driftwood—worn, reliable.

Tomas Rivera: (quiet, cracked) "Maia, watch the fine print. There's a lot you can't get back after the contracts are signed. Remember the boathouse. Remember what we lost when we let someone else define 'coast.'"

Lupe Kade: (defensive) "They signed a pledge—we can demand enforceable language."

Elias Wren: (softly, to you) "Every hour we delay increases exposure. Data suggests—"
