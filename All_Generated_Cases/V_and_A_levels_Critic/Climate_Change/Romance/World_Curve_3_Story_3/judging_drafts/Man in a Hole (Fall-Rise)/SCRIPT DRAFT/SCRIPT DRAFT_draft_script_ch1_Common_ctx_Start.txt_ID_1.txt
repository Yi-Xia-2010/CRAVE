You wake before the town does. The rooftop is still cool under your feet—corrugated metal tempered by the night's fog—and the rosemary on the ledge smells exactly like your mother's hands after she pulled it free from the earth: resinous, sharp, familiar. The compass pendant at your throat swings for a moment, catching the first thin light. Your hair is already in a messy bun; salt streaks the tips where you let sea spray dry into the ends, a small badge of weather you can't entirely wash away.

You reach into the pocket of your olive field jacket. The Moleskine is heavier than it should be at this hour; tide sketches crowd the topmost pages, thumbed edges softened by long nights of fieldwork. You thumb the braided blue cord bracelet on your wrist without thinking—an anchor more than ornament. The town sleeps below you; every rooftop seems a small island, breathing quietly.

You inhale. The air tastes of salt and rosemary and something metallic from the harbor. Somewhere two houses down, a door creaks and old wood speaks: Tomas is up. His voice—grainy, low, a push of weathered consonants—drifts up like steam.

Tomas: "You up, Maia? Thought I heard you moving about."

You: "Always up earlier than I need to be." (You close the notebook, feeling the familiar weight of things un-checked.)

Tomas's footsteps come to the stoop beneath your window. He talks like someone who has measured decades by tides; there is no hurry in him, only a steady inventory of what the sea has taken and what it has left.

Tomas: "Good. Thought I'd bring over some of Rosa's bread. The storm last week left the Kade boys with too many nets to mend and not enough mouths fed."

You: "Don't let them tell you I'm running a soup kitchen—I'm running a restoration." (You force a half-smile—light enough to be civil, not wide enough to invite more.)

Tomas chuckles, the sound like rope being coiled.

Tomas: "Same thing in different jackets. Sit with me awhile. You been burning daylight at the wetlands again?"

You: "You know me. I measure loss the way others measure time." (The phrase is truer than it should be; you can feel its gravity in your chest, a small, steady pull.)

Tomas: "You were always like that. Little Maia with a ruler and a magnifying glass. Shame about the boathouse, girl—still don't like to talk about it."

You: The memory arrives like a tide—slow, inexorable. Your hands go to the compass at your throat. "Neither do I." (You try to make your voice even. The failed response to the last big storm plays like footage behind your eyes: missed windows, a misread swell. You were not the only one to make mistakes, but it is yours to carry.)

Tomas's voice softens; you can tell he remembers, too.

Tomas: "You came back when we needed you. That counts for something out here."

You: "It has to." (You don't say that you still dream in the smell of wet wood and engine oil, or that some nights you wake and count boardwalk planks as if they'll align into an apology.)

[Visual: Dawn light thins; the rooftop's warmth leaches into the morning air]
[Sound: A gull wheels once, then again; distant hammering rhythm from the harbor]

You take a small jar of coffee, bitter and reheated, from Tomas's careful hands. For a moment you simply stand, the town unspooling beneath you and all its small, stubborn life: laundry lines, neon fishmonger sign blinking off, a fishing boat rocking gently at the dock. The rooftop garden catches the first copper streaks of sunrise and you imagine the marsh grasses doing the same—bows of sea-teal into gold.

[Scene: Marisol Wetland Reserve | Early Morning]
[Visual: Boardwalk slick with last night's rain; reeds bowed, droplets beading on grass]
[Music: Minimal piano, sparse and patient]
[Sound: Soft creak of wood against rope, distant gulls, water lapping against a pylons]

The boardwalk under your boots is cool and slick. Salt-packed planks reflect the thawing sky; your breath fogs faintly as you move between tidal creeks that lace the marsh. You check the Moleskine again—hand-drawn channels, notes on wrack composition, a checklist of plots—but it's a ritual as much as a tool. You move methodically: measure a creek mouth, note foam lines, press a gloved finger into silt to feel the mud's give. The town's quiet this hour amplifies everything: the drip from an old piling, the papery clap of a gull's wing. There is a purity in the work that steadies you.

You are not alone at the edge. Jonah is there—no fanfare, just the familiar silhouette against a low sky. His canvas cap is pulled low; hair wind-tossed. The cooperative's boat bobs behind him, nets folded like patient beasts. He carries an air of weathered competence, sleeves rolled, hands that smell of diesel and salt.

Jonah: "Didn't expect to see you before the tide. Thought you liked to make me look better at public meetings." (He gives you a lopsided grin—half teasing, half the way a person checks a shore line for weaknesses.)

You: "Someone has to know the lines before the meeting starts." (You keep your voice flat, professional. You don't want this to be about anything but the marsh and the data.)

Jonah: "And someone has to keep old men like Tomas from roping you into breakfast for a week." (He nudges a net with the toe of his boot.) "You been busy? Nets, tides—what's on the page today?"

You: "Creek mouth accretion, wrack composition, two sections where spartina looks stunted." (You point, and he follows to a narrow inlet where sediment has piled unevenly.) "I've been mapping the channels and the probable overwash paths if we get a surge."

Jonah watches the water, his profile set against the silvered surface. For a moment he says nothing; his eyes—sea-glass and practiced—move in patterns you know: reading currents like someone reads faces.

Jonah: "You worry too much about maps and not enough about fixing nets." (He smiles, but it doesn't quite reach his eyes. There's a familiar undercurrent—protective, cautious.) "You still blaming yourself for that night?"

You: The question hangs like fog. Your fingers tighten around the Moleskine. You could deflect, make a joke about being older and more measured. Instead you find your voice small, true.

You: "I keep thinking if I had called the evacuation sooner—if I'd pushed harder—maybe the boathouse would still be standing." (You don't ask for pity. You only let the fact be out in the open.)

Jonah: "You did what you could. Hindsight's a cruel tide." (He looks at you, unflinching.) "Besides, you came back. You didn't walk away."

You: "That doesn't make it lighter." (You fold your arms to keep from reaching for him. There is an old pattern—a closeness that is easy and dangerous, threaded through years of storms and shared labor.)

Jonah: "Then don't make it heavier by carrying it alone." (He steps closer, not overstepping the unspoken line. You feel the dip of his presence like a boat tied nearby—steady, familiar.)

Jonah's voice takes on its easy cadence again, listing repairs and a schedule; the two of you fall into the comfortable rhythm of people who have survived weather side-by-side. You make notes, count materials, and imagine the labor as a small, manageable problem among a thousand.

[Sound: A soft ping from your jacket pocket]
[Visual: The Moleskine peeks from your coat; the screen of your field tablet glows briefly as a message arrives]

You pull out your tablet. Ava's message sits there—short, clinical, the kind of thing she sends when she has late-night calculations to share.

[Visual: Close-up on tablet screen]
Ava (Text): "Maia—checked the tide gauge logs. There's an anomaly at gauge 3: spike at 03:14, then a faster-than-expected recovery. Preliminary storm model shows a slightly increased surge window for the next wind event. Sending flagged data. -A"

You read it twice. The words themselves are not dramatic; the anomaly could be instrumentation, a passing squall, a glitch. But the sum of the data is a tightening at your sternum. You feel the braided blue cord at your wrist like a tug anchor—responsibility made tactile.

You: (You thumb a reply.) "On it. Send me the flagged files; I'm heading to gauge 3 now."

Ava (Text): "Uploading. Be careful—it's early. Coffee's on me later."

You let the tablet go back into your pocket. On the surface you are methodical—mapping creeks, cataloging wrack, planning meetings. Your hands move with practiced precision: a tape measure, a pen, an exchange of nods with Jonah as he ducks down to check a line on a net. You keep the checklist in front of you like a shield.

Inside, a small part of you—old, guilty, an echo from a storm—tightens. You remember the last failed response: a decision tree where you chose the path that later fractured; the boathouse lost. You replay what you could have done differently: called for more boats, asked for more manpower, stayed on the radio longer. The memory isn't a single image but a smear of sensations—wet wood, the electric whine of a failing generator, your hand on a soaked ledger that refused to be read.

You fold that memory into the present the way you fold maps: neat, useful, a guide rather than an accusation. You will not let it freeze you. Yet it is there, as real as the damp under your boots.

Jonah : "You sure you want to take gauge 3 alone? Lupe could come down if you want company."

You: "I can do it. It's a quick read; if it's odd, I'll flag it." (You don't say that part of you wants to go alone—wants to prove that the past won't define the future.)

Jonah: "All right. Just—check in." (He looks at you, open and unadorned. The harbor behind him is a small, patient city of workboats. His loyalty is obvious; so is his worry.)

You nod and move toward the narrow spit where gauge 3 waits on its weathered piling. The marsh smells of low tide and mud; droplets bead on spartina blades and fall away. You pull the notebook from your jacket and make a small note—'gauge 3 check'—and then another, almost an incantation: 'call meeting if spike confirmed.'

[Sound: Your pen skritches on paper]

As you log the first reading, the Moleskine closes against your palm with a soft thud—the end of an old page and the promise of a new one. You feel the first small firming of resolve—the quiet kind that precedes action.

You close the Moleskine, feeling the town's slow heartbeat in the wood under your boots, and you resolve to act.

[Visual: The sun lifts a little higher; light strips the day in thin bands]
[Music: A single, steady piano note holds, quiet and expectant]

You let the feeling sit—responsibility as a physical weight on the braided cord at your wrist—and prepare to translate it into meetings, maps, outreach. The town is fragile and sleeping, but not gone. You have a ledger and a plan and people who will stand, even if they will do so imperfectly.

You take one last look across the marsh. A gull lifts, a boat's engine coughs awake at the harbor, and someone hammers in the distance. The morning is small and actionable, the kind of morning you work inside.

You close the Moleskine and set your jaw.
