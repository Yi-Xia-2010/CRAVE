## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Marisol

---

## World View
Marisol Bay is a small coastal town in the near future (circa 2040), defined by the ebb of tides and the urgency of climate change. Rising seas and stronger storms have hollowed out older certainties: the boathouse where children learned knots is a memory in the Flooded Historic Quarter; the Harbor and Boardwalk still smells of salt, diesel, and frying fish; the Marisol Wetland Reserve is a place of both restoration hope and fragile ecology. The town's institutions are a patchwork of civic earnestness and economic precarity. Mayor Rosa Santiago must balance petitions and developers while community organizers like Dana Park rally direct action; local science is practical and rooted — Maia Rivera and her colleague Ava Kim map tidal flows and reef seedings — while corporate climate-tech projects, represented by Elias Wren’s firm, promise rapid fixes with tradeoffs.

Politics is local and urgent: fishing cooperatives, kin networks, and the town hall meetings on folding chairs dictate real power. Religion is private and practical — ceremonies at the harbor, memorials for storm victims — while class lines follow who owns boats, who rents, and who trades on tourism. Geography compresses everything into salt-scented lanes: fog-laced marshes, a lived-in boardwalk, a clinical offshore staging site, and rooftops where rosemary grows beside corrugated tin. Atmosphere mixes watercolor nostalgia with a tension that hums beneath civic hearings and late-night stakeouts. The culture prizes resilience, thrift, and oral history; an animated sense of stewardship competes with the siren call of large-scale technological salvation.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maia Rivera) return to Marisol Bay to lead wetland restoration, driven by guilt over a childhood flood and a failed past response. The town stands at a crossroads: the community’s grassroots stewardship and fishing traditions face pressure from Elias Wren’s corporate-scale engineering project, which promises large flood protection but threatens local autonomy and marine patterns. Jonah Kade, a third-generation fisher and the warm, stubborn captain of the cooperative, stands for tradition and local control. Elias wants a fast, centralized solution backed by funding and political leverage. My choices — to partner with Jonah and community-based restoration, to negotiate with Elias for a hybrid compromise, or to expose corporate compromises and risk short-term funding — shape both civic outcomes and intimate relationships. The “Man in a Hole” arc sends me into crisis (an escalating storm, a political betrayal, and fractured trust), then through struggle and rebuilding as I reconcile guilt, accept help, and decide what stewardship truly means. Romance threads: Jonah’s steady loyalty, Elias’s pragmatic charisma, and the community’s need for ethical leadership all pull on my heart and ethics. Multiple endings — happy, bittersweet, sad, and true — depend on whether I preserve local control, sacrifice parts of the bay, or choose principle over expedience.
