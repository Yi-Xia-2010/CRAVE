You feel the word speed like a rolling tide: it can save lives, but it can also drown histories. You plant your hands on the table because otherwise they'll tremble.

Elias Kahn: "Conditional funding is useful if it comes without strings that remove local oversight. We need a legally binding monitoring board with veto power over unilateral modifications during the pilot."

Camila 'Kai' Navarro's jaw tightens. Her legal lead speaks up, voice smooth as oil: "A veto could stall progress. We can agree to an oversight board, but performance metrics should allow for phased implementation. Two years is optimistic; twelve months proves success faster."

Maya Corvin: "Phased implementation can work, but the metrics must include ecological indicators — not just structural integrity and short-term breach rates. If monitoring looks only at concrete performance, the wetlands disappear on paper."

Camila 'Kai' Navarro: "Add them. We'll include ecological metrics." (Her eyes search your face, like testing the elasticity of a promise.) "But if the metrics show the corridors are failing, we need a contingency to protect critical urban arteries."

Elias Kahn: "Contingencies must be proportionate. We write them into the amendment so they trigger adaptive measures, not wholesale hardening."

The debate spins, legal language iterating between you like the tide's back-and-forth. Memos get revised; phrases exchanged and rephrased. Each concession is a small letting-go of an ideal, each gain a carefully stapled hope.

Elias Kahn taps his pen, counting legal syllables. You can see the calculator of risk and reward in his face, how tired decision-making has made him careful where once he might have lunged.

Elias Kahn: "We can accept a phased schedule, but not a unilateral escalation clause. Any escalation requires board review and a minimum remediation period where adaptive measures are tested."

Camila 'Kai' Navarro: "That adds layers and delays. Investors dislike ambiguity."
