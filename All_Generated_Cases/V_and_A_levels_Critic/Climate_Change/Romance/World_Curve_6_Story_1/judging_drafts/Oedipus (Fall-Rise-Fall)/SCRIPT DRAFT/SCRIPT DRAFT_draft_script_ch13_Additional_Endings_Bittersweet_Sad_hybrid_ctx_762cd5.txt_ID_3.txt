[Scene: New Promenade | Early Morning, Days After the Surge]
[Visual: Wide shot of the new seawall, slick concrete glinting under a pale sun. Temporary sensors blink along the parapet. The Low Row sits inland, half-visible through scaffolding and tarps.]
[Sound: Distant generator hum, the intermittent clang of construction, and the low, steady murmur of people setting up relief stations]
[Music: Sparse strings; a steady, minor-key motif that does not resolve]

You are standing where you pressed your palm into the stone weeks ago. The print is faint now — a pale smudge a gull might mistake for a fishbone — but your palm remembers the cool, indifferent texture whether the mark is visible or not.

The seawall did its job. That is the sentence the city repeats on loop: did its job. Mayor Velez on the pile, lights on municipal screens, the firm’s press releases. Engineers in corporate colors are photographed with hard hats and practiced smiles. The headlines praise an efficiency that looks, from a distance, like a clean solution.

Up close, the ledger looks different.

The promenade smells of boiled kelp and diesel and the faint, metallic tang that always follows construction. A thin mist from last night's tide hangs low; when you breathe, the air tastes of salt and a small, persistent bitterness. You can see the places the surge skipped — entire shops reduced to warped plywood and corrugated tin, a bakery's painted sign still half-legible, the rooftop nursery's greenhouse dented like an empty shell.

You move along the walkway because you have to; grief is a thing that multiplies with inaction. Each step is a counting. Each face you pass is a subtraction you cannot erase.

[Visual: Camera pushes in on your wrist; the trefoil shadowed beneath your sleeve]
[Sound: A muted conversation blooms and fades; someone laughs too loud, then goes quiet]

Rafi is the first person who finds you accurately — not because he's looking harder than anyone else, but because he always knows where to stand where hands are needed.

Rafi: (hands full of relief packets, voice worn but steady) "Maya. We set up another tent by the old fountain. Kids keep asking about the tomatoes. Thought you might want to see the mural sketches Lio started."

You take the bundle from him, fingers sticky with tape. His palms smell of soil and antiseptic. In the exchange, there is the economy of people who have been giving parts of themselves away for weeks.

You: "Show me."

Rafi hesitates a second — a small throttle of pride and shame — then slides a damp sketch across his knee. Lio's blocky figures, paint-smeared text, a sun half-swallowed by waves. An elegy in bright colors.

Rafi: (quiet) "He's doing three panels. Says they should be loud enough to bother the council. Says he wants names. We... we need to log who's been rehoused. The list is longer than the tents."

You fold the sketch into your jacket, the paper soft from too many hands.

You walk toward Low Row because that's where the noise is thickest — not the municipal applause, but the broken conversations of neighbors making what decisions they can. The streets smell of wet paint and the wood smoke of stove fires set up in community kitchens. Flags and banners flap like small tattered lungs.

Lio stands on a crate with a paintbrush in one hand, a roll of masking tape in the other. He is smaller than you remember and bigger in the way teenagers become giants at memorials.

Lio: (voice rough) "They say it saved the city." (He laughs without humor.) "Saved the city. Did it save our roof, Maya?"

You do not answer with an easy line. You should not offer comfort you do not feel. Instead, you stand and look at what remains of his mural: a line of figures linked by hands, their lower halves submerged in stylized blue. There is a name already written in jagged letters you recognize, and another space left open.

You: "It bought time. It bought infrastructure. It didn't buy everything."

Lio's face shifts — that complex expression that could be fury, grief, a barter with hope.

Lio: (soft) "We paint the names they forget. We make sure the city has to see what it did. Will you come tonight, Maya? With the speaker? Say something like you always do."

You imagine standing again in front of a microphone, translating graphs into people's lives. The memory is a splinter that never quite leaves you.

Rafi looks at you like you have suggested rearranging the bones; he squeezes your hand and says he'll do it, but your stomach feels hollow."]

You do not stay long at the crate. The work is a thousand small fires; if you pile yourself on each one, you will burn out. Rafi is already sprinting between tents, issuing rations with a practiced calm. You follow him to the relief center because the administrative end of grief is a different kind of pressure — forms, lists, the mechanical ways people are turned into data.

[Scene: Temporary Fences and Relief Center | Afternoon]
[Visual: Tents lined behind temporary cyclone fencing. A makeshift registration desk; posters stapled ask about housing, about grievance committees. Volunteers move with tired efficiency.]
[Sound: The rattle of a typewriter app, soft murmurs, a child's persistent question: "Is our house gone?"]
[Music: Low, piano undercurrent — a note that repeats like a wound reopening]

The center smells like disinfectant and boiled beans. You stand behind a folding table because being on the other side of a ledger helps you hold the math of it in your hands.

A woman is crying quietly in a chair, one hand over a paper photograph. A man is trying to explain — the insurance, the clause. You take complaints and promises and when you hand them a pen it feels like handing them a lifeline, though you know it's thin rope.

Elias arrives at the fence with a municipal pass hanging from his coat. He looks smaller in public service clothes, as if the tailored trench coat could not quite armor him from the city's edges. His amber eyes are tired in the way of someone who has been translating compromise into policy and found the grammar lacking.

Elias: (voice low) "Maya."

You pause. He lingers on the greeting, as if learning to say your name in a new tense.

You: "Elias."

He steps forward, the breeze lifting his coat. You smell his coat's synthetic waterproofing and an espresso on his breath. There is an entire world in the gap between the two of you that used to be less roomy and more immediate.

Elias: (after a beat) "I— I came to check on the families in A-section. The municipal allocation finally cleared. We're moving ten families into interim housing tonight. I wanted to tell you."

His hands are steady; his voice is steady. The steadiness is part of who he is — and part of why compromises accumulate. You look at the list he carries; you do not need to see the names to know what is implied by "interim" and by the bureaucratic optimism that sits on the other side of so much loss.

You: "Ten families. That's something." (You want to ask which names. Your mouth lays down diplomacy you have learned to wear.) "Was the allocation tied to the seawall contract clauses?"

Elias hesitates, a flicker of the municipal calculation that you know and distrust.

Elias: (careful) "There were conditions. Monitoring stations, data-sharing with the firm. But it frees up beds. It means fewer people sleeping in shelters tonight."

You fold your hands until your knuckles complain. The logic is not wrong; it is incomplete.

You: "It buys roofs, Elias. Not histories."

He blinks, and for a second his expression is unreadable — a careful mask that loosens.

Elias: (soft) "I know. I know. I— I've been trying to make it hold both things. The pilot program— the amendment — it's meant to—"

You: "—preserve corridors." (You do not finish the sentence; it hangs between you like a punctuation made of salt.)

The conversation opens and closes like a tide. The more Elias explains, the more you sense the wedge. His language is municipal: "scalable," "pilot," "conditional." Your language is neighborly: "names," "mural," "tomatoes." Neither of you is wholly wrong. Neither of you is wholly right.

Elias: (quick, voice slipping) "Maya. I... the mayor offered me a permanent oversight role for the coastal resilience program. They want someone who understands both the data and the people. I— I accepted."

The ground tilts. You feel it even though the pavement under your boots is steady.

You: (the question is simple, but all the air leaves your chest) "Accepted?"

Elias: (measured, as if reading minutes aloud) "Yes. It's... it's a lot. But I thought— I thought it would let me keep fighting from inside."

There is a small, brittle truth to that. He will be able to do policy work; he will have leverage. But you have watched leverage become a series of compromises that erode things that do not translate well into spreadsheets. You think of Lio's mural and the names he wants to paint. You think of Rafi's hands dug into soil. You think of the quiet way people sleep now with the knowledge of what was given up to buy this sleep.

You: (you speak slowly; the words are an attempt to anchor) "Inside the system, Elias. Inside the pages they sign. What happens when the numbers demand we trade another roof for a sensor network?"

Elias flinches, as if struck by a question he cannot fully answer in policy terms.

Elias: (whispers) "We try not to make that trade. We write clauses. We insist on oversight."

You: "You already accepted oversight as part of the same package as the seawall."

He does not answer immediately. When he does, his voice is small, apologetic and honest.

Elias: (voice breaking a little) "I wanted to be useful, Maya. I wanted to keep you whole."

Something in you both aches. Multi-layered grief — for the people displaced, for the city remade, and for the distance that has crept between you and someone who once matched your cadence — settles in like sediment.

Elias: (after a beat) "I don't know if staying close to the office means losing closeness to... this. But I can't not try."

You: "Trying from a desk is not the same as standing with your neighbors when tidewater finds their threshold."

He looks at you, and you both know the truth in that sentence is a specter wider than either of you.

Elias: (quietly, almost to himself) "I hope you understand why."

You: "I understand the math." (And then: "I understand the cost.") But you do not say the last part aloud. Saying it would be a confirmation; you are not ready to gift that certainty to the city.

[Visual: A municipal van pulls away; a volunteer folds a tarp. The sun sits heavy and dull. Your breath fogs in the mild, humid air.]

You watch Elias walk away across the gravel lot, shoulders squared to duty. The city will have him now in a different register. You feel the thread between you stretch thin, like twine pulled taut until it threatens to snap.

[Scene: Construction Staging Area Overlook | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A raised platform behind the fencing where engineers pace. Kai stands with a tablet, the corporate sigil on her jacket catching light. A small cluster of workers speaks in low, tense voices.]
[Sound: Machines far below the promenade; the low discordant chatter of men in radios; the clink of metal]
[Music: Sparse cello, a single held note that now deepens]

Kai moves like someone who was never meant to be stationary in grief. Her hair is platinum against a gray sky, her jacket immaculate despite the site dust. There is the same efficiency about her as always, but the edges of her jaw are sharper than the public photographs.

Kai: (curt nod, voice cool) "Maya."

You: "Kai."

She gestures toward a cluster of engineers gathered by a shipping container. A man with grease on his palms meets your eyes and looks away. The sight registers like a detail you were not expecting: human fatigue losing its formal posture.

Kai: (low, the corporate mask softening for a second) "We weathered the surge. Sensors held. Protocols worked."

You: "Protocols didn't stop the bakery from burning down."

Kai's face does not soften at that. Instead, she points out a tablet screen to you — a staggered set of messages, terse and technical, but clear enough about one thing: the crew is smaller than planned for the timeline, and there are logs of missed maintenance windows. You do not have to translate the code to understand the human cost.

Kai: (uncharacteristically candid) "There was internal dissent. Some of the on-site team felt rushed. They filed complaints. Some refused overtime. It's a strike in effect, small, but—"

You: "They were pushed."

Kai: (blink) "Yes. And I did what the project needed to stay on schedule."

Her voice is level but not free of strain. For a breath, you can see the person instead of the firm's emblem: someone who believes in an end so necessary she will accept the means. You remember the porcelain pendant she keeps hidden—what it means to hold a small softness behind hardened decisions—yet that memory is your own, a private thing.

You: "People are tired, Kai. They lost homes."

Kai: (a small, almost inaudible acknowledgment) "I know. I don't deny it. But thousands of people didn't drown. The infrastructure is intact. Hospitals ran. Schools stayed open."

You: "At the cost of—" (you gesture to Low Row visible through the fencing—scaffolds, tarps, a child's swing hanging from a half-standing frame) "—what mattered here."

Kai swallows. For a moment, her eyes are not steel but something like fatigue and remorse, slotted through with a professional certainty.

Kai: (quiet) "I have memos that show the dissent. I can give you access. It's small. It may not change the headlines. But it's there. People felt cornered. They felt rushed."

You: (listening) "Will those memos do anything? Will the firm accept fault?"

Kai shrugs in a way that suggests a contract's margins are not the same as the moral maps you carry.

Kai: (careful) "They will soften the public rhetoric. Internally, there will be reviews. That's how these things get fixed — slowly, buried in committees."

You feel the familiar ache of calls to patience. Fixes that arrive in committee rooms rarely translate to rebuilt gardens or returned storefronts.

Kai: (after a pause) "Maya. I didn't come to justify. I came to tell you: the people on the ground are fracturing. If you want access to the logs, I can make it happen. Not because I want the praise, but because you should have the information."

Her offer is transactional and uneven by nature — a data hand extended from a tower of consequence. You want to take it and make meaning, to translate logs into leverage and memory. But you are wary — information can be used to oil the gears that grind people down. Still, inside it is a kernel: proof that not everyone in her world is indifferent.

You: "Why tell me this now?"

Kai: (a flash of something almost like softness) "Because the people who build the wall are human. Because some of them won't be okay with how fast we asked them to go. Because someone needs to keep watch over the seams."

Her words are not an apology. They are a small, dangerous admission that the machine is built of many small, fragile humannesses. You accept the tablet she passes; its glass is cool under your thumb.

You hand the tablet back after copying a file into your own drive. The gesture is small and legal enough to keep you within the boundaries of law and risk.

You: "We'll need names. Statements. People willing to testify are better than memos."

Kai: (nod) "I'll ask my contacts. Not everyone will cooperate. They fear losing work."

You: "They shouldn't have to choose between safety and voice."

She meets your gaze, unblinking.

Kai: "No. They shouldn't."

There is no reconciliation offered, only an agreement about a fact. The tenderness — such as it might be between you — is transactional: data for access, witness for proof. It leaves your chest hollow in a way that is nearly indistinguishable from grief.

[Scene: New Promenade | Twilight]
[Visual: The sun falls into a thin ribbon of red. The promenade is quieter now. Strings of temporary lights begin to glow. The mural in Low Row shows names, some freshly painted, some left blank. Your shadow stretches long and alone.]
[Sound: A distant corbelled bell, voices dwindling to whispers, the tide's soft insistence]
[Music: A single cello line holds; a low, unresolved chord]

You stand at the edge of the promenade, trefoil pressed between your fingers under the cuff of your sleeve. The tattoo is small and private and yet it feels like a secret vow the city cannot legislate away. You let the weight of the day settle into your bones: the seawall saved many lives; it also rearranged yours.

Elias' offer of "inside" will take him away from the immediate friction of neighbors and into the calculus of policy. It is a necessary path for some; it is a painful betrayal for others. Kai's admission of the strike is proof of the human cost fronting the project's headline victories. Lio's mural will make those costs visible, but murals do not replace homes.

You breathe, and the breath is a plane of grief that will not break but will keep you working.

You name the feeling aloud because naming exacts it from your chest: "This is not victory."

It is not a cry. It is a ledger entry for your heart.

You fold the trefoil beneath your palm the way you fold small, private things into usable iron. Vigilance will be daily labor now: a patrol of names, a record of promises, a stubborn insistence that the city's applause won't be the last word.

You step down from the promenade and head inland toward the murals and the tents — toward the place where grief and work live in the same room.
