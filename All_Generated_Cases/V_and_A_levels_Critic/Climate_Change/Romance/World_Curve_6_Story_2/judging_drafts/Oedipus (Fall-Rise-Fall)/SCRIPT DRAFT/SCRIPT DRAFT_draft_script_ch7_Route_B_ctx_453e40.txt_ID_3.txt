[Scene: Council Hall | Early Morning — Immediately after the Vote]
[Visual: Wide shot of the meeting chamber; fluorescent light pools over concrete benches. Iris stands in the center with her municipal lapel pin catching a shard of light. You are at the front, brass compass against your sternum.]
[Music: Rapid, rhythmic percussion underscored by low synth pulses]
[Sound: A murmured ripple of assent; the distant slap of rain against the terrace doors]

You remember the tilt of the room when you stood — the way everyone's faces sharpened, how the air folded itself into a single, listening thing. You remember saying the words out loud: "I will back Iris's contract."

You hear them again, not as conviction but as action. The syllables are a hinge. They close a door and open another at once.

Iris's expression does not soften; she does not need to. The policy machine inside her wakes. Her hand closes briefly over your forearm — the barest pressure, public and efficient, as if to mark the transfer of momentum. You do not flinch. You are still moving in the same forward line you always walked: toward protection, toward less immediate loss.

[Sound: Chairs scrape; the sound of files being stamped]
Iris: "We'll start this week. Heavy equipment, contractors on-site. Insurance notified. Temporary walkways. No one will be left floating while we argue."

You: "Make sure community input is logged. I want regular briefings at the hall. No surprises."

Iris's jaw tightens by half a degree and she nods — agreement, not concession.

Iris: "Fine. Regular briefings. But we move fast. The sea doesn't give us the luxury of process."

Her voice is a command and a calendar, and the room leans into the schedule like people into a lifeboat.

[Scene: Green Prototype Site / Shoreline Construction | Morning — The First Day]
[Visual: Excavators, modular concrete panels stacked like gray teeth; crews in orange vests moving with rehearsed urgency. Salt spray mists the air; puddles reflect steel and sky.]
[Music: High-tempo strings, staccato rhythms]
[Sound: Engine thrum, shouted coordinates, metal-on-metal clinks]

You stand at the edge of the site with the compass heavy at your throat, watching crews roll out a berm that smells of wet concrete and diesel. A ribbon of orange safety tape snaps in the wind. The work is loud in ways that have nothing to do with sound — it bangs against the idea of your town as you knew it.

Elias is there, not at the podium but at the edge of the action, fingers stained, knees pressed into the mud as he reads a small tablet and adjusts a sensor tucked under a temporary canopy. His camera hangs at his hip like a quiet witness. When he looks up, you catch the sympathetic crease in his eyes and the set of his mouth that says: I don't like being sidelined.

You walk toward him. The boots beneath you sink in a wet stew of sand and peat; the air tastes bright with salt and something sharp — chemical, newly turned earth.

Elias: "They've got the base laid out the way Iris wanted. Fast. Efficient. It will keep the hit off the levees for this storm season."

You: "But at what cost? The coves—"

Elias (cutting gently): "—will be shaped, yes. Some moorings will move. I've been told to prioritize line-of-sight access for emergency craft. It's a balance."

You: (quiet) "Sometimes 'balance' reads as 'we take this, you take that.'"

Elias's hands find the sensor and tweak a calibration wheel with careful, practiced movements. He always comes back to the work because numbers don't sting the way politics does.

Elias: "I'll keep monitoring. If there are ways to mitigate the access points while keeping structural integrity, I'll find them. But the grant won't wait."

You: "And the community? Do they get to say what's mitigated?"

Elias: (a tired half-smile) "As much as Iris allows. You know how her funding timelines compress the room for negotiation."

You both fall silent, listening to the machinery. The relief around you is tangible: a kid races past on elevated planks, laughing; a woman in a raincoat pushes a stroller along the newly raised walk. A mother calls to her child — life reasserting itself in small, concrete ways. Those moments press against you like proof that safety matters.

[Scene: Cliffside Promenade | Afternoon — A Walk After Lunch]
[Visual: Wind-twisted grasses; the promenade overlooks filled-in coves where nets lie awkward and higher against new stone. Abuela Rosa waits at the bench; her braid is a thin white rope against a weathered coat.]
[Music: Sparse piano with increasing tempo]
[Sound: Far-off construction thump; gulls arguing overhead]

Abuela is smaller than the seriousness of what she says; she remains a woman with soil in her fingernails and a memory that outlasts contracts. Her eyes measure the new concrete like someone tasting a changed soup.

Abuela Rosa: (soft, not accusatory) "They have given you safe ground, niña. Not everyone sees the math of safety, but they see their children walking without fear. That is a generous thing."

You sit beside her, the bench cold under you. Your compass swings slightly when you breathe.

You: "Generous but costly. Covina's mooring is... half gone. Mateo told me he'd have to move his boat and sleep near it instead of at home."

Abuela Rosa: (fingers finding your hand) "The sea remembers. The sea remembers what we trade for comfort. Concrete holds back a tide, but it also remembers where salt used to take root."

You: "Do you think—"

Abuela Rosa: (interjecting with that slow clarity only the old have) "I think you did what you thought right. That is all any of us can do. But know this — to make a home you must also make room for loss. We pay for everything."

Her voice is not condemnation; it is ledger and weather-beaten ledger. It folds you into a lineage of people who traded, rebuilt, and learned to tend scars. You taste the metal at the back of your tongue, the compass a weight at the center of a life measured in decisions.

Her eyes fall on the filled cove below. A boy is securing a net, his hands abrupt with new work. The early gains are already knitting new rhythms into the town. The relief that once seemed impossible now moves through bodies and schedules: school resumes on elevated walkways, the volunteer night watches shrink to whispering shifts.

[Scene: Council Hall / Community Meeting | Evening — First Public Forum Since Construction Began]
[Visual: The hall is fuller than the morning — faces lined with daylight, placards pressed into laps. A projector throws diagrams of the new berm across a gray wall. Investors sit with polite expressions; fishermen's wives clutch thermoses.]
[Music: Intensifying percussion with brass undertones; heartbeat-like]
[Sound: A cacophony of voices; a child's muffled cry in the back]

Iris presides from the podium, immaculate despite the day's glare. She speaks in procedural sentences; each is a stone with plans attached.

Iris: "We prioritized immediate shelter and access. We understand some traditional uses will shift. We've set up a committee to address mooring relocation and artisan quotas."

A man stands near the back, voice raw in the charged air.

Mateo: "A committee after the fact is not the same as a seat at the table before a shovel hits ground."

The room responds with a murmur that rises like a tide. You feel the vibration in your sternum — these are your people, and their voices are threaded with the same loyalty and anger that made you speak this morning.

You rise, because you were the one who tipped the balance and because silence would be a kind of desertion.

You: "I backed the contract because I believed stabilized homes would save lives tonight. I still believe that. But I hear you — I see the cost in your faces."

A ripple of something — gratitude, skepticism — moves through the crowd.

Mateo: (turning to you, braced) "Seeing children in school is one thing. Seeing our boats taken is another. Lead by telling us how you'll return what the town lost."

You: "We will set priorities. I will push for compensation and proper relocation. I will make sure the committee includes fishers, artisans — people who'll have to change their work."

It is not enough for some. A woman near the front shakes her head.

Woman: "You speak like a plan can stitch water back in places we've already lost."

A low shouting starts. Voices overlap: anger at displacement, relief for shelter, suspicion of investors, a demand for accountability.

Elias, at the side, calls out quietly, not trying to inflame but to bridge: "There are technical mitigations — adjustable moorings, stepped access points. If we pool local knowledge with engineering, we can limit the impact."

Iris: (coolly) "Investors require efficient roll-out. We will not redesign every panel mid-construction. But we will consider retrofits."

The room erupts again — less coordinated now, more like a storm battering a shoreline. You are both the cause and the mediator. The hum of high arousal rushes through you: the speed of construction, the quickness of comfort returned, the sudden and sharp insistence of those left with less.

Abuela Rosa's voice, when it rises, cuts through with a brittle clarity that is almost audible.

Abuela Rosa: "We welcome safety. But remember the sea remembers our bargains. Do not let speed make our children forget how the coast was lived on before concrete told it how to be."

Everyone stills, shoulder blades loosening like people caught mid-stride. The room hangs on the cadence of her words. They are not an accusation — they are an anchor.

You feel the compass warm against skin already damp with the night's press. The hall is a pressure chamber of claims, a place where the town's shape will be argued in the messy grammar of need and loss.

Your chest tightens not with regret or triumph but with the clarified fact of consequence. The project moves forward. The town steadies. And at the same time, threads are pulled loose: moorings, livelihoods, rituals.
