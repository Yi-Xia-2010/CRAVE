[Scene: Community Kitchen | Morning]
[Visual: Long shot of a weathered kitchen inside the community center; steam rises from a row of enamel pots. Light through salt-streaked windows is thin and yellowed.]
[Sound: Quiet clatter of spoons, low chatter, a distant gull]

You are up before the town. The pots have been warmed through once already; the stew smells of onion and dried fish, warmed into something steadier than hunger. Sofía moves between tables with a stack of flyers in one hand and her skateboard tucked under the other arm; she laughs at a joke you do not hear and then glances at you, as if to check that you are still there.

Mateo's steady hands ladle bowls at the counter. Abuela Rosa sits by the window with a stitched shawl over her knees, knitting something small and blue. Her eyes catch yours and hold — not a question, only the steady patience that has always held this town together when storms have tried to loosen its seams.

Elias is here, too, leaning against a cold metal table with a tablet open on his knee. He is quieter now than he used to be in the labs; his overalls are clean but the sleeves are rolled with the careful, habitual tidiness of someone who is trying to make order out of flux. You can tell he has been sleeping in odd bursts; the circles under his eyes trace the map of late meetings and early site visits.

Mara Serrano: "Morning. We should be on the second batch soon. Sofía— did you hand the retraining signup to Don Luis?"

Sofía: "I did. He says he'd rather mend nets than run the new engines, but he'll give the classes a try for the coop. He misses the boat, Abuela."

Abuela Rosa: (softly) "People miss what fits their hands. We teach them new work with those hands, we must teach them to keep the story of the old work alive. Not to bury it."

You carry a ladle, and you feel the heat cling to your palm like an obligation you cannot set down. Outside the kitchen window, beyond the raised walkways, the town is quieter than before; the berm anchors the skyline now—low and wide, a concrete ridge dressed in pale engineered grasses. It sits like a promise and a wound: it keeps water back, but the coves where boats used to nestle are gone or narrowed, their mouths filled and rerouted.

Elias shifts when you look at him. He meets your eyes for a fraction longer than he used to, and there is something new there — an ache wrapped in calculation.

Elias Park: "We got the funding for the cooperative boats. Nima helped set up the first microgrants. I— I also drafted the mentorship schedules. If we stagger shifts, older fishers can teach younger ones and still keep some— continuity."

You nod. His voice is full of plans; he is building scaffolding around loss, which is what engineers do when they cannot stop the weather from changing maps. But he does not reach for your hand the way he used to reach for arguments in the lab or your shoulder in the Saltmarsh. He keeps a polite distance, as if the part of him that used to hold you close is set aside and armored for meetings.

Mara Serrano: "Good. Staggering makes sense. People will need the time to adjust to the engines, and to the idea that some routes won't come back."

Elias Park: (quiet, with a small, awkward smile) "I thought— maybe you'd want to— I can go over the schedules with you later. After the kitchen? You and I could walk the berm. I'm... working on a community map."

You measure his words. The invitation is small, ordinary; you feel both the tug toward it and the odd coolness between you. The town has grown around new routines—youth programs and retraining classes meet in classrooms that used to be empty; the municipal vans now ferry people along reorganized boat ramps. These are good things. They are also the proof that something else was taken.

[Scene: Berm | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the berm's top path; pale engineered grasses sway in a slow, mechanical rhythm. A family walks a dog in the distance.]
[Sound: A distant, muffled thrum of maintenance pumps; gulls, lower and less insistent]
[Music: Sustained low cello, single notes spaced like breaths]

You come up onto the berm later, the town small beneath your feet. The concrete edge is smooth and worn in places where hands have traced it out of habit. The sea beyond it is a flat, pewter sheet. Where coves used to arch in like cupped hands, you see the new line where sand was pressed and rock-filled. Boats sit in new slips—some lifted, some gone.

Sofía walks ahead, barefoot for a moment on the engineered grass, then turns to you with a slow smile that is both bright and brittle.

Sofía: "We painted signs at the school today. 'Keep the Story' — everyone put slogans on stones. The kids smuggled in old net scraps and wove them into the art wall. Dona Marta cried when she saw her son's name on a stone."

Mara Serrano: "She should. Names matter. We didn't just lose routes; we lost names from the sea."

Sofía's hands twist a little at the strap of her bag. She is younger than the change has made her, but she is not innocent. You see the way she organizes other teens into patrols for the youth skill exchange she runs, how she stands in front of a whiteboard like someone who has practiced being calm.

Abuela Rosa joins you both, leaning on a short wooden cane. She stops at the edge and looks at the water, gaze steady and unshockable.

Abuela Rosa: "They will build new stories. It does not mean the old ones are less. Stone remembers names if we speak them."

You press your palm to the railing and let the salt from the air gather on your skin. The smell is familiar, but the feeling it leaves is different now—like the memory of a song you used to sing with someone else. Your chest tightens in a small, private way.

Elias's silhouette appears further down the berm. He speaks into a handheld speaker, running through logistics for a retraining class while also stopping occasionally to listen to an older fisher's complaint. He is present and dispersed across tasks; he moves through practical kindness now as if kindness were a series of boxes to check.

You walk toward him, shoulders low. Your steps are measured, the world moving at a pace a storm would recognize as calm.

Elias Park: (when you reach him) "We rerouted the older boats to the shared slips. Don Luis will have priority shifts, and I made sure the engine class includes time on deck, not just in the shop—so people can still tell stories while they learn."

Mara Serrano: "Thank you. That matters."

Elias Park: (there is a softness in his voice, then a small, tentative humor) "Also— Sofía stole my screwdriver yesterday and left a sticker on it. I'm keeping it. You should tell her not to take other people's tools."

Sofía: (from nearby) "I left it in 'community custody,' Elias."

Elias half-smiles; the exchange is a small reclaiming of ease between them. But then he looks away, the smile fraying.

Elias Park: (lower) "I wish— I wish we'd had more time to do things the way you wanted. I thought the tech could bridge it without changing the map so much."

Mara Serrano: (you say this aloud, though it is the thing that lives in your head) "We thought different things could save everything. Sometimes saving one part changes another."

He nods, the kind of nod that is both agreement and a surrender.

No matter which small route you take, the truth remains: safety arrived, but it arrived on terms that reshaped the shore. You touch the railing and the cool metal is steady under your fingers.

Abuela Rosa: "There will be funerals for what we had and celebrations for what we keep. Both are necessary. We must sit at both tables."

Mara Serrano: "We will. We already are."

[Scene: Promenade | Dusk]
[Visual: Narrow promenade with fewer fishing chairs than before; strings of salt-streaked lights blink awake. The silhouette of the headland is a blunt shape against a low copper sky.]
[Sound: Soft footsteps on wet wood, the distant hush of a repair crew packing up]
[Music: A single sustained violin note, held and then released into a long, gentle chorus]

You come down the promenade as the light thins and the town drifts toward home. The pace is measured, resigned and steady. Some shops have new signs advertising classes or shared equipment; others board up windows differently now, with plaques thanking investors and grants. The promenade feels larger and emptier in places where the shoreline has been altered; benches sit with one side looking inland, as if the town is learning to sit differently in itself.

Iris appears at the terrace near the council room. She is expected there; her coat is the same severe cut it always is, but tonight there is a softness to the way she folds her hands—less an armor than a habit of attention paid to weather and numbers both.

Iris Varela: (when she sees you, her voice is direct but not unkind) "It's steadier now. The pumps have redundancy, and the berm passed the second round of inspections. The program targets are in the municipal ledger. Sofía's youth program got a line item."

Mara Serrano: "You did what you promised, Iris. You made it hard and you made it safe. People are alive because of it."

Iris's jaw tightens for a breath, and then she allows herself a small, private easing.

Iris Varela: "I lost someone. I promised myself I would not let this town lose for lack of decisions. I stand by the choices I made. But—" (her voice softens) "—I did not mean to take what makes Puerto Alba itself."

You hear the admission like a small bell in fog. It does not undo the changes, but it makes them human: not only a ledger entry, but a weight someone else carries.

Mara Serrano: "You didn't take it alone, Iris. It took the whole town. We all made trades."

Iris Varela: (after a pause) "Then let's make sure the trades keep a floor for people—not only shelters, but livelihoods. The retraining funds will be renewed for five years, and we will allocate extra support for families who relocate."

Her words are policy shaped into an apology. They hold practical warmth and a municipal cold; they will save lives and also mean that some sea rooms will never be the same. You think of families who set up new kitchens inland, of older nets folded in attics, of children who will learn different rhythms. The town will go on. It will be different.

Elias stands a short distance away, watching as you speak with Iris. He looks smaller in the dusk, like a figure bent over a map. When your eyes meet, he gives you a short nod—no grand gestures, only a seal of presence. He will keep working; his love has taken on the shape of service and design and program-building. It is quieter. It is not less real.

Sofía sidles close to you and slides a small painted stone into your palm. The slogan is simple and rough: "KEEP THE STORY."

Sofía: "We put one at each bench. So people will remember to say names when they sit."

You press the stone against your palm. It is cold and rough and perfect.

You let the promenade fill you with its slow losses and small salvations. You have argued in rooms with too many voices; you have slept with the taste of seawater and ink on your tongue. You have held Elias's hand, or not, depending on the day. You have watched Iris sign contracts with a steady hand and then soften at a story told over stew. You have watched Sofía steal a screwdriver and glue a sticker to it like a talisman.

There is a kind of ending happening all at once: not a singular victory, but a series of settlements that keep people breathing through nights that used to take them. The shoreline is different; the town's map is redrawn. The cost is visible in the way the tide hits the promenade and in the empty chairs on the quay.

You sit on a bench and watch a family walk by with two small suitcases—they are moving inland, carried by support promised in council minutes and municipal vans. Abuela Rosa sits beside you and finishes a row on her knitting, her fingers sure and patient.

Abuela Rosa: "We will teach them the songs for the boat, and we will teach it to the land. Children will hold both. That is enough to start."

Mara Serrano: (softly) "Is it, though? Enough?"

Abuela Rosa: "It will be enough to begin. It will be less than what we had. It will also give us years we did not have."

You think of Elias, of the Retraining board you helped draft, of Sofía's murals, of Don Luis's stubborn, laughing attempts at the new engines. You think of the town not as a single home but as a net—sometimes with holes, sometimes mended. The net will hold differently now.

Elias approaches and sits a respectful arm's length away. He looks at the water, then at you.

Elias Park: "I designed the schedule so Don Luis would get morning shifts, and the younger ones would get afternoons—so there is always someone who remembers the old routes. I put in a clause for storytelling hours on Sundays."

Mara Serrano: "You did that?"

Elias Park: (a small laugh) "You did not ask me to, but maybe I wanted to keep hearing your voice in my head when I couldn't hear you."

The admission is a small, fragile thing. You do not need grand reconciliations enacted in the dusk. You need the quiet continuations: shared schedules, stolen screwdrivers, stickered tools, and storytelling hours. Love changes into a long, sometimes patient commitment to keep pieces of someone in the margins of your work.

You stand and walk the length of the promenade with Elias and Abuela Rosa. The lights blink and the town exhales. There is a tenderness here, wrapped in compromise and municipal ledgers. It will not be triumphant. It will not be the whole of what you wanted. But people are alive, and they are taking the next steps.

You tuck the painted stone into the inner pocket of your windbreaker next to the compass. The metal is cool and familiar; the stone is rough but steady. You feel the town's loss like a bruise and its care like a warm cloth pressed over it.

This is not the end you dreamed of. The shoreline bears the marks of what was chosen. Houses found new foundations inland; coves were reshaped; some boats were retired, others remade into shared vessels. The work you fought for—social programs, retraining, youth leadership—exists now as scaffolding for lives that might have otherwise been cut short. The price is visible. The saving is real.

You breathe in the salt and smoke of a far-off street grill. You taste grief and relief braided together. The town will sleep safer tonight. The map is altered forever.

You close your eyes and remember the sea's old names and the new plans written on municipal forms. You remember Abuela Rosa's certainty that names carry across changes, and Sofía's painted stone that insists on keeping the story. You remember Elias's small admissions and Iris's hard bargains and the families who will wake tomorrow and make coffee in new kitchens and teach their children both old songs and new work.

You stand, steady, the night wrapping around the town like a blanket that is both thin and necessary.
