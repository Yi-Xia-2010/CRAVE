Mateo grips your elbow and laughs, a small, raw sound. "Look at that," he says. "It held."

Elias: "We did the modeling well. We can refine the weak points."

You nod, but your nod is not certain. The retrofits scheduled for later windows — the ones that require additional materials and time when the sea is calmer — sit on a timeline that will be tested not by this storm, but by whatever comes next.

The cyclone forms like a bruise on a map no one wanted to read. Warnings sped through, but the storm's surge is not a thing your staged timelines had fully accounted for. Subtropical, sudden, it found the places you had pushed back into the future.

You arrive at first light to a shoreline that looks like an amputated limb: a section of the promenade, the old fishermen's stand, the corner where children used to string kites — gone. Machinery lies half-buried. A strip of sea has moved inland and taken with it the memory of places.

Mateo is on his feet, hands caked with salt and mud, his face a map of exhaustion and pain. He is not the only injured, but his name moves through the crowd like a curse. He was out helping when the breach widened.

You find Elias by the collapsed bench, dirt under his nails, his face ashen. He looks at you not with reproach but with an expression that contains every question you have been threading around in meetings. It is a look that is softer than betrayal and harder than consolation.

You: "We did the thing we could agree to, and it wasn't enough."

There is no raised voice, no dramatic confrontation — only the slow, terrible acknowledgment that your compromise bought a later, different pain. You had both thought the rotation and conditional funding were enough to prevent the worst; instead, they delayed parts of keeping the shore whole.
