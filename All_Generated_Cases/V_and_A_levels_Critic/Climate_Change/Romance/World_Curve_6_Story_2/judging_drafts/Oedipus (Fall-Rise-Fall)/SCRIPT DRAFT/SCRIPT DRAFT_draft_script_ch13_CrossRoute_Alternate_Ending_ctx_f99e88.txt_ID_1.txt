[Scene: Coastline — Weeks Later, Unseasonal Evening]
[Visual: Low, bruised sky; a horizon that looks like an old wound; gulls alarmed into crooked shapes]

The unexpected surge comes like a secret the ocean held too long. A subtropical cyclone rides a warm tongue of current and arrives at dusk with a slow, relentless force the models did not fully predict. The retrofit schedule that saved you in the last storm shows its seams: the conditional scaling stuttered in the bureaucracy; some retrofits waited for the official rotation to grant them primacy. Work that was supposed to be continuous had pockets of pause so the rotating oversight could review—time for consensus where the sea demanded immediacy.

You watch water find a weakness the sensors missed. For a long time you simply watch, because watching is the only work you can do when the wind has taken the language of action out of the air.

A wave pushes through an undercut—where a restored marsh wasn't finished, a section of shoreline simply gives. A strip of the boardwalk, beloved and painted in the bright, clumsy way of small towns, slides like a card off a table. You hear a sound that is not the sea: a human voice, a crash, then a small, awful silence.

Mateo's name is shouted in a dozen tones at once. You move as if muscle remembers faster than thought. The crowd is a smear of wet coats and bright tape; someone is shouting orders, someone else praying. Mateo is there, tangled in ropes, shock and salt making his face look both foreign and familiar. He is coughing, a shallow, dangerous sound.

You: "Mateo!" (the name spills out like a breaking line)

He looks at you. His eyes are large and wet, and for an instant you see every unsaid thing between you reflected in the water watching him. You latch on to his shoulder and the storm seems to catch its breath.

Elias is beside you, and you feel his hand close around yours — a solid, clenching thing that says: we're going to get through this. But the hand also trembles. He looks at Mateo, then at the collapsing shoreline, then at you with a question lodged in him: did we do enough? Did we choose well?

In the hours afterward, the tally comes in measured and awful. The structure held in places it needed to but failed where the schedule had left work waiting. Part of the shoreline is gone — a small, intimate sweep of land with a playground and Abuela Rosa's old bench, pieces that cannot be replaced because memory is not an engineered thing. Mateo survives, but not unscathed: broken ribs, a long recovery, and a face that will carry that night like a map.

The town stitches again, but something has been taken that paperwork cannot itemize. The confidence you bought with conditional promises has eroded. The investors make statements that read like apologies but feel like insurance claims. The rotating council convenes with solemn faces and a vocabulary of regret.

You sit at the table across from Iris. Her hands rest on the lit pen that once signed the rotation agreement. She looks older. Her voice is low when she speaks.

Iris: "We made choices. We balanced... we meant to keep as much as possible safe."

You: (the words are heavier than anything you've said in years) "We meant to. But 'meant' is not the same as 'kept.'"

Elias's jaw is tight. He looks at the photograph on the table and doesn't look away. Elias: "We tied oversight to consensus. The pause cost us. We underestimated the friction it would introduce."

You: "We also prevented wholesale takeover. If we'd let them in—"

Iris's gaze is unreadable for a breath. Iris: "If we'd handed the shoreline to the fastest bidder, you and I would not be here counting ruins. But we still lost things that matter. None of that gives us comfort."

Silence folds over the chamber like damp wool. Around you, the town's faces gather in different alleys of shock: some furious, some numb, some already tucking the day's grief into a toolbelt of practicalities. You feel the soft press of guilt — not just for the land, but for the small ways you let the conversation soften while the work waited.

Elias reaches across the table. His hand finds yours and holds it as if for warmth. The contact is both solace and accusation, a living line that says we are still connected but riven.

Elias: (quiet) "I'm sorry. For the delays. For the things I didn't fight loud enough for."

You: (the apology is a small stone you drop into a dark pool) "I wanted to believe process could fix how hurt we are. I wanted to believe we could have both justice and speed. We couldn't make the tide wait."

He presses his thumb to the inside of your wrist, a small, intimate map written in muscle.

You stand later on the ruined stretch of shoreline. The bench that once sat under a leaning lantern is a pile of salt-finished wood. The ledger is cruelly balanced.

Elias stays near you the whole afternoon but does not offer explanations that fix anything. You both watch the slow work of clearing and remember the nights when you thought a rotating council could be an answer. The memory tastes like metal.

You fold your hands around your pendant until you can feel the cold brass. You think of every night you spent listening to the tide and every negotiation where you softened a sentence so more people would agree. You think of the things left unsaid to Elias — the fear you didn't voice when it mattered, the blame you took home alone.

A gull circles once and then leaves. The sea is unrepentant.

You do not expect catharsis. The endings you once read as a child promised neat bows and restored places. Life is quieter now, a long, persistent work. You will stay. You will help. You will keep arguing for the marshes and hold the line where you can. You close your notebook. The last line you write is not a triumph. It is a promise, quieter than you wanted it to be and truer than the loud speeches could ever be: we will rebuild what we can, and we will remember what we lost.
