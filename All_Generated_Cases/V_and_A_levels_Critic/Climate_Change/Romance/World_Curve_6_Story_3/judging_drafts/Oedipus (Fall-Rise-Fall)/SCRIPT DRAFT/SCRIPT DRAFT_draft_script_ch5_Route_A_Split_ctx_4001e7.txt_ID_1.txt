[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk & Docks | Golden Hour]
[Visual: Wide shot of the repaired planks, lamplight catching salt-slick wood]
[Music: Warm strings undercut by a quickening percussive pattern — hopeful, taut]
[Sound: Gull calls, a distant motor, the soft slap of water against pilings]

You walk the boardwalk with Jonah beside you, the repaired boards sighing familiar and honest beneath your boots. Weeks of work have made a new cadence here: toes that knew every loose nail now find steady footing; patched nets dry where they used to rot; the pilot berms you helped measure have taken on water and held it where foundations once learned to drown. Your notebook, heavy with tide logs and a dozen tiny handwritten thank-yous, rests against your hip like ballast.

The salt smell is more than scent tonight — it’s a layered memory: tar, old rope, the oily sweetness of kelp, and something new under all of it, like soil that has been given back a chance. Jonah’s shadow leans across yours as the sun angles low. His voice folds into yours without needing permission.

Jonah: "Look at this place. You did this, Mira. We—" (he presses a hand flat to a section of rebuilt railing) "—we made it mean something again."

You: "We did. Not me alone." (you say it softly, because the town is in his hands as much as yours)

Jonah's laugh is short and sure. He nudges you with his elbow; a bruise of warmth blooms where he touches. The light catches the laugh lines by his eyes and throws them amber.

Tessa, perched on the railing with her feet swinging, cocks an eyebrow and calls out before you can hide the way your cheeks heat.

Tessa: "Scientist by day, love interest by sunset. How's the double life treating you, sis?"

You feel the heat rising — not from shame but from an odd, bright happiness that prickles at the edges of ash-deep guilt. It’s a dangerous, lovely thing.

You walk toward the Research Lighthouse because that's where the maps and models live, where Saira's reports glow on screens and where the town's momentum collects in stacks of paper. Jonah falls into step with you. You both move quicker now — there’s energy tucked into the space between each stride, an undercurrent that makes your lungs ache with expectation.

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Dusk]
[Visual: Interior lamps hum warm; maps pinned across corkboards; rain begins as a scatter at the windows]
[Music: String ostinato accelerates; a low brass tremor adds urgency]
[Sound: Rain pattering, the distant rumble of a storm watch, instruments humming]

Dr. Saira stands over a tabletop model. Her finger traces a line on a printout and the room leans in with her; even the machines seem to be listening. When she looks up her eyes hold that soft intensity she uses to make complex things feel like promises you can keep.

Dr. Saira: "The berms have reduced peak surge by nearly thirty percent in the central inlet. The sensors show dampening across three tide cycles. That's statistically significant for pilot-scale work."

You: "Thirty percent." (you repeat it, letting the number translate into things your chest understands — rooftops, memories, the ache that lives under your ribs) "Saira, can we be sure it'll scale without negative trade-offs?"

Dr. Saira: (measured) "There's more to model, of course. Migration pathways for fish, sediment redistribution — but early indicators are solid. With more hands and some adaptive management, it's viable."

Jonah: "More hands means the town. It means folks who grew up here. Not just contractors with color-coded vests."

Cassian steps in from the doorway, dripping slightly from the spitting rain. His trench coat has the kind of neatness that feels like a promise and a binding at once. The tablet under his arm glows with permit-ready renderings; his steel-gray eyes are bright, pleading even, in a way that makes your shoulder blades tighten.

Cassian: "You're right to be cautious. That's why we want to do this with you — with the town — on a larger scale. My firm can unlock a grant that covers materials and logistics for a line of berms and hybrid defenses along this coast."

Mayor Lynette: (leaning forward, voice smooth) "Visible wins. Media buys public trust. We can't gamble on 'maybe' when the next election cycle is two summers away."

You feel the room's temperature rise. The rain against the lighthouse roof becomes percussion — harder, faster. The instruments chirp; Saira's tablet pings with new data. Words pile up like surf against a wall.

Jonah: (sharp) "And what's the catch? Centralized management? What's to stop them from deciding the plan without us when the money runs low?"

Cassian: (quick, earnest) "The agreement includes community representation. Adaptive governance. We can draft oversight clauses—"

Jonah: (cutting in) "Can you stop saying that? If someone's paying the bills, they get the button. They get the ribbon-cutting and the control."

Mayor Lynette: (calm as glass) "We need an implementation partner who can stand behind the work and the publicity. The town needs to look secure to attract more support."

Tessa: (arms crossed) "So the town looks secure, and then we give our whole island to a boardroom?"

Your notebook is open on your knee; the pages feather as you turn them. You trace data points with a fingertip, but your mind keeps returning to other traces: the way Old Man Rafi tied a blue bead into his beard the year the north pier collapsed; the way your father's hands trembled when he tried to patch the boat. The survivor guilt is a persistent tideline under everything — not loud, but present, shaping the shore of every decision you make.

Before anyone can speak again a storm-warning tone rings through the lighthouse — high, urgent, the sound of weather punching at the door. Rain moves from scatter to line; a sudden wind rakes the coast and the waves answer with a hard, metallic roll. The room tightens; conversations snap into shorter breaths.

[Sound: Loud storm-warning tone; rain intensifies into a steady, insistent hammer]
[Music: Strings spike, then sustain, echoing the alarm — very high intensity]

You feel the world tilt toward what matters and what must be decided. All the previous weeks of planning condense into a single now: protect what you've rebuilt, choose the scale that will carry it forward, or force the system open and risk tearing trust in the process.

Cassian takes a step closer, his voice softer, the corporate polish replaced by something smaller.

Cassian: (quiet) "Mira. I can get the grant. But I don't want to be the kind of person who takes over a town. I want to help it stay itself. Let me prove that."

You study the fade of rain across his coat, the earnest set to his mouth. You think of his maps — clean, tidy, beautifully possible — and of his hunger to be trusted. There's something fragile in the way he asks; it presses against a part of you that wants to say yes simply to spare everyone the immediate danger.

Jonah watches him, jaw tight. You can tell, in the way Jonah keeps his hands fidgeting with the rope-and-shell bracelet at his wrist, that this is a pivot point for him too. For you both. For the town.

Jonah: (low) "Mira. Whatever you choose, you know I'm with you. But I'm asking you to be honest about what 'with you' means."

You reach up without meaning to, and Jonah brushes a wind-tossed strand of navy-blue hair behind your ear. His touch is a direct thing — decisive, deliberate. The lantern's glow catches the wet edge of the strand and turns it to a ribbon. For a heartbeat you are not planning or calculating; you are only aware of heat, of the small, precise proof of the present.

You want to hold the town and hold Jonah and hold the future all at once. The ache is so bright it makes the rain feel like confetti.

Dr. Saira breaks a silence that was beginning to feel like a hold-your-breath moment.

Dr. Saira: "We can scale cautiously. We can write oversight into any agreement. We can build training programs so maintenance stays local. But none of that happens without capital. Right now, capital is the lever."

Mayor Lynette: (steepling fingers) "Then leverage it. Use it to bind the town's name to the safety you all deserve."

Tessa: "Or we bind ourselves to a promise we didn't get to write."

The lighthouse hums with the energy of ten different wills leaning at once. Outside the rain drums a staccato on the metal balcony; inside, your heartbeat accelerates to match it. Your survivor guilt twists hope into a question edge: choose wrong and you might lose more; choose right and you might save everything you managed to make whole.

You feel all the stakes like a physical pressure, like a tide cresting and waiting for your signal to break or recede. Love presses against policy — warm, insistent, and unignorable. The town's mouths are near you; their trust is a living thing that expects an answer.

Your notebook's pages flutter. You write nothing and everything at once: lists of promises, sketches of a covenant, fragments of legal text crossed with the word 'community.' The decision closes around you like a door that must open to one path and not the others.

[Music: A held chord that trembles, then tightens to a single, driving pulse]

The storm warning wails again, a final punctuation. Outside, the world is insisting on movement. Inside, people are waiting for you to tell them where you will stand.
