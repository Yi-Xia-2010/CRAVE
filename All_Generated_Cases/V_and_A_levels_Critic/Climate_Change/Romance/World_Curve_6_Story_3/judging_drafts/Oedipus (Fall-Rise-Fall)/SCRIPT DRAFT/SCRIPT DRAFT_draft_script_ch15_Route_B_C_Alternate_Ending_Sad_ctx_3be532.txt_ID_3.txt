[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk & Docks | Dawn — After the Surge]
[Music: Jagged strings; distant thunder underscoring frantic percussion]
[Visual: Wide shot of the harbor — splintered pilings, a half-built seawall like broken teeth; gray light through a wet sky]
[Sound: The slow groan of shifting wood, the slap of debris against hulls, voices threaded with a raw edge]

You come up the path with your boots slick from spray and mud. The air hits like a bruise — warm, fetid, and full of the oily perfume of upturned earth. Salt and diesel, the metallic tang of broken pipes, and the copper-thin reek of burned siding curl in the back of your throat. The coral pendant at your throat knocks once against your sternum and you feel the small clink of metal inside your ribs.

The pumps that were supposed to buy the town time are a forest of dead machinery. Where contracts sat in folders and lawyers argued, the seawater found its loopholes and poured through. A tray of the pump array lies twisted like a ribcage, cables fraying into the water. Men in high-viz vests and plastic ponchos push at a stranded barge with ropes that sing. Someone is hollering for tools that are tied up in courtrooms and suits; other hands are pulling on soaked furniture and children wrapped in blankets.

You know the line that will be drawn in the papers — "unforeseeable anomaly" — and you also know the truth of the warm pocket the weather maps tried to explain away. You know the names of the clauses that slowed crews, and you know the faces of the people who waited while the town needed speed.

[Visual: Close on a breached segment of the mid-build seawall; rebar like cartons of ribs split by the tide]
[Sound: The hollow clink of metal; a baby wailing far off]

Jonah stands near a ladder lying half-submerged, his sweater sodden and salt-stiff. He looks like he hasn't slept in three nights; the laugh lines around his eyes are gone, replaced by a raw set to his jaw. He sees you and the look in his face is something that wasn't there before — something like a breaking.

Jonah Reyes: "You were always so careful with words, Mira. You found the right words to stall them. You found the right words to make them wait."

You taste copper and the word for it refuses the shape of argument. Your chest tightens. Carefully. That word has been a shield and a blade.

You: "Jonah — I didn't want this. I wanted oversight. I wanted the clauses Dr. Saira suggested."

Jonah Reyes: (voice rising, then swallowing) "Oversight when a bulldozer could have been here. Oversight when the pumps could've been running. 'Wanted' doesn't fill houses."

You open your mouth and close it again. You remember the meetings — the calm pressure of Council chambers, Cass's measured assurances, the mayor's practical eyes. You remember signing an addendum that you thought would protect people, thinking legal scaffolding would keep everything honest. You remember the headlines that read like cautious weather reports while the ocean decided on a different grammar.

Cass comes up from the docks with a tablet tucked under his arm; his trench coat is rimmed in salt. He moves with that same economy of motion you've watched since he arrived: precise, public-facing, every gesture a slide across management's polished deck. He stops two paces from Jonah, two paces from you, and somehow his expression is all of the negotiations at once: apology, defense, calculation.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (measured, tired) "Mira pushed for transparency. She wanted the eco-triggers in the contract. She wanted reviews, not shortcuts."

Jonah Reyes: (spitting) "You wanted funds to move now. You wanted machines — not promises on paper."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (cold) "And when the injunctions hit, the crew contracts froze. Because evidence had to be reassessed. Because you and your allies pushed for oversight. None of that made the water stop."

The sentence hangs like a line thrown and missed.

You: (half to yourself) "All the paths carried weight. We thought we were buying time to choose wisely."

Jonah Reyes: "Time is what we didn't have, Mira."

His words are a stone. The harbor answers with a sound like a low wave folding skyward.

[Visual: Medium shot — a cluster of neighbors hauling a sodden mattress, a child perched on an upturned crate wrapped in a bright blanket]
[Sound: Shouting for names, the cough of small engines, a dog barking in the distance]

An aide from Mayor Lynette's office hurries by with a stack of wet printouts and a phone plastered to his ear. The mayor's name is spoken like a litany by two separate groups of workers: some swear at her for slow decisions, others defend the need to run things through legal channels. The municipal office is split — half the staff saying the mayor was hedging the town's liabilities, half saying she was shouted into speed without guarantee.

Mayor Lynette Cole: (voice crackling faintly over someone's handheld) "We followed counsel. We needed documented sign-off. Do you understand the liabilities if we skip protocol?"

Worker: (shouting back) "We needed defenses in place before the water read the rules!"

The argument ricochets like shrapnel. The practicalities of governance, the sterile architecture of law and contracts — they mean nothing to someone pulling a child's toys from a ruin they once called home. They mean everything to insurers and councils and the people who must defend their decisions months from now.

You choose one and a dozen small consequences spiral; the decision only changes which muscle remembers the ache. The town is an organism being born with broken bones.

Old Man Rafi sits at the edge of a small pier with a shallow metal basin between his knees. He has worked a dozen hands into tinder, and now the little private fire is a stubborn, honest thing — smoke that smells of tar and old rope. The people around him give the fire space as if it were a little altar.

Old Man Rafi: (soft, graven) "The sea takes, Mira. It always has. We buy back minutes and we trade them for other minutes. It is not only the storms that take; it's our fear of being taken that makes us give up the wrong things."

You have no answer for him; you have only the ledger in your head, the name of plans, the signatures, the redlined clauses.

You: "What else do we give, Rafi?"

Old Man Rafi: (with a small, bitter smile) "What else? The things that are easiest to trade — the songs, the routes, the ways that don't look like money. The things anyone new could replace with a machine."

Tessa comes up behind him, mud on her knees, anger like a flare in her voice.

Tessa Kestrel: "You sat in meetings while people lost work, Ma. You and the lawyers—"

You: (cutting) "I tried to make the work belong to us. I tried to keep jobs local. I thought—"

Tessa Kestrel: (bitter laugh) "You thought enough to make paper. Paper doesn't fix a house."

Jonah's hands curl into fists. Even his apologies are Sharp. Even his grief is accusatory.

Jonah Reyes: "I thought we'd do this together. I thought we'd choose what to save. Now the choice is a list of things we already lost."

The accusation lands as if it were the tide itself — inevitable, unstoppable. You reach out and catch Jonah's sleeve for a second; your fingers press into wet wool. He flinches, then steadies himself.

Jonah Reyes: (quieter) "You made me hear lawyers when I needed a hammer."

Cass watches, jaw tightening. He steps forward because he always steps forward in moments that need a hand on the wheel.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (trying for pragmatism that isn't there) "We can rebuild sections. We can reassign crews. There’s a chain of procurement—"

Jonah Reyes: (laughing without humor) "Procurement won't get my father's net back into the water."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (stung) "And if we had thrown men and machines into the wrong places, we'd have spent money and left habitats dead where they could've survived."

You: "Both of you are right and both of you are wrong."

Silence bows for a re-beat and then collapses. People are shouting farther down, voices congeal into a single human noise that could be a storm.

The day fractures into small salvations. People scream at each other; old alliances drown in the same surge that took the pier. Ladders are shared, food is passed, and someone — always someone — refuses to hold blame as the only answer.

[Visual: Close-up on your weathered notebook; pages smeared with saltwater and illegible ink]
[Sound: The page flaps once in the wind]

You open the notebook. Spreads of sketches, lists of clauses, tide logs, the tiny inked heart you drew once and told yourself was for future steadiness. You run a thumb over a margin note about ecological triggers — Dr. Saira's handwriting circled and underlined — and then you close it. The movement is slow and deliberate.

Dr. Saira's plan was to force corrections into the process — automatic reviews, habitat metrics triggering funding releases. You thought that would protect the marsh and the crews and give the town a mechanism to compel action. Instead, it tied money to data and delayed boots on the deck when the data needed time to collect. The lawyers queued the town's fate like evidence.

Old Man Rafi watches you from across the cove. He pokes the fire and watches an ember die.

Old Man Rafi: "Inertia can be a decision as much as any vote. Waiting holds its cost."

You: (whispering, to the wind) "I thought I could design a better waiting."

Jonah's fists unclench and then clench again. He turns away and disappears into a group hauling a ruined chest of drawers. Cass lingers, watching the faces he tried to serve with graphs and schedules. Mayor Lynette's spokesperson moves through the crowd, a face drawn tight with the need to explain and the shame of being unable to make the ocean listen to law.

[Sound: Rain starts again — not a storm yet, just a thin, relentless weeping on the boards]
[Music: Low, unrelenting piano under a cello that grows heavier]

You walk the line of wreckage. You map loss in your head the way you've mapped tides for years: house by house, boat by boat, whatever the sea has taken. Families line up at the community tent with numbers on clipboard lists; some faces are stunned and blank; some shake with the ineffable scream of someone who has watched a life unglue itself.

There is no neat romantic closure here. There are no tidy reconciliations. Jonah's hand brushes yours once — a hesitant contact that is more a vestige than a promise. Cass offers you a look that is equal parts apology and a plea to keep trying at the plans he believes in. The mayor sends a curt nod — a political mercy. Tessa stands with mud to her knees and refuses to meet your eyes.

Tessa Kestrel: (very small) "People need work. People need the docks that are still there. The kids need to see us try."

You: "I know."

Tessa Kestrel: (with more venom than she intends) "You don't know. Not everything."

The accusation is sharp and true. Guilt is an anatomy lesson.

Old Man Rafi stirs the fire and hands you a small strip of driftwood. He doesn't look at you when he speaks.

Old Man Rafi: "We decide, Mira. Embrace the hard counting. Keep the names in your mouth. If you keep naming, you keep something of the people alive."

You feel the small wood in your fingers as if it were the weight of the town. You press it to your palm and let the smoke curl under your nose. It tastes like loss.

You close your notebook again. The cover thumps against your ribs, as if to say that a life lived in preparation will not always save the present. You press the book to your chest and let the weight settle — a pressure that is almost physical, like concrete stacked on lungs.

[Visual: Wide shot as the sun breaks a hairline through the clouds; the harbor looks like a wound with stitches half gone]
[Music: Single cello note held and then allowed to fray into silence]

You stand on the docks and watch people you know find ways to survive the next tide. Some hands will rebuild piers; others will leave for the city. There will be inquiries and lawsuits and articles that parse the fault into neat clauses. There will be graves of small things — the bakery that closed, the nets that will never mend the way they used to, a boy's first catch swallowed by a mouthless sea.

You think of the ledger of choices you have kept for yourself: the signatures, the pauses, the clauses you thought would save people and instead postponed the only action that could have mattered in time. The calculus of survival feels raw and small and enormous all at once.

A gull wheels above the harbor and then dives. The town goes on making noise — grief is a job now as much as a feeling. You breathe and the salt in your lungs tastes like apology.

You fold your hands around the notebook one last time. The coral pendant touches the page through your shirt and you feel its familiar edge. You let yourself finally, no longer performing, allow a sound to break from you. It is not a word and it is not a vow; it is a surrender to the fact that not all harm can be undone.

[Visual: Close-up on your face, saltwater and tears indistinguishable]
[Sound: Rain, steady now; the low murmur of many voices; someone singing a small sea song — incomplete and beautiful]

You close the notebook, and the town keeps its tally: who we lost, who will stay, who will leave. The choice of what to give next is not made in the suddenness of a meeting but in the quiet of people rinsing debris from a child's toys and deciding what they can carry forward. There is no tidy send-off for love or for policy. There is a slow, hard sifting.

[Visual: The fire at Old Man Rafi's little circle burns low; smoke threads into the gray]
[Music: A single, low, resolving chord that does not feel like relief]

You walk away from the boards finally, boots leaving prints that will be filled and erased by tides and by time. Your chest is heavy with all the things you tried and all the things you deferred. You have not been absolved by law or by storm. You have only the knowledge that every path cost something, and the cost is now visible in the doors that hang crooked and the people who will have to re-learn how to ask for help.

You close your eyes for a moment and let the rain wash the ink from your hands. The sound of the town is a litany of names; you repeat a few in your head like a prayer — Tessa, Old Man Rafi, Jonah, the children at the docks. Each name is an ember and you do not know which will survive.

[Music: Slow piano, one last note that is more a wound than a resolution]
