You: "Fifteen percent and climbing?"

Her confirmation hits like sunlight through cloud. You let out a breath that tastes of brine and half-remembered relief, and the ache that used to sit behind your ribs loosens—just enough to let something lighter in: pride, yes, but sharper—a responsibility that feels alive.

The repaired boardwalk creaks in a rhythm you recognize now: the sound of community labor settling into place. Old Man Rafi passes a jar of coffee to a volunteer; his eyes are bright, proud. People carry tools like instruments, each movement part of a score you've been rehearsing together.

He looks smaller than he did in the town hall—less the polished advocate, more a man caught between two tides. His steel-gray eyes are a directness so practiced it can feel like a plea.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (quiet) "Mira. Jonah."

You: "Cass."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (holding up his tablet like an offering) "We have a formal grant on the table. Enough to scale the berms across three sectors of coastline. The firm will fund materials and labor for a ramped schedule. They'll front the risk in the first year."

Jonah's jaw tightens as if the wind had pushed it. You can feel the temperature of the conversation fall, then spike.

Jonah Reyes: (flat) "Front the risk until the bill comes due. Who pays when it does? Who decides where your models get deployed and who gets left out?"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (earnest, a danger in his calm) "We want community seats at the governance table. We can make oversight binding—legal instruments. Not 'us' imposing, but 'us' enabling. I know the optics look corporate. I know funding equals strings. Let me show you the draft."

Your teeth press into your lip. There is a plea in Cass's tone you keep wanting to translate into something else—genuine alliance or clever policy framing. Either way, the grant could—no, would—change the pace. Faster scale, greater reach. Risk: centralization, loss of local autonomy. Reward: more coastline protected, livelihoods saved in the near term.

Mayor Lynette appears at the edge of the crowd, phone in hand, face measured like a ledger.

Mayor Lynette Cole: (businesslike) "This is a media moment. We can get coverage that drives more funding. People will see action. They want to believe we're doing something big, Mira."

You (internal): The town wants tangible assurance—lamps lit, ceremonies, ribbon-cuttings. Quick wins soothe nerves. But quick wins can also sweep away the granular care that made this pilot work. You taste the old fear: if you move too slow, another storm takes something you could have saved; if you move too fast, you'll hand the town's future over to a timetable written by outsiders.

Dr. Saira steps between you and the rest, palms on her tablet as if anchoring the moment.

Dr. Saira N'Goma: "The pilot's data are promising. Scaling can produce non-linear benefits—if scaled with the same adaptive monitoring and community feedback loops we've used. Cass, will the firm accept adaptive governance built into contracts? That means community veto points, iterative deployment."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (after a beat) "Yes. I can—I'll try to put that into the final agreement."

Jonah's voice is low, dangerous with feeling.

Jonah Reyes: "Words can be promises or they can be marketing. You signed deals like that where my uncle worked; we learned the difference. Who is on the ground, Mira? Who do I trust in the middle of the storm?"

You look at Jonah and feel the pull between two currents: his steady, rooted loyalty that smells of tar and morning nets, and Cass's polished offer that smells faintly of institutional coffee and possibilities. You can picture both futures unfolding like weather maps.

Your survivor guilt—the quiet ledger you carry—warps hope into a knot that tightens with each new piece of data. Jonah Reyes: (soft, urgent) "Whatever you choose, Mira, I want it to be with you. Not because I'm trying to bind you—because I'm counting on you."
