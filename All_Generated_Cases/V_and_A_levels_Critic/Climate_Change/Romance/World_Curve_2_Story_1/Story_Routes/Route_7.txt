Mateo Alvarez remains by your side—close, but not enough to patch the widening gap. He will take his job if he takes it; he will make compromises you think you cannot live with. Or he will

You look at the seawall rising like a new sky—functional, efficient, merciless in its clean lines. It will keep the water out. It will keep the town from immediate ruin. And it will keep people out of the places that made the town what it was.
You think of all the compromises—legal clauses, shuffled responsibilities, the weight of being the one who opened the door to negotiation. You wanted to save people. You did. They are safer in the immediate sense. But
the town's social soul is frayed, threaded through with buyouts and gated ramps and the quiet exodus of those who could no longer bear the slow squeeze.
You breathe. The pile-driver pauses, a hinge in the noise. Somewhere a child cries—unseen. The machinery resumes, as if to make sure the world remembers to keep moving.
You step back from the fence. Your hands are empty but for the seed packet in your pocket and the frayed strap of your tote. In the end, those are all you can carry.
You will walk these streets tomorrow and the day after, and you will count what's been saved and what has been spent. You will live with the decision you made—the negotiation you opened—and you will measure it against faces that do not fit neatly into budgets.
For now, there is nothing left to do but watch the wall rise and accept that safety, in this shape, has cost the town its unguarded edges. The harbor will not be the same; the greenhouse
will stand as a pocket of warmth behind gates; Mateo will find a path that may or may not cross yours again. Jun's boat may one day be gone.
You put your hand over your heart because that is what remains to hold. The tide will come and go; concrete will resist it. The town will stabilize in one sense and dissolve in another. The
music of half the neighborhood will quiet; the other half will keep a smaller, brittle tune.
You leave the plaza as the last light thins, and somewhere in the hush a gull calls—thin and alone.
