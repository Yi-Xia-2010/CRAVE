[Scene: Municipal Hall | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the council chamber, damp coats hung on chairs, a projector hummingly backlit; banners tucked against the wood paneling]
[Music: Tense BGM — low strings under a staccato high line]
[Sound: Rain starting to patter against the tall windows; murmurs ripple through the room]

You step back into the municipal chamber with the flyer folded flat in your palm, the paper still slightly damp where your thumb pressed. The room smells of too-strong coffee, rain on asphalt, and the faint, stubborn tang of salt that never fully leaves Tidebridge. A cluster of people—committee members, advisors, a handful of residents who refused to leave the plaza—press into the benches like tide-smoothed stones. Your camera sits heavy against your heart beneath your jacket, a quiet weight of evidence.

Councilor Rosa looks up as you take your seat. Her face is measured, lined by the same sea-weathered patience you remember from town meetings that went on for hours and ended in potluck dinners. Elio sits opposite with the firm's tablet open; his profile is composed, his hands folded like a statement. Etta has her palms clasped over one another, fingers knotted in a way that says she is both prepared to argue and prepared to forgive. Jun's jaw is set; he keeps glancing at the window toward the flats as if the marsh could answer for them all.

Elio: "The firm is offering a comprehensive package that includes immediate construction timelines, relocation assistance where necessary, and resilience bonds to support livelihoods during transition. Our engineers estimate structural work can begin within three months."

You can hear the numbers like pebbles dropped into a bowl—each one makes a small, acoustic claim on the space. The thing about numbers is they feel indisputable until you remember who will pay for them with memories and ovens and the boards of their porches.

Etta: "Three months for what, Elio? For walls to go up that strangers will privatize? We still have communal oyster beds. We have plots behind Mrs. Han's house she planted for fifty years. You can't put a bond on someone's memory."

Elio's eyes, cool as steel, flick to Etta without surprise.

Elio: "We can draft covenants, but they slow permits. Time is a resource we don't have. We avoid one storm now and you avoid ruin later."

Your internal breath quickens. The meeting's rhythm accelerates—a volley of legalese, a counterpunch of local testimony. You are supposed to shepherd the opposition voices into language that the council can use; you are supposed to translate grief and belonging into clauses and binding terms. That translation feels like trying to render tide into a map.

You clear your throat. The room narrows. This is work you know—converting human memory into municipal code—but it's also personal. Very personal.

You (Ari): "If we accept a baseline without enforceable covenants for community lands and public access, we'll be protecting infrastructure at the cost of the places that make Tidebridge Tidebridge. People will be displaced; access to the water will be commodified. We need language that guarantees protection for common-use spaces, not promises that vanish when profit shifts."

Elio: (leaning forward) "Subjective phrasing will be litigated into meaninglessness. 'Community lands' is a phrase that sounds good at podiums. What is it legally? Where are the boundaries? Who holds the title?"

Councilor Rosa: "We can define the parcels in the annex and create an oversight board to review changes—"

Etta: (interrupting softly, then louder) "We have maps older than your annex, Rosa. We have elders who sit with the marsh and know which stretch is held by collective memory. Titles can be annotated with protective easements. You don't need to call it 'community lands' in the romantic way—call it 'public easement with enforcement.'"

Jun: (voice tight) "And enforcement means bodies on the ground. Who pays for that? Who pays for monitoring after the construction is done? You want to stop the sea; fine—I'll take nets and raise my lines. But if I'm asked to move because a seawall cuts off the fishing channel, whose hands pick my boat up?"

Mateo's name threads through you like a current. You look toward him; he has been standing at the back near the doorway, field notebook held tight, face unreadable. When he moves now, it's with the slow deliberation of someone who has weighed the cost of saying something out loud.

Mateo: "Ari—" (his voice is low and steady) "I don't think locking the firm into endless red tape will save anyone. Tech can be deployed fast; it will buy us time and energy. We need systems that keep people afloat—literally—and help us restore the marsh behind them."

You feel the room tighten. His words are not a betrayal—they are a fissure. He believes in devices and timelines; you believe in the living shorelines that require seasons to root. Both of you are trying to keep people in place. The friction between those truths cuts.

You (Ari): "Mateo, you know I don't oppose every engineered solution. But structures that leave ownership and access ambiguous will do more harm than good. We must write covenants now that prevent privatization."

Mateo: (shakes his head) "And if the firm walks? If the only path to getting any barrier in is accepting their baseline, then your covenants will be words on wet paper while people's houses are eaten."

His eyes find yours—warm brown flecked with gold—and something unreadable lingers there: worry? guilt? A plea you can't decode. You want to reach for him, to steady him and yourself, but there is a full room watching and every touch is an argument.

The chamber fragments into smaller fights. Elio's team slides a new clause across the table—fast-track permits in exchange for limited relocation assistance and a 'resilience bond' that purports to fund community programs. The paper is glossy, carefully worded; your thumb leaves a small smudge on its edge.

Elio: "We can include a community liaison and a phased construction schedule that prioritizes critical infrastructure. But we cannot accept covenants that prevent the design adjustments that engineers will inevitably need."

Etta: "Design adjustments that mean tomorrow your structure moves a foot and Mrs. Han's garden is gone. Who pays to replant a life?"

A clerk at the end of the table clears his throat and announces the timeline. The next storm season is not abstract—it's a calendar circled in red at the planning office. Months feel like minutes.

You (Ari): "We can propose a clause that ties any structural modification to a community consent process and mandates restoration funding for any lost communal resource. It's not obstruction. It's accountability."

Councilor Rosa: (rubbing her temple) "That adds legal complexity and time. The firm is concerned about capital commitments. We can ask for oversight, but if Elio refuses the committee may be left with no contractor when the storms arrive."

Elio: (quietly) "If the committee insists on measures that substantially increase cost and delay, we will advise our board to withdraw. There are other municipalities where rapid deployment is a priority. I do not want to issue threats, but timelines and fiscal prudence are real constraints."

The cadence of the room goes jagged. You feel the air prick with urgency—the kind that makes sentences leap out before they're fully thought. The very-high pressure of the moment turns your chest into a drum.

You leave the table and step into the corridor to breathe. Outside, rain has picked up into a steady hiss. Your camera's strap bites into your palm as you press a palm to the cool glass and watch runoff take the surface into streaked currents. The flats—it all feels like a cassette stuck on fast-forward: decisions, contracts, lives catalogued into clauses.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Dusk]
[Visual: Low, roiling water dotted with transplanted eelgrass flags, a pale sky reflected in pools]
[Sound: Wind over reeds; the distant whine of a lab buoy beacon]
[Music: Tense BGM intensifies — frenetic strings]

You walk the flats with Jun and Etta, boots sliding in peat. The damp smell of the earth presses around your nostrils; each footfall sinks a fraction deeper. Jun's hands are rough from rope; he keeps testing the waterline as if memorizing where his moorings might be compromised next week if something fails.

Jun: "If they start building the wall where the current is strongest, the sand will shift and my net runs will be cut off. They'll say we can be relocated 'nearby.' 'Nearby' isn't a life. It's a label."

Etta kneels and fingers a clump of marsh grass. She lifts it and a small ball of sediment clings to the roots, worms and life still threaded in.

Etta: "This place is engineered by time and species. You cannot slap concrete beside it and call it protection without changing everything else."

You think of clauses that could protect this—setbacks, easements, requirements for ecological impact mitigation paid for by the firm. You imagine lines of legal text demanding oversight—monitoring funds, community vetoes, long-term maintenance budgets. You picture Elio's firm replying with the same clipped, rational arguments: delay = liability. Time = lost bids.

Mateo appears at the edge of the marsh, muddy at the cuffs and carrying a sensor bob with a careful hand. His smile is thin, and his eyes are tired.

Mateo: "I can deploy sensors that give us better projections. We don't have to pick sides in that old way. Tech can inform compromise."

You: "Information is a tool, not an answer. Data doesn't keep Mrs. Han's doorstep where it is."

Mateo: (he sighs) "You make me sound heartless. I'm trying to build a bridge—"

You: "Sometimes bridges are built to carry people away."

Mateo's shoulders slack for a beat, then tighten.

Mateo: "And sometimes bridges are the only thing that stops people dying in floods."

The argument is a tide—back and forth, pulling at both of you. Neither of you speaks to the other's face as much as to the impossible geometry of keeping everyone safe. Every word tastes like salt.

The rain slashes harder. You can feel the season like a timing mechanism: each storm is a metronome clicking closer. Back in the city of papers and signatures, delays mean exposure. In the marsh, delay means tangible shift—salt creeping higher up stems, eelgrass struggling.

You understand both fears as if they're twins you raised. You are neither purely engineer nor purely gardener. You are a person who carries too many people's names in your pockets, and the pockets are already tearing.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Night — Lamp Glow]
[Visual: Amber light haloing green leaves; steam fogging panes; the place smells of citrus and damp soil]
[Sound: Low hum of grow lights; distant siren cut off by glass]
[Music: Tense BGM with a single high piano note repeating like a heartbeat]

Inside the greenhouse, you stand close to Etta at a long table strewn with seedlings and a stack of drafts. Etta's hands move with practiced ease—she binds seedlings into coir logs with delicate knots—and speaks without looking up.

Etta: "Legal language is something that can be made stubborn. It can be made to resist a corporation's appetite if we are exact. We will need—" (she lists items, fingers counting) "—an enforcement escrow, defined easement boundaries tied to historic use, community advisory oversight with binding veto for certain changes, and a maintenance endowment with penalties for noncompliance."

You transcribe, your pen a metronome keeping pace with her determination. Each item is practical, fierce. Under the greenhouse's lamps, her eyes glitter like sea glass.

You (Ari): "That sounds costly. It will add time and money to the firm's requirements."

Etta: "Then we make the cost visible. Insist on it. Make them price what they are asking the town to bear."

Your phone vibrates—an alert from the planning office: a preliminary contractors' meeting has been rescheduled for tomorrow morning. The time compresses like a fist.

You fold the note and feel the weight of choice. If you push for strict covenants and public easements you might keep the town's common places intact—but Elio has already threatened to withdraw. If you accept the baseline contract, construction moves fast but social fabric frays; if you launch a transparency campaign you could buy time and scrutiny, but the next storm will arrive faster than the audit.

Your chest races. The room hums with possibility and dread, and every scenario tastes like brine.

You sit back. Your hand taps the table—a rhythmic, anxious drum. The greenhouse seems small suddenly; outside, Tidebridge is large and messy and filled with people who trust you to be both practical and moral.

You breathe deep. The decision will not be only policy; it will be a moral suture between the places you love and the survival you must secure.

You think of Mateo—his distance, his plea for rapid solutions. You think of Etta—her list of covenants, the slow patient work of restoration. You think of Jun, of Mrs. Han's garden, of the children who will learn to fish in the channels. You think of the firm's shiny clauses and of Elio's quiet, iron logic.

The room narrows to the edge of a knife. The storm season is the blade.

You stand, the seedlings rustle as you move. The committee reconvenes tomorrow with votes to cast and signatures to place. Voices will be consolidated into the final language. This is the moment you were asked to hold—shepherd, translate, bind disagreement into a single municipal voice.

Your pulse rates higher; your palms go slick. The choices etch themselves in your mind like harbor lines on an old map.

You (Ari) speak last before the room disbands for the night, and your words are both plea and plan.

You: "We have three real paths in front of us. Each will protect different things and cost different kinds of people. Tomorrow we choose what we prioritize. I will organize the language, but I need the committee to decide what we sacrifice and what we protect."
