[Scene: Municipal Hall — Council Chamber | Midday]
[Visual: Wide shot of the wood-paneled chamber; rain mottles the tall windows. Banners from both sides hang outside, visible through glass.]
[Music: Tense BGM — low strings, insistent pulse]
[Sound: Muffled protest chants through the walls, a kettle-snap of rain on the roof]
Narrow light slices across the long table. You feel it like a line on your palm: a decision laid bare. You said yes to negotiations. The echo of that yes still vibrates under your ribs — a promise to try to hold the town in place, and a promise that already feels fragile.

You sink into a chair slightly sooner than you mean to, your camera tucked away, fingers looping the coral strap without thinking. Paper rustles. Electronic slides flick in the dim. Councilor Rosa occupies the head of the table, palms folded; her face is an island of calm bordered by a storm of pressure.

Councilor Rosa: "Thank you all for coming. We asked Elio's team to present a program we can evaluate under municipal oversight. We'll hear the plan, and then open questions. Please—respect one another's time."

[Visual: Close-up on Elio, tablet under his thumb; the light catches the edge of his trench coat]
Elio: (measured, steady) "Tidebridge's current trajectory is unsustainable. Our model prioritizes lives over nostalgia. A rapid series of engineered protections will stabilize critical infrastructure within two seasons. Seawalls, surge barriers, managed relocation in the highest-risk blocks."

He taps. A simulation rolls across the overhead: a tidal surge arcing and being redirected by steel ribs. Numbers flicker—projected lives saved, economic stability regained. The room drinks it in: relief braided with the metallic tang of inevitability.

You watch him, absorbing the cadence of his grief-logic. He speaks like someone who has balanced a ledger of loss and decided to spend himself saving others. When he says "prevented deaths" it lands heavy, unarguable, and you feel the thin, sharp loom of an answer in the mouths of people who have already lost too much.

Etta clears her throat, voice soft but weathered to carry.
Etta: "Those same protections can sterilize the estuary. They'll cut off access to foraging, to passageways we've used for generations. You call it managed relocation—my people call it erasure."

Elio: (not unkind) "Etta, I don't want erasure. I want to prevent the worst. Some parcels can be preserved with concessions—buffer zones, community easements. But we must be realistic."

Etta: "Realistic isn't your call alone."

The exchange stretches. Rosa folds herself into their crossfire, a mediator's paraphrase always waiting on her tongue.
Councilor Rosa: "The council's authority is oversight. We need specifics: timelines, indemnities, binding guarantees on preserved parcels. If we proceed, it will be with strict municipal conditions."

[Visual: A projection slides to a new page—'Preliminary Buyout Zones and Access Clauses']
[Sound: A hush, like the air going out of a bell]
Your breath stalls. The words on the slide are clinical; the implication is a small, cold stomp across people's lives. Blocks marked in red, coordinates, a list titled "Acquisition Priority." The font is neutral and lethal.

You feel your hands go slick. Your chest wants to clamp down and hold everything in. A small, frantic wanting — to protect the map of the town as you knew it — fights with the image Elio just painted of prevented bodies on the shoreline. The two are not the same thing, and that contradiction tastes like rust.

Elio: (turning, eyes on you briefly) "Municipal oversight can and should set the terms. We welcome binding conditions—if the town will accept the urgency of our timeline. The alternative is ad hoc failure."

You almost say, The alternative is stubbornness that costs people their homes. Instead you find yourself asking, quietly: "What guarantees secure long-term community access once barriers go up?"

Elio: (calm) "Legal covenants, recorded with the municipality. Regular audits. Funding mechanisms for maintenance. And—" (his voice steadying into the story he's spent his life telling) "—a staged relocation assistance for those in immediate danger."

Etta: "And the lists?"

Elio: "Preliminary. Based on flood-risk modeling and infrastructure dependency. We prioritized emergency routes and critical services first."

A murmur runs through the room like surf over broken shells. Jun's face appears in a top row of the audience, tight with something sharp and unhelpful: fear or outrage. You can't tell which; both look similar on him.

Mateo sits beside you. His notebook is closed. The satchel with tiny sensors rests by his foot, the prototypes you've seen him tinker with humming in your memory. He watches the slides with a quiet that is not acceptance but a hard, pressed silence.

Mateo: (low) "They're reading lives as coordinates." 
You: "It's not just coordinates—it's families. Neighborhoods. Jun's shop."

Mateo: "I know. I just—" (he pins you with the sort of look that tries to measure your weight) "—we promised something different. Buoys, modular defenses. Augment the marsh, don't abandon it."

You: "We promised a lot. We also promised not to let people die."

Mateo's jaw knuckles. His fingers track the edge of the table, as if finding purchase. He leans forward, voice rawer now.
Mateo: "You think municipal oversight can hold them, Ari. You think a legal clause can stop displacement. I'm tired of only promising. I wanted to build something that kept people here without forcing them into a bureaucratic ledger."

You: "And if a surge comes next season? If Jun's shop—"

Mateo: (interrupting, strained) "Then we need time, not pact paperwork. We need funding that goes to building and planting, not to buying up streets."

The room vibrates with the two logics—one of immediate triage and one of long-drawn repair. Both are urgent. Both are right in different languages. Your mouth tastes of iron again; guilt has a flavor you can't scrub away.

Etta: (leaning in, voice sharp as oyster shell) "You can build walls, or you can build futures. We need guarantees that the people's use of the estuary won't be gated off. That's not negotiable."

Elio: (honest, a little weary) "I'm arguing for lives. I'm not here to take people's histories away. But there are trade-offs: immediate protective investments mean we can't preserve every shoreline foot."

Councilor Rosa: "Then we write those trade-offs into the contract. We make them enforceable. We insist on transparent procurement, community representation on oversight, and a clause that preserves access to named parcels."

A pause like tide slackens. It's a small, juridical victory—language that might hold things in place. But then Elio's team flips one more slide: "Preliminary Timeline & Acquisition Sequence."

[Sound: A low, slow intake from the room. Someone's breath audible like a distant horn.]
You lean closer. The acquisition sequence staggers across the screen: immediate priorities, near-term buyouts, conditional protections. Under 'Conditional Protections' is thin, technical language: 'community easements subject to annual review', 'access clauses subject to infrastructure constraints'.

You feel something hard and cold settle into your chest. The words create slippage—conditions that can be watered down, easements that can be renegotiated if money runs short. You think of Etta's greenhouse windows fogging in winter, of Jun's knotty hands, of the kids who still chase kites down the pier.

Elio: (compassionate, blunt) "We are offering funding and speed. This will reduce risk. But I won't pretend it's painless. There will be loss. The goal is to reduce the scale of that loss."

Etta: (voice breaking a little) "Loss isn't only physical."

Mateo stands. The chair scrapes. His movement slices the air.

Mateo: "If we keep doing 'loss reduction' at the cost of the town's heart, then what are we saving?"

You: (defensive, tired) "We're trying to save lives, Mateo."

Mateo: (voice small, then flaring) "And I'm trying to save the way we live. I thought—" (he swallows) "—I thought you'd see there's a middle ground."

You: "I do. That's why I agreed to negotiations. To make the middle ground binding."

Mateo: "The middle ground doesn't need more signatures on a contract. It needs people building."

His mouth closes on something he hasn't said: that funding could be rerouted, that your choice to sit at this table felt like a surrender to the language he hates. The air between you hums with all the conversations you haven't had.

You reach for the coral strap instinctively. Your fingers don't find it. The weight of it is metaphor now — responsibility not easily slung aside.

[Visual: The chamber doors open; rain spatters the threshold. Outside, the plaza crowd shifts like nervous fish.]
Councilor Rosa: (firm) "We will draft terms. Municipal counsel will work with Elio's legal team and community representatives. We will return in two weeks with a binding motion for public review."

The deadline hangs like an announced tide. Two weeks. Time enough to prepare, too brief to resolve hurt and suspicion.

Etta: "We will send names for the oversight committee. We will insist on named parcels with unambiguous protections."

Elio: "We will accept municipal oversight. But understand—delays cost lives. The funding window is tight."

Jun: (from the audience) "And what about those of us who can't wait for two weeks? For two seasons?"

A ripple of agreement answers him. Voices rise; someone begins to clap for the urgency, someone else boos at the betrayal. The room becomes a small storm.

You stand, because standing feels like action. Mateo pauses, as if choosing whether to stand with you, and then walks out. Not away forever—his shoes hit the corridor in a cadence that matches the quick, hard drum of your heart—but away enough that his absence maps a new, unsteady distance.

You: (calling after him) "Mateo—"

He doesn't turn. You hear the door close like a punctuation mark.

[Visual: Quick cut to the municipal plaza through the tall window; banners snap in the rain]
[Music: Tense BGM swells, then tightens]
You move toward the window, palms pressed to the cool glass. Outside, people shout into the drizzle; placards blur under the rain. The town that you wanted to save looks, for a moment, like a painting that might be varnished over and stored in a museum, gone from living use.

You think of all the things you promised—your camera full of faces and data, the seedlings in Etta's greenhouse, Mateo's blueprints. Each feels suddenly smaller than the map on the screen.

Your pulse drums high, every muscle taut. You can feel the negotiation's perimeter closing in around your life like a net.

A thought, sharp and clear: hope is a risky architecture. It requires both bravery and bluntness; it requires the people who build it to disagree without burning the beams. Right now, the beams are smoldering.

You breathe in salt and rain and the faint copper of fear. Two weeks. A draft to be written. Names that will be on lists. Access that could be carved away by technicalities. The sound of Mateo's footsteps has not yet faded from the corridor.

You fold your hands into one another until the knuckles whiten. The municipal clock ticks loud as surf. Outside, someone shouts a new chant that starts with "Protect" and ends with "No Buyouts." The syllables beat like surf on the rocks beneath your chest. You think you can already see the next meeting: lawyers, signatures, Etta's soft anger, Elio's tired certainty, Rosa's middle way—and you wonder where you will stand when the final words are signed.
