[Scene: Wave Innovation Lab | Morning]
[Visual: Modular lab on pilings; pale sky reflected in damp metal. Holographic wave-vector displays pulse faintly in the dim light.]
[Music: Urgent electronic pulse under a tightening string motif]
[Sound: Soft whir of lab fans, distant gulls, the metallic ping of tools being set down]

You step into the lab with your camera slung inside its tote, palms damp from the morning moisture. The air smells of ozone and coffee—clean, sharp, like something being assembled to hold back a world that keeps pushing. Mateo is already there, sleeves rolled, eyes bright but rimmed with the same exhaustion you feel in your chest. He offers a small, crooked smile that says: we can do this. We have to.

Mateo: "You look like you carried the plaza on your back this morning."
You: "And you looked like you tried to carry the buoy." (You force a laugh; it lands halfway between humor and a cough.)

The lab director—Dr. Sanaa Iqbal—meets you with a handshake that is firm enough to be real and politely measured. Behind her, a prototype buoy rests on a padded cradle, its sensors wrapped in translucent polymer, blue LEDs sleeping like an animal under drizzle.

Dr. Iqbal: "Ari, Mateo. We'll run a sixty-day pilot. Sensors, pumps, a local dashboard for the collective. Funding is conditional—investor interest is real, but so are milestones."

You watch the way she pronounces "conditional" and feel a cold anchor in your gut. Conditional always means strings. Conditional always means someone somewhere will ask for control.

You: "We need community governance baked into the milestones. The restoration collective needs access to raw data, maintenance training, and decision-making authority."

Dr. Iqbal: (nods slowly) "That's feasible. We're proponents of co-design. But to keep the lab open and the buoys in the water, there will be reporting metrics: uptime, energy capture rates, cost-per-kilowatt-hour. Investors will want timelines."

Mateo: "Timelines are fine. But not at the expense of—" (he swallows) "—local priorities."

The exchange stretches, back and forth. You press for language that guarantees local control; she presses back with investor caution. Every answer comes with an undercurrent of negotiation that smells of money and leverage.

You nod. You think of Etta's greenhouse hands: quick, steady, impossible to replicate with a signature. You think of Jun's face stoic in rain; you think of the children's rooms that flooded. You count the costs in faces rather than in spreadsheets, and it makes your throat ache.

[Scene: Pier | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The prototype buoy bobbing, tethered near a weathered piling. Salt-scarred wood creaks; a low tide reveals the marsh's ragged edge.]
[Music: Tension rises—percussive rhythms, wind-brushed strings]
[Sound: Water lapping, a muffled chorus of conversation from clustered neighbors]

The community turnout is a ragged mix: a few hopeful kids, a trio of tech enthusiasts with earnest hoodies, Jun with his arms crossed, Etta with her sea-glass necklace catching dim light. The buoy hums faintly, LEDs blinking as if in response to your heartbeat.

Jun: "So these things will… what? Power a pump so the marsh can breathe? Or will they sit out there and tell investors how pretty our waves are?"

You: "They feed sensors and run small pumps that keep water moving across new coir sills. In theory—" (you hate how clinical "in theory" sounds) "—they reduce standing brackish pools that choke seedlings."

Etta: (places a hand on your shoulder) "We should test, cautiously. Build around what grows, not over it. The marsh is patient; it doesn't like to be rushed."

Jun's skepticism is not theatrical; it's a taut wire—the livelihood of fishermen, the meals of families, the memory of nets lost to storms. He asks sharp questions: battery lifespans, failure modes, what happens if a buoy drifts into someone’s net. Each is practical and painful.

Mateo steps into the space between you and Jun, voice steady but edged.

Mateo: "We'll map drift patterns, mark buoys on charts, build quick-release fittings. We'll train fishers in retrieval. This isn't a parachute drop, Jun. It's a tool you can own."

Jun: (looking at Mateo, then at you) "You say 'own.' Who decides 'own'? Investors? A lab? Some people in suits?"

You meet Jun's gaze and feel the old guilt—if you push too hard, the project stalls; if you concede too much, you betray the town.

The demonstration itself goes—mostly—well. The buoy produces a slow, steady trickle of power; a small pump sputters and sends a measured stream across a distressed patch of marsh. People clap because it's something to clap about. But applause doesn't silence the low knot in your stomach—this is a demonstration, and demonstrations are rehearsals for something bigger and more dangerous.

[Scene: Wave Innovation Lab | Night — Work Session]
[Visual: Warm lamplight on soldered joints and tide charts. Steam from a kettle fogs a glass partition; the world outside is a bruise of rain.]
[Music: A fragile piano tremor beneath anxious string crescendos]
[Sound: Quiet typing, a kettle's sigh, soft footsteps on metal grating]

You and Mateo work long into the night, elbows bumped, spare parts and sticky notes forming a constellation across the workbench. The lab's daytime edge softens—here it feels more intimate, like a small boat tucked into a larger storm.

Mateo: (whispering over a soldered joint) "I can't believe it's actually spinning. I'm… proud, Ari. Proud of you."

You feel your chest constrict, a warmth that fights its way through the anxiety. His hand brushes yours while reaching for a cable, and this time you don't pull away. The touch is quick, an acknowledgment that something personal is forming amid deadlines and cables.

You: "We both pushed it. We said what we had to say in the plaza. You did the engineering. You did that."

Mateo's smile is tired and perfect and suddenly private.

Mateo: "I worried you'd regret siding with me today."

You: "I worried I'd regret not choosing what felt like the only path forward."

Their proximity makes the lab feel both safer and more exposed. For a few breaths you let the connection exist without naming it—two people leaning toward the same, fragile lifeline. But the world intrudes—the kettle whistles, a phone buzzes with an incoming message, the string motif under the music tightens.

Dr. Iqbal steps into the lab doorway, the overhead light framing her silhouette.

Dr. Iqbal: "We had an unexpected meeting request this afternoon. An investment consortium wants to visit next week. They mentioned Tidebridge by name. They've asked for a tour of the pilot site."

Your throat goes dry. "Consortium" might mean funding, or it might mean the slow displacement of decision-making. The word tastes like brine and rust.

Mateo: (quietly) "Are they interested in the tech or the town?"

Dr. Iqbal: "Both. They asked for deliverables and a timeline. They also—" (she hesitates) "—they asked if we'd be open to scale-up partnerships. It would accelerate manufacturing and deployment."

You: "Scale-up under what terms?"

Dr. Iqbal: "They haven't signed anything. They want to see results. But they'll expect a path to commercialization. They'll ask for IP protections, exclusivity windows. They won't bluntly demand we hand over governance, but they'll structure deals to privilege rapid, replicable outcomes."

You feel your pulse stutter and then race. Each investor word carves into the plan you had—community control, co-design, slow restoration. The possibility of fast money skates dangerously close to the idea that someone else will translate tidal health into shareholder reports.

Mateo reaches for your hand across the bench, fingers pressing tight.

Mateo: "We can set the terms. We can insist on community clauses in any investor agreement."

You: "We can insist—and they can walk. Or they can give us what we need—they can be allies. We don't know yet."

Dr. Iqbal: "There is one more complication. Elio Sato has been very visible at municipal meetings. He emailed the lab this morning—introducing himself and offering to make introductions to venture partners. He framed it as 'bridging expertise and capital.'"

The name lands like a stone in a still pool. You see Elio's neat, measured face in your mind—steel-gray eyes, corporate lapel pin, the coolness that framed people as data points. He believes in decisive scale, in infrastructure that fixes the map quickly. He is persuasive. He is efficient. He also represents the very path you rallied the plaza against—centralized solutions that risk displacing people in the interest of speed.

You: "He emailed you? Did he—offer anything specific?"

Dr. Iqbal: "He offered introductions. He also volunteered 'project oversight' as a way to ensure timelines are met."

Mateo's hands tense.

Mateo: "Oversight. As in—control."

Dr. Iqbal: (softly) "He didn't use that word. But it's why investors like him; he outlines deliverables in a way that reduces political friction."

Your mind runs a rapid, hot list of what that could mean: conditional funding that requires modular deployment on municipally owned piers; exclusive manufacturing contracts; data sharing agreements with corporate back-ends. Each possibility pulls at the frayed edges of the plan you and the collective have patched together.

Etta's voice—older, steadier—floats into your memory: "Keep the people at the heart, Ari. Tech can help. It mustn't be the heart."

You press your forehead to your palm, feeling the scalp tighten, the fatigue and the anger and the thin thread of hope tangled together. Outside, the rain increases, strings of it thrumming against the lab's outer panels like a countdown.

Dr. Iqbal: "They'll be here in three days. They want a presentation, a demo, a tour. Elio said he might come."

The words 'Elio might come' are an accelerant. Your stomach coils. You can feel the chapter turning—this pilot could be the bridge Mateo promised, or the wedge that pries the town open.

Mateo leans close, voice low, urgent.

Mateo: "If Elio shows up, we have to be clear on red lines. No exclusivity. No remote lockouts on data. No managed relocation clauses tied to our data."

You: "And if they push?"

Mateo: (eyes fixed on yours) "We push back. Together."

The closeness of the vow does not erase the pounding in your ears. The future feels like a storm surge: relentless, quick, and if you don't brace correctly, devastating. The warmth in your chest where Mateo's hand touched is suddenly spiked with panic—if you lose this fight, you might lose him too, or everything you love.

At the lab's single window, you watch a car pull up under the sodium glow of a streetlamp. It is not the investors' shuttle. It is a dark, perfectly tailored silhouette—Elio Sato stepping out, hands in pockets, rain beading on his coat like glass.

Your breath catches. The music cuts to a single, held note as the lab door swings open and the storm sounds rush in.

[Visual: Close-up on Elio's face as he enters, rain on his lashes, his smile practiced and precise]
[Music: Single held chord resolving into a taut, rising string cluster]
[Sound: The lab's airlock closes with a soft, decisive click]

You stand. Your pulse thrums high— faster than the LEDs on the buoy, louder than the crowd's applause at the pier. This is the moment you promised would expose truths. Elio moves through the lab with that confidence that feels like low tide revealing foundations.

Elio: (warm, measured) "Ari. Mateo. Dr. Iqbal. What a tidy experiment you've built."

Dr. Iqbal: (cool) "Elio. We were expecting the consortium next week."

Elio: (smiles) "I thought I'd introduce myself earlier. There are investors who prefer to meet stakeholders before they commit. I only want to help."

You: (internal) The offer is a blade wrapped in velvet. Help, or a hand that will guide the town into channels that suit a balance sheet.

Elio's eyes flick to you, then to Mateo, calculating and unreadable.

Elio: "May I see the pilot? I have some contacts who could take this beyond Tidebridge. We all want to protect the coast, right? The question is how fast and how many we can save."

Mateo: (voice tight) "We want to help the town. Not just protect assets for the highest bidder."

Elio: (tilting his head) "Of course. Scale doesn't have to mean sellout, Mateo. It can mean more people protected, faster. There's room for both."

You feel the word "both" like a noose that can also be a lifeline—elastic, uncertain. The room contracts to the space between your ribs and Elio's steady gaze.

Your phone buzzes—an email subject line you don't want: SUBJECT: INVESTOR PREVIEW REQUEST — FOLLOW-UP FROM ELIO SATO.

The lab's hum seems louder. Mateo's grip on your wrist is iron now, a silent promise and a plea. Your choices haven't ended; they've multiplied into meetings, clauses, and the slow, gnawing risk of losing community control.

The rain outside intensifies, and for a moment the world is nothing but the sound of water and the beat of your heart. You realize the fight you chose at the plaza is only beginning—and the shape it will take is suddenly uncertain.

[Music: Crescendo of strings and electronic pulse, then a sharp, unresolved chord]

You breathe in, wanting to gather a plan that isn't made of quick compromises. You want to hold on to what Etta warned about: people at the heart. You want to keep Mateo beside you without him being crushed between your ideals and the pull of funding. You feel the weight of all of it, the guilt and the urgency and the brittle thread of hope.

You step forward, preparing words you haven't yet formed—prepared to name your red lines, to ask Dr. Iqbal for clauses, to call Etta, to steady Jun, to speak so that the town is not an afterthought.
