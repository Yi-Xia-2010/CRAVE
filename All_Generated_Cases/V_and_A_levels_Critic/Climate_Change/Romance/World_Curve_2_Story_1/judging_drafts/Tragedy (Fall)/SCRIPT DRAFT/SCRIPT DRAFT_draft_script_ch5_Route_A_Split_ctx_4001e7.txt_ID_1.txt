[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Late Afternoon — hours after the storm]
[Visual: Wide shot of the saltmarsh; pale light slices through broken cloud. A ragged coir sill is half-submerged, one section torn away]
[Music: Percussive strings, urgent—heartbeat tempo]
[Sound: Sucking water, creaking wood, distant siren wailing; low thunder rolls]

You step into the flats and the ground gives with a wet, fed-up sigh. Mud sucks at your boots; brine sprays your face with each gust that scavenges across the torn marsh. Where the new coir sill should have been a neat, hopeful line, there's a ragged gape—black water yawning through like a mouth. The transplanted grasses, the bright flagging you and volunteers hammered down last month, drift in clumps like someone ripped pages from a book.

Your lungs pull in salt and cold and the thin copper tang of anxiety. You had imagined setbacks. You had not imagined this—an entire wall of work unraveling in one tidal heave. The sensory map of the day is immediate: the wet slap of your sleeve against your arm, the gritty pressure of mud under the soles of your boots, the smell of wet peat and gasoline where a generator sputters on a distant launch platform. The sound of a child's muffled sob threads through the low chorus and unspools your stomach.

Etta is a shape on the boardwalk ahead, small and taut as a drawn bow. She crouches at the edge and runs a finger along what remains of our handiwork—knuckles white—and when she looks up, her eyes are not the warm harbor you know. They are flint.

Etta: "They took root, Ari. They were supposed to." (voice frayed, brittle) "We counted on the marsh to slow that surge."

You cross the last patch of boardwalk in two long steps. Mud flicks your ankles. You tuck a wet strand of hair behind your ear and your camera bobs against your chest, its leather strap sodden. The camera matters; it always matters. Evidence is a kind of witness. But it can't staunch the water.

You: "It held in places—look, the eastern sills—" Your throat is thick. Words come clipped. "We slowed it. We saved parts."

Etta's mouth tightens. She doesn't argue with the fact; she argues with the ledger of what 'saved' means. The wind steals the sentence from you and leaves you with the residue of guilt: the faces who slept on our work, the house at the harbor's edge that leaned on our promise.

[Visual: Medium close on Mateo as he kneels beside a generator, hands greasy with seawater]
[Sound: Rapid footsteps approaching]

Mateo looks up at you—his hair stuck to his forehead, eyes bright as flares. He moves with the restless focused energy you know too well: hands finding tasks like ritual. There's a small wound across his knuckle, thin and bleeding, and for a moment you forget everything but that line of blood and how it matches the tremor in his jaw.

Mateo: "We can triage it. If we push the buoys now, re-route power to pumps—temporary dewatering at the harbor, at least—" (he grabs a coil of cable) "—we can keep a dozen houses from taking the worst."

You: "Temporary pumps?" The question is a jag. "How long do you have? How many buoys are ready? How many volunteers?"

Mateo's answer is not numbers, it's the quick movement of a man who believes action dilutes panic. He speaks fast, a nervous current.

Mateo: "Enough to keep the worst from becoming permanent if we can deploy within the next twelve hours. I'm not promising miracles. But we can buy the town a night—maybe two. And we can stabilize the nursery, the generator at the clinic, Jun's shed—" (he looks at you, softening) "—we can keep people in their homes for now."

You listen, measuring the rhythm of his optimism against the pulse in your ears. Your mind runs the ledger: buoys accelerate, more wiring, Mateo's prototypes need extra hands, the town needs permission to spool cables across public land. Your body remembers doing impossible logistics with half a grin. Your heart remembers how frightened it was the first time Mateo and you wired a broken sensor and it hummed back to life.

Jun arrives with two men from the harbor—faces knocked raw by the wind. He hasn't been talkative today; his silence says a lot. He looks at the breached sill with the sort of appraisal that names livelihoods.

Jun: "The water ate into Luis's place. Second floor soaked." (anger tight in his voice) "Kids. They were in the living room when the surge came."

You feel a crack open inside you. The words fracture the space, make urgency sharp and personal. You imagine a mattress floated like driftwood, a child's blanket like a wet flag. The guilt you carry—old and familiar—tightens into a sharpened rod.

[Visual: Cut to wider shot—Elio's team appears along the access lane; their raincoats are cut sharp, their presence like a clean line against the chaos]
[Sound: Murmur of cameras, a distant reporter’s voice; a polite, too-bright laugh from someone not used to mud]

Elio stands at the edge of the plaza where the crowd has gathered, his coat dry enough to look almost absurd. He watches the marsh with the same cool, analytical gaze that has always unsettled you. There's a clipboard in his hand and a tablet tucked under his arm—data on demand. He doesn't step into the muck. He doesn't need to; he has people to do that for him.

Elio: "We need scale, Ari." (measured) "Living shorelines have value, but storms are accelerating beyond patchwork intervention. A phased seawall—targeted—would have stopped this breach."

You: "Phased seawalls are exactly what Etta warned us about." You say it sharp, the words tasting of old argument. "They widen the footprint, they push buyouts, they make waterfront a commodity for the well-insured."

Elio: (a flicker of something like regret) "I know what this looks like from the outside. I've seen managed relocations tear towns apart. I'm not advocating an erasure. I'm saying—there's a window where structural defenses buy us time to scale restoration without losing people."

Your internal pulse argues itself into shards. Elio’s logic is not murderous; it's pragmatic and cold in the way of someone convinced by totals and less moved by faces. It lands as an accusation: that you and Etta's slow hands are insufficient.

Murmurs ripple through the gathering—some nod toward Elio; others spit the word 'sellout' like a match. People who had joined you with shovels yesterday now have faces hardened by fear. Friends you hauled seedlings with yesterday are uncertain if the effort is a lifeboat or a leash.

Etta steps between you and Elio, a frail, furious fulcrum.

Etta: (voice low, volcanic) "We are not a pilot project for a white paper. We are people with names. When you talk 'scale' you talk displacement. You offer security that costs our memories. I will not hand that to you."

Elio's jaw tightens. His reply is slower now, as if he is measuring each syllable so they don't reveal anything more than he intends.

Elio: "Then tell me what will keep children dry tonight, Etta. Tell me which of our options costs fewer lives."

Etta looks at him as if he has asked the wind to explain the tide.

Etta: "We shore the marsh. We fight for policy that gives us funds. We force the city to allow temporary measures without surrendering property rights. We—" (she gropes for the word) "—we stay human in the math."

You feel the toxin of guilt burn behind your ribs: you are the one who promised 'we' and now 'we' is fraying at the seam. Voices around you sharpen into a cacophony. Someone shouts that councilor Rosa is being pressured. Someone else cries that the roof of the clinic leaked and the generator is overtaxed. The tempo of chaos climbs; adrenaline floods you in a hot, metallic wash.

Mateo steps close, pressing his shoulder to yours in a small, deliberate grounding. His hand is warm and Earth-scented; it anchors you in a way that everything else currently cannot.

Mateo: (low, urgent) "We can get power to key pumps tonight. We'll need town approval for lines, and volunteers to string cable, and—" (he swallows) "—we need your word, Ari. If you say we do it, I'll make it happen."

You: "If I say yes, Mateo, we risk everything we fought to keep. If I say no, people sleep on wet floors and remember who failed them."

Mateo's eyes go very soft and very real. He does not offer platitudes.

Mateo: "I don't want to stand in front of you and make this a test of faith. I want to keep people dry."

The argument circles, returns, and the pitch climbs. Jun's hand finds your sleeve; there is a new urgency in his face—you can see the inventory of losses behind his gaze.

Jun: "My uncle's boat—gone. He can't fish if his gear is under two feet of water. We can't survive another season like this."

You think of every apprenticeship lesson Etta taught you: shorelines are not just soil and root; they are memory, they are livelihood, they are the slow stitching that keeps people in place. You think of Mateo's buoys humming in the dark like a promise of modern fairy dust. You think of Elio's measured architectures that have on other coasts meant razed houses and new gated access. The weight of decision settles in your gut.

[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor | Early Evening — Emergency response in progress]
[Visual: Flooded narrow lanes, neighbors hauling belongings; a child's stuffed toy saturated and abandoned near a threshold]
[Sound: Shouts, generators, the hiss of pumps working; rain returns, a thin needle]

You move through the harbor like a compass seeking true north. Your hands are everywhere—directing, shelving, holding—because you have trained yourself to be useful. But usefulness is not absolution. As you hand a plastic tarp to a neighbor, you see the small face pressed to a window: a child watches watery horizons with a kind of adult worry that breaks you.

The mother of the child—Luis's neighbor—turns and her face crumples when she sees you. Grief is a blunt instrument; it breaks polite conversations.

Mother: "They told us it would be enough." (voice raw) "They promised."

You have no ready words. Promises were always a fragile currency in Tidebridge, something traded like favours and sealed with tea. You can feel the accusation without being able to catch it—an arrow that pierces without a name.

Mara Chen arrives, recorder already out. Her questions prick like needles that reveal infection. She corners Elio first, then you, then Mateo. The cameras want a snippet, a soundbite, something digestible that will either be a rallying cry or a ledger entry.

Mara: "Ari—what went wrong? Is the collective underfunded? Should the town have accepted corporate assistance earlier?"

You: (the answer is granular and ugly and private) "We did what we could. We planted, we monitored, we fought for policy. We thought—" You stop, because 'we thought' is not a bridge over houses; it's a confession.

Mara presses. Her voice is not cruel; it is efficient. "Do you regret not reaching a compromise sooner?"

The wind lifts a ripped banner and for a breath the words on it catch: 'HANDS-ON RESTORATION'—then it tears and blows away. You taste the salt of your own tears.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Night — warmed bulbs glow, steam in the panes]
[Visual: Close, intimate; the greenhouse is an island of warmth, steam fogging the glass. Seed trays crowd the benches; pungent herb scent]
[Music: Sparse piano, uneasy undertow]
[Sound: Hum of heat lamps, occasional drip; outside, the ocean is a distant percussion]

You slide into the greenhouse and the heat hits like a reprieve. The air smells of citrus and damp soil so strong that for a moment you imagine you can breathe properly. Etta moves between trays, hands that smell of peat and salt, and for the first time today she allows the sort of small, private exhaustion you would have expected to see.

Etta: (quiet, a single exhale) "People will look for someone to blame, Ari. You know that. They taught you to take responsibility. But don't let that same responsibility become a chain that snaps you."

You: "I feel broken, Etta. If we'd done more earlier—if I'd pushed harder—would it have mattered?" The question is an old bruise, and asking it opens an old scar.

Etta kneels and pulls a sapling free, showing you the root structure. "Sometimes a root will take if the soil is right. Sometimes no matter what you do, a storm finds its way. We do our work because it's the right thing, not because it protects us from everything."

Mateo is there, soaked again, drying his hands on his shirt. He does not lean into platitudes. He folds into the space like another plant might, pressing near, seeking warmth.

Mateo: "I can speed parts up. I can call in a team from the city lab to help spool cable, get pumps running." (he meets your eyes) "But they'll ask for oversight. They'll ask for assurances—paperwork that can be twisted."

Etta: "They'll come with eyes for contracts and clauses." She looks directly at you. "You asked us to be the people's voice in a sea of numbers. Are we ready to have that voice made into a bargaining chip?"

The greenhouse feels suddenly smaller; your chest tightens. You think of the child's wet toys, of Jun's boat, of the nursery trays you have shelved for months. You think of Mateo's hands on the generator, of Elio's measured language in public, of Etta's refusal to hand the town's memory to a ledger.

You imagine the future as a series of tides. One choice will be like laying another coir sill—slow, uncertain, but routed through people who will remain. Another will be Mateo's buoys: a fast stitch that buys nights and maybe weeks but paints targets on your model for corporate investors. Another will be negotiating with Elio: fast stabilization at the cost of land rights and the gentle erosion of neighborhood life.

Your heart is a drum; the tempo races as every argument and face loops into a single unbearable question: who are you, if not the one people look to when the tide takes and gives?

You stand in the greenhouse, hands pressed to the edge of a tray, the steam fogging your breath. The room narrows until the only options are the ones pressing at the door.

[Visual: Close-up on your face; rain begins again outside, diffusing light into watercolor]
[Music: Swelling strings into a sharp, high tremor—escalation to decision pitch]
[Sound: A distant roar as another wave thumps a protective jetty; a child's laugh in the distance cuts through like a blade]

You can feel the town leaning on you—older hands hoping, young eyes accusing, a lover's trust pressed like a palm to your back. The arousal that has built through the day — panic, urgency, relentless adrenaline — coalesces into the moment of choice.

You breathe, counting anchored in the greenhouse's warmth, and then you name the paths out loud because words, once spoken, make a future legible.
