[Scene: Wave Innovation Lab | Late Morning]
[Music: Tense BGM — a low, urgent pulse under steady strings]

You still feel the plaza's pressure in your throat—how the town's eyes landed on you when you said Mateo's name. Saying it aloud didn't make anything simpler. It made things faster.

Inside the lab, air smells of warmed plastic, ozone, and the faint sharpness of solder. Mateo is bent over a bench, fingers moving with habitual calm, translating an anxious idea into neat cables and coded lines. His back is a quiet anchor; that steadiness is partly why you chose this path in public. It is also why the announcement already feels like a door that will not close.

Mateo looks up as you come in, hair damp at the temples, a thin smear of grease on one knuckle. He brushes at it with his thumb and manages a smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Mateo: "You look like a person who stepped into a vote and came out dripping. Did the plaza treat you well?"

You: "They treated us like something between charity case and miracle. That's the new itinerary." (You pull the camera from your tote, more reflex than reporting. You need proof, but proof is not the same as permission.)

Mateo: (chuckles, then softer) "We made it visible. That's rare. It'll bring attention—good and bad. The important part is the pilot works. We put the buoys in, power the pumps, the marsh gets the freshwater it needs—small, local, reversible."

You: "Small and reversible sounds like an invitation and a warning both." (You watch his face while you say it. He doesn't flinch. For a beat, silence stretches between the two of you the way tide stretches estuary flats.) "Who's here from the investors list?"

Mateo: "A couple of lab backers, Mara's sent a note that she'd like to take photos later, and—" (he hesitates) "—Elio's team sent someone to sit in on the demos. They said they're curious about community-led tech."

You: (your stomach drops at the word 'Elio'—curiosity in that context never arrives unburdened) "Curious. Right." (You force the word into something neutral. It doesn't fit.) "Show me the buoy again."

[Visual: Close-up on a compact buoy chassis; hand-labeled coils, a porous membrane for wave capture, a patch of algae clinging like a green promise]
[Sound: Low whir as a test motor spins, a tinny readout beep]

Mateo reaches out; his fingers brush yours as he hands over a module. The touch is quick and ordinary, but it lights a private map of possibility across your skin. He meets your eyes.

Mateo: "We can hook the sensor array to the pumps and run them off the buoy for a week without shore power. If the marsh responds—if salinity drops and plantings take—they scale. If not, we pull them. No land tenure strings, no corporate rollovers."

You: "That's the pitch you gave at the plaza." (You can't help the way your voice tightens.) "It's clean. It's honest."

Mateo: (there's something like hope in the way he says it, and an edge of fear he hides quickly) "It could be enough to keep people here."

[Sound: A muted phone alert somewhere inside the lab. It sounds like a pebble dropped into a deep pot—small but resonant]

You: (reading the lab's nervous energy like weather) "We need Etta's blessing for the planting windows. And Jun—if he trusts it, half the fleet might be willing to let us test the sensors from his boats."

[Visual: The lab's main door opens; Etta steps in, rain-beaded apron still smelling of peat and thyme]
Etta: "I heard the good news. Cautious trials, eh? I like the sound of that. No promises we can't keep."

You: (relief briefly loosening your shoulders) "Etta." (You move forward and she takes your hands, her palms callused and steady.) "We said we'd opt for small steps."

Etta: "Small steps that build knowledge. Show me the calibration logs." (She peers at the screens with a practised squint.) "If this gives us data without selling the shore, I'm with you. But keep us in the loop. Promise?"

You: "Promise." (You say the word, and it is heavy.)

Mateo: (noticing your gaze) "Do you want me to handle it? I can soft-respond—buy time."

You: (reading makes your throat close; the words translate into threats: control, extraction, conditional help) "They want data access and a slice of the IP."

Mateo: (his jaw tightens) "Co-ownership? That was not in our projections. We promised the town control."

Etta: "They use the word 'partnership' like it's a blanket. Partnerships can smother." (She takes off her glasses slowly, eyes tired.) "Be careful."

[Scene: Pier | Afternoon]
[Visual: The pier is a cluster of hands and murmured instructions. Community members watch from sandbags and stacked crates; a dozen bright flags snap in the wind]
[Sound: Waves slap against pilings, ropes creak, a distant radio playing a faint, old pop song]

You stand at the edge of the pier while the buoy is craned gently into saltwater. Salt spray hits your face, cold and honest. The harbor looks like a town portrait hung over the sea—tattered but stubborn.

Jun pushes up the peak of his cap and keeps his hands deep in his coat. He doesn't smile. He never smiles for proposals; he smiles for fish and fair weather.

Jun: "So this is it? Tiny paddlers drifting like toys? Where's the proof it'll keep the flood out? Or are we...are we just making prettier puddles?"

You: "Not prettier puddles. Pumps, sensors—managed freshwater flows. We plant cordgrass in sheltered cells. The marsh buffers. It's not a wall, Jun, but it makes the ground hold better."

Jun: "Better how? Better for the ones who can afford to be optimistic? I already had to patch three nets after last season. My rent goes up if the 'pilot' brings attention."

You: (the old guilt flares—if you push too hard, Jun's livelihood could be overshadowed by investment and by outsiders who will price him out) "We built this plan to keep people like you in their homes. If we fail, I will eat my boots. If we succeed, we keep the fleet. That's the point."

Jun: (his laugh is rough) "You sound like a promise. Promises blow away in wind."

[Sound: A polite voice, clipped and professional, approaches accompanied by two tidy suits. They introduce themselves as representatives from the Wave Innovation Lab's investor committee—Elio's people.]

Investor Rep 1: "We're here to observe the pilot on behalf of interested parties. Your methods are innovative. We like the community angle."

You: (the phrase 'we like' lands like a disclaimer) "Observation's fine. But the community stays in control of the data and deployment decisions."

Investor Rep 2: (smiling, a little too fast) "Of course. We're fans of community-led initiatives. We just want to ensure the pilot is scalable and fundable."

[Visual: The buoy dips and then rights itself, LEDs blinking; the pump hums as a low stream of brackish water moves toward a test planting cell]
[Sound: A collective inhale from the crowd as the marsh test plot darkens with the first trickle]

Etta: (softly, to you) "If it holds for six weeks, that's a signal. Don't let them overwrite the narrative with investor brochures."

Mateo: (quiet, leaning in) "They asked for 'preferred vendor' clauses in the first meeting. I tried to laugh it off, but their eyes didn't."

You: "We need terms that lock community oversight. No IP handoffs. No favored vendors without community consent."

Investor Rep 1: "Those are negotiable points. We see the value of local governance; it makes adoption easier."

You: (you sense the way 'negotiable' slides, the pressure implied: money, speed, the lure of scaling) "Negotiate with us. Not around us."

Jun: (muttering) "Negotiation is where the teeth show."

[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor / Wave Innovation Lab | Night — Post-deployment]
[Visual: Night settles with a cold clarity. The lab glows like a watchful creature at the water's edge. The harbor mirrors pinpricks of light.]
[Sound: Rain softens to a steady patter; the buoy's telemetry pings quietly into the lab's monitors]

The lab after dark feels intimate and dangerous in equal measure. Tools are lined up like promises yet to be kept. You and Mateo work side by side, soldering sensors, taping notes to a whiteboard scrawled with salinity curves and planting schedules. The rest of the world narrows to the small radiance of screens and the warm hum of equipment.

Mateo: (half-smiling as he tapes a sensor) "You always work better when you're afraid."

You: (a bark of a laugh, but your hands don't stop) "Is that supposed to be a compliment?"

Mateo: "Yes. It means you focus. It means you care enough to not sleep." (He looks at you, shadows under his eyes.) "Don't make a habit of burning yourself out."

You: "Someone has to keep the rail from buckling. Someone has to make the physics line up with people's dinners." (Your voice cracks on the last word. You didn't mean to say it so raw.)

Mateo: (reaches over, a small, steady motion; he brushes salt from your cheek with the pad of his thumb) "We'll make a mess of things together, if we have to. But we'll do it honest."

[Visual: The lab's inbox blinks again—another message. This time the subject line is blunt: 'INVESTOR CONSORTIUM: TERM SHEET ATTACHMENT — ACTIONABLE'. The sender matches the firm that sent the earlier email. The lab's murmurs hush.]

Mateo: (reading over your shoulder) "They want expeditionary rights if we scale—priority manufacturing slots. They offer full funding for a wider pilot. But the clause locks proprietary manufacturing unless the lab agrees to 'standardization parameters' dictated by the fund."

You: (your breath comes fast; the words are simple and sharp: funding in exchange for control) "Standardization and priority manufacturing—that's how they end up making decisions about who gets saved."

Mateo: "We could get the pumps into twice as many cells. We could prove the concept faster. We could—"

You: (the line cuts under you like a riptide) "—and then they own the thing that keeps our town safe. They charge for access. They set prices."

Mateo: (his hands tremble for a fraction of a second; then he folds his fingers into the bench like a person bracing for impact) "I didn't… I thought if we showed a working model they'd *help*, not take over. I went after resources because I wanted options."

You: "Options aren't free when they're on someone else's ledger."

Etta: (from the doorway; her voice small but firm) "There will be theys who call themselves saviors. There will be bargains that sound like salvation. Remember whose voices this is for."

[Sound: Your phone buzzes—Jun's name flashes. He sent an audio clip: a short, raw message—his voice closer to the wind that night than to the radio static of bureaucracy.]

Jun (audio): "Ari. If those people start carving us up with clauses, you're not the only one who'll blame the pilot. Be careful. Be smarter than the men in suits."

You: (your chest tightens. Jun's words have the weight of real livelihood; he's not against trying—he's against being sold out.) "I'll call him back."

Mateo: (his voice is unsteady) "I thought we could have both—community control and funding. I thought there was a way to build and keep it here."

You: "I thought so, too." (You let the honesty sit between you like exposed wire. It doesn't look like comfort.) "But I'm not willing to trade people's future for speed."

Mateo: "And I'm not willing to watch the town drown because I refused an offer. Do you know how many times I've replayed the moment I could have stayed in the city job and taken the funding quietly? We might have had this already."

You: "Then we decide who we are, Mateo. Not some term sheet."

Mateo: (eyes wet, half-closed) "Who are we, Ari?"

You: (and in that question the personal is a mirror of the political; your answer tastes like salt and inevitability) "People who try not to sell their neighbors. People who build and keep the keys in the community's hands."

Your phone vibrates again. This time it's a call from an unknown number marked 'Investors (Wave Consortium)'. The lab's lights seem to narrow and focus on the single problem in front of you: accept rapid scaling with strings, or refuse and risk slower, precarious progress.

Everything you built up to this—Etta's seedlings, Jun's nets, Mateo's soldered modules, the town's watchful faces—compresses into this single stuttering pulse of choice.

You feel the old guilt like a tide pulling under you: the thought that your hesitation could cost someone their roof, that your insistence could force families out when the next storm comes. Your mouth is dry. Your hands feel oddly foreign.
