[Scene: Municipal Hall | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the council chamber through rain-streaked windows; protest banners sag under drizzle]
[Music: Tense BGM — low, insistent percussion; a high, unresolved string tremor]
[Sound: Distant thunder, the rustle of paper, the soft beep of a tablet notification]

You walk the corridor with the taste of copper at the back of your throat—an old, animal sign of adrenaline. The negotiation memorandum from the lab is folded into your palm like a splintered promise. From Chapter 10 you agreed to bring the town to the table with the firm; now the table is set and the air smells of wet paper and second thoughts.

The municipal chamber is half-full: neighbors in hooded jackets, Etta small and bright in the front row like a knot of roots, Jun leaning against a pillar with salt in the seams of his jacket, Councilor Rosa's pen tapping a rhythm against glass, and Elio Sato's tablet glowing soft and unblinking at the head of the table. Mateo stands near the lab-rep bench, jaw tight, the field notebook clutched like a talisman.

You are the conduit tonight—charged with translating raw anger and fear into legal phrasing, shepherding opposition voices into language that a lawyer might not cut to ribbons. It is a fragile, bureaucratic kind of hope.

Etta stands when the meeting opens. Her voice is smaller than the room needs but steadier than yours feels. You watch her hands—thick with calluses, stained with peat—fold into each other.

Etta: "We have lived with this marsh long enough to know what keeps us. We do not want to be fenced out of our own shoreline under the guise of protection."

Etta's eyes find yours, and for a breath you feel a hundred summers—seed counts, tide charts, a life braided with salt. She sinks back into her seat and slides a sheaf of yellowed maps across the table toward you; not just evidence but a map of who will be left behind if the language is careless.

Elio rises, everything about him precise: posture, cadence, the soft tap of his shoes. His logic is a blade polished till it gleams.

Elio: "The firm will construct barriers quickly. We can prevent major losses. The baseline contract ensures timelines. Time is the enemy here—every season we delay increases the risk to lives and infrastructure."

You sense the room tilt toward efficiency—because efficiency sounds like safety. But the word hides an edge: lives rearranged behind sealed gates, public access reclassified as 'asset management.'

Councilor Rosa (quiet, bureaucratic): "We cannot ignore the urgency. We also cannot abandon our obligations to the community. Elio, can the firm accept covenants that guarantee public access and protections for communal lands?"

Elio: (a slight tightening at the corner of his mouth) "Covenants add legal complexity and slow deployment. Rapid construction—it's a public good. The firm offers relocation assistance and resilience bonds for those who choose to move; it reduces exposure quickly."

Mateo steps forward, and his voice carries as if he were speaking against not just Elio but also the weather.

Mateo: "Rapid fixes are tempting. I've seen the models. But walls move stress downstream and privatize risk. The buoys we're prototyping can be deployed incrementally and work with marsh recovery—if properly funded and if the community controls them."

He looks at you then, not publicly, but long enough that the rest of the room feels like background noise. There is weight in his glance: not accusation exactly, but a plea wrapped in urgency.

Mateo: (low) "If I distance myself from the firm's method now, it's because I can't watch the town give away access in exchange for protection. Not while we can try something else."

Your chest tightens. Mateo's caution pulls at the frayed threads of your certainty and at the private fear you have lived with since storms began to ladder higher each year. He is asking you to choose what kind of safety you want to ask for—and who gets it.

Jun: (harsh, immediate) "People are tired of talk. I don't care what it's called—if a storm takes my boat again and they put up a wall that keeps me from fishing, what am I supposed to do? Sell dirt I don't own? Move inland and work a job I don't have?"

Jun's voice is a stone thrown into the chamber's calm. Several heads turn. You can feel the room's humidity spike with anger.

You open your mouth to respond, and the committee's microphones hum like trapped bees. You know the outcome will hinge on phrasing—on who is permitted to define 'public good' and 'rapid'—but right now the language feels insufficient against the pressure of people's lives.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Midday]
[Visual: Boards and hand-built sills; flagging and transplanted eelgrass bending in a low wind]
[Music: Wind instruments—sharp, urgent—under layered, anxious strings]
[Sound: Water whispering at reeds; the honk of a distant gull]

You meet Etta and Jun out at the flats because you need the marsh in your lungs. The salt and peat are anchors that municipal language cannot replicate.

Etta moves through the plots like a gardener of grief and hope, pointing to a newly silted sill.

Etta: "Those coir logs aren't just engineering, Ari. They're memories we replant. They keep the mud that feeds the eelgrass in place. If the firm builds a solid seawall, the marsh dies a little further down the line."

She looks at you and the look is blunt—practical and maternal all at once.

Etta: "We can write covenants that include these restoration sites as community commons. That way, even if some relocation happens, the land that feeds us stays with us. The law can be a fence or it can be stitching."

Jun: (scornful) "And lawyers cost money. Who pays? We don't have that kind of time—storms won't wait around for us to convene ethics committees."

You think of the firm’s resilience bonds—an offer that sounds like a lifeline but could be strings attached to a corporate anchor. Mateo's voice echoes in your head: overreliance on structural fixes.

Your fingers find the camera in your tote. You lift it, not to take pictures but as if the strap could anchor you to a decision. The estuary is patient and cruel; it will show no preference for legal niceties. Only the texture of the shore beneath your boots will tell you what will survive.

The sun breaks through the clouds for a sharp second, a cut of gold that makes the marsh blades flare like knives. That flash feels obscene in a room that is otherwise settling into the grind of clauses and contingencies. The storm season is nearing; every delay has teeth.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Amber light through steamed panes; seedlings pressing to glass; the scent of citrus and soil]
[Music: Low, insistent piano with an accelerating tremolo]
[Sound: The hiss of the heater, the soft rustle of leaves]

You retreat with Etta to her greenhouse because words said there are softer and truer. Inside, the air is warm and smells like compost and citrus—earth reclaiming a calm that the hall could not afford.

Etta pours tea into a chipped mug and sets it before you. Her hands move with the assurance of someone who has outlived seasons.

Etta: "You have to decide if you're going to push for covenants that bind the town to keep certain lands public. If you do, be ready—Elio will call it obstruction. He will threaten to walk."

She looks at you—plain, fierce.

Etta: "Those threats are not empty. He has options. He can go to the state. He has investors who will wait but not forever. You know that."

Your gratitude for Etta is threaded with guilt—you are, as always, carrying the anxieties of your neighbors like flotsam. You think of Mateo, of the lab's prototypes, of the buoys that might buy you time if they were scaled. You imagine the firm building fast, the town surviving but changed beyond recognition.

Mateo arrives, breathless from the rain outside, and slips in without fanfare. He sits opposite you, palms closed over his field notebook.

Mateo: (soft) "I can't stand up there and sell this without reservations. If the company signs a baseline and starts work next week, there's a real chance the town will be secure in a different sense—secure in being fenced. I don't want to be the one who helped them sell that."

You feel his words as a wedge. He reaches across the table, then withdraws—uncertain how much to press.

Mateo: "Ari, I'm not asking you to choose for me, but I'm asking you to think about who gets to call the shoreline theirs. You know the marsh better than anyone here. Help me—help us—make sure whatever deal we take is anchored in protections."

Etta sips her tea and fixes you with a look that is almost tender.

Etta: "You are very good at building bridges, child. But bridges need foundations. Make sure these foundations include us."

You close your eyes for one long, unnecessary second. Heat from the teacup, the smell of soil in the greenhouse, Mateo's hand hovering near his notebook—the images stack and press like tide layers.

Outside, the wind picks up as if the sea is impatient. Somewhere down the coast, a storm is gathering strength—far enough that most will call it a forecast, close enough that futures are already bending toward risk.

You know the firm will prefer the second option on the table: baseline, rapid deployment. You know Etta and Jun will push for binding covenants and protections. You know Mateo will argue for hybrid tech—but he's scared of being co-opted too. You, meanwhile, are expected to translate all of this into a committee recommendation that will determine whether construction begins with strings or whether the town braces for more waiting.

The greenhouse's steam fogs a narrow portion of the glass. For a moment, everything beyond that fog is imagined—possible futures overlapping like low clouds.

Etta: (firm, final) "You were the one who took us to the lab. You brought hands and data together. Now you need to decide what kind of protection you want to ask for—or whether you'll buy time for scrutiny. That isn't neutral. It's choosing who will be safe and who will have to leave."

The air contracts. Your pulse accelerates in your throat, each beat louder, a metronome marching through your ribcage. The room seems to pulse with the pressure of the approaching season.

You step outside into the damp corridor heading back to municipal hall, feet quick, breath shallow. The chamber will reconvene at dusk to finalize language. This is the crescendo you have been building toward—not a single speech but the cutting of a clause, the placement of a comma that will either protect or erase a life.

You are asked, finally, to present three consolidated options to the committee. Etta looks at you with a plea that translates into every face in the town: protect our lands. Mateo's notebook seems suddenly weighty with possibility and regret. Jun tilts his head and waits. Elio watches with a set jaw; a threat is a tool in his hand and he has trained it well.

Your throat is dry. You can already feel the town's futures pressing against the thin skin of your decision.
