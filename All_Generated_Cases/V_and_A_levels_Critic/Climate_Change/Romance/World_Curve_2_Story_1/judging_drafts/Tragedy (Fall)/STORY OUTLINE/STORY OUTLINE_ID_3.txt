## Visual Novel Title
Tidebridge: After the High Water

---

## World View
Tidebridge is a near-future coastal town perched on the ragged edge of an estuary hammered by rising seas and faster storms. Once-sturdy wooden piers tilt toward glassy tidal pools, and salt-streaked shacks and experimental wave tech sit side by side. A local political web links a hands-on restoration collective, a municipal council stretched thin, and a sustainability firm pushing centralized engineering fixes. Class and culture are visible in clothing and tools: fishers with rope bracelets and weathered jackets, engineers with sensor satchels and neat corporate pins, elders with sea-glass necklaces. Religion is mostly local ritual—memorial tides and harvest markers—less institutional, more civic reverence for the estuary’s cycles. The economy is fractured: small fisheries, community nurseries, and the promise of investment if a technocratic solution is accepted. Geography folds low marsh and the harbor into the plaza and municipal chamber; the nursery’s warm amber glass stands in sharp contrast with the Wave Innovation Lab’s cool clinical LEDs. The mood is painterly and tactile—sea-glass greens, storm greys, sun-faded ochres—hope threaded with fatigue. Political conflict centers on whether to restore and live with the estuary or to erect centralized barriers and manage relocations. Love and loyalty sit in the eye of that conflict: small sacrifices, pragmatic compromises, and the cost of protection.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Ari Tanaka, Tidebridge-born, leading a restoration collective committed to living shorelines and marsh recovery. The town faces a decisive moment when Elio Sato's firm proposes fast, centralized coastal defenses that would stabilize infrastructure but threaten displacement and privatization. Mateo Alvarez, my slow-burning love interest, returns with practical wave-energy buoys and modular protections that could be scaled but require funding and compromise. As storms intensify and municipal votes loom, I must navigate moral choices: preserve place and social fabric, accept technocratic security, or stitch hybrid compromises. Personal and public stakes collide: neighbors' livelihoods, Etta's nursery, Jun's fishing, Councilor Rosa's vote, and Mara's reporting. My guilt and protectiveness push me to act; Mateo's optimism tempts collaboration; Elio's detachment masks trauma. The story is a downward arc—a tragedy of accumulating compromises, betrayals, and necessary losses. Branching decisions determine whether the town resists, capitulates, or fragments. Endings range from painfully fractured stabilization to partial revival to long, aching stewardship; in every outcome the estuary and our connections bear scars. Intimate moments—stroking Mateo’s sensor satchel, pressing Etta’s salt-streaked palms—anchor the climate crisis in romantic consequence.

### Chapter 1: Morning in the Harbor [Fall] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)
* **Summary:** I return to the harbor at dawn, documenting salt-spray evidence and talking with Jun about lost nets. The scene introduces my camera, clothing, and my stubborn sense of responsibility. Small kindnesses—Etta’s nod, Mateo’s distant figure by the Wave Lab—seed the conflict.
* **Visuals:** Tidebridge Harbor; my sun-bleached ash-brown bob, sea-glass streak visible; faded navy field jacket and coral-red camera strap; morning diffuse light catching salt on rope.
* **Valence:** Neutral
* **Arousal:** VeryLow
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I keep my camera slung at my hip, the coral-red strap warm against the patched elbow of my field jacket. The harbor smells of wet rope, diesel, and brine. I crouch by a fractured piling, press the shutter, and the exposure lingers in gray silver—eelgrass torn into ragged flags, a sandbag seam that never quite held. Jun’s voice—flat, practiced—cuts across: “Storm stripped the north haul again.” He’s cropped hair, a rope bracelet knotted like a promise. I hand him a thermos; his hands are salt and callus. Etta appears, white hair in a messy bun, an earth-stained apron, and she gives me the kind of smile that says, Try not to carry everything. I tell myself I’m only documenting, only counting plots for next week’s transplant. Inwardly, though, I inventory faces who can’t move: Mrs. Rhee at the back float, kids who learned to crab on our pier. In the distance, a squat modular lab blinks cold LEDs. Someone—maybe Mateo—works near its edge. I breathe the thin harbor air and decide to keep walking toward the nursery. Option A: keep moving, gather seedlings to show at the meeting. -> Chapter 2

* **Summary:** A municipal meeting is set where Elio’s firm will present a centralized protection plan. I must decide how to position my collective publicly: refuse any compromise, negotiate a hybrid with Mateo, or push for immediate municipal acceptance of Elio’s project to secure vital funding—each choice forces different alliances and consequences.
* **Visuals:** Municipal Hall & Plaza (wet plaza, protest banners, my field jacket contrasted with Elio’s charcoal trench coat glimpsed in the crowd); Mateo fidgeting with a sensor, Elio’s polished badge glimpsed across the room.
* **Valence:** Negative
* **Arousal:** Mid

* **MAJOR DECISION (Logic Hub):**
* **The Choice:** How do I shape the collective's stance at the council hearing?
* **Option A:** "Reject the corporate plan and rally for living shorelines only." -> **Consequence:** Public resistance, mobilizes community but risks losing funds and legal pressure from developers. -> **Jumps to:** Chapter 4

I sit at the council chamber edge as rain taps the high windows, and everything looks smaller from here: council table, polymer microphones, a line of damp people with placards. Elio’s steel-gray eyes sweep the room like a modeler surveying a map; his trench coat is immaculate even with salt streaks, his matte lanyard glows faintly. I think of the nursery—warm amber glass—and of Etta’s hands. I think of Mateo’s buoy prototype, the hopeful tremor in his voice. I imagine telling Jun we can hold the pier. I can see three futures and none of them are clean. If I refuse Elio outright, I’ll rally people who have already lost roofs and work; we’ll likely be demonized in reports and lose technical grants. If I propose a hybrid pilot, I risk tying our grassroots plan to engineering we barely control. If I push for a municipal acceptance, I accept a path that might displace neighbors for the sake of infrastructure. My chest hurts. The council clerk asks for a single sentence from the collective. The choice feels like choosing which friends to hand a lifeboat. Option A, B, or C?

We march with placards that flap in drizzle. My voice is hoarse by the time we reach the plaza; Etta stands at the front like an old lighthouse, small and luminous. I feel the familiar work-stiff in my shoulders—planning grids, flagging plots, stacking coir logs. Mateo is beside me—his curls windblown—quietly wiring a sensor to a demo buoy we drag along. He keeps offering compromises: grant language that keeps local control, a governance clause. I want to believe him. I also watch Elio’s black trench coat step past municipal doors; his steel-gray eyes meet mine, analytical and polite. He gestures at his tablet and the crowd’s faces; someone shouts betrayal. I lead chants, hold the banner while rain runs black down the fabric, and I imagine a storm that takes everything despite our best lines of sod. Still, we stand.

* **Valence:** Negative
* **Arousal:** High

* **MAJOR DECISION (Logic Hub):**
* **The Choice:** Which operational strategy does the collective adopt?

* **Option B:** "Commit solely to living shorelines—no outside tech." -> **Consequence:** Pure local control but slower results and limited funding. -> **Jumps to:** Chapter 6

I stare at the topsoil in my hands: damp, dark, full of worms. Each option feels like sacrificing a piece of the town I swore to protect. Mateo’s voice is low, careful: he sketches a governance model in his field notebook, the lines of tide charts and community bylaws overlapping. He says, “We can keep control—if we set rules, shared ownership.” Etta’s hazel eyes are wet; she’s wearing the sea-glass necklace she’s had since before my childhood. Jun’s jaw is tight—he wants speed, he wants fish and nets. I feel my old reflex: keep the town intact, even if I must swallow pride. But I also fear the corporate tightening that comes with external contracts. My internal guilt spikes—if anything goes wrong, I’ll own the blame. The rain picks up on the flats, and I fold the seedlings into the coir log pockets and make a decision.

* **Valence:** VeryNegative
* **Arousal:** High
* **Branch:** Route A

The tide took more than the night before—it took our optimism. I stand knee-deep in a smear of peat and broken coir logs, my camera useless with a cracked lens cap, the coral-red strap spattered with mud. Mateo’s buoy lies half-submerged, its circuitry fried; his quick-dry shirt is smeared with grit and his woven bracelet gone. People speak in sharp, small sentences: we lost dock three, Mrs. Rhee’s storage shed is gone, the nursery’s bed of seedlings collapsed under a surge. Mara’s recorder catches the town’s grief; Councilor Rosa makes promises that sound like paper. I had wanted to protect people in place; now they look at me with the fatigue of those who risked and lost. Mateo and I stand on broken boards and try to sort what’s salvageable. He reaches for my hand—his fingers cold. I want to fix it, to apologize and to say we can rebuild better, but I feel the tug of blame like a rope around my ribs. For the first time in years, I know our choices made a difference and caused harm. The town endures, but something vital has been broken.

### Chapter 15: Broadcast and Reckoning (Route B / Route C Choice) [Fall] (Branch: Additional / Convergent Ending)

The audio file goes live and the room fills with the sound of a tide I helped lift. Mara’s recorder hums in my palm like an accusation. The leaks show redlines, cross-subsidies, and modeling that prioritized investor return over community tenure. Public fury is immediate; protesters swarm the lab and city hall; lawsuits pile on; investors withdraw. The firm retracts certain offers; conservative pockets of funding vanish. In the scramble, some families who might have been helped lose the chance—money contracts dissolved, projects frozen. Mateo stands beside me, his face a small, exhausted map of regret. We thought exposing truth would force humane reform; instead it unleashes economic panic that accelerates forced relocations. I see neighbors leaving in trucks, Etta’s greenhouse boxes packed into a van that asks to be careful. I pressed the publish button thinking I was anchoring justice. Now I hold the aftermath—names, faces, the cost written on closing doors. My hands shake. We revealed a truth; the estuary survived in parts, but we paid with the people we swore to protect.
