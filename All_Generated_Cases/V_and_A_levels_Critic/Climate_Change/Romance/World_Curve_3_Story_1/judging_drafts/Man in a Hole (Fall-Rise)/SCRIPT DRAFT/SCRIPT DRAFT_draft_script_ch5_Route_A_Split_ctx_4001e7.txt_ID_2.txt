[Scene: Salt Marsh Reserve | Dawn — hours after the surge]
[Visual: Mud-slick reeds bowed low; a thin, cold light roils on puddles. The restored stretch of marsh shows torn root-mats and ragged gaps where soil has been scoured away.]
[Music: Rapid, insistent percussion under a low sustained synth]
[Sound: Distant camera shutters, a generator humming, the soft, relentless drip of seawater from tarps]

You come up to the flank of the damage on two knees, breath leaving white in the humid air. The pilot swath — the line of bundled reed mats and planted cordgrass you helped stake months ago — hangs in tatters like a frayed rope. Where the living substrate should have held, the tide found a seam and widened it until a family's front stoop is a cantilever of cracked planks over empty, black mud.

Your palms smell of salt and diesel; mud clings to your sleeves and the scuff of your boots is a new map on the dock planks. A child's plastic bucket lies half-filled with brine and the neck of a small sneaker pokes from the mud, a reminder that policy becomes ruin or sanctuary at this scale.

Nora kneels opposite you, jaw clenched, hands moving with mechanical calm as she ties a temporary binder to a stake. Her hair is streaked with green algae; her hands carry the gouges of repair. She doesn't look up as you approach but you can feel the tremor in the way she presses the zip tie tight — not the same as fear, but the quick muscle of someone who has learned to make consequences smaller.

Nora: "It punched right through the seams. The underlay gave out where the tide scoured. I can see the shear lines." (She points. Her fingers are steady but the words are hard.)

You: "How far? Is the family's foundation compromised beyond a temporary brace?"

Nora: "The stoop—it's settled. Could snap in the next swell. We can shore it with pilings tonight, but—" (She cuts herself off, looks at you.) "We need heavy timber, and not just volunteers. This is structural."

Samir is ten feet away, his boots planted like a slow metronome in the mud. He moves with the old certainty of tides and tides of memory — lifting a tarp, setting a rope, speaking in short, exact phrases.

Samir: "We shore it, we hold it. That girl's father—he'll not sleep while that stoop hangs. We patch until the coffers sing." (He looks at you, then at the stoop, then at the marsh.) "But we also remember pilot before. We remember promises that came in paper boats."

His voice is not accusation. It's a ledger.

You feel the ledger closing on you. The pilot you pitched, the plans you shepherded — whatever their exact chain of custody — they are now measured in the distance between a threshold and the tide. You do not flinch; the adrenaline sharpens focus. This is not the time for moral theatricality. It is a crisis that needs steps, and you are the one everyone waits to call them.

[Visual: A camera lens focuses; a microphone foam puffed with a reporter's breath catches a few feet away. The hum of a news van idling grows louder as two journalists fan out with a clipboard between them.]
[Sound: Reporter murmurs; a distant city official's shoes on the boardwalk]

A tall woman in a suit — a municipal aide, you think — walks up with a tablet and a practiced neutrality. Behind her, a crisp security detail that might belong to a consultancy or a corporate communications firm keeps an efficient perimeter. They set up a small tripod and a camera aimed at the stoop. The presence is a cold, clinical punctuation to the mud and plantings.

Before you can steady the volunteer crew into a plan, Luca arrives through the reeds, boots sloshing, eyes wide and fixed. He breathes like someone running toward a boat bell. When he sees the stoop he goes silent. His hand finds yours without asking.

Luca: (low, urgent) "They taped off the south line, but these weak seams—Maya, we told the Council it'd be careful. Do you—" (He doesn't finish.)

You feel his grip, firm and steady. The moment is intimate and immediate in a way that complicates everything: your chest tightens because he trusts you to hold decisions, and because holding them together has been the only thing that made this job human.

You: "We stabilize the stoop now. Nora, get the pilings manifest and the winch. Samir, call for timber from the dock—anything that can take a load. Luca, keep volunteers back until we've set anchors."

Luca: "I'm on crowd. But—" (He looks toward the news van.) "They'll want sound bites."

You: "Then we give them the facts. No grandstanding. No promises we can't keep."

Your voice does not waver. Inside, a current of guilt tightens — a small, cold coil that reminds you how easily pilot projects turn into headlines and then into blame. But the tide will not wait for introspection. The stoop must be saved.

[Scene: Old Docks Community Hub | Mid-morning — the Hub is fuller now; the community gathers to watch footage and swap stories]
[Visual: Under the warehouse's corrugated roof, strings of solar lamps cast warm pools. Chalk notices flinch in the breeze. Community maps are spread across a workbench, coffee steaming in a dented pot. Volunteers arrive with tarps and timber.]
[Music: Tighter strings, heartbeat percussion; the tempo speeds]
[Sound: Murmured accusations, the scrape of chairs, a child's distant wail]

You bring the stoop's immediate stabilization plan to the Hub because that's where the people will want to speak and be spoken to. The news vans have parked outside and their screens glow through the open doors. Councilwoman Reyes's aide left word that the Councilwoman would stop by "to hear concerns." The notice carries the neutral weight of official intent.

When you enter, conversation sharpens like a pulled wire. Mrs. Delgado, whose stoop is adjacent to the affected family, is there with a towel wrapped around her shoulders; she holds a photograph of the stoop before the surge as if it were evidence in a trial. A small cluster of neighbors form a ring of low, hot voices — someone mentions "pilot" like a curse.

Neighbor 1: "We trusted them. We watered and planted. Now what? Where's the money you promised?"

Neighbor 2: "Promises? She told me they'd never have to move!"

The blame is precise, landing on the concept of "pilot" and whatever name is easiest to aim for: you. Your chest collects accusations like rain collects in a pail.

You: "We organized this together. The intention was to protect the shoreline while maintaining people's homes. We will cover repairs—" (You feel the room tilt as voices rise.)

Mrs. Delgado: "Covers repairs? What about when it doesn't hold next month? Or the month after? You ran experiments on our doors!"

Your throat tightens. This is the repeating ache that haunts you: a pilot that once failed and then came back into headlines. Your past pilot's underfunded repairs — an unarticulated memory — tugs like a hem at the back of your mind. You cannot know how everyone remembers the old mistakes, only that they do.

Luca steps into the cluster, voice low, trying to modulate the pitch of the room.

Luca: "We rebuild. We do it with people here—not for them. We'll show receipts. We'll organize shifts. This is what we do when things break."

Neighbor 3: (sarcastic) "Organize shifts won't buy concrete."

Nora: "It won't buy the engineered fix either. But it will buy something that keeps people where they are."

The exchange is a push-and-pull; everyone wants solutions that echo their sense of dignity. The conversation grows fractious. You find yourself answering the same questions in different measures, trying to anchor the debate back to practical steps — timber lists, volunteer rosters, crowd-funding pages — but the air tastes of accusation.

The Hub's door opens and a new presence fills the space. Councilwoman Reyes steps in, composed and measured, flanked by two aides and a security officer. She wears her silver-streaked bun like an anchor; her suit is the color of damp concrete. Her gaze slides across the room and lands on you with the clinical interest of someone balancing votes.

Councilwoman Reyes: "Ms. Ortega. I appreciate you bringing people here and acting quickly. The city needs a clear account of the damage and a plan for immediate safety measures. Will you provide one by this evening?"

You: "Yes. We'll have a stabilization and a repair estimate within hours. We'll work with municipal inspectors for safety compliance."

Councilwoman Reyes: (tilts her head) "There are also questions about whether the pilot had contingencies for failure, and if private stakeholders were consulted in ways that weren't transparent. I need a full accounting."

You: "We'll make all documentation available. Transparency is—"

Councilwoman Reyes: (interrupting gently) "—important. Ms. Ortega, I also want to ask that you consider partnership options. If this vulnerability is structural, we need resources at scale."

Her suggestion is not an overt command; it is a political framing. Partnership with the city or with well-funded entities can buy speed and materials. But you can hear the subtext: scale often comes with strings.

You: "Partnership requires community oversight. No one decision about people's homes should be made without them."

Councilwoman Reyes: (measured) "I'm aware of your position. I'm not asking you to yield oversight. I'm asking if you'll accept city assistance with binding oversight agreements in place."

You study her face — competent, political, not openly hostile. It's an invitation in the language of governance: mitigation in exchange for concessions to speed.

[Scene: Tideside Boardwalk (the Promenade) | Late Morning — public briefing by Ari's team]
[Visual: The boardwalk is cordoned; TV crews stand on platforms and the skyline is a flat, metallic smear beyond the rippled bay. Ari's team has set up a small podium with the consultancy's logo discreetly on the lectern.]
[Music: Staccato electronic beats, rapidly building tension]
[Sound: Microphone feedback, the murmur of a gathered crowd, gulls crying distantly]

You follow the line of people to the Tideside because press statements are being made there — and because Ari is likely to be circled by cameras. The promotors of the engineered solution tend to like stage-managed optics, and today the optics will shape the narrative.

Ari stands at the podium, shoulders squared in a floodline blazer that gleams faintly under the light. They speak with the precision of someone used to metrics and to translating risk into projections.

Ari: "What happened here was regrettable. We deployed an experimental restoration that followed the best available models. However, last night's surge exceeded projected extreme event thresholds. While our models had a probability curve for such an outcome, any system can fail under unpredicted stress."

Ari's voice is smooth; the phrasing is careful. They look at you as they speak — their expression pinned between professional concern and a cool, almost unreadable curiosity. You cannot tell if the look is apology, calculation, or an invitation.

You: "Ari—" (you step forward) "You must acknowledge the human consequence. Families nearly lost doorways. Models are not the same as people."

Ari: (measured) "Agreed. Which is why we're proposing a hybrid. Hardline structures where risk concentrates, living elements elsewhere. It's the only way to ensure scalability across the city."

A murmur runs through the crowd. Some faces register relief at the promise of a hard fix; others register fear of displacement.

Reporter: "Does your proposal include relocation or eminent domain clauses for affected neighborhoods?"

Ari: "No. Any implementation would proceed with municipal approval and community consultation."

The tentative "would" hangs heavy. Words about consultation sound like safety but can be a gauze over displacement.

Councilwoman Reyes steps up beside Ari, pronouncing the city's stance with the kind of procedural calm that reads as decisive.

Councilwoman Reyes: "We will not displace residents without due process. But with finite resources and increased risk, we must consider all options on the table."

You feel the room pivot. The city's need for a visible solution — a ribbon to be cut and photographed — is pulling like a tide. Ari's competence promises speed; the Councilwoman's neutrality promises legitimacy. Both answers are politically persuasive.

Luca's voice is in your ear, low and hard.

Luca: "They're saying 'consultation,' but we've all seen how that becomes a box to check."

You: "I know."

Nora pulls at your sleeve, eyes bright.

Nora: "They've got a brief on the wire now. Ari's team already put out a statement that frames this as an 'anomalous event' rather than a design failure. People are already tweeting it."

Samir stands a short distance away, watching the exchange with the same patient sorrow you see in tidal lines.

Samir: "When they speak of models, remember they can be braced against markets. When we speak of houses, we speak of people."

The press battery intensifies: camera shutters sync into a single, anxious staccato. Voices overlap until the sound is a tide of words. Your heartbeat picks up and your thoughts narrow.

You have three places to plant your answer: rally neighbors to rebuild and control the narrative from the ground; negotiate a formal partnership to secure funding and oversight; or take the risk of chasing the truth of hidden influence and force scrutiny that could both protect and fracture the community.

The urgency is absolute. Each path will ripple outward and define the neighborhood's future — not just as infrastructure, but as a political and emotional reality.

Your chest tightens into a decision-shape. You can feel the arc of the day bending toward whichever word you will speak.
