You choose: whether by charting supplies or stepping into the mud, you are here. The marsh takes you in — cold, muddy, insisting. Luca: "You tie it like you mean it." (A half-praise, half-tease — his eyes on your hands, then back to the water.) "You always did. Remember when we used to knot nets for crab traps? You were better then and you're better now."

Nora moves between crews, offering tools, bandaging knuckles, reminding volunteers where to plant the reeds so sediment will catch. She passes you a damp rag without breaking stride.

Nora: "City matched half the shortfall. It came in last night — Councilwoman Reyes called. Not all of it, but enough for materials and a pair of shifts on the coir orders." (Her voice is quick, relieved.) "We had donors cover the rest. People came through."

Relief lifts and drops at once; a municipal match feels like validation and also like a reminder that this was never just about you. It is about a web of people who chose to invest sweat and money in the living edge of a neighborhood.

There's an urgency to the day that rides the tide. The weather reports whine in the background — a swell scheduled for tomorrow — and everyone moves like they know a test is coming. You smell tar, wood smoke, and sweat; your skin prickles with the salt of a hard, righteous afternoon.

Samir: "We make a thing that can be kept. Not a wall to lock people out, but a handhold." (He taps a plank that's been signed by kids and elders.) "That's who you are, Maya. You build with people."

You feel that as much as hear it: the world they are building is not simply physical. It's trust stitched into place, procedures taught and kept, the filial rituals that Samir insists on — blessing the first post, sharing a fish stew at noon, naming the shoreline segments after elders who used to fish there.

Luca arrives with his jacket half zipping off, his hair wild in the salt wind. He moves through the site like someone who knows where everyone belongs, handing tools and earning a grin from each face he passes.

Luca: "Hey, look at this." (He holds out a small black velvet pouch.) "I was… thinking. This thing has been a tide-marker for my family for years. When the marker reached the top of the pole, we knew to pull the boats in." (He shrugs in an embarrassed way.) "I want you to have it."

Your hands stop mid-hammer. The world narrows until the only sound is your own breathing and the distant slap of the marsh. Giving this pendant is a quiet, heavy offering — not just affection but trust that anchors something larger.

You open the pouch. Inside, a tiny silver pendant shaped like a tide marker, worn smooth at the edges. It feels like a small, decisive boat on a storm of feeling.

[Scene: Tideside (the Boardwalk) | Night — Storm Test]
[Visual: Black water, bright repair beacons, strings of community lanterns bobbing like determined stars; wind claws at the tarps but the bulkheads hold.]
[Music: Crescendoed percussion and choir-like brass — very high energy, cathartic]
[Sound: The sea thundering, rope strains creaking, voices shouting over the roar]

The storm arrives like a verdict. You can taste metal and ozone; your jacket slaps your ribs with every gust. Volunteers who stayed through the night stand shoulder-to-shoulder on the boardwalk, hands linked to line, voices raw with effort. The coir logs strain in their brackets, reed beds bend and catch silt, small people-built bulkheads shudder but do not fail.

You feel the moment: it is everything you chose. The municipal match paid for material; the donors paid for a second shift; the neighborhood's rituals had stitched permission and pride into every board. But none of that guarantees success — only effort does. Your chest thrums like the drums the kids beat to keep time.

Nora, damp and smiling with exhaustion, passes you a thermos.

Nora: "You were right to bring them here, to teach them how. Keep shifting the load to the base. Don't look at the wave; look at the flange. Trust the knots."

You look at the shoreline as the highest wave breaks. Time stretches into a single, explosive arc. The bulkheads groan but the coir reefs displace the worst of it. Sediment plumes cloud the water, then begin to settle behind the traps. A cheer rises — not a triumphant shout but a wet, astonishing sound of something saved.

Samir: (his voice cuts through the storm) "Hold! Hold! This is what I wanted to show you. This is what we can do together!"

Luca's hand finds yours in the chaos — a physical, fierce thing. You squeeze back. For a heartbeat you both are a tether between frantic labor and a fragile victory.

Luca: "You did this." (He brays a laugh that is half sob.) "You made them care enough to work. This is… ours."

You want to speak in policy terms, to name the metrics, but there are no graphs in the roar. There is only a chorus of hands and the smell of wet wood. Your throat tightens; tears prick hot and salt-laced.

You think of everyone who came: the retired carpenters, the teenagers with bruised knuckles, the parents who left casseroles, the donors who sent a check without asking for credit. You think of Samir's slow navigation of tradition and Nora's pragmatic, blistered courage. You think of the municipal clerk who pushed the match through at the eleventh hour because someone finally convinced them that people matter.

When the water calms, the living shoreline shows itself not as a shiny solution but as a living seam: mud caught, reeds standing where there was only open gray, small fish stirring in its new shallows. People collapse into tired laughter and exhausted embraces. The boardwalk smells of wet earth and victory.

Samir comes to stand beside you, his hand on your shoulder like an old mooring line.

Samir: "Your name will be on the board. Not because you did this alone, but because you kept saying we could." (He looks at you, a small, fierce pride.) "You kept us together."

The room feels impossibly full. Luca leans into you, close enough that you can hear him breathe.

Luca: "I gave you the pendant because I wanted to be practical about my feelings. I wanted to make them into something you can wear while you do the impossible." (He grins through the rain.) "Promise me you'll keep it close."

Maya (You): "I promise." (You mean it — to the work, to the people, to him.) "I'll keep it close because it reminds me why we build."

There is a hush then, a slow swell of applause as the community acknowledges the success and the fragility both. People begin to sing the old marsh song Samir taught them — a low melodic line that sounds like reeds bending in wind. The song is about tides and memory and the stubbornness of roots.

Maya (You): "We built a pattern. That’s what matters. It will be messy. It will require patience and repair. But people know how to do messy now." (You let out a breath that is almost a laugh.)
