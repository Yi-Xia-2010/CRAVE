Luca Chen: "Tell me if I'm being sentimental, but I think if you open with Samir's story about the herring beds, the room will breathe. People respond to what a place is, not to a line graph."

Maya Ortega: "We lead with the people, then hit them with the numbers. Make them feel what they'll lose, then show them why the marsh can be fixed without a hammer. Nora, you get the index slide so it's readable from across the chamber. Luca, you'll call Samir next."

Luca: (half-joking, half-solemn) "You know I keep my speeches untidy on purpose."

Nora: (soft) "And I keep the math clean so it can't be slandered."

You step outside for a breath that is thin and salt-scented. Around the corner, a projection livestream flickers on an improvised screen — the Municipal Green Atrium feed. Councilwoman Reyes sits at a long table, her slate of proposals stern on the surface. The atrium looks clinical on the feed: cool light, neat planters, testable outcomes.

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: (on the livestream) "The city faces a swift window to act. We must evaluate scalable protections in terms of population served, cost per household, and projected resilience metrics."

Her tone is measured, the push-pull of politics in every syllable.

Ari Nakamura steps forward in the video, impeccably composed. He speaks with the calm of someone who has rewritten a hundred such rooms.

Ari Nakamura: (on the livestream) "Our design offers a continuous shield and an urban canopy that functions as both protection and amenity. Paired with our maintenance endowment, we can deploy at scale within two years."

You listen. The sentence is polished. The underbelly — what 'deploy at scale' will require from neighborhood footprints and tenure arrangements — is not unpacked. A slide advances on the stream: glossy aerials with schematic shading along the shoreline. In a corner, a timeline blinks: "Phase 2 — community relocation zones (contingent)."

Someone in the Hub makes a low sound that could be a curse or a prayer.

Samir stands when the phrase appears on screen. His hands are steady, but his voice cracks when he speaks.

Samir Qureshi: "They use words like 'contingent.' They use charts and call it progress. When I was your age, we called them promises. Promises become fences. We have memories in that mud."

The room leans on his sentence. His presence shifts the atmosphere from anxious to raw.

Maya Ortega: "If they recommend relocation, we demand specificity. We demand covenants that protect tenancy and guarantee returns. We ask for binding maintenance funds for our living systems, not just a check and a timeline that evaporates."

Luca: "We also show them the numbers that matter — nursery survival, wave attenuation per meter of marsh restored. Show them stewardship schedules so 'maintenance' isn't a footnote."

Nora: (quiet, fierce) "And we highlight how often top-down plans ignore microhabitats — the ones that keep fish returning. Replace 'contingent' with real commitments, or it's just a plan for displacement."

Your chest tightens; your hands remember the feel of a microphone and the way a community looks at a single person when the stakes are communal. You rehearse a line — part story, part numbers — but your practiced sentences feel brittle under the hush.

The feed cuts to Ari again. He answers a question about scale.

Ari Nakamura: (on the livestream) "We can secure funds and manage a maintenance endowment. Where community stewardship is feasible, we incorporate it. Where structural certainty is required, infrastructure must be decisive."

His face is unreadable on the screen — polite, composed. If he were in the Hub, you'd describe his expression as complex. Here, behind glass and light, it flattens into a certainty that grates against your own, more anxious calculus.

Councilwoman Reyes: (on the livestream) "We need both compassion and accountability. The Board will weigh public safety against community integrity. Your submissions will be considered."

Her words land like a verdict pending.

Samir sits quietly, the cords of his neck visible as he says nothing for a beat. When he speaks again, it's low, deliberate.

Samir Qureshi: "They keep speaking of 'scalability' like it's a moral virtue. But the shore is not only measured in meters. It's measured in lunches, in fishing lines, in the names our children learn. You have to show them that loss will be more than a statistic."

You realize that the hearing is already shaping opinion, that the promise of 'decisive infrastructure' is seductive precisely because it is clean and final. The Hub, with its frayed banners and volunteer schedules, is not photogenic in a way that conjures grant committees.

Your pulse rises, but not to panic — to a steady, mobilized heat. The arousal builds along a normal, urgent curve: prep, rally, respond. This is the midlevel intensity of work that will not stop until someone gives the right answer.

Nora leans close, whispering.

Nora Díaz: "There will be questions about feasibility. There will be attempts to paint maintenance as 'community capacity' instead of a funded obligation. Have the maintenance covenant language ready."

You thumb to the clause in your notebook: "Binding maintenance endowment; priority tenancy protections; phased design tied to community metrics." The words taste like the only weapon you can afford right now: clarity.

Luca meets your gaze. He squeezes your hand, a brief contact that says, I will stand with you, even if it gets ugly.

Luca Chen: "When they ask about relocation, don't dance. Call it what it is if it's on the table. Make Council see the human ledger."

You straighten, feeling the weight of expectations and the tug of potential failure. Outside, stormlight skitters across the water like a warning. Inside, the Hub feels very small and very loud in your chest.

The livestream ticks into a pause for public comment. Your name is up next.

You breathe in salt and diesel and every small marker of home, and prepare to speak for a neighborhood that has always had to make do without being invited to decide the terms.

You stand.
