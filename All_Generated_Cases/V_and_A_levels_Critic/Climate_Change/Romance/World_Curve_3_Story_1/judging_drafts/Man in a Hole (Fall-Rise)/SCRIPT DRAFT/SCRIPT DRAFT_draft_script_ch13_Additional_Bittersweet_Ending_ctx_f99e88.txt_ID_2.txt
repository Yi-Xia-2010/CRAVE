You let out a breath that tastes like relief and something thorny — grief in the shape of possibility. The chamber is a crucible: it will turn your small, stubborn hopes into a larger apparatus that may save many, at the price of some old ways.

Maya Ortega: "We don't get the same world back. We get a safer one with losses we must honor. We built the covenant so those losses won't be erased without consent. We built the stewardship fund so the marsh — and its rituals — have a fighting chance."

Ari arrives not in a suit but in a clean jacket, his cuff quiet. He stands a few paces away, giving the scene the distance he often needs, but he doesn't leave. When he speaks to you, it is not about contracts.

You think about rituals: Samir's stories, the Sunday market, the braided grass. Some survive in pieces — apprenticed youth who still braid, relocated elders who come back for festivals funded under the Accord. Some cannot be fully recovered. The trade is bitter and real and full of meaning.

Ari moves beside you, the space between speaking and not speaking full of the things you both will not speak aloud this evening. He looks at you with a clarity that is not romantic flourish but the exactness of someone who knows the parameters of what you just built.

You place your other hand over Luca's, then Ari's across the table of your life. The gesture is public, oddly ceremonial, and private all at once.

You feel the acordant weight of your choices settle — not like a verdict, but like a work that will need tending daily. You traded private rituals for a scaffold that promised safety for many. The trade is painful and right.
