Councilwoman Reyes: "Maya, what guarantees will prevent the zero-sum tradeoffs we've seen before? How will this protect the intangible things — the festivals, the boat rituals, the houses that never had capital to begin with?"

You can see, in her gaze, the calculus of re-election and the real ache of responsibility. You do not have neat answers for everything. There are costs you cannot undo. But you have a plan that locks in oversight.

Maya Ortega: "We codify stewardship as a condition of funding. We place trustees drawn from the neighborhood on the maintenance board. We get the trust fund disbursed before construction begins so that relocation is not left to charity. And we put binding clauses in the covenants that prevent speculative buyouts for a generation."

A voice from the back — a city planner — asks about timelines. The room accelerates, words building on one another, momentum like wind pushing a storm surge.

Ari: (softly, into your ear) "This will bring resources to places that had none. We can scale this. We can save lives."

Ari looks at you like he's calculating probabilities and longing at the same time.

Ari Nakamura: "Good. We'll write it into the memorandum."
