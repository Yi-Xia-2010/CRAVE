The hub smells of coffee, diesel, wet cloth — all the smells of repair and living. Strings of solar bulbs droop between posts, muffling the late sun into a forgiving glow. Inside, benches are stained with varnish and paint; a chalkboard lists the week's repairs and a thin column labeled "Needs: funding, volunteers" in your own handwriting.

Luca sees you first and grins like a wave breaking. "Maya," he calls, voice rising in the hush. "You're finally back on our side of the water."

Nora looks up from a bench where half a buoy and a spool of twine are open like a quiet puzzle. She wipes her hands on overalls and queues a smirk that means she's got news. "Council schedules us for Reyes's hearing next week," she says before you've even taken off your jacket. "You were on the receiving end of that vote, right? Thought you'd missed a shot."

You: "Thought?" (You let the word linger. It's easier to test the surface than the depth of what you mean.) "I missed a lot the last time."

Luca: (softening) "You've been carrying all of that on your own, M — on all of us. Come sit. Samir's over there with the tide maps."

[Sound: The creak of a chair as you sit; the smell of strong tea]

Samir looks up and his face folds into the kind expression that comes from a lifetime reading the water. He doesn't offer platitudes. Instead he taps the map with a stubbed finger. "Storms have their own memory," he says, voice like rope. "They come back in the same pattern if you leave them room."

Nora: "There's a municipal grant on the table, too. Big number. It could fund repairs, community training, buy materials for more living shorelines." She leans forward. "But Ari's firm — they're pitching a flagship seawall. Reyes's office is listening personally."

You feel the shape of the choice in the air as if it were a turning wind: the grant could pay for your work without selling out, but Ari's seawall is a polished, faster-acting promise that dazzles the Council. Both routes hum with possibility and risk.

Luca: (low, fierce) "If they build the seawall the way Ari wants? Some of the docks will be raised. People could be pushed out of places they've lived forever. Businesses will have to relocate. It's not just concrete — it's culture."

Ari's name lands differently in the hub than a neutral noun. It carries the gloss of the atrium and the precise hum of the AR cuff you once saw on him in class. There's a memory you can't unscroll: Ari explaining load tolerances as if reciting a poem, the same hands that sketching feasible prototypes also hand-delivering investor decks. You remember his certainty. You also remember how the pilot you led failed under time pressure and underfunding, and how that failure tasted like postponed trust.

You: "What does this grant actually require?" (You fold yourself into the bench, notebook open to a fresh page. Asking concrete questions keeps the room from floating into abstraction.)

Nora: (flipping through a printed memo) "Match funding, a sustainability plan, community stewardship clauses — the usual. But the grant committee likes scalable outcomes and measurable metrics." She coughs, then adds, "Ari's seawall checks every box for scale and metrics. He even has a projection model ready."

Luca: "Metrics don't show the market for moonlit nets or Samir's boat rent. They don't measure what people lose when their shoreline is annexed."

Samir: (quiet) "They measure what cities count. They don't always count what matters."

A pause falls across your group. The hub's soundscape folds into the pause: a radio clacking softly, a distant bell. The storm-glow gathers beyond the warehouse frame—light shifting, the clouds acquiring a silver edge that makes the river look like a sheet of pewter.
