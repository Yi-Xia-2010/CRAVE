[Visual: A patchwork of rooftop beds under a low, storm-washed sky; string lights halo puddles on reclaimed pallets. A battered wooden table sits at the center, seed tags fluttering, a spread of tiny habitat models and soldered circuits scattered across its grain.]
[Sound: Wind combing the reed beds, distant gulls calling once, then silence between breaths.]
[Music: Quickening, bright strings with a threaded percussion — urgent and hopeful]

You still feel the lighthouse in your bones: the lamp-room wind, the cold metronome of the sea. The conditional pledge sits against your ribs like the copper barometer you never take off. It kept the worst at bay; it did not erase the gnawing clause you found in the fine print.

On the rooftop, under sodium string-lamps and the damp smell of peat and salt, Evan lifts a miniature platform with gloved fingers. Tiny reed-plugs stand like miniature sentinels. The prototype is earnest and imperfect: reclaimed floatation foam, woven reed buckets, coils of solar filament. It hums with possibility and with the fragile arithmetic of promises.

You set your hands on the worn table. Your palms smell of compost and solder. The town is a handful of names and breakfasts: Captain Reyes’s muddy boots at dawn, Tala’s riot of hair and flyers, the market stall where Mrs. Hargreaves pries open the day’s catch. They are people with mouths to feed and porches that cannot be insured. Each clause in the contract is a voice you cannot forget.

Evan: "The balance is here." (He taps the model.) "If we raise the substrate by thirty centimeters and seed with Spartina, the root-mass should counter the edge erosion the developers want to fill."

You want to say: show me the guarantee. Instead you say the thing that thins the air between you two.

You: "I read the clause again this afternoon. Fringe marsh parcel — they still earmark it for engineered foundation if the first quarter jobs target isn't met."

Evan's jaw tightens; his fingers linger on the model as if the wood will slide away. He breathes out, measured.

Evan: "Irene said 'cannot wait.' She means investors, not the marsh. Not people who have lived in the marsh. There are funds on the table that evaporate if signatures don't happen this week."

His voice is low. His face is the kind you can read like a ledger — every small tremor annotated. The rooftop smells of wet nylon and the electric tang of solder.

You: "So if we stall for better protections, families might lose work. If we sign, the marsh may be 'protected' on paper but hollowed in practice."

Evan: "We can build a pilot, publicize it fast. Make the pilot impossible to ignore. If it performs, Irene has to answer to this town — not just to investors."

You: "Or we overpromise and fail. Or she uses a good demo as cover and slips the fill through when attention turns."

Evan's hand moves, thumb rubbing his wristband — the little solar band that maps microclimate. He looks at you, and for a fraction longer than either of you planned, the rooftop shrinks to the two of you standing over a table of dreams and calculations.

Evan: (soft) "You sound like Dr. Sora before she had the data: cautious but trying to keep the faith. You sound like Captain Reyes when he remembers the shoreline in '83."

You: "I sound like someone who can't sleep."

Evan doesn't answer right away. His fingers, tool-scarred and warm, find yours across the table and hold on just long enough to make thinking both easier and more dangerous.

[Visual: Close-up on your joined hands; the skin is still gritty with peat; a string of water beads along Evan's knuckle.]
[Sound: A single, held note in the strings — then percussion picks up pace.]

The rooftop is full of small noises: the soft drip off a reclaimed gutter, the tick of a cooling solder joint. Your phone vibrates against the ledger of circuit boards. Tala.

You pick it up with the illusion of privacy and put the phone to your ear.

Tala: (breathless) "Maya — quick. Talk to me. I was down at the pier, and some of the fishers—"

Her words tumble. You can hear the harbor behind her: muffled voices, the slap of canvas. She inhales a blue-raw breath of urgency.

Tala: "—some of them favor the harbor jobs, Maya. Not all, but enough to sway the council if Irene pushes. They need bread now. People are scared."

You feel the surge — the tide of pressure pulling outward, a distant, rising drag toward the decision you must make.

You: "How many?"

Tala: "Enough that, if we stall, Irene will paint *us* as the reason families go hungry. You know they will. You also know the apprenticeships could put people to work if we hold her to the pledge — and if those apprenticeships are real, not a headline."

The tone is raw and quick; Tala knows how to make urgency practical.

Tala's call ends with a promise: she will move like rope through the harbor. You hang up and the rooftop seems louder, the string-lights flickering like a civic heartbeat.

Before you can draw a breath, Dr. Sora's name flashes. She calls more like a command than a greeting.

Dr. Sora Kim: (firm, affectionate) "Have you seen the latest regressions? I uploaded them to the drive. A clause tying payments to measured root-mass increase per quarter — legally enforceable, Maya — could make that fringe parcel unviable to convert."

You: "You think that'll hold?"

Dr. Sora Kim: "It would make the cost of conversion astronomically unfavorable. But the measurements must be objective and auditable. We need sensors. Evan, your band would help. We need independent verification panels. And we need the clause written into the contract in a way Irene can't repurpose."

Evan: "We can build the sensors quickly. Open-source scoring, multiple validators. But we can't guarantee two perfect quarters in the face of a storm season."

Dr. Sora Kim: (with a small laugh) "You say the thing every scientist hates to admit: the world intrudes. Then we build for the world. Maya, you sell why independence matters. If it's framed as accountability, the funders will either accept it or show their true priorities."

You feel the electric charge of possibility: legal teeth plus demonstrable science could mean the contract is not only symbolic. It could be a mechanism to enforce marsh integrity.

Evan: "If we do it, it's pretty public. We have to be ready for scrutiny. And if Irene resists —"

Dr. Sora Kim: "—then she must either accept a binding standard or be named accountable for rejecting it. Power likes to hide behind urgency."

You breathe in the rooftop air — cool with moss-sweetness, metallic with solder — and your heart beats faster. A very high drumline of urgency: sign now and secure scaffolding that might hold; refuse and risk immediate economic harm but strengthen the moral and ecological argument; sign and stage, turning the contract into political theater backed by an audacious technical litmus test.

Evan leans in, voice low and urgent.

Evan: "Maya. If we go public with the pilot — if we make the model impossible to ignore — that could change the narrative. But I'd be asking you to let me risk the prototype on your name and the people's patience. I don't want to be the one who promises and fails."

You: "You're afraid your consulting scars will look like betrayals."

Evan: "I left for a reason. I refuse to make the same mistakes. But I know how seductive a fast, clean solution can look to funders."

He meets your eyes and the rooftop seems to narrow again, the seed tags flapping like tiny flags.

You: "What if—" Your voice drops; you say something that is mostly for yourself. "What if this is the only lever we get to force Irene to choose the marsh over foundations? What if making it public is the only way to expose the trade-off in time?"

Evan: (after a long breath) "Then we triple redundancy. Independent sensors. A legal clause tying disbursement to objective measurements. Public demonstrations with clear metrics. And —" He hesitates, the kind of pause that holds a confession. "And I won't promise anything I can't build. I'll risk my reputation, not yours."

You: "I don't want you to risk everything because of me."

Evan: (firm) "We already risk everything. This is about choosing *what* to risk."

Outside, wind picks up. Somewhere down on the boardwalk, a generator coughs as someone rigs a tarp. The town breathes as though anticipating a call to arms.

From the shadowed edge of the rooftop, you hear the soft, unmistakable ding of a message. Irene.

You don't open it. For a moment you hold the phone like an accusation.

Irene Vale (text preview): "We need signatures tomorrow. Investors will withdraw. I've increased the apprenticeship quota to sixty. Meet me at nine."

There is the sheen of pragmatism in her words: jobs, numbers. But beneath the pragmatism lies the old, political calculus — deadlines as leverage.

You let the message sit in your pocket like a stone. The decision compresses; the potential energy is everywhere.

Captain Reyes has left a voicemail earlier, his voice as weathered as driftwood.

Captain Reyes (voicemail): "Maya. You know I've lived to see the water climb and the town change. If you think a measured approach keeps people fed and the marsh alive, then I'm with you. But don't let fine words cost our neighbors' roofs. Call me."

His message is a tether to history, and to the people whose lives hang in the balance.

The rooftop hums with plans, with hands touching, with arguments half-formed and fierce. You can taste salt and solder on the air; you can feel a pulse behind your teeth that is both fear and exhilaration.

Evan squeezes your hand, a small, steady pressure that says: choose the fight you can live with, and I will build the tools.

You close your eyes for a fraction of a heartbeat and imagine each path as a tide line carved into the marsh: one promises immediate shelter, one promises long-term soil, one promises performance on a stage. All three carry injuries and salvations.

Your chest is tight, and your breathing is a drumline. You are the hinge. The town watches in ways you cannot fully see. The barometer sits cool against the hollow of your throat, and Evan's hand is warm in yours.

You force your eyes open.

[Sound: The rooftop wind rises to a single, steady rushing note.]

This is the point. Everything you negotiated, everything you feared, everything you loved — all of it funnels into three possible lines of action.
