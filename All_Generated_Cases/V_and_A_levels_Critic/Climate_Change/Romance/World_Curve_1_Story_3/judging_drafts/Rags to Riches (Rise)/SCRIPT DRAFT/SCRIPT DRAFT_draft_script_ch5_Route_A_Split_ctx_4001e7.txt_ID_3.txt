[Scene: Verdant Rooftop Nursery | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A patchwork of green against weathered brick; seed trays, reclaimed gutters, and a battered wooden table crowded with papers and a small metal prototype. The harbor gleams pale in the distance.]
[Sound: Wind through the reed beds, a gull calling far below; the rooftop irrigation hisses in a slow, mechanical breath.]
[Music: Urgent strings, quickening tempo]

You come down the stair with the printouts still folded in your jacket: Sora’s annotated studies, Evan’s hand-sketched module plans, Captain Reyes’s logbook tucked under your arm like ballast. The rooftop smells of damp soil and lemon-scrubbed rope — the green smell of things that keep trying to live.

On the table, the draft agreement sits open. In neat columns: marsh set-asides, local labor quotas, an apprenticeship fund. It looks, from a distance, like safety. Close up the margins are serrated with clauses that gnaw: engineered fill permitted on “fringe marsh parcel,” a rolling-stone indemnity for the developer, funding disbursements staggered across political terms.

Evan is there already, sleeves rolled, a small lamp angled over a palm-sized prototype of a living habitat. He is working the joints with a tiny wrench, his face caught between concentration and a tired, reluctant hope. He looks up when you set your things down.

Evan Kaito: "You made it. How—" (he pauses to take in the papers) "—how bad?"

You run a thumb over the copper barometer at your throat until the cool calms you a fraction. You force your voice steady, because everyone is listening to the steadiness you choose to show.

You: "The set-asides are real. Apprenticeships are in. But they still want to reclassify that marsh edge. The developer's clause is… flexible."

Evan shifts the lamp to cast a small circle of gold on the model. The prototype is convincing in miniature: a woven root-matrix base, tidal channels through its belly, a palisade of repurposed driftwood that tucks the tide in rather than beating it back.

Evan Kaito: "If we scale this, the modules could bear load without fill. We could anchor community-built platforms that let the marsh breathe and still support wharves."

You: "Could" is a dangerous promise in a hearing room full of investors.

Evan's hands falter on the wrench. He looks down at his palms — the tool scars like a topography of decisions. The old consulting shadow is a small, cold weather on his face.

Evan Kaito: "I know. I keep thinking about specifications I signed off on before. I don't want to be that person again. I don't want to overpromise and—" (he swallows) "—and watch you have to eat the fallout."

The word lands like a pebble in a storm puddle. Your heart moves faster; it's not just the scaffolding of policy that feels precarious. The gamble stretches between three things: the marsh, the jobs on the pier, and whatever fragile thing is starting between you and Evan.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand brushing his as you reach for a brass clip; fingers touch.]
[Sound: A single string pluck syncs with the contact]
[Music: Builds — a high, bright motif; tempo accelerates]

His fingers are warm. The touch is brief, an electric hush. It speaks of shared labor, shared risk — of a hand that might steady or might let go.

Evan Kaito: (softly) "We can stage a pilot. Not a PR stunt — real data, measurable outcomes. If we can show load-bearing per tidal cycle, Sora's clauses get teeth."

You: "Sora will want absolute metrics. And Tala's already getting calls. Reyes wants guarantees."

Evan Kaito: "Then we give guarantees we can stand behind. We build, we measure, and we publish everything. Let the town decide with the evidence, not the renderings."

You want to believe him so badly that belief tastes like salt and honey at once. But the draft waits on the table like an animal that will not be coaxed.

[Sound: Phone vibrating against wood; Tala's name flashing]
[Visual: Your phone screen shows an incoming call from Tala Ruiz]
[Music: String motif tightens into a rhythmic pulse]

You answer on speaker.

Tala Ruiz: "Maya! Quick—some of the fishers came by the market. They liked the job promises. Leo said the crew from Bodega would sign hourly rates tomorrow if the developer starts on Monday. They're… they're tired, M. They want roofs now."

You take in Tala's rapid breath over the line: the urgency of someone who runs on someone else's mornings. Her words add weight to the contract like a new stone on a scale that was already near tipping.

You: "I hear you. What about Captain Reyes? Has he talked to them?"

Tala Ruiz: "Reyes is at the docks; he's talking folks down from the ledge. He said two things: he remembers high tides that took porches and he wants apprenticeships for his grandkids. But he also said—" (a beat) "—we need to make sure we don't trade marsh for today."

You: "I know. Tell him I'll come by the piers after we work this through."

Tala Ruiz: (lower, urgent) "Irene called earlier. She says they can't delay—people are hungry for work. I'm telling you because you should be ready."

The call ends with a click. The rooftop seems smaller, every breath more expensive.

Before you can answer, Dr. Sora Kim's message pings through on the laptop. You open the video feed.

[Visual: Dr. Sora Kim in her small lab, algae stains on her sleeves, a map of tidal data behind her]
[Sound: Slight echo of lab equipment; Sora’s voice steady and incisive]
[Music: High violin runs underscore her urgency]

Dr. Sora Kim: "Maya — the disbursement schedule is negotiable, but you need legally binding metric clauses: carbon sequestration per square meter, tidal percolation thresholds, and phased release tied to third-party audits. If you sign without those, ‘set-aside’ becomes paint."

You: "Can those metrics be reliably measured at pilot scale, Sora?"

Dr. Sora Kim: "Yes. Evan's approach can produce measurable tidal exchange. We need defined windows and independent verification. Put it in writing — otherwise it's a political promise, not protection."

You scribble notes, the pen biting into your palm. The rooftop air tastes metallic with decisions; the prototype's little LED glows, a tiny lighthouse.

Evan leans in, lowering his voice.

Evan Kaito: "I'll get the micro-sensors calibrated tonight. If we can field a clear protocol, it strengthens the ask. But even that is a bet — politicians like clean deliverables."

You: "Everything is a bet. The question is: who pays if the bet fails? The community, or us?"

Evan's answer is a brief, complicated silence.

[Sound: A shout from below, Captain Reyes stepping onto the stairs with his pipe and a folded paper; his boots scuff the stone]
[Visual: Captain Reyes' weathered face, sunburned and earnest]
[Music: Horn-like brass line; the tempo nearly frantic]

Captain Reyes: "Maya. Heard you had council's ear. Heard Irene's pushing hard. My grandkids need work. I also don't want the marsh gone. You listen to that, child."

You: "You want apprenticeships?"

Captain Reyes: "I want my grandboy to know the tide. Build with the tide, not against it. But I also want roofs. People can't keep waiting for perfect."

You: "We won't let them wait without thought. We need safeguards."

Captain Reyes narrows his eyes, then nods. He places a battered hat on the table, and the gesture weighs more than words.

[Music: Crescendo — string and brass collide; the rooftop feels like the eye of a storm with everything pressing in]
[Visual: The draft contract's final signature line stares back; the mayor's deadline is stamped for tomorrow morning]
[Sound: The irrigation pumps up, a metallic whine; your phone vibrates again — an unread message from Irene: "We need a vote. The town can't wait."]

You read her message. Irene's language is economy: efficient, urgent, inevitable. She means to keep doors open and payrolls moving. You can picture her polished shoes in the council chamber, the lapel pin glinting where it can be seen by cameras.

Your chest tightens in a way that is almost hopeful — not because her urgency is right, but because pressure focuses action. The contract is a hinge. You can bend it, bolt it, or make a spectacle.

Evan takes your hand under the table again — firmer this time, indexing solidarity and something syllabically more dangerous.

Evan Kaito: (low) "What do you want me to do? Push prototypes now? Go loud? Or stay in the wings and let you play the negotiator?"

You look at the prototype, at the fair-weather hope in Sora's data, at Tala's callus-hardened urgency, at Reyes's hat. Your mind runs through the possible futures like tide lines: one where families get roofs and the marsh shrinks, one where the town loses both, one where you thread a needle and the whole community keeps a skin.

Your pulse is a drum. The rooftop feels alive, every breath of wind a cue.

Tala's earlier news reverberates as if amplified: people need now, the marsh needs forever. Sora's clauses feel like tools, crisp and cold. Captain Reyes's hat is a reminder of continuity. Evan's hand in yours is a promise and an ultimatum.

You imagine the council chamber tomorrow — Irene in her blazer, the developer's glossy render pinned to plywood, the mayor's tired eyes, the cameras. You feel the room already: fluorescent lights, the collective inhalation as signatures are passed, the echo of hammers in the future.

[Music: Climactic swell; all instruments building to a sharp apex]
[Visual: The rooftop view tilts to the town below; lights starting to glow in windows as evening approaches]

You take a breath that carries the salt and soil of every street you know. The decision boils like a kettle: necessary steam, urgent release.

You: (to yourself) "This signs futures. It buys roofs. It can take marsh. It can buy time for better answers — if we have the courage to make them public."

Evan watches you, unreadable and open at once — a face made of tools and long bets.

Irene's message sits on your phone like a needle to be pressed. The mayor wants a signing tomorrow. The town wants both lunch and longevity.
