You stand behind the table like a captain at the prow as the market wakes into an unusual, tight-eyed morning. Nita moves between stalls like a lighthouse keeper—loud, hands busy—handing a stack of photocopied documents to anyone who will read them. Dr. Leila’s fingers drum on the metal case of her laptop; Kai’s satchel of plant cuttings sits unused, a small, living denial of the day's paper war.

Your chest is a tight fist of adrenaline and cold salt. You watch neighbors — the fisher with his raincoat, the child with a damp kite — lean in when the first reporter threads through the walkways. Cameras find faces like predators find heat.

You remember the moment you found the first file: a PDF with margins and omissions, a ledger of corners cut, and a line item that matched the photo tucked into Evelyn Black’s wallet — a shoreline labeled "remediation deferred." You remember the tremor in your hands as you printed page after page in the greenhouse late at night, the hum of the dehumidifier like a steady breath. You told yourself then, again and again, this is what your father would have wanted: to tell the truth.

You don't have to speak yet. The paper speaks.

Nita: "Spread them out! Let folks read—don't let the cameras clog the aisles!"

[Sound: Murmurs, then a cascade of outraged voices; a camera shutter snaps]

Kai (quiet, beside you): "They went deep. Leila—this is—"

Dr. Leila: "Chain of approvals, subcontractor memos, emails redacted to look like clerical notes. One of the contractors flagged a compromised soil test three years ago. Blackbrand folded the figure into 'cost optimization.' It's graft, Mara. It's deliberate."

You feel the word graft like a cold coal in your stomach. The market turns into a courtroom with no bailiffs; the evidence becomes a pulsing, impossible center.

[Scene: Community Hall | Late Morning — Packed, restless]
[Visual: Raised benches filled with neighbors; Reverend Sol at the lectern; a projection screen flickers with scanned emails and photographs]
[Sound: Microphone feedback; the hush of hundreds breathing]
[Music: Percussive low strings — heartbeat pattern]

Reverend Sol: "We hold this council with a heavy thing lodged in our mouths. We will speak only what we can show."

You step to the front and lay the documents—photocopied lines and stained margins—under the hall's light. Cameras point like accusing fingers. Reporters press forms of plastic and glass against you, hungry. Dr. Leila clicks through slides: timelines of failed tests, discrepancies between promised mitigation and recorded expense sheets.

Dr. Leila: "This isn't merely incompetence. Several projects under their oversight show the same pattern: environmental gamble disguised as expediency. In at least two instances, community reports of erosion were ignored while the company certified completion."

Evelyn Black (arriving late, measured applause): "Mara. Reverend. Thank you for inviting transparency."

The room tightens. Her voice is a cool blade. She stands under the light as if she belongs to it, as if light answers to her. Her trench coat is immaculate; she hands a compact press statement to a journalist as if offering a gift.

Evelyn: "These allegations, if anything, are the product of misunderstanding and political theater. We have upheld every binding requirement. We will cooperate with an independent review."

You want to laugh. You want to tear the neat stack of her press release into a wind. She smiles with the small civility of someone who knows the law will be her hammer.

You: "If you cooperate, produce the raw tests. Produce the field logs. Produce the procurement ledger."

Evelyn: (tilted, almost sweet) "And I thought you'd ask for finger-pointing. We will provide documentation necessary, but let me be direct, Mara—going public is a volatile move. It carries consequences."

Kai (defensive, sharp): "Consequences you've already engineered, Evelyn. The town deserves the truth, not the tidy spin of corporate PR."

Evelyn: "Kai, you're a friend. It's precisely because we value lives that Blackbrand moves with the urgency—and resources—to build protections here faster than any ad-hoc initiative can muster."

Dr. Leila: "Your 'urgency' included suppressed data about substrate instability. That threatens the very structures you built."

The exchange is a taut wire. Cameras record each tug. The hall vibrates with the collective intake of breath—the kind before a storm breaks.

[Sound: A knock at the back; the hall's doors open; two delivery envelopes tumble onto a chair—legal-looking, stamped with Blackbrand's legal counsel]
[Visual: A security guard looms outside the doorway, silhouetted]

Nita (reading, voice raw): "They're suing the market co-op and our fundraising committee. They're alleging 'interference with contracted work' and 'defamatory actions.' They give us twenty-four hours to retract or face injunction and damages."

You feel the floor tilt. Twenty-four hours. The words are small, clinical, but they feel like a guillotine's breath.

Kai: "That's impossible. We have no defense budget for a corporate suit."

Reverend Sol: (quiet fury) "A company can sue like lighting a match to your ceiling. They know where to strike."

Evelyn: (blue-steel calm) "We regret it has come to this, but the company must protect institutional integrity. Your method of dissemination may cause harm—economic and otherwise—throughout Marisport; our counsel advised swift action."

The crowd's anger rolls and seethes. Someone coughs; a woman in the second row presses a hand to her mouth and weeps.

You can feel the town's loose seams fray under your hands. The press cycle sharpens; within the hour stories appear online: "Local Activists Accuse Developer" — the comments are a salt tide, both waves of support and anonymous, vicious erosion.

[Scene: Floodplain Market | Afternoon — The backlash]
[Visual: Stalls half-empty; a Blackbrand-branded van idles across the channel like a mechanical shark; orange security jackets move in a thin, menacing line]
[Sound: Low drone of generator trucks; a vendor's radio plays a local station sewn with static; vendors whisper—fearful percussion]
[Music: Rapid strings and brass — chaotic, urgent]

A vendor you know—Mariela, who sells smoked mussels—approaches with paper in hand. Her lip trembles.

Mariela: "Bank called. They froze our account, said 'risk exposure.' They're saying any association with 'pending litigation' means 'contingency hold.' How do we buy ice?"

Nita's hands strike the bench. The jolt of her palm is a small, furious drumbeat.

Nita: "They pick the small wounds. They choke the fish first, then the sea feels safer for them."

A Blackbrand security truck's engine chuffs; a drone hovers above, a lens that will later be used in official testimony. A man in an immaculate suit, a local liaison for Blackbrand, steps onto the promenade and smiles like a guillotine.

Suit Liaison: "We're sorry about the inconvenience. We're simply protecting private property and ensuring public safety."

Suit Liaison (to a camera): "We have initiated compliance procedures and will work with law enforcement where necessary."

When a teenage volunteer from Juno's after-school program blocks the promenade with a line of bodies, two guards push forward. Kai is there, arms out, trying to keep distance between a shove and violence.

Guard: "Clear this area or you'll be obstructing public works."

Kai: (softly) "We're not obstructing anything—"

Guard: (pushing Kai's shoulder) "Move."

Kai staggers, recovering with a winded grin that refuses to dissolve entirely. You see the brief flare of anger in his eyes, then the careful valve click as he breathes and prevents an escalation. But the small shove lands like an accusation. Your hands go hot.

You want to rally them, to shout until your voice is hoarse, to shove back. You also understand, painfully, that one misstep could be the legal lever that snaps your people.

[Scene: Community Hall | Early Evening — Emergency Assembly]
[Visual: Overhead lights humming; tables arranged into a ring; the legal papers are thick as wet driftwood]
[Sound: People speak over one another; the scrape of chairs; a child's muffled sob]
[Music: Single low cello — relentless]

The town sits in a raw circle. Dr. Leila projects a timeline showing Blackbrand's legal motions already filed across neighboring municipalities: injunctions, cease-and-desist letters, threats of asset seizure. The projection stutters; the room seems to shrink.

Dr. Leila: "Their legal counsel moves faster than the tide. They've already issued takedown notices to our media partners and sent preliminary subpoenas for the personal emails of anyone who assisted in the investigation."

You feel cold. Your phone lights up with messages: threats, offers, small-town pleas for help. One message is from an account you trust—your mortgage company—asking about an 'irregular deposit flagged to Marisport Defense Fund.' Your personal account is audited to the sound of an approaching storm.

Reverend Sol: "We are at an attrition point. They can bleed us little by little. How many neighbors can survive that erosion?"

Kai: "We can fight. We can scream louder. We can recruit pro-bono counsel—"

Nita: (voice raw) "They will bankrupt the co-op until the market collapses. They will make life so hard that people go hungry and leave."

Evelyn's face, when she enters the hall again, is the same measured architecture of confidence. She stands near the door, almost like the embodiment of inevitability.

Evelyn: "What you have done has consequences, Mara. You chose to weaponize citizens against a project many rely on for immediate protection. I am saddened that the fight has to take these forms, but I will not let mis- or disinformation jeopardize safety."

You can smell her perfume in the heavy room like a foreign weather system—clean, engineered. The air in the hall is a mixture of sweat, salt, and the copper tang of fear.

You feel time compress. The town needs a plan: legal defense, food funds, media strategy, an organized nonviolent action, or a retreat. Each will cost lives in some shape—time, money, homes. The people look at you with raw expectation. Kai's hand hovers, half in yours; you feel it like an anchor and as a weight.

Officials call; one voice, clipped, says a school district vendor is told to 'cease association' with the market under penalty of contract suspension. The lights in the hall flicker; someone mutters about power draw. A drone's shadow passes across the south-facing windows—an overhead omen.

You think of your father's compass, scuffed and familiar at your neck. The compass doesn't tell you right; it only records a north. You are the one who must decide how to move toward it.

Your chest is a drum. Your mind races: if you keep pushing—exposure, media, public pressure—Blackbrand will escalate: lawsuits that could hollow out the market, private security that could escalate into violence, banks that could freeze the cooperative accounts. If you back down or retract, the engineered wall proceeds unchanged, unchallenged, and parts of the town that cannot pay will be displaced. Either way, the town bleeds.

Nita: (voice broken, urgent) "We cannot be the ones to starve people for a principle! Not on my watch."

Kai: (leaning forward) "There must be strategy—phases, legal partnerships, a mobilization plan that doesn't collapse us in forty-eight hours."

Dr. Leila: "Now is the moment to call allies. Environmental legal clinics in the city. Regional journalists. We compile every document and get them to counsel. But even if we win in court, lawsuits take time. Time is money, and money is leverage."

Evelyn: "You have the right to protest," she says coolly. "You do not have the right to sabotage infrastructure. The company will pursue remedies to uphold contractual obligations and protect private investors. I would advise mediation—or face the consequences."

Reverend Sol: (low, steady) "Consequences we accept must be chosen by us, not imposed."

The room's breath shortens. The conversation fractures into urgent, overlapping plans: food lines, legal funds, sit-in rotations, media leaks, alliances with neighboring towns. Each plan is a bandage for a different wound; none is whole.

Your name rises like a tide. All eyes find the scab of your hands on the table, the ink smudges on your thumb from late-night photocopies. They look for the person who lit this spark; they also look for someone to extinguish it before it burns the house down.

You feel the decision coalesce into a single, hot, unbearable point. Your throat goes dry. Memory and obligation and rage and fear and love tangle into one impossible rope.

You open your mouth. The hall leans forward as if the entire town could be swallowed by one sentence.
