You step out from the Saltflower Greenhouse carrying the warmth of the meeting there like a coal in your palm. The promenade smells of brine and old rope; salt clings to your jacket collar. People have gathered in the shallow amphitheater of raised platforms, folding chairs pulled into an uneven semicircle. At the center, a temporary lectern stands beside a projection screen patched with translucent solar film where Blackbrand’s renderings will be shown.

You press your palm flat to the brass compass at your throat for a fraction of a second—its cool ring a private anchor. Around you, faces you know settle into the inevitable hush of a town meeting: expectant, wary, tired but ready. Kai slips into the row beside you, his presence a low, steady warmth. He gives you a small nod, then points discreetly at the screen as technicians boot the presentation. You return the nod, and in that simple exchange there’s a thousand tiny negotiations about what compromises you will or will not make.

Evelyn Black [into microphone]: "Good evening, Marisport. I'm Evelyn Black of Blackbrand Adaptive Systems. We are here to offer protection—rapidly deployable, modular barriers that will hold against projected storm surges for decades."

Evelyn’s voice is clear and controlled, the practiced cadence of someone used to persuading committees and councils. The projection flicks through photorealistic schematics: steel-faced segments nesting together, walkways built atop the barrier, sensors embedded at intervals. The renderings gleam where the real world is matte: her images promise order.

Evelyn Black: "These modules can be installed in phases, prioritized by risk, and they're engineered to integrate with existing port infrastructure. They’re cost-effective, and—" (she pauses, letting the word land) "—they save lives."

A ripple of polite applause. You watch the crowd as if reading the tide—who leans toward fear, who toward skepticism. Some homeowners glance at their shoes, measuring mud and mortgage. A few fishermen cluster like a small shoal behind the front row, eyes narrowed.

Dr. Leila: "The engineering is remarkable—but it's not the whole picture." (She steps up, tablet in hand.) "Living systems respond differently. Oysters, eelgrass, kelp—they build resilience over time and provide ecological services that hard structures don't. I have monitoring data from pilot plots: wave attenuation increases meaningfully after two seasons; biodiversity rebounds; sediment stabilizes."

Her voice is earnest but clipped by scientist's habit: data first, rhetoric later. She slides a graph onto the screen—lines, confidence intervals—then a photo of a kelp line bending like a chorus in the surf. People lean forward; some wrinkle their brows at the small print, others visibly absorb the image of living reef.

Kai Navarro: "We need to make that data feel immediate. Translate it—how much shoreline did those pilot plots save? How long to scale? If we can show timelines and costs next to her phasing, people will stop feeling like it’s between two absolutes."

You consider that. The impulse to stand and speak is sharp—words shaped by late nights sketching reef layouts and writing grant letters. But Reverend Sol clears his throat, and the crowd calms in deference.

Reverend Sol: "Marisport has always weathered storms together. We owe ourselves deliberation that honors bodies and histories, not only spreadsheets. Let truth and memory sit at this table—let us not decide in haste for fear."

There’s a softness in Sol’s voice that obliges people to remember the losses that shaped them: the shrimping families who left, the house with its porch posts now bay-bottom. Nita, apron still in place though the market closed hours ago, stands with a hand on her hip. Her jaw sets with that honest ferocity you know well.

Nita: "I'm not saying no to protecting lives. I'm saying don't sell our people for renderings. Who pays if your plan raises property values and pushes long-time renters out? Who pays when the river of tourists follows a safe promenade and rents spike? We've seen this pattern." (She lets the question hang like a hook.)

Murmurs swell—anger threaded with fear. A woman two rows ahead wipes at her eyes. An older man laughs once, sharp and disbelieving. Evelyn's expression is controlled; you can't tell whether she’s weighing compromise or simply cataloguing opposition.

Evelyn Black: "Displacement is a real concern. Blackbrand has models for affordable housing offsets and community spaces integrated into the wall. We can designate funds—"

Nita interrupts, voice rough: "Funds in a binder don't shelter a grandmother from eviction notices next winter. We need guarantees, land trusts, ownership. We need to know whose lives matter."

The exchange tenses; you feel the heat of it along your spine as if the sun itself leaned in.

You opt for neither immediately, letting others press first. Your restraint is tactical; you need more than rhetoric to shift this meeting. When a council member opens the floor for short rebuttals, Dr. Leila steps forward again.

Dr. Leila: "If we're comparing lifespans and costs, please include ecosystem services. Kelp and oyster reefs sequester carbon, support fisheries, and lower insurance costs by reducing peak wave energy. These are quantifiable benefits that accrue to the whole town."

Evelyn responds with the same polished calm that’s been her hallmark all evening.

Evelyn Black: "Quantification is possible—but it takes time and coordination. Our proposal offers a bridge. We are not opposed to integrating living elements; we're offering assurance now."

Words like 'now' arrive with urgency; they are a lever in public debate. You watch a group of families in a cluster exchange looks—some with relief, some with suspicion. It feels suddenly smaller, this town square: compressed choices, compressed futures.

Kai Navarro: "You have the credibility, Mara. People trust you. If you propose a demonstration—one measurable module of living shoreline paired with transparent cost accounting, staged with clear milestones—it could change the tenor. It gives everyone a thing to point to, increases trust."

Your mind catalogs the obstacles—permitting, sensors, seedlings, volunteer labor. You taste the edge of excitement: possibility wrapped in logistics. You also feel the old guilt, the one that sits where your ribs meet your sternum—your father’s absence in a storm still a cold echo in your decision-making. You do not want to trade one kind of safety for another that erodes the town’s fabric.

You stand, and when you speak your voice is steadier than you feel.

Mara Linh Alvarez: "People of Marisport—what if we don't have to choose between speed and care? Blackbrand, show us your phasing and funding models. We'll propose a parallel: a public demonstration of scaling living systems—kelp lines and oyster reefs—paired with a community-funded monitoring hub. Let's map the timelines side-by-side and fund transparent, legally binding protections for renters and small owners as a condition for any corporate contract."

Conversation splinters around you—some applauding, some skeptical. Evelyn inclines her head, an inscrutable gesture.

Evelyn Black: "I appreciate the spirit. Blackbrand is open to pilots. But clarity is necessary. Public pilots can be costly and delay deployment. When the sea comes earlier than models predict, delay costs lives."

Her words are not a threat so much as a cold, rational warning. You feel no dramatic lightning, only the slow tightening of choices.

Dr. Leila: "We can design an experimental phase that’s rigorous and fast. Monitoring methodologies exist. If Blackbrand is willing to commit to timelines and to fund oversight, we can begin within months."

Reverend Sol rises, cane tapping softly in rhythm with his sentences.

Reverend Sol: "If we move forward with both—test and structure—we must cement community governance. A board, binding agreements, emergency clauses. Not only engineers and contracts—folks who live here should steward what we do. Let us not hand over our shore."

Nita is unrelenting.

Nita: "I want the paperwork. I want trustees. I want housing protections before any shovel hits mud. I want guarantees, not promises."

People cry for clarity and protection. The meeting is not so much an argument as a sieve; facts, fears, and hopes sift through, leaving a residue that looks like policy in formation.

Kai squeezes your hand once—brief, encouraging. He’s already thinking of outreach, of leaflets, of translating those monitoring charts into something a neighbor can parcel and understand.

Kai Navarro: "If you push this, Mara, I'll help build the pilot. Leila will give the science. I know people with boats, and Sol can help with community governance. We can make a small, fast project that proves scale."

You let the thought expand in your chest: a demonstration that can be done on a tightened timeline, with clear metrics, with binding protections. It feels plausible, and dangerous, and necessary. You picture the kelp lines dropping into brackish water, tiny green hands catching the surf, and below that the ledger of who owns which plot, written into a trust.

Your internal checklist runs fast—permits, sensors, volunteers, outreach. You picture Juno's face if you fail and the quiet relief if you do not. Responsibility burrows deep; you inhale the cold, briny air as if it will give you the courage to figure the rest out.

Evelyn watches you from the edge of the stage; her face gives nothing away—complex, unreadable in that way that keeps all past interactions uncommitted.

A woman near the back shouts, "We need action, not more meetings!" The cry is a pitched wire across the crowd—anger and exhaustion braided. Reverend Sol raises a palm and the room settles, the way a harbor settles under wind.

Reverend Sol: "Let us record the questions and answer them honestly. Let us scope the pilot and set community criteria. Let us be clear what we will not cede."

You feel the meeting folding into task lists and minutes. That is the town's language: small, practical actions stitched into policy. The surge of adrenaline you felt earlier recedes into a steady, mid-level focus. This is the arousal of doing: not panic, not triumph—methodical, deliberate increase toward a plan.

Evelyn steps down from the lectern in the soft light, and people begin to drift into clustered conversations—some leaning toward Blackbrand reps, others toward Dr. Leila and your group. The tide breathes against the pilings; seagulls wheel and call, indifferent to municipal urgency.

Kai pulls you aside for a breath.

Kai Navarro: "You did that well. People listened. Whatever comes next, we need to nail the first ninety days—timeline, budget, transparency. If we can get that written with legal teeth, Nita's worries get smaller."

You nod. Your mind is already composing the first page of that ninety-day plan—who to call, what gear you'd need, which volunteer divers could be trusted with sensor deployment. You taste salt and the faint metal tang of adrenaline, not sharp but steady, like a tideline you are learning to read.

You fold your hands around your compass, feeling its edges like a promise. Overhead, the first high clouds thin and let a thread of violet sky show. The meeting breaks into smaller urgencies and promises to reconvene. The crowd disperses in little groups—some angry, some hopeful, some determined to wait for data.

You walk the boardwalk with Kai at your side, the conversation following, dissolving into planning and the pragmatic logistics of a pilot.

There is no neat resolution here—only a resolve pressed into the town’s ledger: a proposal, a counterproposal, an agreement to try. The arousal that carried you through the meeting remains steady, focused on the work: that slow, necessary climb toward demonstration.

You feel the town hinge on the next step. You feel, too, the private weight of your father's memory—an old compass by your chest—reminding you that choices made now will be felt for years.

The lamp-light along the promenade bends and pools; you and Kai pause where the boards meet the water's edge. Behind you, voices carry—plans scaffolded into motion. Ahead, the sea glints, patient and implacable.

A page-turn hangs in the air: the plan must be built, the pilot funded, the protections written. You already know some of the first calls you will make.
