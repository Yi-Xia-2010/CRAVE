You step back into the circle as if sliding a familiar key into an old lock. The notebook in your palm is a weight you know how to balance now — tide lines, sketch margins, a scattering of annotations that say, Yes, keep going. The pendant lamp at your throat bobs when you turn; its small solar cell hums with a comforting hush.

The air is thick with salt and stew. Mina moves between knees and benches with a thermos and battered bowls, the steam catching the lantern light and making halos above each face she passes. Someone has strung a rumpled map across a low table; its creases are full of thumbed decisions, routes and ragged margins. Hands hover over it like people over a shared hope.

Mina: "Here—hot and salty, just like you like it." (she tilts a bowl toward you with a grin that softens the weathered room)
You: "Thanks." (you take the bowl; the warmth presses against your fingers through the chipped ceramic) The smell of kelp and carrots is immediate and honest, the kind of food that remembers how to feed people who will be working later.

Neighbor: "Aiko, you brought the ledger?" (someone asks, hopeful)
You lift the notebook to show a loop of tide markings on the cover. The paper inside smells faintly of rain.

[Visual: Close on the map, hands tracing faded ink paths]
[Sound: Low chuckles, the scrape of chairs]

Sora takes a slow breath and stands. The room quiets in the way of people who have learned to listen to those who have kept counting the years of the shore.

Sora: "We didn't always build this way," she says, voice like sand on stone. "There was a time the city pushed us back from the water and called it progress. We lost boats, but we kept the songs. We lost fields, but we kept the seed packets. Remember that. Remember why we mend, not just for now but for those who will come after us."

You feel the sentence land — small, piercing. It tastes like the first clean bite after a long winter: bitter and true and necessary.

Elias threads through the crowd with his easy grin, half carrying a stack of flier-boards, half listening to an argument about sandbag reinforcement at the north bend. His hand finds yours as he passes; it's quick, light, a brief electricity you don't need to name aloud to feel it bloom. He leans close enough that you can smell the orange peel of his scarf.

Elias: "They're talking volunteers for the terrace tomorrow. We could use your sensors, Aiko—your models make the planting schedules sing." (he grins)
You: "And your crew will plant like it's a celebration," you reply, letting the warmth stay between you both. There's comfort in the predictability of his optimism.

Rafi slips in near the back, hood up despite the warmth, his compact recorder tucked like a talisman. He threads toward you with that bright, urgent look reporters get when they have something that could tilt the conversation.

Rafi: (low) "You have a minute? I found something in the memos — clauses that keep HelioCorp in control of the data stream." (he folds his hands like he’s folded it out of trouble)
You: "Show me." Your voice is steady; you keep the worry out of it so you can see the facts better.

Rafi: "There's language that... ties sensor feeds to a central node." (he scans the room to make sure the word 'control' doesn't take root in panicked soil) "Not necessarily sinister, but ambiguous. If they host the data, they might gate access—" he pinches the air between his fingers, "—and then they decide what counts as risk. Or priority."

You: "We build the protocol with them," you say slowly. "Open-source the schema, keep data mirrored across community nodes. We don't hand off interpretation." You can hear the tiredness in your own compromise before it leaves your mouth: practical, careful, unwilling to give away the steering wheel.

Rafi: "That's the line," he replies. "If they agree to that, it's news. If not—" He doesn't finish. He doesn't have to.

You imagine, briefly, the Subbasement Data Lab: humming racks, their lights soft and green, tidal models scrolling like constellations. You imagine the HelioCorp Atrium: glass so clean it feels like a different weather system, curated vertical gardens behind barriers labeled 'stability.' Both pictures are accurate in tone, not in detail — goals and textures, not solutions. Your mind arranges them like tools on a bench: what fits where depends on instructions you haven't yet written.

Elias returns with Mina in tow, and Mina plants herself in front of the map, elbows spread as if to embrace it.

Mina: "We have kids who can't swim yet and elders who don't want to leave. We can use tech, sure, but not like it's a spoon that stirs the whole pot. We need to keep the hands that stir."

You: "I'm proposing a compromise," you say, and the room leans in. "We pilot a small network of community-controlled sensors—no vendor lock-in, mirrored storage, local dashboards. Combine that with targeted reinforcements: modular, repairable barriers in three priority spots. We learn. We iterate. If HelioCorp wants to partner, they meet our terms."

Neighbor: "And if they refuse?" someone asks, cautious but not cynical.

You: "Then we do it ourselves on a smaller timeline," you answer. "We don't wait for permission to protect our shore." You can feel the conviction behind the words — not a trumpet, but a steady light.

The map fills with markers: three priority reinforcement sites, a handful of sensor nodes, tentative planting zones. Hands trace the river of ink and thread their own memories into the paper — a child's scraped knee near this jetty, a memory of a boat tied there that had to move inland. Each anecdote becomes a bulwark of resolve.

Sora folds her hands, watching the map as if she can read tides in its wrinkles.

Sora: "You think in models and measures, Aiko. That is your gift. But remember the stories on those margins. Keep them with the data." (her eyes are soft, an insistence that practical plans carry honor)

You: "I will," you promise. "The models don't replace stories. They just give us a better chance to keep telling them."

A latecomer murmurs: "HelioCorp reps present in a week—Rowan's team. They say it's modular and fast." The words land like a polite knock at the door of a house they might not be welcome in.

Mina: (brisk, practical) "We listen. We ask questions. We sign nothing on the spot." She looks at you, the way a captain checks the face of her first mate. "We want details — mirrors, backups, community sovereignty. If they can say yes, we talk. If not—"

Neighbor: "We do what we can."

There is a quiet current of agreement that runs through the circle, not loud, not a shout, but steady. Your proposal meets nods, a few raised eyebrows, a couple of sharp, useful questions about maintenance and funds. You answer each in turn — timelines, volunteer rosters, where the parts come from — because you can feel that slow swell of momentum. The room's energy is not a roaring tide; it's the consistent rise of many small, deliberate hands lifting hollow sandbags into place.

Elias squeezes your shoulder as he passes; Mina folds you into a quick, capable hug. Sora presses a small scrap of woven cloth into your palm — a token, damp with the memory of the shoreline. Rafi smiles tiredly and taps his recorder off; he will write the piece with restraint. The agreements are modest, the ambitions steady.

You look at the map one last time, at the circles and X's and the neat, penciled words: 'mirror storage', 'community node', 'repair kits'. The plan feels like a thing made of people — patchwork, imperfect, stubbornly alive.
