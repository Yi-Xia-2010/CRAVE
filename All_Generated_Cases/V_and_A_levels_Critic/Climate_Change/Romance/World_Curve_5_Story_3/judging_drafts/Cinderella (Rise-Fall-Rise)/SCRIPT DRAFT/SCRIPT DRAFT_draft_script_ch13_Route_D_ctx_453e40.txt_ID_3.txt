Mina: (softly) "People need to see what's being signed away. Names, coordinates, the clause about exclusion zones—if the city knows, they can't pretend."

Your chest pulses with a steady, electric optimism. This is not nihilism; it's clarity. If bureaucracy will not listen, then the public will. You think of Sora's stories of the old harbor—how decisions made in council rooms turned docks into memory. You think of Elias's harbor, swallowed by indifferent tide. Tonight the ledger fills with a different kind of entry: one that refuses to be quietly erased.

[Scene: Old Beacon | 01:10 AM]

[Music: High-tempo strings with a rising brass line — triumphant undercurrent]

Sora stands in the doorway, her shawl gathered close. She is quieter than she used to be; the years have given her a calm that is not resignation but a steady flame.

Sora: "Disruption shows people the seams. But remember—words are lasting. What we break must be explained. Otherwise we create fear without purpose."

You: (nod, feeling the pressure) "We will be clear. This is transparency, not chaos. We'll give people facts and places to come if they need help. This is for them."

Sora's eyes—deep, patient—meet yours. For a moment, time slows between you, and you hear the city as if in tape delay: distant engines, gulls, the hush of water. The old woman's hand finds yours, warm despite the chill.

Sora: "Your heart is strong. Let it be steady."

That steadiness is a tether as you walk the route with Elias and Mina, double-checking the ropes, the camera batteries, the encrypted stream link Rafi insists on. Every checklist is a promise.

Elias: "You sure about leaving the perimeter gate unlocked? It'll let people in faster if we need ems."

You: (exhaling) "Yes. Speed matters more than fortress. If the crowd can access the promenade, they'll hold space. The law can be a theater; the masses can be the jury."

Elias: "You always believe in the jury." (he laughs, then his smile falters) "You sure this won't... go too far?"

You feel the question like a pebble in your boot. You have tenderness for the fear in his voice. His optimism is flame; your caution tempers it. But tonight you do not temper everything.

You: "We stay moral. We don't hurt people. We reveal and we disable—not destroy. The aim is to stop centralized secrecy, not the people who work there."

Elias: "Then I'm with you. All in."

He squeezes your hand; the contact is quick, grounding, and the band between you hums with shared risk and trust.

Two of your team unfurl the canvas at the edge, and it blossoms across the glass: hand-painted letters looped into a raw sentence that will not be erased by press releases. People on the promenade gasp; someone starts cheering. The livestream icon blinks red.

Rafi: (through comms) "We're live. Network is green. Incoming views spiking."

You feel the city's attention concentrate, a warmth that is not comfort but power. It tastes of salt and the faint tang of soldered circuit boards—the delicious hybrid of old harbor smells and late-tech.

A municipal patrol car brakes at the end of the street. Two officers step out, flashlights stabbing. Their faces are set; they move with practiced authority.

Officer 1: "You can't hang banners. That building is private property. Move aside or disperse."

You: (calling down, voice steady and bright) "We're exercising free speech. This is a press action. You can arrest us, but the city will see what you're choosing to protect."

Officer 2: (cold) "Disperse now."

The officers fan out, but the crowd is already swelling—neighbors, passersby, people who woke up and followed the stream. Their presence is not a mob; it's a witness. The police hesitate not because they fear the crowd but because they now occupy a choice in public sight.

Elias: (murmurs into your ear) "Hold for the medics. Stay visible."

You: "Stay visible."

Words are practiced, rehearsed; applause bursts, nervy and elated. A chant begins, the city's voice braided with drums and shouted names. The banner drops fully; the atrium glitters with accusation.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab Entrance | 02:26 AM]
[Visual: A concrete stairwell leading below street level, damp with condensation; the door is a patchwork of security seals and old graffiti]
[Music: Low synth pulse, rising; a chorus of layered vocals that feel like a chorus of witnesses]

You and a small team slip down into the belly of the city. This is the part that tests you—the practical moral calculus. You are not here to destroy; you are here to make memory indelible. The lab smells of ozone and warm plastic, the air tinged with the sweet nervousness of live electronics.

Rafi: (whisper) "There's a mirror node here. If we copy the manifests and stream them—names, clauses, coordinates—it's public proof. It'll be impossible for them to sanitize overnight."

You: "Make it readable. Make the captions clear. People must understand what's being ceded."

Rafi nods, fingers flying, his recorder catching even the softest breath. Mina stands by the stairwell with a first-aid kit, eyes scanning exits. Elias dismantles a cheap lock with surprising gentleness, hands steady despite the adrenaline.

Elias: "We only have three minutes before their internal motion sensors flag us. Ready?"

You: "Ready."

You enter the lab. The room is narrow, a cathedral of racks, blinking LED altars, and humming cabinets. The air is close and electric. Your fingers hover over a terminal, and you feel the ridiculous intimacy of this: your fingertips on keyboards carved by people who probably never imagined being held to account in this way.

Rafi: (quietly exultant) "I've got the mirror. Uploading to the stream—now."

On the feed, the layouts appear: contracts with clauses glossed in corporate language, maps that annotate exclusion zones over neighborhoods, and—most damning—a red-stamped internal memo about "priority displacement protocols." Viewers spike. Comments flood. The legal watcher reads key phrases aloud.

You: (voice low but bright) "They hid the clauses in legalese. Make the subtitles show what that means. Translate it for people."

Rafi: "On it. We’re making it a simple overlay."

You move to the cabinet where the control relay sits—the heart of a local node that boots the city's tide-sensors into HelioCorp's private mesh. Your plan is surgical: inject a hold state that broadcasts deployment parameters to every public channel and prevents a quiet remote override. You think of the people who would be affected if these systems were commandeered without oversight: renters, small fishers, elderly neighbors. You steady your hands.

Elias: (soft) "Promise me we pause if there's danger to people. We are not vandals."

You: (meeting his eyes) "We stop for people. We don't stop for profit."

Your pulse accelerates. The hum of servers becomes a cheering crowd inside your skull. You type commands, your breath shallow, the keys clattering like applause. Lines of green text scroll up before you: handshake sequences, authentication tokens, access logs.

Rafi: "We're copying everything. They can't scrub it now. The stream is archiving."

Suddenly, a thin red light begins to blink on one of the racks—an alert. Somewhere above you, somewhere in the tower, motion sensors are recalibrating. The room tilts into a new cadence: faster, sharper.

Mina: (urgent whisper) "They'll lock down in thirty seconds. We either hold the stream and stop the remote reset—" (her voice trembles only a fraction) "—or we bolt with the files."

You feel the crescendo inside you: the city has its eyes on this moment, and your decision will shape what they see next. The choice is not about fear or safety alone; it's about whether transparency wins tonight, loud and irreversible.

Elias reaches for your shoulder. His grip is fierce with something like prayer.

Elias: "Whatever you do, I'm with you."

You: "Then let's make them see."

You slide the final script into the relay. The upload begins. The servers whirl like a choir finding harmony. Outside, muffled but growing, the sound of responders and the crowd compresses into a single, thundering pulse.
