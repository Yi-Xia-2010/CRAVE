## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Lowry Bay

---

## World View
Lowry Bay is a near-future coastal town squeezed between encroaching sea and a brittle bureaucracy that promises engineered salvation. Fishing piers, mural-covered storefronts, and raised community gardens rub against temporary construction banners and glossy proposals for a concrete barrier. Power is diffuse: town committees and volunteer brigades sit beside regional planning offices and private engineering contractors. Social hierarchy is informal but real — longtime fishers, horticulturalists, and young volunteers form the civic backbone, while outside capital and municipal technocrats deliver funding and design. Culture folds practical coastal knowledge into rituals: tidewatch nights, harvest lanterns, and boardwalk festivals. Religion, where present, is syncretic—respect for the sea mixes with small congregations that hold collective memorials after storms. Geography is vulnerable: low-lying neighborhoods, a drowned orchard, a lighthouse sentinel, and a greenhouse boathouse that doubles as Tidelab. Conflict centers on whether to adopt a large, engineered sea barrier that promises measurable security and investment but displaces people, or to scale distributed, nature-based defenses that ask for communal sacrifice and labor. Atmosphere alternates between salt-sweet dawns, humidity-thick summers, and storm-battered twilights; the visual style is painterly anime in sea-glass greens, fog-gray, and rust-orange, with wet brush textures for water and wind.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Mira Kestrel, a local coastal ecologist torn between practical duty to the town and devotion to communal life. The story is framed by a public planning process: Jonah Hale arrives offering a polished barrier and redevelopment; Cassian Rhys organizes residents to resist displacement; my Tidelab experiments promise a hybrid, nature-led alternative. The emotional arc follows Cinderella (Rise → Fall → Rise): the opening chapters build momentum—community trials succeed, my bond with Cassian deepens, political support seems within reach. Then a sudden collapse: a critical pilot fails (or is framed to fail), funding pivots, panic spreads, and I must confront my past hesitation. In the aftermath I choose a crucible path—expose inconvenient truths or make painful compromises—sparking a recovery that is both personal and civic. Branching paths emphasize different kinds of recovery (happy communal win, bittersweet compromise, public loss with personal truth, sacrificial victory). All routes keep the rising then falling then rising arc: personal relationship stakes with Cassian entwine with decisions about who moves, who stays, and how Lowry Bay will remember what it was.
