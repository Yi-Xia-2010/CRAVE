[Scene: Lowry Bay Boardwalk & Streets | Morning]
[Visual: The boardwalk yawns under a pale, salt-bleached sky. Hand-painted banners ripple like gull wings above clusters of tables and tarps.]
[Music: Bright acoustic guitar picks up a buoyant, communal rhythm.]
[Sound: The harmonica’s thin call threads through the murmur of voices; footsteps on wet wood; seagulls punctuate the air.]

You remind yourself of the small, exact thing you said in the hearing: we’ll take it to the streets. Saying it made it real — gave the town permission to move with its hands. Now the streets answer back.

The tarped canopy smells of rain-warm canvas and boiled coffee. Underneath, kids crowd a folding table where you’ve laid out sensor readouts, a scattering of laminated tidal graphs, and the battered leather notebook you never quite stop using. The paper edges are soft from being thumbed through a thousand small plans. Your wristband blinks a steady green; the little LED is a tiny, stubborn lighthouse on your skin.

People you’ve known since you were small thread between stations: volunteers in floral-taped boots kneel at berms with sacks of reed and mud; a group from Nadia’s school practices knitting lanterns, fingers fumbling but proud. Cassian moves through the crowd like an ember — a grin here, a paint-stained hand to steady a ladder there. His jacket is a patchwork of slogans and colors, and when he looks at you the world tilts toward easier things. His hazel eyes warm like late sun on kelp.

Cassian: "Look at this turnout. We got the old boatwright down by Ruben’s shack to bring his nets as shade — can you believe it? Nets as a canopy."

You: "They're perfect for drying seedlings." (You say it before thinking, because you always pivot to utility; because it’s how you show faith.) "Have you seen the sensor demo? The kids are already poking at the display."

Cassian brushes mud off his knee and laughs, the sound like something that could erase a bad morning. "I saw. You made science into a toy and they loved it. That’s a magic trick."

You both watch a girl with a freckled nose press a tiny acoustic sensor to the water and giggle when it chirps. The sound is small and triumphant — data as celebration.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands as you point to tidal curves. A child leans in, fascinated by the rise-and-fall on the tablet.]
[Sound: The harmonica plays a lilting counter-melody to the guitar.]

Cassian: "We’ve got press coming at two. Nadia’s class is doing a lantern piece tonight at the Orchard — the biolume test turned out gorgeous. Ruben’s bringing soup for volunteers." (He hesitates, then adds.) "You okay with the headlines they'll write? They love a good David vs. Goliath."

You feel the half-smile tug your face. Headlines are a double-edged tide: they pull attention in and currents under. "We’ll have our numbers. Our models are updated. Asha’s latest run looks…solid." You choose optimism with intention; it steadies your voice. "We show what works in the real world. People see it, they feel it."

Cassian: (soft) "And if they don’t?"

You: "Then we do it again. We keep showing up." (It’s your slogan now — show up. Measure. Repeat.) "We make it undeniable."

Cassian steps closer to the mural wall the group is painting: broad strokes of marsh grass unfurling into the sea. He hands you a brush when he runs out of room at the edge, and for a moment your fingers catch his. You don’t plan your pulse to quicken; it does.

Cassian: (after either choice) "Either way, we’re making something pretty."

You: "Pretty and useful," you correct, because you can’t help yourself. "The marsh roots will stabilize the berm edge and slow surge energy. It’s not theater."Your tone is warm, but insistent.

Cassian: (teasing) "We’ll still call it theater when we get the brass at City Hall to laugh and then go blind with envy."

You snort, and the sound is friendship folded into shared stubbornness.

[Scene Transition: The Drowned Orchard | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Narrow wooden walkways thread between half-submerged trunks. Knitted lanterns hang from branches, a riot of color against long grass and water that glows faintly as dusk approaches.]
[Music: A gentle chorus of strings, hopeful and intimate.]
[Sound: Water lapping against stilts; the soft clack of knitting needles; children’s voices practicing a rhythm for the evening.]

The Orchard feels like a secret everyone’s in on. The air is sharp with brine and citrus from the fruit trees that cling stubbornly to mounds of soil. The water’s surface mirrors the lanterns, doubling the craftwork until it’s as if a second town blooms below you.

Nadia finds you halfway down the main plank, cheeks flushed from running and hands sticky with jam from the bake sale. She flings a knitted lantern your way like an offering.

Nadia: "We finished the big star one! Ruben says it looks like his old lamp when there was fog."

You: "It looks like hope," you tell her, catching her brief eye. Hope is a dangerous thing to claim, but here it feels earned.

Ruben arrives with his cane and a thermos wrapped in a rag. The tide has darkened his hands in familiar stains. He moves slowly but steady, his presence a history lesson in human rhythm and salt.

Ruben: "You remember how we used to tie lights on the lobster buoys, Mira? Folks would come out and stand in the hush." (A beat.) "This—" He sweeps his hand, nodding to the group. "—this is the same stubbornness. Different lights."

You: (soft) "We’re just...learning to make them last longer."

Ruben: (with a half-grin) "Aye. You’re always tinkering with time."

Cassian arrives carrying a length of painted canvas, the mural’s grass now trailing into the Orchard fringe. He looks like he’s been chasing color all afternoon.

Cassian: "We’re setting up a little performance tonight. Teach-ins, then music. People get the science and get to stay for soup and songs."

You: "That’s the hook." You watch as teenagers from Nadia’s school practice a chant about roots and rights, their voices uncertain but growing. It occurs to you — again — how small acts accumulate to shape a public mood. It is the sum of tiny, ordinary reverences.

Cassian: (leans lower, conspiratorial) "Also, I might serenade you with a particularly questionable harmonica solo if you promise to laugh."

You laugh aloud then, delighted. "Make it good, Cassian. Make it so bad it’s endearing."

Cassian: "Deal." (His hand finds yours briefly to steady a lantern as you pass. The touch is light; the world narrows into that plane of heat behind your ribs.) "Mira, this feels right."

You: "It does." Your answer is true and full; it is the crest you wanted — a swell of communal pride.

[Scene Transition: Tidelab Greenhouse | Night]
[Visual: The converted boathouse glows amber through fog. Hydroponic beds reflect lamplight; the air is humid and full of green scent.]
[Music: A low, steady piano line persists, comforting and persistent.]
[Sound: The distant murmur of the late crowd outside; the soft click of a tablet screen; the whir of a humidifier.]

Later, after press images have gone out and local feeds hum with photos of painted marshes and knitted lanterns, you sit with Asha in the warm belly of Tidelab. Her tablet casts a pale glow on both your faces.

Asha: "Look." (She taps the screen and a model folds into view — layered graphs, predictive lines that bow and then level out under incremental planting schedules.) "If we continue at this rate, and if you keep the berm reinforcement cadence, surge energy is reduced enough to prevent the outflow feedback Jonah's models flagged. It's additive. It scales."

You study the lines. Asha’s models have an engineer’s economy: precise, not sentimental.

You: "That’s…better than I dared hope."

Asha: (shrugging) "You were already doing the math in your head before you came back. I just gave it a nicer dress." She grins, but there’s a softness beneath it — pride that your careful work is meeting community muscle.

You recount the day: the kids with sensors, the mural in motion, the old boatwright’s shade. Each detail lands like a pebble in a pond; the ripple is gentle, then undeniable.

Asha: "Jonah’s team is already grumbling to the press. They say we’re risking investment. Mayor Lin is—" (she pauses; the tablet buzzes softly) "—worried about insurance payouts and immediate job numbers."

You feel the gravity pull at the edge of the day’s buoyancy. Joy here is threaded with worry; hope walks on fragile stilts.

You: (resolute) "Let them grumble. We have to make the small thing so clearly working that it’s cheaper not to ignore it." You tell yourself this as much as Asha. "We need documented, verifiable results."

Asha: "And you have them." (She taps another chart.) "Your pilot runs plus the volunteers’ work — the data pipeline says this is not unproven theater. It’s a demonstrable reduction in risk."

You let out a breath you didn’t know you were holding. For a moment, the hum of the greenhouse becomes a lullaby. The day’s mess of feet and paint and mud resolves into a clean column of meaning: people moved, and physics obliged.

Cassian appears in the doorway, his silhouette haloed by the boardwalk lights. He carries the harmonica and a coffee cup, breath clouding in the cool air.

Cassian: (softly) "You look like you're solving the world in there."

You: "Just a piece of it." (You close the tablet, the glow dimming.) "We did good today."

Cassian: "We did great." (He sits close enough that you can feel the warmth from his jacket.) "The mural looked…alive. Your explanation about root mat strength — you had Ruben nearly in tears."

You: (surprised) "Ruben?"

Cassian: "Aye. He said he'd never seen words for the thing he feels in his bones. You gave it a language." His voice tilts faintly. "That’s no small thing."

You swallow. Language is shelter too. "We’re making more than structures, then."

Cassian launches into a ridiculous account of an interviewer who tried to pin them with a leading question about "idealism" and "foolish locals." You both laugh — a bright, combustible sound.

Cassian: (after a beat) "Promise me something, Mira. When this gets messy, when the press twists us and Jonah plays the practical card — don't disappear. Stay with us in the crowd."

You: (meeting his eyes) "I won’t disappear." The promise tastes like salt and common ground.

Cassian: (quiet) "Good. Because I—" (He stops, breathes, and lets the unsaid rest like a warm stone between you.) "—I like doing this with you."

You: (honest) "Me too."

[Visual: Close-up on your joined hands where a smear of paint still lingers on Cassian’s palm. The camera lingers on the worn brass tidewatcher at your throat, the small light on your wristband, on the lines Asha sketched on the tablet.]

You fall asleep later with Asha’s model on loop in your head and the taste of soup and salt lingering on your tongue. The day folds into you — small victories like stitches in a fabric you hope to wear through future storms. Outside, voices ebb and flow. The headlines are kinder than you expected: community ingenuity, collaborative resilience. You let yourself believe, for a night, that grassroots agency is enough.

[Music: Swells into a warmly hopeful chord, then tapers to a sustained note of quiet anticipation.]
[Visual: The greenhouse lights dim; the town outside glows like embers on the water.]

You sleep with the sensible certainty that tomorrow will ask more, and that — for now — the town has chosen to show up.
