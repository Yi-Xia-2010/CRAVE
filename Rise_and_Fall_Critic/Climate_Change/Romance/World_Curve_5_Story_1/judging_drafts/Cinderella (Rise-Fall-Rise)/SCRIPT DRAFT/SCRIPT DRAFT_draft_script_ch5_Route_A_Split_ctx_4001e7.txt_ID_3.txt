[Scene: The Drowned Orchard | Late Afternoon — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Dusk light gone wrong — a black ceiling pressing low, orchard trunks silhouetted against a roil of leaden cloud. Water glints like oil between raised mounds.]
[Music: Low, thudding strings; wind-swept cello scratching a thin, anxious line.]
[Sound: Wind like a fist through lanterns; distant cracking of loose boards; a high electronic chime repeating in nervous stutters.]

You have been here for hours — checking stilts, mapping flow lines through ankle-deep water, whispering small instructions to volunteers who move like ghosts beneath the grey. The orchard has always been a place you measure care in small geometries: mounded soil, tied ropes, the exact curl of a sapling above the tide. Tonight those geometries feel fragile as eggshells.

Your wristband flares: an insistent, high-pitched chime that bites through the storm and the hum of generators. The LED goes from steady green to a frantic, urgent red.

You glance at the readout without thinking: wave amplitude spikes at the sensor array beyond the orchard; a pattern you have seen before nests inside the numbers like a fossil. The algae along the walkway glows faintly blue where the water runs colder — an eerie bioluminescent tracer where a stilt has turned and lies half-submerged.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands damp with brine, thumb hovering over the wristband. The camera pulls back to show overturned stilts and a bench slumped into the water.]
[Sound: The chime repeats — short, sharp — then stops as if catching its breath.]

Your chest tightens in a way you recognize and hate. Guilt is tidewater: patient, inevitable, and carrying things you would rather not find. You remember the pilot that failed three seasons ago — the smell of wet wood and burned connectors, the faces of families shivering in borrowed coats. The memory pins your lungs shut.

Asha appears as if blown out of a doorway: lab glasses crooked, damp hair clinging to her temples, a tablet held in both hands like a shield. Her breath comes fast and loud; the scent of wet metal and ozone trails her.

Asha: "Mira — the simulations are showing it. It's not a material failure. It's resonance. Under these combined wave angles the barrier amplifies certain frequencies. The planned reinforcement—"

Her voice fractures; she taps the tablet and forces the chart into your face. Red spikes bloom across a baseline, harmonic peaks stacking like teeth. Her fingers shake.

You: "Explain it like I'm standing on a ladder, Asha."

Asha (rushed): "When the waves hit at this phase and with this swell, the designed cavities and anchors set up standing waves. The barrier doesn't dampen — it sings. Energy feeds back into the shoreline. The model—" (she presses the corner of her mouth, searching) "—our assumptions about damping coefficients were optimistic. We misjudged coastal coupling."

She looks at you as much for steadiness as for permission. The orchard smells suddenly of wet soil and metal and panic.

The sound of boots on the boardwalk becomes a new layer of percussion: Cassian's sneakers scuffing salt, paint still wet on the canvas of his jacket. He comes up the shoreline like a wave of his own — paint-splattered, eyes bright and rimmed with adrenaline.

Cassian: "You saw this?" His voice is a blade of accusation wrapped in exhaustion. "How did we miss—?"

You: "We didn't miss it. Asha found—"

Cassian (cutting in): "Found? Found? Mira, this is a catastrophe waiting to be signed. We built hope out there and it could ricochet right back into the orchards."

He does not let the sentence end. His hazel eyes are heated and raw; there's something like betrayal behind them, as if the city had been promised and you had handed it a cracked map.

You: "I negotiated oversight. We have benchmarks. Jonah agreed to phased funding tied to evaluation."

Cassian (laughing, too sharp): "Benchmarks. Contracts. Benchmarks don't keep grandmothers in their houses when a surge turns into a choir of collapse."

Asha (quick, corrective): "It's not inevitable. There are adjustments. We can redesign the damping geometry. Emergency retrofits could—"

Jonah's arrival is quieter than you'd expect, his coat already slick with rain, tablet tucked under his arm like a ledger. He steps onto the duckboard and the conversation shifts a degree colder.

[Visual: Jonah framed against the dark sea, his profile unreadable; the lamplight picks out the silver of the pen in his hand.]
[Sound: A low electronic hum as his sleeve brushes the tablet.]

Jonah (measured): "I received Asha's alert. Thank you for bringing this to me." (He pauses, looking at the screen without rushing.) "If the model indicates resonance at certain frequencies, that's fixable. We'll run updated simulations, tweak the cavities, stagger the anchors. It's engineering."

You watch his mouth calibrate the phrase: 'fixable'. His eyes are cool and assessing — unreadable in the way of people who have to hold public faith. You cannot tell if relief underlies the statement, or a quick calculation of fiscal exposure.

You: "How long? What does 'fixable' cost — in time, in funding?"

Jonah: "Weeks, testing cycles, additional reinforcement on select nodes. We'll absorb contingencies from the consortium; the phased funding already covers emergent corrections."

Asha (interjecting, urgent): "There's a clause — the maintenance could be privatized under certain cost-overrun triggers. If the consortium assumes ongoing maintenance, it could shift forever from municipal oversight to a private contractor with a vested interest."

Jonah's face doesn't betray the clause. He gives you a small, professional smile.

Jonah: "Those are standard protections. They ensure continuity. Private maintenance can be contracted with terms that lock in community oversight."

Cassian spits as if tasting metal.

Cassian: "You mean 'privatize and profit', Jonah. You mean lock outsiders into our shoreline and call it security."

Jonah (cool): "I mean islanding a revenue stream that pays for long-term protection. I mean stability."

Ruben's voice comes then, rough and gravelly, from the line where the ground tilts toward the bay. His cane scratches along the boards in a harsh percussion. He has been quiet until now; the storm seems to have carved the worry into deeper lines on his face.

Ruben: "You all hear the water. We don't have time for talk not backed by ropes and boats. Evacuate the low mounds; pull the children up. This barrier talk — it can wait for the bids to stop whining."

Nadia is at your elbow, wet hair stuck to her forehead. Her hands are small and urgent as she presses them to your sleeve.

Nadia (voice thin): "Please, Mira. Please tell them what to do. Tell them we won't—"

Her sentence dissolves into the wind. She doesn't finish. She looks at you with an expression that asks for shelter and for a chase of certainty you don't feel you can give.

Your throat closes. The old pilot's failure scrapes at you from memory: the way the boardwalk had flexed that night, the way you had watched as people carried what they could. Guilt is not one thing here; it is many small currents tugging in different directions, pulling you toward responsibility and away from clean choices.

Cassian pulls free, voice like breaking glass.

Cassian: "Don't try to placate me with hands and murmurs, Mira. Not now." (He kicks at a half-buried crate and salt-spray splashes his sneakers.) "You signed them up. You held their hands. How many times will you be the person who believes in soft fixes while the town pays with its kitchen tables?"

You: "I believed in phased oversight because our community needs to be protected — and because we can build living systems that work."

Cassian (bitter): "And when the living systems become drumheads for an engineered hum? Will you still call that protection?"

Asha (between them, urgent): "We can model mitigations. If we stagger the anchors and add tuned dampers, the resonance peaks can be attenuated. The math says—"

Her voice is swallowed by the wind for a beat; then Jonah leans closer.

Jonah (low, convincing): "We can resource the retrofits. I can pull contingency funding; the consortium will not walk if we present a solid remediation plan. There will be political fallout, but we will keep the project — and the jobs it funds."

You feel the fork forming in the air, metallic and sharp. Each option is a kind of violence: accept a compromise that could displace vulnerable edges of town but preserve many homes; expose the flaw and risk the entire project — and with it the economic promises that some neighbors clench like oxygen; or force a third way, demanding independent review and emergency redesign, buying time but inviting chaos and political backlash.

The orchard creaks with the weight of the decision. The rain takes on a finer, needle-like quality; the algae's glow seems to blink in time with your pulse. Ruben's cane scrapes closer; Nadia's fingers dig into your sleeve.

You think of the households that will be safe if the compromise holds. You think of the ones that will be uprooted — elderly families, the margin buffers that have already been borrowing land for years. You think of the pilot that failed and of the faces you'd seen then: resigned, tired, bitter in a way that felt like blame.

You could hear Jonah's practical cadence shape the future into something orderly. You could hear Cassian's rawness tearing at the seams and sense the town's soul — messy, human, vulnerable — on the line.

Your brain runs through a checklist of outcomes and consequences like a technician because that is who you are; but the checkboxes have begun to blur. This is not a lab with controlled variables. This is water and people and contracts and memory and the uncanny physics of a barrier that hears the sea and answers back.

Asha presses the tablet toward you. The storm hammers the world into a single, loud moment.

Asha (quiet): "We need you to decide where you'll stand. The town will look to you. Mayor Park is coordinating resources, but the next hour defines whether evacuation becomes necessary and whether we go public with these numbers."

You hear yourself — the you who negotiates, the you who keeps careful ledgers of hope — and you hear the other you, the one who has been pushed under by worries about consequences. The two of them are not in harmony.

The wind picks up. A gull cries like an alarm. The orchard's knitted lanterns swing and clack.

Everything tilts toward one impossible choice.
