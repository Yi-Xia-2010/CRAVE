[Scene: Lowry Bay Boardwalk | Pre-dawn]

[Visual: Fog-slimmed pastel skyline; crooked lamp-posts leaning over the boardwalk]
[Music: Soft, hopeful piano with distant gull calls]
[Sound: Wooden planks creaking, tide whispering against pilings]

You wake to the world already inhaling salt. The boardwalk under you is still cool from night rain; it smells of wet wood, kelp, and the metallic tang of sea-glass ground fine by the shore. Your fingers find the brass tidewatcher pendant at your throat by habit and worry — the pendant is warm where it rests against the skin, its engraved ridges smooth under your thumb.

You lift your wrist. The sensor band with its fraying straps blinks — three tiny LEDs stuttering like an anxious heartbeat. You know what the pattern means in the soft way only long practice gives you: small variance, nothing catastrophic, but the kind of detail that keeps you awake. You check the numbers again with your thumb against the worn face of your leather notebook, the dent in the corner a small map of many mornings just like this.

Your field jacket still carries the smell of last night's repairs — machine oil, damp canvas, the faint sweetness of dried marsh grass caught in a seam. You tuck a strand of dark sea‑green hair behind your right ear; the sun-bleached tips stick damply to your cheek. Three seasons since the pilot failed and the memory is a muscle that tenses whenever you reach for a shovel or a spreadsheet. Precision is your apology: every measurement double-checked, every stake hammered with careful force. You tell yourself this is what will keep people from losing roofs, from losing the orchards, from losing the spare table where a child first learns to tie a fishing knot.

From the lamp-posts, Nadia waves — bright hoodie bobbing in the dim. She's clutching handmade flyers for the school fundraiser, and the anxious joy in her face makes your chest loosen. Ruben's silhouette tilts against the dock rails farther down, oilskins heavy, cane hooked just so. He watches the water like someone remembering a face.

[Sound: A bicycle bell rings distantly; a gull cries]

You call out before you think about formality. "Morning, Nad. Morning, Ruben."

Nadia: "Mira! You're early. Or—" (she frowns teasingly) "—you went to bed on the boardwalk again?"

You: "No ceremony, just a long sleep and an even longer checklist."

Nadia: "Checklist win, this year?" (She thrusts a flyer at you.) "We're trying to get the school to sponsor sensor kits for the kids. You'd be the hero of the science table."

You study the flyer. A child's crayon sun brightens one corner; the school logo is a hand-painted fish. You smile without meaning to. "Tell them the hero is busy check-listing. But if you want, I can demo a kit at midday."

Nadia's grin is immediate and small. "Do it. Ruben says he'll bring his old tide clock. You two together will make a museum."

Ruben lifts a hand, a slow wave that smells faintly of cured fish and tar. He doesn't come closer; his posture speaks of seasons learned by standing at the edge. When he answers, his voice is sandpaper-soft. Ruben: "You keep the tide in your head, Mira. Don't let the charts take it from you."

You: "I won't. Not today."

Ruben: (a small laugh) "Good. The children's laughter keeps more than the town afloat."

You nod. You mean it.

You run through the plan in your head, like tracing a line on a map: inspect the tidal garden rebar, recalibrate the Tidelab sensors, meet Cassian about the mural for the mobilization push. The list makes the morning into a kind of promise you can hold to.

[Visual: The tidal garden ahead; raised mounds outlined like islands]
[Sound: Water lapping, the whisper of grasses]

You walk toward the tidal garden. The planted berms are a patchwork of netting and hand-set posts; the rebar you installed last season glints with morning dew. You kneel, fingers careful, and feel the cold give under the gloves. Your hands move the way they always do — hesitantly precise, thorough because of the thing you carry: the failed pilot, the places where you misread drag names and the marsh lost a rank of plantings.

You spend a long minute tracing the margins of each raised bed. The marsh grasses smell like crushed green apples and brine. You note a gull's shadow pass over the water, and for the first time today the absence of panic is a small, rising light. The work is steady; each tightened bolt feels like a tiny covenant with the people who live above these beds.

[Visual: A spray of paint on a distant wall — bright mustard and seafoam; footsteps approach]
[Music: A layer of light guitar threads in]

Cassian arrives before you expect him, hands stained with paint, a roll of sketches tucked under his arm. He wears his paint-streaked denim jacket like a flag. He leans on the new railing and grins the kind of grin that's equal parts trouble and sunlight.

Cassian: "Early bird, or early redeemer? Which are we today?"

You: "Both if you promise not to paint the town in slogans without consulting the council." (You feel the corner of your mouth twitch — irritation wrapped in fondness.)

Cassian: "Promise — but no promises about adding more suns. They deserve at least two more suns." (He taps his sketches.) "Look. I've been thinking colors that read from the water. Mustard for community kitchens, seafoam for marsh beds—"

You: (interrupting lightly) "Cass, murals don't hold back storm surge."

Cassian: (mock-offended) "Mira Kestrel, killing the romance with hydrodynamics. Fine. But a mural changes a mood, and changing a mood gets people to keep planting, to keep watching the tide."

You look at the sketches. He has painted the boardwalk in sweeping arcs that mimic wave patterns, figures passing tools between them. The human silhouettes are small but made bright.

You: "It could work. Just—" (your voice softens) "—we need people to feel this is for them, not against them."

Cassian: "It's always been for them. For you." (He says it quickly, like a joke, then sits with the silence between you.) "You look...lighter than when you first came back."

You: "I have a plan for the Tidelab sensors. Asha helped me with the recalibration protocol. If the data is right this time—" (your hands find your notebook, tracing the dent) "—we'll have more to show at the hearing."

Cassian: (his expression changes briefly, serious and steady) "The hearing is coming up. People will need to hear things they can hold. You'll give them numbers; I'll give them a picture they can bring to the board meeting." He bumps your shoulder with an easy familiarity. "We'll make them see both."

You: "And if the numbers don't agree?" (You can't keep the tremor out; some old ghost of the pilot lingers.)

Cassian: "Then we paint a mural about how messy transitions are." (He smiles, but it's earnest.) "We don't lose the people who already stayed because things got hard. We make staying a thing to be proud of."

You: "Cass—" (You stop because what you almost say is private and heavy. The morning holds it like a fragile shell.)

Cassian: "Hey. Whatever happens, I won't let you shoulder it alone. Not on this boardwalk, not anywhere." He reaches into his satchel and draws out a harmonica, breathes a note that sounds like breeze through glass. It makes you blink. "Music and numbers," he says, grinning. "The oldest plan."

You laugh at that, small and bright. The sound scatters a bunch of pigeons. You're grateful for the way he can convert fear into stubborn, reachable plans.

You tuck the notebook into your jacket pocket. The leather is warm, as if someone else has been holding it all night. The tide pulls at the pilings in a steady rhythm you can feel in your teeth. Your plan feels like a rope bridge — composed of small, tested steps rather than a single leap.

[Visual: Tidelab greenhouse in the near distance; warm amber light spilling out in the fog]
[Sound: Soft drip of condensation, distant laughter from morning preparations]

Cassian looks at the greenhouse where the Tidelab's polymer panels glow, where Asha will already be running equipment like the town's ancient heartbeat. He tucks a paintbrush behind his ear and grins up at you.

Cassian: "Race you for a coffee?"

You: (quietly) "You're on."

He rockets off in a way that's both ridiculous and impossible to resent, a streak of mustard paint across the gray morning. You watch him go and feel something rise — cautious, like the weather that promises rain but brings light.

You stand a moment longer on the boardwalk. Above the low pastel skyline, the east is a promise of thawing color: sea-glass greens turning softer, gull-silver brightening into a band of gold. People are about to wake and come out with rakes and flyers and worried, stubborn faces. There will be meetings and measurements and, very likely, arguments. But this morning hums with a small, fierce hope. You measure it like the LEDs on your wristband — small signals, meaningful when read together.

You breathe in the salt and the possibility, tighten your jacket against the damp, and start walking toward the Tidelab, the list of the day's work pressed like a pact in your head.

[Visual: Close-up on the tidewatcher pendant, catching the first thin ray of light]
[Music: Piano resolves into a gentle upward motif]

You think of the failed pilot again, briefly: not to be tormented by it, but to keep its lesson close. Precision now is mercy later. The town is a collection of rooms and voices and smells and small stubborn lives. Today you will check a rebar, you will reboot a sensor, you will talk colors with Cassian. Today you will do the things you can.

You step onto the boardwalk plank that creaks a different, promising note. The sky ahead is lighter than when you woke.

You feel the page of the day turn.
