[Scene: The Drowned Orchard | Night — Storm Incoming]
[Visual: Knitted lanterns bob low in the water; their stitches glow like small, surrendered stars. Rain falls angled and hard, sheeting off leaves in thin silver rivers.]
[Music: Low, minor strings; a single cello note holds and trembles.]
[Sound: Rope snapping, lantern glass clinking, the thunder-snap of the sea hitting the berm.]

You wake to a sound that feels like the world being re-read and corrected — a timbering snap, the quick, terrified tearing of a walkway. For a second the sound has no name; then your stomach flips and the name comes: the orchard.

You are already moving before you understand how you will move. Your boots find the soaked path as if they remember it better than you do. The air tastes of salt and wet wool and pressed fruit; your throat tightens around it. Lantern light splashes across your jacket in wet orange patches. Somewhere behind you Cassian is a shout and a harmonica note swallowed by wind.

[Visual: Close-up on your tidewatcher pendant, rain collecting on the brass; the little needle trembles against worn metal.]
[Sound: Your breath, sharp and hurried. Wood groaning under pressure.]

You run.

[Scene: Orchard Walkway | Seconds Later]
[Visual: A raised walkway, its handrails splintered and half-submerged; a braided lantern floats away in the current.]
[Sound: Water thrumming through a newly-formed channel; the creak of straining rope.]

Ruben's voice — not a shout, but a thin, raw sound — catches you like a hook. You see him then: one step where the planking gives, a body half-swallowed by foam. He looks much smaller in the water than he does on dry land; his sailor's hat gone, his cane drifting away like a pale gull.

You do not think. You reach.

Ruben lies against your knees when you drag him onto a sturdier patch of boardwalk. His breath is a series of broken notes. His cane is splintered like a toothpick beside him. You taste metal; you press your palm to his chest and find it thudding, slow and human.

Ruben: "Mira... I — thought the tide had me. Thought I was old enough to forgive it and then—" (He coughs; salt and blood and apology mix.) "—didn't want to be that burden."

You: "You're not a burden." (Your voice is the only steady thing you can give him.) "Breathe. Breathe for me."

Ruben's fingers find yours for a moment — callused, tremoring — and then he is asking for information in the old way: Where is the new break? How fast is the water? Your thumb presses his knuckle as you answer both aloud and inwardly.

You: "The western berm failed near the orchard's north mound. A walkway snapped toward the mouth; current redirected through the old sluice. Sediment's pulsing — fast, chaotic. Asha, tablet?"

Asha (kneeling, tablet clutched, rain spattering her short hair): "Sensors are spiking. Turbidity’s off the charts, Mira — sediment surges where the berm's seams opened. Currents we modeled as slow are… rerouting. If this keeps, the levees downshore will see a pressure spike in under an hour."

You: "How many—"

Asha: (glancing up, eyes wide) "—Nadia's cluster is still out along the orchard's east board. Two volunteers near the south stilts."

Nadia: (from the next walkway, voice thin but sure) "I'm fine! I'm fine! We can get them—"

You look at her bright hoodie and the way she grips a lantern that thrums against her heartbeat. She is not old enough to hold grief yet; she has the kind of stubborn that looks like hope.

Cassian arrives at the edge in a skid of mud and paint. Paint is smeared over his knuckles, streaking down his wrist in odd, bright lines against the rain. His eyes are hollow, the usual crescent-smile flattened into a visible cavity.

Cassian: "We held them up with song and paint, Mira. For a moment I believed we could keep it together." (He swallows; his voice breaks like a rope.) "I didn't expect — I didn't expect the sky to bite back."

You: "None of us did." Your throat is rough. "Help get Ruben warm. Asha, route volunteers to the east cluster. Nadia, stay near me."

Cassian hesitates, then moves like a man given a role he has always known how to play: talking to people, lifting spirits even while his hands tremble. He wraps his arm around a shivering volunteer and starts barking encouragement in a voice that refuses to be entirely broken.

[Sound: A distant engine — someone trying to start a truck. Shouts mixing with the rhythm of rain.]

Jonah's arrival is a shadow at the far edge, slick coat darker than the night, hands already checking the slate at his hip like a man who measures chaos by columns. He steps onto the boardwalk with a measured pace that reads as confidence and sets a prism of accusation into the air.

Jonah: (calm, practiced) "This is precisely what we warned could happen if piecemeal defenses weren't scaled. The barrier—"

You cut across him before he can finish, the word "barrier" lodging in your throat like a cough.

You: "Not every failure is an argument for erasure, Jonah. The berms weren't finished. We have data; we can repair the seams."

Jonah: (cool, blades of reasoning) "And while you tinker with patches, the town bleeds funding and time. You want to know what donors will do after tonight? They want certainty. They want measurable results. They want—" (He gestures to the survey stakes bobbing in muddy water.) "—a plan that doesn't smell of ad‑hoc sentimentality."

Asha: (sharp) "We had models. We had stress tests. There are variables—"

Jonah: "Variables are excuses in a crisis." (He meets her eyes directly; his words are an attempt to weigh the room.) "This is why we need a hardened, engineered line with guaranteed metrics."

A murmur runs through the gathered community — not unanimity but a brittle chorus. Some faces look like thunderclouds. Others look split by fear. The orchard's knitted lanterns drift past like small, embarrassed moons.

You feel the room tilt. In the space between Jonah's pronouncement and the town's reaction, blame finds its teeth. Fingers point. Old friendships creak.

Nadia: (eyes wet) "If the barrier comes and my school's moved, where will we go? Who gets counted and who doesn't?"

Ruben: (struggling to his feet with your help; voice small but clear) "We can't sell the bay's memory for 'certainty.' There must be a middle way."

Cassian: (to you, quieter) "They'll use pictures of tonight in grant presentations, Mira. They always do."

You: (thinking) The sensor readouts flash and scream in your head — a chorus of numbers that mean the earth is rearranging itself. Sediment pulses where we didn't anticipate it. Currents that our models treated as marginal are now the main act. If the barrier is framed as the only measurable fix, the town's choices narrow dangerously.

Asha's tablet finally blares with a sustained alert — a high, thin note that makes a few heads snap toward the screen like birds hearing a hawk.

Asha: (voice tight) "Readout from the southern channel. Look — see? The sediment front is moving faster than the model predicted. It's scouring the base of the northern stilts. If this trajectory holds, the low-lying housing off Dockside will be at acute risk within the next tide."

Mayor Lin arrives via a mud-splattered municipal truck, hair pinned back, expression drawn and efficient. She pulls her scarf tighter, standing between the practical and the political with a face that wears both obligations.

Mayor Lin: (measured, tired) "Status?"

You: "Ruben's stable, hypothermic but breathing. Two volunteer clusters need extraction. The berm failed; currents are rerouted. Sediment's in flux."

Mayor Lin: (sighing, the weight of budgets in it) "I've already been getting calls. Jonah — the consortium wants an immediate briefing. Funding timelines are tight. This night will be used in every email chain and every appeal."

Jonah: (leaning in, voice honeyed but cold) "Mayor, with respect — this is the sort of event that demonstrates the need for decisive action. If the town wants to avoid catastrophic displacement, they will need structures that guarantee safety."

A hush, heavy and small, falls. Your hands are damp between fingers and hope. You feel Cassian beside you, his shoulder a warm, messy anchor, and you know that whatever you say next will be read as more than strategy: it will be read as a moral ledger.

You: (thinking) There are three lean options in front of you, each a scar on someone you love: help orchestrate a voluntary relocation that saves bodies but costs place; escalate and risk everything on a national spectacle that could either save you or shatter the community; or appeal to Mayor Lin with hard, fresh data and hope bureaucracy moves fast enough to buy time.

Cassian: (low, to you) "Don't let tonight be used as a single snapshot. Promise me we'll not let them frame this as inevitability."

You: "I won't promise what I can't guarantee." (The words feel like a cracked plank; practical and true.)

Ruben: (hoarse) "Whatever you choose, Mira, choose the thing that keeps people whole as people — not as data points."

You: The lanterns bob off into a widening wake. The knitted lights reflect in a thousand small, uncertain ways on the water. You can see the town fracturing in the faces around you: some already calculating which side will pay for their roofs, others looking at their feet and counting losses like rosary beads.

[Visual: The camera holds on your hands, dirty and wet, hovering over the tablet as the alert blares. The screen is a map of currents and red lines that look like bruises.]
[Music: Cello slides down an octave; the strings thin into a single unresolved note.]

You take a breath that tastes of salt and loss and possibilities that all end in cost.
