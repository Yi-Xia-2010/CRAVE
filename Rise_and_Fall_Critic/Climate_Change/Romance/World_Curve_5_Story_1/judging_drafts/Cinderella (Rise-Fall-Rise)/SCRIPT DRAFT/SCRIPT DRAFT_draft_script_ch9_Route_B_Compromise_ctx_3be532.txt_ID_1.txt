You stand with your palm flat against the sun-bleached eaves, tracing the grooves where years of weather have kept memory. The wood smells of salt and citrus from the orchard jars your mother used to can. Your thumb finds a carved groove — a child's initial, half-smoothed by decades — and for a moment you pretend the hole is a small harbor, something steady and protective.

You can feel the town's weight in your ribs tonight: the lists, the permits, the names of neighbors who accepted stipends, who chose the jobs in marsh restoration, who will pack up tomorrow. Some of those names are voisins, some are family. Your childhood house is on the list. The fact sits like a stone in your mouth: necessary, heavy, cold.

You close your fingers around the brass tidewatcher at your throat. Its metal is warm from your skin; its small, familiar tick steadies the pace of a heart that wants to race away and also wants to stay and fix and keep. You imagine how the orchard's light will pass through the kitchen window next spring and know that whatever "next" looks like, it will not be the same.

A soft step approaches: Cassian. He pauses at the threshold, rain speckling his paint-splattered jacket. His eyes find yours — hazel lit by reflected lamp-glow — and he offers a smile that isn't meant to erase anything, only to share a space of sorrow and resolve.

Cassian: "It's strange, isn't it? Doing all the packing and the festivals and still feeling like we're just promising the sea we'll try harder."

You (Mira): "It would be strange even without the sea. Promises have always been our practical currency here."

Cassian leans his shoulder against the doorframe, close enough that the smell of linseed oil and salt rolls between you. He reaches out and squeezes your hand — a solid, human assertion.

Cassian: "You did the right thing. We did the right thing." (pause) "Even if 'right' is a word that hurts."

You swallow. You want to argue, to list the models and the compromise clauses, to unspool the meetings until the logic shines clean. Instead, you let the silence be its own kind of answer: a ledger of decisions, tallied in tired faces and shifted foundations.

You: "I keep thinking about Ruben's creek stories, about how the sea used to be generous and then just..." (your voice thins) "then it wasn't."

Ruben's words — old rhythm, older caution — loop in your head; they have guided many of your steps. Somewhere in the distance, the faint, metallic click of a sign being taken down sounds like an exhale that belongs to the town itself.

Mayor Lin looks at you as though weighing the town in her hands. Jonah stands to her left — his coat damp from the morning, but his posture careful and human in a way you haven't seen in months.

Mayor Lin: "We couldn't have done this without the community's input. The protection clause secures the town core as a community land trust for twenty years with binding commitments to rehousing aid."

Jonah: "And the design will remove hard infrastructure from the living margins, shifting to a hybrid model — living berms and tidal marsh buffers. It isn't everything any of us hoped for, but it reduces displacement beyond what's being organized."

You feel the equation of cost and saving spin into view: not a perfect ledger, but a ledger that keeps more people than it displaces. You have been the person who pushed for this measurable compromise; you feel the microfracture of guilt that comes when a plan that saves many asks a few to leave.

You (Mira): "Mayor Lin, Jonah — thank you. For listening, for changing a plan some called non-negotiable."

Mayor Lin: "We learned. We listened. And we owe you all better follow-through."

Jonah's gaze finds yours, and for the first time since he arrived with blueprints and funding, his expression cracks open into something unexpectedly vulnerable.

Jonah: "You made me understand the value of keeping people's lives connected to place. I'll keep my office open to ensure the transition measures aren't just words."

There is a quiet between the speeches where what was political becomes human. You look at Jonah and see a man who has recalculated; you look at Mayor Lin and see a leader who chose a path that held the town's soul as a variable. The architecture of compromise is ugly and honest.

[Scene: Drowned Orchard | Dusk — Farewells and Promises]
[Visual: Knitted lanterns hang like a constellation lowered to meet the sea. Neighbors form a loose circle on the raised mounds. Children trade saplings like treasures. The horizon bleeds apricot into slate.]
[Sound: Low singing, a harmonica occasionally, the shush of water, and the steady murmur of people offering small words that mean everything.]
[Music: Chorus of strings swells softly into a hopeful, ascending resolution; a single voice hums an old local tune.]

Ruben approaches with his carved cane, the lines on his face catching the lantern light. He stops before you and places both hands on your shoulders with a grip that is both admonition and benediction.

Ruben: "You did the sensible thing, Mira. You chose the town over your house. That hurts the right way."

You: "It hurts every direction. But you say sensible like it's a compliment."

Ruben: (a humorless little laugh) "It is. Sensible is the work that keeps us in the world. Keep doing it."

Nadia squeezes your hand and presses a sapling into it — a small, stubborn apple, leaves trembling.

Nadia: "Plant it. Wherever you go. Don't ever let us forget the taste of this place."

You: "I won't."

Cassian steps close, and the world narrows to the single, steady orbit of his presence. His eyes are wet at the corners, but his voice is clear.

Cassian: "We'll build the mural on the new market wall. We'll teach. We'll plant. We'll get everyone trained. We will—" (he swallows, then smiles) "—we will make a town that remembers where it came from and also knows where to stand."

You (Mira): "Promise?"

Cassian: "Promise." (he tucks a paint-speckled bracelet into your palm) "A small token. For when you doubt."

You clutch the bracelet and the sapling. The two objects — one a keepsake, one a future tree — feel like the twin anchors of what you've chosen to save and how you will nurture what remains.

You look around the circle and in each face you read a story of loss braided with possibility: a neighbor who will take the stipend and a job in marsh restoration and a new beginning; a shopkeeper who will stay in the protected core and reopen when the tide settles; children who will learn to plant and count and steward.

It is not whole. It is not the town you grew up in, exactly. But it is the town given a path forward that keeps its people and the rhythms that make life worth living.

Cassian: "We'll make sure the mural remembers those blocks. People won't vanish from our stories."

You (Mira): "We'll make sure they don't vanish from our work either."

You breathe in the damp, clean air. The salt tang tastes like a promise you can live in. Your guilt remains — a hollow echo — but beneath it runs a current of something stronger: the knowledge that you, with others, built a bridge instead of a wall. You saved more than houses; you saved lines of transmission, jobs, the school, the market. You held a town together with plans and soapboxes and seed pots.
