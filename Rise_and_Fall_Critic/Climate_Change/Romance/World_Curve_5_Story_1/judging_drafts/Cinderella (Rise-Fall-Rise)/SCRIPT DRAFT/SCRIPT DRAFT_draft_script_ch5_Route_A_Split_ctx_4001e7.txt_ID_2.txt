[Scene: The Drowned Orchard | Late Afternoon — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Low, roiling clouds press the horizon; the orchard's stilts cast black, trembling reflections in the swollen water.]
[Music: A low, insistent cello undercut by distant percussion; the melody leans toward dissonance.]
[Sound: Wind gnashes across rope handrails; water slaps against raised mounds; a sensor chirps a frantic, uneven alert.]

You are in the orchard because this place is where the town remembers itself — grafted trees on mounded soil, lanterns knitted by children, a hundred small efforts to keep something living where the sea wants it back. Today the memory feels raw and exposed; the air is thick with salt and the metallic tang of storm runoff.

Your wristband chimes — a staccato you have trained yourself not to ignore. The LED flares urgent red against your damp sleeve. You press your thumb to the surface; the readout scrolls too fast. Somewhere, a wooden walkway gives with a soft crack. Algae along the margins pulses a ghostly green where the water sloshes over an overturned stilt.

You can taste iron on your tongue — not from blood but from the electric panic under your skin. The pilot you led three seasons ago replays behind your eyes: the wrong substrate, the edge you misjudged, the volunteers soaked and silent afterward. Guilt is a tidal undertow; it pulls without mercy.

[Visual: Close-up on a broken stilt; bioluminescent algae glows faintly as the wave withdraws.]
[Sound: The sensor chirps again, more insistent.]

Asha bursts through the knitted lantern arch, rain spitting from her short hair. Her lab glasses are crooked, a smear of salt or mud across a lens. She holds up a tablet like a flailed flag.

Asha: "Mira — you need to see this." (breathless; voice over the wind)

You step closer despite the way the walkway tilts underfoot. The tablet shows a cascade of graphs and a small simulation you know too well — frequency spectrums and waveforms that shouldn't line up the way they do.

You: "Talk to me."

Asha: (fingers trembling slightly on the tablet) "We modeled the barrier under expected conditions, yes. But the consortium's geometry — the reinforcement spacing — creates a resonance band. If waves hit that band during an irregular surge, the system amplifies certain oscillations. It transfers energy instead of dampening it."

You: "Amplifies—" The word sounds like an accusation. "So the barrier could shove energy sideways and hit places harder?"

Asha: "Exactly. It can— under a narrow set of angles and periods — excite standing waves along the seabed. The pilot data looked fine because we didn't see the high-period runs that match the new barrier's length. We missed the coupling term when we scaled the wall."

You want to ask whether this is a fix-you-can-patch or a structural problem. Asha's face folds with math and bad news.

Asha: "You can tweak geometry. You can add dampers. But 'tweak' is a temporary noun here — it means hours of rework, redesign, and an independent structural re-run. Or you can limit operations, which means delaying the rollout. Or..." She lets the sentence hang like a charge in the air.

[Visual: Pull back to show the orchard, the overturned stilt, volunteers clustered at a distance beneath plastic tarps.]
[Music: Cello grows angrier; a violin scratches high and thin.]

A shout from the shoreline slices the rain: Cassian. He is already there, paint-splattered sneakers smeared with mud, hair matted, eyes like hot coals.

Cassian: "Jonah sent people to fast-track the southern reinforcement — without the newest wave-form data. He signed greenlight requisitions last week!"

You can hear accusation in the syllables. He storms down a plank, rain breaking from the brim of his jacket. He doesn't wait to ask how you're feeling; he waits to place blame.

You: (voice steady despite everything) "Cassian—"

Cassian: (cuts you off) "Don't 'Cassian' me. We told them not to do it that way. We told them it would redirect energy. We told them—"

You reach for him almost by reflex: replace the fury with a hand, anchor him so he doesn't go where anger will make the meeting worse. The cold of his shoulder under your palm steadies you; his breath smells of wet paint and fear.

Jonah's voice arrives threaded through a radio and footsteps; a small, efficient delegation appears at the boardwalk's edge, rain-slicked schematics folded into waterproof covers. Jonah himself steps into the orchard, collar up, expression composed to a practiced degree you want to bruise.

Jonah: (calm, businesslike) "Mira. Asha. We monitored the models. There are edge cases, but—" (he gestures at his team) "—our engineers believe modifications will eliminate the coupling. We can phase changes as we go."

Asha cuts in before you can. Asha: "It's not a free patch. The contract language shifts asset responsibility to the consortium if we accept unilateral modifications without independent validation. Maintenance could be privatized. That changes who decides what 'safety' means."

Jonah: (soft smile that doesn't reach his eyes) "Privatize maintenance is a blunt phrasing. We are offering a public-private arrangement that ensures continuous funding for upkeep. Without investment, the living systems will starve for labor and capital. How is that any different from ensuring we have the resources to maintain them?"

Cassian's laugh is bright and brittle. Cassian: "Because funding tied to control is not stewardship, Jonah. It's ownership. You do not get to privatize care and call it salvation."

Jonah: "Care requires resources, Cassian. You make it sound like an accusation when it's a fact."

You: (internal) The words stack like planks between hearts. The town needs both resources and consent. You try to measure them both, but the measuring tape keeps snapping.

Ruben appears at the entrance to the orchard, cane scraping a pattern into the boardwalk. His face is weathered to a map of grief; he looks smaller beneath the rain.

Ruben: (voice thin but urgent) "Mira! The northside paths are flooding — now. We can't stay. Get people moving."

Nadia is beside you in a flash; she presses her hands to your sleeve, fingernails cold and trembling. Her eyes search you for an instruction rather than an answer.

Nadia: (whisper) "Please, Mira. Ruben says go. Please."

You feel the tug — responsibility to the living, to the immediate safety of neighbors, and the immovable knot of what this barrier means if it fails. The old pilot's ghosts crowd close enough to breathe: children's gumboots stuck in muck, volunteers face-down in exhausted silence. You remember the apology you never quite learned to offer.

The orchard thins as people respond to the practical." || Choice B ("Walk to Jonah, stare him down, and demand immediate shutdown until independent tests are done") -> Reaction: "You cross the wet boardwalk and lock eyes with Jonah; your voice is small but carries: 'Stop it.' The crowd hushes. Jonah's jaw ticks; his team glances from you to the tablet in Asha's hands as if searching for the ground to stand on."]


[Visual: Rain crescendos; wavelets slap against stilts; a large swell rolls in, black and glassy, higher than the last.]
[Music: The cello thuds like a heartbeat; percussion begins to mimic distant thunder.]

The surge finds a thinness in the new reinforcement — a place where geometry and tide converse in a language you thought you understood. A section of the living berm — a place you argued to keep in the plan — slumps, the soil giving where tree roots once held. Voluntaries clutch lantern poles, eyes wide.

You: (internal monologue) This is the moment the ledger turns. All your cautious gains and published protocols mean nothing if the people in front of you don't have roofs tonight. But if this design flaw is real and goes unexposed, the harm could be systematic, long-reaching, displacing neighborhoods you swore to protect.

Cassian: (to Jonah, voice raw) "You promised oversight. You promised responsive design. If this is what oversight looks like, you've sold us a ledger with holes in it."

Jonah: (wearing pragmatism like armor) "If we pull now, we risk losing funding and giving the consortium reason to withdraw entirely. That would leave the town—" (he stops, choosing a softer word) "—without any large-scale support."

Asha: (quietly, almost to you) "We can call for an independent review, force a recalibration. It buys time, but time is what the sea takes when it gets a taste."

You can feel the room of people — volunteers wrapped in plastic, engineers with clipboards, the mayor's liaison trying to look effective — pressing expectations into your spine. Nadia's hands are still in yours. Ruben looks at you like a man asking his child to stop a tide with nothing more than a pocketknife.

Your options line up, each with its own casualties: accept a compromise that may move strangers and save roofs; expose the flaw and watch the funding and jobs evaporate; or demand a delay and review that could save both but might let the sea take its first predictable bite.

Your throat tightens. The rain drums a personal Morse code: decision, decision, decision.

You breathe in the orchard's wet air and try to find the thin thread of your north.

[Visual: The orchard's lanterns swing; the sea beyond reflects a slit of gunmetal sky.]
[Music: The cello dwindles, leaving a single note to tremble.]

The world narrows to the count of three impossibilities.
