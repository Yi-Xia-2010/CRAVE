[Scene: Glassed Conference Room | Morning]
[Visual: A glass-walled room looking out over the boardwalk; reflections make faces and papers double. Jonah Hale sits across from you, his slim slate open. Your salt-stained khaki jacket feels heavier than it did on the boardwalk; you tuck your hair ceremoniously behind your right ear.]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings; a steady piano undercurrent suggests building confidence.]
[Sound: Quiet HVAC hum, the distant cry of gulls, the soft tapping of Jonah's stylus on glass.]

You breathe in. The room smells of coffee and the faint metallic tang of rain on glass. The light off Jonah's silver band catches and throws a small pulse across the table, a metronome for the conversation ahead.

Jonah Hale: "The phased timelines are realistic, Mira. Six months for the reinforced choke points, staggered planting windows so we don't disrupt migratory cycles. Funding is in the first tranche if we sign the memorandum."

You: "Signing without clear ownership clauses leaves the work vulnerable. We need the habitat corridors secured in perpetuity — community trusteeship, public maintenance funds, and—" (you press your palms flat to emphasize a point) "—a clause that prevents unilateral commercial redevelopment."

Jonah Hale: (his tone smooth, practiced) "Community trusteeship can be structured. It complicates bonds, but it's not impossible. What I can't promise is undefined delay. Our investors need measurable outputs: reduced inundation risk within a year, quantifiable shoreline accretion. That's how the funding pipeline stays open."

You feel the calculus split into two rhythms: the immediate, measurable deliverables that keep Jonah's funding machine running, and the slow, living work of marshes and corridors that needs time and patience. You fight the old, familiar tug of wanting the two to align perfectly — the place where your failure three seasons ago still lives.

Mayor Lin Park: (leans forward, fingers linked) "We need both: confidence for the insurers and faith for the residents. Mira, Jonah — we can't choose one and abandon the other. Is there a middle path you both can sign onto today?"

Asha: (slides a tablet toward the table) "The models support a hybrid. If we pair engineered reinforcements at the four identified choke points with a distributed network of living berms, projected risk drops below our threshold. Importantly, the simulations account for escalation scenarios. Here's a sensitivity run showing adaptive capacity if sea-level rise accelerates."

[Visual: Asha's tablet projects a soft map: hatched marsh zones, orange choke points, green corridors. The glass makes the overlay feel like an island floating on the table.]
[Sound: The soft whoosh of a slide change; someone in the hallway laughs quietly.]

Jonah Hale: (studies the map) "Those are conservative estimates. With maintenance budgets and scheduled audits, we can make the investor case. The design firms I've briefed are on standby."

You: (a small, steadying inhale) "We will need legally binding community stewardship. Volunteer brigades aren't enough if the municipality changes priorities. I want clauses that require stakeholder boards — residents, scientists, and town representatives — to sign off on future modifications."

Jonah's jaw tightens for a fraction of a second; then his smile returns, calibrated.

Jonah Hale: "That can be written. It will require indemnities and an oversight committee with technical expertise. I can add it to the memorandum as an amendment. It'll slow the process by—"

You: "Not 'slow the process' — anchor it. The pace we choose can't be measured only by construction milestones."

Mayor Lin Park: (softly) "Mira's right. Social trust is as much a part of resilience as concrete and monitoring. If the town trusts the process, compliance and long-term maintenance improve."

Asha: "And the pilot data will be transparent. External audits every quarter, open-access datasets. We can publish sensor streams from the field so residents see the numbers."

Jonah Hale: (counting off on his fingers) "Quarterly audits, community trusteeship, a phased plan with choke-point reinforcement first, marsh scaling alongside—if the clauses are draftable, we can present the memorandum tonight for signature."

You can feel the room leaning toward agreement. Your pulse picks up — hopeful, nervous, a brittle joy at the edges, like sunlight on storm-dark glass. You force yourself to slow your breath and keep the conversation on the precise things that will keep the community whole.

Jonah Hale: (extends his hand, slate now closed) "Then we have terms. We'll draft the final memorandum with your edits. I'll have counsel ready within twenty-four hours."

You: (a small exhale that feels like a tide slipping back) "If the community trusteeship and transparent audits stay, then we have a path that keeps land and living together. We will work with engineers to make sure maintenance funding is locked in and that the design remains adaptive."

Asha: (softly) "And we'll publish the pilot results openly. No black boxes."

Jonah Hale: (measured) "Open data, oversight committee, phased construction with immediate pilot demonstrations — we can do that."

You look down at the memorandum sheet, then at the faces reflected in the glass: Jonah's polite assurance, Mayor Lin's steady agreement, Asha's focused relief. You feel the brittle joy refine into something steadier. It isn't whole; nothing about adapting to a rising sea ever is whole. But it is durable, measured, and shared.

You make a small note in your leather notebook: "Memorandum: terms + stewardship + audits — push for legal language." You close the notebook and feel the leather give under your palm like a small, answered question.
