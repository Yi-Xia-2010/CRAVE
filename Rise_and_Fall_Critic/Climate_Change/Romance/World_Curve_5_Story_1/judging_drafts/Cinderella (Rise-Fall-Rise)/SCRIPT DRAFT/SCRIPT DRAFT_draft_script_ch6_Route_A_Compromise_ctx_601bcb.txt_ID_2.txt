[Scene: The Drowned Orchard | Dusk]

Nadia stands in front of a small gathered group — a mix of relocated families, volunteers, and elders. She holds a ribbon looped around a sapling, her voice steady and unexpectedly grown-up.

Nadia: "We move with care. We plant with hands that want to stay. This isn't leaving — it's making a place where we can keep our stories and grow new ones."

Ruben hauls crates of orchard saplings up onto the raised mounds, grinning like a conspirator. He brushes dirt from his gloves and looks at you.

Ruben Ortega: "You kept our roots in the deal, Mira. We get to teach the kids how to graft and how to read the sky. That work? That will be our inheritance."

You press a sapling into the prepared soil, feeling the give of peat mixed with hopeful compost. Each planting is small — one life at a time — but together they create a spine that will hold the community through tides to come. The relocation fund hums in the background: not a bandage, but a bridge. Jobs are already listed on the board: marsh technician, trainee sensor tech, restoration crew lead. People are signing up.

Cassian joins you by the walkway, and the two of you fall into an easy rhythm, stepping between roots and raised soil. The evening sky is a band of rust-orange and sea-glass green.

Cassian: "Remember when we used to sneak out to the lighthouse to paint the railing?"

You: "You fell off the railing. I still have the scar." You laugh, and the sound is soft with memory.

Cassian: "I gave you a paintbrush for that scar." He bumps your shoulder with his, then reaches out and tucks the sleeve of your jacket against the tidewatcher pendant. "You did it. You did the thing that didn't need to be perfect to be good."

Your fingers find his in the dim. The knitted lanterns create small private suns all along the orchard paths. Around you, people are talking in low voices: plans for apprenticeships, schedules for family moves, promises to check on one another's foundations.

You: (thinking) This is not a utopia. There will be storms we misjudge and promises we must keep by working harder than we think possible. But the town's voice is un-muted now. The compromise bent us without breaking us; it kept space for the small, essential things.

You trace the tidewatcher with a thumb, feeling its cool ridges. The pilot's failure sits behind your ribs like a tutor whose lessons are sharp but fair. Without that caution, the agreement wouldn't carry the safeguards it now has. Compromise here meant saving most of what mattered — not all, but the vital parts that let a community breathe.
