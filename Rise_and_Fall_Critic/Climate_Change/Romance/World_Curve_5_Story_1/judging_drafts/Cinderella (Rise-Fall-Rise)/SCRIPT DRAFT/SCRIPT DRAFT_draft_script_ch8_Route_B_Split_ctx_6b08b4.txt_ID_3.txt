[Scene: Drowned Orchard | Night — Storm]
[Visual: Knitted lanterns swing low over black water; their stitches glow like small surrendered stars.]
[Music: Low, reedy cello; distant thunder throbs like a held breath.]
[Sound: Wind ripping rope; the wooden crack of a walkway snapping; water slapping against stilts.]

You wake to sound before you understand it — a living, paper-slice noise that makes the world tilt. Lantern light stutters through the curtains of rain. Somewhere in the orchard a rope snaps, then the brittle percussion of wood giving way. Your heart answers the sound with a single, immediate command: move.

You shove your feet into wet boots, the leather sucking at your soles. The air is salt and wet wool, and under it something sour — a bright metallic tang you recognize from too-much-surge: disturbed sediment, stirred like mud in a cup. Your throat tightens around the tidewatcher chain at your collar; the brass is strangely cold against your sternum.

[Visual: Close-up — your hand fumbles for the waterproof pack by the door; inside, the sensor case clicks faintly.]
[Sound: Rain hissing against the boardwalk, footsteps sliding; a harmonica note collapsed by the wind.]

You run.

[Scene: Boardwalk Approach | Night — Storm]
[Visual: The mural of marsh grass is half-blurred with rain; volunteers' silhouettes move like kelp in a current.]
[Sound: Shouts, a splintering snap, the sucking, obscene pull of pressure.]

At the orchard, chaos has a shape you can read: a torn walkway, lanterns spinning in slow arcs; a neighbor clutching a snapped post where the path used to be. People shout names across the noise; a child's voice stutters and then goes quiet. Cassian stands at the edge, paint on his knuckles now smeared into water, his mouth working as if there are words he can't hold. He looks hollow, the light cutting his features into someone you can both recognize and not.

Ruben is a movement in the water — and then a face. You see him being whipped toward the bay, frailer somehow, his cane splintered into jagged teeth beside him. The surge takes him like an insult.

You don't think. Your hands find rope and arm and a volunteer's cold fingers. The world narrows to weight and breath.

Cassian: "Hold him—now! Tie him to the post—Mira, the post—"

You: "On the left—grab his jacket! Nadia, pull—pull!"

Nadia's hands burn against your own as you haul. The sea is a clever animal; every time you heave, it tries to take back its prize. When you drag Ruben over the lip of the drift, he coughs up something like black foam and his breaths come in the small, frightened gasps of someone who has been nearly stolen from life.

Ruben's eyes find yours. For a moment, for the longest fraction of time, the tide is a distant roar and you listen only to him.

Ruben: (raspy) "You... always... read the tide wrong for me, girl." (a dry chuckle, then a cough)

You: "Don't be clever. Breathe. You're here."

Ruben's hand feels small in yours; his cane is a splintered thing. He looks smaller, not only because the water took his shadow, but because the sea has removed the things that once set him upright. You press your palms into fabric to stop the tremor you can feel building under your skin — the old fear that a failed plan will be visible in the cracked wood and the gashed banks.

[Visual: Close shot of your hands — mud under fingernails, sensor straps slick with rain.]
[Music: A minor chord, strings keening softly.]

Asha arrives, tablet clutched like a talisman. Her face is a map of focus and exhaustion; she kneels beside Ruben and runs a hand over his back, checking for broken ribs with the efficiency of someone who knows how small a window for survival can be.

Asha: "He's conscious. Shock, hypothermia—possible contusion. Someone get thermal blankets."

Volunteer: "Where's the heater? Who has extra—"

Your chest is a drum where every memory of the last failed pilot keeps beating. The pilot you ran three seasons ago hiccupped and then failed in a surge that felt embarrassingly small in memory until tonight. You taste that past — iron and salt — like a ledger you carry. Your fingers tighten around the sensor pack at your hip as if it is both a key and a confession.

[Scene: Immediate Aftermath — Shoreline | Night — Storm]
[Visual: Lanterns bob; someone has tied a crumbling buoy to a post. The orchard's elevated mounds shudder as waves rework the sand.]
[Sound: The wind carries fragments of conversation — accusations and prayer braided together.]

Ruben is wrapped in blankets, coughing salt from his lungs. He is small and stubborn and infuriatingly alive. The crowd around him is an organism rearranging itself in response to shock. Cassian sits a pace away, paint-streaked palms splayed, watching the water as if it had betrayed him.

Cassian: (whisper) "Not again. Not like this."

You: "It wasn't 'not again' until tonight. The berms held for months. We—"

Cassian: (interrupting) "We trusted ropes and grass and patience. We believed small hands could fix the sea. That was the plan. And now—" (his voice breaks) "Look at him. Look at what the tide took."

Your anger against him rises — not at Cassian, not really, but at the way the sea makes scapegoats of ideals. The town is singing a new hymn: blame. You feel it in the press of shoulders and the way eyes move toward Jonah before the man himself arrives.

[Visual: A dark silhouette steps across the ragged edge of the boardwalk: Jonah Hale, coat buttoned against rain, his slate tucked like an accusation under his arm.]
[Sound: His footsteps are too measured for the night; the storm seems to hush politely for him.]

Jonah's face is composed; his voice is already designed for transmission.

Jonah: "This is exactly why we need a permanent barrier. Measurable, engineered, reliable. Look at what happens when we leave it to half-measures."

You: (cold) "You're here to sell a project, Jonah. This is not a data point — this is Ruben."

Jonah: "Ruben is a person, and the project's purpose is to prevent more Rubens. We can't wait for experiments to tell us the obvious. We need infrastructure."

Cassian: (bitter) "Your 'infrastructure' means digging people out of their homes to build your dream wall."

Jonah: "It means saving lives, Cassian. It means investments, insurance, futures."

The mayor's emergency vehicle arrives in a wash of blue light, cutting through the rain like a promise half-kept. Mayor Lin Park steps out, hair pulled into a rain-slick bun, eyes shadowed with the weight of decisions. Her expression is unreadable — the neutral plane of someone balancing grant lines and votes.

Mayor Lin: "Status report. What do we have?"

Asha: "Sediment loads spiked near the eastern berm. Currents shifted; we saw a backflow around the orchard that undermined pilings. Sensors show an erosion pulse, but the exact vector is—"

You: "—is not something you can fix with a single wall. The hydrology is changing under us. A barrier could protect one face and starve another. We need careful modeling before you sign anything."

Jonah: "Careful modeling? The funding windows close. Contracts will be offered to other towns if we don't move. Mayor, this is the moment to choose the secure option."

Mayor Lin: (softly) "We don't have unlimited money, Jonah. We have families excluding themselves from lifelines the town has built. We have a public that is terrified."

The crowd's mood is a live thing: fear mutates into fury. Voices thread into a chorus that leaves no single person unblamed. Someone begins to shout about negligence, someone else about naïveté, and the air tastes of all the arguments you have rehearsed and none of the ones you now need. A child's sob pierces the white noise.

You feel your field notebook heavy in your jacket — more a talisman than a solution. Your mind flicks through calculations: sediment transport, wave refraction, the pilot's failures folded into tonight's humiliation. Each equation feels like a confession you haven't yet spoken aloud.

Asha: (leaning in, voice low) "If we can get the mayor to pause the procurement, even for a week, we can run targeted surveys. We can—"

Jonah: (cutting) "A pause will kill the funding stream, Asha. Pause and the consortium walks. Pause and the political argument is lost."

Cassian: (to you, voice ragged) "So do we scream louder, or do we sew blankets and move people? Which story saves them faster?"

Your hands are still wet. The sky presses a low, indifferent roof over everything. You look at Ruben curled in a blanket, at Nadia holding a child's hand, at the lanterns like surrendered stars. The choices arrange themselves into impossible shapes: stay and lose many; resist and risk legal ruin; beg bureaucracy to slow the cart and hope the pause holds.

Cassian watches your mouth form each word. His eyes are a raw ledger of hope and grief. You meet him there and see the fracture that tonight has revealed between desire and method.

Cassian: "If we start a fight, we might get attention. We might get arrests. We might also close doors we need open later."

You: "And if we don't fight, Jonah gets the narrative: engineering or chaos. Families get priced out under the promise of 'security.'"

Cassian: (voice small) "I don't know which is worse."

Your chest contracts until the air is a needle. The orchard's lanterns bob in the water like a necklace surrendered to the tide — tiny casualties in a larger arithmetic.

Mayor Lin: (finally) "What are you asking me for, Mira? Speak plainly."

You collect the words you have been folding into drafts and meetings: dignity, safety, transparency. You feel the old fear of making a choice that costs people their anchors, the same fear that once kept you waiting at a lectern while a tide came up in the distance. The town's future—its soul, if such things can be measured—spreads before you like a map with too many red ink marks.

Your voice comes out steady because it must.

You: "We need a plan that preserves dignity and safety. Right now there are three paths: we can organize a supported relocation for those most at risk, we can escalate direct action to force political change, or we can bring emergency data to you and ask for a pause and redesign. Each will hurt in different ways. We need honest choice, not politics dressed as inevitability."

Jonah: (calm) "The only honest choice is to build a barrier they'll pay for willingly when they see what happens otherwise."

The storm eats his words and spits them back as accusation. The town's edges ripple; some faces harden, some falter.

You look at Cassian, at Nadia, at Ruben — at every person who has trusted you to translate science into safety. For a breath, the weight of past decisions presses so hard you are sure you will crack.

You: (to the gathered crowd) "We decide now. We decide in grief and with hope. But we decide together."
