[Scene: Boardwalk & Market | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: Long wooden planks slick with last night's spray; strings of yellow bulbs wobble in the breeze]
[Sound: A gull crying, distant clatter of a vendor testing a tarp]
[Music: Soft, hopeful piano undercurrent; wind-carried harmonics]

You wake before the market does. The world outside your window smells like the sea has pressed a palm against the town — salt and something mineral and honest. Your jacket still has the salt ring on the cuff from last night's walk along the Floodwall; the silver strand at your temple catches a streetlight and looks like a tiny lighthouse of its own. The rolled map under your arm is damp where it presses against you, and the edges smell faintly of old paper and ink and rain.

Across the boardwalk, under the lean shadow of a repair stall, Jonah is hauling a length of rigging. Up close the waxed canvas of his vest smells of coffee and tar; even from here that scent pulls at the hems of memory — mornings when the bay was calm, when the work was simpler, when he would grin and call you an alarmist for putting sandbags under a fence. He meets your eyes. The amber in his gaze folds sunlight you haven't earned yet into something warm.

Your chest tightens. Hope and the ghost of something like fear sit on top of each other like seafoam.

Jonah: "Morning, Solace." (He hauls the coil and grins; the crow's-feet at the edge of his eyes deepen.)
You: "Morning." (You tuck the map tighter against your ribs so he can't see the pencil marks listing pilot locations.)

Jonah: (He pauses, the coil draped over one shoulder.) "You look like you're carrying the tide itself. You alright?"
You: "I'm fine. Busy is fine." (You laugh; it comes out too breathless. You don't want to let him see how many times you've rehearsed asking for help.)

Jonah: "Busy suits you." (Tilt of head.) "We're at the old south mooring later if you want a look. I'm fixing a float—might be able to jury-rig it into something that lasts through spring."

There are so many small openings in Jonah's voice — a map of possibilities — and you find yourself measuring which ones you can step into without tripping over promises you don't yet have the authority to make.

[Scene: The Saltworks — Exterior to Interior | Dawn]
[Visual: Corrugated metal painted seafoam green; north windows fogged from the day's first heat]
[Sound: The misting rigs humming; the scrape of a chair leg]
[Music: Light acoustic arpeggios mingle with low, pulsing inverter hum]

You cross the market as the first stalls unzip. Smoked fish steam fogs your breath. The Saltworks door opens with a creak that has already become a domestic punctuation in your life. Inside, cool diffuse light pools across benches. Seedling trays sit like small green cities on long tables. Prototype berms — small ridges of sand, root fiber, and planted cordgrass — glint with residual moisture. Your fingers brush damp soil; dirt makes little maps on your thumb.

Asha is already there, face illuminated by the soft cyan glow of the LED strips she's lining across a test rain gutter.

Asha: (Murmuring to herself) "If I angle them like this, the light will simulate cloud cover earlier... and—you'll see, the sensors will read different evapotranspiration." (She smiles without looking up, the kind of grin that could short-circuit a spreadsheet.)

You crouch beside the trays, running a fingertip along a tender stem. The seedlings bend, then spring back like a promise.

You: "How are the sensors reading?"
Asha: "Temperate. Good. Raff sent drone footage overnight — his angles actually make the new terracing look cinematic." (She gestures to a tablet. Her hands are streaked with potting mix.) "If Ben sees that footage, it's a narrative win."

You: (You take the tablet and study the footage. From the drone's silent eye, the terraces look heroic — long lines of panels like a staircase to the sky, native grasses waving in sync.)
Asha: (Lowering her voice) "You rehearsed the agenda?"
You: "Three pilot sites, phased trials, co-management framework, community oversight clause. I want to be careful with language — 'managed retreat' is a phrase that closes doors." (You feel each word like a bead on a line.)

Asha: "You're threading a needle, Marin." (Her voice goes soft.) "But people listen when you don't shout. You make practical things sound like honor."

Their praise settles like dust and light. You like that about Asha: she says the quiet truth when the town needs it.

Raff's voice comes in as a ping from the tablet, bright and ridiculous.

Raff (through tablet): "Dropped the footage live. No caption — organic reach climb. Also, put 'Save Our Shorelines' on a sticker mockup. People like stickers."

You: (Half-smiling) "Leave the viral to you. I'll handle the wording."

Raff: "Cool. Also—Elder Mae dropped off something for you. Said you might need a sugar boost before the meeting and a piece of history attached."

You: "Salted lemon tart?" (You glance up, expecting a small paper bag. The idea alone warms you.)

Raff: "Yeah. Also a story, apparently. She left a note: 'History neuters hubris.'"

The phrase settles in your chest like a stone thrown into a well. You turn it over mentally: history neuters hubris. So many of your designs are answers to hubris—concrete promises or quick fixes. Elders and tides both remind you about what remains when the noise dies down.

[Scene: The Saltworks — Center Table | Mid-morning]
[Visual: A long table strewn with maps, coffee cups, and a salted lemon tart on a paper plate]
[Sound: Low murmur of the town waking outside; the inverter hum like an expectant heartbeat]
[Music: Strings lift gently, rising in a hopeful loop]

Elder Mae arrives before Ben. She moves with a slow certainty, braid over her shoulder. Her shawl smells of dried seaweed and lavender. She sets the tart on the table with the kind of ceremony that makes you feel like a child again — the sort of ceremony that announces: here is sustenance, and here is a lesson.

Elder Mae: "When I was a girl, the first storm took Mr. Hargrove's skiff clean out of his hands. He sat on the pier and watched it go, and the tide took his swear-words away." (She looks at you.) "We built then. We built without asking the sea to join us. Remember that before you build."

You: "You always tell it so plainly." (You reach for the tart, but hesitate because the metaphor still tastes of salt.)

Elder Mae: "Plainness is mercy, child. And if you can make mercy into something people can use—legally, physically—then you will have done right."

You: (You bite into the tart. The lemon is sharp; the salt brightens the flavor into something that feels like sun on a cold morning. Your plan for the meeting proves itself more palatable with sugar in your mouth.)

Asha: (Whispering) "Ben's office called. He says he'll be late — traffic and a council briefing. But his assistant said they read Raff's footage. That's good. People like a story they can picture."

You: "We have to make the story ours, not just pretty footage. The pilot's technical details, but framed as stewardship: who keeps the mooring, who tends the berms, how funding goes into wages not into spectacle."

Asha: "You know the line. Practical, principled. Not idealistic, not conciliatory."

You: (You set the agenda between two coffee cups. The words feel like anchors: pilot, co-management, community oversight. You run timings on your fingers — ten minutes for introductions, fifteen for the berm demonstration, twenty for question time. Your voice rehearses itself in the margins.)

Elder Mae: "Don't forget to ask for what you need." (She taps her index finger on the paper.) "A request is not a weakness."

You: "I know."

Elder Mae: "Then ask for it plainly."

Ben Rhee has not yet appeared, and that absence hums with possibility. His visit could mean formal recognition, funding, politics folded into pavement. Or it could mean compromise, the kind that smells like new concrete and leaves a bitter aftertaste.

You run through the rhetorical line again, softer this time: practical, principled.

Raff: (Tapping the tablet, excited) "Also—Jonah's going to swing by after the market. Might be handy. He owes me a favor for that rigging tutorial."

You: (Your heart—absurdly—hops. You were planning to ask him for help retrofitting a mooring after the meeting. Now the possibility is a hinge under your breath.) "Good. Tell him to bring his splice kit."

Raff: "Told you. Small towns are small worlds. Also, Ben's assistant said to expect some questions about liability. Be ready."

You: "Liability is paperwork. People are the real problem."

Asha: "Paperwork can kill a project as surely as a storm." (She leans in.) "We'll have the co-management clause drafted. We'll suggest an independent oversight body. We make room for fishermen on the pilot's steering committee."

You: "And wages for maintenance." (You write it down in bold.)

Asha: "And a clause for adaptive management — if something fails, we convene and fix rather than double down."

You: (You imagine the steering committee around a table, fingers pointing at tide charts, voices steady.) "Okay. We can do this."

[Sound: A vehicle door closing outside; footsteps approach]
[Visual: The north window brightens; light spills across the table like something opening up]

You stand, the tart half-eaten on the plate, a map in your arm, words in your mouth. The air in the Saltworks feels charged — not with panic but with that clean surge that comes when a town begins to move together. You have rehearsed the line between practical and principled until it's comfortable in your mouth; you have allies; the footage is in circulation; Elder Mae has given you food and history and a nudge.

In the next breath, you decide you will ask Jonah to help retrofit one of the old moorings after the meeting.

You smooth your jacket and step toward the door, tongue tasting lemon and salt and the bright metal of intention.
