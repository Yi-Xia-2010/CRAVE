[Scene: Old Shipyard Ruins | Dusk]
[Visual: Rusting cranes silhouette against a pewter sky; half-submerged hulks glint where water laps between ribs of metal]
[Sound: Wind through hollow hulls, the distant, hollow thunk of crane chains; gulls complaining like old neighbors]
[Music: Low, hopeful cello undercurrent]

You arrive with the taste of the council room still in your mouth — the measured applause, Ben's careful folding of hands, the way the room seemed to exhale when Lydia's slides closed with a single, clean graph. The decision is done: the town voted for rapid protection. You've carried that verdict down from the podium like a small, bright weight.

The air at the shipyard tastes of iron and salt, with a smear of diesel that hangs low in the cold. Puddles catch the sky and throw it back in broken pieces. Cranes, new and bureaucratic beside the skeletal bones of old yards, cut the horizon into geometry. Work lights are already slung like wary constellations along the newly poured concrete bank.

Jonah stands by a low stack of coiled rope, one hand worrying at his braid rope bracelet. He is a cut against the cranes — sun-bleached hair knotted loosely, a fisherman's posture still in his shoulders. A foreman in a high-visibility jacket is arguing about access points, voice clipped with contractor certainty.

Foreman: "We need to limit access for safety. Boats come in for official docking at the retrofitted slips. Open access is a liability."

Jonah: "You mean closing the old channel. That's where half the neighborhood ties up at low tide. You can't reroute tradition with a permit form."

Foreman: (gestures toward the cranes as if they sign contracts by themselves) "This isn't about tradition. It's about keeping water off porches. We do it properly, or we expose everyone."

Jonah: (harder) "Keep water off porches, yes — but not at the cost of pushing people off the water entirely. You tell me how I show up to my customers if there are no honest slips left."

You stay at the edge of the argument, map rolled and tucked into your satchel, the paper dampening at the corners. Your first instinct — the engineering muscle honed through years of sketches and late nights — bristles. Seawalls look clean in slides: straight lines, numbers, timelines. On the ground, they are iron and exclusion and the smell of fresh concrete that doesn't forget the living things it displaces.

But you notice the faces in the small crowd gathered along the ruin's lip. A woman with knitting tucked under her chin exhales like someone who has been holding her breath for months. A teenager who'd been doubling as a deckhand hugs a jacket tighter and watches the cranes as if they were a new kind of lighthouse. Relief sits next to fear, not replacing it but making room for it.

You step forward, fingers cold against the rolled map, and Jonah's eyes find you — a quick, unreadable look. There are things between you that don't need saying tonight. You want to defend the cooperative's method; you also don't want to be the person who would deny an entire street's sleep.

You walk the edge of the site, boots splashing in a shallow pool where a ship's hull once lay proud. Here the town is a mix of rust and new promise: heavy equipment that will put people to work, contractor vans already bearing corporate logos, and a handful of local faces with callused hands signing up for the labor that pays now.

It is practical, immediate. It is also — unavoidably — binding.

[Scene: Floodwall & Community Garden | Early Evening]
[Visual: The new floodwall curves like a spine along the shore; community-painted murals stop mid-stroke where scaffolding obstructs]
[Sound: Children’s voices distant as someone tills a raised bed; an accordion of rain starts again, gentler than before]
[Music: Warm strings, light piano—ascending]

The wall itself is difficult to hate when the tide would otherwise be at the sills. People lean against its lee, hands tucked into pockets, sharing news like a net. Raised beds behind the structure are full of herbs and green stubbornness — small acts of defiance and faith. The murals along the wall are interrupted by scaffolds; a paintbrush rests in a jar, waiting.

Lydia's modular pavilion gleams atop the bluff — glass and matte steel, the interior clinical and lit like a watchtower. It sits in contrast with the patched humanity below: an island of policy where a thousand decisions are being drawn with measured strokes.

Ben Rhee approaches, tie loosened, the political lubricant of his smile in place. He offers no sanctimony; only the pragmatic cadence of someone who has to hold both sides steady.

Ben Rhee: "Marin. You did good today. People slept tonight who wouldn't have if nothing had moved. That matters."

You: (you want to say 'we did this'—but 'we' here includes more than your cooperative) "It matters that people can sleep. But measures without responsibility break faster than any wall."

Ben Rhee: (nods) "Which is why Lydia's asked you to be a technical liaison. Not as a figurehead — actually council-recognized. She wants someone who can advise on where living features could be integrated into the lee. It’s a seat at the table."

Lydia appears then, as if the glass pavilion had exhaled her out. She is a blade of precise motion: platinum bob, a slate tablet armoured to her side, eyes that make decisions feel like weather patterns.

Lydia Voss: "Marin. Thank you for meeting me at the site. We won't pretend—this is triage. But triage with longer-term thinking is still triage. We can build the wall quickly and still embed marsh windows, stepped planting shelves, engineered habitats. We need someone who understands both the engineering and the ecology."

You take in the offer like a lowered bridge: safe to cross, but it brings you into a structure whose supports you do not control.

Lydia Voss: (smiles, measured) "You have a cooperative with prototypes that could be useful in the wall's lee. If you accept the liaison role, you'll advise placement, help write adaptive clauses, and coordinate pilot living features adjacent to poured sections."

You: "And the cooperative? My team needs to stay autonomous. Our work can't become a PR veneer while the core decisions are made to satisfy investors."

Lydia Voss: (eyes steady) "You will have a mandate — council oversight included. We want community buy-in. Your presence legitimizes the integration. This is a pragmatic channel: influence from within."

Lydia's offer tastes like possibility and compromise. It is not everything you dream, but it is a hand extended into the machine. Ben's face is open with the relief of someone who has brokered a deal that keeps roofs dry tonight and gives him a line to draw in the sand for future accounting.

Jonah arrives at your side, the foreman now off to organize crews. He watches Lydia with a complex expression — pride at the new work, worry for what it asks of the water, and something softer that flickers when he looks at you. He does not speak for a beat, then steps forward.

Jonah: "If you go in, watch the small slips. Don't hand over the channels without a fight. Promise me that."

You: "I won't hand them over blind. I won't let 'safety' become an excuse for convenience."

Jonah: (squeezes your hand quickly — a brief, public tether) "That's all I ask."

[Visual: The pavilion glows at night like a clinical beacon; beneath, people return to their gardens and porches, the town taking a quieter breath]
[Sound: The hush after a long tide rolls out; closer, someone laughs softly over stew being shared]
[Music: Rising strings—cautious, hopeful]

Lydia's aide produces a tablet. The job description appears in sharp font: Technical Liaison — coordinate living features, advise on adaptive management, attend daily briefings, represent cooperative interests to contractors and council. The words are precise, framed in legalese and deadlines.

You read, your thumb smoothing damp ink of a procedural world into something you can hold. The role is a bridge: it would put you where designs are decided, where contractors and council meet, where the steel meets the salt. It is influence, yes. It is also tethering, a responsibility that would change your nights from lab prototypes to daily briefings.

Ben: "We need people like you inside the process, Marin. If you can help us ensure this wall isn't only concrete and exclusion, you do two things at once — you protect people now and preserve options for the future."

You stand with the tablet warmed under the pavilion light, Jonah's hand still on yours, Lydia's gaze planted like a measurement on your chest. The sound of concrete trucks humming, the rhythm of people breathing easier, the faint hope threaded through it all — it pulls at you.

You think of Asha in the Saltworks, late into the night sketching marsh shelves; of Elder Mae's hands pointing out tide lines on old maps; of Raff's steady stream of online stories that turned sympathy into motion. You think, too, of the cooperative's independence and what it took to build that bedrock.

Your fingers hover over the signature line on the tablet like a compass needle over a map. The choice is not simple; it will change the arc of the cooperative and, perhaps, your own day-to-day life. But from the edge of the ruin, from the line of wet concrete that now promises sleep, the offer holds the soft, stubborn promise of something rising — not perfect, but better than being drowned.

You haven't decided yet. You let the night hold that suspension — a breath between two tides.

[Visual: Lights crawl along the newly poured concrete like a line of small lanterns; for a moment the town breathes in time with them]
[Music: Single violin sustains, then resolves into a soft, anticipatory chord]

You feel the bridge bending under the weight of the future and of tonight's relief. The hand extended by the pavilion is real. The work is immediate. The risk is undeniable.

You close your fingers around the tablet and look at the line where the sea meets the poured edge — a bright, unforgiving border and a place where living things might yet be coaxed to return.
