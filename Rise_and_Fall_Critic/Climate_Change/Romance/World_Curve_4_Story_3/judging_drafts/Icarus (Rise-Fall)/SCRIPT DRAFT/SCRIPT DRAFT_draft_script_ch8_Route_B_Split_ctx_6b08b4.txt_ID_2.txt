[Scene: Lydia's Glass Pavilion | Early Evening]
[Visual: A low, modular pavilion perched on the bluff; floor-to-ceiling glass catches the tidal light. Inside, a long table of pale wood; digital displays float on matte steel easels. Your reflection in the glass is a tired, steel-blue echo across the room.]
[Sound: A distant gull, the constant hush of the bay, a low HVAC hum from the pavilion’s systems.]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings with a soft piano motif — hopeful, deliberate.]

You step into the pavilion with the covenant folded under your arm. The paper has the warmth of other people’s palms, signatures and annotations along its margins like small promises. The room smells faintly of ozone and metal, an almost-clean scent that feels like a machine's version of certainty. Your boots make soft, measured sounds on the polished floor; beyond the glass the boardwalk lights twinkle, distant and patient.

Lydia stands at the head of the table, one hand curled around her slate. Her face is the exact calm of someone used to arriving at a decision with the room already shaped to their plan. But the pause in her eyes when she sees you—that almost-minor adjustment—suggests she expected this to require negotiation, not assent. Ben is beside her, tie slightly askew from the wind, eager to keep everyone's eyes steady on the map projected between them. Jonah leans against a column, rope bracelet knotted white with salt, jaw set. Asha has a stained notebook open; Raff perches on a chair edge, fingers scrolling through footage on his phone.

You set the covenant on the table and let your fingertips rest on it for a beat. It anchors you: a paper boundary that demands language precise enough to protect people’s livelihoods and porous enough to let the town breathe. You remind yourself of Asha's drafts, Raff's videos, Elder Mae's quiet insistence that names and stories survive. Tonight the words you choose will either stitch those threads together or leave the fabric vulnerable.

Lydia: "Thank you for coming early, Marin." (She gestures toward the main display.) "We've redlined the investor terms and proposed a public outreach timeline. Quick wins matter—confidence brings funds. But we're not blind to local concerns."

You: "Quick wins can't be the only wins." (You fold your hands, steady.) "If we tape the town to a timeline that strips people out of the process, the wall will hold the water but not the community."

Lydia: (calm, practiced) "You're right that process matters. I'm offering a staged approach: initial hardening tied to performance benchmarks, and a co-management body that will phase in—after structure is proven—local oversight. Investors want measurable milestones before disbursing funds."

Ben: (interjecting) "From a political standpoint, we need visible action. The electorate wants to see progress. If we can show beach access restored, moorings secured—"

Jonah: (cutting across) "Moorings secured if the town still has the right to use them. That's not a symbolic demand, Ben. That’s how my family's business breathes." (His voice tightens; his hands are a map of worn knuckles.) "A performance clause doesn't guarantee access. What if the 'performance' metric says—'open access reduced for maintenance' and we lose our lanes?"

Asha: (leaning forward, earnest) "We can write the metrics to include guaranteed access lanes. We can make physical mooring rights explicit, and require alternative docking plans during maintenance windows. Transparency is not just a value—it's a legal lever."

Raff: (tapping his phone) "And we film the meetings. We put the community's voice in every press package. If investors see the human face, they lean in. If they don't, they bail. Video evidence matters."

You watch the conversation fold into itself and expand. The argument is technical—phrases like 'performance metrics,' 'escrow releases,' 'maintenance windows'—but beneath the language is the town's rhythm: tides and boats and the creak of boards. You translate the caveats into human terms as the room pulses with differing urgencies.

You: "Here's where we can make a meaningful compromise." (You gesture to the projected schedule; your finger traces the projected timeline.) "Binding mooring rights written into the contract. A co-management oversight board that has veto power over access changes during the first three years. And—this is important—escrowed funds tied to environmental performance monitored by an independent reviewer chosen from a list we approve."

Lydia's fingers drape across her slate. For a moment she seems to weigh the accountant's ledger against the canyon of public sentiment.

Lydia: "Vetoes make investors nervous. Their argument is liquidity—if decisions can be blocked, they see delay. But an independent reviewer—if we select someone with recognized credentials and a clear reporting rubric—that can reassure them while giving you protections."

Ben: "If we frame it as risk-management optimization—local co-management reduces reputational risk—investors actually like that. They want stories they can publicize as wins."

Jonah: (eyes on you) "Marin, will you make sure the wording says mooring RIGHTS—not 'access subject to future negotiation'—no gray zones."

You feel the directness of his gaze. His concern is practical and immediate; his loyalty glints. There's a current between you that is both soft and strung taut by responsibility. You measure your own desire to move the town forward with the need to hold what keeps people fed and rooted.

You: "I'll draft the clause. It will read: 'Permanent mooring rights for registered fishers, with documented lanes and physical markers to prevent encroachment.' And we'll require a maintenance plan that includes temporary facilities—floating docks, rotated mooring schedules—so operations can continue during works."

Asha: (paper rustling) "And transparency protocols: all meetings livestreamed, minutes posted within 24 hours, and a community liaison funded by the project. No closed-door changes to access."

Raff: "I can produce a short doc—'Why This Matters'—with interviews. We get the town's voice in the first investor release. It steers the narrative."

Lydia: (a small, almost-appreciative nod) "These are reasonable frames. If we can model the clauses as risk-mitigation and public-relations wins, the investors will tolerate tighter oversight—especially with performance escrow on environmental metrics."

Ben: "I'll take that to the council. The optics of local stewardship tied to measurable returns makes my job easier."

The conversation keeps turning, like current around a reef. You press where it needs pressure and soften where it needs consolation. The air softens as planning resolves a few razor edges; everyone leans forward, working through the legalese like seamstresses mending a frayed hem.

It occurs to you: this isn't just a negotiation of documents; it's the moment when the town's narrative either becomes an asset or gets bought as a marketing veneer. You feel the gravity of that choice. But beneath the political gears, something else hums — a rising feeling that solutions can be shaped to honor both safety and livelihoods.

You: "We can commit to a phased visibility plan: immediate physical protections for the most at-risk properties, simultaneous with community-driven living infrastructure pilots. Those pilots will be fully documented and promoted. Investors get photos and measurable 'wins' early. The town keeps a seat at the table and legal protections for fishing rights."

Lydia: (a small, almost unguarded smile) "If you can produce specific language on the mooring and the oversight board composition by morning, I'll bring it to my stakeholders with your framing. It reframes risk as co-produced stewardship."

You feel the rope of tension between public urgency and local rights loosen a fraction. This is not resolution—but it is progress. For the first time tonight, you allow yourself a real inhale, taste of kelp and rain and possibility.

A thread of laughter hums through the group—tired, genuine. The humor isn't frivolous; it's an exhale, a communal recognition that the hard work has begun and that people are choosing to build together.

Your reflection in the pavilion glass looks at you: steady, tired, ready. The tide outside keeps its slow, indifferent time, but here in the warm artificial light a different tide is forming—of agreements and clauses, of social bonds metamorphosing into enforceable language.

You fold your hands over the covenant again, feeling the weight of the signatures like ballast. You have to decide what tone the legal language will take: insist on binding guarantees, accept performance oversight, or step back and let Lydia lead for speed. Each choice holds a different kind of protection—and a different kind of loss.

The town is listening. Jonah's hand on the table is a small, visible prayer. Asha's pen lists action items. Raff's phone hums with potential distribution. Ben's calculation already runs ahead to the council meeting. Lydia's slate glows with scenarios. They look to you because you can translate technical caveats into language the town will swallow—and because your choices will say whether love and principle can coexist when a shoreline is at stake.

The room leans in, a collective inhale.
