You walk the estuary edge and note the first small betrayals: the current that used to push a thin green tongue of eelgrass at the lee now eddies into pockets of dark water; the water line a half-step farther from the boats. Elder Mae: (her voice like reeds) "The sea rearranges when we press it with our hands. It always does."

Elder Mae: (a slow, weary smile that holds no accusation) "Paper is good for remembering. But remember—they'll read paper and water will still move."

The next weeks feel like moving through low tide—stretched, muddy, revealing things that were always there and are now exposed. Oversight committees convene and the pages stack in neat legal piles. Committee members speak of mitigation: artificial marsh islands, funded transplants, conditional reimbursements. Lydia's team offers compensation; a mitigation fund is proposed. Ben reads the drafts aloud in meetings, his voice small in rooms that used to be full of laughter.

Lydia Voss: (on site, quietly) "We authorized extra monitoring. We're contracting with ecologists. If the models are correct, the system will re-equilibrate—"
You: "If the models are correct. But the models didn't keep Jonah's slip from silting. They didn't stop old Mrs. Garn's tenant from selling her boat."

Jonah: (to you, voice far) "Some things you can't model. Some things you feel."

A few residents sell outright. Notices appear on social media: moorings for sale, a dock's worth of gear sold to raise cash. Raff's videos document the human side—close-ups of ledger books, hands counting pennies, children looking at a boarded-up boat. The town splits along lines of urgency: those who cannot wait for slow fixes and those who refuse to abandon principle even when law looks porous.

Elder Mae: (slow, precise) "You held to your principle like it was a prayer. That's not a mistake. But the sea answers only to force and physics and time."

You remember the nights you pressed your thumb against the chipped enamel lighthouse pin and asked the world to be as simple as a symbol. You realize you have been bargaining with words in a place where water answers only with movement.

[Scene: The Floodwall & Community Garden | Night, Aftermath]
[Visual: The mural by the floodwall is half obscured by the shadow of the wall itself. A single lamplight throws a cone of yellow onto wet cobbles.]
[Sound: Taps of rain starting; distant voices arguing through paper-thin doors.]

The town's politics harden. Meetings that once had warmth become battlegrounds of procedural text. Ben apologizes to the community in council sessions that feel ritualistic; Lydia presents impact assessments and mitigation matrices; you stand and feel the old, steady conviction that animated you crack like driftwood under repeated waves.

People thank you sometimes—wide-eyed locals who remember the evenings on the boardwalk when you argued and stitched and planted. Others look at you as if your principles were a kind of indulgence, a luxury that allowed some to keep their homes while others left. Jonah does not turn away from you; he is present in the way hurt people are present: close, skeptical, a weight you cannot set aside.

Elder Mae comes to the garden at dusk and stands with you as rain begins in earnest. Her braid is wet with droplets that smell like sea and soot.

Elder Mae: "You did what you thought was right with the tools you had."
You: "And it wasn't enough. I wrote words and the law took a different hand."

Elder Mae: (the small, cold humor of someone who has seen many tides) "We all learn the same lesson. Paper doesn't change the planet, child. We are still here to keep each other. There's work left that no statute will do."

You look across the water at the seawall's clinical face and at the dying beds and the empty slips. You think of Jonah's sketchbook page stuck to his palm, of the way his eyes darkened with loss. You think of the committees, the steady minutes, the weeks of hope that were swallowed by a single administrative override. Your insistence on principle preserved language that proved hollow in practice when larger power intervened.

The calculus in your chest is bitter and clear: you preserved the right words, but words alone did not move the current. You asked for binding protections and you pushed for transparency. Those things mattered, but the sea does not care about the certainties of law. The ocean rearranges us.

You press your thumb against the chipped enamel lighthouse pin at your lapel, feeling the bite of salt on metal. You have a ledger of intentions and a map of mistakes, and in your hands both feel equally heavy.
