[Scene: Lydia's Glass Pavilion | Early Evening]
[Visual: The pavilion's glass walls glow against a bruised sky. Inside, a ring of digital maps cast cyan and coral light across a long table. Your reflection—steel-blue eyes, jacket collar damp from rain—stares back from the pane, doubled by Lydia's precise silhouette. Outside, the sea is a low voice; gulls call and fade.]
[Sound: The soft hum of climate-control, the distant slap of waves, a low threaded alert from Lydia's tablet.]

You step off the slick bluff and into the pavilion. The floor tiles are cool under your boots; the air smells of ozone and coffee and the clean antiseptic of organized plans. A tablet blinks across from Lydia, graphs scrolling in a slow, readable rhythm—projected surge lines, investor timelines, milestones with little green checkboxes already penciled in by someone else's certainty.

Ben is there first, tie loosened, mouth set in the careful smile of someone who has slept too little and promised more than he can always deliver. Jonah stands a little apart, one hand on the table, knuckles white against laminated pages—his rope bracelet knotted tight, salt-streaked and real as a promise. Asha's hair is a comet halo of copper; she clutches a thin stack of annotated schematics. Raff hovers near the door like a charging particle, phone already recording, eyes lit with the thrill of a story that might break open the town.

Lydia closes the tablet with the soft click of inevitability and looks at you. Her pale eyes are sharp but not unkind; her slate shows numbers that can be translated into safety and into headlines. She gestures, brief and efficient, toward the central display where three columns of compromise and risk shimmer.

Lydia Voss: "We can secure funding within thirty days if we give investors clear oversight and measurable milestones. We run a PR campaign tied to visible 'quick wins'—stabilize docks, shore up priority parcels, show that the town is protected. In return we ask for a performance-based oversight clause. It guarantees timelines but keeps contractors and funding coordinated."

Ben Rhee: "Politically, that's clean. Voters need to see protective work. It covers me and it covers the council."

Jonah: (His voice rough with the sea) "Performance clauses don't mean moorings. They don't mean a fisher can tie up at night without fearing a relocated pile or a locked gate. We need written access lanes, mowing rights—whatever legal language you need—but make it binding. Not a promise on a slideshow."

You hear the salt and strain in his voice and feel your chest tighten. The pavilion's glass frames him like a living protest—stubborn and capable of being worn down by waves, not just policy. You want to answer before Lydia does, to cut the institutional wording into phrases people can hold: "access lanes," "guaranteed mooring rights," "community enforcement."

Asha leans forward, pushing a paper toward Ben. Her handwriting crowds the margins with procedural steps and transparency clauses.

Asha: "Public oversight, a real co-management board—no backroom approvals. We publish meeting minutes, vendor bids, and the criteria for triggering contractor penalties. Let the town see the math." (She meets your eyes.) "If we're asking people to give up something, they deserve the whole ledger."

Raff's grin is quick and mischievous; his fingers fly across his phone.

Raff: "And we livestream the co-management meetings. Real faces, real questions—make it viral so investors can't quietly replace us with a PR-friendly committee."

Lydia's mouth tightens almost imperceptibly; she is used to the clean world of deliverables and predictable optics. You know exactly how she measures a good proposal: by its ability to reduce uncertainty and restore capital. But you also know that what Jonah asks for is not just comfort—it's the town's lifeline to the sea.

Lydia Voss: "Mooring rights can be written, yes—but 'binding' is a legal weight investors view as risk. They prefer performance metrics tied to ecological and structural benchmarks. If the mooring access contradicts tide modeling or construction logistics, we have to accommodate operational realities."

Ben: "We can draft conditional rights—guarantees that operate within safe operational bands. It hedges the town's needs against investor liability."

Your internal list runs through this like a tide: speed versus sovereignty, promises versus enforceability. The Saltworks' late-night maps ghost behind your eyes—the living terraces, Asha's prototypes, the children who might learn to knot a line along a rewilded dock. All of it depends on language that can be enforced.

You step closer to the display, feeling the faint vibrations through the table as the pavilion systems update the model in real time. Your reflection in the glass looks tired and alive all at once.

You translate, aloud, because that is how you do the work—making the technical feel like a handhold.

Marin Solace: "If we frame mooring rights as operational easements tied to specific GPS lanes and maintenance schedules, they can be performance-tested. We write checkpoints—if the town files an access complaint, it triggers an audit. If contractors fail audits, funds are withheld. That gives investors their metrics and the fishers their lanes."

Lydia studies you for a long beat, measuring the phrase. It's the same negotiation she always runs—find the shape of certainty and sell it as reassurance. You offer her a way to keep both things she wants: control and market confidence.

Lydia Voss: (softening slightly) "Conditional easements with audit triggers could work. They'd need an independent auditor and clear benchmarks tied to seasonal conditions. Public reporting would satisfy PR."

Asha: "We should also include a clause for adaptive management—if a solution harms the tidal flats or restricts fish movement, we reconvene and adjust. No 'we're done' clauses."

Jonah: "And the access lanes stay no matter what. Not subject to budget whims. Moored boats are part of our livelihood, not optional amenities."

Ben exhales. He looks at you and Jonah as if balancing two fragile things at once—policy and the town's patience.

Ben Rhee: "That's the hard part—how we say it in a way that counts in court and in headlines. Lydia, can your team build the investor-facing narrative around these guarantees? Can we promise immediate work and keep the mechanisms for oversight transparent?"

Lydia's fingers drum against the tablet; she is calculating, but there is an edge today that wasn't there months ago—a willingness to hold a little more weight if it means avoiding the catastrophe of an empty harbor.

Lydia Voss: "We can. We adapt the narrative: quick wins framed as mutually beneficial—investors get secure assets; the community gets enforceable access and oversight. We'll stage the PR to foreground local stewards, with your cooperative and your chartered fishers as the faces. But we need seats at the table and a legal framework that protects delivery timelines."

Raff hops a little, unable to hold back.

Raff: "Exactly. Put the fishers in the footage. Put Elder Mae on camera. Show the moorings, not just the wall. Make it feel like it's theirs and not just a brand campaign."

Asha glances at you, then to Jonah, and her voice goes quiet but firm.

Asha: "If we have independent oversight, community representation, adaptive management, and the mooring easements written into the founding documents—if those are non-negotiable—then the public sees a compromise that keeps people anchored."

Jonah meets your eyes. His expression is open and complicated—something between plea and plan. You sense the community watching, the night outside holding its breath for a sign that love and principle can coexist.

You feel Jonah's knuckles—warm, work-callused—when he shifts his hand on the table. You want to cross the span and fold your fingers over his. For a moment the room narrows to a single taut line: the choice you must make between a clean, fast victory and a slower, more binding protection.

Marin Solace: "We can actually script the PR around community milestones: planting terraces, retrofitting a pilot mooring, and a visible audit at six months. The narrative validates action while the legal language locks in access. It's slower than a unilateral build, but it shows investors real progress."

Lydia nods, the ghost of a smile forming.

Lydia Voss: "Progress that can be demonstrated is fine. My concern is stoppage—investors withdrawing if the pace drags. So we need an escrow mechanism: milestones release funds. Miss milestones and the project pauses with penalties. That pressure keeps the schedule honest."

Ben: "Which means we need rigorous timelines and a dispute-resolution clause that doesn't grind everything to litigation."

Asha: "Which means public access to the audit reports. Let the people see if milestones are missed—social accountability matters as much as financial accountability."

Raff, unable to hold the scene still, leans forward with his phone.

Raff: "Can we at least get the first planting live? A short segment—two minutes—to put faces on the work. If the town watches people doing the labor, they'll be less likely to let investors steamroll the social parts."

You feel the hopeful tug of it—small public acts that become proof. The pavilion's room warms with the sense that, for once, the technical can be married to the social without either side losing its dignity.

But you also recognize the razor: make the clauses too binding and investors may pull back; make them too loose and the town loses control.

You take a breath and crystallize the trade-offs, because that's what you always do—translate risk into language that people can use.

Marin Solace: "We have three routes to present to the council: insist on legally binding mooring rights with an empowered co-management board; accept performance-based oversight with negotiated fishing access and staged funds; or cede primary leadership to Lydia's model for immediate investor confidence. Each has costs and benefits. We need to decide which blend of certainty and speed the town can live with."

Jonah's hand finds the table again. When he looks at you this time, his expression is—complex. You read loyalty, fear, and a steady, stubborn hope that the sea will still be there on the other side of the decision.

Each micro-decision threads another stitch into the garment you're making for the town: public trust woven with legal thread and practical scaffolding. The air in the pavilion seems to shift; where there was formerly only the cold certainty of spreadsheets, human voices now strum a chord of shared ownership.

Lydia folds her hands and leans in—the kind of leader who smells the direction of a compromise and tightens the screws just enough to make it work.

Lydia Voss: "I will draft performance metrics tethered to the GPS easements Marin suggested. We'll put an escrow in place. We'll stage PR around community milestones. But for my investors to move now, we must present a clear single plan and a demonstrable path. The council needs to vote with enough confidence to avoid an immediate stall."

Ben looks to you, and in that look is every corridor of political risk. The decision you make here will be the narrative the town tells itself for months: that it chose control over delivery, delivery over control, or a middle path that will be judged by patience.

You can feel hope gathering at the edges of the hard choices. The thread of ascent is visible: shared language, shared milestones, and—if you can land the right phrasing—binding provisions framed as testable performance.

You inhale the pavilion's cool air once more. The town is watching, literally and figuratively, because this room is both a stage and a courtroom. Your thumb brushes the chipped lighthouse pin at your lapel; small things steady you.

You are ready to ask for signatures. You are ready to ask the town to live with the chosen risk. For now, the options must be set and the terms named.
