[Scene: Lydia's Pavilion | Night]
[Visual: The pavilion's glass skin gleams under sodium lamps; inside, cool clinical light washes maps in cyan and coral.]
[Music: Warm, rising chord—strings with a single sustained violin]
[Sound: Low hum of inverters, soft clicks from Lydia's slate, distant boardwalk footsteps]

You step through the automatic door and the air changes: it is cooler, drier, organized. The pavilion breathes in predictable cycles—ventilation sighs, servers ticking, a faint, calming mechanical rhythm that feels built to outlast storms. Screens along the wall rotate surge models like clockwork, bathymetry sliding into cross-sections and then into simulated waves. Every map feels like a promise already translated into coordinates.

[Visual: Close-up of your satchel at your hip; the strap digs into your shoulder—petitions inside, folded handouts, Asha's prototype notes.]
You feel the weight of the satchel the way you feel the weight of the cooperative's work: a steady pressure reminding you of the people who signed those pages in kitchen light and under market tarps. The smell of the pavilion is not salt but something like ozone and fresh coffee, an industrial clean that somehow settles the small flutter in your chest.

A woman at a glass table looks up. Lydia Voss is precise in a way that seems engineered. Her coat is a line; her slate blinks softly beside a cup of coffee. She stands when you enter—not a greeting of warmth but a measured, expectant respect.

Lydia Voss: "Marin. Thank you for coming. Sit; there's coffee if you prefer it black." (Her voice is even, like a calibrated instrument.)

You keep your coat on, hands folding around the satchel strap. You do not tell her about the tarpaulins on the boardwalk or the mural Raff filmed—those small human textures that have begun to stitch the town together. Here, they would be data points.

Marin Solace: "I prefer my coffee like I prefer plans—grounded and honest." (You offer a small, careful smile.) "You've called this a 'liaison' role. I want to understand the scope."

Lydia's fingers trace a line on her tablet; a model responds with a soft whir.

Lydia Voss: "Scope is influence with guardrails. You would be the cooperative's presence at the table. Design suggestions accepted, but with a veto for structural integrity and investor constraints. Messaging would be coordinated—no rogue statements. And we would ask for a public letter of endorsement to help secure funding." (She meets your eyes with the cool, appointed gaze of someone used to shaping choices.)

You watch the model flicker—where a living marsh might meet poured concrete, where a reef could sit beneath a stepped ogee. Each possible integration is drawn with tidy bezels and bright annotations. The offer is real and pragmatic: a seat that comes with a set of chains.

Asha is at your elbow before you knew she'd arrived; the smell of solvent and soil clings to her overalls. Her eyes are hard and fierce, like the hinge on a gate. She doesn't sit. She doesn't need to.

Asha Patel: "They need you inside, Marin. Even a toe in can stop a foot of wrong. You know the plans that work out here. Don't discount the leverage."

Marin Solace: "Leverage, yes. But silence in trade for a stamp? There are clauses in there that could neuter the pilot programs—" (You let the sentence hang because Lydia's fingers hover over the slate as if to correct it.)

Lydia Voss: "We structure for scale. Compromises happen. But with you inside, we can make those compromises less destructive. You would have resources and access most community groups never see." (She leans forward, softening almost imperceptibly.) "Help us design the wall with integrated habitats in mind. The habitat modules can be prototyped in the first phase. That is a tangible concession."

You imagine Elder Mae's voice, telling of boats hauled high and the old shipwrights who knew the sound of a buoy's rope before the tide changed. The stories press at the base of your skull like a tide—each one a reason to be careful, and each one a reason not to give up the steering.

[Sound: A thump from the boardwalk—Jonah's boots, measured, pacing.]
Through the glass, Jonah reserves himself on the boardwalk—shadow and lamp light catching the line of his jaw. His fingers curl around the rusted captain's whistle at his neck. His expression is complex: worry braided with something steady, like a rope that will not snap. He says nothing; the silence between you is as loud as Lydia’s offer.

Jonah Reyes: (from outside, voice raised through the glass) "Marin—talk to me before you sign anything. You know that, right?" (He presses both hands into his pockets, the way people do when they are trying to steady themselves.)

You can answer, step outside, shout back, or let the room's logic swallow you. You feel the tug of the sea in his stance—the harbor's memory—and you feel the tug of the cooperative's midnight meetings in your bones.

Marin Solace: (You inhale, the air tasting faintly of metal and rain.) "I'll hear them out. That's all I can promise right now." (You hope it's honest.)

Lydia smiles with the slow approximation of approval.

Lydia Voss: "That is all I ask. Presence. Expertise. Let us set boundaries together—meetings twice weekly, documented recommendations, an independent oversight clause we can draft right now with Ben Rhee's office." (She taps the slate; a form slides toward you like a contract being offered in a tray.) "We can draft language that preserves the pilot's autonomy while enabling the financing to proceed."

Asha's jaw tightens. Her knuckles are white on the handle of her toolkit.

Asha Patel: "Language can be rewritten later," she mutters, but you hear the brittle optimism. "If you take it, take the oversight clause. Make it public. Let the town see it."

You decide to remain inside for now, but you let your palm rest on the glass as if to translate the warmth from one world to the other. Jonah's fingers tighten briefly on the whistle. He does not move; he waits for you to decide whether to carry this conversation into the open air or to contain it in the pavilion's clinical hush.

Lydia watches the exchange through the glass and does not comment on Jonah. Her attention returns to you with clinical grace; her slate displays a side-by-side: 'Investor Timeline' and 'Pilot Integration Window'. The numbers are neat; the gaps between them are where you could make a difference.

Lydia Voss: "We can embed modular habitats within the wall's footprint. They will be engineered—materials, anchoring, access for maintenance. We would need your cooperative to certify species lists and maintain modules during the pilot. Visibility will be high; it's a flagship story. You would lead that piece." (She offers the role like a tool handed across a table.) "You would not have unilateral veto, but your recommendations would be recorded and considered."

Marin Solace: (You feel both the invitation and the constraint weave into your chest.) "Recorded and considered is not guaranteed, Lydia. You know how the word 'considered' functions in board minutes."

Lydia Voss: (A small, almost tired smile) "Language matters. We can write 'binding recommendations' into the pilot parameters for the first phase, contingent on measurable performance thresholds. That protects both ecology and investors. We want success; so do you."

You imagine the pilot—a terrace of living marshes scalloped into the wall, kelp ropes threaded through engineered pockets, mussel lines seeded under protective ledges. You imagine kids watching juvenile crabs find shelter in an engineered cavity. Those are the things that make your throat tighten with a good kind of ache.

Asha places two folded sheets on the table—your cooperative's petition, signatures spilling across them like a geography of care.

Asha Patel: "If you take it," she says softly, "push for the binding language, and make the maintenance wages non-negotiable. People will be the ones to keep it alive."

You run your thumb over the petition edge. The paper is damp from earlier fog; it leaves a salt-smudged crescent on your nail. It's tangible, anchored. It smells faintly of smoke from the market stalls and of the herbal soap an elder used to press into your hands after a meeting.

Asha's eyes sharpen at your hesitation and then soften. She trusts you to translate this into something the pavilion can use—into the language of binding clauses and oversight committees.

Jonah presses the whistle to his mouth and then doesn't blow. The waiting seems deliberate; he is giving you space to choose, and in that space your values line up like tools on a bench—some blunt, some delicate.

You think of the consequences: being inside means influence—you might steer habitat placement, secure maintenance wages, keep fisher access routes. Being outside means independence—you retain moral clarity and the ability to mobilize quickly, but you risk being excluded from the thing that will shape the shoreline for decades. There is a third path here too, one wrapped in risk: use the platform to gather the documents and the data, to expose limitations later, possibly delegitimizing the project but laying bare the truth for the public to decide.

Lydia rises and offers her hand, a poised, decisive gesture.

Lydia Voss: "Take some time. But to keep this moving, I need your answer at the end of the week. In the meantime, I insist on weekly minutes and a preliminary oversight draft. If you accept now, you'll be introduced as liaison at next Tuesday's press briefing—visibility is immediate." (She tilts her head, offering the nearest thing she has to a harmless smile.) "Public endorsement helps with funding timelines."

Marin Solace: (You feel a rising chord beneath your ribs—hope braided with the terror of responsibility.) "If I accept the liaison role, I will insist on binding language for the pilot, wages for maintenance crews, and transparent minutes accessible to the town. No backroom changes." (You lay out the terms like small stakes on the table; they feel reasonable, necessary.)

Lydia studies you and then nods once—precision as assent.

Lydia Voss: "We can draft that. There will be negotiation, and you'll be part of it. We're prepared to make concessions if the slate shows performance."

The pavilion seems to lean in; the maps glow a little brighter, as if waiting for your decision to become data.

You feel the cooperative's late-night work settle into the moment like a final plank set into place. Outside, Jonah's outline shifts; his hands fall from his pockets. Asha's grip on the toolkit loosens, just a fraction. The air tastes like the possibility of something built together rather than over people.

You know you stand on a lip—the harbor's edge where one step can change what the shore means to everyone. The town hums with careful hope. You see it in the petition signatures, in Asha's tightened jaw, in Jonah's watchful silhouette. Each option stretches ahead with its own steadiness and risk.
