You wake before the market. The world is half-made of shadow and salt. Salt beads in the seams of your jacket where the night air has already learned to find you. Your hair is still damp from the walk—sea-glass brown layers plastered at your neck—and the silver strand at your temple catches the streetlight like a private lighthouse.

You roll the map under your arm until it clicks in the tube's worn leather straps, the paper edges softened by nights of folding and re-folding. The map smells faintly of coffee and adhesive tape; the inked lines seem to tremble with possibility.

You walk the boardwalk alone at first. Stalls are being dragged open—tarp flaps, the thud of wooden crates; the market's early workers move with the practised calm of people who have learned to start before the tide. Puddles hold small, perfect suns that wobble as you pass.

[Sound: A low hum—subsonic and steady—likes a heartbeat under the wind]
[Visual: In the distance, the neon edge of The Saltworks windows glows through mist]

You can feel the day arranging itself. Today is the day Ben Rhee's office visits the cooperative. You can feel that small electricity—same frequency as the inverter at Greenridge—threaded through your chest. Official recognition is a fragile, humming thing: heavy with paperwork, heavier with hope.

You pause on the boardwalk and look across at the market stretch. Jonah is there, a figure moving with the lunge and roll of someone who knows the sea in his bones. He hauls rigging—ropes slapping, a coil catching light—and his waxed canvas vest has that particular smell of tar and hot coffee that seems to belong simultaneously to early mornings and long years of work. When his amber-brown eyes find yours, something in your chest tenses in a way that’s part map and part tide.

[Scene: The Saltworks — Marin's Cooperative Lab | Morning]
[Visual: Corrugated metal painted seafoam green, north windows haloed with early light]
[Sound: Drip of condensation, soft fan whirr from a test rig, the scrape of a bench chair]
[Smell: Wet soil, salt, warm citrus from a pastry box]
[Music: Warm strings enter, hopeful and steady]

You push open the heavy warehouse door and the lab breathes you in: cool, misted air, the tang of potting soil, and the faint metallic note of old ship oil reimagined as grease controlling a prototype actuator. Seedling trays line the longest bench—green, precise, vulnerable. Prototype berms sit on wheeled pallets like small islands: layered coir and native plugs, miniature hills that imagine an alternative shoreline.

Asha is already there, sleeves rolled, fingers stained with mud and ink. She fusses with a string of solar-powered LEDs as if they are temperamental sea creatures: tapping, coaxing, cursing under her breath in the affectionate way she has for machines that are nearly alive.

Asha: "Morning. If Ben's people are actually here today, we should be sharp. I've re-run the sediment model with the new substrate mix."

You: "Good. I ran the agenda again—short intro, demo of the berm, then community testimony. Raff's drone footage will play after the demo."

Asha: (glancing up) "Raff swore there were cinematic angles. He keeps texting me 'hero shot' like we're making a documentary about plants."

You smile despite everything—Asha's animosity toward grand nouns is as habitual as her ginger tea. Her fingers keep working the LEDs. She holds one up; it blinks obediently.

Raff slips in like a gust, phone in hand. His hoodie still smells faintly of engine exhaust and excitement.

Raff: "Footage's up. Pro-bono, public. People are already watching the tide trials at Greenridge—lots of hearts and comments about the bee-friendly panels. I captioned it with Elder Mae's line about 'not letting the sea carry off our stories.'"

You: "Good. Make sure we tag the town council page before noon."

Raff: (grinning) "Already scheduled. Also—someone's already asking about the 'pilot mooring retrofit.'"

You feel a pull at the corner of your mouth: the pilot retrofit idea is exactly the kind of scalable, tactile solution that proves the point. It would also be the perfect, practical bridge to talking with Jonah later.

[Sound: A brief vibration—your comm buzzing on the bench]
[Visual: A small screen illuminates with a call ID: Councilor Ben Rhee (Office)]

You wipe your hands on your jacket and answer.

Ben Rhee: "Marin—thank you for accommodating us on such short notice. My office will be there in twenty. Councilor's schedule cleared."

You: "Thanks, Ben. We have the berm out near the east bay and a live simulation queued. I want to be clear—our point is a hybrid approach. We want to show mitigation that keeps people here."

Ben Rhee: (a practiced pause) "I know the town cares. I can't promise policy tonight. But I can promise I'll bring the regional planners' attention. We need evidence and public buy-in. Your demo will help."

You: "We want community oversight—no decisions behind glass, Ben. Can you make sure that's heard?"

Ben Rhee: (softening) "I'll make sure your voice reaches the table. I promised Elder Mae I'd listen."

You: "Then we'll see you soon."

The line goes quiet. You hold the silence for a heartbeat—it's both pressure and buoy. Ben's reassurance is small but real. It feels like a rope thrown rather than a rescue: something you might climb if the town takes hold.

Elder Mae arrives before Ben's people—predictable, comforting, a knot of shawls and salt-weathered hands. She sets a small box on the bench with more ceremony than a parade.

Elder Mae: "You're early, Marin. So are the seedlings. Good things, both."

You: "Mae. You didn't have to—"

Elder Mae: (waving you off) "Salted lemon tart, for the nerves. The first storm we took in this town had a name; we called it the Black March because the sky turned like charcoal gutters. Your grandfather pushed when the tide surged and we patched him with rope and song. We thought a wall could keep the sea out. Then the sea learned our tricks. You learn quickly in Havenpoint."

You: (listening) "What did your grandfather do?"

Elder Mae: (settling on an overturned crate, voice like dry paper turning) "He tied bells to the rigging, so when the wind came he'd hear them and wake. He told the children—'the sea remembers the faces it once fed.' You people of my age—"

You: "You mean the interventions carry memory."

Elder Mae: (nodding) "Everything we build remembers where it came from. That is good and dangerous. Make sure whatever you lay down asks permission of the place."

You take a piece of tart—its sugar crackles, the lemon is sharp against the salt you carry in your jacket. The tart tastes like the inside of a story: sweet, preserved, a little bitter.

Asha: (leaning close, conspiratorial) "We should run the berm demo twice. Once with the sensor feed and once handheld so people can touch the coir. Let them feel the roots. Ben's people need a story they can put their hands on."

You check your satchel: pens, maps, a small binder of community signatures, a prototype sensor the size of a tin. The prototype is warm in your hand where the lab's heater had rested against it. Your palms know the weight of work, the friction of paper edges, the grit of embedded salt. These tactile things ground you.

Outside, the boardwalk hums to life. Somewhere between the groan of a stall and a gull's cry, you catch sight of Jonah again—this time helping a young apprentice with a knot, his movements patient. The idea you've shaped in your chest hardens into a plan: ask Jonah to help retrofit one mooring after the meeting. It's practical, a bridge between your engineered berms and the fishers' need to get out to sea.

You imagine the retrofit: nature-based bumpers that preserve access, delegated oversight, a shared ledger of maintenance. You imagine Jonah's hands on the prow, not a bureaucrat's hands in a suit. You imagine your voice asking him and his answer—whatever form it takes—landing not as a negotiation but as a joining.

Ben's people will arrive soon. The lab is arranged, the demos prepped, the tone rehearsed. The air presses the day forward with purpose. You tuck the map tighter under your arm and step toward the door, the morning pressing at your shoulders like a rising tide—soft, relentless, hopeful.

You think of Elder Mae's tart and the bells on your grandfather's rigging. You think of Jonah's coffee-scented vest and the small, replica berms that might buy a season, a decade, a memory. The town is awake with possibilities.

You decide: after the meeting, you'll ask Jonah to help retrofit that mooring. It feels like the right thing—practical, human, principled.
