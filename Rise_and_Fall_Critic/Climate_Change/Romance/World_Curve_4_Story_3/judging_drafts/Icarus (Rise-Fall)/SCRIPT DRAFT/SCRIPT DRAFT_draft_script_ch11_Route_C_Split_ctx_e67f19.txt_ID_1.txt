[Scene: Lydia's Pavilion | Night]
[Visual: The pavilion's glass shell glows against a rain-slicked sky; inside, cyan maps rotate on silent screens. Lydia stands beneath a cone of cool light, her tablet a steady pulse at her hip.]
[Music: Warm, rising chord—strings and a single violin—layered with a quiet electronic hum]
[Sound: Low whir of servers; distant, muffled footfalls on wet boardwalk planks]

You step into the pavilion and the air changes the way clean architecture changes a room: measured, engineered, ordered. Light from the screens catches the salt on your jacket like a promise. Maps slide past in slow, deliberate cycles—surge envelopes, barometric overlays, modeled bathymetry—each one a possibility made tidy and legible.

Lydia Voss looks up as you enter. Her smile is quick and professional, as if she is greeting a stakeholder who has arrived precisely on cue.

Lydia: "Marin. I'm glad you made it. Sit, please—there's much to go through and I want you at the center of it."

You notice the word center the way you notice currents: it implies pull, a place of leverage. Behind Lydia, a large projection folds a twenty-year sea-level curve into shaded bands. The slate clipped to her belt flashes with annotations that resolve into a schematic for the floodwall.

Asha stands a step behind you—eyes bright, paint-splattered cuff hairline damp from the rain. Her posture is tight with a controlled impatience; the curve of her mouth says she believes in the cooperative's work as if its future were a living, breathing thing.

Asha: (quiet, fierce) "We brought you because you can navigate this space better than most. That's the point."

Your satchel rests at your hip like a small, weighted argument: petitions folded in careful stacks, hand-drawn diagrams on tracing paper, the ink of a thousand signatures a tangible pressure against your ribs.

Lydia gestures to a ring of chairs and a low table where coffee cups fog in the cool air.

Lydia: "We want you as liaison. You'd have access to the working models, a seat at our daily briefings, and a formal channel to propose design adaptations."

She pauses; the pause is deliberate, a way of carving the rest of the sentence into reality.

Lydia: "There are conditions. Design suggestions would be admitted but subject to veto for constructability and budget. Your cooperative's public messaging would be coordinated with our press. And—most significantly—a signed letter of endorsement from you would be contingent on your official joining."

The words land with the flat clink of metal on glass. You feel each clause as a potential fulcrum: hold here and you keep influence; refuse and you risk being shut out.

You: (thinking) You can hear all the reasons Lydia frames in her voice—feasibility, timelines, investor confidence. Each is true in its own register. But so is the rumble of a community that taught you how to read a chanterelle tide and still expects you to take their stories into the rooms where decisions are made.

You: "A liaison with veto?" (out loud, careful) "That's not quite a seat at the table, Lydia. It's more like—" You search for a word that rules nothing out and does not sound naive. "—a seat within shadow."

Lydia's smile thins in the way of someone who has negotiated with municipalities and private funds alike.

Lydia: "Call it pragmatic. We move fast here, Marin. The wall buys time for many people. If your presence helps ensure living features are integrated where they matter, isn't that worth the trade?"

Asha's jaw tightens; she leans forward as if proximity could alter calculus.

Asha: "Integrate where they matter. That's a phrase that needs definition. Will 'where they matter' include the moorings, the eelgrass beds, the intertidal marsh that supports the larval nurseries? Or will it be ornamental pockets where it's cheap to plant greenery?"

Lydia: (a small, conceding nod) "We can write scope. Co-management clauses. Maintenance protocols. Independent oversight. But we also need clarity about timelines. Investors want visible safety now."

You listen to the exchange as if cataloguing tacks on a board. Each offered clause is a ladder rung. Each caveat is wind on those rungs.

Lydia: "You'd be able to propose living habitats—bioretention, engineered marsh edges—within the wall's footprint. I'll admit to wanting those features; they make the wall more defensible, scientifically and publicly. But final sign-off on construction sequences stays with the project director. That's standard."

You feel the hopeful thread through it: habitats integrated into engineering. The word 'integrate' feels like a soft anchor—both practical and ethical. Your coop's prototypes—the salt-tolerant terraces, the permeable terraces—flash in your mind like stenciled solutions waiting for permission to exist.

You: "And the messaging?" (you ask) "Coordinated with PR—does that mean we lose the cooperative's voice? Will our cautions be edited to 'comfort' investors?"

Lydia: "We synchronize to avoid miscommunication that can delay funding. But we can stipulate a right of response for your group. Public briefings can include your technical lead." (she tilts her head) "That's leverage."

Asha: (soft but firm) "A right of response is not the same as agency. If your 'right' is limited to five minutes in a staged Q&A, it's not enough."

Lydia: "Then write your demands, Marin. If I sign you on, you bring the terms. I can't guarantee full acceptance—some are non-starters for constructability—but that'll be on record."

The word 'on record' feels different from 'in practice.' You think about community meetings where promises were smoothed into compromise. But you also recall nights at the Saltworks when a single prototype shifted a councilor's mind.

At the edge of the glass, beyond the sterile glow, Jonah stands on the boardwalk. Rain darkens his vest; his hair hangs soft at his temples. One hand curls around the rusted captain's whistle at his neck—an old habit. His eyes are fixed on you, but their read is complex, unreadable in the way the sea sometimes is before it decides on a shape.

Jonah: (calling up through the glass) "You sure you want to be the one to marry lab coats to rope and wood, Marin?"

You: (out loud) "Someone has to take the drafts into rooms like this." Your voice sounds steadier than you feel.

Jonah: (after a beat) "Just—be careful. Don't let them wrap your language in agency when they mean control."

There is more he wants to say; you can see it in the set of his shoulders and the way his fingers flex the whistle. He doesn't step inside. The glass contains him, and somehow that distance keeps both of you safe and hungry at once.

You: (thinking) Jonah's caution lands beside Asha's fierceness and Lydia's pragmatism. The three pull at different parts of the same garment: protection, culture, speed. You imagine Elder Mae's slow, constellated stories about the harbor—how every decision etched itself into rope scars and boat hulls. The aesthetic of those stories is care, and you feel the obligation of them in your throat.

Lydia: "So. The liaison. You get access and a platform; we get a credible local face." (she watches you) "It can be a partnership that sets a new standard—if we both choose to make it one."

You consider the contours of possibility: accept and push for living habitats that could, even modestly, change how the wall interacts with tidal ecology; refuse and keep your cooperative free to agitate, prototype, and rally—but risk being shut out from the construction phase; accept and document constraints with the hope of future exposure—an activist's gambit that could either force real accountability or burn the trust you need to negotiate.

Asha moves nearer, voice low.

Asha: "Whatever you decide, make the terms explicit. Names, timelines, oversight. No vague promises. We can draft that now—together."

Your fingers touch the satchel strap again, an anchor in the surge between options.

You: (thinking) The room hums with the weight of possibility—the hum of servers, of people making practical choices that feel like love to those who will wake in houses that don't flood. A rising tension that tastes not like fear but like the sharp, clean tang of something finally moving in the right direction.

[Music: The chord swells gently; hope threaded through cautious pragmatism]

You breathe. You imagine each path for a moment—small, vivid instants that read like polaroids of possible futures: a living reef stitched into a concrete lip and a child counting crab molts in its shallows; a crowd with banners and a megaphone, sunlight on sweat and resolve; a folder of emails released like flotsam that shifts public opinion and upends contracts. Each image holds loss and gain.

The choice waits like a tide line you haven't decided whether to cross.
