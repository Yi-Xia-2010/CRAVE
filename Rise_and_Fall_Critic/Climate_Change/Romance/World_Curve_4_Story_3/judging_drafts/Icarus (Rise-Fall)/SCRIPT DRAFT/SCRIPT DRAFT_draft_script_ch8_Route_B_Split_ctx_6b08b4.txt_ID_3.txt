You fold the covenant—the same paper you carried from the Saltworks—once more under your palm, feeling the creases where other hands have pressed. The warmth of Ben's handshake is still a faint heat on the page. Here, under the pavilion's clinical lights, that warmth seems both fragile and necessary.

The glass throws your reflection back at you: short bob dark with damp, the silver strand at your temple catching the light like a small, stubborn lighthouse. You notice, with a private steadiness, the salt under your nails and the ink smudged where you sketched tide-run channels last night. You count those as the real credentials.

Lydia stands at the head of a long table. Her slate is open; the surge-maps scroll across it in clean, authoritative motion. She smiles—small, exact—and the sound the smile makes is the professional kind of comfort: efficient, calibrated.

Lydia: "Thank you for coming on short notice, Marin. We have investors on the line and political pressure to show momentum. I think we can get shovels in the ground within weeks, if we agree on oversight that reassures funders."

You inhale. The air smells faintly of espresso and ozone; beyond the glass the rougher salt tang presses at the seams of the pavilion like a question. You want to say yes to momentum and no to losing the town's voice, and your chest learns new ways to hold both.

Ben moves a stack of annotated pages toward you, his fingers betraying the salt of long meetings. Raff leans at the far end, phone lit, watching the live-chat like a second heartbeat. Asha carries a small prototype tray—mud, jaunty cordgrass shoots, a tiny tied-raft demonstration.

Jonah stands closest to the glass, rope bracelet white with salt where his fist grips it. He doesn't speak at first; he watches you with a look you can't quite parse—protective, tired, a kind of pleading beneath the tide of his features. He is, as always, a map of impulses you know well and outcomes you cannot predict.

You start, because you're the one who has to turn technical caveats into promises people can touch.

You: "Lydia, Ben—thank you. We all want protection. What we need in the text isn't just milestones for investors—it's guarantees for people who have docked at this water their whole lives."

Lydia: (tilting her head) "Guarantees are contractual. We can write access lanes into easements, but investors want performance metrics tied to funding release. It's standard—measurable outputs reassure capital."

You: "Performance metrics are fine—if they're measured in the right units. Not just cubic yards of concrete and time-to-completion, but 'days of unobstructed access per month' for fishers, and maintenance thresholds agreed to by a local oversight board."

Lydia's expression tightens, a micro-adjustment you know is professional negotiating muscles flexing.

Lydia: "An oversight board risks politicizing the schedule. Investors see delay as risk. We can do an advisory committee—community input, public sessions—but it can't veto technical decisions in real time."

Asha: (interjecting, voice bright but edged) "Advisory doesn't work if it's just theater, Lydia. We've prototyped maintenance routines that rely on hourly checks and community crews. That should count as performance—and it keeps knowledge local."

Raff: (half-rising from his seat) "And we film it. We put the people on camera—clips of net-mending and toddler-boat races. If investors see hearts-and-hands, they won't just see line items."

Ben: (softly mediating) "Public support helps. But the council needs cover—they need quick, visible progress to answer constituents. We can ask for binding clauses on mooring rights, Marin, but they will push back hard. We need a hybrid that's defensible politically."

Jonah finally speaks, voice low, each word like a tied knot.

Jonah: "Mooring rights aren't just about access. They're our livelihoods. If a wall goes up and we lose the lanes, we've lost more than boats. You know that."

You feel your fingers tighten on the covenant. The room smells of hot metal and the damp lungs of seaweed. You picture Jonah's father's dock, the way he'd taught you to tie a cleat knot until your hands remembered it by heart. There is private gravity behind Jonah's armor—something that will not concede without being made safe.

You: "We can write mooring rights in. Legally binding. They stay with property and are recorded—clear, enforceable."

Lydia: (leaning forward, palms flat) "Binding mooring easements can be carved out, yes. But two things—one, investors may balk at deed restrictions that they perceive as limiting long-term asset flexibility; two, such legal work takes time. Everyone here knows 'time' is what the press and market trade in."

Asha: "Time can be structured. Phased commitments—binding rights for immediate moorings, with a scheduled review window for scalability."

Ben: "The scheduled review can be tied to performance metrics, Marin. If local maintenance meets X standard for Y months, investors release the next tranche. That creates both legal protection and a performance-based route forward."

Raff: (eager) "And we livestream the maintenance. We get community buy-in. We show—visually—how the town keeps its side of the bargain."

Lydia's eyes scan the room. She appreciates ease of implementation—clear strings to pull.

Lydia: "We can draft a performance oversight clause with a community opt-in metric. But understand—the investor's PR plan wants quick wins: visible beachfront stabilization and a ribbon-cutting. We can promote the terraces and the first rebuilt mooring as symbolic achievements."

You feel a small, hopeful warmth in your chest—the kind of thing that comes when two opposing logics begin to overlap, even imperfectly. The soundscape softens: the strings into a higher, brighter register. The conversation is not surrender; it is a craft in motion.

You: "That's necessary. But the PR needs to center the town—Asha's prototypes, Raff's footage, Elder Mae's history. Not just the concrete progress. If we let the caption be 'Investment saved the town,' the narrative erases our labor."

Lydia: (for a rare moment, less guarded) "I don't want to erase you. My job is to secure the capital that makes the pilot scalable. If we craft the messaging correctly—if community voices are the lead in our outreach—investors still get return without being the story."

Jonah watches you as if weighing a sentence on a scale. He steps closer; his voice is almost private.

Jonah: "Promise me this—if it goes sideways, you'll stand with the dock people first. I'm not asking for heroics. I'm asking that you don't let us be the first to be told to move."

Your heart answers in a rhythm you know well: defense, stubborn tenderness. You could say yes without conditions; you could say yes and write complications into law. You choose the latter, because that's what your training and your love and your memory of mud-stained hands demand.

You: "I'll stand with dock people. But I need written paths: escalation clauses, emergency response commitments, and—"

Raff: (grinning) "And bloopers. People watch bloopers."
