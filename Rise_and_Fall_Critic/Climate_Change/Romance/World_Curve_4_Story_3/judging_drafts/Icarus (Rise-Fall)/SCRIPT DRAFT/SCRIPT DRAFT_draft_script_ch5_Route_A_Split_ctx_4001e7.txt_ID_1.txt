[Scene: The Saltworks | Morning — Meeting Day]
[Visual: Morning fog lifting outside north-facing windows; benches arranged in a loose semicircle around a folding table. The satchel on a bench, bright orange tabs peeking out.]
[Sound: Coffee urn hiss; low murmur of voices; distant gulls. A single ceiling fan moves a slow, warm breath of air over the room.]
[Music: Gentle, hopeful swell—soft strings with a plucked acoustic guitar undercurrent]

You stand at the edge of the semicircle and feel the room in layers: the raw scent of damp wood and wet wool, the chalk dust that clings to fingertips, the metallic tang of tide-maps in your satchel. The prospectus—folded, annotated, edges softened by your thumb—is a quiet weight against your ribs when you cross the floor.

Asha is already at the whiteboard, marker uncapped, eyes bright. Raff leans against an old filing cabinet, fingers flying as he edits a draft social post on his phone. Jonah is near the door, sleeves pushed up, the braid of rope at his wrist catching the light whenever he shifts. Ben arranges copies of the meeting brief on the table with a careful, bureaucratic grace. Lydia waits at the far side with a slim digital slate, composed as ever.

You breathe in. The town’s late-night work is still in your bones; the pilot’s first success hums like a promise under everyone's skin. Today isn’t just talk—today is the hinge.

You set the prospectus on the table, flatten it with your palm so ink clings where you need it. The papers take the pressure like a pact.

You: "Thank you all for coming. There’s a funder proposal that wants staged milestones. Media deliverables are on the table. Lydia's team has outlined what oversight would look like if we co-brand the pilot."

Asha: "We know what 'staged' can mean in practice—it's a win if we build slow enough to read the marsh's response." (She taps a sketch of root mats pinned on the board.) "Ecological lag matters. If we rush, we might miss negative feedback and lock in a failure."

Jonah: (He folds his arms, thumbs circling the braid) "I get that. But there are tide windows coming up and holes in the moorings that rot out every other month. If the grant will cover retrofits now, that's people's livelihoods."

Raff: "Visibility will bring followers. Followers bring donors. We make a viral moment—'Havenpoint Stands'—and the rest scales."

Ben Rhee: (He clears his throat, palms flat) "Council is reading this as an opportunity. The more visible the work, the easier to argue for support regionally. Conditional funding with clear milestones is politically palatable."

Lydia: (She tilts her head, eyes cool) "Our offer is multi-layered. Fast-track approvals. High-capacity pumps available for emergency response. Co-branding that brings investor attention. In exchange, we request oversight during phases and a media strategy to ensure deliverables are met."

You listen. The room is a mix of conviction and exhaustion; everyone's reasons are honest and different. The air itself feels like an updraft—energizing, liable to lift something forward if guided right.

Asha: (softly) "If the pilot has to report monthly to an external board and follow a marketing calendar, we need protocols to prevent messaging from dictating practice. Data first, optics second."

Lydia: "Transparency is why investors feel comfortable. We're not asking to rewrite your procedures; we ask to ensure milestones are reached. That requires some oversight."

Jonah: (He steps closer to a map pinned above the table and points) "The docks are on the frontline. If we don't shore them up this season, it's not an abstract paper exercise—it's boats in dry dock and families scrambling."

You: (You feel the ache in your chest: responsibility braided with impatience.) "I keep thinking of the pilots we've run—the lab trays, the community beds, the meetings that stretched into dawn. The thing that worked was trust. We can scale, but only if we can keep the steering committee local and have authority over adaptive steps."

Ben Rhee: "Could we write that into the terms? Conditional control—community oversight clause—has political teeth. If we define the triggers and veto points, we can accept funds and keep procedural control."

Lydia: (Her gaze sharpens, not unkind) "Vetoes can paralyze. But we're prepared to allow an independent advisory seat from the cooperative if milestones are clear and reporting is rigorous."

Raff: "We can craft the story around that—'local leadership, with technical backing.' It sells. It reassures funders and the town."

You study everyone's faces—the hopeful tension in Asha's jaw, Jonah's resolute set, Raff's spirited grin, Ben's practiced neutrality, Lydia's composed offer. You remember Elder Mae's knot-tying—how she threaded a final ribbon with the weight of living memory in her hands. You think of the pilot's first salt-caked seedlings breaking surface in experimental trays. Momentum is a rare thing; it smells of wet boards and opportunity.

Asha: (She draws a series of small boxes on the board.) "Phase one: pilot expansion with habitat monitoring—three-month cycles. Phase two: adaptive retrofits where monitoring shows success. Phase three: community training and maintenance funding. If we keep this cadence, we can catch lag effects."

Jonah: (He rubs the back of his neck.) "Those cycles sound right, but what about right now? We lose a season, we lose a lot. The funder's money could do immediate work that lasts beyond the reporting calendar."

Lydia: "Emergency measures—pumps, temporary retrofits—can be part of the first-stage deliverables. We can deploy those with your cooperation. The co-branding simply means public communications align with milestone reporting."

You feel the room tilt toward possibility; the mood is expectant, not resigned. The tide of public energy, the pilot’s visibility, Raff's proposed outreach—all of it could be woven into a structure that keeps the town in the driver’s seat. That thought lifts you.

You: "If we accept funds, we set the terms. We demand a steering committee with majority community seats, clear adaptive triggers, and the right to pause rollout if data warns us. Media deliverables need to be truthful, not polished illusions."

Lydia: (After a pause, a small concession—unexpectedly warm) "I respect local knowledge. Include your terms in a negotiation draft and we'll review it. I can't promise everything, but I can promise to bring the proposal back to my investors with your conditions noted."

Ben Rhee: "That's promising. It gives Council more bargaining room and shows the funder is willing to listen."

Raff: (His enthusiasm bright) "We start with a launch video that explains co-governance. We show the science, the community, the hands that will keep it running. That kind of narrative changes the funding landscape."

Asha: (Her expression softens, hope seeping in) "If we can insist on phased scaling with built-in pause points, we get ecological checks. If we get emergency support for docks when needed, we can protect livelihoods. It's not all or nothing."

You sit back on a stool and let the gravity of the moment settle. Each argument is fair. Each compromise carries risk. But the overall arc of the room is upward: people are listening to one another, bargaining with care, not forcing a false either/or.

Jonah: (Quietly, as if between you two) "Whatever you put on the table, I'm with you. We'll make sure the docks keep working."

You: (You remember the first nights in the Saltworks—late maps under lamplight, the stubborn hope that the town could build its own future.) "Then we frame the acceptance as conditional—community-led scaling with tight conditional control. That way the funding is an engine, not a steering wheel."

Lydia: (She inclines her head, a pact forming in the room's quiet.) "If you can bring precise terms for oversight and pause points, my team can present a co-branded offer that preserves those clauses as negotiable rather than non-negotiable."

Asha: (A brief, luminous smile) "We can do that. We have the data. We have the people. We need the commitment."

[Visual: The sunlight through the high window thickens; dust motes—the small, ordinary miracles—float like tiny proof of motion.]

You feel the shape of the decision settle in the room like a tide pooling around a new jetty: not finished, but forming. Momentum has been conserved and pointed. You lean forward, the satchel at your feet representing responsibility and promise.

The choice is no longer abstract. It is procedural and moral and urgent. The funder’s staged infusion is real. Lydia's offer is real. Jonah's docks, Asha's ecological caution, Raff's outreach plan, Ben's political reading—all of it converges here, in the Saltworks, under the same light that once caught your reflection in the window and made you promise.

[Scene Transition: Greenridge Solar Terraces | Midday — Site Walk]
[Visual: Terraced panels glint; native grasses move in a chorus with the wind. There’s the steady hum of inverters below, bees making small decisions among the seedheads.]
[Sound: Wind through grasses; distant conversation; the crisp scrape of a clipboard]
[Music: A bright, airy motif—woodwind and light percussion]

You walk the terraces with the group, prospectus tucked under your arm. The panels throw brief, sharp shadows across Ben's tie and Raff's jacket. Visitors at the terraces point at integrated bioswales where runoff is being handled with the same humble engineering you advocate. The simplicity of it—technology braided with living systems—pulls a small smile from you.

Lydia gestures to a pump station at the terrace edge. "Emergency response can be staged into site infrastructure. Visible action combined with oversight ensures donors see progress and we keep safeguards."

Asha kneels in a swale to show the root nodules; you kneel beside her, soil cool between your fingers. The groundedness of dirt is an antidote to the dizziness of funding decisions.

You: "We can design the milestones around ecological thresholds and community training. We can require public reporting and put a mechanism for pause and review into the contract."

Ben: "I can work that language into a council motion. It gives the town agency while accepting funds."

Jonah: (He looks out toward the water, then back) "As long as the first tranche helps folks in the short term. If we get pumps and retrofits for critical moorings, that's a life saved."

Lydia: "I will take your draft conditions back to investors. I ask only that the messaging aligns with defined milestones. I cannot promise to accept every clause, but I will fight for the community’s input."

The terraces hum with potential. You feel the room’s collective breath draw in, and for the first time in months, what you’ve built—the cooperative, the pilot, the community scaffolding—feels like it could carry something larger.

You straighten, shoulders filling with the rising energy. The next step is clear in outline if not yet in ink.

[Music: The hopeful theme holds. The light sharpens.]

You know what you want to propose. You feel how the town is leaning. You can already see the headlines Raff will draft, the training workshops Asha will schedule, the docks Jonah will protect—if you choose to bind the funder to community-led control.

You fold your hands on the rail and take the small, steady breath of someone who is about to make a deliberate, consequential choice.
