[Scene: The Saltworks | Late Morning — Day After the Celebration]

[Sound: Low conversational hum, the distant clink of tools, a refrigerator compressor breathing in the corner.]

You still taste the lemon tart at the back of your throat, the sugar-sweetness folded into salt from the bay. The town's applause from last night hangs in the rafters like a new flag — not finished woven, but visible. The prospectus in your satchel is a small, solid thing, edges softened by your thumb. It is proof of attention; it is also the first ledger of promises you will have to keep.

You pace between benches. Your boots scuff salt dust. The chalkboard is crowded with notes: "phased metrics," "co-management clause," "habitat-first metrics." Asha's handwriting curls across the margins like someone trying to coax clarity out of noise. The sunlight, when it cuts past the cloud bank, makes the mist in the room halo like possibility.

A cluster of people gathers at the long table: Asha at one end with a coffee-stained mug and earnest eyes; Raff leaning back, fingers scrolling on a phone; Ben with his habitual, slightly rumpled tie; Jonah with salt still in the cuff of his sleeve; and Lydia, who seems to bring her own small weather with her — crisp, controlled, and unabashedly bright.

[Visual: Close-up on your satchel as you tap it with one finger; the paper inside presses against the fabric like a small heart.]
[Music: Strings swell gently — a hopeful motif.]

You inhale; the air smells of brine and wet wood and the faint, green tang of the plants Asha has coaxed into trays. You have to decide how to start this morning — let the town's momentum carry you or shape that momentum with careful contour.

You choose the way that feels truest in the moment — not because it is easy, but because it buys you the clearest view.

Asha leans forward, palms flat on the table, and sketches the pilot's timeline in broad strokes. "We can scale; the data supports staged expansion," she says, eyes bright and quick. "But ecological lag — the marshes and substrate take seasons to settle. If we push faster than the system can respond, we risk feedback loops. We need monitoring windows between phases."

Jonah's laugh cuts through the caution like a coin on tile. "Monitoring windows won't stop water in someone’s doorway," he says. He looks at you as if the answer is obvious. "My cousin Raff's savings are all wrapped in berth fees. If the money's here now, let's retrofit the docks. Protect the livelihood while we learn."

Raff pipes in without ceremony. "We can do both. Viral campaign, show 'community resiliency' footage, spin it—funders double down. Tide, tech, and heart." He taps his phone, eyes already two steps ahead.

Ben folds his hands, offering his compromise smile. "Visibility buys leverage. If we get the public narrative on our side, Lydia's access becomes pressure for our terms, not the other way around."

Lydia smooths a palm over the glass of her tablet, cyan maps reflecting in her pupils. "I don't want to be the villain here," she says, voice even. "We can structure oversight, conditional milestones, and yes — co-branding. If you accept co-branded staging, I can push for emergency pumps to be deployed and for fast-track permits. Investors want results they can show."

Asha's jaw tightens. She doesn't meet Lydia's gaze. "Pumps are a patch," she says. "They buy time; they don't heal tidal dynamics. We have to build the capacity to observe and adapt, not just react."

"You said yourself at the market," Jonah says, softer, interrupting the edge — "protect the docks, keep the access. People eat off the water."

Lydia inclines her head toward Jonah, polite but unflinching. "Emergency response and permit momentum are immediate benefits. I can secure that if there's a governance structure you can live with."

The conversation thickens; you feel the pull of two rubrics: the rhythm of careful science and the pace of immediate human need. Both feel right. Both demand you choose.

You catch yourself touching the bracelet at your wrist — recycled beads, a small gift from a neighbor who'd rigged together a salt-toy for a child. It keeps you in the present.

"Marin—" Jonah says, using your name like an anchor. "If we let this window close, it won't come back. We ride it now, or we watch a dock go."

You answer, and your words wobble only a fraction because weight is what you carry best. "We can't trade oversight for speed," you say. "But neither can we let fear of imperfect outcomes freeze us when people are exposed."

Asha cuts in, breath sharp but earnest. "What if the grant comes in tranches tied to public deliverables? We design the deliverables to be ecological tests, not PR shots."

Lydia smiles, the exactness of it. "And we can help shape those deliverables. You'll get the capital, the capacity, and the media that attracts more funders. I'll guarantee a pump deployment within a week of agreement."

Raff drapes a hand on Jonah's shoulder. "And I'll make it trending in an afternoon. 'Havenpoint: Homegrown Hope'—we get eyes, we get match funding. It's math."

Ben rubs his temple like a mediator massaging a knot. "We can draft a governance clause, but it needs teeth. Conditional release, external auditors — it's possible, but it means you agree to some oversight language."

You feel the room's air thicken at the word oversight. To some, it's protection; to others, surrender. You imagine signing pages with legal type that could wind into every repair order, every press release, every maintenance check. You imagine also the docks, Jonah's hands smeared with tar, the way his father taught him a knot by the firelight.

You need fresh air before the answers get louder than the questions. You walk out with Jonah — ostensibly to show him a plan sketch, but also to let the bay remind you why you do this.

Here, the sun asserts itself through the cloudline, and the terraces sing with a low inverter hum. The contrast between engineered order and the improvised gardens below makes your chest unclench a little. Jonah leans on a railing, rope bracelet half-unwound in his hands.

"You always did pick the quiet places to think," he says, amusement in his voice.

"Or the noisy ones where people could hear us," you counter.

Jonah draws a breath, tasting salt and sun. "I don't want the docks to be a political bargaining chip," he says, eyes on the water's mirror. "If the money's a yes, let's fix the moorings with the fabric and the pilings that actually hold. People need work, and they need to sleep without hearing waves in their beds."

He looks back at you. "Are you with me? Not because you owe me anything, but because I think you're the person who knows how to hold both angles."

A small, private ache — part responsibility, part tenderness — settles in your throat. You fold it into words. "I want both. I want to protect work and make sure what we build doesn't hurt the bay. It will take boundaries and patience."

Jonah's fingers, callused and honest, close briefly over yours. You don't say it aloud, but you feel the promise of hands that will help lift whatever comes next.

[Scene Transition: Return to the Saltworks | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The Saltworks window panes glow thinner now; the room takes on a late-gold hush. The whiteboard is updated, markers scattered like thinking points.]
[Sound: Murmurs, a kettle boiling, the shiver of a wind through a cracked pane.]
[Music: A rising chord progression — warmth, possibility.]

Back inside, the table has become a map of priorities. Lydia's tablet sits beside Asha's soil tray like a small duel of methods. Ben taps his pen against the margins where governance language might live.

Lydia lays out a slide with a clean sweep of milestones: "Phase One — Quick retrofits and emergency pumps matched with pilot sites; Phase Two — scale living shorelines; Phase Three — full co-management rollout." Her voice is measured, efficient. "Visibility is part of the package. It's what brings matching funds and political oxygen."

Asha shakes her head, but there is a softness under it. "So the deliverables become headlines. We need them to be scientific. We need pause-points with ecological indicators."

Jonah leans forward. "And the docks?"

Lydia's eyes flick to Jonah, then to you. "We can front the pumps and fast-track the dock permits under a co-branded pilot," she says. "In exchange, investor oversight is woven into the reporting process."

"Investor oversight," Raff echoes, sounding both excited and wary. "Which could be the rope that holds us up or the noose that tightens. Public sentiment will shape that."

Ben lifts his hands. "We write the oversight rules from inside. Conditional releases, adaptive clauses, community auditors. We can insist on legal teeth."

You feel the gravity of the thing: a grant that arrives with both lifelines and handcuffs. You imagine the Saltworks as a tiny ship in a harbor of investor money, rigging adjusted to survive a storm.

You stand in the center and look at everyone. It's not theatrics; it's attention. You speak with the weight of someone who has drafted clauses at midnight and trudged home with sand in their hair.

"If we structure conditional control," you say, "we keep the community's voice in the center. If we accept co-branding and oversight, we gain speed. If we prioritize docks now with minimal oversight, we protect people today but gamble on political and ecological consequences later."

Lydia’s expression is open, almost warm. "This is how we move mountains fast, Marin. But you have to choose the scaffolding."

Asha's voice is raw with fatigue and hope. "We shouldn't rush the tests that tell us whether the living systems can hold up."

Raff grins, impossible to silence. "And we tell that story to the world — the slow science, the immediate saves, the people who stayed."

You can feel the town's energy like a tide that can be steered but not stopped. The rising chord of the music mirrors that forward pull. Your satchel feels heavier now — more ledger, more promise.

It is your decision to make in the end. The room quiets. Everyone looks to you not because you want them to, but because they need a human to hold the center while the world shifts.
