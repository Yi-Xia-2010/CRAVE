# Route: Route B → Option B / Route C → Option C


[Scene: Lydia's Pavilion | Dusk]
[Visual: Glass façade reflecting a bruised sky; cyan surge-maps pulse across a wall display. Lydia stands near a digital table, backlit, immaculate. Ben holds a sealed envelope. A cluster of investors murmur by a charging station.]
[Sound: Low murmured camera clicks, a distant gull, the pavilion's climate system whispering.]
[Music: Sparse piano with a cold undertow]

You arrived here with the taste of plaster dust and printer toner still in your mouth — the practical residue of a week spent drafting clauses and arguing margins. The hybrid agreement you negotiated sits between Ben's fingers like a pale, patient thing: performance metrics here; GPS easements there; a clause protecting fishing lanes inked in legalese you fought for. On the surface, it looks like consensus. Underneath, you carry the compromises that made consensus possible.

Lydia: "We can frame the milestones as clear deliverables. Investors like clarity. Marin, your performance metrics give them a path to payment releases."

Marin Solace: "They give the town room to hold us accountable. We built in independent audits."

Lydia: (tilting her head) "And public-facing milestones. We stage progress. Photographs. Narrative."

You watch her assemble the future like a product launch: a few polished images, an easy slogan, Ben practicing his soundbite. It's efficient, unambiguous, and it moves money. You let it move you; you let it move the town. You told yourself that nudging the plan from inside would preserve access lanes and keep Jonah's mooring alive. You told yourself that coaxing control over delivery was better than resisting from the outside.

Ben Rhee: (soft, urgent) "Marin — this is the only way we keep the project on schedule. If investors pull, everything stalls. We need that confidence."

You nod. Your thumb finds the chipped lighthouse pin on your jacket and you press it, as if to pin the world in place.

[Visual: Cut to a rapid montage — sunlight brightens over a seedling bed, selfie flashes at the boardwalk, Jonah steering a small charter, children planting spartina on a marsh island; banners read: 'Havenpoint — Resilient Together'.]
[Sound: Laughter, a camera shutter, the slap of oars against a dock.]
[Music: A warmer motif — strings that almost feel like hope]

The public face is irresistible. Raff posts a clip of Jonah explaining tidal ecology on a charmed afternoon; Asha uploads a time-lapse of salt-tolerant grasses rooting into engineered mats. Ben tweets a perfectly cropped image of you shaking Lydia's hand — a picture of partnership that would be used, later, as evidence that the town had chosen unity.

It feels, for a moment, like ascent. The work you began in the Saltworks finds bodies and soil and light. You watch a child set a toy boat along a reed-lined channel and something in your chest loosens. You allow yourself a small, private smile.

You chose the middle path — careful redactions, contextual notes that keep investors' proprietary details blurred but push the public record toward honesty. You convinced yourself the timing was responsible: the project had momentum; people deserved the truth, in measured doses. You uploaded excerpts late the next day from the Saltworks, your fingers trembling only a little as you blurred contractor names and excised the most sensitive schematics.

[Scene: The Saltworks | Overcast Morning]
[Visual: North-facing windows streaked with rain, soil trays under fluorescent lights; Asha at a workbench, Raff hunched over a laptop, a printout with your redactions pinned on the wall.]
[Sound: The kettle's whistle, the distant thrum of a delivery van.]
[Music: Low, insistent cello]

Raff: (voice tight, a fingertip trembling above the keyboard) "Marin, your post — it's already picked up. Someone on the forums reassembled part of the scatted metadata. They found language about 'variance tolerances' and amplified it."

Marin Solace: "We redacted what could cause panic. The town needs to know the limits, not the proprietary playbook."

Asha: (leaning in) "But people will read 'variance' as 'we might fail.' Social media eats uncertainty for breakfast. Did you think about press timing? About spin?"

You did. You had thought about timing and spin and about the bite that transparency sometimes had when it wasn't couched in a marketing narrative. You had hoped that your careful framing would keep trust intact while forcing stronger oversight. Now the framing is fraying.

[Visual: Notification pings multiply. The Saltworks' communal screen fills with screenshots: headlines alleging 'hidden weaknesses' and lawyered quotes from unnamed critics. A livestream shows Lydia's spokesperson calling the publication 'reckless'.]
[Sound: A harsh phone ring. Ben's voice on speaker, brittle.]

Ben Rhee: (on phone) "They're calling it sabotage. Lydia's legal team is already— Marin, investors are asking for assurances. Some are threatening to halt funds until a full audit."

Marin Solace: "Ben, I— I released redacted documents. I wanted to compel oversight, not collapse the project."

Ben Rhee: (exhaling) "People conflate. Panic is easier than nuance. We need to contain this."

[Scene: Lydia's Pavilion | Later that Afternoon]
[Visual: Lydia behind the same digital table, her posture like iron cut to fit a dress; the display now shows a crisis timeline in sharp coral and cyan.]
[Sound: Lydia's heels on slate; the clack of investor keys; the distant, muffled chant of voices on the boardwalk.]
[Music: Sparse, jabbing strings—tension]

Lydia: (cold, precise) "Marin, you crossed a line. There are legal remedies for unauthorized disclosures. This is not 'transparency'; this is destabilization. Investors will read 'risk' and retreat."

Marin Solace: "I wanted to lower systemic risk. If performance claims are overstated, we will be better positioned to adapt them. We built in audits. This forces the follow-through."

Lydia: (flat) "You forced a spotlight that will make it impossible to act quickly. When people hear 'variance tolerances' without context, they panic. Panic removes options. That was the last thing we needed."

Marin Solace: (sharply) "Did you expect me to stay silent while you shaped the plan to sound full-proof? I saw the blind spots. I chose to name them."

Lydia: "And you chose the moment and the medium. There are consequences."

The room shrinks to the distance between image and interpretation. Her words land like rusted nails. Around you, investors trade glances; one palms a phone, scrolling through a thread that now sports a thousand angry arrows.

You choose — or, perhaps, your feet choose — to go to the Saltworks. The workshop smells of wet burlap and electrical tape; under the fluorescents your redactions look like a map of scabbed-over wounds. Asha paces; Raff's laptop is open to a thread of accusations and support.

Asha: "Some people are thanking you for taking a stand. Elder Mae texted a folded paragraph about truth. Others are calling you reckless. There's a petition to replace you on the oversight committee."

Raff: (without humor) "Someone made a hashtag: #UnsafeDisclosure. It's trending locally."

Your phone vibrates. Jonah's name flashes. You don't answer immediately. You stand in the liminal space between the Saltworks' warm lights and the rain-slick boardwalk beyond, feeling the town split underfoot.

[Scene: Boardwalk & Marsh Islands | Early Evening — Low Tide]
[Visual: The new wall looms in the distance, concrete softened by murals. Marsh islands sit like green teeth in shallow water. Jonah stands at the edge of his dock, hands rubbing the weathered railing.]
[Sound: The tide sighing, gulls calling, a distant muffled shout of a vendor closing shop.]
[Music: A single cello line; a distant, unresolved chord]

Jonah: (quiet, not turning to you immediately) "You did what you thought right."

Marin Solace: "I did the only thing I could think would keep us honest."

Jonah: (finally facing you; his amber eyes are shadowed) "Honest with who, Marin? With us? The town? Investors? Because when people are scared, choices get made fast. Sometimes they don't include the people who live here."

Marin Solace: "I thought— I thought if the truth was in the light, we'd be stronger. I'd rather fight with clarity than let us be lulled into brittle certainty."

Jonah: (a slow laugh, not happy) "And did you consider what breaking the seal does to trust? Do you know what it feels like to be the one everyone looks to because your livelihood sits on a dock? They look at you and expect steadiness."

His hand finds the rope coiled on the post, fingers tracing the battered braid like a rosary. For a moment he is a map of all the ways the sea has taken things and the small stubbornness he uses to hold on.

Marin Solace: (pleading) "I'm trying to hold everything, Jonah. I thought if I stayed inside the room I could steer it away from worst outcomes. I never wanted to make you feel abandoned."

Jonah: (voice breaking) "You stayed in the room — and we were outside. This leak exploded while people were feeling safe. You pulled a thread and the sweater started to unravel. Some of us are cold now."

There is a mouth in your chest that will not stop making the shape of apologies. He steps closer, close enough for you to see the small scar at his temple that catches the light. He is close enough for the rhythm of his breath to sync, briefly, with yours.

Jonah: (soft) "I don't know where to put my anger, Marin. At you? At Lydia? At the whole system that makes us choose between roofs and reefs? I just know I'm tired."

Marin Solace: (voice low) "So am I. But I didn't believe silence would save us."

Jonah: (after a long pause) "Maybe neither of us was right about how to do that."

The tide rolls in, indifferent. The wall holds back the louder swells tonight, its curve a visible line between what you hoped to protect and what you've had to change. The marsh islands gleam, planted and alive — a small, stubborn victory. But the people on the boardwalk are not all smiling; some pass with eyes averted, others stop and whisper. The town breathes differently. The scaffold of consensus is buckled; the structure you thought would carry everyone now needs repair that will not be completed by concrete or cordgrass alone.

[Scene: Saltworks | Night]
[Visual: One overhead lamp, the rest of the workshop asleep; a rain-smeared window showing the red glow of emergency announcements from phones.]
[Sound: The kettle long emptied; the soft rustle of a printed page sliding across a table.]
[Music: Low, mournful piano; a single violin thread]

Elder Mae's message will not read like verdict or consolation. It reads like memory: "We lose things we think we can never replace. We also learn how to carry them differently. Come home when you can. Bring a broom."

Asha: (sitting across from you) "You didn't break the project. You exposed a fault line. Now people will be angrier because they feel threatened. But there are also people who will come ask for repair plans that include them."

Marin Solace: (to Asha) "What if the price of honesty is that we fracture? What if the only way to be right was to be wrong in the short term?"

Asha: "Then we'll do the work. Repairs, not just of levees, but of trust. Those are slower."

You fold the redacted files neatly, because there is a ritual in making a mess tidy even if the stain remains. You sit with Jonah's last look and the echo of Lydia's words: consequences. You think of the child who steered a toy boat along a planted channel and how small hands might still make that journey if you can find a way through.

Outside, the town's chatter is a rough music — accusations and gratitude entangled like driftwood. The wall stands, and the marshes take root; people still harvest and mend and teach. But tonight the streetlamps cast long, uncompromising shadows. Your role on the cooperative is more scrutinized than before. The oversight committee calls for tighter audits. Investors want faster assurances. Some neighbors applaud your transparency; others have signed a demand that you recuse yourself.

You have done what you believed was right. The result is not neat. It is a bruise that will heal into a scar with memory embedded.

[Visual: You walk to the edge of the marsh islands path, fingers in cold water, the mud warm and resistant. The wind carries a taste of kelp and thunder.]
[Sound: The distant hum of the pavilion's emergency lights. A gull cries once, a sharp punctuation.]
[Music: A single, sustained cello note resolving downwards]

You hold both the photograph of the community planting day and the printout of the redacted notes in the same hand. One is proof of what you helped build; the other is proof of what you feared might fail. Between them you have placed your hand against the dock rail and felt the pulse of everything you love — awkward, messy, real.

You will be judged by the town for days that feel like a lifetime. Lydia's team will press narrative; investors will calculate their margins. People you thought were on the same shore will now stand across a narrow channel. Jonah's look lingers, unreadable in its fold of hurt and care.

At the edge of the marsh, you decide — not to unmake what happened, but to begin the longer work that follows: to listen to people who are angry as well as those who are grateful; to help write repair plans that are not only structural but social; to apologize where apology is needed and to stand by the convictions that led you here.

It will be slow. It will not be tidy. It will, perhaps, be necessary.

[Music: Cello thins into the sound of wind over water; notes unresolved, then dim]
