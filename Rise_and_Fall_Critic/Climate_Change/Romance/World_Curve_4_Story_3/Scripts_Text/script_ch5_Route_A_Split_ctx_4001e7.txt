[Scene: The Saltworks | Early Evening]
[Visual: Warm string lights above the workbench; rain-smudged north window; a damp poster taped to the wall that reads "COMMUNITY FIRST" in hand-lettered blue.]
[Music: Gentle, hopeful swell—soft strings with a single piano motif]
[Sound: Low conversation, the distant hiss of rain on corrugated metal, a kettle whistling somewhere down the hall]

You stand in the middle of your workshop and the world that comes with it. The satchel at your shoulder feels heavier than last week—petitions folded into neat stacks, tide-mapping notes pinned like talismans, the prospectus Ben handed you tucked between a grease-stained sketch and an old receipt for bait. The air smells of wet wood, clove tea left too long, and the mineral tang of salt that never quite leaves this place.

Asha is at the long bench, fingers inked, smoothing a strip of eelgrass into a mock root mat as if composing a sentence. Raff props his phone on a crate, replaying footage of today's march—low-angle clips of kids hanging banners, old men clapping at the ribbon. Jonah Reyes leans with both elbows on the counter where you keep the tools you use when the tide's teeth need dulling; his braid of rope rests coiled on the bench like a second heart.

Ben Rhee clears his throat—careful, practiced—the sound of someone used to the weight of votes in his throat.

Ben Rhee: "First—thank you. This turnout changed the tenor of the conversation. The funder is offering a staged infusion: milestones, deliverables, media components. It's big money. It's also...structured."

Lydia Voss tilts her head, the lamplight beveling her platinum hair into a strip of cold silver. She's precise even when she's relaxed; the words she offers are designed to land with function and with optics.

Lydia Voss: "We can put pumps on lease for emergency response immediately, and I can push for fast-tracking permits. In return, we ask for a co-branded pilot, investor oversight on milestones, and agreed-upon media access. Visibility helps future fundraising."

You watch Jonah Reyes's jaw set. You hear the small betrayal in the word "oversight"—the same word that can keep bad actors honest or seize the steering wheel from people who have spent their lives steering these waters.

Marin Solace [internal]: "The grant could secure the docks Jonah worries about. It could also hand away the terms we fought for in back rooms and over baths of kelp stew. You know what 'visibility' means here: it buys scale quickly but blunts your elbow when you try to steer."

Asha wipes her hands on her overalls and meets your eyes with the blunt, technical empathy that always steadies you.

Asha: "We need time-series monitoring. Ecological lags mean you don't know a system's response until a season or two. If we scale too fast, we won't see unintended consequences until it's baked into the shoreline."

Jonah Reyes's fingers drum an impatient rhythm against the counter.

Jonah Reyes: "I get the science. But every tide that takes another plank off my cousin's dock is a tide too many. If the grant covers retrofits now—materials, labor—why do we wait? People are losing income while we're waiting for an 'ecological lag' to stop being an excuse."

Raff leans forward, grin half-urgent, half-scheming, the kind of grin that imagines turning a near-disaster into a trending hashtag.

Raff: "We could run it as a staged narrative—show the science, show the retrofits, step the public along. Viral docu-episodes. People love a procedural: problem, solution, emotional follow-through. That keeps funds and public trust aligned."

Ben Rhee [gentle]: "This is why the funder structured milestones. It balances urgency with proof points. But the media deliverables worry me—you know what investor PR can do. We have to write in oversight and community control."

Lydia Voss crosses her arms, not an accusation but a pivot to strategy.

Lydia Voss: "Oversight doesn't have to mean control. It can mean independent auditing, shared metrics. If this is framed as a co-branded success story, it unlocks regional funds. The pumps are non-negotiable for the emergency season ahead. I can get them on-site within weeks."

You pace; the floorboards creak like a map unfolding. Every footstep is a second in a conversation mapped out in your head: clauses, contingencies, who sits on committees, how to make media arcs honest, how to demand habitat-first metrics without looking like you're obstructing rescue. Your thumb traces the chipped enamel pin on your jacket—a lighthouse you keep more for the steadiness of its image than for any particular person.

Marin Solace [internal]: "The town wants action. The pilot opened a door; momentum is a wind that will close as fast as it came if we don't set sails. Yet the history in these planks and the memory folded into Elder Mae's shawls won't survive if we trade every principle for quick coverage."

Asha steps closer and speaks with the quiet ferocity of someone who has spent nights translating soil respiration charts for neighbors who only wanted to know if their crops would survive.

Asha: "If we accept staged scaling with tight conditional control, we build in phased checkpoints, independent auditors we nominate, binding clauses for habitat metrics, and a public steering committee. It's slow, but it's defensible."

Jonah Reyes's voice tightens; it's an edge you recognize because you feel it too.

Jonah Reyes: "Slow isn't useless. But slow is also a luxury—one people here can't afford if their livelihood's at stake next storm. If pumps can be in while we negotiate, that's a compromise."

Lydia Voss [measured]: "Pumps during the emergency season are a pragmatic starting point. Co-branding helps the funder secure their portfolio narrative. I'm offering technical capacity and political muscle in exchange for oversight privileges."

Ben Rhee folds his hands, the politician becoming mediator.

Ben Rhee: "We can negotiate the language. Community oversight, but with clear deliverables. We don't have to cede authority—we can write boundaries. But we need to give the funder confidence, and that means they want measurable progress and visibility."

You savor that small steadiness—a hand on Jonah Reyes's arm, the paper under your fingertips—but the room keeps moving. Each person brings a different clock: Asha's seasons, Raff's viral rhythms, Ben's electoral timeline, Lydia Voss's investor cadence. You listen to each and hold them like separate coals, aware that where they meet might start a fire—or a hearth.

Lydia Voss: "If you provide the names of oversight members and commit to a phased media plan that frames the science responsibly, we can write pumps into the first milestone and guarantee fast-track reviews for permits associated with emergency infrastructure."

Asha [quiet, but hard]: "We need to define habitat-first metrics—percentage survival of marsh transplants, sediment accretion benchmarks, minimum biodiversity indices. And audits must be transparent."

Raff: "We tag every phase with short-form video: explanation, worker profiles, ecological updates. People follow people. They don't follow jargon."

Jonah Reyes: "And docks get retrofitted in phase one if pumps and materials are on-site. I want workers paid; I want maintenance scheduled. No loopholes."

You step toward the north window, palm pressed to the cool glass. The boardwalk lights scatter on the wet plank, each one a patient, small promise. You think of Elder Mae's knot—how she measured tide and memory in the same thread—and wonder what knot you'll make of this.

Marin Solace [internal]: "I want to keep the cooperative's hand on procedure. I want jobs protected now. I want media to portray what we're doing honestly. I want Lydia's offers to be tools, not levers."

Ben Rhee watches you, waiting in that way people wait when a leader takes the time to collect themselves. He trusts the pause; he knows you will find the seam between urgency and prudence if anyone can.

Ben Rhee: "We need a path that keeps the town's agency intact while giving the funder confidence. Marin—what would that path look like from your perspective?"

You fold the petition in your hands, feeling the edges where signatures once pressed down like teeth.

Marin Solace: "We accept the staged infusion, but on our terms. Phased implementation with binding habitat metrics; community-selected auditors; a public steering committee that must approve each milestone; pumps as emergency equipment only—deployed under community oversight—and guaranteed maintenance funding for fishermen retrofits in the first phase."

Lydia Voss studies you as if reading fine print in a longer document. For a moment her face is unreadable—neither concession nor refusal, simply a calculation.

Lydia Voss: "That language is workable. The funder wants guardrails, not gridlock. We can draft a memorandum of understanding that reflects those points—if you agree to a public media schedule and co-branding."

You feel the room tilt, available pathways brightening like tide-swept sand.

Asha: "Whatever we do, we have to protect the long game. If we get pumps and the docks held, then we also plant the marshes that will hold their own against the next tides. This is how we make an ascent that stays up."

Marin Solace [internal]: "There is an ascent before you—money, attention, capacity all stacked like scaffolding against the horizon. The question has always been who holds the bolts. Your chest loosens at the thought that the cooperative could keep a wrench on the build, even as the town climbs."

Lydia Voss picks up a mug of tea and sets it down precisely, the motion of someone who has seen too many crises staged as absolutes.

Lydia Voss: "We want the town to be safe and to be proud of what you build. I can live with the governance you propose, as long as we stay on the timeline that keeps funder confidence."

Jonah Reyes exhales, the sound of someone who has been bracing and can finally let some of it go.

Jonah Reyes: "If that's it—pumps, retrofits, jobs in phase one, and the rest done with the checks Asha wants—then I'm with it. We'll stand behind the steering committee."

You feel that small, steady heat: people aligning, not surrendering. The evening light outside slants gold through rain and makes everything look like it has a chance.

[Scene: Greenridge Solar Terraces | Night]
[Visual: wind brisk, panels glinting; small bulbs line a path between grasses]
[Sound: The soft hum of inverters, wings of small birds in the hedges; near silence in which conversation feels more intimate]
[Music: A hopeful, ascending motif—strings lengthen, a chorus note lingers]

At the terraces, Asha kneels by a test bed, scooping soil between gloved fingers. The photovoltaic panels throw clean, bright planes above native grasses—technology and ecology folded together in a way that looks hopeful even under the bruise of storm-swept clouds.

Asha: "Show them this," she says to you, voice low. "Show them that scale can be gentle."

You put your hand in the soil and feel the cool, the damp, the life that smells faintly of coal and seaweed. It is a small, stubborn resistance against the idea that speed must crush nuance.

Marin Solace [internal]: "This is why you came back. This is why you stayed up nights drawing sections and calling neighbors. The terraces are both symbol and methodology—proof that an ascent can be engineered with care."

Raff trains his phone on Asha and speaks into the camera as if the terraces are a character in a story.

Raff: "This is where tech meets tide. People. Jobs. Nature. You can scale this and still keep hands in—if you write the rules right."

Ben Rhee watches the slope of the terraces, then looks at you, his face open.

Ben Rhee: "We can set milestones tied to ecological performance at places like this—if the terraces meet accretion and biodiversity targets, we move to the next phase. If not, we pause and adapt."

Lydia Voss nods almost imperceptibly; there's a concession there. You feel the possibility of a common script: urgency with guardrails.

Marin Solace [internal]: "The choice you make now will fold into the town's trajectory—around jobs, heritage, and the shape of the shoreline. You can take the ladder and start climbing together, or hand the ladder to someone else and hope they don't carry it away."

Your breath is steady. The instruments are in place. The satchel at your shoulder is full of signatures, clauses, prototypes, promises. The town leans forward like a tide.
