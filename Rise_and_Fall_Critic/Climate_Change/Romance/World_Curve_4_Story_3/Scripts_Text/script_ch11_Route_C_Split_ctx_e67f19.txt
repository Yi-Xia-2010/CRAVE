[Scene: Lydia's Pavilion | Late Evening]
[Visual: The pavilion's glass skin glows against a bruise-dark sea. Inside, cyan and coral surge maps rotate on suspended displays; a modular table scatters printouts and a steaming carafe of coffee. You pause at the sliding door, the cool press of conditioned air meeting the salt-wet evening. The pavilion smells like warm coffee and ozone, like infrastructure and intent—an intoxicating, utilitarian perfume that steadies the shoulders. Your satchel is heavy at your hip with petitions and annotated maps; the paper edges bite your palm a little where you've been carrying urgency like an extra weight.

[Visual: Close-up on your satchel strap; Asha's hand briefly adjusts the map peeking from the bag.]
Asha [soft, determined]: "You ready for this, Marin?"
Marin Solace: "I am. We have to be."

You step fully into the light. The room is clinical but not cold; there is a meticulous generosity to it—everything arranged so decisions can be made and measured. Lydia moves with the same quiet efficiency, hands folded at her waist as if she is already calculating margins.

Lydia Voss: "Thank you for coming. We reviewed your cooperative's materials. Impressive work, clearly. We need local expertise at the table; optics matter. I'm offering you a liaison seat—official, funded, with access to design reviews."

You register the words the way you always do: a door opening, and the frame already shaped to fit.

Lydia Voss [tilting her slate slightly]: "Terms are straightforward. Your suggestions will be admitted for review—subject to veto if they conflict with structural parameters. Messaging will be coordinated with our communications team; we expect synchronized press releases. And—and importantly—we'll ask for a public letter of endorsement tied to your participation."

The air tenses only for a breath. Veto. Synchronized messaging. Endorsement. You see the silhouette of concrete geometry in your mind and, beneath it, the marshes and moorings that won't fit easily into gridlines.

Marin Solace: "A veto… means something proposed for habitat integration could be blocked on the grounds of 'structural risk'?"
Lydia Voss: "It means we proceed on the basis of collective safety and timelines. Nobody wants to delay protections for people. But we cannot allow experimental measures to compromise core thresholds."

Asha's jaw tightens; she is holding a small prototype mudtray between two fingers like a talisman.

Asha: "You mean 'collective safety' as in 'the parts we can quantify.' We've proven living features can both attenuate energy and support biodiversity. You'll examine—"
Lydia Voss [interrupting; not unkind]: "We will examine. Engineering is non-negotiable where life safety is concerned."

You feel the familiar friction: Asha's faith in tested improvisation against Lydia's certainty in proven geometry. It's a conversation you've rehearsed in late-night community meetings, and now it cracks like a recording played in a chamber of glass.

[Visual: The rotating map zooms into a section of shoreline where a grey wall arcs; colored overlays pulse where Lydia's models predict stress points.]
Lydia Voss: "We can carve opportunities for habitat pockets within planned expansion joints and terraces. But—" (she meets your eyes directly) "—those will be negotiated as features of the wall, not as replacements for structural elements."

You swallow. Hope tastes like the metallic tang of the pavilion air, but it is hope nonetheless: a seat at the table, a language of 'can' threaded through 'but.' The liaison is leverage. It isn't total autonomy, but it is presence. You imagine marsh grass threaded into concrete seams, mussel ropes tucked into cofferdams—little lives where there would otherwise be absolute barrier.

Marin Solace: "So we get presence and limited proposal rights. We don't get final say."
Lydia Voss: "Final say rests with the project governance. Liaison role is influence, not authority. In exchange, you get prioritized access to technical briefs and a formal channel to propose adaptive management during construction."

Jonah Reyes is at the edge of the boardwalk outside—those amber eyes a warm, almost unreadable line through the glass. He hasn't come in. He doesn't need to—his silhouette and the rusted whistle at his throat are a punctuation mark at the rim of the room.

[Visual: Jonah on the boardwalk; rain beading on his hair, whistle catching the light]
[Sound: A distant, involuntary inhale — the world holding its breath with you]

You feel Jonah Reyes's presence like a tether. You can read the curl of his fingers around the whistle as concern, as caution, as everything he is when the tide turns. Whatever you choose in here will shift the geometry of his work as surely as it will the town's shoreline.

Jonah Reyes [loud enough to be heard through the glass; voice layered with wind]: "Don't sign away the town for a plaque, Marin."

The sentence lands. It's both accusation and counsel.

Marin Solace [to Jonah, your voice steady]: "I won't. I'm asking for a seat so I can keep the people who live from being erased."
Jonah Reyes [his reply sharp as a line cast]: "Watch the fine print."

There is meaning in the space between his words and your jaw. You want to tell him everything—about clauses and permits and the small, hopeful technicalities that could bend the plan toward life—but you also know this is where diplomacy is measured not in heat but in compromise.

Lydia watches the exchange through the glass with an unreadable expression. You notice the practiced neutrality like an engineered beam—steady and designed to bear load.

Lydia Voss: "If you accept the liaison, we will fast-track your team's proposals for pilot integration at selected joints. If you decline, we will proceed with the contracted schedule and public consultations. Participation accelerates access; refusal preserves independence."

Her words are economic truth: inside influence buys access; resistance buys distance. Both are principled. Both are costly.

Asha shifts closer, voice low and fierce like a tide coming in thin and certain.

Asha: "Marin, sitting at that table means we can keep a hand on the design as it goes. Even small living patches can preserve spawning grounds if sited right. If you refuse, they’ll learn our methods second-hand—or worse, ignore them."

Marin Solace: (you look at the petitions in your satchel—names written in crowded hope) "People signed this because they trust that we'll put them first. If I sign their name to a letter endorsing this, I have to mean it."

Lydia offers a faint, almost disarming smile.

Lydia Voss: "You'll have editorial input on wording. The endorsement is symbolic of partnership. With your name attached, philanthropy and governmental partners will release funds for community stewardship."

Hope threads again through the practical: money pledged for stewardship, the chance to fund maintenance wages—things you've written in bold on the cooperative's drafts.

The sting of compromise sits at your shoulders, but beneath it is a steadier current: the pragmatic work that births protection. You picture Elder Mae's stories of harbors sewn together by hands that refused to let the sea make ghosts of their neighbors. You imagine those hands—your hands—finding a new stitch.

Lydia steps closer, lowering her voice in a cadence that makes it sound like an offer and a test.

Lydia Voss: "This liaison won't be easy. We'll put you on camera. We'll ask for statements that keep investors confident. But you'll also be the face of a hybrid approach to other towns. Imagine if Havenpoint's model scaled—if we could show resilient design that respects livelihoods. Your name attached to a scalable case study opens opportunities."

You feel the rise inside you: the possibility of proof that others can follow, the town not only weathering but teaching. It is the shape of hope you have been carrying—the practical, exportable kind.

Marin Solace: "If I accept, my priority is adaptive management and maintenance wages. My community can't be a token. We've built plans that require long-term labor and oversight. Will you commit to that in scope?"
Lydia Voss [after a beat]: "We will include an implementation committee with community seats and a funding line for maintenance. The committee will have advisory power. Final spending approvals follow governance protocols, but the funding will be allocated."

Asha exhales, a small sound—relief braided with suspicion.

Asha: "That's more than we had hoped for. It buys time to test and iterate."
Jonah Reyes [from the boardwalk, softer now]: "Just don't let 'buying time' become a clock that runs out on us."

The strings swell into a promise—rising, not triumphant, but resolute. Everything in you shifts toward a single truth: presence matters. Voice matters. Leverage matters.

But there is another edge to this, and you feel its weight: the temptation to accept and use the platform in ways nobody expects—document constraints, gather proof, reveal promises broken if they are broken. It is dangerous, and it is not clean. It would be a kind of honesty that could burn bridges and unsettle people who depend on the façade of cooperation.

You imagine the logistics: copying files from a slate under a pretense, anonymizing documents, timing a release when the town's attention is fixed elsewhere. It could expose malfeasance; it could also delegitimize a project that, in some parts, truly protects people. The moral line hums like a live wire.

Marin Solace [you speak, voice even]: "There is another option. If I accept, I could document bottlenecks and constraints—be ready to release them if oversight fails. It would be risky. It could protect the town from later harms, or it could collapse trust at a moment we need cooperation."

Lydia Voss [eyes narrowed, calculating]: "Leaks are dangerous, Marin. They compromise coordinated response and amplify uncertainty. If your intent is to sabotage, that's not partnership. If your intent is to ensure accountability, propose it formally within our oversight protocols."

You hear in her words both warning and a narrow invitation: accountability can be woven in. But can it be enforced? And would a leak—if it happened—do more harm than the harm it prevents?

Jonah Reyes's figure on the boardwalk looks like an unreadable score. His hands are still at the whistle; his presence is a quiet question you cannot dismiss.

Asha squeezes your forearm—tight, grounding.

Asha: "Whatever you choose, make sure it's something you can stand behind in the morning when the tide is high and the reporters are louder than the gulls."

You inhale. The room's warm lights seem to sharpen into a spotlight on your responsibility. Around you, the pavilion becomes a place where technical certainty and human consequence are braided. You feel the rising possibility: that one of these paths could steer more life into the plan than has been predicted, that compromise could be the instrument of preservation if wielded with principle.
