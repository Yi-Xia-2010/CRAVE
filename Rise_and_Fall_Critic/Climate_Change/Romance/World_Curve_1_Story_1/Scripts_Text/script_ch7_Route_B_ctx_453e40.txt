Eli stands beside you, jacket damp at the shoulders; Hal leans on the railing like a weathered mast. A smattering of protesters gathers in the plaza below, bright banners bobbing under umbrellas.]
[Sound: Rain tapping a steady, quick rhythm on the canvas; distant chanting and the muffled bubble of a megaphone from the plaza; the metallic click of an umbrella snapping shut]

You arrive the same way you've come to most hard conversations: hands slightly damp, sensor reader tucked into your jacket, words rehearsed but pliable enough to bend with what you hear. The awning smells of wet canvas and old varnish, and beneath it the air is warmer, edged with the faint, honest scent of espresso from a nearby cart.

Eli gives you a small, private nod — the kind that says he's here in full competence and that the blueprints in his satchel are more than props. Hal's gaze travels past the protesters, searching the plaza for the faces that will be most affected. Marco steps out of his car with a measured ease that could be perfume or practiced rhythm; he wears his raincoat unbuttoned, blazer pressed beneath, and a smile that has been smoothed for public use.

Marco Voss: "Amara, Elias. Hal. You're punctual — I do like that. We can get wet for the right reasons, but let's keep the meeting dry."

You notice how he uses names like a ledger: precise, transactional, but with an attempt at warmth. The awning folds the world into a smaller frame: you, him, the rain, the protesters making a steady backbone of noise. The town hall doors stand behind him like a neutral witness.

Hal: "Marco. If you're offering more than talk, we should have clarity. Seabright doesn't need speeches; it needs guarantees."

Marco's smile doesn't falter. He opens a slim portfolio and hands Eli a glossy packet that smells faintly of fresh ink and money.

Marco Voss: "Not speeches. A framework. Funding, engineered protections, and redevelopment that pays people back into the town. Jobs. Infrastructure. Not just promises—deliverables."

Eli flips through the packet, fingers mapping the sections like a reader of tide charts. His jaw tightens at diagrams of broad seawalls and elevated promenades drawn in clean, confident lines.

Elias 'Eli' Navarro: "The engineering sketches are ambitious. Precast modular seawalls, integrated microgrid routing... it's feasible. But the devil's in the joints — who owns the land under the new rise? Who bears maintenance costs in year five, ten?"

You step forward because that's where your work lives: in the clauses and the margins, the parts other people call 'bureaucracy' and you call 'lifelines'.

Amara Vale: "We need to talk displacement. We need to talk about who gets rehoused if properties are absorbed into redevelopment. We need guarantees about local hiring and protections for small businesses."

Your voice is steadier than you feel. You feel the crowd's breathing as a pressure through the awning — Miriam's megaphone punctuating the rain like a second heartbeat. Marco's expression cools, then warms again as he chooses his tone.

Marco Voss: "We can build with community partners. Equity clauses can be written. But—" he taps the packet, "—you understand fundraising. Restrictions can slow or even kill a project. I can move resources fast if we allow certain flexibilities."

There's a soft prick of impatience in Hal's posture. He leans forward, hands clasping the railing.

Hal: "Fast is a word that excuses corners. Fast is the reason half the repairs after '29 failed. You cannot paper over structural history with glossy promises."

Marco: "And slow is the reason families sit through storm after storm waiting for public coffers to catch up. We need both speed and care. I'm offering a middle path."

You can see the middle path drawn in his slides, engineered to be politically and economically palatable. It glints in a way you admire and distrust at once.

Marco Voss: "Permanent rehousing where possible. Where not, relocation assistance and priority in new housing stock. I can earmark funds for small-business grants. You tell me the redlines, Amara — I'm willing to negotiate."

The word 'negotiate' hangs there like a tide-line. Your mind catalogues what 'willing' usually means: negotiation prepared in advance, concessions built into the scaffolding.

Elias 'Eli' Navarro interjects with a technical question, his tone sandwiched between engineer and friend.

Elias 'Eli' Navarro: "If the seawall is modular and privately maintained, we need clarity on inspection regimes and public access rights. Also — storm surge gates need community operation protocols. You can't let maintenance become a cost that gets passed back to residents."

Marco: "Maintenance could be a public-private trust. We fund it; you appoint the oversight."

Elias 'Eli' Navarro [quiet, searching]: "Who appoints the oversight? The town council? A board stacked with developers? We need transparency codified—not the kind that vanishes between boardrooms."

Hal taps the rail, a small rhythm to match Elias 'Eli' Navarro's cadence.

Hal: "There was a memorial for '29 down in the harbor. People still carry its memory. You build anything here without honoring how people lost trust, you'll be building on sand."

Marco inclines his head. For a beat, he seems to meet Hal on an older map of memory rather than spreadsheets.

Marco Voss: "Then put Hal on the oversight board. Put Amara's Beacon in charge of community audits. Name a neutral ombudsman. We can codify it."

The offer surprises you. It's a concession that feels raw and promising at once. The awning seems to hold the promise like a warmed glass — visible condensation of possibility.

You think of the Beacon's whiteboards, of nights with Rosa where you catalogued testimonies and sensor spikes — the slow accretion of evidence that could be law. You think of Miriam, whose voice is raw with righteous urgency, and of Hal, who remembers the bones of the town.

Your internal list unfurls: rehousing clauses, local hiring quotas, transparent oversight, maintenance trusts, public access guarantees. Each item is a stitch in a safety net. Each item is negotiable in language.

Marco smiles as if reading your list through your face.

Marco Voss: "You want control points. Fine. Name them, and we'll bake them into the contract. I'm not asking to own the town; I'm offering capital and execution. Let's make Seabright livable for a hundred years."

There's a promise in that hundred-year measure — long enough to matter, short enough to be actionable. The rain slows to a fine mist, and in its softening there's a lightening in your chest. Rise — you remind yourself — this chapter must tilt toward possibility.

Elias 'Eli' Navarro studies Marco's packet again, then looks at you with an expression you recognize: pragmatic hope.

Elias 'Eli' Navarro: "If we're going to draw redlines, we should start with five items: permanent rehousing, capped buyouts, local hiring clauses, a public oversight board with veto power, and a maintenance fund with independent audits."

You trace each word mentally like a tide map. These are the first cuts on a contract that could bind futures.

Hal: "And a clause that binds successors. Developers change. Contracts outlive intentions. Make sure what you build today isn't up for auction tomorrow."

Marco doesn't flinch. If anything, his pleasantness deepens into a kind of allyship.

Marco Voss: "Impractical demands kill projects. But those five points are not impractical if we write them clearly. If you're open to it, we'll draft a preliminary term sheet. You and Eli can redline it. Hal can advise on historical precedence. Miriam and the community get consultative seats."

The idea of consultative seats nods in your head like a hinge. It will not satisfy everyone, but it is a room at the table.

Outside, Miriam's voice rises, then softens. A chant becomes a chorus, and for a moment the two sounds — public outcry and private negotiation — overlap like opposing tides. You feel the overlap as an engine of possibility: conflict can be harnessed, not only endured.

Marco folds the packet closed and looks at all of you with an earnestness that is almost infectious.

Marco Voss: "I can commit to drafting a term sheet within a week. I want to move fast but with care. Let me show you my attorneys' preliminary language. You tell me what you will never sign. We'll work around those hard lines."

You feel the room tilt. This is not the contract yet; it's the start of a thing that could turn into protection or tokenism. But the tilt is toward collaboration: private capital tethered to public constraints and oversight.

Hal: "If you're serious, you'll let the Beacon's audits be binding and public. No sealed arbitration clauses."

Marco: "No sealed arbitration. Public audits. Binding language where appropriate."

Elias 'Eli' Navarro: "And an enforceable cap on buyouts, with a formula tied to median town income, not speculative market value."

Marco Voss: "Agreed."

Each assent is a small victory, a tightening of the rope that will hold negotiations from fraying. Your shoulders lower as each practical point gains a verbal anchor. The rain eases into a hush. The protesters' chant becomes a steady, watchful murmur.

You imagine the Beacon's whiteboards again: the meeting notice, the tide graphs, the list of redlines you will draft with Eli. The shorelines of policy begin to look navigable. You find, with a soft surprise, that you are not carrying the whole weight alone anymore — not because it is no longer heavy, but because others are willing to shoulder it beside you.

Marco offers a final look, not triumphant but collaborative.

Marco Voss: "Give me your list. I won't promise everything, but I will promise the process. Let us try to build Seabright together."

You taste salt and coffee and the adrenaline of a negotiation that might change lives. Hope, practical and earned, rises under the awning.

You nod. The next step is obvious and necessary: take his promises to the Beacon, write a list, and prepare redlines that will anchor any contract. You feel the outline of the next phase in your hands like a map — firm, actionable, demanding.

You have a week to turn talk into terms. The town will watch. Miriam will test you from the plaza, Hal will keep the memory of storms at the center, and Elias 'Eli' Navarro will draft the engineering specifics that make promises safe. Marco has shown he can be pragmatic and persuasive; the test is whether you can translate that into enforceable measures.

There is a clarity that feels almost like relief: progress will require boundary-setting, not blind acceptance. You exhale and feel the dampness on your sleeve cool into resolve.

You step down from the awning toward the plaza, the crowd's chant folding into conversation. Miriam lifts her megaphone and meet your eye across the small distance — the recognition between organizer and scientist that this moment will be fought for in clauses and in streets.

There is work to do, and for the first time in a long while, the work looks like a road you can walk with others.
