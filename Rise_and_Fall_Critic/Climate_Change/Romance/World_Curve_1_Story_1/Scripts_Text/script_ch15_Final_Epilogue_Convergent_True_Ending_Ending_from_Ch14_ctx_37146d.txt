You watch his face in the lowering light. He looks more steady than you remember—less haunted by the last crunch of a failed permit, more steady with the weight of practical solutions. There's an ease between the two of you that the storm has both tested and, somehow, tempered.

You feel the memory of another kind of water: the ledger of sensor spikes, the signatures on bylaws, the early mornings you spent drafting microfinance terms on napkins. Those memories are not triumphal trophies. They are tools and scars, folded into the map the trust keeps of what was sacrificed to build what stands now.

Amara Vale: "We carried it together. I just happened to make the map."

You think of the pottery shards in a box, the shop window you washed for someone you once liked, the names in the rehousing ledger. Those are not losses to immortalize like trophies; they are stitches in a living thing. You do not mean to hold on to loss like ballast, but you won't let the town forget either. That memory will be part of the trust's record—a map of what was sacrificed to start the new work.
