You feel your mouth set. His words are what the town needs to hear in one register—speed, money, a promise. But the room hums with past lessons: seawalls that failed without maintenance, funds spent without oversight, families uprooted without rehousing plans.

Amara Vale: "Speed without guarantees empties promises of their meaning." (You keep your voice even; the Beacon's whiteboard pays homage in your mind.) "We won't sign on to a plan that builds something only to let it fail because upkeep wasn't planned. Marco's smile narrows, but he doesn't look surprised. He folds something from the portfolio—a glossy page, then another.

Marco: "We can discuss oversight. We can set maintenance schedules. But escrowed funds slow construction. Investors want clarity—deliverables. The town needs protection now."

Eli: "We can have both." (Eli's voice is calm; he knows how to hinge argument and people.) "Phased deliverables. Let the pilot seawall be the first milestone, but tie each milestone to escrow releases and clear oversight triggers. Miriam: (leaning forward) "And who sits on this oversight? Who audits the money? Who ensures rehousing funds aren't rerouted as 'operational expenses'?"

Hal: (quiet, with the weight of winters in his voice) "You remember what happened after '98. Seawall built, maintenance promised, and the promise went with the people who could write the check. If you don't lock the maintenance endowment into an independent trust, the wall will become someone else's problem."

Rosa: (easy, bridging) "People need to know where they'll live when roads close. Businesses need rent guarantees. If we can say 'this trust pays rent for X months'—that's real."

You let the air carry each name's insistence into the open. You can feel the momentum like a tide: it wants to move, and you're steering it.

Amara Vale: "We write the maintenance endowment into the contract. We set an independent community trust as steward—board seats reserved for residents, scientists, and municipal auditors. Rehousing budgets go into escrow, released under community-defined triggers. No opaque 'operational expenses' clause."

Marco: (a measured pause) "Independent trust—who decides its board? You propose residents and scientists—fine. But corporate partners will need seats, too. It's their capital."

Eli: "Seats, yes. But with defined roles and voting thresholds that prevent unilateral decisions. A supermajority for budget redirect ensures oversight."

Miriam: "And a recall mechanism. If the trust strays, the community can act. Immediate recall, transparent audits—Hal's right."

Hal: "And a maintenance schedule written into law, not into a sidebar note. Annual inspections, publicly posted stress reports, and automatic funding triggers when thresholds are breached."

Marco studies the plaza, the faces. The sun slants and catches his lapel pin. For a beat he looks like a man weighing options rather than a salesman.

Marco: "I'll have counsel draft amendments to the agreement to include an endowment. I'll consider community-appointed trustees. But the timetable has to remain competitive. Contractors won't hold their bids forever."

You sense the negotiation is a dance around trust. The town wants protection and identity; Marco offers resources. Eli and Hal are the bridge—practical constraints married to institutional memory.

You move the discussion toward the restoration corridors. The pilot seawall will keep the promenade's immediate edge, but it must be paired with living systems that absorb wave energy and maintain biodiversity.

A cheer rises—less of triumph than relief. The plaza breathes with it. You feel the lift: years of worry unspooling into an agreement that contains the scaffolding to be built, guarded, and lived in.
