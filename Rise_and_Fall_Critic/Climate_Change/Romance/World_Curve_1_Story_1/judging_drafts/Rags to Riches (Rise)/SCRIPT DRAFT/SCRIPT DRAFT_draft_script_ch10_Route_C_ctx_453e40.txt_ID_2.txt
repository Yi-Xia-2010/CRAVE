[Scene: Town Plaza — Early Afternoon]
[Visual: The neoclassical steps of Town Hall, the plaza filling; banners spring like flowers — hand-painted slogans, the community crest, little sea-glass charms tied to poles]

The plaza answers you with a chorus of voices. You stand on the flattop of the fountain's rim — dry for now — and watch as people lay down blankets, fix signs to poles, and set a wall of folding chairs in a semicircle for speakers. Old Man Tor arrives with a carved buoy hanging from a rope, the timber worn smooth by decades of hands. He anchors it at the front like a relic from a religion that keeps weather at its center.

Old Man Tor: (voice like wind through rigging) "This place remembers every storm. You carrying that memory forward — it keeps us alive in more ways than one."

You: "We can't live just remembering. We have to act — and we have to make sure the action will hold people. That's why we want a charter: public financing first, legal protections, rehousing guarantees."

Hal appears with sleeves rolled, a clipboard tucked under his arm. He smells like diesel and salt; his presence is an old spine of steadiness.

Hal: "We can make the staging safe. I'll put red lines where emergency lanes must be. If council wants to test our resolve, they can run over those lines, and we'll know they're not for the people."

You: "That's the point. We can't give in to half-measures that strand people later."

Hal: (a slow, fond snort) "You get more stubborn every year."

The crowd surges in that way that community does when solid ground is found: hands clasping, nods traded like currency, vocal flourishes punctuating shared stories. A woman near the front tells a short, sharp account of rising basement water in the last storm — her voice cracks and then steadies. A teenage volunteer unspools a banner that reads PROMISES ARE NOT PROTECTION and the plaza erupts with laughter and cheers. The mood is building toward something that feels larger than you, which is exactly what it should be.

Eli stands slightly apart from the core group, his hands in his pockets, a reflective vest slung over his arm though he hasn't put it on. His face is open and complex — pride fringing something like worry. He meets your eyes and gives a small, almost private smile; you meet it back, and in that exchange there's a room where shared purpose lives without needing to name anything else.

Eli: (stepping forward, voice low so only you hear) "If you want structural plans for the barricade and safe zones, I can have a basic schematic by tonight. It'll optimize flow and reduce pressure on the old sewers."

You: "Yes. Keep them simple. Public-minded. I don't want something that looks like it's designed to be a hard sell later."

Eli: (a beat, then) "I know. We'll keep it open source. No signatures from firms."

You: "Thank you."

The exchange hangs like a knot tied between you — neither hand over the other's, just tightened so you can depend on it.

Miriam climbs atop a crate and takes the megaphone. Her speech is cadenced and urgent; the crowd quiets as if the plaza itself is leaning in. She names the things that have been whispered for months in front porches and under breath: the predatory clauses in developer contracts, the disappearing fishing licenses, the way insurance doubles as displacement.

Miriam: "We are not just asking for a seat at the table. We are building the table. We will fund the public defenses we need. We will hold our leaders accountable. And we will not hand our waterfront to shadow promises."

The applause is immediate and visceral. People clap on both fists; someone breaks into a chant that spreads like a flock: "Public funds! Public voice!" The chant molds into rhythm and then into song. You stand in the center of that sound and find yourself nearly crying from how fierce and bright it is.
