You step inside with salt still drying on your boots and the scent of tomato-scented soup pulling at the edges of your attention. The whiteboards are already half-full: tide graphs, a messy column of costs, volunteer rosters scrawled in different ink colors. Rosa has pushed two mismatched tables together; steaming bowls wait under a lamp. Hal’s sweater smells faintly of oil and dust. Eli stands by the projector with a laptop open, his wristband blinking a soft green like a calm tide.

[Visual: Close-up on your field notebook with the faded lighthouse sticker; your hand brushes the cover]
[Sound: Marker cap clicks; the beeping from one of the coastal sensors is steady and almost reassuring]

You feel the familiar tug — the load of responsibility that used to fold you inward. Today it spreads outward: chairs arranged, people arriving, a budget to shape. You breathe in the warmth of the room, the tang of salt and broth, and let the steadiness move through you like a tide that lifts, not drags.

Rosa (cheerful, practical): "Alright, brains and bellies. We're not starving, and we're not broke until we count everything. Amara — you and Eli said you'd keep it practical. No pie charts without pie."

You (smiling, steadier than you feel): "Agreed. I'll start with the ecological lines, then we tie them to maintenance and stipends. If we show realistic timelines, funders won't glaze over."

Eli (leaning in, fingers steepled): "We can demo a raised promenade module in six weeks with local labor. Cost per linear meter is better than full seawalls, and it buys us time to plant the mangroves. But the capital's tight — if we also promise retrofits for ten businesses, the timeline stretches."

You watch Hal as he rubs a knuckle you recognize from old projects; his face folds like a map of the town's storms. Miriam arranges sticky notes into columns labeled "Equity", "Feasibility", "Visibility".

Hal (soft, remembering): "When we built that first boardwalk after '29, we thought we'd fixed everything. Maintenance killed more projects than the ocean did. Put the upkeep line where folks can see it. Train neighbors now — don't make it something the council budgets out of sight."

Miriam (sharp, forward): "And stipends. Volunteers can't volunteer forever. If the grant doesn't pay livelihoods, you hollow out your volunteer force. We have to put money where people live."

Rosa (nudging you): "And tell them the mangroves are not just pretty; they're living breakwaters. People get that if you show them seedlings beside their café."

You let your fingers trace the margin of your notebook. Data has always been your language, but you remember the first time an old fisherwoman asked you, quietly, whether the mangroves would keep the harbor where her grandson learned to row. Science becomes promise when it touches a life. You want to make that translation here and now.

You (decisive): "Okay. Three funding buckets we present: ecological resilience — mangroves and the promenade scaffolding; immediate small-business retrofits and stipends; and a systemic option — a microgrid pilot that reduces long-term costs. We map costs, timelines, and who we need to persuade for each."

Eli (nods, cautious smile): "I'll run the construction estimates. Hal can flag maintenance traps. Miriam, can you outline the stipend model and political hooks? Rosa, you can lead community engagement — bake and talk."

Rosa (hand to heart, grinning): "Baked persuasion always wins."

[Sound: The low murmur of neighbors gathering beyond the Beacon doors]

You feel a moment of light: a room aligned around a plan. It's small — paperwork, numbers, a list of names — but it's movement toward something that might outlast any single administration or developer pitch.

Eli watches your face as you choose how to open. There's a small, private history in the way his brow lifts — he trusts you to steer the tone between pragmatism and heart. You let that trust steady your hand and pick the opening that feels truest to who you want to be in this room: translator, not showman; connector, not lone crusader.

The debate widens, and with it a chorus of practical questions — who maintains the plants at high tide, how a raised plank connects to private storefronts, what insurance says about temporary scaffolding. You answer with the sensor data you've brought: tidal cadence, erosion nodes, volunteer capacity estimates. Each figure is met with another question that refines the plan rather than tearing it down.

Hal (pointing at a list): "If we train six local teams to inspect the promenade monthly, you cut the chance of catastrophic failure drastically. Add a small maintenance reserve in year one — even five percent of construction, earmarked, visible."

Miriam (leaning on the table): "Visibility on the books matters. People need to see the maintenance money, not just read it on page three of a grant. It builds trust and resists privatization."

Rosa (worried, quietly): "But my regulars — the bakery, the bait shop — they need help this winter. If they close, the town's fabric frays. Retrofits get them through storms. Don't let them be the sacrificial edge for an ideal."

Eli (meeting your gaze): "We can phase it. Demo the promenade, plant the first tranche of mangroves, and run a small retrofits pilot for the most vulnerable storefronts. The question is where the largest initial chunk of funding goes. Seedlings and scaffolding buy ecological time. Retrofits buy economic breathing room."

You feel the weight of that sentence in your chest like a tide hitting a rock: both are true, both necessary, both expensive. The room buzzes with possibility — not helplessness — and that buzz lifts something in you. You had feared being the lone martyr for either ecology or livelihoods; now you feel the muscle of community judgment flexing alongside you.

Eli folds his arms, thinking, then uncrosses them. He taps a spreadsheet on his laptop and pulls up a projection with three columns: cost, short-term social impact, long-term resilience. The numbers aren't final, but they map out a spectrum you can all see.

Eli (softly): "If we front-load mangrove + promenade, we get the highest resilience after five years. If we front-load retrofits, we keep more businesses open now, but the coast remains vulnerable. The microgrid is transformational but we need legal and policy work — and that could take months."

Miriam (leaning back, pragmatic): "Transformation runs on time you might not have. We fight fires and also try to change the system. Neither is wrong, but the funding narrative needs to speak to voters and funders differently."

You recall why you came back to Seabright: not to win any single argument, but to stitch the town's future so people could keep living in the place they love. That conviction steadies the rising hope in your chest. Hope here is not naïve cheer; it is the product of hands on plans, of skill shared, of the hard arithmetic of compassion.

You (quiet, firm): "We present a clear ask. One preferred path, with backup plans. Funders like clarity. The council likes options. And the town needs to feel included in whatever we choose."

Hal (smiling in a way that makes the room lighter): "Then let's make the ask simple and honest. Line items, names, timelines, and a promise to publish monthly maintenance reports. Show them we mean it."

Rosa ladles soup into bowls and sets them down; neighbors exchange murmured approval. The room's mood is lifted; people talk less like opponents and more like collaborators. The sense of momentum swells — you can almost feel the town's seawall forming, plank by plank, volunteer by paid worker, seedling by sapling.

You think of the small scar on your knuckle, the maps in your notebook, the conversations you've held on soggy porches and in Hal's cluttered garage. Those pieces have coalesced into something public: a plan to ask for money, but also to ask for stewardship.

The debate narrows to three clear strategic options. You can feel the room bracing to choose the emphatic voice of the plan — not to close doors, but to set the town's next step. Your chest hums with a luminous responsibility: to translate affection for Seabright into a coherent proposal that survives grant committees and storm surges.

You step back from the board and let the three options hang in the light, each with its promise and its trade-offs. It is your voice they want now — the one that will name the direction.
