[Scene: Raised Promenade | Morning]
[Visual: A new section of raised promenade, pale cedar planks still smelling faintly of sawdust and salt. Muddy stakes line the water's edge where young mangroves take hold. Volunteers move like contented ants — hauling rope, tamping earth, laughing with tired throats.]
[Sound: The slap of boots on plank, distant gull calls, quiet conversation stitched with the metallic clank of tools and the soft splash of water against pilings.]
[Music: Bright, rising strings — gentle, buoyant; the kind of chord that makes you straighten your shoulders.]

You step onto the first fully raised plank and feel the give of its newness beneath your boots: solid, forgiving, not old enough to remember the storms but built to remember them better. The plank smells like cedar and salt and the faint sweetness of resin. For a beat you let yourself simply stand there and breathe it in — a small, clean victory after months of meetings and a thousand tiny refusals and agreements.

Rosa appears at the end of the promenade with a thermos and an armful of wrapped pastries, her cap bright against the washed sky. She waves like the mayor of good appetite and good news. Behind her, Miriam corrals a late volunteer into a grin; Hal shuffles with a practical grin and a bag of spare bolts slung over his shoulder. Old Man Tor stands by a sapling, hands folded over something he treats like a relic.

Eli is kneeling by a newly installed fastener, his forearm dusted with sawdust and a faint line of mud on his cheek. When he looks up you see it in his face: the same steady concentration that solves structural problems, now softened by something that has room for warmth. He drops the wrench and meets your eyes.

Eli: "We measured the tide last night, like you wanted. The gauge held the variance we modeled, plus a margin. The pilings—" (he taps the timber) "—are set deep enough. The tests ran clean."

You: "And the sensors?" Your voice is small with the kind of hope that can be spoken plainly now.

Eli: "Online. Feeding into Hal's old server with the new aggregator Miriam got exempted from permit timelines. You can watch the readout in the Beacon this afternoon." He ducks his head, an awkward, shy motion, then grins. "Also—Rosa baked celebratory pie, and she threatens to make a banner if we don't ceremonially plant the first mangrove."

You laugh. The sound surprises you — bright and a little relieved. The community noises knit into a chorus behind the two of you: an elderly neighbor offering tips to a baffled teenager, someone calling for a rope, a child's shrill breath of delight as a crab sidles near a puddle.

Miriam (approaching with a clipboard): "Micro-loan committee's ready to sign the first three agreements today. The community maintenance fund has seed money from the joint grant, and Rosa's already organized a rota. We can start scheduled checks next month."

You watch the way her voice lifts on the word "we" and feel the word settle like a brace under your ribs. It is not the solitude you once carried; it is scaffolding made of other people's hands.

Eli reaches for a sapling, and whether you chose to kneel or to stand with the sensor, his fingers touch yours — not a pronouncement, but a quiet agreement to continue. The mangrove's roots are fragile and stubborn at once; they take kindness and consistent attention. It feels, in both metaphor and mud, like the beginning of something that needs tending.

Hal (chuckling, voice like an old rope): "You two better not argue over mulch. I've seen enough engineering debates to fill the harbor, but if you mess up the planting I'll have you both hauling those bags at sunset."

You: "We won't fight about mulch," you say, and there's truth in the promise. You loop a length of biodegradable twine around the stake, tighter than needed and softer than your conviction used to be.

Old Man Tor steps forward and hands you a small carved buoy — it's varnished smooth, salt-creaken lines etched into the wood. He doesn't say much; he never does. He looks at the sapling and then at the water as if naming a thing twice could keep it.

Old Man Tor: (soft) "This coast remembers who tends it. Plant well."

His words hang, small and honest, and you imagine the many hands that have placed saplings and stakes and better ideas into this town over a hundred tides. That memory steadies you.

Rosa arrives, brandishing a thermos and a paper plate like a trumpeter of the mundane. She claps her hands, flour dust motes rising.

Rosa: "Okay! First mangrove officially planted. Pie time. But also, we have donors at the Beacon wanting to see the sensor suite. Marco's representatives are coming through later to watch the demo. They're... here, standing by the old boardwalk. Watch your faces."

You spare a glance toward the older section of boardwalk and see two smartly dressed people — corporate raincoats, hands tucked into tablets. Their faces are polite masks; their eyes flick toward the new boards and the volunteers with an appraising tilt. The expression they hold is complex: interest wrapped with a practiced unreadability. You don't need to pry; Marco's firm has a way of orbiting progress.

Eli (quiet, measured): "They can watch. They'll see a pilot that works because everyone here had stakes — literally and figuratively. If they take anything, let them take an example of how community funding and careful engineering can do more than flashy, top-down promises."

Miriam: "And if they'll put pressure on council to speed permits after seeing this, all the better. We keep the terms public. We keep the maintenance plans binding." Her jaw tightens, the organizer's ferocity present as a line in her voice.

You feel the lift of optimism in the room warming into something practical — micro-loans flowing into businesses that need retrofits, a maintenance rota that pays local labor, sensors feeding data into public dashboards. The pilot has become currency you can trade for more collective action.

You find yourself committing — not in a single grand gesture, but in a string of small agreements: to the rota, to Hal's suggested monthly checks, to Miriam's clause that requires transparency in any outside investment. You sign names on dotted lines, add your initials to schedules, and each pen stroke feels like a plank laid down toward a future you can live inside.

Eli catches your eye across the assembled volunteers. He walks over, the little swagger replaced by a soft purpose. He doesn't make an announcement. He doesn't need to. He sets a hand on your shoulder, and you notice how it fits: the thumb tapping against your scarf, the weight familiar and steady.

Eli: "We keep doing it like this?" he asks, the sentence more a question of method than of romance, but it contains both.

You: "Yeah," you say simply, and the word is enough to make the future feel possible. "We keep doing it. Together."

Eli: "Good. Because I—" He hesitates, then smiles, the kind that bends his eyes into small crescents. "I like building things with you. The stuff that lasts."

You both laugh, a short, relieved sound shared between people who know how to measure tide tables and how to measure a quiet promise.

Miriam (overhearing, loud and delighted): "Don't you two start a construction romance timeline on me. We've got a council meeting and three micro-loan signings and a rota to schedule—love can wait until after the pie."

Rosa shoves a pastry into your hand before you can protest. It is warm and buttery and tastes of flour and community.

Hal (calling): "Sensors look clean. Logged a good tide window overnight. If we keep this pace we can pitch the grant extension with real performance numbers."

Old Man Tor watches you and Eli for a moment longer, then nods as if to close a circle. The carved buoy is tucked into the sapling's stake as a talisman.

You breathe in deeply. The air carries the salt and the sweet of pastry and the faint mechanical scent of new wood. Around you, hands keep working; plans are no longer notes on a board but a dirt-smeared, concrete-real thing in the world. The first raised plank is not the end; it's the proof that beginnings can be built and maintained.

[Visual: The camera pulls back, showing the new boardwalk tracing a brighter line along the bay, mangrove spears dotting the mud, figures small but purposeful against the sky.]
[Music: Swells into an ascending, hopeful phrase, then softens into a warm sustained chord.]

You think of the months ahead — inspections, fund drives, nights of rain and of argument, and the small mercies of a community that will show up. You feel, for the first time in a long while, the burden of responsibility turned into a shared rope rather than a lone anchor. The future is not without storms, but it is occupied now by people who know how to reweave the town's fabric.

Page-Turn Moment:
You tuck the sensor reader into your jacket, the device warm against your ribs, and turn your face toward the Beacon where the Beacon's glass will reflect this morning's light. There are signatures to gather and a demo to give, and Marco's people will watch and take notes. But for now you let the warmth of the sun-slit plank sink into you. You can feel a line of action forming: maintenance schedules, micro-loans, public dashboards, and a quiet pact with Eli to keep building — together, plank by plank.
