You push through the plaza's patchwork of protest flyers and official notices, the paper edges still curled from last night's rain. The legal packet in your shoulder bag feels less like a weight and more like a promise—something with edges you can work around. You pause on the stairs, breathe the sharp, iodized air, and let the town settle itself into the shape you know: faces lined up, hands busy, an appetite for a solution that doesn't sell everyone short.

You step to the table. Marco stands across from you, the blazer still neat despite the damp; his smile is the kind that has closed deals and opened bank accounts. Eli is by your side, sleeves rolled, blueprint tube tucked under one arm like a second spine. Miriam's megaphone rests on the table, as if even friction will be mediated with sound. Hal sits slightly apart, hands folded, watchful. Rosa arrives a breath later, sandal straps slap-singing against wet pavement, carrying a thermos.

Marco: "Amara—" (he uses your full name in public; it fits the gravity) "we've ironed the legalese. Faster timeline. Full funding for the pilot seawall. It'll stand against the next projected storm surge."

You: "Faster doesn't mean safer unless oversight is enforceable." (You set the packet down and leave a clean impression on the table with your palm.) "We need maintenance funds, rehousing guarantees, and an independent trust. The town can't be left to chase receipts after the fact."

Marco: (tilts his head) "Independent how? You mean something that could slow this down. Philanthropy doesn't like to be micromanaged."

Eli: (interjects; his voice low, practical) "Not micromanaged — monitored. A maintenance endowment seeded at construction. Third-party auditors, community trustees with veto power on displacement measures. We can draft milestones tied to drawdowns."

Your chest warms; the word "milestones" is a hinge that can open many doors. You feel the room leaning on that hinge.

You: "We also need a rehousing budget baked into the contract. If businesses are elevated or relocated, that cost can't be offloaded to renters or the elderly who can't move on a week's notice."

Miriam: (nods sharply) "And recall mechanisms. If Marco's firm—if anyone—fails the oversight clauses, there must be swift remediation and community control, not protracted arbitration that favors capital."

Marco: (a careful exhale) "You drive a hard bargain. But I didn't come here to bulldoze Seabright. I can accept a monitored schedule and an independent trust, provided the timeline remains intact. We use an escrow system to fund maintenance. Rehousing provision could be part of the same escrow, disbursed on verified need."

Hal: (voice like gravel rolled smooth) "Make sure the escrow has teeth. And leave room for manual inspection. Models are only as good as the people who read them."

You: "And the trust needs representation—small-business owners, renters, engineers, elders. Not just a token seat. If those trustees can freeze draw payments they must be empowered with clear criteria."

Marco: (hesitates, then smiles) "Seats, sure. Criteria—help me understand how that would not grind progress to a halt."

You: "Simple. Clear triggers. Inspection checkpoints at defined intervals. Funds released only when maintenance benchmarks are demonstrated. A swift arbitration panel consisting of mutually agreed adjudicators for disputes." (You feel the words organizing themselves, the math of compromise clicking into place.)

Hal: "Set the inspection intervals long enough to capture meaningful data but frequent enough to catch problems early. Monthly for the pilot, quarterly thereafter, unless the sensors flag anomalies."

Marco: "Binding triggers."

Rosa: (hands you a mug) "Drink. You're doing important things, but you don't run on dry lungs."

You accept the mug. It smells like roasted coffee and lemon oil; the touch of the ceramic grounds you. You realize how often tangible comforts have been your tether through policy fights and noisy meetings.
