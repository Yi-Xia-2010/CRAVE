## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
Seabright is a mid-sized coastal town in 2035 reshaped by incremental adaptation and daily negotiation with the sea. The shoreline is a stitched landscape: patched sea walls and half-completed promenades mingle with mangrove saplings, rooftop gardens and desalination kiosks run by volunteer cooperatives. Local governance is a messy partnership of town councils, small engineering firms, grassroots organizers and charismatic private developers. Class lines run along who owns elevated storefronts and who scrambles for grants; religion and ritual survive in fishermen's morning prayers and memorials for storm victims. Politics revolve around tradeoffs: save heritage streets or fund a modern sea barrier; relocate families or incentivize redevelopment. Technology is modest, visible as tide sensors on poles, community-run drones that map erosion, and data-logged pilings; it augments, not replaces, human decisions. The culture is hands-on and conversational — town squares, cafe counters and the Community Climate Center (the Beacon) are where lives and policies collide. The geography alternates between battered boardwalks, the Old Harbor's narrow alleys, and the mud-carved intertidal flats being replanted with mangroves. Atmospherically, Seabright smells of salt and sun-warm wood, and looks painterly — storm-muted blues and rust-orange accents, weathered textures, and cinematic light after rain. The conflict is intimate as well as systemic: whether love and local ingenuity can steer a town toward resilient, equitable adaptation under economic and political pressure.

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I — Amara Vale — return to the town that made me into a scientist and a stubborn optimist. Across a common opening, I reconnect with Eli Navarro — wind-tousled curls and a brass compass hanging in his satchel — and confront Marco Voss, slick-backed and persuasive, who offers fast funding with heavy strings. My project at the Beacon aims for community-led adaptation: mangrove restoration, raised promenades, microgrids. The narrative follows a rising arc: each decision strengthens my influence, expands community buy-in, or refines technological methods; obstacles become chances to grow coalition power rather than collapses. Branch points force me to choose strategy: uncompromising grassroots mobilization, pragmatic engineering partnerships with Eli and sympathetic compromises, or risky acceptance of Marco’s resources under strict conditions. Secondary splits — whether to center displaced neighbours, accept hybrid funding, or prioritize an ambitious engineering prototype — determine the flavor of success: collective governance; a large-scale, partly-commercial modernization that preserves key neighborhoods; or a hybrid stewardship model combining public oversight and private capital. Through each path I deepen bonds with Eli (gentle, blue-gray eyes), lean on Rosa’s warm café, and heed Hal’s weathered counsel. Endings vary in texture but keep upward momentum: the town achieves stronger adaptation, mangroves take hold, policy shifts toward equity, and my relationship with Eli becomes a collaborative partnership — albeit in different maturities (romantic commitment, committed colleagues, or redefined trust). The story is intimate: love and policy weave together, showing that progress comes from hard choices, coalition-building, and willingness to learn.
