[Visual: The Pavilion's exterior glass reflects low, bruise-blue clouds; a shadowed human silhouette moves across the lobby.]

You left the Town Hall with the taste of the town still in your mouth — rain on tarpaulin, the creak of the mayor's chair, Ivy's hand on yours like an anchor. You promised a private conversation with Tideguard. You told yourself negotiation is stewardship in another language. The corridor to the Pavilion's meeting rooms is cool and impersonal; your palm leaves a salt-smear on the wood-veneer railing as you walk.

[Visual: Close-up of your compass pendant rubbing against your shirt as you move.]
[Sound: The clasp of a door closing behind you; the soft click of a smart-lock.]

Cassian opens the door himself. He is as exact as you remember: the blazer crisp, the quiff precise. But there is an unreadable softness at the corner of his mouth as he steps back and waits for you to enter. The room smells like polished wood and engineered greenery — that faint, manufactured chlorophyll that suggests life but not the kind you can teach your hands to rescue.

Cassian Rhee: "Thank you for coming, Marin. I know this wasn't an easy ask."

You step in and the city is filtered into a smaller, cleaner geometry: a long table, an observation window that frames a flat, distant harbor, a slim tablet glowing with charts on the far end. A row of potted succulents sits in military order beneath the glass; their soil looks too neat to be from Havenport.

Marin Sato: "I didn't come because it was easy."

Cassian gives a brief, almost apologetic nod. He gestures to a seat, then taps his tablet. The screen blooms with layered dashboards: projected sea-level curves, cost-benefit heatmaps, timelines that fold storms into neat boxes. Numbers march forward like a remedy.

Cassian Rhee: "What I'm proposing is rapid stabilization. Harbor barriers, built and deployed within three seasons. A Tideguard-administered trust will hold funding and maintenance. We expedite permits, handle procurement — you don't have to fight for the budget every fiscal year."

You listen. The sound of his voice is the sound of certainty: warm, corporate, practiced. The offer is a kind of rescue dressed in contract language.

[Visual: Cut to tablet: animated models of barriers folding into place; neighborhoods shaded where protections would be effective.]
[Sound: A soft electronic pulse as a timeline slides into place.]

Cassian Rhee: "In exchange, we ask for legal easements that allow us to anchor infrastructure, fast-track approvals during emergency windows, and a few symbolic concessions — access points rerouted, a commemorative plaza where the boathouse sits now, heritage markers integrated into the design. Protections first; negotiations about use later. It's scaffolding, Marin. Without it, we risk waiting until the tide forces us to move."

Marin Sato: "Symbolic concessions are cheap when people lose their homes."

Cassian's smile narrows, and something like impatience flickers under his calm.

Cassian Rhee: "I'm not proposing we erase Havenport. I'm offering a way to buy time — to prevent loss of life, to keep the fishing fleet from being swept away during the next major surge. We can structure the trust to include local oversight. We can do this together."

You feel the shape of the argument pressing on your ribs. Data with citizenship wrapped around it. If you say yes, the numbers will buy buffer. If you say no, there could be a storm that breaks everything. Your mind slides to other images: Elder Tomas bent over a hull, the boathouse's patched cedar, Ivy's neon vest hunched over seedlings in the nursery. Each image is edged with salt.

The minor choices are theater; none change the rhythm of the room. Cassian speaks with the confidence of someone who believes decisiveness is moral. He slides a document toward you. The paper is clinical: clauses about easements, definitions of 'public access' circumscribed with legal hedging, expedited permit pathways under an emergency ordinance.

Cassian Rhee: "There's a clause we can adjust. Community appointment to the trust isn't token — it's a seat with veto on non-urgent reallocations. I'm offering authority in practice, Marin. Let us start and we'll refine accountability together."

You taste something metallic at the back of your mouth — not fear exactly, but the tang of complicity. The document smells faintly of printer toner and the antiseptic tang of corporate air. You imagine the boathouse under those same fluorescent lights: timber flattened into precedent, a name on a plaque.

Marin Sato: "What about displacement? What about people who don't have the resources to accept relocation if the barriers require rerouting access?"

Cassian folds his hands, a Tetris of calm.

Cassian Rhee: "We'll offer relocation assistance, buyouts where necessary, prioritized grants for businesses displaced. We're not trying to hollow the town out. I'm trying to keep it standing."

You think of faces: the woman who sells boiled crab at the boardwalk; teenage kids who learned to reef-net from Elder Tomas. You think of Lina's reporting — questions sharp as flint — waiting for answers that are never purely numeric. You imagine headlines, livelihoods explained as necessary trade-offs. The room, with its engineered plants and strategic lighting, feels like the center of a tide that can be turned on or off at the stroke of a pen.

[Sound: Your phone buzzes again; a muted vibration on the table.]
[Visual: A small notification: Lina — 'Are you meeting them alone? There are discrepancies in their grants. We should verify the trust's clauses.'] 

Lina Park's message lands like a pebble in an already rippling pond. You remember her voice in the mayor's office earlier — sharp, demanding clarity. You feel her weight in the room even though she sits miles away with a camera bag at her feet.

Cassian Rhee: "I know Lina has concerns. Transparency is necessary and we can provide independent audits. We will publish contracts, allow town review panels, create channels for journalists. We need to act fast, Marin. Every delayed day increases exposure."

Marin Sato: "Fast for whom?"

Cassian pauses. His expression is, for a breath, complex. Not hostile. Not exactly tender. Unreadable — an engineer's empathy calibrated to results.

Cassian Rhee: "For everyone, if you accept the math. For those on the water in a surge, for families in low-lying blocks. The reality is uneven, Marin. I don't like the compromises any more than you do, but I can move money and material quickly. I can keep people from drowning."

Your chest tightens. You can hear the town's memory in the scrape of your boots against the Pavilion's floor: the day the harbor rose in the back of your mind, Elder Tomas shouting for lines, your own hands pulling tarpaulin like prayer. You can also hear Cassian's confidence, the hum of orders fulfilled.

Cassian leans back and for a moment the lights in the window catch his glasses. He speaks softer, the lobby of corporate certainty thinning into a human plea.

Cassian Rhee: "Tell me what you need to be able to present this to the town. Don't say no because you don't see the guarantees. Tell me what guarantees you want, Marin. I can build them in."

Marin Sato: "Guarantees that don't depend on goodwill. Legal safeguards. Binding community oversight. Protections for cultural sites — not just plaques. Real resources for people displaced. Audits that Lina can trust. I want the boathouse preserved in situ or an equally accessible community-managed place where the boats can still launch."

Cassian's hand drifts to his fountain pen; a small, human gesture in an otherwise engineered choreography. He taps the pen against the table, a soft punctuation.

Cassian Rhee: "We can negotiate those clauses. We can schedule community liaisons, tangible protections. But I need to know you will help shepherd this through. If you publicly block the process, funding could be rerouted elsewhere and we'll lose a chance to secure the harbor."

You feel the word "shepherd" like a rod across your back. Help shepherd this through. You think of Ivy's neon vest and the nursery seedlings; you think of Ariana's pilot and how fragile a living seawall is in a season of storms. You feel Lina's skepticism like a third eye watching. The room tastes of choices you are not yet equipped to make.

[Sound: Rain picks up against the glass in a thin, steady whisper.]
[Visual: Outside, harbor lights blur in watery streaks; a single small boat tugs at its mooring.]

Cassian Rhee: "We can start with pilot protections in critical zones and simultaneously work on trust governance. Time is the enemy here, Marin. But I need a partner in the mayor's office who buys into moving quickly."

Marin Sato: "If we rush and something breaks — physically or socially — who takes responsibility then?"

Cassian Rhee: "Then we fix it. The alternative is waiting for a disaster to force everyone's hand. You don't want that."

Silence gathers between you. The tablet's glow retreats. The engineered plants throw faint shadows across the table. Your thumb brushes a scuff on the compass hanging at your throat, and the metal is cool.

You imagine two pictures at once: a harbor softened by living walls and community docks still crowded with boats; and the same harbor, rigid and protected, but with access narrowed, with people boxed into new lines on a map. Both pictures are true in the light Cassian shows you. Both pictures leave something out.

Marin Sato: (soft, almost to yourself) "What will I be trading, Cassian?"

Cassian Rhee: (after a careful breath) "Possible autonomy for immediate safety. A seat at a table that can build protections now. Less ideal, maybe—more lives kept where they are."

You look at the document again. There's a pen balanced on an envelope at the center of the table: neat, authoritative. Your mouth opens. Your hand moves without fully deciding; the hinge of the moment creaks like a pulley.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand hovering over the pen; the harbor's reflection shivers in the window behind Cassian.]
[Sound: The rain grows louder, a thin drum that makes your heartbeat feel too loud.]

You can feel the rift inside you: the part that wants the town safe now, and the part that cannot stomach the legal language that feels like erasure.

You taste the antiseptic of compromise. You feel culpability's first tug, small and patient, like a tidal pull.

You inhale. The room waits.

[Music: A low, unresolved chord sustains, drawing out the last breath of the scene.]
