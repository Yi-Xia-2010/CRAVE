[Scene: The Old Boathouse | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: The boathouse door yawns open to a bruise-light harbor; a single lamp hangs over a low table, throwing a honeyed circle across salt-dark wood.]
[Sound: Rain, distant and patient, begins to stitch the roof; the harbor bell tolls once, low and steady.]
[Music: Sparse cello, slow, a minor chord that settles like silt.]

You set the compact on the table and feel the lamp's pool of light swallow its edges. The paper is heavier than you expected—legal blocks of language that claim geography the way tides claim sand. Your fingers leave damp crescents where they touch the margin. Cassian's handwriting runs like a tide-line across the margins: precise loops, underlines at clause numbers, initials in a corner. Even his corrections look tidy, the sort of neatness that would comfort accountants and unsettle sailors.

You press the brass compass against your sternum until the metal is warm. The scuffs glitter like old wounds. It does not tell you what to do; only that you have a bearing you can trace.

Ivy stands opposite you, the neon of her safety vest dulled by shadow. She reads, halting, like someone translating a text she loves into a language she doesn't yet trust.

Ivy Morales: "Easements for barrier maintenance shall be granted to Tideguard for the lengths specified in Schedule A—" (her mouth tightens) "—with temporary relocation assistance to be provided, contingent on municipal determination of displacement risk."

You hear the words as if through wet glass. Each clause is a rib: it holds something but could cage something else.

Elder Tomas taps his carved walking stick—marine motifs worn smooth by years. The sound is dry, contempt like old driftwood.

Elder Tomas: "Paper," he says, voice like grains scraping, "doesn't remember salt."

He lets the stick rest on the map spread beneath the compact. The map's ink is smudged with the memory of storms, little notations of family nets and boathouse plots. The lamp makes the ink look fragile as bone.

Lina leans over the documents, the press satchel slung back. Her fingers flex as if itching to type.

Lina Park: "People need to know what's on the table. Full text, raw. Let them see—let them decide."

Her tone is sharp with certainty. You know she means it: transparency as weapon, exposure as democracy.

Ariana is by the window, hands jammed into the pockets of her raincoat. Her camera dangles at her hip, the strap catching a droplet of light. She watches you more than she watches the paper.

Ariana Voss: (quiet) "If we leak this now, investors will pull. The funding window is thin. We lose shields, possibly everything for some neighborhoods."

Interrupting, Cassian's voice folds into the room—calm, measured. He stands near the doorway, wet at the cuffs, an unreadable expression shadowing his face.

Cassian Rhee: "The protections are immediate. The easements ensure maintenance and reduce long-term cost overruns. There will be oversight panels." (he inclines his head toward you) "You can insist on advisory seats."

You want to believe him. His words are logical bridges. Your chest wants the harbor saved as soon as possible.

You inhale the smell of tar and lamp wax. The paper crinkles faintly under your palm. Your throat tightens the way it does before you speak up at meetings, before you stake a claim.

Ivy's voice wavers when she reads the relocation provision again, this time with your correction lodged between the commas.

Ivy Morales: "—'contingent on municipal determination' — which, in practice, means 'conditional.' It means the town decides who moves, when. It could be used to delay aid."

She looks at you with a hurt mouth, as if each syllable is a small betrayal of an old promise.

You lean forward. Your palms leave salt-smudged ovals on the compact. You imagine the families who fold their boats into winter and the children who slept with the boathouse lamp on as an anchor. The thought of them moving while barricades rise like teeth makes your hands cold.

Marin Sato: (soft, to the room) "If we sign this as-is, we're giving legal permission for parts of our coastline to be repurposed without a binding guarantee of who can live there afterward."

Cassian tilts his head, the picture of inevitability.

Cassian Rhee: "We can't gloss over the fact that inaction means more loss. If Tideguard withdraws, the protective window could close. This is time-sensitive."

Ariana steps forward, voice threaded with urgency.

Ariana Voss: "But a contract without transparent, enforceable oversight is a blank cheque. We can demand community chairs on any oversight board. We can write audit triggers for breaches."

Cassian exhales. The room tastes like old paper and something ironish—fear, perhaps.

Cassian Rhee: "Named chairs are advisory at best. Investors balk at stipulations that give operational vetoes to non-experts. You know that."

A pause: the rain, insistently patient, gathers in the gutters.

Lina Park: "Then let them balk in public. If Tideguard is serious about protecting people, they should be willing to be recorded promising it. If they're not—if they're hiding the trade-offs—people deserve to know."

Elder Tomas: (dry) "Recording promises in a ledger does not keep nets from rotting. It does not keep the sea from coming in. It only places blame on paper."

Your internal ledger—your hands, your years—fills with columns you cannot balance. You think of the compass again, how it points but does not move. How you have been the person people look to for answers they don't yet have words for. The weight of that expectation makes your fingers tremble.

You look at each face again—Cassian unreadable, Ivy exposed, Lina tight with conviction, Ariana steadied at the window, Tomas with his stick like a punctuation mark. They are your town, not abstractions. Your decisions will not be undone by other meetings. They will be lived.

Marin Sato: (quiet, to Cassian) "If we agree to oversight, it must be legally binding. Not advisory. Not 'we'll try.' Binding. Auditable. Timelines. Enforcement. Community veto points on relocations."

Cassian studies your face, then the compact. For a fraction of a second you see calculation and something like regret cross him.

Cassian Rhee: "A legal framework can be negotiated. But certain clauses—operational control—will make investors nervous. We can draft oversight terms, but there will be pushback."

Ariana steps in before you can answer, words pressed like bandages.

Ariana Voss: "We can offer a phased approach: immediate protection for critical stretches contingent on a binding oversight clause to be approved within X months, subject to a public referendum. If Tideguard walks, we risk nothing immediate—"

Lina cuts in, sharper than the rain.

Lina Park: "—If Tideguard walks because we went public, the town stays exposed. That's the calculus."

Tomas makes a small, exasperated noise.

Elder Tomas: "All that paper will be left here. The sea won't read it."

The lamp trembles as a gust sweeps through the open door. Light quivers across the compact.

You can feel the town leaning into you. Not in miracles, not in speeches, but in the slow, grainy trust that you will count its worth. Your hands curl around the compass until the metal stamps into your palm.

Your mind runs through the three paths like tide charts: sign and secure immediate protections; insist on binders and guardrails and possibly lose the deal; or expose the terms and force the town to decide in public—civil transparency, public risk.

Marin Sato: (to everyone, voice steadier than you feel) "We are choosing how to armor what we love. Not all armor is the same. Some keeps us safe and keeps our hands free. Some keeps us safe and locks our hands out. And some—" (you touch the compact) "—some promises look gorgeous on paper and feel like a tomb when they close."

Cassian's unreadable face shifts—very slightly—toward the light. Ariana's blue streaked hair glows as she bites her lip. Ivy's fingers hover where she last turned the page. Lina's pen is already uncapped, a threat and a promise.

The rain presses its slow hand against the boathouse roof; the harbor outside draws its breath.

[Music: A single dissonant piano chord lingers, unresolved.]

You close your eyes and let the sounds of the room—worn wood, rain, breathing—compose themselves into one question that has nowhere to hide: which stone do you set, and for whom?
