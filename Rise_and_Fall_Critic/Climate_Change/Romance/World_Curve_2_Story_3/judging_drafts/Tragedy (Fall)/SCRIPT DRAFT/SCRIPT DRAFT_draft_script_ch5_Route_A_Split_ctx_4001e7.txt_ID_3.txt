[Scene: Old Boathouse | Late Night]
[Visual: The boathouse's single lamp casts a honeyed circle; nets and hand-lettered maps hang in the shadows like slow witnesses.]
[Sound: Rain hammers the corrugated roof; far-off hum of press vans and a distant police radio pulse through the night.]
[Music: Low, brooding cello; a wind motif threads in and out.]

You wake to the lamp's small warmth and the town's noise seeping in through cracks in the old wood. Outside, the boardwalk is a loose thing — patched planks and sandbags — but tonight it sounds like something larger: a crowd, a machine, the close parts of an argument. You can taste salt on your teeth even inside the boathouse, a memory of tide-lines and the places water keeps reaching for.

Elder Tomas sits at the center table, the paper unfolded before him like a small, stubborn map. His hands are the slow, certain tools of someone who has mended more than wood in his life. He reads aloud, voice caught between measured air and a thin tremor.

Elder Tomas: "—'Due to identified structural hazards, sections of the Havenport Boardwalk will be closed for immediate safety retrofits, financed by Tideguard. Access will be contingent on municipal accession to the proposed stabilization agreement.'"

The words land hard. The lamp seems to lean away from the sentence. You hear the rasp of tape as Ivy moves. She has a roll in hand and the raw edge of anger in her every breath.

Ivy: (clipping a path of taped signs) "They call it 'safety' and bring us the bill. They say 'stabilize' and mean 'lock out.' Who signs away the path to the sea and doesn't expect someone to fight?"

Ivy's hands fly, rearranging the hand-lettered signs you used at the demo earlier. Her movements are small acts of reclamation — re-sticking, re-placing, as if she can set the meaning of the words by angle alone.

[Visual: Close-up on your brass compass at your throat; your fingers find its rim, scuffed and familiar.]
[Sound: A camera shutter clicks in the distance; Lina's laptop hums.]

You can feel everyone's eyes on you, but they're not only looking. They're weighing you like a ledger, adding up everything you've already carried. Your shirt clings to the damp of the day; your sleeves are rolled, the skin at your wrists raw with tar and salt. The compass edge presses into your sternum, a small, private accusation: choose a direction.

Lina leans forward, voice quick and sharp.

Lina: "We need a narrative, Marin. If Tideguard frames this as emergency safety, most people will accept the closure as inevitable. We flip the frame—community governance, community-led retrofit—and we can force a pause."

You want to agree. You want to say yes and mean it so hard that the words hold back the tide. But even the best frame can be drowned by money and lawyers.

Ariana stands near the doorway, tablet tucked under her arm, light from the screen painting her face in cool tones. She doesn't look like an outsider tonight; she looks like someone who has been staying awake drafting plans while the town sleeps.

Ariana: (careful) "I can accelerate the living-seawall pilot. If we deploy on our timeline, anchor the kelp nurseries and the modular reefs near the sections slated for retrofit, we can show a working alternative. It buys us leverage."

She says it with that unshakable optimism you know — a bright cord she can throw across a churned sea. But you also watch her hands when she talks, fingers worrying the tablet's corner. There is a weight behind the offer that isn't all hope.

Ivy snaps a look at Ariana, hot and immediate.

Ivy: "Or we let the pilot be used as proof of incompetence if it fails. Tideguard will celebrate the failure and roll their barriers in."

Ariana: (voice tight) "And if it succeeds, Ivy? If it scales and people keep access, don't you want that too?"

The two of them hold the room's temperature between them — sudden, flashing, a fragile bridge. You watch the bridge tremble. You think of the boathouse's beams and the nets that hold family histories in their loops.

Elder Tomas folds the letter back into itself and tucks it into his coat like a map kept for an emergency.

Elder Tomas: "They'll put up signs with polite words. Then they'll bring crews with machines. Then they'll ask us quietly to accept compensation for 'relocation assistance.' We know the pattern. My hands can build a boat; they cannot buy this town back once it's sold."

There's a pause — the rain and the distant press a background chorus. You feel the words tighten at the back of your throat, the iron taste rising. Your chest is tight: fear that is more than adrenaline; it feels like grief already happening.

You step toward the table because it feels like the right place to anchor yourself.

Marin Sato: "We don't get to protect everything. Not right away. But we decide how to lose it. We decide what gets fought for and what we sacrifice."

Lina: (soft, a different kind of sharp) "People will blame leadership for indecision. They will blame you for making big calls alone. We need a plan, Marin—now."

The room presses. Ivy stomps a foot, angry at the geometry of power and the slowness of governance.

Ivy: "So what — blockade them at dawn? Sit in front of bulldozers with our bodies? Get arrested and make a show of it?"

You think of the feeds, the frames, the way Lina's articles can turn one image into public pressure — or turn a failed stunt into a cautionary tale that benefits Tideguard. Every option tastes like compromise or danger.

Ariana steps closer to you, eyes unreadable for the moment — she doesn't smile; she doesn't plead. She offers data and urgency.

Ariana: "If the pilot is ready, I can fast-track deployment. I know it's risky. I know we haven't run it full-scale. But if we show an effective biological buffer in the exact spots they're planning to retrofit, we change the calculus."

You want to ask her what that would mean for her — career, funding, liability — but the boathouse seems to shrink when you do. Questions fold into one another. You sense Cassian's name in the margins of every bureaucratic sentence, Tideguard's machines lined like teeth at the edge of the town.

The moment you choose your tone, responses ripple. Ivy nods harder at the personal truth; she tilts her head, less inclined to tactical debate and more to the visceral. Lina scribbles notes faster when you lay out cold facts; she smells a headline. Ariana watches you with an unreadable face that somehow reads like both hope and calculation.

Ivy: (quiet now) "You promised us you'd try, Marin. That was enough at first. Now we need something more than trying."

Marin Sato: "I know. But I can't drag everyone into a chaotic rush that might hand Tideguard the exact leverage they want."

Elder Tomas: "We need to be strategic, but not so clever that we forget the heart of why we're here. You remember the old markers by the south jetty? My son's name carved on that post? Those things are not negotiable to some of us."

You feel the room press toward a single hinge: escalate and risk the law and the cameras; accept a negotiated safeguard and let parts of the town go; or reach privately for a technical fix with Ariana and hope the pilot holds when scaled.

Lina closes her laptop gently, the click a small punctuation.

Lina: "Whatever we do, we do it together. Not a press stunt. Not a secret. If we're visible and unified, Tideguard can't frame us as a fringe of troublemakers."

Ariana: (after a long breath) "If you ask me to accelerate, I will — but I need explicit permission to make calls for extra hands, to reallocate some of my funding. That means going ahead of the community schedule."

You look at her. You feel something like heat and cold at once — the warmth of someone's faith in action, the chill of bypassing the slow processes that keep people safe.

The lamp seems suddenly too small for every possibility.

Your chest tightens with the knowledge that every choice will wound someone. You can almost hear the boardwalk creak under the weight of all the decisions you haven't made yet.

You press your palms to the table, feeling the rough grain and an old gouge where someone's knife once caught the wood. The compass sits under your fingers, cold and honest.

Marin Sato: "We have three real paths tonight. None are clean. None spare people entirely."

The others breathe in, an agreed silence that becomes the room's new map.

Ivy: (low) "Then let's at least name them, so we stop pretending the right thing is obvious."

You nod because leadership sometimes looks like naming what you fear. Your voice is steadier than you feel.

Marin Sato: "We blockade the crews at dawn, whatever the legal cost. Or I accept a negotiated plan that secures the harbor but cedes the boathouse area. Or I ask Ariana to accelerate the pilot and deploy on our timeline."

A weight drops into the room. Outside, a police car idles and the rain keeps writing the same sentence on the roof.

[Music: The cello drops to a single, reverberating note; the wind motif becomes a quiet, insistent pulse.]

You can imagine each outcome's immediate shape: bruises and headlines; chains of easements and lost access; a hurried experiment that succeeds and scales—or fails and hands Tideguard the victory. You taste iron fear at the back of your throat and understand, with a clarity that is almost a cruelty, that love and duty will collide here and someone will pay.
