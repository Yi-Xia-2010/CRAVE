Cassian Rhee: (softening, almost off-the-record) "You did right by most of them, Marin."

You walk among the rows. Families lift canvas sacks that smell of tar and fish and home; an old man folds a net with hands that remember knots your fingers learned as a child. People move with a quiet resignation that is neither panic nor peace — a long-exhaled acceptance.

Woman's voice: (small, stranded) "When will we be able to come back?"

Ivy: (kneeling, sorting labels) "We'll work on transition support. Training, placement, storage... and the memorial — we'll keep that promise."

A boy hands you a carved oar, polished and small, as if handing over a name. You hold it and how the wood fits your palms like a memory. In the huddle of lantern light, someone cries, softly; someone else hums an old working song. The town stitches its wound with organized hands.

Cassian Rhee: (earnest, trying) "This was the only way to secure funding at scale. I know it's not perfect."

Marin Sato: (quiet, controlled) "It wasn't your fault we faced a problem like this, Cassian. It wasn't mine either. We just... we had different ways of keeping the town."

[Scene: New Promenade — Midnight]
[Visual: The promenade now quiet, the plaques in place; the first stone has Tomas' prayer carved beside a small brass plate with boat names. The moon throws silver across the planted terraces.]
[Music: A single piano motif, small and precise; wind sounds softened]

You walk alone, the town sleeping around the edges. The plaques read like an index of what the town has been asked to carry. Names of boats. Names of families. A carved date. A phrase — "For those who taught us the sea" — that someone wrote with trembling care.

A shadow falls beside you; Ariana stands with a travel mug, steam mingling with the cold air. She looks at the plaques without touching them; her breath forms small clouds.

Ariana Voss: (soft, earnest) "You did what you thought would keep people safe."

You want to say something fierce in defense of your choice, but fury feels cheap in the face of so many cardboard boxes and the sound of nets being folded. Instead you say the truth.

Marin Sato: "I kept some people safe, Ariana. I lost others."

Ariana Voss: (eyes tracking the plaques) "We kept the harbor and the jobs and —" (she swallows) "— some of the heritage. Not all. Not the boathouse."

She reaches toward you, then falters; the space between your fingers is a map of what remains and what is gone. Her fingers find yours and then slip, like a hesitant tide testing the shore.

Her touch is brief. It sends a small, honest charge through you — comfort braided with the ache that sits under your ribs. It is not the clean, whole warmth of triumph. It is nearer to a shared wound that both of you stare at without pretending it isn't there.

Ariana Voss: (quietly) "There are programs to retrain fishers for shore-side work. Lina will tell their stories properly. Ivy will make sure the moves are humane. Tomas will keep the names."

You watch families pack nets and slip into the waiting vans. Faces are soft; resignation has a tenderness to it. Children peer back at the bay as if committing it to memory. Elder Tomas stands at the end of an old lane and says a wordless blessing that seems to travel farther than the wind.

The promenade holds them now — a public face of healing: terraces, plaques, funded jobs, memorial stones. The town is alive in new configurations, safer in many senses. But every map you carry in your head — of childhood runs, of where the best cod were caught, of the wooden boards that creaked when a storm lifted the town's breath — is redrawn with ink you did not author.

You feel the compass against your chest, heavy and honest.

Marin Sato: "I did what I thought would keep people alive."

You listen to the sea answer, not with approval nor condemnation, but with the weathered sound of continuity. It will keep coming, and you will keep meeting it with what tools you have, with ceremonies and funds and stories, with a promenade that remembers and stones that hold names. Love and duty remain braided; the stitching is imperfect.

You turn away from the plaques finally, the night pressing cold behind your collar, and walk toward the boathouse lane one last time. The outline of the old structure is a silhouette against the moon, fenced now, to be reclaimed by policy and plan. You stand at the gate and imagine, for a moment, other paths — blockades at dawn, a different kind of collective defiance — but the choice has already been made.

A stray gull cries, sharp and ordinary as breath.

You think of promises: the memorial, the jobs, the restored terraces, Tomas' blessing. You think of the people who will keep fishing in new ways and the ones who will leave the sea and find other work. You think of Ariana's hand, the fray and the comfort. You think of the compass, of the ache under your ribs that will not fully leave.

The town sleeps, the town heals, and the town mourns. Everything continues.

You close the gate gently, fingers lingering on the cold metal.
