[Scene: Greenwave Pilot Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of floating planters tethered in staggered rows; kelp fronds spill like green lace across buoyant edges. Sun filtered through a thinning cloud bank, salt glittering on wet surfaces.]
[Sound: Low surf, gulls arguing over a drifting crumb of seaweed; the creak of wet ropes.]

You crouch at the edge of the pilot raft, palms braced on salt-sanded timber. The kelp smells of iodine and old rain—clean and alive—and the tiny bodies of newly hatched larvae pulse like promise in the water around the planters. For a breath, the scene is exactly what you'd been pointing towards for months: refuge where there had been loss, a small ecology knitting itself back together.

Then you remember the footage.

A thin, clinical knot of anxiety tightens behind your sternum. The video plays in your head the way the town has already started to play it: a sudden gust, a line of planters snatching at their moorings, with two snapping free and spinning under an angry sky. The upload that turned this intimacy into a question was only minutes old when Lina's message pinged: "It's trending. People are asking why we didn't foresee this."

You run a hand over the scuff of your compass at your throat. The brass is warm; the scratch near the hinge is a small, stubborn memory of other times you thought you could hold things steady. You don't find answers there—only the same direction you always try to acknowledge: forward, but careful.

Ivy comes up behind you, boots slapping wet planks. Her safety vest is muddied, braid stuck to the back of her neck. She looks at the kelp, then at the torn moorings, then at you, and that expression—half furious, half frightened—says everything she won't say aloud.

Ivy Morales: "We got lucky nobody was on those planters. Could've been worse." (She swallows.) "But worse is what people are imagining."

You nod. The wind takes the nod and carries it away.

[Scene: Greenwave Pilot Site — Dockside | Moments Later]
[Visual: Close-up on a tablet screen. The video feed shows a kelp thicket, then cuts to a shaky clip of planters drifting; viewer comments cascade down in translucent overlays.]
[Sound: The faint electronic chime of another alert; a distant motor starting.]

Lina's voice is in your ear on speaker, thin with the adrenaline of someone already drafting headlines.

Lina Park: "It's clean—except for the clip. They won't see the juvenile beds, only the planters rolling. People will ask who signed off on this."

You: "We need to control the frame, Lina. Show the habitat, the data. But we can't lie about the snaps."

Lina Park: (a breath) "I won't lie. But I can't make the town not watch that one angle."

Ariana appears at the dock's edge, hair still damp, a blue streak plastered to her temple. You see the damp light catch there, eyes blown wide and raw, like a camera lens overexposed. Her optimism—your shared engine—has a thin, cracked seam along the top. She meets your eyes, and for a moment everything goes quiet between you.

Ariana Voss: (soft) "We were too proud of how quickly things were coming together. I thought—" (She stops, the sentence collapsing.) "I thought we had time to iterate before scaling."

You: "You did the design work. You pushed for biological realism over brute force. That mattered."

Ariana Voss: (friction) "I did the design work, and the tethers were supposed to be tested for that gust. If we missed something, Marin—" (Her voice breaks and she looks away.) "If we missed something, it's on me."

You feel the compass at your throat like a weight. You can see the headline possibilities spinning: 'Community Pilot Fails' or 'Living Seawall Promises Reborn.' Neither is only true.

Ivy: (brisk, protective) "Whatever the headline, we patch the clamps, reinforce moorings, and get forensic data. Quick, technical triage. Then a statement. We fix the hardware. We can handle the PR."

Lina: "If Tideguard smells hesitation, they'll withdraw the funding wedge before we can even breathe. Cassian's people are already calling for an emergency meeting."

You answer before you fully weigh the phrasing: "Then we'll meet them on facts. Bring the data, the failure modes, the mitigation plan. If we show competence, we keep control."

Ariadna's face shifts—something like hope, something like fear making a bargain with your answer. Her fingers curl around the strap of her camera as if it's an anchor.

[Sound: Your phone vibrates with a new notification: a Tideguard rep has requested a conference call.]

You inhale, the air thin and electric.

[Scene: Coastal Research Hub — Briefing Room | Early Evening]
[Visual: Cool white panels throw clinical light across a long table. Touchscreens hum; a wall screen displays the viral clip on loop in one quadrant, graphs and tidal models in another.]
[Sound: Low hum of the HVAC, the occasional keyboard click; outside, the sea mutters against seawalls.]

The room smells faintly of ozone and coffee gone cold. You set the brass compass on the table and push it, not all the way, but enough that its scuff faces Lina. The gesture is small and public—an invisible plea for steadiness.

Cassian Rhee appears on the wall screen, his features composed, a careful smile rehearsed to fit network lines. He sits in a space that says 'stability'—glass, brushed metal, a prototype scale model gleaming behind him.

Cassian Rhee: "I watched the clip. Incident reports are unfortunate, but resolve is what separates towns that drown from towns that flourish. Tideguard is willing to move up our conditional offer—if you're ready to fast-track."

You study the model on his desk, imagining the harbor with an engineered rim. There is a practical balm in the idea: immediate funds, rapid construction, a lowered risk of overt flooding. But there is also a taste you can't swallow—a flattening of the town's voice in exchange for a promise of protection.

You: "Conditional how? What oversight? What community input?"

Cassian Rhee: (smooth) "Standard contractual milestones, Marin. We provide capital and engineering teams. You pilot scale, we manage construction. We consult with the community on site placement. Time is the variable here."

Ariana, standing across from you, interjects before you can build the question into a polite test.

Ariana Voss: "We cannot hand over implementation to an external vendor without mutual governance. The living element—the biology—we developed with locals to restore access and livelihood, not to be subsumed under a barrier that keeps people out." (Her voice is a little raw.) "If Tideguard strips the community out of the process, you can put steel where kelp should be and call it resilience."

Cassian Rhee: (leans forward, unruffled) "No one wants to strip the community out. But 'mutual governance' can be slow. Storms don't wait for consent."

Ivy: (hard) "Consent isn't a checkbox. It's how the boathouse survived other storms—by people knowing why, not having someone tell them why."

Lina: (quiet, pragmatic) "Public perception matters. If we look like we traded the town for money, the story becomes one of betrayal. But if we look incompetent, we lose credibility to anyone who might help."

You feel the room compress around your ribs. Each syllable is a weighing of values. Each argument is a small erosion on the shore of what you've been asking people to trust you for.

[Scene: Coastal Research Hub — The Tension Tightens | Night]
[Visual: The wall screen shrinks. The lab light feels colder as evening pools outside the windows. Reflections of graphs cross your face like weather maps.]
[Music: Sparse piano, a low, descending motif; strings quiver like pending rain.]

You think of the kelp pulling water through its blades, of larvae finding shelter, of the kids who touch the pilot's planters and shout something amazed. You think of Ivy sanding a repaired clamp, of Lina drafting a story that could either frame the town as resilient or as reckless. You think, too, of Ariana—hair glinting wet, hope cracked but still fluttering like a small bird that refuses to die.

Ariana steps close enough that you can smell the salt in her hair, the faint oil of her camera strap. Her voice is small, intimate—not theatrical for the room.

Ariana Voss: "If we scale now because the money is there, we'll get more coverage, maybe faster gains for the habitat. But we might not take the time to translate the designs into something the harbor can maintain. We might build something our own people can't keep running."

You: "If we stall for consent and trials, we risk Tideguard pulling their offer. The funds evaporate, and the pilot becomes a story about good intentions without capital."

Ivy: (sharp) "And if we publish everything and pause—"

Lina: (finishing) "—we force accountability. We lose momentum publicly, but we gain credibility with anyone watching the methods."

A silence like pre-storm settles. No one in the room moves first. The viral clip still pulses in the corner of the wall screen, a looped accusation that will not stop until someone answers.

Your palm finds the brass compass and squeezes. You are not certain that any of the paths will spare the things you are trying to protect. You are certain only that each path will cost something—trust, time, resources, intimacy.

You look at Ariana. Her face is a map of the pilots: the bright lines where plans succeeded, the dark seams where they didn't. Her expression is complex—encouragement braided with accusation; hope braided with fear; it is, precisely, not a single note you can tune your answer to.

You let the compass sit heavy in your hand and name the choices aloud, because names make consequences feel less like ghosts and more like decisions.

You: "We have three paths. I can push to scale now and accept Tideguard's conditional funding and fast-track the build. I can insist on a slower, community-first consent and extended trials. Or I can release all our data, pause the pilot, and force a public reckoning."

Ariana's breath catches. Ivy's jaw sets. Lina's fingers hover over a keyboard. Cassian watches with the patience of someone who knows how negotiations sharpen into leverage.

The room leans toward you, not with expectation but with the hunger of a town that has watched too many small mistakes become disasters. The weight of that hunger presses into your chest.

You steady yourself. The compass scratches a tiny circle on the table.
