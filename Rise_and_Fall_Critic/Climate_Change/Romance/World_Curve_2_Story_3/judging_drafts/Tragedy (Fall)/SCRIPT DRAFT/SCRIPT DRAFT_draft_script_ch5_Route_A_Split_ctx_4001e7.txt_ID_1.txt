[Scene: Old Boathouse | Night]
[Visual: The boathouse exterior under a low, rain-slicked moon; sand clings to the lower boards. A single lamp glows through the cracked window.]
[Sound: Distant press vans idling; the low drone of a generator; rain tapping the corrugated roof like a nervous hand.]
[Music: Sparse piano over a low, breathy cello — a slow, descending motif.]

You wake to the press vans humming like an ache under the town's skin. Word slides between people faster than the tide: a formal notice from Tideguard. Sections of the boardwalk are to be closed for "safety retrofits" unless Havenport signs off. The words in the letter are polite and final; the paper smells faintly of printer ink and salt. Elder Tomas unfolds it with hands that have built more than notices — hands that remember the map of every plank in the harbor.

Elder Tomas: "They're calling it safety. They forget we've been safe here for generations."

He reads aloud, each sentence thick with County-ese, and you watch the lines in his face deepen like gull-scratched wood. The light from the lamp pools gold on the hand-lettered maps tacked to the wall — neighborhoods sketched in ink, boathouse marked with a small, looping heart you don't remember drawing but now recognize as someone's claim.

You sit on the crate where tools were last left, sleeves rolled, the brass of your compass warm against your sternum. Your hair is damp with sweat from the day's hammering and the worry that won't let the fevered work stop. Ivy snaps a roll of bright duct tape and begins rearranging signs — "NO RETROFIT ACCESS," "HAVENPORT LISTENS," letters crooked with haste. Her movements are a staccato punctuation to the quiet reading of the notice.

Ivy Morales: (sharp) "They can't just fence us out. Not without a fight."

You can hear the rawness in her voice, like cordage fraying. She sticks a sign above the boathouse door and secures it with a practiced hand, but her jaw doesn't unclench.

Lina Park is there too, the lamp catching her glasses as she scrolls through her tablet. Her fingers hesitate over the recorder app, then press — professional habit, the town's story a story she can't ever let go. Her eyes flick to you, searching for the shape of what you'll do next.

Lina Park: "I've got police blotters open. I've got a copy of the notice on my feed. If we put out a press release that frames this as a takeover, they'll have to answer publicly."

You watch her pace words like tools. She knows how to shape a narrative; you know how quickly those shapes can be used against you. Outside, a van telegraph flashes; someone has already posted the notice on a community board. Your stomach tightens — the town is awake and it is hungry.

Tomas sets the letter back on the crate as if setting a small body down. He looks at you with that slow, patient gravity of the old who have watched storms come and not called them by new names.

Elder Tomas: "Marin… whatever you choose, remember we lose more than wood. Memory goes with the shoreline."

You're aware of how that sounds — sentimental, maybe — but you feel it like a bruise. The boathouse is a knot of memories: maps with salt-scrawl, jars of nails, a lamp that always smells faintly of oil and tea. To lose access isn't only to lose a building; it's to lose a place where your hands learned how to keep things afloat.

Ivy: (bitter laugh) "They'll call this progress and hand over jobs. Fences are good for headlines. For living, not so much."

Her anger is not theatrical; it's granular. She fishes out a strip of tape, eyes bright with the kind of energy that can set barricades or burn bridges. She fixes another sign, then looks to you, searching for confirmation not because she doubts you, but because she needs the shape of the plan.

You inhale — a cold, salt-brined breath that tastes faintly of iron now. The legal implications ripple under every choice. You think of injunctions, of officers' boots on wet planks, of press vans framing struggling residents against steel fences. You think of the chant you led last week and the thinness of bodies in a crowd against corporate-backed contractors.

Your phone vibrates; a single name flashes. Ariana. Your thumb hovers before you open it. The history between you and Ariana could tilt the moment, but this isn't the time to untangle pasts. If you call her and ask for a fast-tracked pilot, you could buy time — or hand Tideguard an advantage if the pilot collapses. If you refuse to involve her, you might protect her from legal exposure, but you also risk losing the one scalable solution the town has that feels like care instead of containment.

Ariana Voss: (soft, unreadable) "I'm here. Tell me what you need."

Her voice over the line—if that's where she is—carries a complicated light: hope braided with impatience. It reads as both plea and permission: help me help you. But her tone could mean she's ready to go all-in, or that she is bracing for you to ask something you can't take back.

Lina: "If you speed the pilot without community sign-off and it fails, Tideguard will use that as proof we can't manage this. They'll offer a 'solution' and we'll be boxed into a corner."

Cassian's name hangs in the room like a shadow from the pavilion on the skyline. You don't have to see him to feel the geometry of his influence — polished edges, the hum of investment, the careful ledgering of trade-offs. The notice is already a foothold in the narrative he wants the town to accept. Your mouth tastes like rust.

Ivy moves closer, voice lowering until it's almost a sanctified hiss.

Ivy: "We can put our bodies on the line. Dawn, a blockade. We stand the tide back. They'll arrest a few of us maybe, but then the whole region will see what they're trying to do."

You picture faces in the morning light: neighbors with their scarves and patched jackets, kids too young to understand bail but old enough to remember fences. You also picture officers in raingear, the press framing a standoff, and lawsuits that bleed resources dry. The cost is not theoretical; it's scaffolding, loss, bruised bodies, and your name in headlines.

Elder Tomas: (quiet, with stern patience) "There is honor in standing. There is also duty in choosing who bears the weight. You cannot ask every pair of hands to hold what one pair should."

His words land like a keel hitting sand. They're not instruction — they're the weather you must read. You feel the compass cold against your skin as if reminding you that direction is not always destination.

You think of the boathouse itself, the lamp's honeyed circle, the nets that hang like old confessions. You think of the children who learned knots here, of the evenings with hot tea and stubborn plans. There is a strain between holding fast and letting go that twists at your ribs.

[Music: Cello note deepens; piano falls into a minor cadence, the storm outside stepping closer.]

You stand. Everyone turns. Their faces are lit by the same single lamp but each casts a different shadow: Ivy's fueled by anger, Lina's calculated and ready, Tomas's patient and grave, Ariana's unreadable patience on your phone, and somewhere beyond the window the tide waiting like an audience.

You can feel the town's breath at your back. The decisions you make now will be measured not just in wood and law, but in names that will be spoken long after the dust settles.
