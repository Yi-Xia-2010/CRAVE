[Scene: Tideguard Pavilion (Meeting Room) | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: A long, glass-walled meeting room perched above the edge of the harbor. Glass catches spray; the town is a low silhouette beneath. A single curated fern puffs vapor at a discreet nozzle. Cassian stands by a narrow table; a slim tablet glows with maps.]

[Sound: Quiet HVAC hum, distant gulls, the muted boom of breakers beyond the seawall. A soft chime from your pocket.]

[Music: Low, minor-key piano; a restrained synth pad underscoring the room's clinical warmth.]

You remembered saying yes in the mayor's meeting like a promise spoken into a wind that might scatter it. You told yourself negotiation was stewardship in another language — that the slow, bruising certainty of policy could be gentler if you guided it from inside.

Now the Pavilion feels too clean for that vow. The air tastes faintly of citrus antiseptic and the metal of your compass at your throat. Cassian's blazer is the same slate-gray as the plans he will unfold; his face behind the glass seems softened by the room's light, but his hands are precise.

Cassian: "Thank you for coming, Marin. I know it—" (he corrects himself, measuring) "—I know this is difficult for you and the town."

You: "I came because this can't be handled without people at the table who will be here when the generators fail." (You fold your hands around the compass; the brass is warm against your palm.)

Cassian: (leans forward, voice steady) "Exactly. We don't want a patchwork of measures that fail when the next season hits. What Tideguard proposes are immediate harbor barriers, reinforced pilings, and a corporate-administered trust to ensure maintenance—guaranteed funding, expedited implementation."

[Visual: He taps the tablet. A smooth animated model swells: waves crest against a black ribbon along the shoreline; green dots signaled as "critical nodes" light up. Data scrolls in precise columns.]

Cassian: "We can start construction in six months if we move on permits now. We have financing conditional only on a few easements and a faster permitting track. For the community, we can preserve key access points, fund relocations when necessary, and put in place a community liaison into the trust."

You watch the numbers like a tide chart. The projections are seductive: a flat red line receding into safety. The voice in his cadence — calm, assured — makes the boardwalk in your chest pause.

You: "Community liaison is not the same as community control."

Cassian: (half-smile) "True. But it's a bridge. We build the structure, we pay for it, we staff the oversight. Let us fix the immediate hazard so you can plan longer-term."

You feel the split of it: immediate shelter or long-term sovereignty. The room's engineered greenery smells faintly of peat and something manufactured to be reassuring.

[Sound: A soft vibration in your pocket. Your phone casts a pale rectangle across the table.]

[Visual: A message bubble blooms — Ariana: "Any sign? Pilot timelines depend on your pivot. We can scale living seawalls in parallel if you stall them a little." Your chest tightens.]

You: (under your breath) "She thinks in pilots like lifelines."

Cassian: (noticing the screen) "Ariana's work is promising. It's precisely why a hybrid approach could be explored. But hybrids take time. Time that some neighborhoods may not have."

You: "Which neighborhoods—" (Your throat tightens; you stop.) "Because 'some neighborhoods' is a phrase that carries people's homes."

Cassian: "We will consult. We'll designate protected cultural sites—your boathouse, for example—some special exemptions. We can include symbolic concessions: a preservation fund, plaques, a museum contribution."

You see Tomas's hands — callused, stained with varnish — in your mind, the way his fingers traced the curve of an old hull. You see Ivy's neon vest at a shoreline filling station, the way she summoned people into work with grin and grit. A tug occurs at your ribs like a line on a battered boat.

You: "Symbolic concessions don't stop bulldozers."

Cassian: (his jaw tightens for a blink) "They're not meant to. They're meant to acknowledge and protect, with teeth behind them. The trust will be legally accountable; Tideguard will post bonds to guarantee access. There will be oversight."

You push, not because you want to sabotage but because you know what words translate into when law meets weather.

You: "Who sits on that oversight? Who controls the bonds?"

Cassian: "A mixed board. Corporate, town representatives, an independent auditor. We can write binding clauses into the trust to ensure local vetoes on access changes."

You: (you hear the urge to say 'and who will enforce that veto when there is pressure from the funders?') Instead you ask, "What about easements? What exactly would you need from the town?"

Cassian: (opens a thin folder; his fingers are clinical as he slides diagrams across the table) "Legal easements for construction corridors, expedited coastal permits—essentially, the ability to mobilize without waiting on archaic hearings. In exchange, immediate funding, prioritized fortification of critical infrastructure, and long-term maintenance guaranteed."

[Sound: The tablet emits a soft confirmation chime each time he toggles a map layer; the room feels smaller.]

You: "Expedited red tape means fewer public hearings."

Cassian: "It means fewer delays. It means people sleeping inside their homes in the next storm season."

A small, involuntary image flashes — a neighborhood that sleeps through a storm and remains standing; another image flashes beneath it — a small row of cottages missing, boxes on a truck.

Cassian: (after your choice, adjusting) "Let me be frank—nobody here wants to displace more people than necessary. But some engineering requires footprint change. We compensate, we rebuild community assets, we fund retraining and relocation grants."

You: "Compensation doesn't replace the continuity of a place. Tomas taught his apprentices in that boathouse; names are carved into those beams."

[Visual: He watches you; behind him the harbor's gray shoulder shifts with light.]

Cassian: (soft) "I understand the genealogy of loss. I lost my father's dry dock to a storm when I was young. I remember the exact smell of burning tar and salt. That's a part of why I'm so focused on decisive solutions."

You: (the admission lands strange and human against the antiseptic air) "It doesn't make your plan less invasive. It makes you more believable."

Cassian: "Believability matters less than outcomes, Marin. I believe decisiveness saves lives. If we wait for perfect consent, the sea will take its own bargains."

[Sound: A distant foghorn; the piano picks a thread that tightens.]

A notification pulses again. [Visual: Lina — a short, clipped message: "Need transparency on contract clauses. Who signs? Watch for naming rights."] Her voice in text is sharp enough to cut through the Pavilion's curated calm.

You fold your hands in your lap. The compass's edge presses into your palm as if keeping score.

You: "If Tideguard controls the permit clock, who guarantees the town has a substantive veto? And how do we guarantee those 'protected cultural sites' don't shrink on paper?"

Cassian: (breathing out) "We can code veto powers into the trust's bylaws. We can allow for an independent audit, enforced by statute. I can offer an escrowed legal guarantee contingent on specified protections."

You: (a part of you relaxes at the word 'escrowed' as if money can stand in for memory; another part tightens) "You say 'can.' I need 'will.'"

Cassian: (tilts his head, a micro-gesture, like a person opening a window) "Will is a commitment. Here is what I ask." (He slides a single sheet of summary across the table. It is blunt and tidy.) "A preliminary, non-binding expression of support from you as the town's representative—enough to accelerate filings and allow us to begin the procurement cycle. In return: guaranteed prioritized fortification, funds reserved for cultural preservation, and a seat for local appointees on the trust board."

[Visual: The paper sits between you like a tide mark. Your phone buzzes again; the screen shows Ariana: "Please be careful. We can ask for oversight measures, but don't hand over the convention center without strings."]

You: (you can feel the room's warmth like a pressure) "You want me to open the door for you."

Cassian: "I want us to avoid the worst-case scenario. I want your town standing when the maps change."

You imagine two futures in the same breath: one where the harbor stands behind concrete arms and your children's children remember the shoreline as a widened boulevard; another where families are uprooted, cultural places relocated into memorial exhibits with admission fees.

Cassian: (quiet now) "I know the ask is heavy. I'm not here to erase the town's identity; I'm here to protect it from being erased by weather and inaction. I believe we can do both—if we move."

You: (your voice is quieter than you'd intended) "And if we move and it goes wrong?"

Cassian: "Then Tideguard is accountable. The trust, the bonds, the audits—all of that has legal consequences for breaches. We are answerable to courts and to reputation. We have incentives to make this work."

You want to test the incentives with a thousand what-ifs. You want to call Ivy and Tomas into the room; you want Lina to have this paper in her hands; you want Ariana to stand and tell Cassian, face to face, that living infrastructure is the future.

[Sound: Your phone buzzes with one last message. Ariana: "If you sign on to this publicly, I will fight to run parallel pilots. But please—no commitments that hamstring community governance."]

You fold the sheet and feel its thinness like a promise that can be folded flat.

Cassian: (leans back; the plant nearby sighs) "We don't need an immediate signature. A verbal alignment to proceed would be enough to unlock the procurement period. It gives us momentum. It keeps people safe sooner rather than later."

You inhale. The Pavilion's warmth sits on your skin like a weight. Outside, the harbor's surface glitters; inside, the tablet's models show a line dropping away from danger.

Your thumb finds the brass of the compass again. It hums with small, familiar friction.

You are aware, in a cold part of your chest, of the word that will form later: culpability.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand over the paper, the compass pendant half-glinting in Pavilion light.]

[Music: The piano chord lingers, unresolved; a single cello note drops like a pebble.]

You open your mouth.
