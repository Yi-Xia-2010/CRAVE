[Visual: Wide shot of the boardwalk drenched in pearled dawn; wet planks gleam; Tideguard Pavilion visible on the distant headland, glass catching pale light]
[Music: Low, hollow cello with distant wind chimes]
[Sound: Wooden planks groaning underfoot; gulls calling; a slow, distant generator hum]

You stand with the brass compass pendant warm against your sternum, its metal scuffing faintly beneath the fabric of your shirt. The tide is rewriting the map of the town — dark ribbons of water curling around foundations, pulling at the sand like an impatient sculptor. Each wave lays down a new seam of wet footprints and drags another discarded crab trap toward the harbor mouth.

Your hands smell of salt and old rope; the ridges of your knuckles are familiar maps to your own memory. You rub them together as if physical friction could erase the tightness at the base of your throat. The wooden planks underfoot groan when you shift weight. Somewhere under the hum and gulls, the town breathes with you, uneven and tired.

You count what you have left to work with — as if inventory could ward off the slow erosion of certainty. Volunteers at Greenwave who will show up before coffee, a field notebook with pressed leaves and diagrams that lean toward stubborn optimism, Elder Tomas's voice and the warning it carries like a tide mark along your memory, and the thin, unopened Tideguard envelope folded on your table like a held breath. Cassian Rhee's voice is already in people's mouths — precise, courteous, the kind that smooths edges until they disappear. You can see the way his polished plan could tilt the town toward a different shore.

A distant cry resolves into footsteps. Elder Tomas shuffles onto the boardwalk, his walking stick ticking like a metronome on the planks. He moves slower than he used to; the sea has taken a few more years from him than he took for granted. The carved motifs along his stick catch a bead of salt and shine like a small, stubborn star.

Elder Tomas: "Morning, Marin." (He pauses, breath clouding in the morning air.) "You couldn't be the only one awake, eh?"

You: "Morning, Tomas." (You pull your jacket closed against the damp. The compass feels heavier, or perhaps you do.) "Couldn't sleep."

Elder Tomas: "Storms do that to folks — and to sleep." (He looks at the horizon, then back at you.) "You're thinking of convening. I heard the talk. Tideguard's name's on more than one cart this week."

You: "I am." (You want to be crisp, decisive. The words want to come out like a tool, useful and clean. They don't.) "We need everyone in one room — Greenwave's crew, the research hub, the council. If we do not talk together, decisions will be made without the people who actually live here."

Elder Tomas's face tightens, the old lines folding deeper. He shifts the stick and the motion brings memory — the boathouse rafters he once taught you to measure, the night they almost lost the harbor, his quiet corrections when you were a child.

Elder Tomas: "Weighed and true." (He hesitates.) "But remember — meetings don't keep the sea back. Plans do. And plans need ballast — truth, not wishful thinking."

You: "I know." (It sounds like an answer, but it's a cave-to-cave call that doesn't reach bottom.) "I don't want talk that soothes. I want a meeting that makes hard things possible. I want people to see the costs and also own them."

Elder Tomas: "And what of Tideguard's envelope?" (He eyes the distant Pavilion as if it might speak.) "You open it, you start bargaining from within. You leave it closed, you may be accused of refusing offer. Either way, your chest will ache."

You close your eyes for a breath and the memory of the Old Boathouse surfaces — the lamp-gold circle on its warped floorboards, the jar of spare nails, your father's laugh echoing with the smell of tar and cedar. You remember how the harbor felt like home-made architecture: imperfect but honest. The thought of negotiation tables replacing that intimacy feels like someone sanding down the grain of your life.

Elder Tomas: "You can call the meeting, Marin. But you must be ready for the choices that come out of it."

You: "I will be." (The sentence isn't full assurance. It cracks at the edges.)

A voice cuts across the gulls — bright, almost too quick for the weariness of the morning. Ivy Morales strides up, energy folded tight in a neon vest. Her braids are dusted with salt and mud; her grin is peat-strong.

Ivy Morales: "You're starting something? Thought I smelled revolution and coffee." (She plucks a bundle of seed packets from her pocket and taps them against her palm.) "Greenwave's volunteers are ready. Kelp frames are curing. Ariana texted — she lands midday and wants to meet you about the nursery."

You: "Good. Tell them the time is eight. I'm calling everyone to the community hall after the tide recedes." (You speak the time like it anchors you.) "And Ivy — can you bring the kelp frames to show? Visuals help."

Ivy Morales: "Got it. Visuals, coffee, supportive angry faces." (She smirks.) "Also, Lina asked if she could run a piece on it. She says the town needs to hear how your group interprets the stakes."

You feel a small, complicated lift at Lina's interest. Publicity can be warmth or wildfire. Lina Park has a pen that sets lighthouses; her coverage could bring clarity or make the meeting a battlefield.

Ivy Morales: "And Tomas—" (She nods at him, respectful.) "He's on your side?"

Elder Tomas: "On caution." (A wry twist in his mouth.) "I'm always on caution."

Ivy's grin narrows in thought. She glances at the tide, then toward the shimmering glass of Tideguard.

Ivy Morales: "So — what kind of tone are you setting for this meeting? We can go full-on urgent and blunt, or be steady and sell collaboration. Which do you want me to put on the flyers?"

Your chest tightens; the choice of tone feels like choosing which part of the shore to reinforce first. You imagine both outcomes: a blunt, urgent meeting that sparks immediate action but fractures some folks; a steady, collaborative framing that invites broader buy-in but gives Cassian breathing room to pitch his shiny solution.

You let Ivy take the directive, because she is the one who can shape tone into a lived reality. She nods and disappears down the boardwalk toward the community center, the neon vest a beacon in the damp air.

Elder Tomas leans on his stick. You turn the pendant between two fingers, feeling the warm scuff of brass.

Elder Tomas: "You remember why you returned, don't you? Not for prestige, not for paper. For this place." (He taps his chest, over his heart.) "You taught the kids to splice lines. You fixed nets when the town had nothing. Don't forget that the town recognizes the people who show up with honest hands."

You: "I haven't forgotten." (The words are both shield and confession.) "But showing up isn't enough when the stakes have changed. The sea doesn't bargain. It takes."

Elder Tomas: "Then show them what it takes to stay." (He studies you, searching behind your eyes.) "And remember to ask for help."

You almost smile at the plea; asking for help tastes like salt and rust in your mouth, but you feel the truth of it. There's a stubbornness in you that hoards burdens like ballast.

A thin vibration tugs at your pocket. You fish out your phone: a text from Ariana pops up — a photo of a ferry slip painted in blue, a caption that reads "Arriving noon. Kelp frames ready. Can't wait to see the harbor with your eyes." Attached is a small clip: platinum-ash hair and a flash of the blue streak, a laugh that feels like sunlight in glass. Your chest loosens and tightens at once. Hope arrives in yellow boots and an impulsive grin, but it comes with its own fragility — an outsider's optimism meeting a town's long-list of wounds.

You place the phone back and look out toward the Tideguard Pavilion. The glass repeats the sky, clean and distant. Rumors thread through the town like the smell of diesel after a storm — Cassian's firm promises of engineering dragons that will hold the sea back. Promises that, if accepted, will change the shape of Havenport's shore, and maybe its soul.

Ivy returns, breathless with two flyers and a thermos. She hands you one; the ink is bold, the language chosen by the tone you picked.

Ivy Morales: "Also — quick one. Old Boathouse ritual? You want me to stop by and pick up the maps and the lamp? Or do you want to head straight to Greenwave and prep the visuals?"

The Old Boathouse sits in your memory as a repository of personal and communal history. There is comfort there: the warmth of a single lamp, the smell of tar, your father's hands. Bringing those objects into the meeting could anchor the conversation in history — a reminder that this is not abstract engineering but lived heritage. But stopping will take time; Greenwave needs set-up now.

You meet Ivy’s eyes and make the small, practical decision; whichever you choose, the why is heavier than the action. You feel the tug of every path: history vs urgency, memory vs momentum. The harbor keeps offering tradeoffs.

Ivy: "Okay. I'll handle that. And Lina's on standby — she wants quotes, people to speak. Tomas says he'll come if it doesn't start too late."

Elder Tomas: "I'll come." (He pauses, then adds.) "And bring a story, Marin. Bring a story that remembers names."

You think of names — your father's, the old fishermen whose boats sag now at shallower moorings, the kid who came to Greenwave last summer and planted their first mangrove with dirt-streaked hands. Names make erosion a moral calculus, not just numbers on a report.

You walk the boardwalk slowly, watching each wave reclaim a sliver of beach and then surrender it. Hope tugs like an oar — steadying, tiring. The unopened Tideguard envelope, the image of Cassian's poised smile, the bright band of Ariana's blue streak — all of it knits into a low, stubborn decision forming in your chest.

You stop and breathe. The compass pendant is warm. You can feel, with a clarity that doesn't bring comfort, that calling this meeting is right and that it will not be simple. It will force choices into the light. It will make people uncomfortable. It will make you uncomfortable.

You set the meeting in motion with your voice because the town will not save itself through silence.

You pick up your phone to send the group message, the words assembling in your mouth like carefully stacked planks.

You pause — because even decisions are not made without a last look back at what you might lose and a hard swallow for what must be done.
