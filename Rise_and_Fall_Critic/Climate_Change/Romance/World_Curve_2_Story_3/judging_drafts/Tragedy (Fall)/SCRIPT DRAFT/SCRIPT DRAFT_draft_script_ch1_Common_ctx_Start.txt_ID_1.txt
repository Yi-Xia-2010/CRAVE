[Scene: Havenport Boardwalk | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of the boardwalk, wet planks glinting in pale gold; Tideguard Pavilion a distant silhouette on the headland]
[Music: Low, hollow cello with distant wind chimes]
[Sound: Gulls calling; the slow groan of wood underfoot; a faint generator hum from seawall repairs]

You stand on the same boards your grandfather once nailed down, feeling the planks answer under your boots. The brass compass at your throat is warm against your sternum — a familiar, private heat that steadies the hollow in your ribs. Salt rides your skin like a map of every storm you've measured; when you flex your hands you can still smell rope and tar and the old sap of cedar. The tide is low enough to show the town's scars: seaweed like bruises along the posts, sand mounded against patched pilings, a hairline seam where the water cut last winter's new slat from the old.

You inventory what you have, as if checking a toolbelt: the volunteer crew at Greenwave who will show up when you ask them, hands pruned and resolute; the field notebook in your bag, edges soft with pressed leaves and cramped plans; Elder Tomas's warnings — his voice shaped like a carved hull — looping in your head; and that unopened Tideguard envelope on your kitchen table, its corporate seal a cold echo of other people's solutions. Cassian Rhee's name is a tide that keeps coming up in every coffee-line complaint, in the careful civics chatter of the council. You did not expect him yet you feel him here, a presence between the waves and the promenade.

[Visual: Close-up on your face; the sea-reflected light traces the faint crow's-feet near your eyes]
You run callused fingers through your dark, messy ponytail. Ariana's face comes to you like a flash of light — platinum-ash bob, the electric blue streak at her temple, bright yellow boots splashing in nursery puddles. You picture her kneeling among kelp trays, camera slung at her hip, laughing because a recruit tripped over a buoy. There is a sweetness to that image that lifts you and then — the low note returns. You are tired of duct-taped fixes and ceremonial ribbon-cuttings. Some mornings the seaside smells like possibility; today it smells like the last cigarette after a fight.

[Sound: A pair of footsteps approach, brisk and familiar]
Ivy appears at the boardwalk's turn, neon vest catching the weak dawn. Her braid is threaded with sea glass; her hands have always moved in a restless choreography of repair.

Ivy: "Morning, Marin. You look like you haven't slept and have tried to fix the harbor in your dreams."

You try for a wry twist of your mouth. "Maybe I have. Maybe I organized a volunteer fleet in my sleep," you say. (You know that's not true — you organized them awake, at three a.m., whispering into your phone.)

Ivy: (half-laugh) "That explains the sheen. What's up? You called me 'urgent' — dramatic."

You: "No drama. Just... practical panic. I want to bring everyone together. Greenwave, the Research Hub, the council. We need to stop stitching sandbags like bandages."

Ivy pauses, then studies the distant glass tilt of Tideguard's pavilion, the way it collects the morning spray and refracts it into a promise that tastes vaguely of contracts and conference rooms. "That Cassian fellow's been busy," she says. "Lina's been sniffing around his briefings. And—" she presses her lips, measuring the words— "people are tired of talking."

Ivy: (softening) "Tomas will come if you ask. He knows how to make people remember what matters. But he'll also tell you what he always tells me — you can't make everyone agree with a hammer and a printout."

You think of Elder Tomas: the stoop of his shoulders like a center of gravity for the town, the carved walking stick he uses more for cadence than support. You remember the Old Boathouse — its lamp-gold interior, the hung nets, the smell of spare nails — and how Tomas's stories hung on its rafters like drying fish: stories about weather, about boats, about respect you owe to the sea because it kept you alive so often and took more than it gave sometimes.

[Visual: Flash memory overlay of The Old Boathouse interiors for a beat; the image fades back to the boardwalk]
You hear Lina's laugh before you see her: quick paper-shuffle footsteps, coat flapping as she rounds the corner with a satchel full of notes. She holds up a tablet, the screen bright against the dawn.

Lina: "You called Ivy 'urgent' and now the town's two most reliable catastrophists are here. What is this?" (She smiles, but there is a journalist's edge — hungry and careful.)

You: "Community meeting. Early. We need to align research, volunteers, council voices. Kelp nurseries, living seawalls — everything that's been bobbing in the margins."

Lina: "If you phrase it as 'collaborative pilot,' Cassian will parachute in with a prefab solution and a market pitch. If you phrase it as 'emergency session,' people will be scared into silence." (She taps the tablet like a metronome.) "Which is the Marin Sato angle today?"

You: (you try to be honest, and honesty has weight) "Neither. I want people to show up informed and furious in equal measure — enough fury to demand options, not one polished offer."

Lina's eyes flick to the horizon where Tideguard's glass reflects a slice of sun. "I'll write the notice neutrally. But Marin," she adds, softer, "don't let 'informed' become 'paralyzed by data.' People need to feel the reason to care, not just read it."

You: "I know. That's why I'm calling this now. We have momentum at Greenwave. We have Tomas who remembers the old tides. What we don't have is a path that keeps people from losing their homes."

Elder Tomas arrives without fanfare, his steps slow but certain. He sets his carved walking stick down, the wood thunking softly on a board. The air scents of cedar, rain, and a faint grease of old varnish — a smell that sinks into you the way history sinks into a harbor wall.

Elder Tomas: "Marin." (His voice is a rough rope.) "The sea is speaking in ways you all can hear now. It's not just storms; it's the slow sanding down of what we thought permanent."

You: "We're planning. We need to talk about the long-term work — nursery expansions, living barriers, managed retreat where necessary. We can't keep papering over this."

Elder Tomas: (a pause, his eyes on the town, not you) "You lead, but you must remember that leadership here means carrying people's loss as much as their hope. You cannot invent that weight and put it back on them. They will look to you when the tide takes something that's been their name for decades."

A full silence drops between your words and the gulls. The boardwalk creaks. You taste iron at the back of your throat, the old familiar of worry that does not go away with expediency. You imagine Ariana arriving with a crate of kelp plugs, the way she will insist the nursery experiments be recorded and shared, how her optimism will butt up against Tomas's memory. The image lodges like a buoy in your chest: buoyant, bright, tethered.

Ivy: (fidgeting with a frayed rope) "If you call people to the Old Boathouse, it'll feel like a home meeting, not a council slam. People come when it's a living space, not when it's a hall with a podium."

Lina: "I'll get the notice out. Tomas — will you open with a few words? Not a sermon. A recollection. A reason."

Elder Tomas studies you again. There is a softness there, but also a carved-in firmness that has held this town's small ship steady before.

Elder Tomas: "Marin, you have fought for this place since you returned. But remember: you do not have to carry all of it alone."

The words land like stones in your palm. You feel the familiar itch to apologize before you can ask for help — an old reflex of self-sacrifice. You picture your field notebook: pages of schedules and seedlings, lists crossed out and rewritten in a hand that presses too hard. The compass against your throat feels heavier now; not an anchor but the weight of expectation.

You breathe seawater and sunlight into your lungs. The horizon is a bruise-blue smear; low clouds hang like questions. Somewhere, a gull wheeled too close and cried out, and the sound echoed the strain of the town's conversation.

You decide to call a meeting. It isn't a romantic flourish — it's a decision with teeth. This morning, you will ask the town to choose how to hold what remains of home.

[Visual: Close-up on the compass; the camera tightens as salt beads on its brass face]
[Sound: The wood creaks; the music pulls back to a single sustained cello note]

You fold the breath in your chest into a plan you can carry down the boardwalk to the Old Boathouse, to the Greenwave terraces, to the Research Hub's glass face. The tide keeps arriving and receding, painting the shore with what it takes and what it leaves. You run a thumb over the compass, feeling the seams of it, feeling the seam of the town.

There is a lowness under the decision — hope thin as a tide line — but you recognize it. Some storms bruise and pass; some storms reshape the coastline forever. Today feels like one of those reshaping mornings.
