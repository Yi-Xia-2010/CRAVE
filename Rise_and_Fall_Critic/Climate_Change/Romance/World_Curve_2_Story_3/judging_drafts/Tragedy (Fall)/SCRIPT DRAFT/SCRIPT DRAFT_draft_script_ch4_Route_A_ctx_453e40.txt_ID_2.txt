[Scene: Havenport Boardwalk | Late Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the boardwalk, hand-lettered signs bobbing like flags; the Tideguard Pavilion glints on the skyline]
[Sound: A rising human roar, gulls shrieking, wet wood creaking underfoot]
[Music: Low, insistent strings; a taut percussion like a marching heartbeat]

You step onto the planks and the sound of the town hits you like cold spray — not just the sea but voices, old friends and new neighbors, the collective inhale before a shout. Ivy is a neon arrow in the crowd, vest flashing, hands full of sandbags that smell faintly of damp earth and rubber. Elder Tomas moves beside you with the slow authority of someone who has seen more tides than any ledger; his walking stick taps a patient rhythm against the wood. Lina angles her camera toward the action, face focused, eyes scanning for the image that will cut through a headline.

Your hair, usually restrained in a messy ponytail, has escaped more than usual; loose strands sting your cheeks in the breeze. You press the compass at your throat with a thumb you don't know you're using, reading the scuffs like a map you wrote yourself. The pendant is warm against salt skin — a small defiance, a private thing tucked into public noise.

You climb onto an upturned crate because someone has to make the message clear. The crate is half rotten, but it holds — much like the town, you think, and the comparison tastes both fierce and fragile in your mouth.

You can feel the crowd around you: a body-to-body warmth that pushes against the chill wind. Signs lift — LIVING SEAWALLS NOT BARRIERS, COASTLINE FOR EVERYONE, HANDS, NOT HONEYCOMBS — each a short poem of grief and insistence.

You inhale, and the smell of tar and seaweed and lemon-scented hand sanitizer from the volunteer station blends into a single, odd comfort. Then you speak.

Marin Sato: "We didn't wait for someone to save us. We've been saving ourselves, one plank, one seedling, one promise at a time." (Your voice threads through the crowd; you don't try to be loud — you try to be true.)

Ivy: "That's it. Say it like you mean it, Marin." (She sets another sandbag down with quick fingers, eyes bright and fierce.) "Tell them the harbor isn't a ledger."

Marin Sato: "It's not a ledger." (You let the words land.) "It's where our elders taught us to read the weather. It's where kids learn to release their first crab. It's not a line item on a balance sheet."

Lina: (from where she films) "Keep the soundbite, Marin. Make journalists feel rooted, not radical." (She tilts the camera to catch the rope of faces behind you — a living margin.)

A woman near the front sobs softly, and someone else hands her a paper cup. The roar becomes a wave and then an attentive hush as you outline the plan: living seawalls built with kelp nurseries and reef-forming techniques, a cooperative trust to pool funds and keep public access, transparent decision-making that does not trade away memories for concrete.

You lay the structures out in plain phrases, the kind your neighbors will repeat in kitchens and over nets. You speak of elders' boatmaking lore and the science Ariana brought: symbiosis, not exclusion. The crowd answers with a chant that begins low and builds.

[Sound: Chants swell — "Our shore, our right!" — planks thudding as people stamp in time]

From the edge of the gathering, Tideguard security moves in formation, polite smiles like gloved hands. They hand out glossy press statements with carefully neutral language about "community engagement" and "long-term solutions." You take one and feel its clinical paper under your fingers like a cold promise.

A man in a slate blazer steps forward behind a line of briefcases and quiet shoes. He is polished the way a stone is polished by being held; his smile shows skills more than warmth. His team unfurls a leather folio and the shine of a fountain pen catches the light like a small, accusing star.

Cassian Rhee: (hands clasped in front of him) "Marin. A bold crowd. A necessary debate." (His tone is even, practiced.) "We can bring the resources Havenport needs, but we need to move quickly. Risk multiplies if we dither."

Marin Sato: "We move with people, not over them." (You say it without theatrics, because you mean it.) "Speed without memory is not safety. Your barriers may stop a tide but they won't stop the stories from being lost."

Cassian Rhee: "I don't want the harbor's stories lost, Marin. I want them protected." (He tilts his head, as if to test your hearing.) "Protection sometimes looks like hard lines."

Ivy: (sharp) "And sometimes it looks like a moat that locks us out." (She plants her hands on her hips, salt on her knuckles.) "We won't be erased for a blueprint."

Cassian's lawyer steps forward then, polite and blunt, a sheet of typed threats folded between manicured hands. "This is a sensitive negotiation," the lawyer says. "Erecting unsanctioned structures, interfering with approved zones — these actions could prompt legal action."

Tomas: (soft, a rasp with the authority of timber) "I've seen boards crumble and rebuild. I've seen men learn to listen. Laws can be rewritten. Memory can't be purchased." (He looks at you, and in that look there is both permission and a burden.)

Your heart picks up, an erratic bird in your chest. The adrenaline is delicious and terrible; your mouth is dry around it. You watch Cassian's team distribute their calm like armor, eyes scanning for journalists they can charm. Lina moves through the crowd, asking questions into her camera, collecting faces and short testimonies that feel like stitches being sewn into a banner.

Ariana arrives then, raincoat collar up despite dry air — the practical show of someone who knows weather and knows how to prepare for it. You see her scanning the crowd the same way she scans a coastline: mapping currents, measuring energy. Her expression when it lands on you is — complex. There is a flicker of pride, a flash of worry, and something you can't easily name. You do not pry. Public performances have private margins.

Ariana Voss: (quiet, to you) "You're good with words." (She offers a small, uncertain smile.) "And with making people believe they can act."

Marin Sato: "I'm good at translating grief into a plan." (You manage a half-laugh that tastes like salt.) "You're better at measuring whether our plan will live."

Ariana Voss: "We're better together, when we don't forget to listen." (She looks past you toward the Pavilion and the line of suits.) "But they will try to make 'listen' mean 'follow.'"

The lawyer's voice rides across the plankwork: "For everyone's safety, we ask that demonstrations remain peaceful. We will take no ... unnecessary measures."

Unnecessary measures. The phrase is a velvet rope around a threat.

The moment is small and ridiculous and huge: do you let the polished faces narrow the argument into a private exchange — where deals can glitter and memory can be traded — or keep it public, where words can be repeated and proof exists? You choose with a tilt of your chin, and your choice shapes the day's atmosphere like wind shapes a sail.

The crowd's chant becomes a chorus of answers to a lawyer's tentative threats. Someone starts a call-and-response about the old fishing routes, and an old woman near the rail sings a line that makes the crowd hush in a way that feels almost holy. You feel exhilaration bloom through your sternum: the town is present; phrases you taught them roll off tongues. But there is a gnawing under the joy — the knowledge that loudness invites counterweights. You see eyes in the periphery: cameras, suits, the thin smiles of security that sometimes hide patience wearing the shape of preparation.

A journalist from a regional outlet pushes forward and asks you a pointed question: "If Tideguard offers to fund a living seawall under their terms, will you accept conditional funding?"

Before you can form a policy statement, Ariana steps in, practical and exacting.

Ariana Voss: "Terms matter. Ownership matters. We're not bargaining our coastline for a logo." (She faces the reporter fully.) "If funding comes, it comes through a cooperative trust with transparent governance — not as an exclusive gift that erases community rights."

Reporter: "Do you think that's realistic? Corporations expect returns."

Marin Sato: "We expect accountability. Returns can be social, not just financial." (You let the sentence hang between them; it is both an argument and an invitation.)

Lina, still filming, whispers to you as she passes: "This footage — this is what they can't litigate away." Her voice is small and fierce. "Stories are currency."

You weigh the value of immediate witness against the tactical advantage of careful timing. Being public is a shield and a provocation. You let the choice be small and yet sharp.

As the afternoon slips toward dusk, Tideguard's press release circulates online like a net. Their language is careful, promising "community collaboration" while outlining staged implementation schedules that, if followed, would hand them large swaths of control. Lawyers begin to timestamp notes. You'll see the legalese tomorrow in printouts and in Lina's footage.

[Visual: The late-afternoon light slants, gilding the edges of signs; shadows lengthen]
[Sound: Distant thunder like bookcases settling; someone begins the work of nailing boards into place at a nearby shopfront]

You and a knot of volunteers move toward the Old Boathouse as light dies. Its cedar sides are a storybook of storms — patched tarps, hand-lettered maps, a smell of oil and sawdust. The place is small, a safe harbor for the day's messy truth-telling.

You slide open the door and the lamp inside casts a honeyed circle over the workbench. The jar of nails sits where your fingers can reach. You take a nail, start a board, then pause. The compass pendant at your throat weighs familiar and real; you tuck it inside the jar as if placing a talisman beneath a community altar. Your fingers get sticky with tar and salt; the act anchors you in the hands-on reality of what you're defending.

Ivy: "We should keep watch," she says. Her voice holds a note of care sharpened by worry. "Funding could dry up. They could cut us out of committees. Or — worse — arrests. This escalates fast."

Marin Sato: "We knew that when we stood on the crate." (You hammer the board with steady strikes.) "Escalation was never the scary part. Being erased quietly was."

Tomas: (sitting on a coil of rope, watching your hands) "You did the right thing standing there today. Rightness isn't always softness." (He taps the bench; wood answers.) "There will be costs. There always are when folks ask for the shore back."

Ivy: (bitter half-smile) "Costs and arguments. We're good at both." (She levels a look at you.) "Promise me you'll eat something other than adrenaline tonight."

Marin Sato: "I'll try." (You laugh, but it is thin.) "Promise me you'll keep the generators fueled."

Ivy: "Deal."

Lina leans against a stanchion and rewinds her footage on a small monitor, eyes hungry. "The angle's good. Tomas's line about memory — use that. People will listen to history." Her tone is editorial and tender at once. "If we make them feel what they've already lost, they'll want to stop losing more."

Ariana stands at the doorway, silhouetted for a moment, raincoat a practical dark. You catch her watching the way you hold a nail, the way you fold your jaw when thinking. Her face is a map of complex lines — support, calculation, fear — and you keep hearing that it's unreadable in the way that protects whoever's story is still unfolding.

Ariana Voss: "I'll make the first technical brief public tonight. If they want to talk governance, they have to talk accountability too." (She meets your eyes with a small, fierce steadiness.) "But Marin — we have to be honest about what resources it takes. Kelp nurseries scale differently than concrete, and that's harder to sell fast."

Marin Sato: "I'd rather scale honestly than sell quickly." (You set another board, hand steady.) "If we get swallowed by quick fixes, they'll never come back to us."

A shadow falls at the doorway. The sound of polite shoes on wet wood. A man in a Tideguard jacket stands outside with a clipboard — not Cassian, but someone who represents their process. He offers a thin smile that knows contracts better than neighbors.

Tideguard Rep: "We can open a dialogue. We value the town's input. Please accept this invitation to a roundtable tomorrow."

Marin Sato: (cool) "We value the town's voice. We'll decide how that voice is invited." (You let the sentence sit. You will decide the terms, not just the hour.)

The representative steps back. The night closes like a lid. You hammer a final board in place, the sound compact and finite in the small room, and you feel the day's volume — protest, press, pledges, threats — compress into something you can put in a pocket. Your palms ache with blisters and the memory of sand.

You have done what you promised the harbor you would: you raised your voice, you made the refusal public. The cost hangs in the air like a storm front on the horizon. You imagine the next day's headlines, the legal memos, the meeting agendas. You imagine arrests as possible and funding as fragile. You also imagine kitchen tables repeating your chant, the kids who will inherit a coastline with a living reef, and the apprentices who will learn both ropes and policies.

[Sound: A far-off siren; the wind reads the boardwalk like a finger through notes]
[Music: A minor key piano line, slow and aching]

You wash tar from your hands at the pump outside, the cold water taking some of the heat away but not the worry. You tuck the day into the Old Boathouse's dark belly and go outside for a breath of salt air. The compass in the jar clinks faintly when a board shifts.

Ivy: (quiet) "There will be pushback, Marin. There will be suits and lawyers and sleepy committees who decide to move slowly on purpose." (Her voice is gentle and sharp.) "But there's also people. Neighbors. Tomas. Lina. Ariana." (She steps closer.) "We have to keep them together."

Marin Sato: (softly) "We will try. And if it comes to it, we'll stand in the way together." (You don't say whether that's defiance or invitation; sometimes both are the same.)

You look at Tomas then, and he offers you a small, crooked smile as if to pass the watch onward.

Tomas: "When the sea takes something, we learn to make something new from what's left. Sometimes it's ugly. Sometimes it's beautiful. Usually both." (He pats your shoulder like a benediction.)

You fold your hands over the jar of nails, feeling the compass's cool metal through glass. There is a fierce, quiet pride in what you have started and a dull fear of how hard the town will be tested. You know louder resistance will bring heavier pressure. You have chosen the public road; now the road will choose its tests.

[Visual: Close-up of your hands around the jar; the compass glinting dimly inside]
[Sound: The ocean's far murmur; a neighbor's radio playing an old fishing tune]

You turn away from the boathouse, feeling the night sit on your shoulders like a shawl. The stars are thin over the water; the lights at the Pavilion are small, steady, and far.

A single thought rises: we made the stand. The rest will follow.

[Music: Strings hold a long, somber chord that trembles with unresolved tension]

You know what tomorrow might bring, and that knowledge is both an armament and an ache.
