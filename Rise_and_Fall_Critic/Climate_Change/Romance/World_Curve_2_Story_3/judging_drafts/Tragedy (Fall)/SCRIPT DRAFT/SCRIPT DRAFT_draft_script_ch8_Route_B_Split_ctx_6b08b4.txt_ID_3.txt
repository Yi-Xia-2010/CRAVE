[Scene: The Old Boathouse | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The boathouse's single lamp throwing a honeyed circle across a scarred table. The legal compact lies open, pages riffling occasionally in the salt breeze that finds gaps in the boarded windows.]
[Sound: Rain beginning to stitch across the roof; a distant harbor bell tolling once, slow and low.]
[Music: Sparse cello under a low piano—minor-key, patient.]

You set the compact flat on the table and let the lamp pick out Cassian Rhee's handwriting in the margins: neat, confident loops that belong in meeting rooms and memoranda, not on paper that smells of tar and old rope. The ink looks almost too clinical against the boathouse's warm wood—an invitation of steel inside a home that remembers storms by smell and scar.

The pages are dense with legalese: easements for "protective barriers," clauses granting "priority access" to certain stretches of shoreline, paragraphs authorizing "emergency relocations" under enumerated conditions. Each clause is a small machine that moves land and memory. Your thumb traces the edge where Cassian has underlined a sentence; the paper is warm from the lamp. The compass at your throat rests against your sternum like a small, patient heartbeat.

Ivy stands across from you. She reads, and the words fall heavy between the rafters.

Ivy Morales: "Easement rights expressly subordinate to corporate maintenance obligations..." (her voice tugs at the last syllable) "—and indemnification for Tideguard against local infrastructure claims."

You hear the hurt in her mouth before she finishes the sentence. She doesn't look up; she folds the line with slow fingers, as if folding the coast back into itself.

Elder Tomas taps his carved stick twice against the map pinned by the compact's corner. The sound is an old metronome.

Elder Tomas: "Paper does not remember salt." (He lifts his chin.) "It does not remember the shape of nets, or the names we carved into the beams. It remembers signatures."

Lina is already half in the doorframe, phone lit in her hand, the soft glow catching the rim of her glasses. She parks herself in the doorway like a question mark.

Lina Park: "If we go public with this—if the town sees the exact terms—people will know what they've been offered. They deserve to decide with open eyes." (Her voice is quick, sharp with the journalist's hunger for truth.)

Ariana stands near the stacked oars, arms folded around herself as if bracing against wind. She's quieter now than you've seen her when a plan's first outline was drawn; the lab-coated hope has been exchanged for something tighter.

Ariana Voss: "We risk scaring Tideguard off. If we make them think they'll be burned on optics, they'll pull funding and there won't be a single protective metre installed." (She looks at you.) "We don't have to sign blind, Marin. We can demand oversight clauses and see if they'll budge."

Cassian watches all of you with the still-face of someone used to being the calmest storm in the room. There's a softness in his expression that reads like strategy: patient, practiced.

Cassian Rhee: "Our models show significant reduction in overtopping with the barriers in place within eighteen months." (He folds his hands.) "I can make the funding contingent on a maintenance schedule agreeable to the town."

You pick up a pen and let its tip hover above a margin with his handwriting in it. The lamp's circle makes the ink glint. The pen feels heavier than usual.

You think of the families who sleep close to the shore, of the boathouse's timbers your grandfather once pounded back into line after a smaller storm. You think of the living-seawall sketch Ariana brought you—knobby, green, full of small life—and the way Lina's stories have turned small, local fights into regional noise before your eyes. Every memory pushes in different directions.

Ivy: "We can't pretend the concessions are small. This clause—'priority access,'—that could lock out small fishers from their landing. Tomas, you'll see houses moved before your grandchildren can—"

Elder Tomas: (interrupting, voice thin with old fire) "They'll move things they don't understand. They'll call it efficiency; they'll call it preservation. But preservation without place is a museum—it's not a home."

Cassian: (softly) "We are offering protections that would otherwise be impossible without private capital. You asked for safety; I'm delivering a realistic pathway."

Ariana: (a ripple of frustration) "No—he's offering a pathway that centralizes control. There's a difference. We asked for partnerships, Marin. Not blank checks."

You breathe and the lamp scent—wax and salt-stiff air—sinks into the space between them. Outside, the rain grows louder, as if the sky itself is taking notes.

You let the compact sit where you placed it. Lina steps closer, the phone now in both hands like a pair of scales.

Lina: "If you ask me, the town deserves the whole document. We have the right—" (she swallows) "—and they'll thank us for it, or they'll curse us for it. Either way, it belongs to them."

Ariana: (quietly) "Or they'll watch their livelihood be bulldozed out from under them while the papers are still being translated."

Ivy: (wearing a careful anger) "Marin, you've been doing this forever. I know you're tired. But remember why we started the collective—because we wanted a say, not for others to make it for us."

Cassian leans forward, voice steady, almost conciliatory.

Cassian: "You can make oversight binding, Marin. But you must understand what 'binding' means in law. Investors will ask for protections against unpredictable governance. If you add too many constraints, the capital withdraws."

You feel the word "binding" like a noose and a guarantee at once. Your pulse catches. The town's resilience could be bought for a price that would authenticate corporate control over centuries; yet without that capital, many of the town's low-lying streets remain unshielded. The ledger of choices is worse than arithmetic—it's a roster of faces.

Elder Tomas: (leaning on his stick) "If you sign, the town will have a short season with fewer floods. If you refuse and drive them off, you'll have storms anyway, and maybe a kind of bitter pride. I'd like to see a town that remembers, not one that is boxed in."

You want to argue with Tomas and then you want to cradle him. Memory and survival tug you toward different edges of the map.

Cassian: (after a long pause) "Worst case: we withdraw. Funding dries up. The next storm season carries real damage—homes, livelihoods, the harbor itself. Best case with the compact: we buy time. Time to build, time to adapt."

Ariana: (voice breaking slightly) "Time bought on terms nobody chose is a debt people pay with their histories, Cassian."

You fold your hands over the compass, feeling the brass warm under your palm. This is not a technical problem you can engineer away; it's a moral ledger you are being asked to initial. Your hands tremble. Not from the cold—though the boathouse is cool—but from the weight of choices.

You think of the living-seawall again, of the sprigs of sea-grass tethered in your notebook, of Ivy's laughter in the nursery, of Lina's bite when she smells a story. You think of Ariana—the way her hands steady when she speaks about kelp nurseries—and you try to map the possible futures onto your chest.

Lina: "If we publish, people will flood the meeting. They will force the council's hand. Maybe they'll reject the compact outright. But transparency is what holds power accountable."

Ivy: "Or it'll scare Tideguard; they'll say we sabotaged the negotiation and pull out."

Elder Tomas: (quiet) "What happens if both happen? If we lose the deal and the town still floods? Will you forgive yourself, Marin?"

The question lands like a stone.

You realize, with a cold honesty, that any path is a partial sacrifice. You are not choosing a victory or defeat in the clean sense; you are choosing which parts of the town will be spared and which will be rearranged. Signing is security with cession. Demanding oversight is a gamble for guardrails that may not be accepted. Leaking to Lina is an act of raw democracy that may also manufacture chaos.

A silence hangs, heavy as a net left to dry. The lamp is small. The rain's cadence fills the cracks.

You imagine the compact again—your finger on the dotted line. Your mouth tastes of metal and the memory of tears. This is the personal turned institutional: your name could be the pivot that shifts a shoreline's fate.

You breathe. The room leans in.

You are asked, now, not to save everyone or to find the perfect compromise, but to account for what you value and what you can bear to lose.
