[Scene: Greenwave Community Garden | Morning]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_1"]
[Sound: The low, constant burble of a nearby repair generator; gulls arguing over a scrap; the faint clink of trowels against metal.]
[Music: A slow, minor-key piano; distant strings that swell and then hold.]

You step into the garden with the taste of the boardwalk still in your mouth — salt, old wood, the memory of Elder Tomas's stick against planks. The compost smell is richer here: damp soil, iron-bright copper from the nursery's recycled soil mix, a breath of kelp carried on the cooler air. Your jacket sleeve is dusty at the cuff; the fabric smells faintly of tar and last night's rain. You tuck your palm into the pocket where the brass pendant rests, feeling the familiar dent in the metal. It isn't advice. It's a hinge.

Ivy Morales is already at the first terrace, laughter spilling like loose seeds. Her neon vest is a flair of defiance against the green: small, brilliant. She hands you a length of twine with one hand and, with the other, holds a spray bottle like a ceremonial scepter.

Ivy Morales: "You brought the good scissors this time, right? Not the ones that belonged to—" (she mimes a dramatic, mournful obituary) "—the old harbor cat we all thought would be with us forever."

Ivy Morales's eyes find yours, half-concern, half-tease. You let a corner of a grin out because it's easier than naming the thing that sits behind your ribs: the list of losses. The boathouse's roof. Tomas's careful maps. Hours of keeping everyone awake against another forecast.

Marin Sato: "They're in my bag. And no dramatic obituaries today. Unless the kelp decides to stage one."

You laugh, but it's thin; Ivy Morales's smile widens anyway. She hands you seedlings: glassy stems, the pale roots briefly visible against the damp. Your fingers remember how to tuck them into their tiny foam planters, how to knot the twine so the scaffold won't shift when the bay breathes up. The work is small and exact and honest. It anchors you.

[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_2"]
[Sound: The steady hiss of a misting system; the distant metallic thunk of the Harbor's gates being tested.]

Ariana Voss arrives like a change in weather: bright, brisk, a raincoat the color of a clear tideline, hair catching the light. Her boots leave small, deliberate impressions on the planks as she approaches, and the little waterproof camera at her hip bumps softly against her thigh. Her eyes find the layout of the nursery like a cartographer finding coastline.

Ariana Voss: "Morning. These terraces look like they're already breathing. Did you adjust the buoy ballast like we discussed?"

Ariana Voss's voice is all forward motion—hope in motion. She drops a compact device on the bench and pulls out a folded schematic from her tablet, tapping as she speaks. Her fingers are quick; the images project in the dusty air between you: a lattice, kelp velocities, a cross-section of living root mass hugging the engineered face.

You lean over the stained field notebook at your knee, the page with pressed leaves splayed between coffee rings and penciled arcs. The map you've been carrying since last week smells faintly of the boathouse: dust and lamp smoke preserved in fibers. For a beat, you see that sagging cedar roof again — not as a memory but as a small, punched hole in the future you're trying to stitch.

Ariana Voss: "If we modularize the scaffold like this, each segment can be tended by a household or a shop. It grows with the town instead of cutting it off. We can seed kelp nursery pods in sheltered bays and rotate the planters to speed resilience."

Marin Sato: "Rotation helps with disease vectors. And the community-run maintenance model—you're saying—"

Ariana Voss's mouth lifts. She wants the model to be both prototype and proof. There's a rush in her that makes her hands blur; the kind of optimism that can feel like wind in a storm: useful for propelling you forward, dangerous when it starts to buffet the fragile things you hold close.

Ariana Voss: "Exactly. Proof-of-concept, then scale. We pitch a pilot living seawall that doubles as a nursery. We document, we publish, we show funders it's reproducible. It's elegant."

Ivy Morales: "Elegant sounds nice. My abuela would call it 'pretty on paper.' But pretty on paper and surviving a northeast swell are two different things."

The three of you riff across the notebook's sprawl: sketches, scribbled notes, an argument for ballast adjustment, a margin scribble that might be a funding source or a wish. Your fingers trace the loops of the handwriting because touching makes it less transient.

Inside, your chest is a tidepool of contradictions. The plan is right—the mixture of science and soil feels like the exact salt balance Havenport needs. And yet every good plan has the underside of "what if." What if funding ties them to strings they can't cut? What if Tideguard's machines arrive with smiling faces and bulldozers behind them? What if the town's older docks—where generations kept vows with the sea—become collateral in a tidy equation?

You let the anxiety be practical. You ask the knotty questions. You keep the ledger of risks.

Ariana Voss: "We need strong community buy-in. Workshops, hands-on days—get Elder Tomas to tell the stories while we lay the first buoys. If we bring people into the process, it won't feel like something done to them."

You picture Tomas's voice on a bench, the carved stick leaning beside him, the smell of varnish and varnished memory. You remember his line from the boardwalk: make sure it's not just the loud voices. The words sit in your mouth like a warning.

Ivy Morales: "Tomas will come if you promise him an actual cup of tea and not one of Lina's 'energy blends.'"

Lina Park's text arrives as a soft vibration against your thigh. Your phone screen is a small, bright shore in the dim of the nursery: a headline preview pushing into the margin of your day.

[Sound: Phone buzz; a short notification chime.]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_3"]

You don't open it at first. You fold your hands to the page and feel the pressure of your own pulse against your wrist. For a moment you consider tucking your exhaustion into the paper the way you tuck seedlings into foam: neat, covered, out of sight. You think about telling Ariana Voss you can't run another long meeting, another negotiation—how the compass hitched against your throat whenever you imagined Cassian Rhee's polished smile across a table. But you slide the tiredness into the practical again.

Marin Sato: "We schedule two community days and a technical demo before we take anything public. Lina can prep a background piece—stories, not buzzwords. Tomas's stories, Ivy Morales's crew on the boats, local fishermen's input."

Ariana Voss nods fast, eyes bright. "Yes. Stories. Tangible wins. If we can show—"

You feel the words settle in your chest, and they're true and small and heavy: If we can show them they can do this work, they'll keep their place in it.

Ivy Morales: (tilting her head) "Also, we're going to need snacks. If you want turnout, feed them. People vote with their stomachs. Marin, promise me you won't forget the pastries."

Marin Sato: "I will buy pastries. I will also not forget the ballast specs."

Ivy Morales's laugh softens the edge in the morning, but the phone buzzes again. This time, you let the text breathe across the screen.

[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_4"]
[Sound: The morning's background hum seems to tilt, like a line of music changing key.]

The words are a line drawn across the water. Tideguard's press release isn't a plan yet in your notebook—it's a presence that will walk into the room at the next meeting, polished and practiced. You think of Cassian Rhee's firm, the neat corporate pavilion at the edge of town with its glass reflecting spray. You imagine the press release's sleek language: "stabilization," "efficiency," "resilient infrastructure." You imagine the fishermen hearing "stabilization" and thinking of fences at the waterline, the boathouse's demolition, new access fees.

Ariana Voss watches the screen, then looks back up at you. Her expression shifts in a fraction: the bright optimism tempered by a flash of concern. For the first time since she arrived, her hands slow.

Ariana Voss: "We knew they'd notice eventually. If Tideguard's narrative takes root, they'll frame it as the only option. We have to be ready to counter with evidence and people."

Marin Sato: "We already are. The pilot shows a different path. The narrative—our narrative—has to be real and loud."

Ariana Voss's jaw tightens in a way she rarely lets happen. "Real and loud," she repeats, testing the cadence.

You feel the room contracting, the garden's warmth taking on a new, cold undertone. The plan is seed and scaffold and story, but someone else is already shaping the frame of the debate. The thought of the boathouse roof you couldn't save folds inward like a scar.

Your hands tremble when you turn a page where a pressed leaf is stuck between two drawings. The tremor feels like a small betrayal—you, the person who holds other people's safety in your palms, allowed your body to show you were tired. You wanted to tell Ariana Voss: I am not sure I can keep patching and arguing and holding all of this together. But the words are too large for a morning that needs seeds tied and a pilot sold.

You tuck the confession into logistics instead: "I'll draft the outreach plan tonight, send Lina quotes, and get Tomas to the demo. We'll rehearse responses to Tideguard's talking points. We won't let them define the story."

Ariana Voss looks at you, and for a long beat her face is unreadable — not cold, not warm, simply complex, as if she's cataloguing something she can't yet file.

A small wind picks at the shade cloth above, carrying a hint of far-off storm. The generator's hum deepens like a held breath. Ivy Morales eyes both of you and then nods toward the water, where a line of clouds is knitting itself heavier, gray on gray.

Ivy Morales: "Tideguard press release. Storm on the horizon. Maybe the town's being dramatic today."

Marin Sato: "We're the town. We'll be dramatic if we have to."

Ariana Voss's phone is already out; she types quickly, fingers precise, sending Lina a short line: 'We need a framing piece. Interviews today, please. Tomas on the demo; photos of the nursery. Emphasize community governance over corporate contract.' Her message is command braided with plea.

You breathe in the soil and let the bitter-sweetness of the nursery assert itself — this is what you do: build things small and hope they become big enough to hold people through the next tide. The plan is possible. The risks are real. The press release waits to be answered. And beneath those facts, personal fatigue knots tight.

You close the notebook with a measured hand. The pressed leaf leaves a faint green smear on the last page, like a whisper. You stand and the terrace complains beneath your boots.

[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_5"]
[Sound: The garden's sounds dim as the wind rises; a distant foghorn answers a note on the sea.]

You have plans to make, people to call, and a Town Hall meeting looming like a wall you will have to climb. The next decisions will not be about seedlings and snacks but about narratives, contracts, who gets to speak for Havenport. You can feel the meeting there: a door you will open with hands that ache.

It is not the end of the fight. It is the moment before you walk into it.
