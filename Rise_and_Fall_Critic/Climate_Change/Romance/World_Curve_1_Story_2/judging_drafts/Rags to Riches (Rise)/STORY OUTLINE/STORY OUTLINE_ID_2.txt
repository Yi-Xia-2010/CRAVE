## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
Atera is a near-future coastal town (circa 2038) reshaped by repeated floods and warmer seas. Weathered wooden docks sit beside proposed glass-front developments; kelp farms bob near restored reedbeds. Politically, the town is a municipal microcosm: Mayor Amina Bakar mediates between community-led initiatives, youth activism, and outside investors. Economically, fishing and small repairs coexist with nascent eco-tech startups; class lines are subtle but real—older fishers and workshop owners face relocation pressures while developers promise jobs through vertical redevelopment. Culturally, mutual aid and small festivals persist as rituals of resilience. Religion is diffuse — small communal rituals for the sea coexist with secular civic gatherings. Geography compresses into the harbor, the floodplain boardwalk, a rooftop greenhouse above the workshop, and a sleek planning office that overlooks everything. Conflicts center on climate practice and values: living shorelines versus engineered seawalls, preservation versus reinvention. Atmosphere is melancholic but not defeated; the salt breeze carries a persistent hope. Visual style: painterly anime-cel hybrid with muted coastal palette, soft rim lighting, wet-surface reflections, and intimate eco-tech details.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I — Mika Hoshino, a pragmatic repairer and community anchor, must navigate a political storm as Soren Voss, a charismatic urban planner, proposes a high-profile redevelopment to defend Atera, while Elias Maren, a gentle marine biologist, leads low-tech restoration pilots. The emotional arc is Rise: each choice advances Atera toward stability and strengthens my resolve and relationships. The story opens with everyday repair work and weekly town meetings. A pivotal council split forces me to pick a primary path: align closely with Elias’s ecological restoration, collaborate with Soren to broker a hybrid plan, or lead a grassroots campaign that centers local governance and carpentry grit (with Jun and Riv as practical allies). Across branches I make tactical decisions—pilot demonstrations, public negotiations, legal or direct-action strategy—that each yield different kinds of success: ecological resilience, political leverage, community-led governance, or scalable systemic change. Romance threads develop according to trust choices: with Elias (ecological partnership), with Soren (strategic, complicated partnership), or with Jun (community-rooted companionship). Each route ends with constructive outcomes—stabilized shorelines, preserved livelihoods, or a replicable model of resilience—never collapse. The narrative remains first-person, focused on repair, negotiation, love, and the feel of salt under my boots.
