## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Atera

---

## World View
Atera is a near-future coastal town battered and remade by climate change. The sea has encroached on old streets, fishermen now moor beside raised platforms, and reedbeds and engineered kelp forests sit alongside steel-glass proposals for uplifted promenades. Politically, power bubbles between a pragmatic municipal government that needs funding (Mayor Amina Bakar) and corporate-savvy visionaries (Soren Voss) promising a fast fix. Below them, neighbors, carpenters, and activists — people like Jun Park and Ravi “Riv” Delgado — hold informal councils in wet-market squares. Culture prizes mutual aid and hands-on skill; religion is diffuse but often expressed through rituals that honor the sea. Class divisions are visible in the glint of new glass towers versus patched utility jackets at the docks. Geography compresses into a narrow coastal plain with reedbeds, a long boardwalk that floods at king tides, a harbor of patched pilings, and a distant glass tower of development. The atmosphere is melancholic but resolute: salt in the air, wet reflections that catch lamplight, and gatherings that feel like living rooms shared under storm flaps. Love and civic duty are entangled; every romantic choice carries consequences for the town’s future.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mika Hoshino) return daily to tasks at my workshop: repairing nets, tending a rooftop greenhouse lab, monitoring tide sensors via a thin wrist patch. The town faces a decisive vote on Soren Voss’s high-profile Glass Tower redevelopment versus locally-driven restoration led by Elias Maren’s kelp and living-breakwater experiments. Across branching routes, I navigate trust, rivalry, and romance. Choices are moral and intimate: whether I partner with Elias’s patient ecological approach, negotiate a hybrid plan with Soren’s structural solutions, or mobilize grassroots resistance that forces new governance. As a Rags-to-Riches arc, every branch depicts steady progress: small wins scale into collective strength—whether through community science, political compromise, or systemic innovation. The endings vary by degree of communal empowerment and personal fulfillment: a cooperative hybrid solution (Happy), tempered survival with losses (Bittersweet), a hollow economic uplift that damages ecosystems (Sad—kept to a minimally descending tone but overall narrative still climbs toward resilience), or a true systemic breakthrough where Atera becomes a replicable model (True). Throughout, relationships deepen or strain depending on trust, transparency, and my willingness to ask for help.
