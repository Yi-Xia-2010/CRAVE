[Scene: Workshop above the Greenhouse | Early Morning]
[Visual: Show You in a cluttered bench space — tools, braided fishing-line bracelet on wrist, dented metal key on a chain]

You lean over the dented pump with the stubborn concentration you learned from your mother. The pump's casing is rimmed with salt crust and a smear of algae where a previous flood left its calling card. Your fingers know the places to press — a small twist here, a tap there — muscle memory from years of stubborn repair. Your utility jacket hugs your shoulders; the patched elbows are soft with use, the sea-glass strand of hair tucked behind your ear catches the greenhouse light like a promise.

A soft vibration pings against your wrist patch. Tide data, three icons: caution, rising, watch. You thumb the patch; the screen maps a slow, patient curve of the incoming high tide. It's not yet alarm, just a reminder that the sea keeps arriving, no matter the meetings or renderings pinned to town hall corkboards.

You: "Okay. Slow and steady."

You set the pump back into its bracket and tighten the bolts until they stop complaining. The dented key to your mother's workshop — warm from where you've looped it around your neck — rests against the brass of the bench. You don't touch it because touching it feels like opening an old argument you'd rather not start today. Instead you align the rubber gasket and listen: a soft, honest suction when you turn the impeller. It holds.

[Visual: Close-up on wrist patch glowing, small tide vector pulsing]
[Sound: A trampling on the stairs above — Jun's approach]

Jun drops a length of salvaged timber into the corner with a satisfied grunt. Sawdust beads on his flannel like a constellation. He wipes his hands on his cap and flashes you a grin that is mostly business.

Jun: "Harvest was good. Salvage crew found a whole skiff from the north pile. Thought you could use the ribs. Figured you'd want it before Soren's render goes up and everyone forgets where the harbor even was."

You pause, letting the joke sit between you. Soren Voss's render — glossy, clinical, an elevated shoreline like a model in a designer's gallery — margins wide enough to keep floodwater out but narrow enough to make half the town feel like a diorama. The mayor's meeting last night hummed with that same graph-paper certainty.

You: "Thanks. Ribs will hold. Did you bring the steel braces?"

Jun: "In the truck. And the mayor left copies of the plan at the community center." (He hesitates, then) "Balanced, I guess. Looks like progress. But progress with a lot of 'we's and not enough 'us's."

You tighten the bolt on the pump. The sound is a miniature sea — honest, small, repair. You like Jun's bluntness; it keeps your optimism from floating into air. Still, there's something about the way he says Soren's name, like he's testing whether it's a charm or a command.

Jun: "You going to the roof later? Riv was talking about planting new salicornia by the boardwalk. Mayor wanted to hear local proposals before the assembly."

You glance at the greenhouse skylight where condensation beads form and fall, small rain. The rooftop greenhouse is still a secret sacred place — a patch of heat and curated green that smells like tomato leaves and old compost. It's where you test things: salt-tolerant seedlings, improvised drains, a pilot wick-system that saved the west storage last winter.

You: "I'll be up after I finish with the pump. If Riv's planting, I'll bring the braided line; those roots like a bit of movement."

Jun nods, moving to stack timber. The morning light tilts warmer; for a few breaths, the town is a painting of practical hands.

[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: Show steam rising inside glass, rows of trays, small hand-labeled tags]
[Sound: Gentle drip of condensation, distant gulls, boots on metal grating]

You climb the narrow ladder to the greenhouse. The air changes the moment you break the roofline: warmer, heavy with peat and salt, alive. The hydroponic trays hum quietly; a tiny ecosystem that smells of lemon balm and wet soil. Your hands brush a row of salt-tolerant seedlings; each stem is a small, defiant thing.

You check the rooftop tide sensor — a jury of small LED lights and a patched antenna your mother once joked looked like a ship's mast. The data's fine, but you recalibrate one probe anyway. Recalibration is a ritual: a quiet insistence that things can be nudged back into balance.

You find a dent in a secondary pump housing and think of last night's conversations — renders, promises, charts. You didn't grow up wanting to be a politician or a developer or a scientist. You fixed things. You mend nets and hearts in the same motion.

Jun shuffles up behind you with a plank across his shoulder, eyes flicking to the town below. He gestures toward the glitter and glass of the development office on the hill like he's indicating a different weather system.

Jun: "Soren says the town will be 'elevated' — architectural elevation and economic. Beautiful words. But Riv says it sounds like displacing people into new models of 'resilience.'"

You: "Beautiful words are cheap. Repairs aren't." (You stop and catch yourself, softer.) "They're necessary."

Jun rests the plank and helps you loop a new gasket. The two of you make small reparative motions, hands working in sync, and the greenhouse fills with the rhythm of maintenance: an honest music.

[Scene: Harbor & Lower Docks | Night — Incoming Tide]
[Visual: Docks under a low, roiling sky; sodium lamps reflected in wet planks]
[Music: Swelling strings with a resilient motif]
[Sound: Water lapping with force, boards creaking, distant shouts]

Night finds the harbor with a new complexion: salt-sheer reflections, lamps stroking the water into gold. The tide is a living thing tonight — louder, bolder, pushing past its usual line. Your wrist patch vibrates with urgency: a watch advisory and a flood-curve that climbs like a question mark. People are still moving about: lanterns, tarps, the rustle of neighbors helping neighbors.

You step onto the lower docks. Each board is a note in the town's history: a deep gouge here from an old anchor, a painted storm line there that marks last winter's indignation. The wind has teeth tonight; it tugs at your jacket and combs your hair into a wet halo. The smell is sharp — brine, crushed kelp, old diesel — and underneath it, something green and hopeful: the faint, sweet scent of kelp being farmed, a reminder of what lives beneath.

The first surge comes as a soft roar and a slap of cold that makes you swallow. Boards shudder. You wade through the salt-slick planks, boots thudding; water finds the weakest seams and claims them for a while. You feel your resolve in muscle and bone: exhaustion threaded with stubbornness. This is why you stayed.

You: "All hands on the lower moorings! Secure the skiffs!"

A shout from the edge: Jun, turning a crate into a makeshift break against the worst of the water. He meets your eye and gives a brief nod — tired, but fierce.

[Sound: Footsteps running up the ramp, someone laughing breathless]
[Visual: Elias appears at the head of the dock, hair plastered to his forehead, field notebook soaked and dripping]

Elias pushes through with the drenched field notebook clutched to his chest like a talisman. His sea-blue eyes are bright with a weathered hope and tired calculation, the lines around them making a map of sleepless curiosity.

Elias: "Sorry. I ran out trying to get samples. The kelp ropes showed less surge— a pilot down at Hori Point. It wasn't much — a prototype — but it slackened the wave energy by measurable amounts."

You study him; his coat has a salt crust, his hands smell faintly of algae and antiseptic. He's always carried that mix: science and something almost devotional about the sea.

You: "Measured amounts?"

Elias: (leans in, voice tight with a quiet urgency) "Enough to matter. Not in a render, not in a ribbon-cutting. But in the places where boats can stay tied to their moorings. Places where people's homes don't get waterlogged every other week."

You let the words sit. You feel the tug: Soren's elevated plan that promises immediate, visible protection and tidy ledgers; Elias's kelp pilot, slow, community-scale, a living defense that could regenerate and support local livelihoods.

Elias: (softly) "If we scale the kelp lines along the shallows and bind them to living breakwaters, we could reduce the surge before it hits the boardwalk. It won't be overnight, but it's a model that keeps people here."

You: "Soren's plan isn't 'overnight' either. It just looks like a clean overnight in brochures." (You square your shoulders.) "And it costs things we can't just print back."

Elias: (a small, rueful smile) "Brochures are persuasive. People like a picture. But pictures don't reclaim lost fishing rights. They don't teach kids how to tend a kelp bed."

You exchange a look that says more than words: the town is both a map and a memory, stitched with policy and pots of soup. Your chest warms with something that is neither fear nor surrender but a pragmatic, rising resolve.

Jun wanders close, helping a neighbor tie down a tarp. You all work in a choreography born of practice: rope here, tightened there, voices raised in quick instructions. The flood is not a spectacle but a test. You feel tiredness like a physical thing, heavy as wet rope, but beneath it a buoyant current of community — hands pulled together, one after another.

[Visual: A distant light on the boardwalk — a phone glows; Mayor Amina's name flashes across the screen]
[Sound: The quiet beep of a call notification]

Your phone buzzes against your hip. Mayor Amina's name blooms on the screen — a warm, steady presence even on a small display. You swipe to answer.

Amina Bakar: (voice warm, a line of fatigue under her practiced steadiness) "Mika. The town knows the docks are messy tonight. I wanted to tell you first — I'm calling a town assembly. We need every proposal on the table. Soren will present his plan; community groups will present alternatives. We need voices from people who know how to hold a pump and who know how to coax a seed to grow."

You listen to the cadence of her words. There is exhaustion beneath them, yes, but also a clarity that sharpens like a tool: an invitation and a demand. The assembly will be a mirror. It will require you to stand in public, to ask questions that matter, to weigh a legacy against living flesh.

Amina Bakar: "I want you there, Mika. You're one of the people who keeps this place together. Can you come and speak for what the community actually needs?"

You feel everything in your chest tighten — loyalty to the town, the ache of personal life half-paused on a workbench, the tender, ridiculous possibility of asking for help. You imagine the greenhouse, the kelp seedlings, Jun's patient hands, Elias's damp notebook. For the first time since the meeting notices were stapled to the telephone pole, your goal resolves into sharper shape: protect our social fabric, but not at the cost of the people who make it.

You: "I'll be there."

Amina's laugh is a small, relieved thing. "Good. I'll see you then. And Mika— bring that stubbornness of yours. We need it."

You lower the phone and look out over the flooded lower docks. The water hums against pilings like a distant chorus. You taste salt and iron and something like possibility on your tongue.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand closing around the dented key; your wrist patch shows the tide curve leveling]
[Music: Piano lifts into a warmer chord — resolve tempered with hope]

You stand there, the night pressing wet and honest around you. There's no solution yet, only choices: engineering elevation that promises fast, visible change; slow, living restoration that builds resilience from the bottom up. Both have merit. Both demand sacrifice. Both will need voices in the assembly.

The wind dies down for a beat, as if the town itself is listening.

You breathe, weight settling into a new kind of steadiness — a decision to show up, to argue, to hold repair and relationship at once.

[Scene Transition: The boardwalk lamps flicker, drawing a line toward the town hall where tomorrow's assembly will be held]
[Music: Fades into a soft anticipatory swell]

You feel the page-turn in your chest: tomorrow is not an end but the start of a conversation you cannot avoid. You tuck the dented key under your palm, a small anchor, and walk back toward the greenhouse and the workbench and the people who will lean on you.
