[Scene: Voss Development Office | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Pan across a bright, clinical lobby. Chrome surfaces, minimalist chairs, a suspended model of the coastline rotating slowly in a glass case.]
[Sound: Low HVAC hum, the soft click of a projector; distant gulls muffled by glass.]
[Music: Warm, ascending piano motif underscoring cautious optimism]

You step into the Voss office with the harbor still layered on your skin — salt and a faint tar scent that clings to the cuffs of your jacket. The chrome eats that smell and leaves you looking impossibly out of place: patched elbows against polished steel, handprints where others wear a businesslike polish.

Soren Voss meets you halfway across a long table, his reflection cut by a filament of sunlight. He wears that careful smile that folds his pale-gray eyes into something almost soft, and when he lifts his wrist his data-bracelet projects translucent zoning models over the table like a constellation of possible futures.

Soren Voss: "Mika. Thank you for making the trip. I know meetings like this can feel—"

You hear him, the practiced cadence, but you're already arranging words in your head like tools on a workbench. You want to keep this from becoming a press release. You want specifics: jobs, buffers, seats at the table for the people who will live with these changes.

You: "I'm here because this isn't theoretical for us. If you're talking about raising streets, I need to know who builds them, who owns them afterward, and how the shoreline stays alive while the streets go up."

Soren's projection shifts, a timeline flicking from five to ten to twenty years. Steel lines become raised avenues and shaded corridors marked 'resilience corridor.'

Soren Voss: (patient) "We can schedule local hiring quotas into the contracts. We can stage construction to minimize displacement. Those are deliverables. But understand—funding hinges on measurable metrics, timelines, and a credible engineering plan."

Amina Bakar stands to your right, her silver braid wrapped with a thin ribbon that seems to steady the room. She folds her hands as if trying to keep words contained until the right moment.

Amina Bakar: "Credible plans. Yes. We also need assurances that ecological interests aren't an afterthought. Mika, Soren—if this is going to work, it has to be clear on paper."

Elias sits a little apart, his sea-blue eyes fixed on the kelp glyph that Soren's model overlays along the coast. There's an ocean of things he could say—experimental offsets, kelp anchoring systems—and he chooses the phrase that aims for partnership.

Elias: "I can design living shoreline elements that integrate with the raised terraces. They'll reduce wave energy and provide jobs for local harvest and monitoring. If you allow pilot plots, I can show data within a season."

You want to hear that commitment in numbers, not just watercolor promises. You press.

You: "Pilot plots that actually inform contract milestones. Not 'we'll look into it.' Clause language. Enforcement. And community oversight."

Soren's mouth tightens, but he nods almost imperceptibly. The room narrows to the table, the projected model, the list of deliverables. You learn quickly which levers make him lean in: timelines and legality.

Soren Voss: (leaning forward) "Contracts with enforcement are standard. I can include performance-based triggers: if the living shoreline fails to meet attenuation thresholds over X months, funding reallocates to remediation rather than new construction."

Elias exhales, the sound soft and slightly relieved, but his brow keeps knitting.

Elias: "We should define those thresholds together. Wave attenuation, biomass accumulation rates—"

Jun's voice comes over a softly buzzing speakerphone; he's been on call since you left the harbor. His tone is a rough cut of practicality.

Jun (speaker): "Don't let them put the work in the fine print and the money out of reach. Keep the labor clauses tight. And Mika—watch timelines that let contractors eat the town."

You: "Agreed. Local employment quotas with staged oversight. Community hiring checkpoints after each phase."

Soren's projection slides again, this time to a Gantt-like view. It looks sterile, but it is useful; it speaks in terms Soren respects—and increasingly, you do, too.

Soren Voss: "You speak my language. That will make integrating your demands into a fundable model easier. We can add community oversight positions to the schedule—paid roles, with veto power over certain permit changes."

For a beat, all of you measure the improbability of that sentence and how much weight it might carry if it were not just a talking point. Outside, beneath the tower's glass, a ribbon of protesters snakes along the plaza—Riv's bright bandana visible even through the titanium skin of the building. You hear the distant chant through the glass like tide under a pier.

[Scene: Tower Bluff Viewing Ledge | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Floor-to-ceiling glass; the town drops away in a patchwork of rooftops and tidal marks. A model of the raised bluff sits between you, delicate and precise.]
[Sound: The low creak of the building, gull calls more distant; a breeze picks up at the ledge.]
[Music: Gentle strings rise, suggesting small forward momentum]

Soren gestures to a larger hologram—this one projected into space above the bluff—streets lifted like boardwalks, terraces sloping down into a living breakwater of marsh and kelp. It is easier to feel possibility in three dimensions.

Soren Voss: "Raise the axes here, insert corridors for water flow, and the most vulnerable blocks are spared the worst surges. We can phase neighborhoods so relocation is temporary, assisted. The federal grant programs will favor this model."

You look at the edges where concrete meets green—places where mistakes will be visible for years. You want those edges to be soft. You want them to be jobs, not blights.

You: "If the terraces create work that's seasonal, we need to guarantee year-round income streams—training, maintenance, and processing of kelp products. That's how people stay."

Elias's face brightens at the mention of kelp products; he taps his notebook, sketching supply chains where you can see them forming.

Elias: "Seaweed processing co-ops could provide steady income, especially if we tie them to regional markets. I can draft an initial supply-and-demand schedule."

Jun: (over your shoulder, practical) "And contracts should prefer small local firms first. Don't give big outfits openings to out-bid people who actually live here."

You: "Right. Local-first procurement with audited bid processes."

Soren pauses, assembling language in his head the way others assemble parts.

Soren Voss: "We can write phased procurement preferences. We'll also include a matched-funding clause for training programs—town funds catalyze grants, and grants pay for certifications."

The model seems to breathe a little; the projected kelp bends in imagined currents. Each phrase you add becomes a plank, a bolt, a rubric that keeps the structure tied to the people it must serve.

[Scene: Mika's Workshop / Rooftop Lab | Early Evening]
[Visual: Warm amber grow-lamps glow through the greenhouse glass. Tools hang on pegboard; a dented key catches a sliver of light on the bench.]
[Sound: The soft tap of a hammer, the rustle of leaves in the rooftop breeze, distant chants fading as night approaches.]
[Music: A rising, hopeful motif with quiet percussion—work continuing in measure]

You return to the workshop with pages of notes and a draft clause list that smells faintly of solvent and kelp. The rooftop lab's humidity wraps around you like an old shawl—comfort in green things. Jun and Riv are there, both with different kinds of tired: Jun's resolute, Riv's electric.

Jun: "Don't let them take credit for the workforce training and then hand it off. Keep control."

Riv: "We're not stopping them from building. We're making sure our people get paid and heard. If they slide any clause, we blow it up in public."

You run your fingers over the paper. The language you've been pushing toward—living shoreline buffers, community employment quotas, phased procurement, pilot-triggered metrics, community oversight with paid positions—feels heavy and hopeful at the same time.

Amina arrives from the tower with a quiet, determined air. She sets down a folder and looks at the group like a conductor assessing an ensemble.

Amina Bakar: "If we present a package that balances engineering rigor and ecological integrity, we can shepherd this through council. If we don't, we risk losing both funding and the town's soul."

Soren appears in the doorway—he came to your workshop because Amina asked him to. Outside, people who were protesting are watching the building, but not everyone is angry anymore; some faces are curious, some skeptical, some waiting to see if talk becomes something that looks like care.

Soren Voss: (soft, direct) "I want this to work. I know the optics of 'developer comes in' are bad. I don't expect trust overnight. But I'm willing to put enforceable milestones and community oversight into any contract that moves forward."

You measure the weight of his candor. There is strategy there, yes, but there's also something that might be genuine: a willingness to be ruled by metrics that others set. That is different from steamrolling a town and calling it progress.

You: "Then put it in writing now. Draft the clauses. We'll bring them to the council together—with live metrics from Elias's pilots and a procurement list Jun approves."

A small, fragile smile crosses Soren's face—an almost imperceptible surrender to the notion that a plan needs more than glossy renderings to survive.

Soren Voss: "Agreed. I'll have my legal team draft initial contract language. Amina, we'll coordinate public presentation. Mika—I'll include a clause for paid community oversight positions and firm preferral lists."

Elias: "I'll have the first pilot protocol ready to deploy at the restoration site within six weeks. If we hit the thresholds, that gives the council real data to act on."

Jun: "I'll make sure local firms are ready to bid. And Riv—keep them organized outside. Pressure helps keep promises honest."

Riv grins, bandana flashing.

Riv: "Oh, we will. But quieter now. Let them see us ready to work, not just shout."

You breathe in, tasting the damp of the greenhouse and the faint metal tang of the day. The air feels like the hinge of something; plans that yesterday were a shout in the harbor have now become clauses, deadlines, and a schedule you can point at.

[Music: Crescendo into an optimistic swell]

You find yourself speaking Soren's language—timelines, deliverables, triggers—not because you've abandoned the town's heart, but because you see it's the only way to bind a large plan to local accountability. He listens. You listen to him measure risk against scaffolding. Elias frames science into executable pilots. Jun keeps one eye on the ground truth. Amina folds it into civic procedure.

There is tension—always will be—but the momentum is real. The idea that engineering and ecology can be braided into a single contract no longer feels naive. It feels immediate.

You set the last page on the bench, the draft terms flickering in the lamplight like a promise. Outside, lights in the tower blink as if acknowledging a small victory.

Page-Turn Thought: Tomorrow you will present these terms to the council at dawn—data on the kelp pilots, a procurement schedule that names local hands, enforceable milestones, and a clause that creates paid oversight roles. It's a fragile package, but it is something tangible: a net woven of legal language and neighborly labor. You feel, for the first time in a long while, that the town's future might be negotiated instead of surrendered.
