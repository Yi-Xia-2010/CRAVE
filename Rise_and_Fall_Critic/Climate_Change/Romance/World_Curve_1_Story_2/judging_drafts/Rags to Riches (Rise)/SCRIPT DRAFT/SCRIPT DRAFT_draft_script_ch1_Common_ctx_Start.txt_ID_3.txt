[Scene: Mika's Workshop above the Greenhouse | Late Afternoon]

You breathe in the greenhouse-sweet air rising through the floorboards — faint copper from the pipes, the sharp green of wet leaves, and always a trace of salt. Your hands smell like oil and seaweed. You set the wrench into the pump's grooved seam and listen: the muffled rattle means a displaced impeller; an easy fix if the part isn't too warped.

Your wrist patch pings again — a gentle pulse against skin — tide data blinking up in microtext across the band. The number nudges your chest the way a familiar worry does: expected high in six hours, but pressure readings are low. The patch's soft vibration has the intimacy of a person who knows your schedule. You rub the seam of the dent with a rag and tuck a stray sea-glass strand behind your ear out of habit.

The key to your mother's workshop — dented, brass, tied to a fraying cord — sits in the corner, catching light. It is an old weight of promise and stubbornness. You turn it in your fingers once, twice, like testing a memory. The workshop overhead hums with the greenhouse lights: amber halos on damp glass, rows of salt-tolerant seedlings climbing trellises.

Jun's boots thud on the stairs before you see him. The door shakes with the impact. He drops a stack of salvaged timber against the bench, sawdust puffing like a small storm.

Jun: "You hire me to make you a table or to keep feeding me these mysteries, Mik?"

You glance up, catch the tilt of his cap, the sawdust halo. He smells like cedar and rain. For a second you consider the table, the long list of things you could use rebuilt in town, before the pump pulls your attention back.

You: "Both. The pump first. Then, if you promise not to lecture my irrigation system, I'll commission a stool that doesn't wobble like the mayor's excuses."

Jun snorts, but his eyes flick past the workbench to the stained envelope you didn't bother hiding — the render. A glossy mock-up of Soren Voss's proposal sits folded under a weight of nails: glass towers, raised boardwalks, clean lines that ignore the barnacles. Under bright studio light it almost looks like salvation.

Jun: (softer) "Didn't think they'd bring out the render this fast."

You: "They brought it like a show-and-tell. Mayor said they're listening." You tighten a bolt. "That's the phrasing they like."

Jun: "Huh. 'Listening'—doesn't cost anything to say. Did she mention the tradeoffs, Mik? Or did Soren's pictures eat the rest of the meeting?"

You pause, because the mayor did say "listening," but you also read the room: polished presentations have a way of filling silence. You think of the harbor pylons, the storm lines painted like scars, and the families who've moved their keepsakes to higher shelves.

You: "She ran the meeting clean. But she left space for debate. That's—" You search for the right word. "That's our win."

Jun's jaw loosens at that. He kicks his boot against a pile of reclaimed planks and grins, the way people do when they want to pass a tension into something lighter.

[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Twilight]
[Visual: Condensation halos on the glass, seedling silhouettes]
[Sound: A low gust, the drip of water, distant harbor horns]
[Music: Gentle piano with a hopeful undertow]

You climb the narrow ladder to check the moisture lines before the night. The hydroponic trays hum with amber light; a fresh breeze slips through the vent, carrying the cool taste of salt and the damp reed scent from the boardwalk below. You crouch by the troughs and fit a new seal on the pump, fingers moving with the memory of hundreds of small repairs.

Your wrist patch flickers and displays a pulsing waveform. A message: Mayor Amina will host an assembly tonight at the docks. You let the words sit. An assembly — the town gathered, people deciding in the open — feels like the only chunk of power that really matters.

Before you can tidy up, someone calls from below.

Voice (off): "Mika! You up there?"

You look down to see Elias climbing the side ladder, backpack slung crooked, hair damp as if he'd walked through a fog. He holds a field notebook that has taken on the sea's temperament — pages dark with salt. He smiles anyway, small and apologetic, eyes the deep sea-blue of someone who reads currents like weather.

Elias: (breathless) "Sorry. The—sorry, didn't mean to startle. The kelp pilot's paperwork looked worse than it is. And—" He stops, like there are too many small things to choose from. "I thought I'd find you here."

You: "You always do, at least when you're not knee-deep in tide tables."

Elias laughs softly. He tucks a wet curl behind his ear, and when he reaches the roof his fingers brush lightly against a tray of salt-sprayed seedlings like a ritual.

Elias: "We had a small win today. The pilot near Gull's Bay reduced surge by a noticeable fraction after a couple of months. It's not dramatic, but it's measurable. I—" He opens the notebook, showing you a smear of ink and a tidy diagram of swaying lines. "We can scale it. If the town wants a demonstrable thing, it's there."

You study the diagram. The kelp lines are patient — slow to grow, slow to change the ocean's mind the way a seawall does overnight. Still, seeing the raw data makes something in your chest unclench.

You: "If it's measurable, it's a start." Your fingers find the dented key and you turn it in your hand without thinking. "But the question is: will the town accept gradual wins? Or will they be sold on the quick fix?"

Elias: (pensive) "Quick fixes buy time but not necessarily futures. Kelp binds sediment, nurtures fishers, and can be harvested sustainably. The job training alone... it gives people work. I don't think it's either/or." He meets your eyes, earnest. "I know that sounds idealistic. But I've mapped the currents. There are places where small restoration could actually blunt the worst of the tide's teeth."

You consider his tone — not naive but full of a scientist's quiet insistence. It's the kind of hope that comes with notes and measurements. It is, in its own way, also practical.

[Scene: Harbor & Lower Docks | Night — Following the Tide Alarm]
[Visual: Streetlamps cutting wet paths; reflections breaking like glass]
[Sound: A high, urgent ping from the wrist patch; distant shouts; the slap of water]
[Music: Tempo picks up to a determined, hopeful rhythm]

Your wrist patch vibrates — a different pattern, sharper. The tide's curve is spiking earlier than predicted. The workshop lights blur as you swing down the ladder, Jun already moving like a practiced response. The smell of the harbor is stronger here: fish, tar, and the metallic scent of seawater invading places it shouldn't.

Jun: "King tide. Lower docks are going to take it first."

You don't have time to put on gloves. You grab the pump and the seal you just fixed and sprint across the boardwalk. Water licks at your boots with a cold, clean bite. Planks slick underfoot. Around you, lights bob as people emerge from houses and warehouses in their jackets, boots, and lanterns. Everyone moves with a familiar choreography: secure the goods, hoist the nets, help the ones who can't do heavy lifting.

A truck idles with its engine running, piled high with belongings. A child clutches a tin toy, eyes wide but trusting in the adults. The sound of community organizing — sharp orders, quick jokes, hands passing sandbags — is its own kind of music.

You wade further to the lower docks, the water halfway up the knees of your rubber boots. The tide is a silvered thing under sodium lights. You push against it, met with the stubborn weight of a change you refused to pretend wasn't coming.

[Sound: A voice calling out — low, urgent. Amina Bakar's boots on wet boards]
[Visual: Mayor Amina stepping under the dock lamp, her braid wrapped in a scarf, the rain beading off her coat]

Mayor Amina: (calling) "Mika! Elias! Jun!"

Her voice cuts through the wind like a bell. She climbs onto the dock with practiced care and surveys the crowd, then looks at you specifically, like she's counting people and choices all at once.

Amina: "This isn't a storm we can talk about later." She folds her hands into her coat to stop them from shaking. "People are shaken up. Soren's team is already saying they can raise infrastructure quickly. Elias brought data on living shorelines. We need a meeting tonight — not another talk, but a real assembly where the town's voice decides what we consider acceptable risk and what we refuse to lose."

You swallow. The dock smells of wet wood and sea-salt breath. Around you, the town does not look broken so much as stirred awake.

Amina: "I want everyone there. The council hasn't decided yet, but I am calling a town assembly tonight at the harbor. We need plans, we need proposals, and we need the people. Mika, I'm asking you to bring what you have: your workshop's perspective, your connections, the greenhouse experiments. People listen to you."

Her look is not a demand so much as the passing of a baton. It is a trust fragile with expectation.

You: "Tonight? With this tide—" Your voice roughens for a beat, but the thought is already building. "I'll bring Jun, the seedlings, the patched pump if it survives the night. And Elias' data."

Elias steps forward, shoes squelching, the soaked notebook clutched in one hand like a map to a future planet. "I'll bring people who can testify about the pilot. I'll bring kelp samples and the drone footage. This isn't just talk. We can show—"

Amina: (smiling, tired) "Good. Bring proof and people. We need practical options and real lives at the table." She looks around the assembled neighbors, then back to you. "We don't have the luxury of ivory-tower perfection. We have the luxury — if you can call it that — of stubbornness, skilled hands, and each other. Use them."

You feel something shift under your sternum: responsibility, sure, but softened by the warmth of being entrusted. The tide tries to push at your boots, but the town moves in counterweight. People hand off sandbags like passing a hymn book.

[Sound: Quiet conversation; children's low laughter; the tide's slow thrum]

You stand near the rail, the salt spray cooling your face. The greenhouse lights glow behind you, a small beacon. The dented key is in your pocket; your wrist patch displays the assembly time and a list of initial agenda items. You feel the familiar urge to retreat into the workshop and fix pumps all night — the impulse to do the work alone — but tonight there's a new countervoice: what if you don't have to carry it all?

Elias: (soft) "Whatever happens, I'm with you on this. Not because I know the best solution, but because I believe we can make something that holds both people and sea together."

Jun: "And I'll bring the planks." He taps the stack at his feet. "And opinions. Lots of those."

You laugh, and the sound is small but buoyant. Around you, neighbors cluster with warm scarves and strong hands. Riv (out of sight) is already scribbling protest signs and training people to anchor the kelp lines. The town is not a passive audience tonight; it's a chorus.

A sharp breath, a tide-bright thrill — fear and hope braided together. The mayor's call is a hinge. You can feel the weight of choices to come, but under that weight also the lift of possibility: that this could be the start of something built together.

Your muscles ache from the run, from the pump's stubbornness and the salt, but there is an answer forming in your chest, not yet chosen, but gathering shape: protect the town, keep people in place, find work that pays, and keep your own life from being only work. Love is folded into it somewhere — not announced, but present in moments like this: when Elias offers maps and steady hands, when Jun offers humor and timber, when your mother’s key feels warm in your pocket.

You look at the harbor one more time. The lights are scattered like small, defiant constellations across water. You breathe, and the world seems to expand with the possibility that choices here will mean more than survival. They might mean a future.

You hitch the strap of your bag over your shoulder and gather the pump pieces. The town sulks and sings in the same breath; a tide that might ruin crops also flushes clean the air for new beginnings. You don't have the answers, but you have a plan to be in the room where the answers begin.

You think of the assembly board: proof, people, proposals. You think of the render hidden under nails and the kelp diagrams smudged with salt.

For a moment, you let yourself imagine the result — not the final outcome, just the first collective step: voices rising, hands shaping plans together, practical seedlings planted in policy as much as in the sea.
