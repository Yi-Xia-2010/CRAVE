[Scene: Voss Development Office | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot through floor-to-ceiling glass — the harbor glitters low and distant, tide lines like faint ledger marks. Soren's office is orderly; renderings glow on a wall screen. A slim stack of printed memoranda lies between you and him.]
[Music: Warm strings with a steady, hopeful pulse]
[Sound: Quiet HVAC hum, occasional gull calls filtered through glass, the soft scratch of a pen]

The glass is cool under your fingertips when you rest your palm on the edge of the table. You breathe in and the room tastes faintly of sea-salt and coffee; beyond the tower the town leans with its old rhythms — boat bells, a child laughing somewhere on the boardwalk. Here, the air carries different acoustics: legal diction, projected schedules, a promise you can measure in line items.

Soren slides the memorandum across to you like a map. The paper has been through legal eyes; clauses bloom in small, confident fonts. He watches you with those pale gray eyes — direct, waiting. Across from you is Elias, notebook open, pen hovering. Mayor Amina sits steady, hands folded like a judge considering precedent. Jun leans against the wall, shoulders braced as if he can hold a pier upright by will alone. Riv drums rhythm on his knee — nervous energy that smells like worn canvas and paint.

You think of the harbor you know from splintered planks and lamplight. You think of your mother's workshop keys heavy in your pocket. The memorandum is not the finish line; it's the hinge. How you shape it will decide how the town breathes for years.

Soren: "We can get the funding committed within a quarter. I can promise timelines and advanced mobilization. But we need signatures on this initial memorandum to unlock the next tranche."

You: "Promises are only as good as what they're written to do. Tell me where the stewardship and remediation lines are."

Soren shifts, fingers folding into an almost-imperceptible knot. He points at a clause halfway down — 'Environmental Compliance Protocol' — but the language is measured and bureaucratic.

Soren: "Those are in Section 4. We'll have independent audits and scheduled remediation checkpoints. The implementation firms are pre-vetted."

Elias: (soft, insistent) "Independent audits sound good on paper. We need metrics we can monitor ourselves — kelp biomass targets, turbidity thresholds, a community seat on oversight. Otherwise audits become a calendar item, not an answer."

The words make the air thicker in the room, but not hostile — concentrated. You're aware of how much weight sits on simple phrasing.

You: (quietly) "If oversight is only advisory, it won't stop the bulldozers when money moves faster than care. We need teeth in this. Clauses that bind, not suggestions."

Soren: (leaning forward, steady) "I know what 'teeth' looks like. I've built flood walls and raised streets. But legal teeth can stall funding. We have leverage only if we move fast."

Amina: "We cannot trade speed for durable safety. The town will not forgive tokenism. But we also can't let fear of stall lose us the whole project."

Jun: (rough laugh) "Great, so we either get nothing for ten years or get a nice wall and a dead shoreline. Wonderful."

Riv: (offhand, hopeful) "There are middle grounds. We can make sure apprenticeships and rehiring clauses aren't optional."

You let the lines of text on the memorandum blur for a second. Translation: who enforces, who sits at the table, and what happens if the numbers don't add up. You feel the reflex to draft every line yourself, to test each clause like a repaired net for holes. But there are faces — Elias's steady patience, Amina's careful exhaustion, Jun's bluntness, Riv's flame. This is work that can be shared.

The paper is a battleground of italics and subclauses, but the tone is progressively cooperative. Soren's jaw eases; he is practiced at this litany of give-and-take. Elias presses a pen to his notebook and begins sketching metrics in shorthand you can read — kelp biomass, turbidity, community hours logged — a scientist's list that hums with possibility.

Elias: "A kelp pilot with clear targets could be proof-of-concept. If we set thresholds and tie funding release to ecological milestones, we can both protect and mobilize."

Soren: "You want milestones that might delay work. I want deployment that starts quickly. What if we phase money release: some immediate for infrastructure, the rest contingent on ecological milestones?"

You lean back and let the proposal rotate in your head. Contingency funding. Phased trust. It feels like a bridge rather than a compromise that cuts the rope.

You: "Phased funds could work — as long as the milestones are enforceable and the community has authority to certify them. Not just auditors who tick boxes, but people who know the kelp beds and can call foul when something's wrong."

Soren's composure cracks a fraction. He reaches into his pocket, a motion that looks oddly hesitant from someone used to commands. He pulls out a small, worn photograph, tucking the corner with both fingers before laying it face up on the glass. A girl, younger than either of you — hair in a messy braid, sun on her cheek; a smile that makes the image ache.

Soren: (voice low) "My sister used to fish off that jetty until the tides took the slipway and then her job. I... I don't want 'my model' to be another band-aid. I don't want to be the planner who raises a street and leaves the life below it to die."

The confession lands differently than a corporate pledge. It's not a public relations line. It is private and human and unexpected, and you feel, for a heartbeat, the tilt of something larger than contracts.

You: (softening, but firm) "Then make that not just a line in your head but a rule on paper. If the plan's going to carry your name, make it enforceable by the people who live those lives. No more 'we'll fix it later.' Bind it now."

Soren looks at you, the gray of his eyes rawer. He closes his hand briefly over the photograph and then slides it back into his wallet like a talisman.

Soren: "I can commit to legally binding remediation and a community stewardship board with actual seats. But such a model changes timelines and requires more structure. It isn't a minor amendment."

Amina: (decisive) "Then we decide what risks we're willing to shoulder. If this governance board exists, we write its authority clearly. If we ask for speed, we accept less direct control. The town can't have both without risking either."

Jun: "We can write teeth in and still move fast if someone's got the cojones to hold people to them. That's the part I worry about — who's got the cojones?"

Riv: (bright, practical) "We do. We watch. We make sure those seats are filled by locals with recall powers. The dashboard is public. The kid who fixes your radio can see kelp biomass updates. Make it transparent."

You feel the shape of a third path drift forward — not just yes or no, but a hybrid: Soren's resources with legally binding local authority. It's complex. It asks for trust to be formalized and power to be shared. It asks something of you, too: the willingness to keep pressing, to structure a system rather than simply vetoing.

The afternoon light shifts. On the glass, the harbor's reflection ripples as if answering. The meeting becomes an architecture of motions: who writes the first draft, who calls the lawyers, who strings the community into the process. It doesn't feel like capitulation. It feels like work that, for the first time in a long while, could be done together.

Soren: (a small, honest smile) "I don't want this to be my name on a monument to short-sightedness. If we build this right, it could be a model."

Elias: (gently) "And if we build it right, the kelp beds can return life to those slips. Each metric will be a small victory, and those victories will make the town's safety real."

Amina: "Then we need language. We need milestones, funding triggers, and a governance board charter. Bring me drafts by tomorrow evening. The council will want time to review."

Jun: "And we need boots on the docks to start the pilot. Names, not promises."

Riv: "And a public dashboard. Make it a thing people can check every day."

You rest your hand on the warm glass, feeling the pulse of the town beneath the surface. The memorandum is a start. It's imperfect and necessary. It pulls possibilities into plans.

You close your eyes for a second and picture the harbor again — raised streets with kelp-swathed pilings, apprentices in your workshop learning with mud on their hands, people on the stewardship board who know the tides by smell and story. There is work ahead. There are big papers and legal drafts and fights to be had. And there is, finally, movement.

You open your eyes and all at once the options present themselves like tide lines: insist on strict clauses and oversight; back Soren for speed and funds; or write a conditional joint governance model that forces shared power but needs structure and trust.

[Visual: The tower recedes behind you; the harbor blinks below. Lanterns begin to glow along the boardwalk. The wind smells of cooling tide and wet earth.]
[Music: Rising strings, hopeful and steady]
[Sound: Distant conversation, gulls settling, a soft tide pull]

You stand on the bluff, the memorandum's weight balanced in your mind between principle and pragmatism. The town waits for what you will choose to push into the draft. Whatever you decide will steer the needle — toward more control and slower certainty, toward speed and faith in later fixes, or toward a harder, more collaborative path that reshapes power.
