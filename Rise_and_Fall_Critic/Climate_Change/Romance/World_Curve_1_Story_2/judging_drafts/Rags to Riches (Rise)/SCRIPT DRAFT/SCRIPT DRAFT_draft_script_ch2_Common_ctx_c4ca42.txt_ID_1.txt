You step up onto the raised floor and the air opens—salt, oil, hot tea, and the faint green reek of seaweed from the nets below. The crowd presses close: fishers in oilskins, volunteers with damp scarves, Riv bouncing on the balls of his feet, Jun a hulking shadow by the railing. Mayor Amina stands at the center, braid coiled and scarves catching the light like small flags. She breathes in, then looks out at everyone with the kind of exhausted steadiness that steadies others.

Mayor Amina: "Tonight isn't about a single solution. It's about who we keep when the water keeps coming. Speak plainly. Be specific. And—" (she lets the word hang) "—be honest about what you can carry."

A cheer—and then an immediate hush as faces turn toward the far corner. A figure parts the group like a skiff through fog: Soren Voss. The weather-treated trench coat catches the warehouse lights, a faint shimmer rippling off the fabric. He moves with an engineered grace, the kind that makes people think of finished plans and neat spreadsheets.

[Visual: Close shot of Soren's hand dropping a slim card case on the rail; it opens with a tiny metallic click, the engraved business cards glinting]
[Sound: A half-smile in the crowd; someone mutters, "Straight from the tower."]

Soren Voss: (smiling) "Mayor Amina. Friends of Atera. I'm grateful—truly—for the chance to discuss a plan that can stop the worst of the tides and bring work back to this town."

His voice is smooth; his gray eyes scan the room like blueprints. For a moment the models at his side—a glossy stack of renderings—seem to promise order: raised promenades, neat barriers, lights that keep the sea at bay. Some faces relax. Others harden.

Elias steps forward with a portable projector and a field notebook clutched to his chest. He looks tired in a different way—like someone who's slept at the lab and woken to tide charts. When the first slides bloom on the opposite wall, the images are underwater: kelp strands, juvenile fish darting through, a grid of small buoys that anchor pilot plots.

[Visual: Projector light washes the audience in green-gold; the smell of algae rises faintly as if the image itself were a tide]
[Sound: A low, curious murmur; someone says, "Pretty."]

Elias: (voice steadying as he finds the words) "Living shorelines aren't just pretty, they're productive. Kelp reduces wave energy, it sequesters carbon, and—" (he flips a page) "—we've seen a twenty-percent increase in local juvenile fish counts in three months on the pilot line. Not tomorrow's miracle—but a resilience that grows if we let it."

His hands tremble when he gestures to a chart. You notice the pendant at his throat—a tiny vial of bioluminescent algae catching the projector light like a private lighthouse.

Riv: (piping up) "We can do this. We can put in the farms, get the kids out there. It's ours. We don't have to sign over our shore."

A clump of people shout assent. A different cluster—older fishers, hands like weathered knots—exchange looks; you can read their worry in the way they cross their arms.

Jun comes up beside you and gives your shoulder a gentle, almost admonishing nudge. His breath smells like sawdust and brine.

Jun: (low) "Say it, Mik. Say what matters. Don't let them drown the nets—literally and figuratively."

You can feel the pull between two images: Soren's concrete prose and Elias's slow, luminous repair. Both offer protection. Both ask for something. You find your voice in the middle of the boardwalk's timber heartbeat.

You: "We need solutions that keep people working. Nets mend, boats that can still launch at dawn, and dignity in how decisions get made. If the plan moves people inland or makes the harbor sterile—" (you pause, feeling the weight of every life you can name) "—that's not saving Atera. That's replacing it."

The room quiets. Mayor Amina’s eyes soften, not because she agrees, but because she knows the truth in your words.

Mayor Amina: "Practical, clear. Good. We can test pilot engineering and living shorelines together—if we volunteer to negotiate terms and oversee the pilots. Who will step forward to be on the committee?"

You: "Dawn means an early boat, Elias. You sure we—"

Elias: (smiles, awkward and open) "I'll bring coffee. And a tethered drone. And some sandwiches. It's small—it's messy—but it's concrete. I want you there because you understand livelihoods. You can tell me if I'm missing the human part."

He leaves a space for you to refuse, to make an excuse. Your chest tightens at the invitation; the easier path is to keep threading the same small repairs alone. The better path—messy, collaborative—feels like matching hands.

Riv bounces up on the balls of his feet, scanning faces like a scout. His bandana is bright enough to cut through the twilight.

Riv: "If Elias can show us a living shoreline that nets us food and work, we mobilize. We run workshops, get the kids on the lines, write up wins. We show Soren this can be scaled without erasing who we are."

Soren has drifted closer, unspooling one of his glossy renderings. He doesn't push; he lets the paper speak. When he finally leans in, his voice is a different octave—quiet now, strategically intimate.

Soren Voss: "Mika—may I? A few moments, privately. I don't want to make a show of it, but there are operational details I can walk you through. Funding sources, timelines. If you can see the model up close, you might understand why scale matters."

You feel a hundred threads tug—trust in measured plans, suspicion of polished fixes, the pull of safety. Soren's eyes are unreadable in their precision; for a beat they might be assessing vulnerabilities or making calculations about what to promise.

Jun stands near, hands thrust into pockets, watching the small exchanges. He breathes out like someone who has been holding his own tide. Around you, the assembly begins to coalesce into action: volunteers trading numbers, Riv sketching a plan for youth outreach on a torn envelope, a fisherman offering space for testing equipment.

Mayor Amina: "Alright. Committee volunteers will meet at first light. We will draft terms for both pilot engineered defenses and living shoreline expansion, and we will present them to the council together. No unilateral commitments. No deals made without the town."

The line of work feels suddenly reachable—messy, communal, heavy and alive. You can imagine a map where Soren's hard lines and Elias's soft green patches overlap, not perfectly, but a place to start.

You: (thinking) I don't have to shoulder this alone. I don't have to decide what survival looks like in one breath. If we can test, if we can measure and negotiate—maybe that's where the compromise lives; maybe it's where love for this place translates into something practical.

Jun: "I'll be in the room. I'll say the uncomfortable things. You don't have to be the angry voice, Mik. You can be the one who knows how things get done."

Elias: (softly) "And I'll show you what's growing under the surface. You don't have to take my word for it."

Soren: (from across the group, with that precise smile) "I'll leave the formal renderings with the committee. Transparency. No surprises."

You feel the tug of your old reluctance—the one that kept you from asking for help, from letting others carry a plank. It loosens, an unfamiliar ache like a seam being opened to patch something better.

A tide of small agreements ripples through the crowd—meeting times, volunteers, names traded like ropes. The night is full of beginnings rather than endings.

You breathe in the harbor air, and for once the weight feels shared.
