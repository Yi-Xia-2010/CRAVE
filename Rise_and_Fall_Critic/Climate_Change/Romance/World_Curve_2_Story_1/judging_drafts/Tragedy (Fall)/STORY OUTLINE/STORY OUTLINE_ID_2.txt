## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Ash and Salt

---

## World View
Corvena Bay sits on a jagged throat of coastline where seawalls, salt-brown concrete, and tentative green infrastructure rub shoulders with reclaimed boardwalks and rooftop gardens. Once a fishing and shipbuilding town, it is now a near-future coastal city ten to fifteen years into rising seas: managed retreat plans, living breakwaters, desalination mist and rationed resources are everyday realities. Political power centers in City Hall tussle with grassroots cooperatives that repurpose wrecked vessels and plastic-haunted beaches into community infrastructure. Religion and ritual persist as neighborhood rites at The Basin and the Old Beacon; elders like Rosa Alvarez keep memory and law alive. Class lines have hardened: families with engineering knowledge or municipal connections cluster toward higher ground; the most vulnerable cling to the Strand and the Basin. Climate science is respected but contested—data-driven municipal planning (Jun Park and Councilwoman Lian Mor) collides with Elias Navin’s regenerative ecology and Mara Kestrel’s community-led consensus. The atmosphere is humid salt air, mottled low clouds, and streaked sunlight after storms. Visuals are painterly and weathered: sea greens and rust-orange, soft film grain and character-focused close-ups that show salt-streaked faces and frayed clothes as much as political screens and tide forecasts.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mara Kestrel) lead a neighborhood co-op fighting to preserve our people and traditions as sea levels rise. The story is a tragedy: each decision, made from empathy or necessity, spirals into increasingly painful consequences. The early chapters set the scene—my stubborn protection of the low-lying Strand, Elias Navin’s patient promise of living breakwaters, and Jun Park’s pragmatic municipal mandate for managed retreat and engineered barriers. A pivotal council moment forces me to choose between faith in grassroots ecology, trust in municipal order, or a precarious compromise. Each branch deepens loss: rushed scaling of Elias’s experiments yields catastrophic failure; siding with Jun implicates me in forced displacement; attempting compromise invites sabotage and political betrayal. Romantic tensions with Elias are inseparable from professional stakes—our intimacy grows and frays alongside field trials, council hearings, and storms. Secondary figures—my sister Ivy, elder Rosa, technician Theo—anchor moral debate, but nothing halts the downward arc. Every route ends in ruin of some kind: drowned neighborhoods, fractured love, or regret that tastes like salt. The narrative is a continuous fall: small failures compound into tragedy.

### Chapter 1: Salt and Ledger [Fall]

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)  
* **Summary:** I wake before dawn on the Strand, fingers stained with marsh mud and ink from my battered leather field journal. The harbor smells like old copper and new salt. I check the small silver compass pendant at my throat—its needle wavers like I do—and walk the boardwalk to tally damage: missing planks, a buoy with a torn net, a community notice about the upcoming council vote. Ivy is already at the Basin, caked in soil and paint on her overalls, planting halophytes under Rosa Alvarez’s watchful eye. Elias appears at the lab in a damp parka, hands clutching a crinkled white field shirt beneath, steel-blue eyes narrowed as he describes a promising sensor read-out. Jun Park strides past in his deep-indigo coat, amber eyes cool as tide glass; the City Hall notices flutter behind him. I know the council meeting will decide our neighborhood’s fate: Jun’s engineered barriers and managed retreat or our co-op’s living breakwaters. I listen and note, but I also feel a tightness under my salt-streaked knit sweater—the guilt of past losses fuelling my resolve to protect every person here. Tonight I will stand and speak.  
* **Visuals:** Strand at dawn: driftwood browns, algae greens; Mara’s jacket with patches, sea-glass eyes, compass pendant; Elias in parka with wrist sensor; Jun’s polished coat.  
* **Valence:** -1 (slightly negative)  
* **Arousal:** 2/5 (low to simmering)  
* **Branch:** Common  
* **Next:** Chapter 2  
I count boards, names, debts, and memories—my field journal pages are pressed with seaweed and the echo of the past storm. I move between people like patchwork: convincing a nervous fisherman to attend tonight’s hearing, calming Ivy’s impatient sketches about community art for the Basin, and accepting Elias’s report of encouraging reef recruitment in a test bay. Jun’s municipal notices rustle, reminders of ballots and biometric floodlines. Rosa’s braided silver hair catches the light when she offers a prayer to the old beacon—her hands firm, shawls smelling of sea-indigo. I practice what I’ll say: speak for the vulnerable, refuse sacrifice, demand equitable funding. In my pockets, the compass pendant and moth-eaten scarf rub together—a small geometry of choices. My stubbornness coils like a rope; the community is a small boat, and I insist it must not be cut loose. The tide is rising in more ways than one. Tonight’s vote is a hinge; I feel it in the ache behind my sea-glass eyes.

### Chapter 6: The Night the Harbor Broke [Fall] (Branch: Route A — Ending: Sad)

- **Entry Condition:** From Chapter 5, Option A or C

The retrofit held for minutes, then one by one, fixtures failed. A breakwater section snapped, and a wave rushed over the low quay where families slept. Elias pressed his wet forehead to my chest, eyes glass-blank, and I felt his grief like a cold stone. Jun’s municipal plan called our actions reckless in the press, and Lian Mor demanded accountability. I stand at the Strand smelling diesel and salt and know I chose the path that led here. The guilt is an undertow that will not relent.
