## Visual Novel Title
Tide of Ashes

---

## World View
Corvena Bay sits a decade or so into a future where the ocean has stopped being reliably predictable. Once a shipbuilding and fishing hub, it now wears salt like a bruise: patched concrete, reclaimed green infrastructure, and every public surface stained with tidewater and repair. The city is politically fragmented; municipal authority is centralized in a technocratic council that balances budgetary pressure, real estate interests, and humanitarian rhetoric. Grassroots collectives — co-ops, artists, and elders — hold cultural memory and local adaptation practices. Science and engineering can rebuild, but resources are scarce and choices about who is protected are inevitably exclusionary.

Religion and ritual survive as small coastal practices around the Basin and Strand: offerings, songs, and reclamation ceremonies. Class lines have hardened: those with connections or contracts (often aligned with planners like Jun Park) can be moved into fortified zones; marginalized neighborhoods rely on community labor and DIY solutions. The atmosphere is humid, salt-scented, and frequently overcast after storms; painterly visuals emphasize weathered textures, muted sea greens, rust-orange, and soft light streaked over long scars in the cityscape. Social conflict centers on a triangle of loyalties: the protagonist (Mara), the scientist (Elias), the planner (Jun). Their personal histories and faces—sea-glass eyes, wind-tousled hair, immaculate coats—are inseparable from political choices, and each relationship doubles as an ethical test.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I narrate in first person as Mara Kestrel, the community organizer who grew from a fishing family and now fights to keep her neighborhood alive. The global curve is a steady Fall: decisions, compromises, and technical failures compound into losses. The story begins with grassroots hope at the Strand and Tideworks Lab; I reconnect with Elias and confront Jun. A council hearing pushes me to choose an approach to adaptation: side with small-scale regenerative solutions, endorse Jun’s sweeping engineered protections, broker a fragile compromise, or attempt a dramatic public seizure of city funds for community defenses. Each choice spawns a short route (three chapters long) that descends inevitably: experiments fail, storms strike, trust fractures, and moral compromises corrupt intention. Branching decisions within each route accelerate decline: whether to deploy an untested reef matrix early, sign a binding municipal pact that accepts displacement, or broadcast a provocative appeal that sparks unrest. Endings vary in flavor — sudden catastrophic storm, dismantled neighborhoods, fractured relationships, and guilt-wracked survivors — but all conclude in tragedy: new failures, losses, or mistakes culminating in a nadir that reshapes Corvena Bay and haunts me personally.

### Chapter 1: Salt and Stitches [Fall] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)  
* **Summary:** I begin at The Strand. The boardwalk’s planks creak under my scuffed boots as I gather neighbors. My battered leather journal is a familiar weight at my hip; the moth-eaten scarf feels like a talisman. I call the meeting to discuss wave forecasts and a new permit from City Hall that could redirect funding to hard infrastructure. I see Ivy laughing with kids amid bleached nets. My guilt for the harbor and my father's lost boat sits like a stone, but I hide it behind practical optimism. I notice Jun across the Strand—his deep-indigo coat immaculate even in salt wind—and Elias arrives stained with algae, sensor wristband pulsing faint green. The emotional tone is wary solidarity; we begin to map who can be saved and who might be asked to leave. I promise the room a deliberation rooted in equity, but my voice trembles. This is the first step after grief: we still hope.  
* **Visuals:** Worried close-ups: my tide-streak teal hair tips in wind, Elias’s glasses fog, Jun’s undercut kept clean. Boardwalk textures: driftwood browns, algae greens.  
* **Valence:** Negative (melancholic, guarded)  
* **Arousal:** Medium (focused urgency)  
* **Branch:** Common  
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I stand at the Strand’s lip with salt grit under my nails and my journal pressed into my palm like an argument with the horizon. The children’s laughter — a rope-snap of life — tangles with my own memory of someone else’s boat listing and going. Ivy tugs at my sleeve and asks, simple and dangerous, if we can rebuild everything. I let her see hope; inwardly I tally washed-out roofs and elder households that can't afford to relocate. I watch Elias approach, parka pockets bulging with instruments, his steel-blue eyes narrowed in a way I know means fear hidden in method. He hands me a tiny sensor that throbs faintly green; Theo trails after him, grin and solar pack, and I feel the old co-op rhythm: make, test, mend. Jun waits near the rail, coat immaculate despite wind, amber eyes scanning as if reading a map of votes and lines. When I speak, my voice is steady because steadiness is a kind of armor here, even if the armor is stitched and patched. We set time for a Tideworks demonstration; none of us can yet see how badly the tide will bite.

The scuffle was sudden and ugly. A neighbor tripped, an officer’s baton clipped a shoulder, and the video feeds of blood and badge went viral in a single afternoon. My hands shook as they cuffed someone I’d shared coffee with that morning. We had bought piping and pumps, and they were staged at the Basin waiting to be installed; yet the cost of keeping them was now bodies in jail and families terrified. Jun’s lawyers moved with white-glove efficiency, drawing maps of culpability. I could sit in the atrium and accept arrests as a political tactic to force national attention, but the reality of locked cells and hungry kids turned that strategy into a moral crucible. I could cut a deal and preserve equipment, surrendering our moral high ground to preserve what’s left. Or I could try to draw in national watchdogs by leaking documents and hope judicial intervention stops punitive enforcement. Each path felt like choosing which of my neighbors would bear the worst burden.

The narrative ends in personal and communal nadir.

I keep seeing the images in fragments: the atrium with its chairs overturned, a neighbor’s blood on a petition, a storm horizon eating the skyline while lawyers argued in courtrooms that looked like caves. If we stayed and accepted arrests, we kept some pumps but lost the people who led the work, and prison stamped our movement with a price no one counted on. If we surrendered, the materials we shipped eventually sat in impound yards, sealed by judges who wrote their own kind of finality. If we tried to ring the country with leaks and national headlines, the emergency apparatus responded by codifying executive powers that smoothed over the city’s wounds at the cost of criminalizing our tactics. Elias closed a case file and left; his last look was an exchange of data and grief. Jun remained in office, quieter and harder, his amber eyes folding inward. I walk streets that hold the shape of what I tried to protect and the hollow where it used to be. My journal is full of maps and apologies, and the compass pendant rests heavy at the base of my throat — pointing nowhere I can trust. The tide has taken more than land; it has taken the confidence that any one of our choices could have spared us.
