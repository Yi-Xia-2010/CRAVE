[Scene: Tideworks Lab | Midday]
[Visual: A corrugated shipping container converted into a lab; warm task lamps pool light over table cluttered with sensors, algae samples in jars, and a half-assembled reef module. Outside, the harbor mists press low against the pilings.]
[Sound: Soft, rhythmic clank of mooring lines, distant gull cries, low hum of refrigeration units]
[Music: Sparse, minor-key piano with slow cello undertow]

You arrive with the Strand still in your mouth — salt and the aftertaste of a speech you'd already given a hundred times in your head. The lab smells of iodine and wet plaster, and the air feels thick, like a held breath. Your boots squeak on the steel ramp. In your hands your journal is heavier than it looks; something in you wants to tuck it away, and something else wants to lay it out where people can see the ledger of promises you keep.

Theo looks up before you do, like he knows exactly which face the harbor will carry today. He blinks salt into his lashes and grins, but it doesn't reach the corners of his mouth.

Theo: "Mara. You're just in time to watch the mayor's highlight reel."

You: "The mayor's highlight reel?"

Theo: (tossing a rag over his shoulder) "Jun's talking points. Channel's spooled to the council feed. They've got it up on the monitor in the corner." (He gestures toward a wall-mounted screen where a municipal stream waits in muted chrome.)

Elias is bent over a modular reef section on the bench — half-concrete, half-knit of recycled ropes and seeded with kelp spores. His hands are steady; his face is quieter than the lab, like a held tide. When he looks up, there is relief in the sight of you, and something else — a worry he keeps folded inward.

Elias: "We finished the last sensor array tests. The transmission held through last night. The pulse noise fell to tolerable levels." (He shuts off a small wristband alarm; its faint green pulse goes still.)

You let the news hang a moment, tasting it. Proof. He meant to bring proof. You need it to be clean, to be undeniable in the hearing. But now there is a feed playing on the monitor and the mood in the lab tilts toward glass and concrete.

[Visual: The corner monitor flickers to life. Jun Park appears in a crisp suit, the camera angle authoritative. Behind him, a digital rendering of a monolithic floodwall scrolls; animated arrows show projected protection zones.]
[Sound: Jun's voice, confident and steady, filtered through lab speakers]
[Music: Subtle, civic fanfare under Jun's words]

Jun (on feed): "—and with targeted investment and decisive municipal action, we can secure Corvena's economic core. Our plan prioritizes measurable outcomes with strict fiscal oversight."

You watch the render rotate: tidy lines, simplified neighborhoods, bright markers where the city will "remain." Jun's face in the feed is lit from below, all polish; his smile is practiced and efficient. You do not know the particular threads that led him to this suit and these charts, and you refuse to parse his private regrets. Instead you read the promise: funding floors, rigid timelines, block diagrams. The math feels neat. The margin — where people like your neighbors live — is messy.

Elias leans against the bench, a hand running through his damp hair. Beside him, the reef module breathes small mist, like the lab itself is remembering to inhale.

Elias: (quiet) "That's budget talk. He frames it like certainty. But certainty is political — not ecological."

You: "They're going to cut smaller allocations," you say. "The modular deployments, the community training funds — they're convenient targets when you need to raise a number."

Theo huffs. He pulls up a tablet, toggles through spreadsheets pinned to the screen. Tiny colored blocks — grants, timelines, matched funds — wobble under the weight of a red line labeled 'Priority Adjustment.'

Theo: "Lian's amendment pushes the capital toward large-scale infrastructure. The language Jun's using — it's already in the packet that goes to council. If the packet lands like this, the microgrants everyone relies on are on the chopping block."

Elias: (a tight smile) "Politicians love clean outcomes. Living reefs are messy until they aren't. They don't like to fund patience."

You fold your hands around your journal, feeling the damp of the paper through canvas. You think of Ivy's mural at the Basin — bright, stubborn paint on soggy wood — and of Rosa's palm when she set it on your shoulder in the last meeting. The lab's light is softer than the screen's glare, but the danger is in the whisper of reallocation that slides between the two.

Elias watches the monitor, and for a breath you worry he will try to speak to Jun through the feed. Instead, he folds his hands and turns to you.

Elias: "If you want a line that lands, make it about the people who can't be rebuilt into 'cores.' Make it about children learning to fish again, about the woman who runs the bakery two blocks from the Basin. Numbers matter — but they need a body to hold them."

You: "People with names, not pixels."

Elias: (a half-laugh) "Exactly."

Theo flips the tablet over and shows you a modest visualization: a before-and-after of a tiny breakwater planted with reef modules, the modeled reduction in splash against the Basin's edge. The 'after' is not pretty the way Jun's render is. It looks patient, splotchy, alive.

Theo: "If council will see a preview of resilience at the neighborhood scale, they might balk at mass demolition. But if the packet arrives with Jun's tone, they'll vote for certainty over clutter."

You look at the reef, at the sensors that blink like small green eyes. You touch the rough rope edge; it smells like brine and algae. The texture is honest and cold.

[Visual: The monitor switches angles. Now Councilwoman Lian Mor appears in a panel beside Jun, nodding. Her presence is efficient — her expressions practiced between concern and acquiescence.]
[Sound: Lian's voice, measured, carrying the weight of budget spreadsheets]

Lian Mor (on feed): "We must act with fiscal responsibility. The city cannot afford experimental spread-out programs that delay measurable protection for downtown businesses. Difficult trade-offs will be necessary."

Elias: (quiet, almost to himself) "They'll call it triage. They won't call it abandonment."

Theo: (sudden, sharp) "We could leak the transmission data. Show them live readouts from last night's storm. Show them the module held, how much energy it dispersed. Hard numbers, real footage."

You consider it. The thought of broadcasting proof without permission feels electric and wrong and necessary all at once. You think of legalities, of council protocol, of the choked-off trust in public process. You think of Jun's face on the feed, which is not an expression you can read for its private countenance. You call his reaction "complex" in your head and leave it there.

Elias and Theo exchange a look — equal parts calculation and something like guilt. Theo's hands, usually restless, still for a second.

Elias: "If we go off-grid with data, the council will find a reason to discredit us if Jun wants them to. But if we don't, then our proof might arrive too late or be drowned in powerpoint."

You press your palm flat on the reef mesh. The fibers take the shape of your hand and hold it, like a temporary harbor. Your pulse thumps in your throat. You feel the scale of what must be argued: not only science, but trust. Not only funding, but moral obligation.

[Scene: Harbor / Old Harbor Wall | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: You step outside the lab onto the harbor wall. The wind is colder here; the sea hisses over barnacle-scarred stone. The sky is a bruise of cloud, light struggling to find purchase.]
[Sound: Waves gnawing at the breakwater, hollow calls of gulls, a distant motorboat driving through flat water]
[Music: Low, slow strings; a single, unresolved note hangs]

You walk the old harbor wall, the town opening and closing like a mouth beneath you. The feed from City Hall follows in a pocket of your mind — Jun's polished phrases looped like a tide that keeps returning. You think about all the meetings, the pots of community stew, the late-night shifts of volunteers hauling sand and rope. You think of returning to a map where your neighborhood is shaded 'low priority' and the way that would rearrange people's lives into loss.

Elias catches up beside you, shoulders hunched against the wind. He does not reach for your hand. He is a scientist; his habit is to measure, not merge. But he has been at your side since you began to shoulder the town's ledger, and in his presence the numbers seem less like cold things and more like promises with faces.

Elias: "If the council funds the megawall, the city baseline gets safer. But those baselines are lines that cut delicate places loose. We can adapt more equitably if we get the pilots through."

You: "And if we don't?"

Elias: (looking at the water) "The groins and the gutters get reinforced while the Basin drowns a little more each season. People who can't rebuild will have nowhere to go."

You manage a laugh that is mostly an exhale. "That's called 'managed retreat' when the mayor says it. When my neighbors face it, it's called forced departure."

A throb of frustration presses behind your eyes. You picture Ivy's small hands, the smudges of paint on fingers, the mural's mural slowly dissolving under salt spray if no one defends it.

Elias: (quiet) "You don't have to carry this alone."

You: "I know." The words are flat, but they are true. You know you overcommit. You know you hoard guilt like kindling. You know asking for help can be its own risk. Still, when he says it, you feel the shape of a possible different ledger: shared weights, shared promises.

Elias: "I've got data. Theo's got instrumentation. If we can show the council a simple, undeniable sequence — sensor readouts, before-and-after footage, a voices montage from the Basin — they can't pretend the people don't exist."

You imagine standing in the atrium with those numbers in your pocket. You imagine Jun's face across the chamber, Lian's measured nods, Council members folding themselves into arithmetic. It is a battlefield you cannot afford to lose, and yet you sense the walls closing: budget lines tightening, political actors rehearsed.

[Visual: Your phone vibrates in your pocket. A short, neutral message from the council portal: 'Hearing begins 16:00. Final packet received.' You turn it over in your hand. The message is a stone.]
[Sound: The vibration thrums like a tiny admonishment]

You close your eyes and feed the lab's images back into your mind: the reef module's slow breathing, Theo's nervous thumb, Elias's steady gaze. The sense of impending constriction is physical; it lodges beneath your sternum like a pebble.

You breathe out and let the harbor take the sound away.

[Music: The cello draws a lower line; the piano falls quieter to let the wind in]

As you turn back toward Tideworks, you realize the hearing will not be a clean proof-versus-promise match. It will be a contest of language and theater as much as it is of data. Jun's feed has already shaped expectations. The packets are in council members' inboxes. The clock has teeth.

You quicken your step, journal under your arm, eyes on the lab's warm light. The reef modules wait. The sensors blink. In the atrium, choices will soon be made that ripple like a storm surge across neighborhoods.

You want to shout, to make the proof irrefutable and the moral plain. Instead you begin to plan the ways to show what cannot be reduced to tidy spreadsheets: voice, footage, the rhythm of people who will not be deleted from a map.
