[Scene: Hybrid Site | Night — The Storm]

You are there. Your boots are full of water, your jacket weighs like lead. The night is a single hand trying to unlace everything you've built. Sensors that told you "green" flash, then go silent. Someone screams — a human syllable that splits the storm and leaves you raw. You run, the wind whipping salt into your mouth like shards. The world reduces to specific tasks: grab lines, secure people, keep heads above the surge. You reach for a volunteer you know only by the patch on their sleeve; a wave takes them and the world tilts into a kind of urgent, mortal unfairness.

Hands pull you back by the jacket. Theo's voice cracks: "Mara — they've gone under!" You feel the ocean take an answer and leave a question.

You try to give orders. Your throat closes like it's been tied off. You can't get to everyone. You can't make the sea be anything but the sea. The semi-automated pumps fail to engage because their calibration commands had been falsified; what should have been an hour of controlled water run-off turns into a sudden, violent flood toward the Basin where families shelter and where your friends had anchored small boats against the surge.

When the sirens come, they are chorused by a different siren: the weight of consequence. In the cold, wet aftermath as the storm howls itself into spent ruin, bodies are counted. Lives are lost. The list contains names that feel like holes in the night.

[Scene: Old Beacon Lighthouse | Evening]

Elias appears on the path before you — unexpected and inevitable. His coat is damp. He carries nothing but a weathered pack and a look that is used to bearing lab notes rather than apologies.

Mara Kestrel: "Ifs shift blame, Elias. They don't bring people back."

Elias looks at you. There is a thing you cannot name in his eyes — regret, fear, something like relief at admitting a limit.

Elias: "I thought I could make the numbers hold enough truth to protect people. Numbers are lies when the feed is doctored. You feel that sentence like a small, cold stone dropped into the pocket of your jacket. It sinks.

Mara Kestrel: "You're leaving."

Elias: (quietly) "I'm leaving, yes."

Your chest clenches, the first gasp of grief not entirely about the bodies in the Basin — it's about the severing of a tether you had started to rely on. Elias: "You need people who can stand in the open with you. I — right now — am not the one who can stand in the middle of a political storm and not be pulled apart."

Mara Kestrel: "And what about us? What about what we built?"

Elias: "Some things will last. Some things must be left. I'm sorry." (He stands, places a tentative hand at your shoulder, and then he is gone into the dusk.)

You watch him walk away until the dark swallows his silhouette. There is no grand argument; no slammed door. Just the steady, small undoing of a knot.

In the days that follow, the cooperative loses members to fear and to the arms that promise safety: some accept city contracts that require turning data over to remote oversight; others join NGOs that offer legal shelter. Meetings that once hummed with shared labor become tribunals. Accusation is precise and human; "reckless," "naïve," "political," "emotional" — each a blade that edges around the central fact that people died.

You read headlines that call you a cautionary tale. You listen to Jun's measured statements on the radio — he speaks of "lessons," of "necessary constraints." His voice, in the public frame, is a kind of gentle absolution wrapped around a pragmatic center that absolves him in whispers and binds you in accusation.

At night, you go back to the Basin and sit with the memorial lights and Ivy's painted tile. You press your palm to the cool wood and feel the salt in the grain. You are exhausted, hollowed by the city's expectations and by your own failing to hold everything.

Rosa sits opposite you, hands folded in the manner she uses when memory is a liturgy.

Rosa: "We bury the dead. We mourn. We make lists so the living can carry on. Don't let their blame be the only story told about them."

You want to believe her and you also know how easy storytelling is to twist.

The town moves forward in a pattern of small, pragmatic gestures: sandbags, reassigned crews, tighter approvals. The living breakwaters are hauled in sections for forensic review. Grants reroute. Regulations thicken. The hybrid project, once a sign of communal hope, becomes a case file.

You go to the old ledger in your jacket — the journal damp at the edges — and write names. You list things you wish you'd done differently. The pen skitters; the ink smears like the tide.

You are left with a tight, clear sorrow and the knowledge that grief reshapes the architecture of community. It does not rebuild entire towns overnight. It builds, in small increments, a different kind of structure — one that costs more, requires more translation between hearts and budgets, and always demands proof.

At the top of the cliff, the Old Beacon's lamp no longer turns on. The ruin watches like a wound. You place your compass pendant in your palm and feel its cool metal. It gives no answers; it only points.

You stand, the cold wind like an accusation and a benediction both. You think of the people you could not save, the hands you let go, the colleague who left walking toward a job that isn't yours to follow. You think of the things you did right and how they feel small in the echoing requirements of accountability.

When you leave the Beacon, the sky is low and heavy. The town is quieter, not from peace but from the consolidation of loss. You do not know if you will be forgiven by the larger world or remembered kindly by those who stayed. You only know the ledger of what you carry.
