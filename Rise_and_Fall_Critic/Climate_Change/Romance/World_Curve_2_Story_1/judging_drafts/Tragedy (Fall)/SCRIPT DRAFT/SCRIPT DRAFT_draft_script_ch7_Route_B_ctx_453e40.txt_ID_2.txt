[Scene: City Hall Glass Atrium | Mid-Afternoon]

You feel the aftertaste of your own words like salt on a split lip. You chose Jun's plan. The sentence still hangs in the air between the glass and the tide charts on the screens—an edict and a promise at once. The council's cameras blink like slow lighthouse eyes; faces in the seats break into applause, some mechanical, some relieved.

Jun stands to your left, immaculate, the cut of his coat catching the light like a practiced smile. He claps with the ease of a man who has rehearsed victory. His amber eyes find yours; for a moment the warmth there is honest and unfamiliar. He steps forward and places his palm briefly against the podium, a small, theatrical gesture that seals the moment.

Jun Park: "This was not an easy decision for anyone. But today, we secure funding and crews. We begin work—now."

You hear the phrasing—secure, crews, begin—like tools being unloaded. You should feel triumphant. Instead, the atrium smells like printer toner and cold coffee, and the applause feels like a tide that will leave people stranded.

Elias is across the room, hands tucked into his parka pockets, the sensor on his wrist pulsing a dim green. His jaw tightens in a way you have learned to read over the years: calculation held like a fist. When he approaches you later, his voice is small under the atrium's cavernous echo.

Elias Navin: (softly) "You understood what that meant when you said it, Mara. Selective retreat—marking neighborhoods. The Strand is on the list."

You: "I know what it means." (you swallow; the words taste like rust) "I tried to make them hear the alternatives."

Elias Navin: "Alternatives take time and funds that the council says we don't have. They wanted a single statement tonight."

His brow knits. He looks like someone who wants to draw a graph and find a middle ground; instead he folds his hands and looks at the floor, as if the math itself has become a moral question.

You: "We can—" (your voice breaks) "We can demand oversight. Conditional deployment. Community protections."

Elias Navin: (shaking his head) "You gave them the headline. Their crews will move fast. You'll be on the inside now, Mara. Be careful how much that inside costs."

He doesn't say the rest: how compromise can calcify into complicity, how stamps on blueprints become sentences. But you hear it anyway, louder than the applause.

You want to ask about who decides "nobody." You want to ask who counts as a household worth saving. The words die in your throat. You think of your father's boat, of rope hands and hypothermia, of promises made over hot tea and then bent by necessity.

[Scene: The Basin | Pre-Dawn]

You walk the Basin before the crews arrive in the morning. The community has turned out—some to volunteer, some to gather information, some to stand and watch with fists in pockets. A child presses a salt-streaked drawing into your hand: a green wall of trees and people holding hands.

Foreman: "We're here to implement what's been approved. We'll do it with minimal displacement. There are temporary housing options collected on this sheet."

You look at the sheet. Numbers. Addresses. The Strand appears in bold on one column. You read the list aloud, each neighborhood a small falling away.

A woman you have known for years—Mrs. Calder from the net-mending co-op—stands and watches the stakes being hammered in. Her hands are as steady as they have always been, but now they tremble as she folds a small blanket.

Mrs. Calder: (to you, quietly) "You said tonight this would save lives. Will it save ours?"

You: "I—" (you have a hundred policy answers; none of them sit in the hollow of your chest) "It will protect the most people most quickly, the council says. I believe the council intends to follow through on relays and assistance."

Mrs. Calder: (eyes narrow; something like accusation and longing) "Intentions are not homes, Mara."

Her words land like pebbles into your sense of self. You cannot make mouths of concrete into sanctuaries for memories and night terrors. You cannot undo the map Jun will sign tomorrow. You can only stand and watch the crews set the first markers and feel each hammer strike a name into a ledger you once promised to tilt differently.

Elias finds you at the Basin's edge. He does not meet your eyes immediately. He leans against a rusted sculpture—a community piece made from old anchors and bottle glass.

Elias Navin: "You garnered them funds. That gives us leverage, but it also makes you responsible for the way those funds are spent."

You: "I hoped leverage would be enough."

Elias Navin: (the admission is almost a whisper) "You are trying to be both champion and negotiator. Those are different weights."

You want to tell him how lonely it feels to carry both. You want to tell him that every time you look at the Strand's boards marked for relocation you feel a small stone being added to your chest. But your throat is full of salt and the sound of hammers. Rosa interrupts the reverie with a practicality that is its own kind of mercy.

Rosa Alvarez: "We will name those to be protected. We will keep a roster. We will not let the numbers hide their faces."

She hands you a ragged ledger with names in her hand. Her handwriting is steadier than your own heart. You place your palm on the page as if making a vow, and for an instant you can imagine this ledger as a rope: a way to pull people through.

But the foreman's walkie crackles, and the first crew begins to shape a trench. Metal meets mud. The work has begun.
