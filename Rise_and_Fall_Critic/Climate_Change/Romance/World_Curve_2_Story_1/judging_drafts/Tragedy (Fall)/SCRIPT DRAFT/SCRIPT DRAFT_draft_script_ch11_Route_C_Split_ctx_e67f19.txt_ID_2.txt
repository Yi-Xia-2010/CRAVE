[Scene: Salted Rooftop Garden | Dusk]
[Visual: A low sun sinks behind salt-veined glass; mist clings to the panes. Rows of halophytes glimmer with droplets.]
[Music: Low, mournful strings under a slow percussion beat]
[Sound: Distant gull cry; the metallic creak of a floodgate in the near harbor]

You stand with the city at your back and the harbor pressing at your edges. The image from the anonymous municipal account is still warm in your phone's memory — an overturned crate, living-reef modules scattered like broken shells on wet planks. Jun's promise hangs in the static between his words and his silence: "You'll have it. We'll open the audit schedule. For now—" The pause is heavy and unfinished, like a tide not yet receded.

Elias is already here, sleeves rolled, a faint bioluminescent pulse from his wristband catching on the curve of his watch. Theo fiddles with a handheld scanner, fingers stained with algae and solder. The rooftop smells of wet soil and ozone, the greenhouse lamps making a soft, yellow halo around their faces.

Elias Navin: "We ran integrity checks this afternoon. The cluster showed anomalies around 17:12 and then... nothing. The logs stop like someone closed a book."

You press your thumb into the damp spine of your journal and feel the compass pendant cool against your palm. There is a tide of things you want to say — Jun's name, the crate image, the volunteers' faces — but the first thing out is a small, practical question.

Mara Kestrel: "Did the manifests include chain-of-custody photos?"

Theo Mendes: (without looking up) "Some. Most of the field manifests had time-stamps and tags — but if someone's got local access, they can rewrite them. Or, more likely, just steal the files before they sync."

You: The word theft tastes like rust. You imagine hands under covers, gloved and efficient, moving through the night.

Elias Navin: (softly) "We should assume they can get physical access. Digital scrubs only tell half the story."

You look at him. He meets your gaze and there is a steadiness there, but also a thin line of worry around his mouth. He carries his caution like a tool belt.

Elias Navin: "If this is coordinated, it's not just vandalism. Missing bolts, erased timestamps, defaced minutes—that's organised."

You hear the rasp of Theo's breath as he swipes a thumb across a cracked display. He grins, but it's the kind of grin that doesn't reach his eyes.

Theo Mendes: "Organised, or someone who hates good paperwork. Either way, it's a mess."

You can feel the rooftop shudder under the thought. The hybrid rollout was meant to be the story that threaded a needle between the city's needs and the community's hopes. Now it looks like someone wants that seam to tear.

[Scene: Hybrid Project Sites | Night]
[Visual: Floodlights throw stark circles of pale light across weathered concrete and rusting rebar; living-reef frames sit half-assembled in shallow cages.]
[Music: Sparse piano notes undercut by a low drone]
[Sound: Water slapping against concrete, distant automatic shutters]

You walk the line of the hybrid installations with Elias and Theo. The night is merciless in detail. Where once there were neat stacks of galvanized bolts, there are gaps; clamps hang like empty teeth. One reef frame lists awkwardly, tender as an injured bird. Salt and machine oil mix in a smell that makes your chest ache.

Theo Mendes: "Look. These mounts are sheared — not corroded. Somebody took them, or they were cut clean."

Elias kneels, fingers careful, and taps a sensor pod. He speaks as he works, voice low.

Elias Navin: "Sensors show a power draw spike, then a sudden drop-off. Whoever did this knew what to take and how to leave the evidence looking like weather."

You crouch by a placard someone has defaced: scrawled over minutes from the oversight meeting — ink smeared into a wet, accusing blot. The letters don't matter as much as the act. Someone wanted to write over the record itself.

Mara Kestrel: "Who benefits from this failing? Who wants the hybrid in disrepute?"

Theo Mendes: (shrugging) "That's the million-dollar question. Could be political actors scared of messy compromises. Could be contractors losing a contract. Could be… anybody with an agenda."

Elias looks up sharply; he has that look he gets when a pattern forms — the scientist finding a vector.

Elias Navin: "Or someone afraid of decentralised control. Someone who prefers plans that are easy to tally on a spreadsheet."

The words pull like undertow. You think of Jun's sleek banners along the avenue, his measured promises that smelled, sometimes, like a different kind of order. You also think of Councilwoman Lian's tight smile and the way budgets carve neighborhoods into equations.

Your hands are damp. You breath the night in, tasting salt and an acrid note of burnt plastic — as if something had been destroyed quickly to erase intent.

Mara Kestrel: "We can't— we can't let this go unchallenged. But if we accuse the wrong people, we fracture trust harder."

Elias Navin: (quiet) "Then we find the wrong people and show the town the evidence. Quietly, if we can."

Theo's flashlight stutters and he swears softly, then leans in and snaps photos. He works fast, fingers jittery with caffeine and adrenaline.

[Scene: The Basin | Late Night]
[Visual: Muddy paths threaded with lantern light; community art glints wetly; young mangroves shiver in the wind.]
[Music: A single low cello note, then sparse, uncertain plucked strings]
[Sound: Quiet murmurs of volunteers; the distant slap of a boarding house shutter closing]

You arrive as the Basin breathes low and tight. Ivy is there, sleeves rolled to the elbow and paint still on her hands; Rosa stands with a folded shawl over her arm, eyes like old glass. Volunteers cluster in small groups, whispering. News travels fast here; it moves like a riptide.

Ivy Kestrel: (voice shaking) "They were unloading at dawn, and then—then the crates were open. Tomas said the seals were slit. He says he saw someone in dark clothes. He says—"

She breaks off, and for the first time her youthful defiance falters. Her palms bloom with dirt, and she squeezes a handful of soil until the smell of it rises: warm, loamy, stubborn.

Rosa Alvarez: (soft, fierce) "We will not decide our fate in whispers. But we will not make panic the architect of our choices, child."

You look at Rosa and feel, briefly, the weight of generations. Her voice is a keel that steadies you.

Mara Kestrel: "We need facts. We need chain-of-custody. We need to protect volunteers and our installations. And we need to know if this is targeted from inside or outside."

Ivy: "Jun said he'd open the audit schedule and increase security. He said—"

Rosa's hand finds Ivy's shoulder; it is a small, human anchor.

Rosa Alvarez: "Words are wind if not paired with action. Ask him for action. But remember: accusing without proof burns bridges some of us still need."

You can feel the Basin press in close: its mud, the young trees, the hands that have built and rebuilt this place. Anger hums in your chest, a hot, small thing, but above it is something colder — the knowledge that any choice now will ripple outward.

You take out your phone and stare at Jun's last message again. The image of the crate is a scar on your screen. The municipal account's signature is a neat, anonymous code. Whoever sent it intended a certain kind of message: notice.

Elias stands next to you, shoulders broad, and his hand finds yours for a half-second as if to steady both work and will.

Elias Navin: (low) "We can go to City Hall. We can force the audit public. Or—"

His words trail, a list of paths like lanes of an incoming tide. You think about going straight to Jun, marching into the atrium with the evidence and demanding answers. You imagine sending everything to the press and letting the city watch the hybrid unspool in public. You can picture slipping into the lab with Elias and Theo, after dark, and following the hardware trail until it yields its secrets.

All three choices are dangerous. All three are possible.

You feel the air tighten. The Basin's lanterns flicker as if reacting to your heartbeat.

Mara Kestrel: (internal) "If I confront Jun publicly, I force transparency but risk him digging in and the whole thing becoming a spectacle that devastates what little calm we have. If we investigate quietly, we might find the truth but let sabotage keep happening elsewhere. If we open the meetings to press, we might expose the saboteurs — or we might amplify panic and drive funding away."

Rosa watches you with an expression that is both tender and relentless.

Rosa Alvarez: "You carry here what we cannot always carry — the people's ledger and their salt. Choose for the people, but choose carefully."

You close your eyes and imagine the lighthouse at the breakwater: a ruined beacon that used to be guide and now leans, half-ruined, over a bracing sea. The choice you make tonight will decide if it becomes a rallying light or a warning to avoid.
