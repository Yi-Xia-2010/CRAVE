[Scene: The Strand | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: A thin strip of boardwalk glints with salt; the sky is a bruised indigo that hasn't decided whether to be night or day.]
[Music: Low, hollow cello; distant, rhythmic pulse like a tide.]
[Sound: Woodboards creak under a solitary footstep; gulls wheel, complaining.]

You wake before the world softens into morning. The Strand smells of wet rope and old diesel, a cold, coastal smell that climbs through your nostrils and presses memories into the back of your throat. Your hands find the compass pendant under your shirt the way your mind finds a bruise: by touch first, then by the warmth pressed against skin.

You sit up on the bench where you doze sometimes—too tired to go home, too wired to be anywhere else—and leaf through the field journal in your lap. Pages of pressed seaweed have stuck at the edges. Your handwriting is there: lists, sketches of co-op rigs, fragments of speeches scrawled at three a.m. You read one line, then another, then stop because the words are familiar and too sharp.

You inventory the Strand with the practiced eye you learned when your father’s boat vanished: broken planks near the railing, nets mended with mismatched thread, a buoy with a gouge of barnacle where it struck concrete two tides ago. The damage is a language you know. It reads: not the storm alone, but the slow forgetfulness of budgets and promises. It reads: decisions to be made, and people to be left to ride them out.

Your chest tightens. You think of the harbor where the co-op keeps the few boats left, of the photograph in your journal—your father smiling, a line of oil on his fingers. You feel the familiar stab of guilt: you organized the repairs, you argue at meetings, you sleep with the thought of candidates and budgets, and still there are things that cannot be fixed with meetings. You promised—again and again—that you would not let this place be the one you lost. The promise sits heavy, like salt in a wound.

Ivy appears at the corner of the boardwalk, paint on her cheek, a battered aerosol can in one hand and a worn bucket in the other. She moves with the sort of jittery hope that refuses to be ironed flat. When she sees you, her face brightens then constricts, the small calculation of how much to worry and how much to buoy you.

Ivy: "Mara! You’re up early. I—" (she bites the end of the sentence, deciding how much cheer to force). "The mural’s nearly done. Rosa says she’ll bring extra boards to the Basin. Are you—are you alright?"

You fold the journal closed and tuck it under your arm. Your voice is smaller than the ache you carry.

You: "I’m all right." (You force the smile. It doesn’t reach your eyes.) "Just going through the list before the hearing. Did you sleep?"

Ivy’s grin is stubborn.

Ivy: "Naps count. Besides, someone has to prove the sky’s not falling while the council argues about where to put the sandbags." (She leans against the rail, watching the horizon.) "You look like you fought with the dawn."

You: "It felt like a fair fight." (You exhale a small laugh that tastes like sea brine.) "How’s the piece? Is Rosa happy with the palette?"

Ivy: "She kept asking for more indigo. For memory, she said. Also—" (she shifts, quieter) "—she wants everyone who’s speaking to have a marker board with talking points. She thinks bullet lists work when hearts are raw."

You nod; the idea is practical, comforting in its bluntness.

Ivy pauses in both reactions, watching you with a softness that asks permission to worry. She chooses brusque cheer instead.

Ivy: "Whatever you do—don’t let Jun make it all about numbers. He can charm a floodplain into obedience."

The sound of that name tightens the air. You can feel it tug at old cords—a history of sandbox arguments and scraped knees before politics, before power. You say nothing for a beat, because words would have to be heavier than they are.

You: "I won’t let it be only numbers. We’ve got data too—Elias has been running tests on the living breakwaters. And we have the memory of this place. Rosa will speak about what is not in any chart."

Ivy’s certainty warms you in spite of yourself. She tugs the cap down over her curls and heads toward the Basin, humming a tune she learned at a community mural night. You watch her go: a small, defiant arrow of color against the gray.

[Scene: The Basin | Dawn]
[Visual: Low, reflective pools between beds of young mangrove; community art glints with salvaged metal under pale light.]
[Sound: Volunteers chatting, the low thump of boots in mud; an old radio murmurs a local DJ’s voice in the background.]

Rosa is already here when you arrive, hands rolled into her shawl, eyes that have seen decades of storms and long after-storm recoveries. She moves among the volunteers like someone placing pieces back into a mosaic—methodical, gentle, unafraid of the hard work.

Rosa: "There you are." (She does not reach for your hands immediately; she waits for you to offer them.) "You look like you carried the tide on your back."

You offer your hands because you always do. They are calloused now in a different pattern; your fingers remember rope and protest signs and stapled budgets. Rosa squeezes your palms, and for a second a map of the past shivers between you.

You: "I keep thinking about Father’s ledger—how he’d mark a repaired line and then step out with his tools like it was nothing. I can almost hear his whistle. It—" (Your throat closes.) "I don’t want to be the one who keeps holding the ledger while the page burns."

Rosa’s eyes soften and then harden, like the tide negotiating the rocks.

Rosa: "You are not alone in the holding, Mara. We have hands enough. And your ledger—" (she taps a mental ledger of her own) "—is what keeps the co-op honest. But promises—promises are easy to make in a dry hall. Storms and budgets strip them bare."

You meet her gaze. Her voice carries the weight of someone who has negotiated food lines and funerals and the slow, slow climb of community rebuilds.

You: "Jun’s proposal goes to vote tomorrow. He argues the city must save the core. He dances around sacrifice like it’s arithmetic."

Rosa: (a short, bitter laugh) "Jun thinks in lines on a map. People are not lines, child. He must remember that. Or he must learn the cost when he looks at eyes instead of coordinates."

You want to remind Rosa of his childhood—of sandcastle pacts and scraped knees. The memory sits warm and bright in you, but you don’t say it. Political masks are harder to lift in public. You keep your memory like a secret lit candle.

Rosa: "Listen to me. Speak from what the co-op has done. Speak with numbers if you must, but also bring them the stories these numbers water. And if you stumble, say his name—Jun—and ask him if he has stared into the face of someone who cannot retreat, and what then?"

You: "If I say that, he’ll... he’ll say it’s necessary." (You break off; you can picture Jun’s tight suit, the polite tilt of his chin.) "He’s always been sure of his necessary."

Rosa: "Certainty is a warm coat a person puts on when they cannot hold others. Remember that."

You feel Rosa’s counsel settle like ballast. You are grateful and weary in equal measure.

Rosa hums approval, either way. The Basin is alive with a small, stubborn machinery of care—people wading, replanting, patching beds. The community is present and raw and human, and you feel both fortified and fragile next to it.

[Scene: Harbor Edge / Makeshift Tideworks Lab | Early Morning]
[Visual: Converted shipping container; a warm halo of task-lamp light spills out into the gray. Instruments blink with dim green pulses.]
[Sound: Metallic clinks, soft electronic beeps; a gust of wind snaps a tarpaulin like a far-off drum.]

Elias is there, sleeves rolled, a sensor wristband pulsing faintly as he calibrates a device that smells like ozone and salt. He looks up when you approach—not surprised, just steady, the way tides are steady in their coming. He sets down the instrument and offers you a paper cup of hot beverage, which you accept with fingers that have been doing too many things for too long.

Elias: "You should eat something. The council won’t listen to anyone who’s faint with hunger." (He manages a small, dry half-smile.) "And these readings—" (he taps the tablet) "—are better than we thought. The submerged reefs took the edge off of last month’s swells more than the models predicted."

You: "That’s good. It gives us talking points. Living breakwaters reduce wave energy, promote habitat—" (You reel off the technicals because they steady you, like counting breath.) "—and they’re cheaper on maintenance if we implement the pilot scale across three coves."

Elias: "Exactly." (He watches you, then looks away, measuring the silence.) "I’ll bring the visualizations to the hearing. Theo is putting together a simulation that shows immediate nearshore gains and long-term habitat recovery."

You study his face. He’s calm, like rock and tide. There’s always more behind his quiet—something precise and tender that doesn’t find its way into council minutes: the way he traces the loops in his notes, the way he remembers small details about people’s gardens.

You: "I wanted to do this without promising too much." (You look at the device in his hands.) "I’m tired of promising and not delivering."

Elias’s fingers pause on the tablet, then resume, steady.

Elias: "We won’t promise what the data can’t back. But we have numbers. We have evidence. That’s what they’ll respect." (He meets your eyes.) "And I—" (he stops, then says it more slowly) "—I’ll be there when you speak. For the data, and for the rest."

His voice is an offer. You could let it be more; you could let it be less. You fold the moment into your pocket because you have tasks.

You: "Thank you." (You find the gratitude in your chest—raw, wanting.) "It means a lot."

Elias: "Bring your ledger. Bring the stories. Bring Rosa. And—" (he gestures at you, soft) "—breathe. You’re not carrying it all alone."

You open your mouth to say you know that, to confess the ways you do try to carry it alone, but a shadow falls across the lab entrance.

Jun stands framed against the pale sky, coat immaculate even in the harbor grit. He steps forward, voice easy, public. An old part of you recognizes the schoolyard sneer turned polished. You notice how he arranges himself—hands relaxed at his sides, amber eyes leveled, like a man used to choosing which parts of himself to show.

Jun: "Morning, Mara. Elias. I thought I’d catch you before the hearing. I wanted to make sure we’re all aligned—" (he says aligned as if alignment were the same as agreement).

You: "Are you aligned with the map that marks neighborhoods to be sacrificed?" (The words are sharper than you expected.)

Jun: (a brief, tight laugh) "I’m aligned with saving as many lives as possible. That requires hard choices. We have to be realistic about where investments go."

Elias: (quietly) "Realism is not the same as surrender."

Jun turns his head toward Elias, his smile polite but thin.

Jun: "We all want the city to survive. My plan is scalable; it protects the core and reduces overall losses. We can’t let sentiment override the math."

The air between you cools. You feel Rosa’s earlier words—people are not lines—press against your teeth. Jun’s certainty is armor, and he wears it well.

You: "We can scale living breakwaters. They’re not sentiment; they are engineered, maintained, and they restore what’s been lost."

Jun: (his expression shifts, as if searching for a different pitch) "The living approach is promising in pockets, yes. But it’s not a replacement for the hard infrastructure some zones require. The city can’t gamble on experiments when lives are on the line."

You: "They’re not experiments anymore, Jun—" (you force the calm) "—they’re deployments with evidence. And they don’t ask us to abandon people so the rest can live."

Jun’s jaw tightens for a second. There is a trace of the child you once knew beneath the planner—the same stubbornness that made him build forts from driftwood on this very shore. For a heartbeat, you remember less of policy and more of scraped palms, and it hurts.

Jun: "We disagree on tactics. Tomorrow the council will decide. I hope your speech sways them, Mara." (He inclines his head, almost kindly.)

You: "I hope their votes do not decide who is allowed to keep a home."

Jun’s reply is a practiced, polite non-answer. He turns and walks away, leaving the taste of salt and politics on the air. Elias watches Jun go, then looks back at you, hands folded around the warm cup.

Elias: "He’ll make it about efficiency and numbers. You have to make it about the people behind those numbers."

You nod, but your bones hum with the knowledge that facts alone may not be enough. You keep thinking of ledger lines, of the ledger left open on a table in a house that’s no longer there.

[Scene: The Strand / Boardwalk | Mid-morning]
[Visual: The sky lightens to a flat pewter. Community members drift in: someone with a placard, someone with a thermos. The boardwalk is a patchwork of repairs assembled by hands staying. ]
[Sound: The murmur of conversation folds with creaks of the old wood.]

The community gathers in small knots. You flip through your speech one last time, smoothing sentences like worn fabric. Ivy fusses with a flag. Rosa moves among people, placing confident hands on shoulders. Theo swings by with a thumb drive and a grin that barely hides his nerves.

Theo: "Elias said the simulation looks sexy. He used the word 'sexy' for a reef model." (He laughs at his own ridiculousness, which makes you crack a smile.)

You: "Tell him I’ll try not to blush at his professional vocabulary."

Your laugh is thin; it doesn’t reach your eyes. The ledger of the day is long: the hearing, the lines of speech, the inevitable questions you cannot predict. You think of the people who are counting on you to steer this—not to win everything, but to make sure no one is left entirely uncounted.

You put the compass to your lips for a second—an unnecessary ritual, and you know you do it to convince your body that there is north, that there is direction. The metal is cold and familiar. You tuck it back and close your journal.

Your voice to yourself is steadier than it feels.

You: (internal) "You can do this. Speak true. Bring the ledger and the lives behind it. If this fails—" (The thought fractures. You don’t finish it aloud.)

The crowd begins to move toward shuttle vans and shared cars bound for City Hall. The salt in the air has the scent of a world leaning toward change, and your heart leans with it—heavy, stubborn, unwilling to fold.

You walk the boardwalk one last time before leaving for the hearing. The sea is a slate smear at the margin of everything you love. The Strand looks both fragile and defiant, like a stitch that has held despite the tear.

You take one step off the boardwalk, into the shallows where the tide kisses the feet. Cold water laps against your boots. For a moment you stand still and let the rhythm of the sea match the rhythm of your breath. You think of your father’s ledger, of Rosa’s counsel, of Elias’s steady hands, of Ivy’s paint-stained grin. You think of Jun’s amber eyes and the way he measures losses like a man measuring fabric.

There is no single answer. There is only the next speech, the next vote, and the way you choose to stand in the space between the two.

The bus to City Hall hisses up the road. You climb aboard with your journal closed on your lap. The engine hums underfoot like a contained tide. Outside the window, the Strand recedes—salt-streaked posts, kids’ chalk drawings half-washed but stubbornly bright, a buoy bobbing like a promise.

You breathe and feel the weight again—a ledger, a promise, a possibly unpayable debt. The hearing will come. The city will take sides. You will speak.

There is a sound in your chest that is not quite fear and not quite hope, but it drives you forward like a current.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand tightening around the journal; the compass pendant swings once, catching a sliver of light.]
[Music: A dissonant string chord that does not resolve.]

The bus idles, doors open, and you step down into the gray and the waiting. You hitch your shoulders, taste of salt and resolve in your mouth, and walk toward the atrium that will hold hundreds of opinions and the uneven justice of votes.

You want to rehearse every line, to prepare for Jun’s calculus and Elias’s calm. You want to write everything again and again until it cannot be unread. But there is one thing you cannot schedule away: the moment you stand and look at the room and decide to speak for the people who cannot get to the microphone.

A single last look at the Strand, and then you turn and walk. The doors of City Hall are glass and cool under your palm. Inside, the atrium smells of coffee and cleaned stone—a different kind of salt, institutional and beige.

Your chest tightens once more, and this time the tightness feels less like breaking and more like a rope pulled taut for a throw.

You step into the echo of the atrium, the moment between breath and speech.
