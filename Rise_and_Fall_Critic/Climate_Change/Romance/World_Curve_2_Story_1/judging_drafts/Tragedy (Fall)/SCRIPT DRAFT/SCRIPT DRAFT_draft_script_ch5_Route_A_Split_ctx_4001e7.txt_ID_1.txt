You wake in the throb between sleep and duty — the lab smells of hot solder and brine, and your throat tastes of metal from a long night of talking. The field lamps have left half-moons on your palms where you set them down and forgot. Your journal is closed beside your elbow; the compass pendant rests warm against your sternum like a small, stubborn heartbeat.

Theo is at the bench, sleeves rolled, fingers moving with practiced impatience. He looks up when you step inside, and for a second the joke that was on his lips collapses into something more careful.

Theo: "We pushed the modules bigger for this run. More panels, more sensors. If everything holds, we'll outpace the city models by a margin they'd call miraculous."

You: "If everything holds." The clause is a rope swing over a ragged shore. You can feel the weight of it in your chest.

Theo: "Yeah." He taps a log on the screen, rows of numbers blinking. "We're getting intermittent sync drops from Bay Three. Nothing catastrophic — yet. But the anchor torque curves are flatter than last batch."

Elias stands by the tank, hands folded, the portable wrist sensor pulsing like a second pulse beneath his sleeve. He says the words you already heard a dozen times since the funders arrived: they're on a tight timeline. They want deployment by the next full moon, or the funding re-allocates.

Elias: (quiet) "They fund the logistics, Mara. They pay for the barges, the cranes, the sensor arrays. Without them we don't have the footprint to make the model convincing."

You: "And if the anchors fail because we rushed installation?" The sentence tastes like a ledger entry you don't want to sign.

Elias: (eyes on the console) "Then we document, we learn, we iterate. But if we stall — funding moves on. The rollout—" He stops, and you see him choosing the practical phrase over the more honest one: 'the chance to demonstrate at scale.'

[Sound: A notification ping; a call request from an unknown corporate endpoint]

You step toward the hatch. Outside, the harbor smells of algae and diesel, of wet rope and something rotting under the surface — the unnameable smell of cities being changed by water.

On the deck, Ivy arrives with a thermos and a knitted wrap around her shoulders. She breathes in, the same salt you do. Her expression is compact: worry, and the straightforward bravery of someone who knows how to ask the question nobody wants to voice.

Ivy: "Did you check the U-bolts on Bay Two overnight?"

You: "Theo flagged variability. We were going to re-run calibration before nightfall." You wish the sentence were firmer. In your chest, you hear your father's hands on a rope, the harbor wet and alive in memory.

A funder representative — an efficient woman in a laminated badge and a polite, corporate smile — approaches. Her voice is crisp, practiced.

Funder Rep: "Mara, the board wants progress. We have media scheduled; they expect visuals of ‘living infrastructure in deployment.’ We need the first three modules in the water by 1800. Can you guarantee that?"

The words feel like a tide line being drawn across a neighborhood. Guarantees in your world are leaky things.

You: (steady) "We will meet the timeline as long as it's safe to do so."

Funder Rep: (tilt of a smile that doesn't reach her eyes) "Safety is why we've invested in your team. We're trusting you to manage risk. Report any delays as they occur."

Her eyes flick to Elias and then to you; the subtext is simple and sharp: move the meters, or we'll move the metrics.

You watch the module as it's eased into the water, photovoltaic skin slick with fresh spray. Sensors blink; tether lines shudder in the swell. One of the technicians calls out — a small alarm on the tablet; a sensor reports an unexpected oscillation in a mounting bracket.

Theo comes running with the module's diagnostic slate, breathless and angry in the way technicians are when reality refuses your best code.

Theo: "Mounting bracket corrosion. The fixtures are seeding microfractures. We saw it in prototypes but not at this scale. If the oscillation grows, it could shear under wave pulse."

Elias hears it before you do; his jaw tightens. He looks at you and you read the complex thing in his face: a scientist's dismay wrapped in a plea.

Elias: "We can slow the deployment, retrofit the affected brackets with a stronger composite. That will take an extra twenty-four hours minimum."

Before you answer, your feed buzzes: a live line from the funders' operations manager. They want the footage for a morning briefing, and the timeline is non-negotiable.

Funder Rep (over the line): "Status update. Confirm timeline compliance and we'll push the PR drop. If we see slippage, we escalate to the board."

You can feel the pressure like a physical thing pressing into your sternum. The town needs tangible progress, your co-op needs the resources, and the sea keeps doing what water does — patient, indifferent, all at once.

Back inside, you pace. The weight of choices crowds your mind — signatures you made aloud or in silence, promises you arranged with the calendar of risk. Ivy sets down the thermos and watches you, the plain hard question in her eyes.

Ivy: "What happens if one fails? If a bracket shears in a storm?"

You: "People get hurt. Boats get damaged. The city gets a news cycle that will call us 'naïve' or 'reckless'." The answer tastes like iron.

Elias: (soft, urgent) "Transparency will cost us the rollout. But it keeps people safe. We have a moral duty to stop and report."

You: (internal monologue) Of all the metrics, the most haunted is the one nobody counts until it's too late: trust. Build it too quickly on shaky fixtures and you buy yourself a reputation that will sink along with the next storm. Pause and declare the problem, and you may lose the scaffolding you need for everything you've wanted to build.

Theo: (hands up) "Look, I can jury-rig stronger anchors with composite sleeves. It buys safety without a public pause. It buys time, but it's messy and ethically shady if we don't tell the funders."

The room narrows around you. Each option is a cliff path: the straight line of funding, the sharp honesty of public pause, the hidden repair that preserves both but rots your own sense of right from inside.
