### DRAFT
# Route: Route B (Ending: Bittersweet)


[Scene: City Hall Glass Atrium | Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the atrium; light slanting through blinds, tide-forecast banners flicking on a screen behind the dais]
[Music: Low, brass undertow — a minor chord that sags]
[Sound: Quiet murmur of the crowd, distant gulls through open vents]

You still feel the weight of the microphone in your palm as if it's an extension of every promise you've ever made. The words you chose — the ones that aligned you with Jun's emergency municipal plan — hang in the glass like condensation. They catch the light, then start to run.

Jun Park steps forward with a measured ease. His smile is businesslike, the kind that translates political risk into calm authority. He places a hand briefly at the small of your back when the cameras swing in; the touch is both a possession and a pledge.

Jun Park: "Mara Kestrel's endorsement matters because she knows Corvena from the inside. She knows our costs are human costs."

He leans into the mic with a practiced cadence. The camera angles tighten. His voice rolls out, confident and composed.

Jun Park: "This is not about taking neighborhoods; it's about triage to save a city. With these emergency measures, we can redirect resources to where they'll preserve the most lives."

Applause rises — polite at first, then louder. Some hands clap hard; others stay folded, rigid. You hear Rosa's soft clap, the patient rhythm of someone marking ceremony and caution at once.

You notice Elias in the crowd before he notices you. The faint green pulse from his wristband is steady; it reads like someone trying to find calm on purpose. When his eyes flick to you, they are complicated — not accusations, not forgiveness, just a slow calibration.

Elias Navin: (approaching after the announcement) "You told them we had to act as a city. That gets funds. That gets crews here faster than anyone else could. I'm not naïve about that."

You expect congratulations. You get a measured statement that contains both relief and a question.

You: "It's the difference between a staggered, grassroots approach and an immediate, city-backed mobilization. People will be safer sooner."

Elias: (quiet) "Safer how? For who? The models show reduced risk in core districts, yes. But the peripheral zones — the Strand, parts of the Basin — show increased odds of relocation."

The word lands like a stone in the atrium pond. You didn't imagine saying it out loud in the sunlight; you had rehearsed logistics, not eulogies.

You: "We negotiated clauses. There are provisions for relocation aid and community transition funds."

Elias: (brows knitting) "Provisions can be footnotes in budget lines. They can evaporate under political pressure. You're giving them authority to define 'necessity.'"

Your chest tightens. The list of things you wanted to protect — histories, boats, the small gardens on rooftops — crowds your thoughts like a tide rushing for the breach.

Jun rejoins you, voice softened but still in command, as if tempering the narrative.

Jun Park: "Mara helped us see the human side of the ledger. We will hold to these protections. We will ensure compensation and oversight."

You watch the cameras drink in his promise. It glints like a rescue light and like a blade.

Rosa steps close, her face lined with memory.

Rosa Alvarez: "Choosing the city over the district is a knife-edge. You know that. But sometimes the blade has to be used."

You: "I didn't choose it lightly."

Rosa: (softly) "No one asks you to make the choosing alone, child."

There is an almost invisible pressure in the air — the palpable knowledge that you were needed to sign the paper, to be the face in the frame. You hear another layer of sound now: the distant slap of wave against a pilaster two blocks away, the hum of HVAC, a faint metallic clink as cameras reset.

[Visual: Close-up on your compass pendant against your sternum; a salt grain tucked into the silver]

[Scene: Tideworks Lab | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm task lamps, jars of briny specimens, bioluminescent tanks pulsing faintly blue]
[Music: Mechanical rhythm; under it, a single piano motif that suggests tiredness]
[Sound: Crews moving outside, a diesel engine idling, lab chatter]

You arrive at the lab to the sound of boots and the smell of diesel mixed with wet algae — the working scent of a city that has chosen to move. Theo is already there, sleeves rolled, a laptop open with a tangle of sensor data streaming like a nervous language.

Theo Mendes: "You set off a chain reaction. Can't deny it — the trucks are here. The city's crews are staging at Pier Four, not sixty days from now. That's fast."

Theo's grin is quick, then gone. He glances at Elias, who is standing by a tank, fingers tracing data on his wristband.

Elias Navin: "Fast isn't necessarily thorough. Living systems need piloting, iteration. Engineered walls can be effective, but they change the hydrodynamics. They can kill the nurseries we're trying to restore."

You: "We don't have the luxury of piloting on the timescale the storms are coming. Lives will be lost if we delay."

Elias: (measured, injects a fact) "If bulkheads are placed where currents concentrate, you could see sediment scouring that erodes adjacent beaches. That could accelerate loss elsewhere."

You feel your patience fray like rope under salt. You're managing a set of impossible balances: human lives, ecological models, political timetables.

You: "Then make it adaptive. Use sensors, monitor, iterate as they build. We have the Tideworks team to do that."

Elias: (looks at you — unreadable) "You have the Tideworks team, yes. But will the city allow adaptive pauses? Or will crews have to keep the schedule set by procurement and contractors? Will they stop and change course if the data says they're wrong?"

He doesn't accuse you outright, but his questions press the same place: accountability. You face the unspoken fear — that the machinery of policy will bury nuance.

Theo: (practical) "We can retrofit sensor arrays to the breakwater modules. We can make them talk back. I can write the code for pauses if threshold breaches occur."

Elias: "And who authorizes an emergency pause? The same office that promised swift delivery?"

Your mouth tastes of iron. Somewhere beyond the Lab's corrugated metal, the city's heavy machinery shifts, and a measured clanging begins that sounds almost ceremonial.

[Scene: The Basin (Community Floodplain Garden) | Early Evening]
[Visual: Muddy paths streaked with boot prints, young mangroves quivering, community art half-hidden under tarps]
[Music: Sparse strings; a low, reedy motif that suggests impending rain]
[Sound: A distant generator, murmurs growing into argument, the slap of a protest sign against a shoulder]

By the time you walk into the Basin, the afternoon light has gone brittle. Community members have gathered, more than you expected. Someone has hand-painted a map and pinned it to a folding easel: city zones shaded in bureaucratic blues and grays. The Strand is outlined in the same color your stomach knots into.

Voices rise: sharp, raw, and close.

An elder — not Rosa, but a neighbor you know by the pattern of her hands — points at you.

Neighbor: "You stood with Jun. You gave him our names on the map. How do you sleep, Mara? How do you look Ivy in the face?"

The crowd parts, and you see Ivy standing at the center, paint on her overalls, jaw clenched. She's younger than you and braver — the same stubbornness that once made you both climb the lighthouse to watch storms.

Ivy Kestrel: "You promised you'd never let them write us off. You said we'd be a part of the plan."

You open your mouth. The question is both accusation and plea.

You: "I thought I could make the plan do what we needed. I thought an endorsement would force oversight, that funding would mean real protection."

Ivy: (voice tight) "Instead they painted our blocks as expendable. They told us relocation would be 'for the common good.' Who decides whose good? Who decides which roofs are worth saving?"

Rosa moves through the crowd and places a steadying hand on Ivy's shoulder. Her eyes find yours; there's a fatigue in them, an old sorrow.

Rosa Alvarez: "You chose a path. Paths cut through things. The question is how we walk the path now. Who carries the wounded, who counts the losses?"

Some in the crowd chant; others simply look away like the sight of the tide line shames them. Your throat tightens. You are being both praised in the atrium and vilified here on the mud. The duality is a physical ache in your ribs.

Elias stands a step behind you, hands in his pockets. He speaks to you rather than at you, his voice low so only you can hear.

Elias Navin: "You did what you thought would save most lives. That's not absence of courage. But I need to know — will you be the one who insists the city pays when we must move? Will you argue for the grants and the transition housing? Or will this be a press release and a list of addresses?"

You: (honest, hardly a defense) "I will. I don't want credit. I want protection for the people I love. I will hold them to account."

He studies your face; something in his expression flickers — maybe belief, maybe skepticism. You cannot read it cleanly. It feels like the raw edge of human risk: you might be wrong.

Theo steps forward, practical as ever, trying to bridge the breach.

Theo Mendes: "There are immediate things we can do. Temporary berms, community shelters, rehoming registries. We can start transition funds today — paperwork, appointments, volunteers."

Neighbor: "We'd rather keep our houses."

The statement is not a refusal of help so much as a refusal to be erased.

You feel the city turning under you, a color wheel of priorities and losses that grind against each other.

[Scene: The Strand (Low-tide Boardwalk) | Nightfall]
[Visual: Boardwalk planks dark with tide, buoy markers silhouetted against a low cloud, a muddy shoreline lit by a single portable lamp]
[Music: Minimal, a single cello line; the tone is hollow, downward]
[Sound: Dull thumping of remote engines, the whisper of wind through netting]

When you finally go down to the Strand alone, under the single portable lamp, it feels like walking into the belly of the decision. Lantern light pools on the boards. Tide smells are stronger here — salt and rotting seaweed and diesel. A painted stake, recently driven, leans in the mud with a bright tag fluttering like a wound.

You set your palm on the cool wood. The compass at your throat vibrates with a small, private memory: your father's hands, calloused, guiding a knot as the storm rose. You promised then to hold the ledger of your town with care. Standing here, you see that promises sometimes demand other people's ledgers be rewritten.

Far off, the city's lights hum like a distant heart. Closer, the crews are already moving — measurements being taken, stakes being driven, quiet radios crackling orders. Your endorsement did what it said it would: it moved metal and money. It moved people.

You think of Jun's smile in the atrium, measured and bright. You think of Elias with his careful questions. You think of Ivy's hands, paint-streaked, pointed at the map with a fury that might be grief.

The world feels smaller and heavier at once. You think of the ledger again, and it isn't numbers now but names and roofs and mangrove roots. The choice you made to uphold Jun's plan gave the city a chance to endure, but it carries the weight of sacrifice — of neighborhoods, of intimate memories, of the particular textures of life that may be declared 'nonessential.'

You let the night swallow your breath. The rain-threat clouds seethe afar, and somewhere a gull cries once, ragged against the dark.

There is no neat resolution here. There is only the slow, inexorable work of making a plan live — and the bitter knowledge that living sometimes means letting parts of yourself be marked as collateral.

You press your thumb into the wood until you feel splinters. You will have to be both inside the machinery and under the same sky as the people it displaces. You will need to hold them in your ledger, and to be held accountable to them.

[Music: A single unresolved minor chord; the sound lingers like a held breath]

Page-Turn: The crews keep time to a schedule you helped set. The city is moving forward with slotted certainty; your name is on the list that unlocked the gates. What you didn't unlock is forgiveness. You will wake tomorrow to demands for oversight, for compensation, and for truth. You will have to decide how quietly or loudly you will pursue accountability now that the engines of policy are moving — a choice between complicity and confrontation, convenience and courage.
