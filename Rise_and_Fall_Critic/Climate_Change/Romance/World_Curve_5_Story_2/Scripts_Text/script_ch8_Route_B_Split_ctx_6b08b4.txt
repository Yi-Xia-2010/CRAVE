# Route: Route B — Ending
# Context: 6b08b4

[Scene: Reed Beds / Vendor Stalls | Early Afternoon — After a Storm]
[Visual: The sky is the color of pewter glass; damp light slants through ragged clouds. The boardwalk that threads the market shows fresh gouges—the wood split and blackened where water rushed underneath. A vendor's awning hangs at a crooked angle; packets of soil and seed are scattered, sticky with mud.]
[Sound: Low, continuous thrum of pumps; the market's usual hum reduced to voices clipped with shock, a child crying in the distance, a gust rattling a loose tarp.]
[Smell: Diesel tang from a work skiff, the metallic wetness of exposed marsh peat, and the faint smoke of overturned cooking coals.]

You were at the council twelve hours ago, the charter folded under your hand. The ascendant piano of promise still rings in your bones. And now the boardwalk is a wound with edges that smell of salt and fear.

You crouch at the break where the engineered clamp once sat—an arc of dark metal, now curled and twisted into the water like a blunt question. Jun kneels beside you, fingers ghosting over mud, his tablet screen throwing up a scatter of numbers. He keeps glancing at you, as if to measure how much of this is technical and how much is moral.

Jun Park: "The tide pulse shouldn't have exceeded design loads. Not by— (he swallows) —not by this much. If the installation was rushed, the scour beneath the decking could have compromised lateral support. The clamp failed along the seam."

You taste salt and copper. The words "rushed" and "installation" thread together in your head until they become the same animal. You remember meetings with engineers, the investor's timeline compressed by a fiscal quarter, Lina's tight-lipped nod, your own small phrasing of compromise—the gentle way you convinced yourself 'phased' meant 'safer'.

You stand, palms pressed to damp rail, and let the market fold around you: Marta's face red with anger; a woman stacking ruined jars of smoked fish; a boy who keeps patting his mother's soaked blanket as if it were an animal wounded.

Marta Quin: "They promised the shore would hold. They promised the work wouldn't start until—until people could plan." (Her voice is a knife wrapped in old cotton—sudden tenderness with the edge of history.) "Aria, you were there. You said this was the way."

Your name lands like a stone. Around you, a dozen other voices pick up the echo, not unkindness at first but the brittle weight of accusation. You feel the gathering of eyes — council aides, vendors, the occasional investor in a water-streaked coat trying to appear unhurt.

Eli Navarro [soft, controlled]: "You okay? You look like someone who carries tides in their chest."

You should meet his gaze. You want to. But the market is a species of tribunal and the eyes around you are lit by something sharper than worry.

Aria Solen [to Eli Navarro]: "We're trying to stop more timber from collapsing. Jun thinks the clamp failure is symptomatic—installation stress, accelerated timeline."

Eli Navarro: "So the schedule broke the shore," he says, then his mouth tightens. "Or someone bent the schedule. Which is worse?"

You want to tell him how you chose the compromise, the exact words you used in the council, how you believed Lena's mitigations were enough. But every detail of past choices now opens like a wound that lets critics in. You breathe peat air and choose the partial truth.

Aria Solen: "It was an accepted risk. We accepted conditions to get the pilot moving. Now the pilot's bleeding."

Jun looks up, eyes rimmed with exhaustion.

Jun Park: "There's also evidence of an engineered failure pattern. These clamps—" (he taps the corrupted metal) "—they show atypical shear points. I don't have a fingerprint for intent, Aria. I have a pattern and a timestamp that coincides with accelerated installation windows."

Caden Holt steps forward from the cluster of council aides, his suit still holding rain beads like a calculated shine. He is careful, measured; the rhetoric has always been his tide pool—calm on the surface while currents run beneath.

Caden Holt [smooth, rapid]: "What we have here is regrettable. Coastal engineering is iterative. We mobilized faster because the storm window was closing. We will mobilize emergency funds. Blaming process without perspective won't rebuild the market."

Marta's laugh is hard and sudden.

Marta Quin: "Perspective? People lost stock and stalls, Caden. Kids lost their Saturday bread. Perspective doesn't buy seed or mend a child's shoes."

A vendor—barely older than you—shoves forward, his hand trembling.

Vendor: "You told us your plan would protect us. You were at the meetings. You stood beside the renderings and nodded like it was a promise."

The crowd's tenor shifts. Words become an undertow: "adviser", "compromise", "you accepted". The accusations are not precise—some people are angry at the system, some at the investors, some at you specifically. But the way those words gather on your shoulders feels precise enough.

Your internal voice mills through possibilities and consequences—justice, expediency, political damage, the fragile thread of trust you braided with so many hands. Each possibility has teeth. You hear, under the market sound, the distant drone of a television news van wheeling into place. The council pavilion becomes a staging ground for narratives.

[Visual: Close-up on your notebook, the leather damp where you hold it; the pressed marsh grass inside is darkened. Your silver ring is a cold weight at your finger. A camera lens ticks as it focuses.]

Eli approaches, and this time his voice comes from closer quarters.

Eli Navarro [low]: "You don't have to carry all of it alone, Aria. I can rally the Helix crew for temporary pontoons, patch the worst of the scour. We can keep vendors afloat while you—" (he falters) "—figure out the council."

You see the way hope sits on him—practical, immediate, trawled up from making-do. His solution is hands-on, like a bandage. His eyes, amber dimmed under rain, are complex; unless you look for softness you might read the strain as blame. He is trying to hold something—your shoulders, perhaps, or the larger project.

Aria Solen [quiet]: "I appreciate it. We need boots on the water. We also need answers."

Eli Navarro: "Then let's get both. I'll pull men for pontoons. You—" (he searches your face) "—don't hide behind process when people are hurt."

That sentence lodges in your throat. His bluntness is not an accusation, strictly speaking, but it is a wedge. You feel the old reflex to protect the project, to triage optics first so the long work can continue. That reflex is what brought you to the council room and led you to accept a phased compromise. You wonder now whether "phase" translated into "hurry".

Jun steps in, offering what he can—a place where data and consequence meet.

Jun Park: "We can sample the clamp residue. There may be metallurgy breakdown from improper torque or from deliberate sabotage. It will take hours. In the meantime, the council will convene. Lina said she'd call an emergency review."

At Lina's arrival, her face is pinched, eyes shining with a tired, diplomatic fatigue. She moves with the care of someone who has to be the bridge between rhetoric and restitution.

Lina Voss: "We will form an emergency committee. My office will request an independent structural audit. But we need calm testimony, documented claims—"

Marta, already short with council procedure, laughs bitterly.

Marta Quin: "Calm testimony? People lost everything. Documented claims won't feed mouths this week."

The crowd begins to break into smaller knots—vendors huddling, council aides whispering. The coalition you helped build—the braided compromise that felt like a compromise-sizing of everything you loved—starts to fray along old fault lines: preservationists muttering against developers, investors flinching at the smell of legal exposure, aldermen retreating into process-speak.

Caden now chooses containment over empathy.

Caden Holt [incisive]: "Public outrage is predictable. We must secure the market, announce emergency aid, and emphasize our commitment to transparent investigation. Assign blame only when we have cause."

An ugly chorus answers: "Assign blame." The words are not a question; they are verdicts in search of a judge.

You stand in the center of these currents, feeling an old, private fear resurface—what if the compromise you brokered becomes the wedge that destroys the very community it aimed to save? You had wanted to weave trade-offs into resilience; now trade-offs read as betrayals.

Your chest tightens with inherited grief—the sibling lost to a storm is a memory that still arrives like a sudden tide. That grief is the ember beneath your decisions; it had propelled you into public life. Now that ember burns against you, and you smell smoke.

Eli presses a hand to your shoulder, solid and rough; you sense both his steadiness and impatience.

Eli Navarro: "We can put planks across the worst of it. We can keep people trading, keep kids fed. But if we only paper over this so capital keeps rolling, people will remember how we chose speed over them."

Aria Solen: (after a long breath) "I know."

Your voice sounds small. It is smaller than the chorus of accusation, smaller than the hiss of the pumps and the distant press. You want to write a statement, a defense; you want to summon words that will hold—"We did our best"—but you know honesty is its own heavy instrument. Your pragmatic streak warps into avoidance: you could quiet things now, fix optics, persuade investors not to pull out. Or you could lay yourself bare and ask for accountability. Or you could mobilize the market's memory—vendors and elders—to make the council listen in a way cold data never will.

Jun's tablet buzzes; new data scrolls up like a tide chart. He looks at you with an expression that is equal parts colleague and moralist—his scientific mind demanding you weigh evidence and consequence.

Jun Park: "There will be finger-pointing, Aria. If you led negotiations that accepted those timeline concessions, you'll be in the line of sight. I can help with an independent analysis, but the political heat will crater reputations faster than the waves crater our shore."

You close your eyes for one steady beat and hear Rafi's voice—a braided line of memory, the old man walking the tideways and reciting names of channels like prayer. You think of responsibility not as a ledger but as a relationship you promised to tend.

A hush falls—the market's momentary quiet before fractured speech resumes. You are suddenly aware of the camera angle from a nearby livestream: someone in a council trench coat has pointed a microphone at you. The world wants a face to pin to this failure. The coalition's seams are visible now, and you can see the choices like doors that will change how people trust you, and whether the co-op you built survives.

The choices you make in these first frantic hours will color how people feel about you. Each is an honest response—practical repair, public accountability, or a search for scientific truth—but none erase the fact that the coalition is splintering and the cameras want a single frame.

[Visual: Night presses down; the market lights are a faint constellation. Your boots are mired in wet as you step away from the immediate salvage to stand by Lina, Caden, Jun, and Eli clustered in the lamplight of a battered tent. The council pavilion is slow to act; they call for committees while the ground still settles.]

Inside the tent, the conversation fractures into blunt shards.

Lina Voss [worn, hurried]: "We will propose an emergency relief package to the council and request the independent audit. The language Linden proposed—"

Caden Holt [cold]: "—will be tempered. We must protect investor confidence. If capital pulls now, the town loses more than a few stalls."

Vendor [spitting]: "Which is your priority, Caden? Banks or people?"

Caden's jaw tightens. He answers economy with economy.

Caden Holt: "Stability. The funds keep services running. If we panic, we lose everything."

You feel that old choice again—speed for stability—and realize fully why people are looking at you. You, who bridged the compromise, are now the human hinge. Every argument you made for phasing, for governance, for conditional concessions, is on display with a new, sharper angle.

Eli's voice comes through the rain-scented air, quieter than before.

Eli Navarro: "What do you want to do?"

It is the simplest question, and also the most dangerous. If you move to defend the consortium, you will become their human face; if you expose their missteps, you risk political death; if you marshal the community, you risk legal and social upheaval that could break families' lives.

Your heart stutters through the catalog of costs. You see a vendor's child clutching a ruined lunch; you see Jun's pale, exact face; you see Caden's placid, corporate assurance; you hear Marta's weathered laugh turn to a sob. The tide has turned and you are in its eddy.

You inhale, tasting peat and the ghost of all your past compromises. A decision waits—one that will not only determine your career, but the kind of stewardship you will be remembered for.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand over the damp leather of your notebook. Your thumb finds the silver ring at your finger and turns it like a compass.]

You fold the notebook closed.

Aria Solen [quiet]: "We need truth, and we need to protect people. Not one at the expense of the other."

Silence, then movement—a dozen small decisions rippling outward.
