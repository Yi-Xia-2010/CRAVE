You taste metal and diesel on your tongue before you understand where it comes from — the broken clamp, the ripped anchor line, the sudden openness where the protective sill once held sediment from the channel. You were supposed to be here to shepherd a pilot, to keep things measured, not to witness the moment a seam in the plan becomes a wound.

Hands seize yours — Marta's, sticky with fish oil and grief. Her face is a map of years of salt and work; the lines are deeper now. "They cut the holding time," she spits, each word a stone. "Rushed bolts. Machines where hands should've been."

You see the vendor stalls like collapsed lungs: crates afloat in muddy water, a child's carved boat half-submerged, cloth flapping like a wounded bird. The reeds — the ones you mapped and defended — have been scoured in streaks. Mud clings wet between your fingers when you crouch to touch it; it's warm with the friction of being pulled away.

A man from the redevelopment crew, neat raincoat smeared with mud, passes you with a tablet held like armor. He keeps his eyes away. You feel the old, corporate polish of his posture even when mud is on his cuffs.

Jun arrives, breathless, tablet screen already splattered. He doesn't say "I told you so" — he says something colder, procedural: "The shear thresholds were exceeded in three locations. The clamp assemblies weren't tested in sequence with the pile-driving. There's localized scour at the base of the vendors' platforms." He looks at you, then at Marta, then away; his face is a thin ledger column of data and worry.

You want to catalogue blame like a list of supplies. Instead you catalog sensations: the grit under your nails, the smell of fermenting seaweed, the way Eli's voice has gone soft with something like guilt or something like fear.

You choose, or think you do — choices that are small embroidery on top of a larger tearing. Each action stitches a different kind of proximity: to machinery, to proof, to people. None of it will stop the way blame finds a face.

Across the water, someone murmurs that the clamps were installed faster than planned to meet a capital milestone. You hear the word "accelerated" as if it were a knife. The thought comes unbidden and ugly: timelines that preferred speed over patience, deadlines that preferred optics over the slow molting of sediment.

Eli finds you when you stand. He smells of smoke and the green sharpness of crushed leaves. He doesn't ask for your account; he hands you a soaked map marked with tidal lines. "They told us it would hold," he says (his voice is low, the humor gone). "We trusted the schedule."

You try to answer and find your throat a tight harbor. You think of the charter folded under your hand at the pavilion, the concession you let sit between ideal and possible. [Scene: Council Pavilion — Emergency Session | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The chamber's antiseptic lights feel harsher now. Renderings of the charter hang on the wall like tarnished mirrors. Council members file in, umbrellas dripping; the press corps arranges itself like a second, more invasive choir.]
[Sound: A microphone squeal, a chorus of low questions, the hum of a camera shutter; in the background, the distant slap of water against pilings]
[Music: A cold, descending minor arpeggio — the air between breaths thickens]

Lina stands at the front, hands folded, the earnestness in her face edged with fatigue. She meets your eyes, and you see a complexity there — support braided with calculation. The room is waiting for a focal point for grief and accountability. You can feel the collective need for a person to carry the narrative.

Caden clears his throat, every movement measured. "We will investigate this failure," he says, voice smooth as oiled wood. "But let us be careful. Rushing to judgement undermines our ability to respond." His smile is a daylight economy of calm; the stakeholders on his side breathe easier.

A vendor, Rosa, stands — her shaking voice cutting the room like a rope. "You came to our market telling us it'd be safer," she says (to the microphones, to the cameras, to you). "My stall is gone. My customers are gone. Children cried all morning." Her hands are steady but the rawness of it unspools the room. "Who vouched for this?"

Jun steps up to present initial findings, voice clipped with professional disbelief. "Preliminary analysis shows assembly sequence deviations. There were modifications to the installation protocol to meet deployment windows." The murmurs ripple through the room like tidal wash.

Caden's expression is unreadable. Lina's jaw tightens. You feel a current of people repositioning: allies shifting, investors whispering about risk, council members glancing at one another like a flock turning.

Eli stands, and when he speaks, it's not with the charm that usually disarms a room but with a raw edge. "This isn't just about a clamp," he says. "It's about whose timelines mattered. Whose livelihoods were traded for a milestone on a spreadsheet." He looks at you for a single long beat, and you can feel everyone's gaze as if it were a weight.

Question after question lands on you. Reporters press microphones; council members ask for a point person. Someone asks if you had signed off on the accelerated schedule. You could answer with policy-speak, with a reframing. Or you could accept a simpler, heavier truth: that your name sits on drafts, that your counsel had given compromises room to take shape.

You remember stepping up to the podium when the charter was presented, fingers on the folder, a single ascending arpeggio promising motion. That motion now feels like a boat in storm swelling in the wrong direction.

[Scene: Small Office Off the Pavilion | Twilight]
[Visual: A cramped room smelling of old paper and coffee, a single window fogged by rain. The day’s light is thinning to pewter; the tide outside slaps slow and unrepentant at the pilings.]
[Sound: The distant council murmur like the ocean under glass, Jun's pen scratching on a form, Eli's low breath through parted lips]
[Music: Sparse piano, then silence punctuated by a single cello note — the emotional temperature drops]

You are alone with Jun and Eli and the pile of consequences. Jun lays out the legal contours: insurance claims, potential suits, the timelines for emergency funds. He speaks pragmatic sentences that feel, to you, like announcements of exile.

Eli's hands are still smelling of tar. He moves when you move, a human metronome of restlessness. He says, finally, "People are calling for a scapegoat. They'll want to know whether you chose speed over safety." His amber eyes are bright with protectiveness that could become something like fury.

You want to defend every decision you made, every calculus where you braided compromise with fidelity. But the truth is an animal that will not be coaxed into being smaller than it is. You hear in your mind Rafi's old admonition about working with the tide, not against it; the memory now a reproach — were you arrogant enough to think you could bend time into stability?

Jun folds his hands, pinching the bridge of his nose. "There's an engineered failure pattern," he says quietly. "Someone made a change to the clamp design under deadline pressure." He doesn't say who. He doesn't have to. The sentence hangs between you like lead.

Eli asks, plain and without ornament, "What do you want to do, Aria?"

It's a question that thins you to a line between ethics and survival. You imagine three different stories opening from your answer: naming and risking everything to reforge trust; quietly mending the damage to keep capital calm; lifting the market as a communal force to batter down systems. Each path scrapes away different parts of you, leaving other parts exposed.

You think of the people in the markets who slept with tarps over carts because the storms changed the shape of what "home" meant. You think of Jun's careful charts, of Caden's polished calm, of Lina's tired hinge-work. You think of the way your mentor's silver ring seemed to warm to your skin the day you decided the charter could hold.

"Tell me what you think will restore people," Eli says, and it's not only a tactical question but a plea: which part of you will you let carry the town's grief?
