Marta catches your eye from behind a stack of jars, her paint-smudged fingers offering a quick, fierce squeeze to your elbow as you pass. Eli Navarro: (grinning) "Cookies as civic infrastructure. Take one. They're reliable in storms."

You laugh, the sound thin and quick. It's a small bridge. Eli's laughter settles something low in your chest; his presence is a warm, improvised shelter.

Marta: "Aria—people will come. Lina called two neighborhoods; Old Man Rafi promised a story or two. But don't let the pretty words drown the hard parts." (She taps your shoulder, the motion like a benediction.) "Be sharp."

[Scene: Council Chambers / Redevelopment Pavilion | Midday]

You stand at the edge of the chamber, the leather of your notebook creased by your fingers. The ring on your thumb is cool. Lina slips up beside you, offering a smile that is more tentative than you're used to seeing from her.

Lina Voss: "Thank you for pulling everyone together. There are voices on both sides who can fold this into something ugly—if we don't speak plainly."

You: "Plainness won't be enough. We have to make people feel the future we're asking of them. Not just see numbers—feel it."

She nods, then inclines her head toward Caden. Across the room he remains composed—steel-blue eyes looking out over the crowd as though his plans were already arranged on the tide. His presence is an architectural argument: neat solutions, fast outcomes.

Caden Holt: (approaching, hands in pockets) "Aria. I'm glad you're here. We can find compromise. The redevelopment plan can be adapted to include community-managed green buffers." (He gives you an easy smile that carries the weight of legislative experience.) "This is the only way to secure funding quickly."

You feel the old reflex to argue—rows of spreadsheets, the idea of being swallowed into logistics—but Jun steps in, offering a quiet correction.

Jun Park: "Caden, the model thresholds you've relied on don't account for substrate loss in certain chenier shoals. If you seal everything at once, you could create unintended backflow patterns that stress the restored patches."

Caden: (cool) "We can run simulations. We need guarantees for investors."

Jun: (level) "Simulations aren't guarantees."

The room closes slightly in on itself, talk ricocheting off the walls. Vendors shift; someone coughs. You breathe in the sterile ozone mixed with distant peat, and you realize the fight is as much about language as it is about levees. The words "guarantee" and "stewardship" carry different weights.

Eli finds you in the crowd, leaning in to speak as if proximity could share courage.

Eli Navarro: "Whatever you do, we're behind you—on the docks, in the reeds. But I won't lie: if you get chewed by politics, you won't be easy to reach."

You: "I don't need things to be easy." (You feel your voice steady at the edges.) "I need them to be true."

A council aide calls names and the meeting begins to settle. Jun hands you a small flash drive—raw scans, annotated notes, a lifetime of careful data. His finger rests against yours for a whisper longer than necessary.

Jun Park: "If you lead with the story and the numbers, you get different ears. You can anchor people's fear with facts and their hope with the work we've already done."

[Scene: Partial Seawall & Reed Beds | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: A jagged slice of concrete speared into the surf; beside it, a stretch of reed beds where new plantings hold the marsh. The sun breaks through the clouds in a compassionate, coppery wash.]

[Music: Strings swell gently, carrying warmth and quiet resolve]

You walk the seawall, boots splashing in the shallow runoff pooled beside guard timbers. Salt stings your nostrils; the metal railing is cool under your palm. The repaired section holds—for now—and the reed beds beyond bend, counting the same rhythm of survival you and the team have tried to teach them.

Eli falls into step beside you, his voice low so only you can hear.

Eli Navarro: "They held the line. Rafi said the old ways wouldn't be enough, but he smiled walking through the reeds today. That has to mean something."

You: "It means the work is doing what we told it to do. It doesn't mean the work is finished."

He stops, turning to face you properly. The sun catches the edges of his hair; his amber eyes are bright.

Eli Navarro: "Aria—if you choose the hard truth in front of everyone, if you let it cost you—do you know what you'll do next? Will you still stay? Will we still…"

The question is not finished, and you feel its unfinishedness like an ache. There is no way to pretend you haven't been counting costs in your head: political capital, the lab's funding, the co-op's leases. You also know, with a clarity that has been gathering like stormwater behind a berm, that some choices are not about keeping everything safe; they're about being honest about what safety will cost.

You press your thumb against the cool silver of your ring, feeling its edge like an anchor.

You: "I don't know everything that happens after speaking the truth. I do know this: if we let fear of the immediate losses stop us from naming what's wrong, we'll keep repairing the same break until the whole thing unravels."

Eli says nothing for a beat. Then he nods, the thin line of his mouth folded into something like acceptance.

Eli Navarro: "Then we'll be on the other side of it, whether that's together or learning to be otherwise. I'll keep the Helix running."

You: "Keep the Helix running," you repeat, tasting the phrase and all its sturdier meanings—sustenance, stubbornness, home.

Backstage, your notebook is closed and warm, thumb still finding the silver ring. Jun hands you a final, paper copy of his annotations. Marta crosses her arms and gives you that look she reserves for both scolding and blessing.

Marta Quin: "Don't be tidy about it. Tell them what it cost to get here. People need to know you'll be the same person if it goes wrong as if it goes right."

You meet Caden's eye across the room. He stands with practiced composure, his lapel pin catching the window light like a small, indifferent sun. Lina watches with an expression you learn to read as someone balancing two fragile things—her duty and the town.

Lina Voss: (quiet) "Whatever you say will change the course. I can protect you from some of the fallout if you want me to—there are ways to manage the messaging."

You consider that: quiet containment, smoothing of public reaction, the trade-off of truth for immediate stability. The other courses in front of you are equally costly: a public confession that could fracture alliances but rebuild trust, or a grassroots mobilization that forces systemic change through testimony.

Jun slides a hand briefly against the microphone's stand as if to anchor himself.

Jun Park: "Your credibility is the hinge. You can open a door wide and let the air of accountability freshen everything. Or you can close it and tamp down the smoke. Both are leadership. Both will have consequences."

Your chest tightens, not with panic but with the precise gravity of someone about to choose a direction for the town and for your life. The room seems to lean with you. For a breath you think of the reed beds, of the way the plants bent and took the water and kept living. You think of the vendor's child, the scar on your palm from patching the first failed berm, the quiet way Jun keeps his hair tucked behind one ear when he's nervous.

You feel a rising—not just of apprehension but of a clear, forward energy that hums like a tuned string under your ribs. This is not collapse. This is a decision arriving with its honest costs and its possible continuations. The possible futures spread like channels across a low tide, each one colored by labor and love.

Your fingers curl around the microphone's stand. You breathe in salt and peat and the warm, yeasty smell of Eli's pastries from earlier. The room waits.
