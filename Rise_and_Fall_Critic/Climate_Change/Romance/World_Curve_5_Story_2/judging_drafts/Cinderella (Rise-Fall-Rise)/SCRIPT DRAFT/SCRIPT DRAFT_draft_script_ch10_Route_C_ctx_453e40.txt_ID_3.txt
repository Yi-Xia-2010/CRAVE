The notification pings and you know, before you look, what it will be: a headline threaded into the town's sleep. Your thumb hovers above the screen; the light of the article washes the inside of your palm in the same dull blue you used to read grant reports by. You breathe in the greenhouse air—rich damp soil, the sharp bright of lemon verbena—and the breath steadies you.

You leaked it. The draft, the models, the procurement notes that didn't add up. You did it because the town deserved to know what was being traded under the language of "protection" and "development." You did it because quiet deals had already cost homes. Guilt ripples along your ribs—old and familiar—but tonight it mixes with something else: a current that could carry more than ruin. It could carry reckoning.

Jun's message pops up: "You sure about tonight? Data's everywhere. Call?" The three dots appear as he types. Your thumb hovers a second longer and you answer.

You: "Yes. Stay up. If we get calls from reporters, funnel them to Marta first."

Jun: "On it. I'm compiling counter-analyses. If they ask for methodology, we'll have a clear line. Don't go looking for martyrdom, Aria—"

You smile despite the tightness in your chest. Jun's practical voice is an anchor. You used to sail on certainty, map in hand; now you ride rumors and ethics.

Footsteps on the gangplank — familiar, half-turned into a grin.

Eli: "Figured I'd find you where the plants are warm." (He sets down a thermos and two mugs; steam fogs the glass between you.) "News' been loud. You look… alive." (His look is complex; you can't tell if it's pride, worry, or both.)

You: "No one can sleep yet." (You cup the mug. It smells of ginger. The heat moves into your palms and out along your spine.) "Jun's compiling. Marta's fielding calls. I thought—" You falter, because saying it aloud crystallizes the risk. "—I thought the truth needed to be louder than the renderings."

Eli leans his elbows on a crate of seedlings. "That's... big, Aria. Public truth is messy. People will be scared. People will be furious. But they'll also be awake. You know I back what you did, even if the path scares me." He hesitates. "Did you expect it to blow up this fast?"

You: "No. But momentum works like tides—sometimes the first swell looks like noise and then it draws everything in." Your voice steadies into that pragmatic cadence you rely on when framing plans. "We leak facts; the town debates; Lina and Caden can't hide. That hearing Lina's going to attend—if we keep pushing, it's not just a show. It becomes a place to force accountability."

Eli: (quiet) "And if it breaks something in the meantime?"

You: "Then we'll rebuild it better." The phrase feels both fragile and true. You think of the wetland, pieced back boardwalk by boardwalk. You think of the people who returned after the flood and made a stronger berm together. Repair is patient work, but it matters.

[Scene: Old Pier Market | Late Night]

You move through the market, a presence that draws and steadies. Vendors catch your eye—one nods, another hunches their shoulders as if the weight of the news is a tide pulling at their boots. Marta waits by her paint-splattered cart, arms folded, face lit by a phone screen showing the headlines.

Marta: "You did the right thing." Her voice is brusque, like a hand gripping a rake. "Not everyone will like it, Aria, but people deserve to know when the math is shaped to sell someone else's idea of safety."

Vendor (Maru): "But what's going to happen to the docks? My cousin needs the piers open. If investors pull now, how do families eat next season?" (He wipes his palms on his apron, eyes urgent.) "We ain't asking you to stop the boats. We ask you to remember the boats."

You: You take a breath and keep your answer honest but forward-looking. "This isn't about scaring away help — it's about changing the terms. If procurement's shady, investors will leave only to be replaced by worse deals. We expose the flaws so that any support that comes is conditional—built with us, not over us. Maru: "And if the council blames you for scaring them off?" His question is practical—less accusation than fear dressed in fact.

You: "Then we stitch the economy to the ecology. Start small—floating beds, shared processing, co-op agreements that guarantee work even during transitions. Old Man Rafi appears as if conjured by the tide, his cap bulbous with pins, hands smelling of salt and old sea rope.

Rafi: "You did a bold thing, child." He sets a palm on your shoulder—rough, callused, steady. "The old tide songs say: 'When the shoreline's story gets rewritten, the people must rewrite how they walk.' You'll have the young who shout and the old who clutch the past. But the land remembers hands that pull in together." (He looks at you, his expression complex.)

You nod; Rafi's words map onto a deeper part of you: restoration isn't only technical—it's a lineage.

Lina's name is on everyone's lips. Her press conference has been scheduled into a council hearing after the leak stirred enough public outrage to make silence untenable. A reporter you know leans in: "Will you testify, Aria?" Her pen hovers like a small, hungry bird.

You: "If called, yes. I don't want to grandstand—just present the facts and explain what's needed for true resilience." Your answer is measured. Saying you'll testify is both invitation and shield.

Inside, you see Lina through the glass—she's speaking to someone on the phone, then slides a hand over her face. Her expression is complex: composed, yes, but threaded with fatigue and something like resolve. You cannot read everything from a single frame; the Schrödinger of it suits the day—she's both ally and bureaucrat to be swayed.

Caden arrives in a quieter, more controlled stream of people—his suits unchanged by rain, the cuffwatch flicking like polished intent. A camera catches him as he passes.

Caden: (in a recorded clip shown on a phone among the crowd) "We welcome scrutiny. But fear-driven headlines should not derail essential infrastructure. We will lay out facts at the hearing." His voice is steady. The words themselves try to reclaim calm.

You fold your fingers around your notebook; the silver ring on your hand is warm from the sun. The town's breath presses around you—expectant, nervous, hopeful. The leak did what you wanted: it opened the conversation from behind closed doors into the piazza. Marta finds you and tugs your sleeve. "Be honest in the hearing," she says. "Tell them why the bay matters, not just numbers. Make them see the hands who will live it."

You nod. The tone of your reply is simple and rising. "We make them see. We'll show living examples, volunteers, data—everything. This hearing will be a terrain of truth if we make it one."

Eli stands beside you—he hasn't chosen words so much as presence. His gaze is steady; his shoulders square like someone bracing to lift a beam. His reaction, too, is complex. You inhale the brine-tinged air, tasting hope as sharp and immediate as salt. The day ahead will be hard; the stakes are higher than ever. But for the first time in months, you feel the town's pulse align with your own: hands ready, plans forming, truth pried into the light.

You: You let the hopeful cadence of your answer carry the weight it needs. "I think people will no longer accept answers that don't hold them. That's enough to change the game."
