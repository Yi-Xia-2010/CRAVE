[Scene: Old Pier Market | Morning]
[Visual: Boardwalk framed by tarps and string-lanterns; wet cobbles reflecting a thin, silver sky]
[Music: Light, hopeful piano with a steady, ascending motif]
[Sound: Market chatter, gull calls, a kettle hissing on a vendor's stove]

You step off the lab's boardwalk clutching the crate Marta pressed into your hands. The market unfolds like a living map—patchwork awnings, people folding their hands around steaming cups, the smell of smoked fish and sweet dough braided together. Your notebook is warm against your ribs where you tucked it; under the lid, seed packets rustle like small promises.

A breeze smells of salt and peat, a reminder of why you're here. The corridor you want to stitch together exists in fragments: a hand-built check dam on one street, someone else's rooftop garden on the next pier. Today you need faces—not renderings—and a language that can make a living shoreline sound like family, not abstraction.

Marta threads through the crowd beside you, carrying a battered megaphone tucked under her arm. Her overalls are streaked with yesterday's mud and today's paint; she moves like someone who knows what will and won't hold in this weather.

Marta: "You look like you swallowed a tide chart, Aria. Smile—people trust smiles."

You let the corners of your mouth loosen. The motion is small, but it loosens the rope at your sternum; you need that ease in the meeting later.

You: "I'll try not to talk like a grant proposal. Promise."

Marta: (grinning) "Good. Tell them about how the marsh slows the surges. Tell them about Rafi's pier when he was a boy. Make it stick to place, not spreadsheets."

You walk a slow circuit. A vendor—an old woman folding kelp into parcels—reaches out and pats the crate.

Vendor 1: "Seeds for the rain gardens? Haven't seen these in a while. Your team's doing right by this town, love."

You: "We're trying. If people hold a packet in their palm today, it's different than a rendering. It's about what they can touch tomorrow."

Vendor 1: "Touch is how we remember." (She hands you a wrapped kelp snack.) "Eat. If you're going to change the world, you need your hands full."

You accept the snack. The sea-sweet taste is immediate, grounding—salt and something green. You notice how small courtesies ripen the conversation with strangers into something like trust.

[Visual: A low, curved barge sits parallel to the market, rigged with hanging planters. Eli leans on its rail, sleeves rolled, holding a small mesh bag of dark, spongy samples—buoyant substrate modules you've designed together in rough sketches.]
[Sound: Eli's boots thumping on wet wood; distant laughter]

Eli: (calling) "Aria! Look at these—lighter than last batch and the cordage's holding. I swear, they bob like little heroic things."

You cross over. Up close you can see the modules: layers of recycled buoyant foam wrapped with coir and matting, flecked with seeds and tiny tubes for tidal nutrient exchange. They look like hopeful little boats for plants.

You: "They're compact. Easy to explain with a model. Jun can pull up the inundation scenarios—show them how these sit through a high tide."

Eli: (passing you a sample) "Here—feel it. Not just talk science; let people grip it. They'll get it."

You close your fingers around the sample. It's cool, fibrous where the coir peeks through, buoyant under pressure. You imagine it attached to a shallow fringing mat, reeds curling up through the weave. The image aligns with the data in your head: drag reduction, sediment trapping, community-run maintenance.

Eli watches you as if he's reading the plan in your face. There's a steadiness in the way he waits for you to speak, and that steadiness pulls something loose inside you.

You: "We'll put two on the demo table. One anchored near the children's workshop, one by Rafi's stall—so folks see scale."

Eli: (a quick, pleased laugh) "And cookies on the Helix to bribe them into staying for the talk?"

You both grin. The warmth of that shared joke is small but buoyant. It makes the next part—arguing for stewardship in a room full of polished renderings—less like a solo climb and more like a climb with a partner handing you a rope.

Jun appears beside you, tablet in hand, rain-dappled hair stuck to his temple. He looks like every statistic you love: bright-intense and slightly frantic.

Jun: "I ran the projected storm matrices with the new substrate assumptions. There's a scenario where even a modest fringe reduces peak surge energy by a measurable percentage—enough to keep low-lying shops dry during a one-in-ten storm."

You: "That's the language we need. Not just 'resilience'—'keeps our doors open.'"

Jun: (squints, tapping) "I'll make a two-slide graphic. One visceral picture of the corridor now, with people fishing and kids with boots—then the same shot with the living shoreline slowing the water. Emotional hook, then clear outcome."

You: "Good. Emotional hook first, then the data that shows we didn't rely on hope alone."

Jun: (softly) "And don't forget your line about stewardship—how this is a job the town can keep. Contracts, maintenance co-op—practicals."

You nod. Your fingers ghost the thin silver ring on your hand—an inherited talisman. You remind yourself to let the ask be both a technical proposition and a moral appeal. People commit when they see a future they're called to protect.

You move through the market gathering small stories. Old Man Rafi sits on a low stool, carving a miniature pier from driftwood. The lines on his face are like tidal maps.

Old Man Rafi: (gravelly) "You remember when this pier was taller and the kids had laces of rope at their wrists to stop them from falling off. Storms took things, we learned to stitch them back. It ain't just concrete that holds us—it's stories."

You crouch to look at the tiny pier in his hand. He holds it out to you without a fuss.

You: "Stories are part of the shoreline. If we don't tell them, the place becomes a slideshow."

Old Man Rafi: (prodding the wood with his knife) "Tell them how the marsh used to sing when the tide came in. Tell 'em why we still put talismans on the poles. Make 'em hear the old reasons for doing the right thing."

Marta passes you a roll of handbills and a stack of small, laminated diagrams Jun made. People are starting to crowd the demo table: a woman in a fisherman's jacket wants to know who pays for maintenance; a teenage volunteer fidgets, eager to help set down mats; a toddler tugs on a rope and laughs.

Vendor 2: "My boy's been here since the flood. If this keeps his small repair shop from being a puddle again, count me in. But you can't just talk pretty words. You need money for posts, for training."

You: (sharp but steady) "That's why we're proposing training stipends and a maintenance co-op. People do the work; the council helps with materials. It's not a donation—it’s an investment that pays in keeping livelihoods."

Vendor 2: "Hmm." (He studies you, measuring whether this is a dream or a plan.)

Your throat tightens—not from doubt this time, but from the work of translating grief into policy. You remember the sibling you lost to a surge and how that loss made every permit feel like a life-or-death ledger. You push that memory into a language that invites, not repels.

Eli slips a hand to your shoulder as if to steady you, then pulls it back the moment your eyes meet his—respectful of the line between professional and personal.

Eli: (soft) "They listen when you make it local. They follow when you make it doable."

You: "We make it doable."

Jun hustles over, tablet flashing a simplified graphic. It's immediate—before-and-after frames overlaying the market with a translucent living shoreline. Faces in the crowd lean in; someone whistles low.

Jun: "Council's visual people will be swamped with renderings. Give them a model they can hold. Give them a story to care about. I'll be at the back with hard numbers."

You: "And I'll open. Marta will close with logistics and sign-up sheets."

Marta: (hooking an elbow through yours) "And I'll make sure people leave with something to do tomorrow. That's how things hold."

There is a momentum that hums now through the market. Hands are folding seed packets; someone ties a sample module to a child's toy boat to see how it rides. Laughter threads in the same rhythm as preparation.

You look at your notebook one last time, finger tapping the page where you wrote your opening—short, human, with room for a data line at the end.

Your internal voice shapes the first sentence like a knotted rope: it needs to be plain enough for a neighbor to repeat and strong enough to anchor a grant officer's attention.

You inhale the salt-and-smoke air, the market crowd parting and rejoining around you like tide lines, and feel momentum as a tangible thing—this braided, human kind of momentum that carries promises forward.

[Visual: The market bleeds into late morning light; a queue now wanders toward the Council Chambers steps where small groups are gathering.]
[Sound: A distant bell; footsteps on wet stone]

Jun: "We should move. Council's lobby will be a pressure cooker. We get there early, set up the models and the graphic. Lina said she'd nudge the schedule, but timing's still tight."

You: (a steadying thought) "We go in as a team. We enter with people at our back, not as a petition."

Eli: "I can drop off the Helix cookies at the entrance and run the sample display. Give them something to hold before they sit."

Marta: "I'll cue the sign-up sheets. Old folks first, then those who promise to help on Monday. Keep it orderly; we want faces in the seats."

You feel something like a soft lift in your chest. The day's hours have stitched a net beneath your feet. Maybe this will work. Maybe it won't. But people are here, and people are ready to do the work.

You step toward the marble steps of the Council Chambers, the air colder here, as if the building itself tests resolve. You pause on the top step, the demo modules strapped to the crate at your side, the two-slide graphic on Jun's tablet glowing dimly—an altar of both science and story.

A hundred small choices compressed into this moment.

You tighten your grip on the crate, taste the briny tang in your mouth, and feel the piano motif rise in your chest as if the day were holding its breath with you.

You think of the line you practiced—short, local, practical—and how it will land in a room of polished renderings and polished people. You think of the hands that trusted you at the market. You think of Eli's steady smile and Jun's frantic competence and Marta's fierce calm.

Everything pulse-taps a single instruction: begin.

[Visual: The double doors to Council Chambers are ahead, glossy with rain, the emblem of the redevelopment consortium visible in the lobby through the glass]
[Music: The piano motif swells then softens into a single held note—a silent invitation to move forward]

You breathe in once, a slow, ocean-wide inhale. The moment stretches, poised between the market's warmth and the Council's cool light. The choice to step through is the hinge on which the day's work will pivot.
