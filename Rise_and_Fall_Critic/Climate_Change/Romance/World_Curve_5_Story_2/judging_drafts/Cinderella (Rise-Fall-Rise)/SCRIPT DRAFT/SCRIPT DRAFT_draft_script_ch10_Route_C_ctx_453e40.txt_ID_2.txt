[Sound: A low, restless murmur of voices, the soft slap of hull against piling, the occasional gull; the click of a phone camera in the dark.]

You are still braced to the feeling of having set something loose. The document you sent—ten pages of procurement notes, modeling caveats, and flagged procurement bids—feels like a stone dropped into a quiet pool; the ripples are already lapping the pier. You can taste the salt and iron on your tongue and the metallic tang of adrenaline beneath it.

Jun's last message pinged the moment you hit send: We cross-checked. This will blow the lid off their timeline. You remember staring at his words until they blurred into the blinking cursor. There was no stage whisper. There was only the decision and the small tightness behind your ribs that felt, absurdly, like relief.

You lift the kettle and listen.

Eli is there, sleeves rolled, seed trays stacked beside him. He hands you a steaming paper cup before he asks whether you slept. His hands smell of peat and the rope-sap varnish he uses to seal the hull seams.

Eli: "You did it. You absolute menace." (He smiles, the amber crinkling around his eyes softening.) "How does it feel?"

You: "Like a thing I've been holding underwater for years finally broke surface." (Your voice is steadier than you feel.) "Scary. Necessary. And—" You pause because your throat catches—"—scary again."

Eli studies you for a long beat, then lets out a breath that sounds like both a laugh and a reprimand.

Eli: "That line about 'over-optimistic hydrodynamic modeling'—you put that one in for drama, right? Because if not, Caden will have his lawyers polishing their shoes by morning."

You: "I didn't put anything in for drama." (You find yourself pressing the ring on your finger, the silver cold and real.) "I highlighted what Jun flagged: boundary conditions, ignored sediment loads, and the procurement clause that would lock maintenance to external contractors for twenty years. If council signs this as-is, the co-ops won't be consulted on long-term care."

Eli: (softly) "So you did the right thing."

You: "Rightness isn't a blanket. It's a risk now—we're visible."

He reaches across a crate and taps the side of your cup.

Eli: "Risk is a kind of momentum. Might be the shove the place needs." (He grins.) "Also, the Helix is officially the rumor factory now. Marta's already on the blower."

A chorus of footsteps above the hull—Marta's steady, urgent cadence—then a knock at the hatch. You can smell the market on her clothes: smoke from fish skewers, the sharp tang of citrus and cured net dye.

Marta: (whisper-yell) "They're already blowing it up on the water line! Vendors are half-raising banners. Old Man Rafi's been telling anyone who'll listen about 'the paper that wouldn't let the sea lie.'"

You (internal): Momentum and risk. You catalog that again, like a checklist you need to carry into every conversation. The lift in your chest is real. People are awake. That is the first and truest kind of progress.

[Scene: Old Pier Market | Dawn — Morning after the leak]
[Visual: Tarps half-dried from last night's rain, glints of salt on wooden counters, hand-lettered signs added overnight: "TRANSPARENCY NOW" and "WHOSE SHORE? OURS!". People gather in small knots; faces are lit with the low gold of dawn.]

You move through the market carrying Marta's photocopies and your own jittery normal. Hands reach—some to take a packet of seeds, some to take a page of the leaked draft—and each grip carries a small ceremony of trust and outrage. A child presses a damp thumb into a margin where you've written a note to yourself: "Check sediment lines," and you smile, which shocks you with how light it feels.

Jun finds you beside the fish stall, hunched over a thermos and a tablet. His breath fogs in the cool air; his glasses have beads of condensation. He looks tired and strangely elated, a scientist who has finally watched a hypothesis become a headline.

Jun: "We expected pushback, not this—this tidal of people." (He taps the tablet; comments and translations scroll.) "Local blogers, two independent modelers, and—get this—an overseas university tagged Caden's firm for overfitting a parameter set."

You: "Overfitting." (You try to make your mouth shape it like a modest victory and then stop yourself.) "We need to make sure the framing is about safety and stewardship. Not just the flaws for the sake of scorekeeping."

Jun: "Agreed. If we focus on the maintenance clause, Lina's liability exposure will have to be addressed at the council level. It's the lever."

A vendor—one you know by the scar on her knuckle and the way she folds the newspaper into tidy packets—lowers her voice.

Marta: (elbows you) "You should speak. People love you when you speak. You are a model firm handshake with data." (She frowns.) "Or you should let them speak. Let Old Man Rafi have the pedestal and get people to listen. Either way, be seen."

Your chest constricts—it's the old habit: to solve by moving parts rather than exposing yourself. But the market is full of faces you want to keep safe, of livelihoods that depend on being heard.

[Visual: The same cold, antiseptic light from before; now there is a buzz of cameras and an added chaos of placards being signed at the foot of the steps. Security has a hush-and-proceed efficiency. Lina arrives, raincoat over her arm, eyes wide with the strain of being pulled in every direction.]

Lina stands before the council desk, hands pinched around a cup of coffee gone tepid. When she sees you in the crowd, her expression flickers—surprise, calculation, then something like relief.

Lina: (low) "Aria. You understand why I had to call a hearing, yes? The public needs answers." (She looks not entirely pleased but flattened by inevitability.) "They demanded it. I had no choice."

You: (quietly) "You had a choice in when to call it." (There is no accusation in your tone—only the weary catalog of consequences.) "Now it is public. Now it's about more than renderings."

Lina: "I know." (She breathes in.) "I'm putting Caden and his consultants under oath. Jun's analysis will be entered into the record. But understand—this will be messy. Economically, the town will feel strain. Investors may pull until we stabilize governance."

You: "We understood that when we chose this route." (You press your palm flat to the splintered wood of the railing, tasting the dust of a hundred decisions.) "We prepared for transparency because we think long term. Short term hurts. We accept that."

Lina's hands tremble very slightly.

Lina: "Then we'll need to be precise. No theatrics. We need to anchor arguments in timelines, in costs—Jun's phrasing must be surgical. If rhetoric leads, the whole thing will devolve into performative outrage."

Jun: (from behind you, quietly insistent) "I can tighten the error bounds, run the sediment model on the additional datasets. But we'll need time, and we need to protect the chain of custody on the documents. Caden's legal team will sniff for a way to discredit the dataset."

Your internal voice runs through mitigation checklists—archive timestamps, witness logs, copies of the original files stored offsite. You feel the pragmatic part of you click into place like a gear. But underneath that, like a spring wound and released, is the warming certainty that people are awake and acting.

Old Man Rafi arrives, leaning on his cane, sea-scraped hands folded as if in prayer. He does not speak at first, and when he does, everyone leans closer.

Old Man Rafi: "They can build walls. They can scrape the shoreline clean and name it progress until the names wash off. But a shore remembers. We taught our children how to read the tide for good reason." (His voice is rough and kind.) "This hearing—it's a chance to remember together."

There is applause, not loud but steady. It vibrates through the room like a small tide.

Caden: (from the council dais, smooth, precise) "We are cooperating fully. Any errors in the draft are clerical and will be addressed. The proposed plan remains the most viable way to secure economic safety for this town."

You: (internal) Caden's confidence is a polished thing. It was always persuasive in committees. But it's a polished stone that now has a crack running through it where light can get in.

You can feel the current shift. The leak has pulled an entire conversation into sunlight. You are part of making a public truth visible, and that knowledge gives you a steady heat that feels like hope.

You watch people intersect with purpose. There is an ease here you haven't allowed yourself in months—the sense that messy, human effort will be the engine of change, not polished blueprints alone. Old Man Rafi contributes a tidal anecdote; Jun sketches a simplified chart for lay readers; Marta organizes a rota to make sure every household gets a copy of the leaked pages and an invitation to the hearing.

Eli: "The Helix turns into a war room on Wednesday. Snacks supplied by Marta. Animations by Jun. Chatter managed by—" (he nods at you) "—you. We don't need a PR firm. We have the truth and people with recipes."

You: (half-laughing) "And we have cookies. I suppose that's the modern revolution." (You feel lighter—like a poultice being applied to a raw place.)

Jun: "We should prepare a concise one-page brief for council members that outlines our challenges with the modeling assumptions and a timeline of maintenance costs under different procurement structures."

Marta: "And we should bring stories. Facts are the backbone, yes, but people remember faces. Old Man Rafi's story about the storm lanterns—put that after Jun's one-pager. Make it human."

You: "We'll do both. We need to be rigorous and tender." (The words come out steadier than you expect. They fit you like a coat.)

A vendor from the market arrives with steaming skewers and places them on the table like an offering.

Vendor: "For late-night morale. You lot earned it."

You take a skewer and feel the warmth in your palm, a small domestic miracle amid policy and statute. Around you, people argue in overlaps—about phrasing, about who will ask what question at the hearing, about whether to invite a particular scientist as an expert witness. Friction and generation: simultaneous.

Eli catches your eye and tilts his head, a silent question—Do you want to sleep? Do you want to work? Do you want to sit and let this be?

You think of the sibling you lost, of a promise you made in a phone that night of the flood, of the ring on your finger, and of the look in Lina's eyes when she said, "I'm putting Caden and his consultants under oath." You feel the gravity of that promise and the buoyancy of people moving together.

You breathe in salt and peat and coffee and feel, for the first time in a long time, that the swell is taking you somewhere not just dangerous but necessary, and maybe—if you keep rowing—safe.

A piece of paper flutters across the table: a council-scheduled hearing time, stamped. It's small and sharp and precise.

You feel the lift: the town will argue this in public, the documents will be scrutinized, and people will have to decide what kind of future they want. The moment is not clean, but it is alive.

You rest your hand on the table, on the map, on the seedlings, and you decide the immediate next moves—who speaks when, which evidence goes forward, how to hold the community steady through the heat of public scrutiny.

Every sound in the Helix feels purposeful—voices overlapping like a coral of effort. Wherever the outcome leans, momentum is real; risk and hope have become a braided rope you are holding along with everyone else.

You look up at the night sky, the haloed lights reflecting on the water, and hold that image like an instruction.
