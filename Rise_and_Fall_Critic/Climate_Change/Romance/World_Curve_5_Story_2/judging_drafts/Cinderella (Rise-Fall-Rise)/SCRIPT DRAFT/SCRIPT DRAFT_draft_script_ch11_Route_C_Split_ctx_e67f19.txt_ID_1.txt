[Scene: Jun's Lab | Early Morning]
[Visual: Fluorescent bulbs humming over stainless counters; a damp towel draped over an overturned tray. Glass vials catch flecks of morning light like trapped dew. A wall-mounted monitor cycles through email headers—one subject line stands out: "Grant Funding — Immediate Hold."]
[Sound: Low whir of the incubators, distant gulls and the soft slap of tide against pilings]
[Music: Sparse, minor-key piano — a slow, sinking motif]

You wake to the sound of the lab already working—pumps cycling, fans exhaling. Your notebook lies open on the counter where you left it, lines of measurements half-drawn. For a second, the world obeys the pattern you've always trusted: data, plan, execution. Then Jun's voice breaks the rhythm.

Jun Park: "I didn't want to—" (he exhales, too loud) "—but finance just froze the rest of the grant. Until this clears up."

You feel the table tilt in the same way land tilts after a storm: not violently, but enough that everything ordered on its surface shifts. Jun sets the tablet face-up between you; the notification's timestamp is a small, brutal proof.

You: "Show me."

Jun Park: "They cited reputational risk. They can't be seen tied to a 'controversial' local incident—" (he gestures helplessly at the monitor) "—and they want audits, third-party reviews... it could be months."

You run your thumb along the spine of your notebook, feeling the grind of the paper. Months is a gulf. Months is the window in which seedlings die, in which repair crews have to be paid. You picture the corridor's check dams, half-built; you picture families who rent one-room flats with one heater between them and a frozen sea in winter. The freeze on funding is not a line on a spreadsheet—it is a narrowing of possibility into risk.

Jun Park: "I can try to escalate. I can call the program officer—"

You: "Do it." (Short, automatic. You try to clip the worry from your voice and fail.)

Jun hesitates, fingers hovering over the screen.

Jun Park: "Also—legal contacted me. They want all raw data logs until the investigation wraps. No sharing. No conferences. They say it's to safeguard the grant."

The air in the lab feels thicker, and smell of wet soil on your boots is suddenly cloying. You breathe it in anyway—peat and the faint electrical tang of the incubators. You are good at protocols; you are less practiced at this sudden, public unraveling.

Jun Park: "Whatever we do, we have to be cautious. This is bigger than our lab, Aria." (There is a softness in the way he says your name—technical familiarity turned personal.) "People are watching the wrong things."

You keep your face still because you have to. Inside, your chest feels like a tide pool—contained, reflective, and suddenly a little contaminated. You can list repercussions: investors, council votes, vendors. But under the lists is a small, colder thing: the knowledge that a single choice—the leak—has sparked a chain no single person can fully direct anymore.

You fold your hands over your notebook. The leather is warm from your grip. Your mentor's silver ring—the thin band you never take off—presses into the pad of your finger. Guilt is not always a shout; often it's the soft insistence under everything you try to build.

[Scene: Old Pier Market | Midday]
[Visual: Tarps pulled taut, some stalls closed, others half-open. A cluster of vendors speaks in tight, rising tones; a handful of journalists weave like gulls, small cameras flashing the same image: "LEAK SPARKS FUNDING FREEZE".]
[Sound: Heated voices, the slap of fish on a slab, a child's laugh that feels wrong in the edges]
[Music: Tension in low strings; the piano motif fragments into staccato notes]

You come to the market because that is where truth wears people's hands. Marta is there, paint-smudged and fierce, holding a stall post as if bracing the whole place against wind.

Marta Quin: "They're saying the town's going to lose the long-range money. People are real scared, Aria. Rent's due. The ferry man—he heard the council might cut travel subsidies if investors pull." (Her voice is quick with facts you can't unhear.)

Vendor 1: "We trusted you to make a plan. To keep things safe. Now the bank freezes and the council dithers and what do we do—"

Vendor 2 (interrupting): "We should have let them build that wall. At least then we'd know where we stand."

The conversation pitches and flares. You feel it like a current under your feet: gratitude for the leak from some corners, fury in others. A woman you remember selling pickled kelp—hands always steady—sits with her head bowed. A teenager calls you brave; a fishmonger calls you reckless. The two words hang next to each other like laundry on the same line.

A journalist edges closer, voice soft but hungry.

Journalist: "Aria Solen—do you have a response to the claim that the leak was meant to sabotage investment? Can you confirm your role?"

You: "I—" (your throat tightens) "I don't have a statement right now."

Your refusal is a small containment, but it leaks anyway. Someone in the crowd starts a chant—less organized than it sounds, more a raw exhalation of worry. You see Eli at the edge of the stalls, hand on the rope netting of his cart, watching. His face is unreadable—set like old driftwood, beautiful and complex.

Eli Navarro: "They're scared, Aria. We need to be honest with them about what comes next. Not speeches—what we will do tomorrow."

You and Eli trade the kind of look that used to be easy between you; now it's layered with new things. For a beat you both stand with the same aim: to stitch action over rumor.

Marta Quin: "Whatever we say, say it with facts. Don't promise what you can't deliver." (She locks eyes with you; there is both admonition and fidelity there.)

You: "We will be honest. We will be practical. We'll set up the Helix as a hub tonight for volunteers and truth—" (the promise tastes like iron) "—and Jun will work the paperwork."

Vendor 3: "People will follow action more than words. If we see the Helix lit and boats leaving with sandbags, that'll calm more hearts than any council hearing."

Some nod. Others remain rigid, arms folded. The fracture is not about who is right; it is about what fear does to trust. A hand reaches to touch your sleeve—a small, grateful gesture from a woman who used to barter boots for rope. You feel its warmth and the sudden flinch of responsibility.

[Scene: Helix | Late Afternoon into Evening]
[Visual: The Helix's grow-lamps burn golden through curved glass; the barge creaks under the weight of more boots than usual. Seed trays sit alongside stacks of donated sandbags. The water around the Helix is oily with evening light.]
[Sound: The murmur of people assembling, the thunk of boxes, distant traffic over the water]
[Music: Low cello, a slow descending line that matches the setting sun]

You arrive to find the floating greenhouse full—not just of plants but of people who needed somewhere to go. Volunteers fold tarps, hand out gloves, count out rope. The air is warm with humid soil and the breath of many bodies. You can feel accomplishment here and also the brittleness of it: a patch, not a fix.

Eli meets you at the ramp, hands stained, face a map of tired resolve.

Eli Navarro: "We can do a lot with hands and will. We can shore a berm for a few stretches, teach people to install temporary drainage, get kids to the higher decks. But the barge's insurance—"

His voice trails on the word like a cliff. The legal threats have teeth; your mind supplies every possible outcome: fines, injunctions, forced dispersal of volunteers.

You: "We can't just hide behind insurance. People are scared and action calms that. But I know—" (you stop, because saying 'I know' would be too neat) "—I know legal can be a real wall."

Eli Navarro: "Then let's be smart about it. Non-liability teams, clear signage, no one goes into the water if it could be dangerous. We'll have medics, a roster. Show them what we can achieve when we act together."

You study his face. His optimism is not naive; it is tactical faith. He wants movement, and movement can be persuasive. But there is a cost you are not sure you can pay—the cost of your professional standing if things go wrong, of Jun's grant evaporating further, of the council using any misstep as justification to clamp down.

A notification buzzes in your pocket. A lawyer's template letter—an offer to send it to "protect your organization"—stares back at you. You imagine the headline variations: "LEAKER COSTS TOWN JOBS" or "LOCAL HEROES FIGHT BACK." The future tilts in several directions at once, none of them gentle.

Eli Navarro: "You look like you want to say something else."

You: "I keep seeing my brother's face in this—the way he trusted a forecast and we weren't ready. I can't—" (you cough, and the room leans in with that particular tenderness reserved for admitting a fear) "—I can't lose more people because I wanted the truth."

Eli puts a hand on your shoulder. The touch is steady, but there is uncertainty in the pressure—he is not fixing, only anchoring.

Eli Navarro: "You shouldn't have to carry all of it alone. Not now."

Jun approaches, tablet in hand, voice low.

Jun Park: "Legal's lined up a preliminary inquiry. They want names of all who had access to the leak file. Lina called—she's trying to keep the council calm but she's getting heat from both sides. And—" (he swallows) "—Caden's office released a statement questioning the timing of the leak. Investors are asking for assurances. Some are threatening to withdraw if there's continued instability."

The words land like stones. You feel the boat under you shudder.

You: "How long?"

Jun Park: "Unclear. Weeks to get the audits. But decisions get made in days. Commitments are fragile."

You think of the town's edges—the small farms atop stilts, the weathered piers, the vendors who will either rise with organized action or fold under panic. You think of your own career: publications, collaborations, the silver ring heavy on your finger. You also think of truth—its warmth and its cold consequences.

A rumor begins circulating through the Helix: someone in Caden's camp has hinted at lawsuits, at official inquiries. The air grows colder anyway. You find yourself at a juncture that feels a lot like the reed beds in a storm: each stem will bend one way or another, and the whole depends on how many hold.

You close your eyes for a moment and feel the swell of the sea against the hull, the life you have tried to safeguard, and the fact that open honesty now calls for a reckoning you cannot predict.

Your options gather in the shape of decisions. None of them offers easy shelter—only paths that will strain different parts of you and the community.

You inhale, the smell of wet wood and soil filling your lungs. You steady your hands on the railing and look out at the darkening water, where the Helix's lamps stitch a small bright line against the dusk.
