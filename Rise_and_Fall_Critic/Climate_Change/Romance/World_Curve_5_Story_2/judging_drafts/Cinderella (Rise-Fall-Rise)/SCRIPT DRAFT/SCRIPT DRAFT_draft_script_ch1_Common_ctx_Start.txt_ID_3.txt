[Scene: Tidehaven Research Lab | Morning]

You are up before the tide charts wake the rest of the lab. Your fingers move on habit—tapping film trays into labeled racks, stacking seed packets by germination window, pressing a strip of marsh grass into the leather notebook as if anchoring the morning to paper. The lab smells of wet wood and peat, with a thin tang of saline that clings to the back of your throat. Light beads along a row of glass trays, turning newborn shoots into a small, stubborn constellation.

Jun Park is already hunched over the modeling station, the tablet haloed by a shaded lamp. He looks up with a half-smile that is all data and no drama, the way he has of trying to make worry look like a curve on a graph.

Jun Park: "Morning. I pushed a new run overnight—nested tide pulses with the seasonal storm surge profile. Buffer zones look better when we factor in the living berms you prefer."

You: "Good. Walk me through the numbers."

Jun taps, and a series of layered lines unspools on the screen—grey tides, green buffers, a thin blue envelope representing the corridor's projected reach.

Jun Park: "If we implement staged check dams across the southern reaches and plant the sedge clusters in the next six weeks, the model shows a thirty-percent reduction in inundation depth during a sustained storm event. That's conservative—doesn't include community-run maintenance, which is the wild card."

You study the curves with the same compulsive care you give the seedlings. Thirty percent. The number tastes like possibility. Your throat tightens—older things live in that tightening: the day you lost your sibling to a surge, the smell of overturned boats, the way everyone said it was an unavoidable storm.

You (internal): You do the math until the pain is a subtraction problem. Tonight's meeting is where numbers must become a story, and story must become enough to make people stand in front of permits and portfolios.

Jun Park: "You're going to do the presentation exactly like you rehearsed, right? Clear objectives, three phases, costs broken down. Don't—" he pauses, adjusting the glasses perched on the bridge of his nose, "—don't try to win everyone with passion alone. You have the data."

You: "I know. Clinical and then connective." You find yourself smiling despite the knot. "And Jun—thank you. For the recalibration. For running sims."

Jun Park: "Hey, I'm your awkward lab spouse." He slides a mug of tea across the counter. The steam smells of lemon and something medicinal. "Also, Marta called. She's bringing printed flyers to the boardwalk. Eli said he'd meet her there with seedlings from the Helix."

Your fingers close around the mug. The warmth is steadier than you feel. Outside, the lab's glass shutters kiss rain off the world like tentative applause.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Late Morning]
[Visual: The boardwalk glints with recent rain; varnished planks dark and warm; flyers flutter from a knot in the railing]
[Sound: The slap of water against pilings, the laugh of children in a distance, conversation layered like tide lines]
[Music: Brighter strings, heartbeat steadying]

You push open the hatch from the lab and the boardwalk breathes at you—salt-slick wood under your boots, the sun attempting to slice through low cloud, the smell of smoked fish from a vendor's stall at the pier's end. Marta is already there, sleeves rolled, her hair braided back with strips of cloth and a smear of paint on her cheek that insists she hasn't given up on color.

Marta Quin: "There you are. Jun told me your eyes were buried in graphs." She hands you a stack of flyers—rough-hewn paper, stamped with a hand-drawn map of Brineheart and a simple header: 'BRINEHEART: A COMMUNITY RESILIENCE PLAN'. Her fingers are quick and sure.

You: "They need to be distributed tonight. Every stall at the market. The fishers, the greenhouse co-ops, the folks on the east finger who won't be back after the rain."

Marta Quin: "We'll get them out. What's your ask, Aria? Not just 'support'—what do you want people to sign up for?"

You take a breath. You are conscious of the ring at your finger (mentor's gift, thin silver that catches the light), of the seed packet bulging in your coat pocket. Your voice is steady when you answer because steadiness is something the corridor needs.

You: "Volunteer hours for planting and maintaining check dams. A fund for emergency materials—hardware, cordage, sandbags maintained in community caches. And a council petition to prioritize living infrastructure in the redevelopment plan."

Marta nods like a person who can see terrain. "Speak plainly in the meeting. People will respond to the practical thing they can do."

Eli arrives like a warm gust, carrying a bundle of seedlings wrapped in waxed cloth. His hair is sun-bleached on the edges and his grin is immediate.

Eli Navarro: "Hey, science captain. I brought sedge starts and a promise—my crew on the Helix will run a training next week on buoyant planting. We can demo the method for anyone who doubts it."

You: "That would be incredible. If we can show a small pilot, the abstract becomes a ship we can point to."

Eli Navarro: (grinning) "Also, Marta made me bring cookies. For strategic persuasion. Carb diplomacy."

Marta shoots him a look that is half amusement, half maternal command. "If you disappoint people, I'll make you mend the town's nets for a month."

Eli laughs, then his face softens. He meets your eyes and there's a tether there—something warm and unspoken.

Eli Navarro: "I know you have a lot riding on tonight. You don't have to carry it all."

You: "I never carry everything." You say it lightly; inside, the weight shares a longer list—permits, Jun's models, Marta's logistical magic, Lina's observation, and the relentless calendar of tides.

Lina Voss steps from the shadow of a nearby stall in practical rain gear, a folded umbrella and a polite but tired smile. Her presence is neither triumph nor threat; it is the precise shade of bureaucratic possibility.

Lina Voss: "Aria. Jun. I'm glad to see the community involved. I promised the council I'd observe tonight and report back. Bring me something I can translate into policy language."

You: "We'll bring you a phased plan. Clear metrics, cost estimates, maintenance protocols."

Lina Voss: "Good. I don't promise votes, but I promise to bring your plan to the table fairly."

Her promise feels like a hinge. You can see the entire scaffolding you have to build: the technical, the social, the persuasive. It hums in your palms like an instrument you must tune.

Eli leans against the railing, looking out over the wetland. His voice quietens.

Eli Navarro: "People here want livelihoods. They want the sea to give and not take. We can show them how that happens—how the land can be a partner again, not just an obstacle."

Marta Quin: "And if Caden or other developers push hard, we'll have to be louder. But I believe loud is less useful than joined hands. That is how the tides turn."

You feel a small swell of something like hope—tenuous but growing. Your shoulders loosen by millimeters.

You: "We plant the pilot. We present tonight as a community. We ask Lina to bring it forward with specific language. We invite the market vendors to sign on."

Jun Park: "And the numbers back it. The model shows the pilot will validate assumptions for larger grant proposals. It will make us presentable to funders who want proof."

Marta Quin: "Then let's make something they can't ignore—not with shouting, but with evidence and muscle. And cookies."

Everyone laughs; the sound is a lift. The morning feels like it has found a rhythm.

[Scene: Brineheart Wetland Corridor | Afternoon]
[Visual: Sun lowering toward molten copper; reed beds arch like slow waves; the raised boardwalk weaves through a quilt of green and brown]
[Sound: The hush of the marsh, insect wings whispering, the distant thunk of a maintenance crew resetting a check dam]
[Music: Warm strings with low, hopeful brass]

You step onto the raised boardwalk and the wetland breathes beneath you. Mud clings to your boots in soft, obedient clumps. The salt-creased air tangles with the sweet, loamy smell of sedge. Reeds lean like an audience in wind, and in the distance a line of hand-built check dams gleams faintly—small stakes of hope stitched across channels.

Old hands have walked this boardwalk for years; Old Man Rafi's stories live in the notches of the planks. You run a thumb along a carved initial and feel the texture of a place that remembers.

Jun crouches beside a tray of sedge you hadn't yet catalogued, checking root depth. Marta speaks softly to a group of neighbors about planting technique. Lina walks the line, reading the site like a surveyor and like someone learning to love a place on the fly. Eli unloads a crate from a small skiff, his hands expert and sure.

Eli Navarro: "We can set up a small demonstration patch near that western inlet. It gets the morning sun; people walking the boardwalk will see the plants catch silt. It becomes living evidence."

You: "Yes. Visibility matters. If you can start that this weekend, we'll be ready to show it to the council the following week."

Eli Navarro: "We can do it. And your training schedule?"

You: "Two sessions—one for volunteers, one for the co-op crews. Jun will help with monitoring protocol."

Jun Park: "I'll get the sensors prepped. If the pilot produces even weak positive readings in sediment capture, that's a headline."

Lina pauses at the edge of the boardwalk, hands folded against the rain gear. Her voice is softer here, the city's formality stripped by wind and marsh.

Lina Voss: "This place has a presence. I can see why you fight for it. Bring me the report after the demonstration. If it's solid, I can push for a conditional clause in the redevelopment plan—something that integrates living infrastructure."

The words land like a careful gift. You breathe them in and let them make a small map in your chest.

You (internal): The corridor answers in increments—each plank replaced, each sedge planted. Tonight is one of those increments. If momentum is a brittle thing, then tonight is a careful hand learning to cup it.

Marta comes over and twines a seed packet into your palm. Her eyes crinkle with deliberate affection.

Marta Quin: "You look like you could use a steadying. Promise me you won't go into the meeting with your vest zipped to the neck of solitude. Let people see the person behind the proposals."

You: "I will. I have to."

Eli steps close, voice low enough that only you hear, carrying the buoyant certainty that has made him a magnet in small communities.

Eli Navarro: "I'll be there tonight. And after—tomorrow—we'll start the Helix volunteers on the first batch. If you want, I can walk with you to the market in an hour. Get some faces, get some signatures."

You feel the familiar reflex: project first, private things later. The reflex that has kept you building walls and not asking for company. But this time, the project's seams run through people. The corridor cannot be restored without them—and you cannot be whole without admitting you need hands to stitch with.

You close your eyes for a moment, smelling the peat and the salt and the faint sweetness of the seedlings. You open them and see the line of neighbors—Marta, Jun, Lina, Eli—each a small, steady presence.

You: "The market would be good. We need the vendors to see a clear, tangible ask. If they sign tonight, we bring the petition and the pilot to the council and soon the developers will have to answer to a living constituency."

Eli smiles, a quiet, wide thing. "Then let's make the market feel like the first checkpoint of a movement."

The sun slides further toward the horizon, gilding the wetland in copper. The corridor's breath becomes a promise. The momentum you've been shaping—papers, models, seedlings, faces—hums beneath your skin. You prepare your notes, tuck the leather-bound notebook against your hip where the pressed grasses rub the spine, and feel the weight of the ring on your finger like a compass.

You take one last look at the marsh. Here, in the blade of light and the hush of insect wings, a small and steady conviction settles: tonight you will speak. Tonight you will ask for hands and time and a little trust.

You pull the hood of your jacket up against a fine, salt-laden breeze and head toward the path back to the pier—the market sits like a living map where you can find everyone whose livelihoods thread through the town's future.
