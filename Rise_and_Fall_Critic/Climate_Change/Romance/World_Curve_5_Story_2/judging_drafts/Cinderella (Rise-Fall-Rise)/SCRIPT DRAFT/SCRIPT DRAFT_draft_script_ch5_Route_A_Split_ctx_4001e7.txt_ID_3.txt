[Visual: Low, slate clouds rolling in faster than the pattern would predict. The incomplete restoration cell you visited earlier is half-stitched into the reedbed—pale geotextile, stakes, and loose packing material visible where work paused. The boardwalk is slick; light is thin, green-gray.]
[Sound: Wind sharpening into a scream at the edges, the distant slap of waves against pilings, the nervous creak of tensioned ropes.]
[Music: A single minor piano motif, held and bending downward—hope under strain]

You go back because you always go back to the edges first. Momentum from the meeting is still warm in your chest, but the horizon tightened into a line you hadn't measured. You remember the Helix's glow an hour ago, the feeling of stitching with other hands; now the same hands are thinner in the light.

Salt rides the air like a second breath; peat and iron mix with the wet tang of old rope. The cell you helped mark—unfinished, anchored but vulnerable—waits with its seams exposed. When the wind finds those seams, it doesn't ask to be let through.

A sudden gust shoves water over the low mudflats. You see the first pack of bio-fiber tear free, a trembling white that the tide takes like a child's kite. The sound it makes is not loud—more a ripping silence—and your stomach drops into a place that remembers storms as loss, not just weather.

You move before you decide, boots sinking, fingers numb on frayed twine. You know the physics—the cell was provisional, expected to flex—but you also know what happens when the public sees one failure: certainty hardens into blame.

You crouch at the edge where the bulwark failed. Mud clings to your gloves. The reed stems, sheared, make a queasy, papery sound when you lift them. In the distance, a bright rectangle flickers on a vendor's phone as video uploads—the first clip of the tearing pack, captioned already. You taste iron in the back of your throat.

Eli appears at your shoulder, breath steaming in the cool, and for a second you both watch the tide rearrange the work you had called progress. He breathes out, a slow sound that might be consolation or the simple need to make breath steady.

Eli: "We can save part of it—if we anchor those outside straps now and get more ballast in behind the wick. But it's getting worse fast."
You: "If I send volunteers out, we risk more damage. If we wait for Jun's measurements, the tidal window closes." (You pull the soaked notebook from your jacket and the pages glisten.)
Eli: "So either we throw hands at it tonight and pray, or we don't. You know me—I'm going to offer hands. But I won't drag people into a pointless fight."
You: "I'm not sure what’s pointless anymore."

[Scene: Boardwalk / Near Helix | Dusk]
[Visual: The Helix is a warm island against the gray; inside, grow-lamps hum and condensation beads on glass. Outside, people cluster under awnings, faces lit by phone screens and the cold light of public feeds.]
[Sound: Murmurs swell into argument; a reporter's distant question repeats like a refrain. A seagull argues with the wind, and somewhere a generator coughs back to life.]
[Music: Low, drawn strings—pressure under the surface]

Jun meets you with his tablet like a shield. His face is taut; he runs one hand through damp hair. Marta stands nearby, hands stained with mud and old ledger pages, a look that mixes worry with something like indignation that will not be quieted.

Jun: "The scar's deeper than my models predicted without tertiary anchors. The cell's shear point was at the northern seam—material mismatch. I can patch, but it needs redesign."
You: "How bad is 'deeper'?" (You watch his mouth as he chooses words.)
Jun: "Not catastrophic, but politically catastrophic if the clip goes viral with the wording it has. We have two windows tonight to stabilize—either we re-stack, with proper anchors and weighted frames, or we evacuate the area and call a pause."

Marta interrupts, voice flintier than you expect: "Pause means the council sees a headline and Lina freezes permits. Pause means people think we panicked, and the money dries. We don't have time to wait for slow science." Her hands flex on the rail.

Eli: "Lina will freeze things if she thinks risk outweighs optics. She told us as much—this is what politics does when it smells blood." (He looks at you, amber eyes reflecting grow-lamp light.) "We need to plan for both."

Before you can answer, your phone vibrates. Lina's name floats on the screen. Your stomach drops in recognition; a political pause is not only a policy line—it's permission withdrawn.

You accept. The call is clipped, professional, but her voice carries an edge that won't be softened by protocol.

Lina: "Aria—I've just seen the footage. For safety and liability, I'm freezing all active permits for the corridor until an independent assessment returns. I don't want to accelerate panic." (Pause.) "You understand my position, right?"
You: "Lina, we can shore up—Jun and I have a plan. Freezing permits halts the hands on the ground that could help."
Lina: "I know you want action. I also have to weigh the council's legal exposure. Right now, I need to show I've done the prudent thing." (Her voice softens, almost unreadably complex.) "Share your preliminary assessment tonight. Be precise. Don't ask for exceptions on emotion."

The call ends with no warmth. Her action is procedural—and it hurts the momentum you had. It is also a political signal: the town will now watch for fault lines, and permits are no longer tools for repair but levers in a public spectacle.

[Scene: Old Pier Market / Temporary Triage Table | Night]
[Visual: Lanterns sway, fish-smoke tang mixing with the sea. Volunteers gather around folding tables where seed trays and torn fabric lie like trophies and wounds. A portable speaker leaks recorded news into the damp air.]
[Sound: Low arguments, a vendor's curse, the thrum of anxious voices; the reporter's voice loops like a trapped gull.]
[Music: A single cello note, plucked and left to ring]

You move among people who have already started placing blame where it can be owned. Vendors whisper that the co-op led people into unsafe work; others mutter that "those kids" shouldn't be whoever they imagine led it. Caden arrives on the fringe, impeccably dry under his coat, and the crowd parts like tide around a well-kept boat.

Caden: "I'm sorry about what happened—truly. But perhaps it's time for a more centralized plan. We can mobilize engineering teams, secure funding, and ensure things are done at scale." (He smiles, the kind that has been practiced to smooth objection.)
You: "Scale doesn't mean right for this place." (The words come sharper than you intend.)
Caden: "Scale means protecting jobs, connecting us to investment, and—" (he taps his cuffwatch) "—delivering results on council timelines. We can't let pilot projects be the entire defense." (He inclines his head toward the footage on a nearby tablet.) "You need help, Aria. Let us help deliver it properly."

Marta fumes in your ear: "He’s circling, Aria. He smelled a mistake and he's got the suit that's hungry for shorelines. Don't hand him the town on a platter." Her knuckles are white on the table. Jun's jaw tightens; his hand restarts the same small gesture over the tablet screen.

Eli's response to Caden is quieter, closer—a warmth that tries to be a wedge between you and the man in the suit. He doesn't let the moment go unchallenged, but he also doesn't fling himself like a shield.

Eli: "We need to make sure the community's knowledge stays central. Engineering without roots is just a wall." (He meets Caden's gaze.) "If you want to help, help by matching what's been grown here, not replacing it."
Caden: (smiles thinly) "Of course. It's all about complementarity."

The word is precise and hollow at once. You feel your resolve compress into a decision that will either be yours or be taken from you.

You try to fold the immediate practicalities and the public fallout into a single thread. They won't stay the same. Lina's freeze means permitting and legal cover are gone for the moment; the viral clip means opinion is consolidating into narratives; Caden's presence means someone is already ready to fill a vacuum with cash and a plan that could overwrite local control. Volunteers are tired, and your notebook feels like a map of compromises you haven't allowed yourself.

Night thickens; rain starts in earnest, a cold, relentless tapping that makes the market tarps wag like nervous animals. You retreat to the Helix later, because the greenhouse is the only place that still smells of possibility rather than accusation. Inside, the grow-lamps throw your shadow long and crooked across seed racks.

Eli finds you by the central bench, hands smelling of wet rope. He doesn't look like he's come to fix you; he looks like he's come to be present in whatever breaks and whatever might be mended.

Eli: "You held us together today." (He pauses, and the sentence is not an absolution so much as an observation that aches.) "I don't know if any of this is on you alone. It feels like everything is pointing at—" (he swallows) "—someone to carry blame, and you're the closest thing to a face people expect to see."
You: "I feel seen, and then I feel exposed. The footage did that—turns effort into a headline." (You thumb the leather of your notebook until the marsh grass flattens.)
Eli: "We can do the rebuild. We can try the overnight restack if that's what's needed. Or we can step back, reorganize with Jun, make the audit public and show what we learned. Or—" (his voice drops) "—we could try to work Lina's channels for a quieter path. I don't want to tell you what to do." (He reaches and lays his hand near yours on the bench—careful, not demanding.)
You: (You let the contact be a thing that exists. You appreciate the steadiness even as apprehension coils.) "I don't want to make a choice in a room with cameras or under pressure. But choices are happening whether I make them or not."

Silence pools between you, punctuated by the greenhouse hum and the rain that keeps promising more. You listen to the sounds of the community outside—people arguing, repairing, muttering prayers, shaking their heads—and you feel the shape of three paths pressing against you.

The first is exposure: admit the public mistakes, lead an immediate transparent audit, halt works to rephase. It is honest and slow and burns in the short term.

The second is closed-door navigation: privately negotiate with Lina for emergency funds and controlled messaging to buy time. It is pragmatic and secretive and risks complicity.

The third is immediate physical response: push the community to an urgent, risky re-stacking overnight to salvage the cells. It is dangerous and direct and could either save what you started or deepen the wound.

Each path maps onto a piece of you—your stubborn honesty, your inclination to protect the project pragmatically, and the instinct to fix with hands rather than words. The stormfall has narrowed the horizon into a choice that will mark not just the project but the contours of trust in the town, and in the tender place where you and Eli stand.
