## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
Marenport is a near-future coastal archipelago in 2043 where sea-level rise has reconfigured everyday life. Streets have become canals, rooftops have become terraces and farms, and the city stitches improvisational floating platforms to surviving foundations. Political authority is fractured: municipal coalitions, corporate climate-tech firms, grassroots collectives, and elders' councils (keepers of place-memory) all vie to steer adaptation. Class tensions are visible in access to retrofitted buildings and tide-resistant infrastructure: wealthier districts negotiate private TideGrid nodes while poorer neighborhoods rely on communal carpentry and sewn-together barriers. Culture is syncretic and practical — rituals of "making do" sit alongside civic policy meetings and lab demonstrations. Religion is diffuse and place-based: reverence for coastlines and elders shapes local decision-making. The main conflict is between centralized, rapid techno-fixes (represented by Rin’s consortium and the TideGrid) and community-led, small-scale resilience (represented by Maia and the Marenport Resilience Collective). Geography is intimate and tactile: moss, rust, salt-crusted hems, and golden-hour light break through the persistent low cloud. The atmosphere is melancholic yet pragmatic; people love fiercely because losses are personal and policy choices are intimate. The environment is not backdrop but an active agent shaping relationships, trust, and sacrifice.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I, Maia Soler, navigate love, science, and moral urgency in a city squeezed by water. The narrative follows an Icarus arc: a rapid rise to apparent triumph followed by catastrophic collapse. The common route introduces my roots in the submerged neighborhoods, Elias’s TideGrid project, and Rin’s persuasive urgency. At a pivotal split I must choose which coalition I join—work with Elias, resist via grassroots expansion, or broker a new hybrid—each choice launches a different ascent. In every path, an early peak (technical success, public acclaim, or diplomatic victory) raises hope and binds Elias and me through collaboration or conflict. But success produces overreach: engineering misfires, overlooked local dynamics, or political co-optation. The downward spiral accelerates into social fracture, betrayal, or systemic failure that shatters public trust and personal intimacy. Across all branches the crash is catastrophic, altering city governance and our relationship in irrevocable ways. The visual novel explores how love negotiates responsibility when every solution carries moral cost.

Endings (Summary)
- Route A endings (Chapters 6 and 13) show technical triumph followed by catastrophic infrastructural and moral collapse; personal relationships and local trust are destroyed.
- Route B endings (Chapters 9 and 14) depict community agency sustained temporarily but overwhelmed by centralized mandates; culture and autonomy are eroded even as lives are materially saved.
- Route C endings (Chapters 12 and 15) render the hybrid as a kind of success that nonetheless institutionalizes control and archives the city’s memory, producing a survival that feels empty to those who cherished democratic care.

Across all branches the Icarus arc is strict: a rapid ascent into apparent success, then a precipitous fall that forces Maia (first-person narrator) to reckon with the costs of decisions made in urgency. Visual language — damp hems, frayed ribbon, slate-blue jackets, platinum-black coat, moss-streaked glass, rusted docks — is used continuously to keep the world tactile and intimate.
