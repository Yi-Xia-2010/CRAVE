[Scene: Floating Market of Canal Twelve | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Rope bridges sway between bobbing stalls; neon fabric awnings flicker as the sun leans toward the horizon. Salt glaze beads on wooden posts. Potted herbs and salvaged nets hang like prayer flags.]
[Music: Bright, rhythmic plucking of a hand-harvested lute; light percussion underscoring steady work]
[Sound: Low murmur of bargaining, gulls, water lapping against hulls; an occasional metallic clink as tools meet scrap]

You turned down the public offer this morning. The council's paper is folded into the inside pocket of your jacket where it will warm against your side and press its weight against your ribcage — a quiet, decisive pressure. The market smells of brined fish and citrus peel, motor oil and fresh soil. Your sleeves are damp where you leaned to tie a knot on a rope line; the frayed blue ribbon at the nape of your neck has been retied tighter, less ornamental and more like a promise.

Vendors nod when they see you. Their faces are lit with the practical grin of people who have to make things work every day. Someone lifts a tin of desalination parts to show you; another pushes a sketch of a pump housing toward Ibe with a finger still stained by dye.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' N'Diaye: "Alright, show me again—what's our radius with two pumps and a siphon wheel? If we place them like this," (he taps the ragged map on the crate) "we can share flow between three platforms without starving the terraces."

You lean in until the map smells like varnish and seaweed. Your fingers move over drawn pilings and dashed lines; in your head the grids shift like tide charts. There is a relief to this kind of problem: measurable, solvable, shared.

You can feel the old habit — the scientist's comfort — surfacing in small increments: calculate, iterate, test. But every calculation also carries faces. Ibe's father. Toma's crooked tale of the dock children. The neighbor who lost two summers of beans. The math sits beside memory and both matter.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' N'Diaye: "If we scavenge the upriver pilings and splice them with floor plates from that warehouse collapse, we can make a hex array that'll hold panels for both rain capture and solar. Vendors will trade labor for slots on the grid."

Rosita (Vendor): "We traded coffee for a nail gun last season. I'll trade coffee again, if it keeps my stall afloat."

You laugh — a short, buoyant thing — and you hand Rosita a bolt. You feel the market's attention like a tide pulling forward, small waves stacking into movement.

Old Man Toma arrives, his cloak soggy at the hem, the brittle wooden compass tucked into his palm like a relic. He sets it on the crate between you as if the object itself will steady the plans.

Old Man Toma: "Keep the compass in your pocket when the fog comes. Don't rely on the new machines when the old stars forget us. But teach the children where the stars mixed into the harbor used to be. Make the ledger and the story share a shelf."

Maia Soler [softly]: "We will. We'll make sure every module has a manual and a storybook."

Old Man Toma: "Good. Machines with stories last longer."

The market hums approval. People begin to sketch shifts on whatever scrap is handy — cardboard, a fish-crate lid, the back of a ledger. Work begins to look like ritual and policy at once.

[Scene: Workbenches (Canal Twelve Dock) | Early Evening]
[Visual: A row of patched benches under a tarpaulin; overhead, bioluminescent seeds strung for light. Small machines and open tins of components crowd the surfaces. Elias sits at a well-used bench, sleeves rolled; a soldering iron rests in a clay holder nearby.]
[Music: Low, hopeful synth pad mixed with the steady clack of hammers]
[Sound: Solder hiss, the scrape of a rasp on wood, the distant rumble of a municipal barge — insistent but receding]

You find a rhythm at the benches: hands on wood, eyes on joint, a conversation held between pulses of work. Serena crouches beside you, wristband flickering diagnostic readouts. She tests the seal on a prototype float and makes a face that is more concentration than scorn.

Serena Qiu: "Pressure differential across the membrane is within safe limits; if we seal the lower valves with the polymer weave we might get another three seasons before replacement. But the weave will cost—"

Maia Soler: "We barter. We take what we can salvage and we teach the technique. Circulate the weave, don't hoard the recipe."

Serena Qiu [half-smile]: "You're dangerously optimistic about human sharing."

Maia Soler: "Danger is part of the job."

Across the bench, Elias has oil on his palms and smudged across the chest of his slate-blue undershirt. He is quieter than usual, eyes narrowed in a way you recognize: solving, framing variables, attempting to convert moral questions into technical constraints.

Elias Kade [softly]: "Maia. I've been thinking about a modular control node — scaled so communities can opt-in. Not full TideGrid, just... a backbone people can use. I know you said no to central deployment, but—"

Maia Soler [interrupting gently]: "You know why I said no. Centralization takes choices out of hands that have always made them."

Elias Kade: "I know. That's why I want to design the nodes so the community decides the locks, Maia. No remote override. Open-source firmware. Local councils manage thresholds." (He gestures, a small, earnest motion.) "I can do this my way if you let me do it with you."

There is a pause in which the soldering iron hisses like a held breath. Your chest tightens with that old guilt — that ache to fix more people more quickly — and also with the clearer warmth of cooperation.

Maia Soler [measured]: "If you build them, they must be legible. No hidden bits. Full transparency. And we pilot them here — in real conditions with real neighbors — not in a high tower. You help with a retrofit, not a takeover."

Elias Kade: "That's fair. I'll help retrofit. I don't want to be the sort of engineer who ignores who he's helping."

Elias leans in, wiping grease on a rag and reaching for a pair of brackets. His fingers brush yours in the motion; for a moment the crowd noise fades and all you hear is the scrape of your palms against the wood and the pulse at the base of your throat.

You keep yourself from reaching for him with words you don't need to say. Trust now will be measured in bolts and open logs. It will be messy and imperfect and human. That feels like the right kind of salvation.

Serena Qiu: "If you're both actually doing this together, I'll run continuous diagnostics. We document every failure as loudly as we document success. No cover-ups."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' N'Diaye: "Good. Then when a pump stalls at midnight, we know what to fix."

Elias Kade: "And when it works, we celebrate."

Maia Soler [smiling despite the salt on your lip]: "We always celebrate."

[Scene: Skyfarm Terraces | Dusk]
[Visual: Tiered terraces glow with lamplight and the scuff of many feet. New planters — mismatched, hand-painted — dot the edges. Children with small trowels trail behind Toma as he tells a clipped, wonder-filled story of the docks.]
[Music: Rising choral strings mixed with clapping and the thump of spaded soil]
[Sound: Water trickling through newly connected drip lines; laughter; the primal rhythm of people working in unison]

Neighbors who had given up their gardens come back with seeds wrapped in newspaper. Someone brings a kettle and pours tea into chipped cups for a tired pair of hands. The city, for this night, looks less like a map of losses and more like a patchwork quilt being stitched together.

Old Man Toma, compass warm in his pocket, puts his hand on a child's head and says, "Plant them where they'll get fear and care; both make a strong root."

You kneel to press a seed into earth beside a woman who used to sell woven hats at the market. Her hands, worked by rope and sun, are gentle around the trowel.

Every planting is a tiny vote against disappearance. Each threaded rope, each spliced plate, a declaration: we will not be merely managed. We will make our shores again.

Serena, at your side, watches the diagnostics flicker across her wristband. She sets it down and, without a word, digs her hands into soil and buries a seed. Her hair is pulled back, she looks absurdly content amidst circuitry and dirt.

Serena Qiu: "This is... a good dataset." (she mutters) and then smiles like someone surprised by pleasure.

Ibrahim 'Ibe' N'Diaye (to the work crew): "Alright. Tomorrow we salvage pilings near the old tannery. Bring straps. Bring supper. Bring your mothers if they'll come."

Rosita (Vendor): "I'll bring coffee and songs."

Someone starts a rhythm with a plank and a mallet; it becomes a beat and then a chorus. You feel your shoulders loosen in a way they haven't in months. The energy is fierce and connective; it lifts you like a tide that understands careful navigation.

Elias stands at the rim of the terrace, silhouetted against a band of gold cloud. He has grease on his knuckles; a smear of it runs on the hem of his undershirt. He hands you a small bracket he has finished.

Elias Kade: "I didn't think I'd like drilling into old wood. But this—this feels real."

Maia Soler [softly]: "Because it's ours."

He looks at you, the kind of look that asks for patience rather than promises. You return it with the sturdier thing: a plan and a neighbor's vow.

Tonight, the city is warming under the hands of its people. The sky is the color of wet copper and hope. The work will be long and the storms will come, but for the first time in a long time you feel the arc of things tilting toward agency.

[Visual: A distant shadow of a research barge passes the mouth of the channel; its lights are small and non-threatening from this distance.]
[Sound: A low mechanical hum reaches the terraces and then folds into the night like a reminder rather than a threat.]

You touch the copper locket at your throat; it is warm and heavy and full of coastline you are determined not to lose.

[Music: A quiet, ascending motif; strings and light percussion building to a gentle swell]

You stand on the terrace and watch the city stitch itself — pumps humming, seedlings planted, hands joining across old separations. The plan is not perfect. It will never be perfect. But it is communal, legible, and tenacious. You hear the distant hum of larger systems — the TideGrid's machinery, the city's big answers — but your path tonight is to anchor this neighbor-to-neighbor work; to make it visible, replicable, and impossible to ignore. The horizon holds choices you have not yet taken, but for this night, you choose labor and study and the luminous slow growth of craft. You do not know what the barge will bring tomorrow, or whether Rin will pivot, or if a storm will test your new stitches. You only know the current — a carrying, warm, and determined — that moves through the market, across the benches, and up to the terraces where children sleep with dirt under their nails and hope in their pockets.
