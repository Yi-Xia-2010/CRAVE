You follow the hum down the raised walkway, the vibration slightly underfoot, like a heartbeat answering something in the water. Your map is folded into the inner pocket of your jacket, warm where your palm rests over it. The locket at your throat—copper, scuffed—tugs at the ribbon against your skin, a small, private weight.

Salt beads the lab's exterior glass. Streaks run down like tide-lines left by a sky that changes its mind. Through those streaked panes the Saltglass Research Lab looks less like a fortress of algorithms and more like a greenhouse that has learned to keep secrets. Moss panels run in neat strips along a support beam; potted micro-ecosystems cluster in shadowed corners, tiny forests under glass. The smell is a mix of ozone, wet metal, and something green and growing — comforting in its own impossible way.

You knock once on the sliding hatch and step inside.

[Visual: Interior of Saltglass Research Lab — cool fluorescent light mixed with golden sidelight. Screens float with simulations; one corner holds a row of sprouting trays]
[Sound: fingers tapping on a tablet, a soft mechanical exhale from an environmental regulator]
[Music: Strings swell lightly, buoyant]

Elias is already there, hunched over a compact tablet strapped to his forearm. He looks up when you enter, and the small smile he gives you is the sort that rearranges your chest—careful, impatient, hopeful. A lock of ash-blond hair falls into his forehead; when he brushes it back he does it with the same hand that keeps adjusting a simulation like a jeweler turning a stone.

Elias: "You're here. Good—walked the dock, did you? I thought I heard the collective's boots." (He taps the tablet; the holographic tide-grid blooms between you in pale cyan and bronze arcs.) "I wanted to show you this before the meeting. It… it changed a little last night."

You move closer because you always want to see the parts of his mind that speak in light and motion. The simulation is graceful—modular turbines like petals unfurling from a central spine, sediment redistribution paths mapped with the surety of a gardener planning channels. The grid rearranges itself at a touch.

You: "It looks… precise." You feel the words save you from saying something softer. "Like you're describing a beautiful machine."

Elias's smile widens, surprised into something almost childlike.

Elias: "That's exactly it. Beautiful and functional. If we lock these nodes in phases—modular deployment—the system adapts. Smaller neighborhoods can come online when they're ready. It isn't an all-or-nothing." (He pauses, eyes searching your face.) "Maia, I didn't design it to steamroller communities. I built it to carry them."

Serena, standing a step away with her arms folded and a diagnostic band faintly pulsing on her wrist, tilts her chin. Her face is a study in deliberate doubt, but there's a softness in the set of her mouth that suggests she wants to be convinced rather than simply proven wrong.

Serena: "The models are elegant, Elias, but elegance and stability aren't the same. Look at this feedback loop—if sediment moves faster than expected, some nodes can amplify erosion downstream. A local win could become a downstream loss."

Elias: (quick, conciliatory) "We anticipated variance. That's why the redistribution algorithm has a dampening constant—"

Serena: (interrupting) "A number doesn't feel like a guarantee to an elder who worries about the foundations under their home."

You feel your municipal ID badge grow heavy under your shirt, a small, bureaucratic weight that somehow feels like the world. Old Man Toma's hand, callused and steady, the smell of Ibe's sawdust on a good day—the mental images land with more force than the simulations.

Elias watches you do both in parts: how you direct questions, where your eyes hesitate. He likes the way you try to translate the technical into the tangible. He has always admired that in you—the way you root theory in people's lives.

Elias: "We can make the controls transparent. Open-source deployment. Community reps with veto power at each phase. Serena, help me outline how we test those safeguards." (He reaches a hand toward a secondary console; his fingers are quick, confident.) "You see? It can be co-designed."

Serena: (more measured) "Those are good first steps. But transparency without power-sharing is optics. Harder to implement—and harder to sell to someone who has only seen abstractions fail them."

Ibrahim 'Ibe' arrives then, carrying a coil of weathered rope and a box of reclaimed brackets. He smells like cedar shavings and salt and brings a practicality that makes architectural blueprints feel like maps you can cross.

Ibe: (grinning) "Thought I'd find you two talking to the ghosts of old docks. Maia, you look like you could use a new pile driver. Elias, keep your turbines pretty, but will they let us bolt grafts onto the terraces that actually hold a table when my cousin brings dinner?"

Elias: (laughs) "They're designed to interface with local anchoring systems. Ibe, we'd want your input on attachment points."

Ibe: (mock offended) "Finally! They asked the carpenter. Took them long enough."

You laugh because it loosens something. Laughter in the lab is small and fierce, like sunlight through a mesh. You watch Elias and Ibe exchange technical jokes, and for a moment logistics and romance and craft blend into the same conversation—you, part of all of that.

You: "If this is modular, and if communities get a seat at the table, then the TideGrid isn't the opposite of what we do—it could be a tool for us." The words surprise you with their hope. They feel like a bridge you might be willing to test.

Elias's eyes catch the lab light, and the gold fleck in them catches yours in a way that feels like permission.

Elias: "Exactly. I don't want to override traditions. I want to make sure the things you tend don't wash away while we figure out finer policy."

Serena: (soft but still guarded) "And we need independent audits, real-time community monitoring, and—" (she exhales) "—a contingency for when the models lie to us."

The conversation spirals into specifics: a phased pilot at Canal Twelve, an advisory council with mixed representation, an emergency sediment management protocol developed with local carpenters. Ideas, once pinned, don't sag—each one looks like something that could be built.

Elias: "Maia, I know the cost you're always carrying. I don't want to add to it. I want to share it—practically. With tools and decisions in hand. Can we try to design this together? Not as 'my machine' and 'your neighborhood,' but as ours."

You feel the pulse at your wrist—an anxious metronome that matches the hum from outside. Your mind catalogs: elders' photos pinned to the wall at the Skyfarm Terraces, the way Old Man Toma folds his hands when he's uncertain, Ibe's iambic certainty about a bolted joint. You catalog because your instinct is protective. You catalog because you know the difference between promise and implementation.

Elias reaches for your hand.

You have to decide whether to bend to his optimism, risking the slow, messy sovereignty of neighborhoods, or hold fast to the patient, local work that has always been yours.

You can feel all the reasons to choose both, and they sit in your chest like ballast and sails at once. The conversation around you continues—Serena sketching contingencies, Ibe humming about fasteners—but all of it refracts through the small pressure of Elias's fingers near yours.
