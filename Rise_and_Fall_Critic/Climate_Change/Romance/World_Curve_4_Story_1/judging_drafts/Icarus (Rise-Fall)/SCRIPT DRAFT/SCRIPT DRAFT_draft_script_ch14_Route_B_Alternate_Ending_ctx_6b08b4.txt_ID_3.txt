[Scene: Skyfarm Terraces — Dusk | Months Later — A Quiet Rooftop]

Narration
You meet Elias on a terrace that used to be all hands and laughter. Tonight it is pristine—rows of seedlings fed by automated drip, a soft hum of calibrated pumps. He greets you with a softness that steadies and a data set that does not.

Elias Kade: (gently) "We reduced casualty risk by thirty-two percent in the neighborhoods connected to the Grid, Maia. The models—"

Maia Soler: (interrupting) "Not everything is a model, Elias. The market, Toma's stories—these are how people remember how to live. You can measure circulation of water; you cannot measure how a song keeps a child warm."

Elias Kade: (a flicker of frustration, then apology) "I— I know. I didn't mean to reduce it to numbers. I mean—that we saved lives. That matters."

Maia Soler: "It does. It matters. But it is not everything."

Narration
He watches the terrace plants—each one tended by an invisible hand.He is tired in a different way now: the stamina of someone who bears large responsibilities. His face is softer, but the distance between you is a thin, stubborn thing. He offers you the tablet, open to a cascade of graphs.

Maia Soler: (bitter laugh) "Add legal teeth to the same system that sidelines liaisons when 'efficiency thresholds' dip? Elias, you saw how quickly they replaced liaison recommendations with 'emergency administrators' when a metric slipped. Teeth don't help if the jaw chooses to bite itself."

Narration
He looks stung. Then he looks at you like someone trying to remember which of the equations describes love.

Elias Kade: "I thought we could make something that would protect both—"

Maia Soler: (soft, resigned) "So did I."

Narration
There is a gap where a compromise once lived. You worry that the hinge that joined your hands was engineered, too: well-designed, functional, but not always warm to the touch.

[Scene: Coastal Ruins — Old Dock District | Night — A Week Later]
[Visual: Skeleton piers, wind-thrashed banners, and the distant glow of Harbor lights. You stand alone on a low quay, palms pressed to a rusted beam where tide used to lap against your childhood steps.]
[Music: Slow, minor-key piano; the sound of wind in an empty net]

Narration
In the new dawns and clean dashboards, loss keeps an odd company with survival. You find yourself policing the very system you helped birth—fielding complaints, mediating between liaisons and elders, patching the discrepancies between legal language and lived need. Sometimes you succeed. Sometimes you hold off a bureaucrat long enough for a market to open. Sometimes you lose: a storytelling night canceled for "compliance reasons," an artisan's stall shuttered by permit backlog.

Old Man Toma: (in a memory, soft) "Don't let them convince you that efficiency is a virtue that can't be human."

Narration
You trace the locket at your throat, the coastline photograph inside thin as skin. The harbor spreads bright and efficient across the channel—lights that could save lives; mechanisms that can stabilize waterlines; an architecture of protection that hums like a careful heart. But the city's moral economy—the informal kindnesses, the unlit vigils, the barter that taught children how to trust—has shifted under the tidal pressure.

Maia Soler: (to yourself) "Was there ever a way to protect both lives and autonomy? Or did urgency force a false choice?"

Narration
The question repeats itself like a gull's call. It has no tidy answer. The solemn thing you do now is hold the question and let it inform the work you choose next. You will continue to press for stronger liaison powers, for legal tools that cannot be easily shelved. You'll anchor clandestine story nights in private courtyards when public insurance says no. You'll teach younger organizers how to read a permit—and how to read a crowd.

Elias reaches for you one evening, data in his voice and apology in his hands. But the intimacy between you has acquired a new grammar: one of careful reserves, negotiating boundaries as if they were levees.

Elias Kade: (softly, finally) "I don't know how to do this and hold everything. But I don't want to lose you."

Maia Soler: (steady) "Neither do I. Losing you would make the city colder in the ways the Grid cannot measure."

Narration
You fold that particular ache into the ledger of responsibilities. For all the things the TideGrid saves, it has not learned to save everything that once made the city a home. You will keep fighting—through policy, through small subversions, through the stubborn practice of bringing people together in kitchen light rather than committee rooms.

You do not romanticize what was lost. You do not fully forgive what you helped create.
