[Scene: Saltglass Research Lab — Pilot Bay | Morning]
[Visual: Glass walls streaked with salt; a compact TideGrid mock-up sits on a central table, articulated turbines and miniature terraces catching the pale gold light. Elias stands at your elbow, tablet glowing.]
[Music: Light, ascending synths with a gentle string underswell]
[Sound: Low hum of machinery, occasional clink of metal, distant gull calls muffled by the glass]

Narration
You step into the pilot bay still tasting the echo of the council chamber — applause braided with the quiet of people who had expected less and were given a promise. The lab smells of ozone and wet concrete, moss panels hissing faintly where condensation beads and rolls. Light falls across the mock-up like a sun spill on water; tiny channels shimmer. For a moment you could mistake the whole room for a child's map of rescue.

Elias's sleeve brushes yours. His hand is warm; the tablet screen throws blue light over his cheekbones. He looks up at you with that narrow, squinting expression he wears when he is imagining load paths and tidal vectors and, impossibly, a better world.

Elias: (soft, under his breath) "Can you believe this is ours to test?"

You (Maia): "We made room for it," you say. (Your voice is steadier than you feel.) "You made the engineering. I—" You tap the rim of the mock-up, feeling the deliberate weight of what it represents. "—I made sure the people who'll live with it have a say."

Elias: (a small, grateful smile) "You fought for the oversight clauses. You brought them in." (He squeezes your hand once, quick enough to be a touch and a promise.) "We couldn't have done it without you."

Narration
The squeeze grounds you. You close your eyes for half a breath and imagine the turbines turning in real channels, the grid nudging a bank of neighborhoods back to balance, rooftops breathing easier. The municipal badge at your hip — unclipped earlier, a soft clink in the council hall — feels like a pledge you pressed into your palm and then tucked away. Victory, for an instant, tastes like salt and sunlight.

Narration
Ibe is at the far bench, sleeves rolled, a length of salvaged wood slotted into a platform frame. His hands move with the easy confidence of someone who builds havens out of flotsam and care. He glances up and gives you an offhand grin — the kind that says he's proud and will argue with you tomorrow if you sleep in.

Ibrahim 'Ibe': (dry, amused) "You're the one who's going to tell folks the platforms won't float off on a whim, right? Because I'm tired of explaining buoyancy to the council."

You (Maia): (light) "You can tell them I personally backed your rafters with municipal sense and three layers of reclaimed plastic."

Ibe: (mock offense) "Municipal sense is slippery. Better to have bolts."

Narration
Serena runs diagnostics at a console, her wristband blinking with lattice data. She lets the professional distance hold, but when she looks at you a sliver of something softer appears — an acknowledgment.

Serena Qiu: (calm, precise) "The simulations are converging. Latency is within acceptable bounds for a pilot. We'll still need a few stress runs at different salinity gradients."

You (Maia): (careful) "And the ethical layer? Public transparency hooks? The bug-bounty oversight you proposed?"

Serena: (a twitch at the mouth that could be a smile) "I've written a protocol. It waits for signatures. You can tear through it with your community, make it mean what you need it to mean."

You (Maia): "Good. Make it readable. No legalese that sounds like rope."

Serena: (half-smile) "Already in plain language. Your neighbors should be able to veto a module without a translator."

Narration
Old Man Toma shuffles in on the edge of the pilot bay, the patchwork of his cloak whispering like dry leaves. He does not look at the mock-up or the screens. Toma's hands find the copper locket at your throat with the ease of a practiced ritual.

Old Man Toma: (gravel-soft) "May the tide that lifts this city lift the hands that hold it too."

You (Maia): (voice catching) "We'll make sure hands are holding. Not one—many."

Toma: (a breath, approving) "Good. Teach them how to read the water and the ledger both."

Narration
A pair of emissaries from Rin glide in last — sharp coats that do not carry salt, shoes that have not known patched boards. They carry a slim case, the kind that snaps with clean, final authority. The room's hum tilts toward business for a second; the emissaries' presence is a reminder that this pilot is not only hope — it is also investment, optics, and a timetable with teeth.

Rin's Emissary 1: (polished) "We're ready to release tranche one of the funding upon pilot agreement. Rin insists on a strict rollout calendar."

You (Maia): (steady, publicly) "We welcome the funding. We ask only that the calendar allow for community-led validation at each stage."

Rin's Emissary 2: (cool) "Validation is fine — as long as it doesn't impede critical time windows. The council will expect evidence of progress."

Elias: (brisk, protective) "We can demonstrate progress without cutting corners. The modules are modular — you can isolate and iterate without entire system shutdowns."

Rin's Emissary 1: (interest showing) "Agreed. We prefer efficiency, but governance optics matter. Ms. Soler, your oversight framework was noted in the briefing. Will it be the official interface for community grievances?"

You (Maia): (holding eye contact) "It will be one of them. There must be redundancy: lay councils, direct hotlines, and binding emergency protocols controlled by locals."

Rin's Emissary 2: (tapping a stylus on the case) "Rin will need assurances. Consider us partners, Ms. Soler."

Narration
The word carries weight — partner — and with it all the possible meanings: support, alignment, and also leverage. You sense the room recalibrating; every protocol you draft now will be read through boardrooms and broadcast in short, sharp press bites. It could save lives, or it could be used to consolidate power. The choice you made earlier — to step into this lab, into Elias's plan — was never about blind trust. It was about shaping what scale could look like with community DNA sewn into its seams.

Narration
Hours stitch themselves together: you and Elias lean over diagrams until your shoulders ache, Serena runs simulations that bloom on the wall in cool cyan, Ibe fits a test anchor into a floating platform and steps back to inspect its sway. The pilot bay becomes a choreography of hands and soft arguing — the best kind, where friction births improvements.

Elias: (exhausted but elated) "If we hit the timelines, we can have the first module operating before the rainy season. That means neighborhoods get a buffer earlier."

You (Maia): (the hope rising like tide) "A buffer could buy time for terraces to reestablish, for elders to settle, for kids to learn two kinds of knowledge."

Elias: (laughing, then quieter) "And we can seed the micro-ecosystems from your seedbank too. Your people will have a visible stake."

Serena: (softly, unexpected) "You watch the ethics, Maia. You make sure there's muscle behind your promises."

You (Maia): (a firm nod) "I will. I have people who know the land, and I won't let their voices be washed out."

Narration
When the consent frameworks are finally compiled — plain language sections, emergency veto clauses, accessible reporting channels — you read them aloud in the pilot bay. Voices rise and echo against glass, not in triumph but in the steady cadence of ritual. The pilot date is set: three weeks hence, when tides and resources align.

Old Man Toma: (murmuring as the group nods) "So it is marked." He presses your locket once more, as if sealing a prayer.

Narration
For a single breath the whole lab leans toward the future with you. The mock-up is no longer an object on a table; it is a promise arranged into shape. Momentum tastes like salt, sunlight, and the metallic tang of the tablet's edge. You allow yourself to imagine rescue — rooftops that no longer sag with water, markets that stay above the current, children learning both ledger and lore.

Elias squeezes your hand again, long enough to say everything you both cannot put into words: trust, caution, and the hard belief that this could work.

Narration
You close your eyes and let that image sit — a city that breathes easier because the people here insisted on being part of the design. It feels buoyant and fragile at once, like a seedling on a terrace waiting for rain.

Narration
There will be more negotiations, more late nights, and unforeseen failures. But in this morning, the pilot is set. You carry the taste of it forward — a small fierce relief braided with a rigorous plan.

The pilot date glows on the calendar in your mind. You feel not unburdened, but steadied — a person with a map and people enough to follow its lines. You allow yourself, for a moment, to believe rescue is a thing you can help build.]
