[Scene: Marenport Flooded Promenade | Morning]
[Visual: Pale gold light breaking through low, pewter clouds; shallow water beading on cobbled stone]
[Sound: Distant pump-thrum, gull calls, a metallic tap of shutters]
[Music: Soft, hopeful strings under ambient city hum]

You stand ankle-deep in the promenade's cold salt, water seeping around the rubber rims of your boots and carrying the faint, familiar tang of algae. The frayed blue ribbon at the nape of your neck presses a constant, small weight against your collarbone whenever you tilt your head. Your multi-tool bracelet is a warm, reassuring circle at your wrist; you find your fingers tightening around it without meaning to, as if the metal itself could anchor the plans in your head.

Old Man Toma is a silhouette in the fog, his patchwork cloak hanging like a map of memory. He spreads one rough hand and draws the outline of the shoreline on the air before him as if the wind could hold ink. When he unfolds the map sewn into the inner lining of his cloak, the sewn edges and faded ink smell of oil and rain. The map is older than you are; a coastline turned into a street in a photograph that sits small and folded in your copper locket.

Old Man Toma: "There — see how the quay ran, right down past the baker's? Used to be twelve lanes of cart and laughter. Now it's kelp."

You watch his finger trace the stitched line. The thread is uneven, repaired too many times, like the neighborhoods you work to keep whole.

You: "I remember the baker. He used to wrap warm rolls in waxed paper and call you a stubborn old fool when you couldn't pick a sweet."

Old Man Toma lets out a sound that is part laugh, part salt-worn exhale.

Old Man Toma: "Stubborn keeps the sea honest. But stubborn won't hold the pilings on its own. You know that, Maia."

The name — said softly, like a tide pulling back — makes the municipal badge clipped to your field jacket feel heavier against your ribs. Responsibility doesn't always arrive with fanfare; sometimes it arrives as a weight left in pockets.

You reach into the cloak and run a fingertip over the faded lines of the map. You can imagine the street as it was: children with wind-tangled hair, the baker's bell, a line of bicycles leaning against a low stone wall. It's a life of small things you are trying to preserve in a city that refashions itself between storms.

[Visual: The fog lifts in a band of pale gold; water around your boots shimmers]
[Sound: Boardwalk planks creak; footsteps approach]

Ibrahim "Ibe" N'Diaye arrives on the boardwalk like he belongs to every scrap of wood and nail. His grin splits his face in a way that makes you relax, and his hands — always splintered and stained — hang ready with tools and jokes in equal measure.

Ibe: "You keep fondling old maps and I'm liable to think you're trying to start a museum in your pockets. Where's the sense of adventure?"

You: "Adventure?" (You glance at the swollen pilings and the moss crawling up the stone.) "This is hazard reconnaissance, Ibe. Someone's got to love these pilings back into usefulness."

Ibe clucks his tongue, tapping the edge of his apron where a pencil juts out.

Ibe: "Fine. Hazard reconnaissance it is. Which ones? The eastern run by the bakery has good heartwood, but the north cluster bows under kelp. We could strap crossbeams and float them as a chain."

You: "And if the chain holds, we plant a line of salt-tolerant hedgerows between the pilings. Roots that break the current, not the tide. Old Man Toma's courtyard could use shade."

Ibe: "Say no more. I'll bring reclaimed oak and a new pattern for the clamps. You—" (he grins, plucking a thread from your jacket sleeve) "—you keep being the brains, Soler. I keep being the splinters."

You find the grin contagious. The city's hum — pumps, distant repairworks, neighbors bartering — settles into a background that feels more like a choir than a warning. Hope here is not naïve; it is the practiced breath of people who keep waking up and building.

Old Man Toma watches both of you, his expression a complicated braid of pride and worry — unreadable in the way the sea itself can be smiling and hungry at once.

Old Man Toma: "You two talk and the city grows roots back under your feet. That's the sort of miracle I can believe in."

You: "It's not a miracle," you say, and your voice keeps a steady edge. "It's work. It's remembering where things belong and putting them there again."

Ibe laughs, the sound bright as a struck tin cup.

Ibe: "Look at us. Passionate and pedantic. We'll make a proper thing of Canal Twelve yet. Maybe hang a little sign: 'Careful: Community at Work.'"

You: "And someone's got to coordinate with the Resilience Collective's plans. They're drafting floating terraces and rain gardens for Canal Twelve this week. If we align, the terraces could tuck into the pilings we save."

[Visual: Cut to a wide view of Canal Twelve in the distance; scaffolds and patched boats clamber together like a hive]
[Sound: A gentle clatter as a maintenance skiff passes; muffled voices calling measurements]

The idea feels like a promise that warms the damp air. You imagine terraces thick with soil and herbs, rain gardens that pause runoff and sip the sky; the smell of mint and salt mixing, children treading shallow water in patched boots. Hope is slow and communal, but it changes the city in ways that patents and proclamations never could.

Old Man Toma presses the map back into his cloak with a practiced motion.

Old Man Toma: "Promise me you'll tell them. Promise me you'll not let the terraces be just names on paper. The sea respects hands."

You: "I promise. We'll make the plan human-sized. We'll make it hold people's stories."

He studies you a long time before he nods, satisfied in a way that seems to stitch something between his chest and yours.

You let the boardwalk guide your step. The city is a patchwork that needs hands and stubbornness in equal measure — and you, who have always preferred hands to headlines, feel ready. The municipal badge at your ribs is a reminder that your commitments live in meetings and in mud, in both policy and peat. You press the copper locket through your shirt once — the coastline-face inside is small and precise, a life compressed to a photograph — and let the weight of it pull your shoulders back.

[Visual: The promenade widens toward Canal Twelve; morning light gilds the edges of raindrops]
[Sound: The hum of pumps rises fractionally; the metallic tap of lab shutters repeats, like a clock]

As you walk, you make lists aloud: clamps to check, seedlings to order, volunteers to call. The list is practical and intimate — a map of care. Your voice joins the city's low chorus: the cadence of people choosing, again and again, to do the slow thing.

Ibe: "Promise me you'll take a breath tonight, Maia. You carry too much like a tidewall."

You: (you hesitate, tasting the word 'promise' as if it were a salt tablet) "I will. If only because I want to keep doing this."

His smile softens. Even your stubbornness wants to be sustained.

[Music: Swells gently — a single hopeful violin line]
[Visual: A long shot of the Flooded Promenade and Canal Twelve, small figures moving like stitches across water]

For a moment the future feels like a planned structure: pilings, hedgerows, watering channels, neighbors' names sewn into margins. You can see it — not whole and gleaming, but assembled, piece by piece. The buildable parts line up like promises awaiting hands.

A distant sound draws your attention: the metallic clack of a delivery skiff aligning with Canal Twelve's float, the echo of an announcement buoying across water. Whoever is calling — the Resilience Collective, a coordinator, a neighbor with news — the voice pulls at the edges of the morning and sets your feet toward it.

You straighten your jacket, the municipal badge brushing your palm, and step off the boardwalk onto the ramp toward Canal Twelve. The fog lifts a little more; the sunlight pockets into shallow pools on the terraces ahead.

You breathe in the city: salt, wet wood, green things that will not be silenced. Your decision is not between two absolutes but between small hands and large plans, and right now you have a day's work that needs your shape.

You take one more look at Old Man Toma and Ibe — their faces are carved with lines that mean "keep going" in different dialects — and then you move forward.
