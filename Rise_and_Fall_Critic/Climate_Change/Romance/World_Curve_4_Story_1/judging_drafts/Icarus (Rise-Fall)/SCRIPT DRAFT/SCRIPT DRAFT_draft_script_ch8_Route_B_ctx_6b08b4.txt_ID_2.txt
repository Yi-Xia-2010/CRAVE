[Scene: Skyfarm Terraces | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of terraces stepping down in warm gold; people move between planting beds, drip lines glisten; a child's muddy palm smudges the edge of a reclaimed table.]
[Music: Low, hopeful strings with an undercurrent of distant mechanical hum]
[Sound: Gentle water pumps, quiet laughter, rustle of canvas awnings]

You are up among the terraces again, knees dusted with peat and salt, your jacket clasp damp where a patch of rain never quite dried. The terraces have a rhythm now: plant, patch, swear softly, laugh, pass the drills. The market below is brighter—more laughter than bargaining, a few added lanterns that someone painted with old glass. For a while, that steady, human work made the city seem like a thing that could, slowly, be mended.

Ibrahim "Ibe" N'Diaye is hauling a crate of root vegetables up a rope-ladder, grinning in a way that folds the whole evening into something smaller and manageable. Old Man Toma sits on a low step, hands folded over a thermos, watching seedlings as if they were grandchildren.

Ibe: "Maia! (You!) Save me a spot — the stew's almost done and you're on crowd-control duty."

You let the urge to say yes win. You move through the throng, palms still smelling of compost and metal, and the smell grounds you: warm broth, salt, the iron tang of tools. It is balm. It is also proof that the small things can feed a city that has learned to be hungry for other people's solutions.

You catch Elias by the tool rack. He has soil on his sleeve, but there is also a fine oil smear on his palm—evidence of the bracket he finished that morning. He holds your eye, and for a sliver you don't have to explain the ache under your ribs. The terraces are a tether. He squeezes your hand like an apology and a promise mashed together.

Elias: "They're doing good work here. Better than I expected."
You: "You expected us to fail?"
Elias: (hesitates) "Not fail. I expected friction. I didn't expect it to look so... alive." (He smiles, small and uneven.) "You and Ibe and Toma—this is what resilience looks like at ground level."

You let him look. Let him see the stitches you've made in roofs and in people's days. Still, the smallest sound of the distant barge hum wedges into your chest: a memory of larger machines moving, of the TideGrid's shadow.

You choose, but whatever your choice, the terrace hums on—hands, voices, soil. There is satisfaction in mended things. There is also a slow, sliding pressure you cannot tighten with a wrench.

A message pings on Elias's forearm-tablet. He frowns, swipes, and the light washes his face cold. You are no stranger to the flicker of bad news. You know how headlines arrive like rain: sudden, precise, and impossible to ignore.

Elias: (quietly) "Old Dock District—severe erosion. The piers—"
You: "How bad?"
Elias: "Buildings gone, docks—some of them—collapsed into the channel. They're reporting... there's footage. And Rin's people are already saying it's proof we can't rely on scattered fixes."

The words taste like salt and rust. You feel the terrace tilt just a degree and then steady under your boots. You move toward the ladder that drops to Canal Twelve, toward the smell of diesel and silt.

[Scene: Old Dock District | Early Evening]
[Visual: The skyline has been raggedly cut; skeletal piers jut like broken teeth. Wet boards float where there were once boats. A bruise of cloud sits low over the water.]
[Music: Sparse piano notes, wind scraping strings]
[Sound: Splintering wood, low tide water slapping, an occasional crack as a beam gives in the distance]

You step down from the raised walkway onto mud that pulls at your boots. The air here tastes of iron and old paint. Where terraces breathed life, the Old Dock District breathes memory—gone now, inhaled by rising seas. Toma walks beside you, each step measured as if the ground might remember him and refuse.

Old Man Toma: (voice small, far) "My brother used to sell nets right there. We danced on those planks at the summer fairs..."
You: "We still dance, Toma. We can—"
Toma: (interrupting, the skin around his eyes tightening) "You can't dance on a grave, Maia. You know that." (He coughs, not from smoke but from a grief that tastes older than the water.)

Neighbors mill around with phones and flashlights; a camera rig moves down the channel—press. A reporter's voice overlays your senses with a practiced calm.

Press Reporter: "—residents report catastrophic loss in the Old Dock District tonight. Authorities are calling this an unprecedented erosion event. We reached out to the Marenport Consortium and to Ms. Rin Voss for comment..."

The camera finds you because your jacket is still the municipal green, because your locket glints and because you have always been the face of neighborhood repair. You do not relish being framed as a symbol, but your jaw is steady.

Rin's public statement is already recorded—the feed loops on a shard of a screen. She speaks with measured authority, the kind that makes complicated things sound like necessary facts.

Rin Voss: (on-screen) "This tragedy underscores the urgent need for centralized stabilization. We cannot afford to treat the city as a patchwork of experiments. Tonight, I will petition the council to mandate a full-scale deployment of the TideGrid. We will protect the city—decisively."

Her words fall in the mud like iron anchors. Around you, voices rise—some terrified, some furious. A neighbor throws their hands up and asks what the terraces were for if not to stop this; another points to your group and calls you idealists, unready for scale.

You feel each accusation as a stone dropped in a quiet pool. Ripples widen into doubt.

Elias reaches for your sleeve. He looks exhausted—there is a line between his brows you have not seen since the early days of testing.

Elias: "Maia. I—I'm sorry. My models... some of them did show risk near the docks if the TideGrid wasn't in place. I wanted to believe the terraces could hold—God, I wanted that more than anything—"
You: "You wanted that more than anything?" (You say it flatly. Part of you wants to wipe the silt from his face; part of you wants to hold him to the thing he's abandoning.)
Elias: (after a breath) "Not abandon. Complement. I said it in meetings. I tried to—there were constraints, funding, politics. I thought we could buy time. Maybe that was arrogance. Maybe I misread the thresholds. But Rin—she's using this. She's not waiting."

You: "She never really waits." (You don't say the part about how you built your life to not wait either, and how that made trusting anyone's timing harder.)

Elias: (softly) "I should have pushed harder to make community oversight part of the rollout. I—" His throat tightens. "I didn't. I'm sorry."

You: "Sorry doesn't rebuild piers," you say. The words come out more brittle than you intend.

Elias: "I know. I know." (He reaches and finds your hand, fingers clasping like machinery and promise. "I'm here. I don't want—" He stops, the unfinished sentence a map of possible betrayals.)

The press closes in. Someone shoves a microphone toward Toma. He stares at the camera as if it's a stranger. He opens his mouth and the memories tumble out, brittle and precious.

Old Man Toma: "They took our fish market. They took the painted sign with my wife's name on it. You can't put a grid over a name and call it care."

His hands shake. You want to take them and steady them, and you find an urge to make the grief legible without letting it be co-opted into a political weapon.

Rin's emissaries arrive by skiff—slick coats and clipped smiles. A council liaison unfurls a legal form and speaks of emergency powers. The words "mandate" and "expedite" hang in the air heavy as storm glass.

Rin's envoy: (smooth) "The city needs stability now. Centralized coordination will move fastest. We will deploy the TideGrid to protect the most vulnerable districts."

Neighbor: (bitter) "Most vulnerable? Which districts, exactly? The ones you can commercialize?"
Rin's envoy: "Those that will preserve the city's function."

Preserve function. The phrase is thinly veiled arithmetic.

You walk the line between two kinds of urgency: the life you can touch and the life you might be able to influence if you had a seat at a table you distrust. Your hands ache from planting and patching; the salt has ground into the fabric of your jacket. The sense of ascendancy you felt on the terraces curdles into impotence in the face of a sudden, regional crisis.

Elias stays by your side but there is distance in his jaw that no amount of shared soil can bridge tonight. He is caught between a model he partly trusts and a neighborhood he loves. You are caught between the same things, and the net is a familiar, painful tightness: love threaded through disagreement.

[Scene: Canal Twelve | Night]
[Visual: Canal reflected lights bob, bioluminescent seeds in awnings flicker; the press stage is lit starkly with white LEDs; community volunteers gather with flashlights and wrapped tools]
[Music: Minor-key cello with an undercurrent of urban static]
[Sound: Microphones hum, the tide whispers, a distant industrial clank]

A camera operator asks you a question that will wind up on the evening loop.

Press Reporter: "Maia Soler, many see these community terraces as evidence of local resilience. Others say they're insufficient at scale. What do you respond to the tragic losses at the Old Dock District?"

You breathe. Your voice comes out steadier than you feel.

You: "We built what we could. We fed people, saved buildings, and kept communities connected. That work matters. But tonight—" (You look to where the lights bob over broken planks) "—it also shows the limits of what neighbors can do when systemic forces move faster than our tools."

The reporter nods, hungry for a soundbite. You offer no perfect line. There isn't one.

Elias speaks after you, hands folded as if to hold his words in. He doesn't try to outshine the camera with technobabble; he speaks with an exhaustion that is almost candid.

Elias: "There are things engineering can do at scale. And there are things only communities can do. If we stop listening to either, we lose both."

Your chest constricts. There is truth in his sentence and a weight it carries: that compromise feels like loss to both sides.

[Music: A single, low note lingers, then cuts]
[Sound: The distant whir becomes a roar as multiple barges converge; ropes creak in a synchronizing rhythm]

You realize—at the very bones of your hands and the very pulse behind your eyes—that tonight is the hinge. You have mended a hundred seams, and yet the sea has taken a piece of the city you love. The terraces will go on—they must—but the scale of what will be decided now feels inexorable.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands, knuckles white where you grip a railing]
[Music: Strings descend into a sustained, unresolved minor chord]
[Sound: A mechanical compound hum builds into the horizon, an approaching apparatus, purposeful and vast]

You draw in a breath, the salt and diesel and the faint perfume of stew filling you all at once. There is a loudness in the city now that is not the sound of hands, but of architecture moving into place. The barge lights swagger; the silhouette of a crane lowers something into the water—structured, vast, and final-seeming.

You cannot stop every machine. You can, for now, hold the people who are here.
