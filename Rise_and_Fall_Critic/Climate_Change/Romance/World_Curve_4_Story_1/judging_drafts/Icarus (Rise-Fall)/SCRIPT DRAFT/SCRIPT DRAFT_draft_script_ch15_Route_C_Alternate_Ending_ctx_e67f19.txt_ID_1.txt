Elias stands at the far edge, tablet cradled on his forearm, eyes narrowed at a cascading diagnostic readout. His jacket is zipped against the wind; the reflective piping catches the last light in a way that used to make you laugh when he leaned too close to explain something. Elias Kade: "They're reporting a minor oscillation in the nutrient recirculation. It's an easy patch — I wrote the firmware for that—"

Elias Kade (half-smile): "Because otherwise the crops would get stressed, and the custodial audits would flag a compliance risk. You know how it goes."

You watch him as if looking through glass. He talks about code and cycles and risk matrices the way some people talk about the weather. The language is faithful; it keeps harm contained. But the sentences end with smaller silences now — the spaces where you used to fold your hands into his and mean everything.

Elias Kade: "Did you see Old Man Toma this morning? He wanted the old dock path marked in the archive. I told him we could prioritize the upload next week."

You: "Upload. Digitize. Archive. Words that make things present and simultaneously make them disappear."

Elias hesitates, fingers hovering over the tablet as if to touch a place where a photograph might have been. He presses anyway.

Elias Kade: "I… thought saving the terrace in any form would make things better. For everyone."

You watch the sun edge below a thin cloud. Your voice is quieter than the wind.

You: "You thought saving it was the same as saving what it held. They have money for stabilizers, Elias. They have less appetite for the…messy, expensive work of restitution."

Elias looks at you properly now — not at a readout, not at a decision tree, but at you. There's the old warmth, but it's been sanded down by deadlines.

Elias Kade: "We negotiated oversight. We wrote the clause that would return management to community councils after five years."

You: "And already the budgets are rerouted to long-term upgrades. 'Oversight' became the language they used to change the ledger."

Elias opens his mouth, closes it, then looks out over the terraces where a row of seedlings stands like tiny witnesses.

Elias Kade: "I didn't see them shift the funding. I didn't expect them to—"

You: "You expected the system to do what systems are supposed to do. I expected people to keep their promises."

Elias Kade (voice faltering): "So what do we do now? Do we keep fixing things? Do we keep trusting the same institutions that made the promise and keep shifting it into finer print?"

You look at the rows of basil and amaranth, the neat spacing and drip lines humming with regulated life. The terrace is alive because of compromises and because of unpaid hands. The difference between those causes is the difference between having a home and having a stabilized project.

Narration
Your chest tightens; the old reflex — shoulders forward, spine braced — returns. You feel the years of small labor gathered like a weight in your palms: nights repairing a broken pump, mornings wrestling salt-caked hoses, the laughter of a neighbor when a seagull stole a sprig of thyme. The terrace is saved; the memory of how it was made is being quartered into sanitized records.

[Scene: Old Dock District | Morning — Weeks Later]

They call it "archival extraction." The word is clinical and precise and sounds, on paper, like respect. They photograph each mosaic in high definition, noting cracks and pigments. They label, they digitize, they file. An elder artisan kneels to press a hand to a panel one last time before a camera lifts it into something that will live on screens.

Old Man Toma stands nearby, cloak wrapped tight, face set like sun-baked driftwood. He watches the cranes lift and the technicians measure. His eyes find you and for a long moment are unreadable — a dark sea that has swallowed storms and returned glass.

Old Man Toma: "You did the best with what you could, little Maia."

You: "We tried to keep them. We argued for reinvestment, for labor guarantees. We took their promises and stitched them into the agreement."

Old Man Toma (dry laugh): "Promises. We used promises like nets after the flood. They catch a lot, but sometimes the fish slip through."

You keep your voice even, a practiced calm that masks a deeper ache.

You: "They said the mosaics would be preserved and displayed in the public archive, and that there would be a fund to support artisans rehoused with comparable work."

Old Man Toma (looking at the cranes): "And yet their cranes go up. They digitize the face of our city and call it preservation. They pick only the parts that look good in a frame."

Artisan: "They offered us contracts to reproduce the patterns in a 'community program.' But the pay is scrap, and the design approvals go through a committee I've never met."

You: "You can refuse. Organize a collective. Take back the commission."

Artisan (bitter): "Refuse? We need the money to buy back our workshop. We need the work to teach our apprentices. It's not that simple."

Narration
You stand between three truths: the city's physical survival, the artisans' livelihoods, and a corporate apparatus that understands both as lines in a ledger rather than interwoven histories. For a breath, the terrace's soil and the dock's broken tiles feel like two halves of a single wound.

[Scene Transition: Canal Twelve Promenade | Night — Years Later]
[Visual: The promenade glitters with string lights and bioluminescent seed-lamps. Stabilizer nodes pulse a soft cyan under the waterline. Market boats drift like slow planets around the pier. Your reflection in the water is slightly distorted by the tide managed by hidden machines.]

[Music: A low, melancholic cello and a faint, unresolved piano motif]

Narration
Years map themselves across faces like tide lines. The city's flood index sits lower. The Skyfarm terraces bloom under regulated irrigation; rooftop gardens flourish where stabilizers maintain microclimates. The Old Dock's physical mosaics are gone from their walls. In their place are neat plaques and a touchscreen kiosk that shows high-definition images, complete with curator notes.

Elias meets you at the base of a lanterned post. He carries a thermos and a small toolkit. There are quiet rituals you maintain together: a nightly check at the promenade's pumps, the sharing of a thermos of tea, the re-tuning of a weather vane. These small tasks feel like promises you can still keep.

Elias Kade: "I ran the diagnostics twice before I left the lab. The variance was within acceptable range."

You smile, because you know what he means. He is apologizing without saying the word.

You: "Thank you. Tea?"

Elias Kade (offering the thermos): "Black. You always take it with a little too much sugar."

You accept. The thermos is warm; the tea is better than most bureaucratic lunches. You sit on the quay edge and dangle your boots, the same boots with dried salt at the seams.

Elias Kade: "Do you remember the promises? The five-year handover? Did you think they'd keep to it?"

You turn the question like a stone in your hand. The wind brings the faint sound of a child's laughter from the market. Somewhere a vendor bangs a pan for attention; the life of the city continues on its small axis.

You: "I thought enough people would remind them. I thought an agreement with signatures and witnesses would be binding."

Elias Kade (bitterly pragmatic): "Signatures are only as strong as the enforcement mechanism. We included oversight language, but the consortium funds the enforcement board. There is a structural conflict of interest."

You: "We built the wording out of our hopes, Elias. We shouldn't have been naive enough to believe paperwork could protect centuries of living practice."

Elias is quiet for a long beat. Then he says, in a voice you barely recognize from old nights, practical and hollowed of its earlier buoyancy:

Elias Kade: "The TideGrid kept the city from drowning. The nodes reduced incident risk by thirty-eight percent in the first year. People live. Babies are born in wards that used to flood."

You: "Yes. People live."

There is a ledger that reads like a triumph: numbers arranged to show avoided loss and relative stability. The page is clean. The margins do not record grief. They do not hold the names of the artisans whose murals are now museum files. Narration
You find yourself speaking Old Man Toma's map aloud more and more — not for policy, not for a petition, but because speech resists the tidy erasure of a checkbox. You tell the story of where the net-menders used to gather, of the song that stitched the lanes together, of the man who carved a small wooden fish into every child's palm as they left the docks. Sometimes a few people stop — an apprentice who remembers, a tourist who listens with the air of someone collecting novelty. Sometimes no one does.

Elias sits close enough to warm your shoulder but not so close your hands touch. He tightens a bolt on a rail and then looks at you.

Elias Kade: "Do you hate me for choosing the negotiation route? For the clauses and the checks?"

Elias Kade (softly): "I thought we could have both. I thought the TideGrid could buy us time to do the messy human work."

Elias meets your eyes. There is a trace of something like contrition, then a resignation that feels like acceptance of a wound that will not fully close.

Elias Kade: "We still have small things. We still fix pumps together. We still drink this terrible thermos tea at night."

You: "Small things keep us from falling apart, but they don't knit the whole."

Narration
On the promenade, the stabilizers murmur their predictable lullaby. The city breathes with mechanical regularity. There is safety — a precious and undeniable fact — and there is a hollowing at the edges where living memory becomes curated content. The Old Dock is cataloged in a glossy series of digital exhibits that draw clicks from distant screens; the Skyfarm flourishes under calibrated nutrient schedules; the canal traffic moves with the rhythm of timed locks and automated barges.

You make a list in your head of the things the ledger cannot keep: the exact tilt of the light when the mosaic children danced in the morning, the smell of frying fish on a windy night, Old Man Toma's laugh when the tide caught a net just so. You store them in your voice like seeds.

Old Man Toma (from behind you, voice creaky but steady): "Sing it, Maia. Say the map. Say the names."

You stand and speak. Your words tumble into the air, small and stubborn. You map the alleys with syllables, point out secret gardens with rhythms. A few market-goers look up. A child leans closer, curious. A vendor dims his radio.

Narration
You cannot stop the archives, the money, the smoothness of a stabilized city. But you can speak. You can gather apprentices and teach them to carve again. You can organize small boons for artisans, keep a ledger of living names. You can hold a thermos to someone's hand and remind them how tea tastes when someone else makes it with care.

Elias stands beside you, listening, and for a moment — a brief, precise slice of time — you feel like a pair again. Then the light shifts and he checks his tablet, the habit of responsibility pulling him back into the sentence of his work.

Elias Kade: "Tomorrow the consortium runs the maintenance audit. I have to be at Saltglass before dawn."

You: "I'll be at the market. Ibe wants help with a boat repair. Toma will want tea."

Elias Kade (quiet): "Call me if anything goes wrong."

Narration
The ledger ends with tidy summaries: the cost avoided, the structures preserved, the percentage drop in high-risk zones. The public statements speak of partnership and shared stewardship. The private margins hold more complicated things: unpaid labor, deferred restoration, the slow bleed of cultural things that fit poorly into accounting software.

You fold those margins into a small book you carry in your jacket pocket: lists, names, recipes, the pattern of mosaics as described by Toma, the melody of the market song written in shaky notes. It will not save everything. But it will make it harder for the stories to be fully replaced by curated content.

You watch Elias walk away, a figure framed by lamplight and the soft pulsing of stabilizer lights. He does not look back again until he is already stepping into the shadow of the canal, and even then his glance is quick — practical, protective, and remote.

Narration
In the ledger of the city, the bodies are intact and breathing. But the human ledger — the one that keeps track of who learned whose craft at whose knee, of which alley hosted the first rain-jar exchange, of how the old docks smelled on a certain night — that ledger is frayed. You keep adding pages to it anyway.

You set your jaw and let the words of Old Man Toma's map fall from your mouth once more into the market air. The people who hear you remember in pieces. You teach, you protest, you organize. You scrape what you can back into place.

You are tired. You are stubborn. You are not reconciled.

Narration (Final)
We saved the city's body. We kept roofs over heads and tides at bay. But at the margins where hands made meaning, the maps were digitized, the mosaics lifted into glass, and the histories trimmed into neat exhibits. The final ledger balances — an engineer's and a consortium's triumph — are clean. The human ledger is not. It is a fold of weathered paper, smudged and written on in cursive, passed hand to hand, recited in a market, tucked into a copper locket.

You walk the promenade with a thermos warm against your palm and a book of names in your pocket. Love remains: in the tea shared across a late repair, in the hands that still show you how to knot a rope, in the small lit things you refuse to let be archived into silence.
