Narration
You watch Elias from across the lane. He stands near a public terminal, fingers tracing the glass like someone searching for a seam between policy and possibility. He looks smaller in the rain, sleeves damp, the micro-sensors on his boots blinking an almost domestic blue. Elias Kade: (approaching) "Maia."

Maia Soler: "Temporary in whose ledger? Temporary is a word that looks good on a screen."

Elias Kade: "I thought— I thought you'd see the tradeoffs. That you'd understand the math. We can save lives now and argue about oversight later."

Narration
His voice is precise — calibrated to remove the jagged edges — but the rain catches on his lashes. When you reach for the wafer-thin edge of his logic, he closes ranks.

Maia Soler: "You say 'we' and it keeps getting smaller until it fits the margins of a report. 'We' used to mean the neighbors, Ibe's apprentices, Toma's routes. Who's in your 'we' now, Elias?"

Elias Kade: "Everyone. Maia. Everyone's in the statistical 'we.'"

Maia Soler: "Statistics don't hold hands through storms."

Elias Kade: "I signed because I believed it would buy us time. Isn't that— isn't that worth something?"

Maia Soler: "Buying time by giving away the means to shape it is a bargain with no receipts."

Narration
A doctor passes between you, eyes tired, briefed and hurried. For a flicker you see the ledger's other face: a child in the hospital ward whose breathing is steady because a node diverted flow; a power indicator glowing green against a gray backdrop. You can both be true — the ledger saved lives tonight and also took a piece of the city's skin.

Elias Kade: "I miss the way we could argue in the same room and still come back to coffee. I miss being able to trust that if I was wrong, you'd tell me and we'd change it together."

Maia Soler: "Trust isn't a private good, Elias. It's public. You privatized the thing that kept us honest."

Elias Kade: "Then help me make it public again. Help me write the oversight protocol. Stand with me."

Maia Soler: "Standing with you would be standing with the ledger. Standing with the ledger looks different to those whose moorings were shifted at dawn."

Narration
He looks down. There is a small, private grief in the set of his mouth. You want to take his hand, to undo the bureaucratic coin he has dropped between you, but your fingers curl into your palm instead.
