## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
Marenport is a near-future coastal archipelago city reshaped by sea rise and storms. Streets have become canals; rooftops are terraces and skyfarms; reclaimed wood and glass mingle with patched floating platforms. Political power is contested between community coalitions—neighbors, carpenters, elders—and emergent climate-tech conglomerates who promise stability with centralized infrastructure. Public institutions are strained, faith in top-down bureaucracy eroded by slow responses to crises; technocratic charisma fills the vacuum. Culture is pragmatically communal: barter and shared labor alongside market stalls bolstered by small-scale tech. Religion and ritual survive in place-memory—old dock festivals, the elders’ shoreline prayers—that anchor communities against erasure.

Geography is layered canals, terraces, ruined piers, and retrofitted labs like Saltglass perched above tidal channels. Class divides run along access to advanced materials and networks; inland districts with consortium backing have dry streets and clean grids, waterfront neighborhoods rely on patched floats and mutual aid. Conflict centers on one proposed intervention: the TideGrid, an engineered, city-spanning tide-control system championed by Rin Voss and implemented via consortium resources. It promises scale but risks centralizing governance and sidelining local autonomy. The atmosphere is melancholic but verdant—salt-scented air, moss and rust, golden light after storms. Everyday life is hopeful pragmatism under a low cloud: people grow food on terraces, barter in floating markets, and argue about what “saving” the city should cost.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I narrate as Maia Soler, a community-led resilience organizer and scientist raised in Marenport. The story is an Icarus arc: a rapid ascent toward influence and promise, then a catastrophic fall. Early chapters build momentum: I reunite with Elias Kade, an engineer and my love interest, whose TideGrid offers systemic salvation. The common route sets our intimacy and conflicting convictions—my people-first localism vs. his scalable techno-optimism—while Rin Voss maneuvers to accelerate deployment.

At the main split, I choose to align in different ways: fully with Elias (Route A), double-down on grassroots resilience (Route B), or attempt a compromise (Route C). Each branch rises quickly—public support, technical breakthroughs, media attention—then meets a shattering blow: political co-option, a storm that outpaces defenses, sabotage, or bureaucratic emergency powers. The climax is a catastrophic reversal that forces urgent moral reckonings: whom I trust, what I sacrifice, and whether love can survive the fallout.

Endings vary by route: negotiated hybrid compromises that culminate in heartbreaking loss of autonomy (Bittersweet), community-led truth that survives but suffers real destruction (True), authoritarian stabilization that preserves physical order at social cost (Sad), and a tempered reconciliation that still collapses into crisis before a fragile recovery (Happy-with-fall). Across all paths, the emotional curve rises to a peak—visibility, breakthroughs, reconciliation—and then crashes, forcing Burrs of guilt, rupture, and the slow work of rebuilding.
