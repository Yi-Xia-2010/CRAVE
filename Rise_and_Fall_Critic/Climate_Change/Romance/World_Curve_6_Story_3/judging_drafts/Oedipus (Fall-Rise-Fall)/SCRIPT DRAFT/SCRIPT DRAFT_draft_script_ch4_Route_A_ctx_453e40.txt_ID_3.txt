You tighten the braided bracelet until the woven cord leaves an impression against your skin. The pressure anchors you the way a tidal buoy anchors a net: small, stubborn, necessary. You had chosen this—refusing the easy consolidation of power, refusing Arman's pitch of speed at the cost of people—and that choice settles in your bones like ballast.

The pier smells of seaweed and new rope and a faint, stubborn tang of diesel from a volunteer's generator. Dampness clings to everything; your yellow hooded raincoat drinks the mist and wears it like armor. Elena moves beside you with the economy of someone who has learned to carry both toolboxes and other people's fears. She hands you a clipboard with the morning roster scrawled in a dozen different hands and ink colors.

Tomas stands a little apart, cap in hand, and when he speaks the younger folks quiet as if remembering a prayer they do not know the words to yet.

Tomas Belmar: "When I was a boy, kelp told you the tides. You could see where to step, where the water would kiss the stones. It kept us alive. It wasn't fancy, but we knew how to listen."

You: "We still know how to listen," you say, before you can stop yourself. The sentence is steadier than you feel.

Tomas's face softens. He nods, one slow motion like the pull of an outgoing tide.

Tomas Belmar: "We do. We just need more hands and fewer suits."

A ripple of half-laughter, half-cheer runs through the crowd. The mood tightens into a fierce, hopeful rhythm: people trading tools for stories, lists for songs.

[Visual: Close on your hands as you flip open your notebook—pages of cross-sections, tide notes, and a hastily drawn map of assets. The camera lingers on a child's sketch of a smiling seawall made of plants and ropes]
[Sound: Nails hitting wood, a saw whining briefly, someone humming an old work song under their breath]

You set the map on a crate and start to call names, threading volunteers into roles. There's a roster of divers who know the piers by touch, two small-business owners willing to loan floats and tarps, a high-school carpentry class with saws and scheduling headaches, and a scatter of retired dockworkers who bring steady hands and stubborn opinions. Priya kneels beside you, tablet angled toward the sun, running figures into a grant template with the practiced speed of someone who translates urgency into numbers that funders understand.

Priya Anand: "If we frame the initial phase as a rapid pilot—modular planting bays, anchor tests, sensor validation—there's a fund that responds to community-led pilots. We can target it. I'll draft language for equitable access and maintenance plans."

You: "Make it clear the maintenance is built into the budget. The city funds things that look like they will survive without constant back-and-forth."

Priya (tapping): "And highlight community hours. We own the labor line item. That'll shift the narrative from 'temporary patch' to 'living infrastructure.'"

Noah stands a little to the side, clutching a bundle of cylindrical devices wrapped in duct tape and labeled in his cramped handwriting. He looks the way he always does when he wants you to trust him—warm, a little sheepish, the kind of person who apologizes in smiles. He slides the prototypes forward like an offering.

Noah Ríos: "They're lighter than the last batch. They run off tidal amps and a little solar. I thought—if we can log a few weeks of data during anchor tests, we can show measurable impact on sediment retention and loading."

You meet his eyes. There's the familiar pull: the math of his optimism; the way practical results could turn a room of wavering officials into allies.

You: "I don't want sensors to do the talking for us, Noah. We need the mouths and hands of this neighborhood to show up. But—" you falter, then make the choice in the space between your breath and the gulls' cries, "—we'll use them. Open schematics. Anyone who wants the designs gets them. No patents, no locked platforms."

Noah's jaw tightens for a heartbeat, a private argument playing across his face. You don't know what compromises he imagines, but you know the posture you need from him.

Noah Ríos: "Open-source. Okay. I can—I'll push the team on that. It... it feels risky, but I get why."

You: "Risk is part of living with water. Secrets make walls, not harbors."

Noah offers a small, relieved grin. The exchange is not tidy; he shifts, casting a glance toward the downtown skyline where Arman's logos glint on billboards. You understand his calculus—funding could scale his tech faster than grassroots channels—but today he stays here, sleeves rolled, lending tools and a reluctant, earnest faith.

You map more than tools; you map trust. Elena moves between clusters of people—apologizing, delegating, smoothing tensions. She catches you once and squeezes your hand with a pressure that says everything her voice does not.

Elena: "If we pull this together, the city will have to take notice. Voices are louder than a single meeting."

You: "They are louder if we keep them fed and warm and part of the plan. Tomas—can the old nets be repurposed for the anchoring tests?"

Tomas Belmar: "They can. Bring the hot glue and the stubborn ones. I'm not letting the nets go to waste."

Several minutes of practical argument follow—about rope patterns, sinker weight, where to place the modular bays so they don't interfere with navigation. The conversation is messy and alive; you love that. People interrupt each other, counter with lived memory, and the pace is punctuated by the sound of wood meeting nail. It's the kind of friction that makes a thing real.

Priya taps her tablet and sends a message to Cass's office. You don't expect funding today—Cass has to balance politics, after all—but you do expect moral clarity. A reply arrives: a short, entirely human note from the Mayor's aide. Cass cannot allocate funds right now, the message reads, but she offers the city's logistical support—permits fast-tracked, waste pickup prioritized, and a promise that municipal engineers will visit the pilot. The words land like a cautious hand over your shoulders.

Cassandra 'Cass' Green: (over the phone, voice steady) "Mara, we can't fund it today. But I can open space in the city's schedule. Tell me what you need for the next forty-eight hours, and I'll move mountains."

You: "We need permits moved, someone to sign off on the temporary moorings, and an official visit—something to show that this isn't just a neighborhood hobby."

Cass (softening): "I'll be there when your first anchor goes in. I'm coming down to see how the people of East Strand do what they always do—turn trouble into work."

You let yourself believe the promise for a heartbeat. Belief is not naïveté here; it's a tool. It stains your fingers but it moves things.

[Visual: Montage—hands measuring, an elderly woman passing nails to a teen, Noah calibrating a sensor while an apprentice watches, ropes being braided, kelp lines being clipped; the light brightens in narrow shafts through the morning mist]
[Music: Strings swell into a warmer, fuller chord; light percussion picks up a steady, hopeful tempo]

You map out anchor tests and modular planting bay placements on the wet map. The plan is not perfect, and it does not need to be. It needs to be honest, repairable, and shared. Your notebook fills with new lines and names—volunteer divers, a small bakery offering coffee, a fisher ready to barter smoked fish for labor.

Noah pulls you aside for a moment. His hands still tremble a little when he talks about scaling tech, but his eyes are steady.

Noah Ríos: "If this shows even incremental impact, I can take the data to a couple of foundations. If we keep the designs open, maybe we can set up a cooperative licensing system? People still get paid, communities keep ownership."

You: "We keep it open. We also make sure the money flows to maintenance and to people who live here. No more projects that look like progress but empty homes."

Noah: (after a beat) "Agreed. We'll write maintenance into the model. Community first. Then we talk scaling."

His voice has the weight of making a promise out loud. You hold it like a fragile crate: carefully, intentionally.

[Visual: Wide shot of the pier as the sun suddenly slices through a cloudbank, gold catching on the wet boards and the damp kelp—light that looks like approval]
[Sound: A spontaneous cheer—neighbors clapping as one of the modular anchors holds in a test pull; somewhere, a radio crackles with a weather update, but it sounds ordinary, not catastrophic]

For a moment the work feels pure: place kept by hands, by history, by stubborn love. The chant of hammers becomes a rhythm you can lean on. You look up at the shadow of the council clock—its face a reminder that time keeps moving—but the neighborhood pulses beneath it, alive and organized, not waiting to be told what to do.

You tuck your notebook back into your sling. The hydrophone at your side hums faintly, recording the low frequencies of pile work and conversation like heartbeats. You breathe in the salt and sawdust and let the feeling of "we can" settle into you, not as a prophecy but as a method.

This day will not win every argument. It will not erase every hardship. But it is a hinge—the kind of small, mutual labor that forces policy to reckon with people who will not be moved.

Your heart lifts with the gulls. You listen to the quiet roar of a community deciding it will not be bought out of its future.
