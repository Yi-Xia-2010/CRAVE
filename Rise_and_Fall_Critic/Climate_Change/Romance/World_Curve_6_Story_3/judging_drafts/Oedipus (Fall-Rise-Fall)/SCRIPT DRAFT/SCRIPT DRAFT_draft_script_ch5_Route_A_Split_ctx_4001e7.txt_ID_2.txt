[Scene: Decaying Pier Platform | Morning]
[Visual: Mist willowing over a broken concrete pier; ropes and kelp lines crisscross like a loose web. Small groups cluster around a patchwork of modular floats and marsh mats. Noah crouches beside a corroded buoy, fingers stained with algae.]
[Sound: Water slapping against pilings, the soft creak of moorings, a distant gull.]

You taste salt and machine grease on the back of your tongue and feel the pier give under your boots—brackish, cold, a small, living thing beneath the soles. Your hands are already damp from uncoiling kelp lines; the fibers snag at your gloves like memory. The hydrophone hums softly in its sling against your ribs, a steady bass that matches the slow intake of your breath.

Noah: "Okay—if I patch this readout into your notebook we can get a baseline in real time. The sensor array's picking up wave spectra right now. Look—see that notch?"
Noah's voice carries the easy, quick cadence you know too well: problem, pun, solution. He doesn't wait for permission; he plugs the sensor jack into the battered port on your notebook, the screen coughing to life under a film of salt.

You watch a line of numbers bloom and then settle. The first tidal damping graphs are... promising. Certain frequencies are attenuated. The marsh mat's drag coefficients look better than the lab estimates.

Priya: "That's within the margin. If we vector the mats and adjust anchor spacing by—"
Priya taps a tablet, fingers moving like a clinical metronome. Her face is all focus, the NGO's calm at work: translate hope into percentages. "—we can likely reduce immediate bed scour by thirty percent in those bands."

You let yourself feel it, briefly—a thin upward tug under the sternum: relief. Not triumph. Not yet. But a path, outlined in pale numbers. Around you, neighbors who'd turned up to help nod and whisper; Tomas is already measuring a line with the practiced economy of someone who has watched tides tell stories for decades.

Tomas Belmar: "When I was a boy, the pilings were a whole dock. Funny how the sea remembers the shape of things we forget."
Tomas squints at the mats, then at you. His hands move like chords—steady, weathered. "This'll hold soil. Not forever, but maybe long enough for a different kind of work."

You press the pendant to your palm as if to anchor the feeling.

[Visual: Close-up on your notebook screen; Noah's sketch overlays real-time readouts in smudged graphite.]
[Music: A faint chord of uplift, immediately tempered by low undertones—hope threaded with tension.]

Noah: "Run the hydrophone playback, will you? Listen to the difference at the quarter-hour mark—we should hear the energy dampen."
You set the hydrophone on a rock; its diaphragm picks up the bay's voice—the small, contained roar of churning water. The waveform shifts. The group exhales with you, a small, shared intake.

A woman near the kelp beds laughs, wet and fierce: "If this works we can actually call this ours." Her laughter breaks like glass and mends.

Priya: "I can, but it'll look like a pilot. Pilots are for 'proof of concept.' They don't always persuade people who already bought stories they like."

Her words fall like pebbles into the water—small, deliberate disturbances. You sense, more than hear, the thread of the city's calendar tightening. The happy noise of tinkering rubs against the city's politics and frays.

[Scene: Kelp Test Beds | Late Morning]
[Visual: The test beds ripple as a tide edge rolls in. Small hands steady lines; a child's sneaker leaves a wet print on a plank. Elena stands near the market stalls, jaw tight.]
[Sound: The distant clatter of a delivery truck. A phone chirps with an incoming call.]

Your phone vibrates inside your coat. The mayor's number flashes—Cass. The group grows quiet the way water falls quiet before a storm.

You answer.

Cassandra 'Cass' Green: "Mara—thank you for being by the pier. I—I'm listening to Priya's feed. This looks good, but the Council meeting is Friday and the investors are—"
Cass's voice is compressed, careful; the public ally trying to hold both sides of a widening street. "Arman is pushing his timeline. He's framed our approach as 'delay' in a front-page piece. We need a demonstration of short-term protection. Can you give me something I can show them by tomorrow?"

Her plea isn't private. Behind it you hear the dry, efficient click of the press office. You feel the room tilt: demonstrations placate headlines; headlines bend votes.

You: "Cass, the prototype shows damping. We can have a filmed demonstration. But a single test isn't a proven system—"
Cass: (interrupting, softer) "I know. I know that's all you can promise. But if the city doesn't look like it's doing anything, the vote will swing and Arman's plan will move forward. I can try to stall, but investors are outraged publicly—there are op-eds, Mara. People are worried about payroll, about ports closing."

You picture the city as a ledger—jobs on one side, homes on the other—and feel your throat tighten.

Noah, leaning close enough that you can smell the warm citrus of his coffee: "We can make the demo cleaner. I can reroute sensors, run continuous playback. If we produce a focused clip—wave <-> damping—it'll be hard to frame as 'delay.' We show mitigation."

You: "That might calm the Council. But it doesn't buy anchor integrity in a real storm."

Noah: "It could buy time."

Noah's 'it could buy time' lands with the weight of a hinge: crucial, and thin.

[Sound: A soft notification. An op-ed headline slides across the group's phones: "Delay Is Costly—Why Esperanza Needs a Real Wall Now," signed 'A. Kade'.]
[Visual: Cut to the headline on the tablet; Arman's name bold, the op-ed's tone brisk and inevitable.]

The piece is elegant in its cruelty: graphs of GDP projections, images of cranes, moral language about 'decisive action' and 'protecting commerce.' It skirts the word 'displacement' like a professional avoiding a thorn. It calls local pilots 'sentimental experiments.'

Elena: (voice breaking) "They're saying we're sentimental. They sent us notices." She holds up a paper—rent, eviction, a small official stamp like a judge's gavel. The sound of folding paper feels like a strike.

A shop owner nearby crumples, head in his hands. Tears aren't dramatic here; they're raw, practical things. A stack of rent notices, a battered cash register—these are the immediate arithmetic of survival.

Tomas steps forward, voice low: "Arman knows how to write fear for other people's mouths. He learned that from debt collectors." He looks at you. "He's buying the frame."

Priya: "If Cass can't show immediate protection, the narrative becomes 'we can't wait.' And the Council will vote for the thing that promises no short-term risk. No one will read the footnotes about who moves."

Your sense of responsibility narrows. It centers like a stone in your chest. Every minute spent perfecting the living design is a minute investors call reckless. Every compromise made to appease funding means someone might be left to face a flood without their home.

Noah: "—and a different timeline. You'd keep community input on shape, but we lose some control over procurement. We would get funding and a chance to prove the concept before Arman builds a wall that pushes people out."

You watch his face. He wants this to work as much as you do; he also needs the runway to keep his team afloat. There's fear in the way his hand brushes the sensor—fear of running out of time, and of not being 'good enough' to justify the risk.

Noah: "Either we give them a demo and try to force a moratorium politically, or we take a phased pilot and keep a foothold. Or—" He stops. You can see the list of practical compromises trailing in his glance.

Your internal monologue hums with equations made of people: lives saved now vs. dignity preserved later. You think of the storefront you lost at sixteen; you think of Elena's face, of Tomas's steady hands. Every strategy feels like a ledger where someone bears the heavier end.

[Scene: Decaying Pier Platform | Afternoon—Low Tide]
[Visual: The tide pulls back, exposing gull-scattered debris and a map of footprints. The community clusters into small circles, voices low and urgent. Clouds gather like a held breath.]
[Sound: A far-off siren that might be a stormtrain or simply another municipal vehicle; the slap of water grows louder, closer.]

Elena approaches, her hands clasping a torn awning like a small, defiant flag.

Elena: "If we don't block Arman—if we don't make them look, really look—then they'll bulldoze the row. They'll build the new promenade and call it progress. Where do we go, Mara? Where does Tomas go? Where do the bakers go?"

Her plea is not rhetorical. It is the thin, concentrated thing that asks you to choose what kind of pain will happen on whose watch.

Tomas: "We could stage a blockade. Bring our boats and our bodies. Schools of small boats won't stop cranes forever, but they'll stop the first concrete pour. They'll change the optics. Make it messy for him."

Priya: "Blockades invite fines and legal trouble. Investors can fund legal pressure. We risk criminal charges; Cass could lose political cover if things get violent."

Noah: "We could take the pilot funds. We'll get materials that are certified, even if not our ideal. We can build faster and show immediate mitigation. But we might lose the community procurement clauses you asked for."

You look at the marsh mat underfoot, its woven fibers slick and stubborn. You hear the bay whisper: hold, let go, hold, let go. The day grows shorter and colder.

Your responsibility feels like a ring tightening around your ribs. It makes choices narrow and hard. Every route costs someone something: time, money, home, dignity. You're the fulcrum—no, you refuse that image—you're the person standing where the map forks, and people will move because of your decision as much as any council vote.

Noah steps closer; his voice is low enough that only you hear it.

Noah: "If we escalate, people will see that you mean it. They'll rally. But if we compromise, we keep a seat at the table—maybe for years. If we prioritize evacuation, we save lives now but concede land."

You meet his eyes and understand the worn trade-offs behind the calm language. He is balancing his love for the place with a practical terror of being branded heroic but bankrupt.
