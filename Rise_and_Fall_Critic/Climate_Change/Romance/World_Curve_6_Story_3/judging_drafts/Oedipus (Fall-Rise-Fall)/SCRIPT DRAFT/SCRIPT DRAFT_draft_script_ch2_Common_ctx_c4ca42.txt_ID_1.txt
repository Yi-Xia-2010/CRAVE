You push open the heavy metal door and the greenhouse breathes at you — warm, humid, the smell of damp earth and salt-slick air tangled together. Light falls in streaks through the high panes, filmy and gray; droplets bead on leaves and catch the harsh fluorescent glow from the lab bench lamps.

Priya is there before you, tablet in one hand like a shield. She looks up without surprise, as if she’s been waiting a long time for this moment — which she has. The impact models bloom across her screen: a grid of neighborhoods, color bands for exposure and equity, tiny numerical cells that pulse with urgency. When she taps the screen, the colors snap into a triage: red, ochre, green. The green is sparse.

Priya: "You're late by three minutes and thirty-two seconds, which is symbolic of how procurement views good intentions."

You: "Symbolic or not, you could've texted."

You step closer, boots leaving muddy prints on the concrete. Her tablet smells faintly of ozone and printer ink; the models look clinical from here, but the margins have notes in her handwriting — 'equity weight +.15', 'short-term measurable protective index'. The note from Cass's aide is folded on the counter like a talisman: the mayor will consider pilot funding only if you can show measurable short-term protection.

Priya: (softening) "Cass's aide left a timeline. Pilot funding is conditional. She needs a convincing metric that shows—" (she taps the tablet, bringing up a bar chart) "—that we can reduce immediate flood exposure within the next city procurement window."

You: "Which means what, in plain language?"

Priya: "It means they want results that look like a win on a ballot pamphlet. Something fast, demonstrable, with numbers and a press photo. They want a measurable reduction in inundation risk within the next fiscal quarter."

The phrase lands like a stone. You remember the tin sign you found under a piling — your old storefront — and how memory had filled the letters. Now, measurable metrics want to overwrite memory with bars and graphs. The greenhouse smells of wet rope and kelp; your fingers find the edge of your notebook without you thinking.

Noah sits at the adjacent bench, sleeves rolled on a pale-blue shirt. The smartglasses on his face throw light across his cheekbones; when he notices you looking, he widens into that half-smile that crumples the corners of his eyes. Up close, the silver streak in his hair seems more like a comet than a flaw. He sets down a modular sensor wrapped in tape — the prototype humming faintly — and gestures toward the wave tank.

Noah: "We're running a calibrated test. Kelp trunks tethered to a mock seawall — living mats, flexible terraces, sensor nodes. The band's streaming data now." (He taps at the sensor. Colored dots blink on the tank's display.) "If the nodes can show dampened wave energy at the shoreline proxy, it's measurable. It's immediate."

You kneel at the tank, water lapping against acoustic foam, tiny kelp fronds bowing as a mechanical piston simulates a surge. Noah's voice turns softer — not quite professional, not quite private.

Noah: (quiet) "I can show peak attenuation curves. We can make a case with numbers. But it's not the whole story. Living systems take time to root. Community buy-in is slower than a press release."

You: "That's the problem. Cass wants fast numbers. Arman's bid will force the calendar — and his 'fast' means hard walls, relocated people, and ribbon-cutting photos."

Noah hesitates. His fingers hover over the sensor, tracing the tape as if it were fragile skin.

Noah: "If we can prove immediate attenuation — even a modest percentage — Cass can pitch pilot funding as 'proof of concept' and buy us time for the living parts to mature."

Priya: (sharp) "Proof for who? Investors? Voters? The procurement board? Proof doesn't guarantee equitable rollout. Metrics can be eaten by political appetite if the narrative goes hungry."

You feel the old weight settle on your shoulders like a damp blanket. Your field notebook is warm against your palm; you flip to a blank margin and begin to sketch: a cross-section of a seawall built from living mats, community terraces spilling into public paths, sensor nodes wired to Noah's band. Your pen moves with a speed that feels like prayer. Each line is an argument.

Priya swipes, bringing up a table of procurement timetables. Procurement timelines are merciless — she uses that exact word, like a verdict. The mock wall's attenuation numbers look promising in the model, but the green bars thin at the neighborhood margins — the ones that represent East Strand, where the community gardens and old storefronts still cling like stubborn barnacles.

Your phone vibrates. Elena's text arrives: a photo of a market stall, half-submerged, crates tumbled into standing water; the tarpaulin is torn and flaps like a wounded flag. Her caption is one word: "Again."

You feel her as if she had stood beside you: paint-splattered overalls, the stubborn set of her jaw. You want to answer with a plan, with urgency, but the lab's hum keeps you tethered to the models. You thumb a reply that says — I'll come by — and it does not say what you mean to those words: that you'll try to keep the place from changing beyond repair.

A voice clip chimes. Tomas. The message is terse, his voice rough with salt and distance: "Can we hold?" The question is a cliff; the sound cracks at the end like old concrete.

You: (thinking) "Hold. It's not only a physical ask. It's a demand for time, for dignity, for not being moved like pieces on a map."

You pace the greenhouse pathways, mud on your boots, the smell of wet soil climbing the back of your throat. The vertical gardens rustle as your shadow passes. Priya watches you with a narrowed expression that holds both calculation and concern.

Priya: "If we can show measurable short-term protection at scale — even in a controlled scenario — Cass can argue for a pilot that protects critical blocks. But Cass needs political cover. Arman is pushing a faster calendar. If his private bid reaches the council with a ready-made ribbon and a contractor network, procurement will favor certainty."

Noah: "We can get the numbers from the sensors. I can harden the streaming and give you visualizations — wave height, energy flux, attenuation over distance. But we need a deployable module in two weeks for a controlled site test. We need materials, volunteers, and a lawful place to anchor the prototype."

You stop at the wave tank, watching the kelp trunks bend and pulse under mechanical surges. The living mats flex; the mock seawall gives and recovers. The sensors blink in time with the piston's cycles. The kelp smells faintly of iodine and something older — memory, perhaps, or the smell of your childhood shoreline.

You tuck your pen behind your ear and write a single line across the top of your sketch: "Show localized attenuation. Protect market row." The promise tastes thin in your mouth.

Noah leans in, earnest now, his professional mask slipping to reveal something rawer.

Noah: "If we do this, we do it together. Your design, my sensors, Priya's metrics. We make the math and the story coincide. We buy the community time."

You meet his eyes. There's warmth there, yes, but also pressure. The closeness of someone who wants to be useful is almost as heavy as someone who expects you to carry everything alone.

You: "The city's clock is ticking. If Arman plants a flag and the council smells security, it's over before our roots take. We need concrete, but not the concrete he's offering."

Priya: (decisive) "Then we engineer a middle ground. A compact, demonstrable section of living wall that shows measurable attenuation in the short term. We'll be honest about limitations, but we present clear, unambiguous numbers and a communications plan Cass can take to the public."

Your chest tightens. Being honest about limitations is a moral line — a necessity — but also a vulnerability. You imagine Cass on the dais, press lights like hungry gulls, choosing between two photos: one of a smiling developer beside a polished hardwall, the other of muddy boots and a band of neighbors pointing to a green mat. Which photo will the camera choose?

You sketch faster. The greenhouse's filtered light becomes thinner, as if the day itself is being consumed by the urgency inside. Priya pulls up a procurement flowchart and circles a node labeled 'Pilot Approval' in a red pen; the red looks worryingly like blood on the page.

Noah doesn't need to be told what's at stake. He looks at you with a steadiness that could be comfort or accusation, depending on how you choose to receive it.

Noah: "Promise me one thing. If this goes sideways—"

You: (interrupting) "I won't let it go sideways."

Noah: (softly) "Promise me you'll call me when you need help — not just when you want a data point."

You hesitate. The promise is simple and expansive; it assumes trust and time — two things the calendar may not allow. You think of your family's shuttered storefront, the stubbornness of a tin sign and the way memory had filled in missing letters. You think of Elena's text and Tomas's clipped voice.

You place your palm flat on the cool rim of the wave tank. The water reflects a fractured version of the greenhouse: plants upside down, lights doubled, your own face a ghost overlay. The sensors blink like distant lighthouses.

You breathe in salt and soil.

You: (quietly) "I'll work harder. Quieter. Smarter."

The words are meant to steady you, to be a vow against panic. But they are also a confession — a warning that you have already given yourself away to the problem.

[Visual: Close-up on your sketch — the cross-section annotated with numbers, arrows, and small, hurried hearts in the margins where your plans touch people's names]

You glance at the whiteboard where Priya has started drafting a one-page pilot brief: objective, measurable outcome, timeline, risk mitigation. At the top, in sharp black marker, someone has written: "Pilots win policy." It feels like a literal translation of what you've been fighting against.

You step back from the tank, notebook clutched to your chest, and feel the hinge of the moment. Outside, the city is a smear of cranes and billboard faces; inside, the greenhouse smells of possible things and threatened things. The timeline presses in like a tide.

You draw one last line in the margin of the sketch — a bracket that ties the living mat to the market row. It is a small act, a decision encoded in ink.

You fold the notebook closed, tighten the pendant against your sternum, and let the sound of the bay — distant gulls, a truck coughing on diesel — echo through the lab.

This is the point the calendar will measure against. This is the hinge where proof must bend into policy or be crushed flat.

You step toward the bench where Noah's sensor waits, ready to be hardened for a real test. Your boots leave another muddy print on the floor, a small, stubborn mark that says you were here and that you chose, however quietly, to try.

You do not resolve the choice today. You only promise the work.
