[Scene: Abandoned Pier & Kelp Nursery | Misty Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of the crumbling pier, new rope scaffolds threaded between rusted bollards; rows of kelp lines hang in the water like green banners]
[Sound: Soft creak of rope, gulls calling, distant muffled voices]

You tighten the braided bracelet on your wrist the way you always do when a plan is about to start changing shape — not to keep it from falling, but to remind yourself that the knot ties you back to a place that remembers your name. The yellow hood of your raincoat is down, but you move through the pier like armor nonetheless: bright, deliberate, visible.

Seaweed, damp wood, and a faint diesel tang thread the air. Salt water beads on the concrete, reflecting the first pale shafts of sun through the fog. The new rope scaffolding smells of rope and fresh-cut timber; someone has left a thermos by a bollard, steam still rising from it.

You and Elena set up a patchwork of folding tables under the shelter of the old boathouse where the roof still holds. Elena's hands are quick and sure as ever — stacking screws, marking out a stencil for modular planting bays. She wears a paint-splattered apron that looks like a map of every small fix she's ever done for the neighborhood. When she catches your eye, she gives you a nod like a covenant: no more talk, just work.

Tomas stands with his cap in his hands, the wind cutting the edges of his voice. He's older than the photographs you have of the pier, sleeves pushed up over knotted wrists. Around him, a scatter of younger volunteers — a mix of kids who grew up here and newcomers who've learned to love the smell of kelp — lean in as he speaks.

Tomas Belmar: "Back when the tide was our calendar, kelp told you the safe hours. It wrapped round the pylons like a curtain and held the younger fish close. We worked with the water; not against it. Remember that. You'll know when to plant, when to pull, and when to let the bay breathe."

You listen to the cadence of his story more than the facts. What he offers is not instructions but authority, an older rhythm to match the frantic energy of the morning. You feel your chest loosen a fraction. This is what you came back for: hands that know how the place lived.

Noah is there, awkwardness folded into the crease of his smile. He carries a crate of sensor prototypes wrapped in foam and duct tape, the better for showing things in public without making them feel finished. He sets the crate down near the workbench as if laying down a soft contraption, then watches the group with an expression you can't quickly name — eager, yes, but measuring, too.

Noah Ríos: (quiet, offering) "I can run a quick baseline if you want. The sensors will tell us where the currents are strongest and whether the anchors hold. If this goes in wrong, we can lose everything."

You meet his eyes and let the warmth you feel for him sit beneath the work. He looks at the project like a sonogram: instruments first, people second — and you know he's not cold about it; he's scared. You answer with the thing he needs more than the tech.

You: "We do the measuring together. You run the baselines, and you promise — no secret meetings with investors promising fast walls. If you're here, you're here with us."

Noah inhales, then lets his shoulders drop like a quiet surrender.

Noah Ríos: (half-smile) "All right. I'm here. Open schematics. Open data. No secrets."

The words feel small and enormous at once. Priya, holding her tablet like an extension of her hand, scrolls through a draft grant timeline, her expression tight with practical optimism.

Priya Anand: "If we frame this as a community-led pilot — with documented metrics and matchable in-kind contributions — we have a shot at emergency funds. Cass's office won't sign a blank check, but she can amplify a proposal if she sees community buy-in. You need evidence and a story that doesn't sound like pleading."

You watch Elena jot down numbers and names: volunteer divers, a roster of small businesses that can donate floats, carpenters who've worked the pier since before Tomas could keep accounts. The list grows like a small map — anchor points you can tie hope to.

The assembly spills forward into motion. A group breaks off to lift timber and begin framing a modular planting bay. The wood is rough, the cuts are honest, and someone's laughter rings when a board doesn't fit and must be re-cut. Children run between adult legs, delivering nails and offering opinions about where the kelp lines should hang. One small girl, her curls catching seawater, pushes a damp frond of kelp into your palm and beams at you as if you've earned a medal.

The carpentry team works in a rhythm that sounds a little like chanting: measure, cut, fit, nail. The sound of the hammer driving home is percussive against the low murmur of conversation. People trade stories between blows — a history of the pier, a memory of a storm that took a roof, the small victory of an anchor that finally held last summer. The stories weave into a narrative of competence and stubbornness; the pier has been knocked down before and made new by hands that refused to leave.

At the edge of the worksite, Priya kneels by the water with her tablet, overlaying a map of the current tests Noah is running. She points to a clump of darker water and explains, patient and sharp.

Priya Anand: "If we stagger these bays with a staggered line of floats, the wave energy gets dispersed. We don't stop the water — we slow it enough to let the kelp re-establish. The sensors will show stress points; we reinforce there. It's incremental — but metrics will show it's effective."

You reply, filling out the softer part of the argument that Priya's numbers need.

You: "Incremental is what keeps people here. Little by little, the work folds into daily life — fishermen, shopkeepers, kids — and the place keeps its own story. That's as vital as new curves on a chart."

Noah, hovering with two sensors in his hands, catches your sentence and looks like a man who has been offered a path he didn't think he'd take.

Noah Ríos: "I wanted— I dreamed — to build something that could be scaled by a single weekend's install. It's faster. It's sexy. But I see how fast can mean leaving people behind. If we prototype here, open-source, and document everything — you might be right. This could be a model that grows without selling out the place it starts in."

Something in his voice softens, and you notice the complex lines in his face: hope braided with the ache of needing funding. He is not giving up scaling — only recommitting it to community stewardship. The promise shivers like heat over water.

Tomas walks between the benches, touching the edge of a planter the carpenters have just bolted. He tests the knots in the new rope and smirks like a man who just read a favorable weather report.

Tomas Belmar: "Work like this is what keeps the city from forgetting us. These hands you're using? They remember tides. They remember how to save. Use that memory."

A volunteer raises a hand, voice edged with both fear and conviction.

Volunteer: "Arman will come with machines and lawyers. They'll call it saving— but it's for profit. What do we do if they try to buy the permit for this pier?"

The question hangs. You look at the people gathered: Elena's jaw clenched in line with your own, Priya's expression taut with protocol, Noah's hands resting on a sensor box like a lifeline, Tomas's gaze steady.

You: "We don't wait. We build. We show that what we do here is effective and grounded in our lives. We document every anchor, every buoy, every volunteer hour. We make it undeniable that this is civic value, not a charity case."

A murmur spreads like a tide answering. Someone whooped. Someone else started to clap. The sound is ordinary and enormous.

As afternoon drifts toward the warm gold of late light, you stand back and let the work fold into itself. Modular planting bays rise and are tested at shallow anchor points. Floats bob like a school of pale moons; ropes hum a comforting, domestic music. People teach each other — how to tie a quick-release loop, how to check a knot's integrity, how to rinse kelp to save the good spores. A child tugs at your sleeve to show you a tiny, perfect sprig she says will grow big enough for a stew pot.

Cass's office hasn't sent a check, but a courier arrives with a handwritten note of moral support and a promise that the mayor will bring the assembly's results directly to the council if you can present measurable evidence. It's small, but political oxygen nonetheless.

Priya nudges you and shows the tablet: a timeline, a list of in-kind donations, and a mock press blurb that frames the work not as desperation but as resilient urbanism — the kind of language that softens bureaucratic ears.

Priya Anand: "No checks yet. But this — this is the kind of story they can put on their balance sheets. We can make funders nervous for the wrong reasons. We can make them see you're building civic infrastructure, not just community feeling."

You breathe in the smell of kelp and sawdust, and the horizon feels less like a threat and more like the next step of a long, shared chore.

The council clock still ticks somewhere distant — a reminder that politics keeps time differently than tides — but here, at the pier, another clock is being wound: one measured in anchor tests, in hands that know rope, in a child's kelp sprig.

You tuck your notebook back into your sling and run your thumb over the pendant at your throat. The work is messy and loud and painfully slow in parts, and utterly right. For the first time in a long while, your responsibility feels distributed across a hundred small shoulders and a thousand small decisions. That thought pulls a clean, fierce hope through you like salt through cloth.

You want to catalogue every moment, every knot and every smile — but it's enough to know the shape of the next day. You have made a beginning that others can see. The pier contains noise and work and a stubborn pulse that says this place will be defended by the people who live it.

You straighten and look at the assembled faces — Elena, Tomas, Priya, Noah, the children — and feel the surge of something like victory, provisional and luminous.
