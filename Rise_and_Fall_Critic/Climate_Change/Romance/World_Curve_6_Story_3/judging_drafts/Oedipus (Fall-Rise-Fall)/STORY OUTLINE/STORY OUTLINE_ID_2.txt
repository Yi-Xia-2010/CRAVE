## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Esperanza

---

## World View
Esperanza Bay, year 2042, is a near-future coastal metropolis shaped by rising seas and improvisational adaptation. Once-industrial docks give way to kelp nurseries and floating markets. The city is a mosaic: high-tech privately managed seawalls and corporate promenades stand adjacent to patchwork community gardens and patched-up storefronts. Power is contested between municipal officials, private contractors, civic nonprofits, and community assemblies. Class lines now map to elevation: the economic core—banking, logistics, entertainment—receives hardened protections first; low-lying neighborhoods like East Strand eke out existence through cooperative maintenance of kelp farms, improvised levees, and informal knowledge passed down by fishers.

Culture balances pragmatic engineering with deep local ritual: tide‑listening, communal kelp harvest festivals, and weekly “shore talks” where elders remember the bay’s pre‑flood histories. Religion and civic ritual have fused into seasonal memorials for lives and buildings lost to the sea. The political structure is municipal, but private capital—led by contractors like Arman Kade—wields decisive influence through investment and media. NGOs, grassroots collectives, and small-startup engineers form a porous third sector, negotiating access to funding, permits, and public goodwill.

Geography is salt-saturated: narrow streets sloping to the harbor, derelict piers reborn as kelp nurseries, warehouses turned resilience labs. The atmosphere is humid and urgent—constant rain forecasts, low light, and the distant mechanical thrum of pumps and barges. Everyday life fuses survival tasks with tender domesticities: mending nets, monitoring sensors, and sharing bitter-sweet coffee while debating whether the next solution will save or erase a way of life.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mara Evans) begin already under the weight of loss: East Strand’s shoreline is threatened by an accelerating cycle of superstorms and investor pressure for a privatized seawall. The story arcs in an Oedipal emotional curve: Fall → Rise → Fall. In the common opening, I confront a crisis—my community’s market is inundated and the city debates a fast, corporate seawall championed by Arman Kade. At the split, I must choose how to act: partner with Noah Ríos (a pragmatic engineer and my love interest), oppose Arman through political mobilization, or intensify purely community-driven living infrastructure. Each route delivers a temporary renaissance: Noah’s modular sensors and rapid deployment seem to save streets; a political victory forces a pause on Arman’s project; or community-built living seawalls bloom. But every "victory" contains compromises and unseen structural pressures—funding strings, political backroom deals, or ecological limits. The endings are tragic in different shades: sudden breach after apparent success, forced displacement after a political pivot, or moral fracturing between Noah and me as we make irreconcilable compromises. Across routes, my empathy, stubbornness, and sense of responsibility drive choices that yield temporary hope and ultimately catalyze a larger collapse—leaving survivors to reimagine resilience amid loss.
