[Scene: Old Shipyard | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the shipyard at first light—broken skylights, stacked coir logs, and a line of volunteers forming like tide marks]
[Sound: Low gull calls, the steady rasp of boots on weathered planks, the distant thump of a generator]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings; quiet percussion picking up tempo]

You step back onto the shipyard with the notebook tucked under your arm and the compass pendant against your sternum. The harbor's silver is still half-sleeping, but here—between the warehouses and the makeshift staging tables—people hum like a well-rehearsed chorus. Tools clink, breath clouds, and the air smells of damp wood, diesel, and peat turned hopeful.

Your palms remember the checklist. Your lungs remember the meeting. Your throat keeps a small, steady line of resolve.

Bento is already there, leaning on a hand-spliced rope like an old mariner's staff. He looks up when you approach; his face folds into the habit of being glad you came, not surprised that you did.

Bento Morais: "You look like you slept with plans and dreamt of bollards. Come on—coffee and a terrarium of advice."

You grin despite the weight behind your ribs. Old Bento's voice is the sort that smooths knotty things into rope.

You: "Coffee. And bring the terrarium."

Bento hums and guides you through a knot-and-nudge check of the first coir log placements. He speaks softly to those whose hands shake, the words more practice than sermon.

Bento Morais: "Not everything we fix here is wood and mud. Sometimes you fix the hands that hold the work. You keep doing that—people will come to remember how."

You: (internal) He means the town as much as you do. He always has. The thought steadies the tremor of responsibility in your chest.

[Visual: Volunteers—patched coats, reflective vests, a scattering of hand-knit beanies—move in organized clusters. A Polaroid of the harbor from yesterday peeks from your notebook's teeth.]

Dr. Lian Zhou arrives with a tablet and a rain-smeared smile, the hum of her instrument cases like a companionable insect. She speaks in precise syllables, but there's warmth tucked in the ends.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Morning. Quick transect along Grid C to confirm planting elevation. Tide models show the low marsh band will be about twelve centimeters higher on this run—"

You: "—than yesterday's upload. I have three community-sourced elevation points that fill the margin. You want them?"

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Always. Community data narrows the uncertainty envelope. Pass me the CSV and we'll overlay with the LIDAR. If the coir lines ride an extra five centimeters up, the willows survive the first season."

You feed her the laptop; the screen hums as colored elevation points crowd the map. Lian taps and squares a line of numbers into reassurance.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "If we nudge the planting mats up by—say—ten centimeters in this stretch, we keep root zones moist but not drowned at high tide. It buys the seedlings a season of breathing room."

You: (internal) That breathing room feels like an act of mercy and engineering at once. It's precise, patient. It is something you can show to the town and say: we made this with care.

Jonah arrives with a cart of willow stakes, boots salted to the seams, a scarf knotting his neck like a banner. He sets down the Polaroid he took—an image of yesterday's shoreline, its edges already softened—and ducks his head against the breeze.

Jonah Reyes: "All right, boss planner—where do we pretend the sea can't reach and then prove it wrong?"

You laugh, a real sound that loosens the morning. You shoulder a sack of coir, and then the two of you arrange stakes shoulder-to-shoulder like people who have rearranged the world together before.

You: "We don't pretend, Jonah. We give it something to come at and find a reason to leave. We build ribs from the land that ask the tide to nudge, not crash."

Jonah Reyes: "That's poetic. And dangerous. And exactly what this place needs." (He plucks a stake into place, the wood biting into damp soil.) "Watch the angle—lean it windward, or the tide learns to use it as a handhold."

You: (internal) His hands are competent and warm. His jokes are a map back to the unhurried days before the storms got louder. Standing beside him, you feel the pull of something patient and true—work that takes time, and a person who will stay through it.

A cluster of teens led by Kai Tan threads through the workers like a current. Kai's blue hair gleams under a cardboard sun hat; the kid hands out seed packets and grins at anyone who looks exhausted.

Kai Tan: "Night crew cadets assemble tonight—lower thermal stress window, more oxygen in the roots. Mira? You up for a lantern check?"

You: "I can do a head count. And bring extra gloves."

Kai Tan: "Perfect. Also—Bento says you'll teach them a knot. They need to stop making 'sailor' hands and start making 'shoreline' hands."

Bento cackles. The town's old rituals settle into the new project like familiar songs.

Midday, the generator coughs into life and someone sets out a thermos of soup. Volunteers rotate shifts. You check the laptop again—elevation overlays, Lian's notes, Jonah's Polaroid, Kai's roster. The shipyard hums like a machine that's learning a new rhythm.

A white van eases up the gravel, a polite logo on the side. A woman steps out with a folder the color of commercial paper and a smile that's calibrated for fencing against skepticism.

NGO Representative (Marley Quinn): "Hello—Marley Quinn, Tide Resilience Initiative. We've been following your pilot online. Congratulations—it's inspiring. We have a funding program that could scale this across neighboring embayments."

You: "Thank you. We appreciate the interest."

Marley Quinn: "Our grants include branding and impact windows. We require logo placement on public materials and a six-month deliverable timeline. It accelerates replication—"

You: (internal) The offer is large-sounding, polished. It smells of resources and slick print—not bad things. But there is the friction of imprint, of someone else's name on what you've made with your hands.

Jonah folds his arms, eyes narrowing the tiniest degree—the harbor's light catching his jaw. He doesn't dislike money; he dislikes being told how to hold his heritage.

Jonah Reyes: "We need to make sure the families who fish here still recognize the shoreline when it's done. Branding's fine—if it's not our brand first."

Marley Quinn: "We can negotiate local governance clauses. We prefer consistent branding to secure further regional donors, but a naming co-op is possible. We want to amplify, not overwrite."

You: (internal) The phrase "naming co-op" feels like a possible bridge. It could be enough, with careful wording, to keep community identity intact while unlocking the ledger you need.

Before you can draft a reply, Asha Verma approaches the line of seedlings. Her coat is clean; her glasses slide down her nose in that clinical way that reads as if she's about to correct the world. The warm lift of the morning takes an edge when she begins to talk.

Asha Verma: "Good work, everyone. I inspected the berm footings on my way in—some of your anchor points are under-spec for a Category Four surge. If you want longevity, you need reinforced berms and a hardened tie-back system. Also, the planting elevations aren't uniform; you'd expose roots."

You: "Lian's been calibrating—she's adjusting elevations as we speak to account for the transect variance."

Asha Verma: "Calibration is fine—if the structural base is sound. There will be costs to retrofitting if the first big event hits."

You: (internal) Asha's directness is sharp but useful. It can't be dismissed as cold; it comes from a ledger of losses. Still, her priorities feel different from the slow, communal fixes happening here.

Asha slides a hand through her braid, the silver streak catching light. Her eyes are unreadable, a sober plane. Bento watches her with a steady, quiet curiosity—his expression unreadable in the other way the Schrödinger world sometimes allows: he weighs, he remembers.

Bento Morais: "No one's arguing for a papered fortress, Asha. We're building with what works and what the people will tend. That matters."

Asha Verma: "Community stewardship matters—up to a point. If the system fails, stewardship won't stop the water."

Jonah Reyes: "Depends on what you call 'system.' People aren't variables on a spreadsheet. They hold knowledge."

Asha Verma: (softening a fraction) "I know you do. I'm not talking about erasing that. I'm talking about augmenting it so we don't have to choose between losing everything and clinging to anything."

You: (internal) The conversation folds into something constructive instead of collapse. Both of them—Jonah's hands, Asha's calculations—are trying to save the same thing from different angles. It feels less like opposition and more like two instruments in the same orchestra tuning.

Dr. Lian steps between the current and the tidal argument, fingers on her tablet, calm as a tide chart.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Both perspectives improve the design. If we document the pilot with rigorous monitoring and phase upgrades based on threshold triggers, we can build community-led structures that meet certain resilience benchmarks. Marley—if your grant can include staged funding tied to monitoring milestones, and we keep local ownership embedded, that syncs the two."

Marley Quinn: "That is workable, if the milestones and metrics are cooperative."

You: (internal) The idea of staged funding—a handshake between science, community, and capital—slides into the shape of something you could recommend. It's not perfect, but it feels honest.

The day slides into a warm, tired glow. Seedlings are laid in rows like promises. The youth night-planting roster grows thanks to Kai's tenacity. Jonah and you share a thermos of something smoky and bright—corn and shellfish stock—and your shoulders hit like a simple, relieved semaphore: we did today what tomorrow asked of us.

You: (internal) The moral knot tightens, yes. But in the knot there is purpose, and in the purpose there is trust. The community has turned up. Experts and donors have shown interest. The shipyard is not just a place of work; it's a place where choices get made with hands in the dirt.

[Sound: Evening tide thud, distant bell; music shifts to a higher, hopeful motif]

You walk the final line with Lian, her tablet glowing in the dusk. She taps a flagged threshold for monitoring.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "If we opt for stricter monitoring and a smaller footprint, data will follow and donors will see replicable results. If we take the grant with branding but insist on governance clauses, we get faster scale and more immediate protection but have to watch for mission drift."

You: "And refusing the grant outright—what do we lose, besides funding?"

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Potentially speed. But you keep autonomy and community ownership. There's grace in that—just less bandwidth to react."

You pause by a stack of seed packets, the Polaroid still tucked in your notebook. Jonah folds another mat and looks up as you consider the three forks of the path.

Jonah Reyes: "Mira—whatever you pick, don't think it has to be a betrayal of what we are. It's a question of who we invite to help and on what terms. I trust you to hold us in the choice."

You: (internal) His trust rests on something patient and fierce. It is a tether you do not take lightly.

Asha, watching the line of seedlings, looks at you and then away. Her face briefly unfurls complex unreadability—neither warm nor cold, a careful ledger.

Asha Verma: "Make the choice that keeps people from getting hurt. If you want my support, prioritize robustness. If you want me to stand with you publicly, show me data that says this will last."

You: (internal) Her conditions are sharp, reasonable, and inflexible—the sort that will save or cost lives depending on timing. Even so, the presence of her willingness to stand means the door is not closed.

You have the models, the volunteers, the community's hands, Lian's protocol, Jonah's steady muscle, Bento's stories, Kai's energy, and an offer that could change the scale of all this.

The bell on the boardwalk rings out in the blue afterglow.

You inhale salt and smoke and the tiny green scent of seedlings. The moral knot tightens into focus: keep it small and local; accept the NGO offer with governance clauses; or push for the rigorous, smaller pilot that prioritizes monitoring.

[Music: Single, ascending chord—resolving into a bright, expectant tone]

You stand in the center of the shipyard—the built and the building looking back at you like a harbor waiting for a choice. The town has done its work today. Now it asks you to bind those efforts with a decision that will shape who stewards the shoreline and how future hands will find it.
