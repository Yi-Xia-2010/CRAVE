You push through the corridor toward the lab space where Dr. Lian keeps her maps; a figure leans against the wall — Bento Morais. He looks smaller in the hall than the stories make him: hunched just enough to be weathered, hands callused into a map of their own. His voice is low, rough with age and salt.

Bento Morais: "You left, Mira."

The sentence sits like silt: simple, heavy, hard to breathe through. It is not accusation wrapped in a flowery wreath. It is a stone set down.

You: "I—"

Bento's eyes don't offer judgment so much as a ledger of memory. He sighs and looks at his hands as if they might open a pocket in time.

Bento Morais: "Not all leaving is betrayal. Sometimes it's survival. But people here remember who stayed and who didn't."

You want to answer him with the polished logic you've rehearsed — models, funding streams, pilot feasibility — but the words feel thin. Guilt is a tidal sediment; it coats everything and tastes like old iron.

You: "I came back to help. That's what I promised myself. I know I can't undo what happened. I can only try to make sure it doesn't happen again."

Bento Morais: (a small, rueful smile) "Aye. You better be good at trying. Tide doesn't pity good intentions."

There is no absolution in his tone. There is only the town's long memory and a demand for work that answers that memory with something more durable than rhetoric.

[Visual: Glass panes streaked with salt; benches littered with sediment cores and a laptop open to a teal-and-ochre model. A soft LED glow mixes with failing daylight.]

Dr. Lian Zhou meets you at the lab with the latest projection pulled up on her battered laptop. The screen is a jagged timeline: five-year runs of wetland loss, nodes of risk, confidence intervals narrower than your stomach.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "The model doesn't like uncertainty in political will. It likes parameters. It likes decisions. We ran the scenarios Asha suggested and the community-build alternative. You can look at the numbers, but numbers only tell you trajectories."

You lean in, breath fogging the screen for a moment. The colors on the map are clinical and cruel: teal where marsh survives, ochre where it dies back. The line between them feels like the edge of a wound.

You: "How fast—"

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Five years is the clearest inflection point. If we don't change what's happening now, the marsh contraction accelerates. If we intervene—either with living shorelines or engineered barriers—the outcomes diverge. One buys time and cultural continuity; the other buys land and, frankly, certainty against catastrophic surge."

You: "And risk?"

Dr. Lian Zhou: (she pinches the bridge of her nose) "Community-led restoration is lower-tech, lower upfront cost, but it's variable. Its success depends on labor, long-term maintenance, and the biological response. Engineered defenses are faster and more predictable for flood prevention, but they require enforced zones and relocation allowances. Both have social costs I can't put into a model except as variables with large error bars."

Her voice is precise but tired. You hear in it the thing that haunts all good scientists: clarity that will not ease moral pain.

Evelyn finds you in the lobby. Her face is the weary professionalism of someone who has borne votes and grief in equal measure.

Evelyn Sato: "Council wants a recommendation, Mira. A pilot, a maintenance plan, or the phased infrastructure commitment. They want a clear voice from you."

Before you can answer, Asha arrives—coat trimmed with the kind of precision that makes municipal planners look like surgeons. Her eyes are the steel-blue you've seen before: fast, scanning, already cutting through a problem to its functional skeleton.

Asha Verma: "We need enforceable zones and a sea-gate pilot. Without decisive infrastructure, Tidehaven remains a patchwork waiting for failure. I can lead design, but I need someone to shepherd the technical deployment—someone with ties to the town and technical credibility."

Her gaze lands on you, measured. There is no softness there; it is a professional demand, an offer wrapped in inevitability.

You: "You're asking me to sign onto relocating people if necessary. That's—"

Asha Verma: (interrupting) "I'm asking you to do what will save lives in the event of catastrophic surge. Managed retreat is not abandonment; it's targeted sacrifice to prevent greater loss."

You want to argue the moral calculus, but Asha keeps returning to metrics and thresholds and the language of lives saved. It's a ledger you respect, even when it gnaws at your heart.

Jonah arrives from the other side of the lobby, harbor smell still clinging to him like a second skin. He looks tired in a way that feels like a physical bruise. When he speaks his voice is warm but roughened by strain.

Jonah Reyes: "Or we go the other way. We rebuild the marshes the old way — people, boats, poles, seed. We teach, we plant, we work the way our people always have when the water came too close. Keep the harbor alive and the life that depends on it."

His fingers curl reflexively around the leather strap of his Polaroid camera. There is hope braided through his words, stubborn and stubborn as an old dock piling.

You: "Both of you make sense in different ways. One offers structure and speed; the other offers continuity and humility."

Evelyn Sato: "And the town will split if you don't find a way to hold both values. We need a recommendation that can survive public scrutiny and funding committees."

Dr. Lian steps close, fingers still stained with mud from a field sample.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "A hybrid is possible, but it requires a phased, evidence-driven pilot with community oversight. It buys political cover and time, but it is more complex to manage and requires trust—two commodities this town is short on."

The conversation tightens and loosens like a net. You feel tugged by history and data and the grain of human lives that resist being reduced to either.

Jonah Reyes: (quiet, almost pleading) "If you choose retreat, you'll be writing an obituary for things people still love. If you choose living shorelines and they fail... you'll be blamed for delay. I don't want you to carry blame, Mira."

Asha Verma: "If we delay, we risk loss that could have been prevented. I won't stand by if we choose sentiment over survival."

Evelyn Sato: "Mira—"

You are the hinge. You can sense the physical metaphor of the town mapping onto you: seams, fault lines, places where pressure builds and cracks form.

Your hands feel like maps of past storms: the callouses from shoring up a fishing net, from steering a small rescue boat, from nights spent arguing grants in anonymous rooms. They are the ledger you touch when numbers and memories fight.

You notice, with a small, private shock, that none of them is giving you absolution. Bento's sentence clamps under your sternum. Lian's models hum against your ribs. Jonah's plea vibrates like a taut line. Asha's eyes are steady, unblinking.

Everything crowds toward a single hinge moment. You imagine the town as a body: a ribcage of policies and traditions, lungs of marshgrass, a pulse dependent on both currents and compromise. Your voice must be the one to set the next beat.
