[Scene: Greenhouse Collective | Morning]
[Visual: Sunlight through mottled glass, motes of soil drifting in shafts of green-tinged light]
[Sound: Soft drip from a leaking gutter; distant gulls]
[Music: Gentle, buoyant strings; slow tempo]

You pull on your knee-high rubber boots and feel the familiar resistance of salt-stiffened fabric. The Greenhouse Collective smells of wet soil and brine—the combination that feels like language to you: a vocabulary of salt and root and steady work. Your compass pendant rests warm against your throat; the brass catch of it is a small, private ritual before any public decision.

Jonah is already there, sleeves rolled, floury soil under his nails. He moves through the trays of spartina and eelgrass with a practiced economy: two hands, one steady rhythm. When he looks up his smile is slow and a little wry, as if anything else would be impolite after the weeks you’ve both carried.

Jonah: "Morning. I figured you'd be late for the soil tasting." (He waves a finger at a tray.) "We lost last week's batch to slugs, but these look promising."

You let a laugh out that tastes like relief. The greenhouse walls exhale fog as you step between hanging planters; the air wraps around you, warm and humid. Your weatherproof notebook is already in your inside pocket, tide notes folded between pages.

You: "I wouldn't miss it. Show me where you want the plugs for the northern run."

Jonah lifts a seed packet from a well-thumbed box—your hands brush for a heartbeat as he passes it to you. The packet is simple: lines of hand-labeled ink, a smear of soil on the corner. When his palm meets yours it’s rough, warm, the imprint of nets and hauled lines. You feel something steadying in that small exchange, like a plug finding its place in mud.

Jonah: "Dr. Lian said to stagger plugs at twenty centimeters if we get a full tidal flush; she was specific about wave vectors. We can push the denser layout if we aim for quick sediment capture, but that'll take more hands."

You watch his fingers trace a potential line across a seed tray. His practical courage—the same thing that makes him fold his body into the cold of a dawn net-pull—shows up in small decisions here. You want both the careful science and the messy public will. You want to be the planner who listens and the neighbor who gets her hands dirty.

You: "We'll pace it. Start denser where the current drags silt and ease it out toward the channel. If the town shows up, we can shift on the fly."

Jonah: "That's the thing—people will show up if they feel like it's theirs. Not something imposed. That's why these seed drives matter."

You both fall into the steady work of potting and labeling. Fingers chill, then warm from damp soil; the repetitive motion is a kind of meditation. Your notebook fills with tide notes, volunteer shifts, and a running list of householders to consult. Jonah steals a Polaroid from his coat, snaps a picture of your bent profile—sea-green tips smeared with mud—and hands it to you like an offering.

Jonah: "For the record," (he grins) "you make marshes look good."

You: "Keep it—it'll be good blackmail at the ten-year reunion." (You pin the Polaroid to a shelf with a clothespin; it flutters slightly in the greenhouse breeze.)

A rustle at the door announces Dr. Lian Zhou. She steps in with a handheld sediment probe slung over her shoulder, salt on her boots. Her presence narrows the air into attention—scientific and kindly, the kind of correction you trust because it’s free of politics.

Dr. Lian: "Morning, Mira. Jonah. I ran the tidal model again after last week's samples. The sediment load is higher along the western bend—if we don't plant a reinforced fringe there the currents will erode our new growth before it takes root."

You feel the familiar thrill of a hypothesis folding into practical step—science giving you a language for persuasion. Dr. Lian's corrections are gentle but precise; she kneels, tapping a plotting map with the tip of the probe.

Dr. Lian: "If we stagger the planting grid to mirror microtopography—places of pool and swell—we'll increase retention by roughly fifteen percent in year one. The probes show stable organic content near the old piling; aim plugs there and create a denser knot around the piles."

Jonah: "You had me at 'old piling.' Anything that gives us a fighting chance."

You: "That buys us time to build community knowledge," you say. "And to show folks this isn't just us playing at conservation."

Dr. Lian: "Exactly. Data plus boots." (She gives a small smile.) "Come by the lab later; I can print a map for the shipyard team."

[Scene Transition: Old Shipyard | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Shafts of amber light through broken skylights; graffiti waves painted along support beams]
[Sound: Hammers, soft chatter, distant tide lapping against timbers]
[Music: Layered percussion—rhythmic, building hope]

You walk the plank into the old shipyard with Jonah at your side and a crate of seed trays balanced between you. The space is enormous and intimate at once: long shadows stretch across puddled concrete, and the smell of salt mixes with rust and wet wood. Old Bento Morais is there, cap pulled low, a half-circle of volunteers already forming around him.

Bento's voice is the kind that threads memory into instruction. He moves like someone who has seen five seasons too many to trust haste, but not so many that he has given up on new hands.

Bento: "We weave the sedge bundles like a song," (he says, the cadence of a storyteller) "not to keep the sea out—that's arrogance—but to teach it where to slow. You bind the bundles, you plant the roots, you speak the names of the bays, and the land remembers."

You feel the town assembling into a human tide: Kai Tan's blue crop flashes through the crowd, handouts and seed packets in their backpack; an elderly couple you've knocked on the doors of arrives with thermoses; a group of kids trail a dog with sea-salty fur. What you planned on paper becomes sound and touch and the warm stress of real people wanting to act.

Kai: "We printed the petition copies. People want to help—some just need to know they aren't being talked at."

You: "That's the point," you say, your voice steadier than you feel. "We build with them, not for them. Teach, then learn."

Bento leans toward you, his hands folded in the way of someone offering something precious.

Bento: "When I was a boy we built dikes with rope and wooden stakes. We sang to the tide, yes—nonsense to the modern ear. But the sound matters. Make work into belonging and people keep at it longer than contracts demand."

As the afternoon progresses, you find yourself alternating between roles: planner, listener, inventory clerk. You mark volunteer shifts in your notebook; you sketch the planting grid Dr. Lian suggested across a folded blueprint. Jonah coordinates teams by voice and gesture, his hands an extension of trust. He hands a team a spool of salvaged timbers, and you watch him teach a knot he learned from his father—no lectures, just show-and-do.

Dr. Lian moves through the site with her probe, dipping it into soft, newly turned sediment and reading the numbers aloud like a blessing.

Dr. Lian: "Young organic content here—good. The micro-pools are forming just where we hoped. If we lay the woven bags along this contour, the aggregates will start catching silt in a season."

You: "And Bento's bundles along the channel mouth?"

Dr. Lian: "Yes. Combine both approaches. The bioengineering plus the bundles gives structure to early colonizers. It's decidedly low-tech and smart-tech shaking hands."

Evelyn Sato arrives as the light bruises into gold. She steps from her car with a small stack of documents and the kind of tired optimism that belongs to people who have to hold both hope and vote counts.

Evelyn: "I read Dr. Lian's memo," (she says, handing you a copy) "and the council asked pointed questions, but the permitting committee approved a pilot. It's small—just a permit for the shoal run and the western bend—but it's a start. We can show measurable results and build from that."

You feel a physical loosening—relief you can name. The permit is a line across the ledger that turns a plan into a possibility.

You: "Thank you. We'll make it matter."

Evelyn: "Make it matter," she repeats, softer. "Make it matter for Bento's grandchildren and for the people who still keep old nets. And Mira—" (her tone shifts, intimate, the old family friend surfacing) "look after yourself in the middle of it. You can't carry everyone."

You open your mouth to answer, but the thrum of the shipyard fills the space between words. People chant, nets are rolled, and hands meet brand-new calluses.

Jonah sidles up beside you, the late sun haloing the edges of his hair. He looks at the gathered teams, at the pallets of salvaged wood, at the map in your hands. There is quiet pride in him, the kind that doesn’t need to be declared.

Jonah: "You did good. Not just on paper."

You meet his eyes; for once, words are surplus. You let that small, earned quiet pass between you, a stitch before the larger work.

Your notebook is heavy with people’s names now. Tide notes, volunteer contact lists, the map of the planting grid scrawled with Dr. Lian’s corrections. The day moves into evening; the last light slicks across puddles and looks like a promise.

You think, briefly, of the long arc ahead: the shipyard build that will turn these bundles and bags into structural anchorage, the nights of shivering under tarps, the meetings where persistence will have to be persuasive. For the first time since you returned, the ledger of your choices contains more than risk; it contains allies.

A gull shrieks and the tide answers. You close your notebook, fingers tracing the dirt-smudged margins, feeling the rise of something that has more to do with people than plans.
