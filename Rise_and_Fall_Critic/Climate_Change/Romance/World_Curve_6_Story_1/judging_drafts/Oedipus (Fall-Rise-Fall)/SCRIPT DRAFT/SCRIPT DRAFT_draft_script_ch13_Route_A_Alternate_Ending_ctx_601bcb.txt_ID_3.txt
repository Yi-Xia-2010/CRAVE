You step off the boardwalk and the air hits different here—heavy with peat and the metallic tang of silt. The surge has left a memory in everything: a dark high-water line like a bruise across doorframes, chairs half-buried in mud, a child's sneaker wedged in reed stems. Your boots sink once in decayed plywood. Your compass pendant lies warm against your sternum; you don't touch it.

You open your notebook. The page is damp at the corner where the salt air finds it; you've been leaving lists like offerings—what worked, what failed. The lines blur together until the two columns read the same thing: compromise.

Bento stands by a collapsed stoop, hands in the pockets of his soot-dark coat. He chews on a bit of rope like it's a bitter herb. When he looks up, he doesn't smile. His face is a map of years and weather.

Bento Morais: "You did what you thought the town needed, Mira. That counts for something."
You: "It counts, but—" (You taste the syllable like iron.) "It wasn't enough where it needed to be."

Bento shrugs, that slow ocean shrug that has outlived policy memos and grant cycles. He picks at the warped plank beside him.

Bento Morais: "Time and tide don't listen to notebooks, shorty. They listen to holes and seams. You learned that now like the rest of us."
You: (You want to argue—about methodology, about evidence, about the weeks of meetings you led—but words curl into foam and vanish.) "I thought if we did it by the book—clean data, controlled variables—we could convince more people. We could prove it's safe."
Bento Morais: "Books are good. They teach you how to read the weather. But the weather sometimes reads you first." (He looks at you with a look that is both soft and sharp.) "Don't let the books become a wall."

His voice is small against the clang of something loose in the rafters. You close your notebook and let the silence be part of the conversation.

You walk through the parcels you supervised. The planting mats are torn; bundles of cordgrass lay half-upright like defeated flags. At the seams between experimental plots a rival tenderness of water found purchase—the pilot's neat edges were a promise you had to keep, and the sea didn't keep appointments.

Dr. Lian Zhou stands bent over a wet tablet, fingers moving with the precise economy you know well. Her shoes are caked in mud. There's an alertness about her you recognize from labs and late nights—a scientist braced against surprise. She meets your eyes the moment you approach.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "We followed the protocol," she says before you can introduce the grievance. "We documented anomalies. The data are clean."
You: "The data are clean," you echo, tasting the sterile ring of the phrase. "But the site failed where it crossed into neighborhoods that weren't part of the study. The seams—"
Dr. Lian Zhou: (She nods once.) "We designed for internal validity, Mira. We couldn't control for spread beyond our parcels without changing the experimental conditions into an intervention. That would have skewed the metrics."
You: "People live beyond parcels, Lian. People don't fit inside a control group."

Her jaw tightens. You remember how she pushed for rigor because she has carried loss into policy rooms—her hands have cataloged what grief looks like on a map.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "I know how this reads. I know what you expected of us and of me. But if we had gone in with broader scaffolding without proof, we would have lost the argument at the council. We would have handed certainty to those who ask only for spectacle."
You: (You want to ask if the proof was worth the price. The question feels obscene, practical and small as a pebble.) "What about the families on Harbor Lane? What was the point of proof if the people living there got the proof on their porches?"

Lian looks at you longer than the rest of the town allows. Her eyes, for a heartbeat, are wet with something she didn't calculate.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "Every method has a cost. I tried to minimize harm. I thought if we could deliver models that stood up to criticism, we could scale safely and protect more people later."
You: "Later is a schedule the tide does not respect."

Her hand trembles a fraction as she scrolls through a time series. The numbers are tidy; the present is not.

[Scene: Jonah’s Temporary Shelter by the Old Dock | Afternoon]
[Visual: A tarpaulin under which Jonah sits cross-legged on a overturned crate; his oilskin coat draped over a rail like a flag. His Polaroid camera is silent at his side.]

You find Jonah working with a rope, hands moving the way they always have—practical, immediate. But his face is different, a shuttered thing you don't have the key for. He doesn't look up when you arrive; he ties another knot and tests it, as if counting tasks will stitch something whole.

You: "Jonah."
Jonah Reyes: (He looks at you then, the familiarity a careful leaf in wind.) "Hey." (His voice is smaller than you expect.) "You okay?"
You: "I'm… here. I wanted to see what—" (You stop, the rest collapsing inward.) "I wanted to know if there was anything more we could have done. If we moved faster, if—"

Jonah lets the rope slack. He sets it on his lap and runs a hand through his hair, salt and dust mixing in the curl.

Jonah Reyes: "You did the thing you thought would help. So did I. We all did something." (He looks at the ruin across the harbor.) "People told me to hold. I held as much as I could. Some things—you can't hold against a new pattern of water."
You: (An ache builds behind your ribs, heavy and present.) "Do you think the science was worth it?"

He stares at the rope in his hands like it's a small, honest oracle.

Jonah Reyes: (He makes a sound that could be a laugh or a sob. It isn't a clear answer.) "I don't know." (His jaw sets.) "I don't know, Mira. I keep thinking of Bento's stories—how storms used to come and leave us different, but whole. This feels—" (He searches.) "This feels like something else. Not whole."

Silence presses between you, not uncomfortable but dense with things you each refuse to step on. Jonah's eyes—usually warm and laughing—are shuttered with grief. He refuses to bless the sentence with certainty either way.

You: "It should have been both—good science and broader protection. We tried to make one prove the other."
Jonah Reyes: "And the tide did the proving on its own."

His hands find yours for a beat, a small, accidental bridge. You let the contact sit. It is neither forgiveness nor condemnation; it is simply human heat in a place that smells of rot and salt.

[Scene: Greenhouse Collective | Late Afternoon]

Elena crouches between seed beds, counting stems with a brow furrowed so deep it looks like the sea carved it. She pops up when she hears your footsteps, hands streaked with green.

Elena: "Half of the seedlings washed away. We had trays under the east eaves—gone." (Her voice is flat where usually it is bright.) "We were supposed to send those to families for new plots. Kai was supposed to plant them this weekend."

You kneel beside her, your palms remembering how to push open seedlings gently so roots aren't shocked. The soil smells of something stubborn—compost and rain.

You: "I should have pushed for emergency plantings along the exposed seams while you sorted the samples. I—" (You can't finish; the sentence turns to gravel.)
Elena: "We all had roles." (She looks at you sharply, not unkindly.) "We trusted the pilot would give us a way forward. You told us to wait so the data wouldn't be contaminated. I believed you."

The tremor in her voice is the shape of a covenant breaking. You close your hands into fists against your knees.

You: "What do we tell the families who come asking why their porches are flooded and their garden beds gone?"
Elena: (Her eyes are bright with a tired fury.) "We tell them the truth. We tell them we will help salvage what we can. We tell them we didn't imagine the tide would find seams as a weapon."

You both fall silent. Outside, a child runs past the greenhouse and the sound is a thin, defiant hope. It feels too small; it feels necessary.

Back in your notebook, the lists are neat, methodical. "What we did right." "What we did wrong." You write the same thing twice until the sentences merge and the ink puddles. You write names of families, the coordinates of failed seams, the dates and times of surveys. The pen scratches hard.

You remember the meetings—the exchange of glances, Jonah's stubborn set, Asha's cold efficiency, the mayor's tired hope—and feel the pattern of how decisions ripple outward. You remember why you chose rigor: to make an evidence base that couldn't be ignored. You remember the weeks of debates and the quiet, mounting pressure against compromise.

Bento's words come back to you, the soft scold of a man who has watched tides long enough to know their moods. "Don't let the books become a wall." You feel that wall around you now—pages and protocols that kept you from the immediate scaffolding the town needed at the moment the tide decided not to negotiate.

You close the notebook and tuck the Polaroid—smudged, salt-bleached—inside. The photograph shows you and a half dozen volunteers the week the pilot went in; you are laughing in it, foolish and brave. The image feels like evidence of a different truth: that once, you were simpler in your hopes.

You stand. The harbor smells of wet wood and peeled paint and the faint sweet of seedlings gone under. Your compass pendant swings and settles against your chest.

You walk the boardwalk one last time before the bell tolls for night. People move like ghosts in doorways—faces drawn tight, voices quiet, hands busy with salvage and consolation. Jonah is there at the far end, a shadow with a camera, catching things to remember and things he will not say aloud. Lian is packing a tablet into a waterproof case, her movements precise but slower now.

You have lists and data, and you have mud on your boots and grief in your mouth. There is no single point of failure to lay blame upon, only a braided rope of choices that frayed where the sea found purchase. You cannot unmake the tide. You cannot unlearn the face of the neighbor whose porch sagged.

You think of all the possible endings you rehearsed in the lab and on the boardwalk—the sea-gate that would have been loud and immediate, the hybrid that would have split gnawing costs, the small claims of community projects that rise like patient scaffolding. None of those rehearsals can knit together the quiet ache you feel now.

You breathe in. The salt air fits itself into the hollows of your lungs like a question. You put the notebook back into your jacket, close your fingers over the compass pendant, and let the harbor's low murmur keep time with your heartbeat.

You step away from the water and toward the town's clustered roofs. The losses are counted differently now: not in test results, but in porches, seedlings, and the small soft things community keeps in common. You will make reports, and you will hold meetings, and you will argue new designs until your throat is raw. You will keep the lists. But the lists no longer feel like armor; they feel like confession.

You do not know if the science will be vindicated in time to matter. You do not know if the people you love will forgive the kind of temporal calculus that asked them to wait for proof. You only know the present: the quiet, patient fall of a town that has been given no spectacular moment to be saved by, only the slow, inexorable rearrangement of lives.
