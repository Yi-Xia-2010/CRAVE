[Scene: Greenhouse Collective | Morning]
[Visual: Mottled sunlight through corrugated glass; hanging planters drape shadows across wooden tables]
[Sound: Water trickling, a chorus of muted conversations, the rustle of seed packets]
[Music: Light piano; optimistic plucks]

The Greenhouse Collective smells like soil and soap and the faint, hopeful tang of seedlings. Kai greets you with a grin that is more relief than surprise—he's been running volunteer lists since before dawn. Evelyn arrives with a thermos and a stack of printed summaries; her campaign scarf is now a practical knot around her waist, sleeves rolled. Bento's hands are already stained with peat; he moves with a deliberate pace that suggests patience and memory.

Kai: "We've labeled trays—paired numbering. One set for the berm-adjacent marsh, one for the living shoreline. Seeds split, volunteers scheduled. You okay with the sequencing?"

You: "Yes. Stagger the sowings so we can compare spring emergence. And tag everything—who planted, when. Metadata matters."

Kai: (laughing) "Since when did you love metadata as much as me?"

You: "Since I learned people will trust data they can name."

Evelyn: "If we keep the dashboard public, everyone can see the hands behind the numbers. Transparency will cool some fires before they start."

Bento: (quietly) "And make sure there's a way for families to flag something if they see harm. My people remember what the marsh used to be. They will know when it's wrong."

The banter smooths a corner of the anxiety you carried. Hands work: soil being fluffed, trays being labeled, fingers forming tiny hollows for seeds. Your notebook fills with measurements and commitments. Dr. Lian arrives mid-morning, her coat flecked with salt despite being at the greenhouse—science follows the tide as much as any boat.

Dr. Lian: "If we set paired plots, we need matched baselines: sediment composition, elevation, tidal inundation intervals. I'll run a model for two seasons and feed it into the dashboard. We can use acoustic sensors for biotic return and simple staff gauges for water levels."

You: "And community observers? Volunteers can log sightings—gull behavior, plant emergence—into the same system."

Dr. Lian: (nodding) "Exactly. The mixed-methods approach will make the evidence legible to the council and to the people whose lives will hinge on it."

[Scene: Paired Pilot Plots | Late Morning]
[Visual: Two adjacent sites split by a narrow dinghy path—one outlined for an engineered berm, the other a swath of marsh slated for living shoreline restoration]
[Music: Rising strings; a sense of constructive momentum]
[Sound: Hammers, distant drill whine, the soft pop of earth settling]

You stand between them with Dr. Lian and the sea at your back. On the engineered side, a contractor's temporary flags mark compaction tests and geotextile plans. On the living shoreline side, bundles of marsh grass and willow stakes wait like promises curled into soil. Volunteers ferry tools; a contractor talks quietly with Jonah through a folded plan.

Asha arrives with her tablet balanced like an argument. She wears the same exacting expression you've seen before, but now there's something softer around the edges—tiredness and a hint of relief.

Asha: "I still think the larger system needs a firm backbone. But a time-limited, monitored trial will show us what scales and where."

You: "We write the parameters―what success looks like, when we call for adjustments, and how data triggers action. Public dashboard, regular community briefings, and an independent review mid-term."

Asha: (after a pause) "Independent review, yes. If the berm fails to reduce overtopping within X years, we revisit. If the living shoreline shows unexpected erosion, we pause and adapt."

You: "And community veto if cultural or economic harms emerge. Local hiring clauses for the builds, priority for residents."

Bento: (firm) "No decisions about our places without our voices. Veto is not obstruction—it's care."

Jonah approaches, breathless from hauling stakes. His hands are raw, nails dark with mud. He holds a roll of old twine Jonah-style, and his face lightens when he sees you.

Jonah: "You really want to make everyone happy, huh? You broker a trial and suddenly you're the town's mediator."

You: (smiling) "I'm trying to make everyone safe and keep the harbor from becoming a memory. Is that so wrong?"

Jonah: (softly) "No. Just—don't hollow yourself out to do it."

You: "I won't. I have you to make food when I forget to eat."

Jonah: (grinning) "That's my primary function."

A contractor sidles up, a sea-gate subcontractor whose company had been pushing for a larger project downtown. He has been willing—reluctantly—to alter his berm profile slightly, integrating vegetated steps and permeable elements that let some tidal flow through, a compromise between rock and root.

Contractor: "If we add notches and allow restricted passage, we can slow the tide without creating a hard scar. It'll cost time, but it might buy resilience."

Dr. Lian: "We'll monitor velocity and sediment deposition at the notches. If it helps trap sediment and promotes marsh accretion, that will be a win."

You: "Then we do it. But we document every change and why."

Volunteers and contractors operate in staggered teams so the town never feels emptied or overwhelmed—mornings heavy with hauling, afternoons for community dialogue, evenings for data entry and shared meals. You keep running from site to greenhouse to the small tool shed, your notes swelling with field markers, measurement logs, and apologies that feel both necessary and insufficient.

Kai: "There are people at the boardwalk asking when we'll start planting at the old dock. They're watching for signs it's safe to come back."

You: "Good. Let them see change. Trust grows with visible labor."
