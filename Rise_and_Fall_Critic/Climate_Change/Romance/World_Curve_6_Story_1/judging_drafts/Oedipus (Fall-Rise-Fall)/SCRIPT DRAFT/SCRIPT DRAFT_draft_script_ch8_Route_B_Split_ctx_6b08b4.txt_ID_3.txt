[Scene: Tidehaven Town Hall | Early Evening]
[Visual: Town Hall exterior under a low, salt-smeared sky; banners flutter—some protest handmade, some official]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a bell toll muted by fog; murmuring crowds]
[Music: Warm, hopeful strings under a steady piano motif]

You arrive with the tablet tucked under your arm and the compass pendant warm against your throat—Asha's GIS overlays still ghosting under your skin. The foyer smells of old varnish and coffee gone lukewarm; a line of folding chairs has been pulled into a makeshift holding area, people folding petitions into palms like small boats.

Evelyn Sato is at the entrance, shoulders set but hands trembling a fraction as she folds a council packet closed. Her face lights when she sees you—not relief, exactly, but the kind of steady gratitude a town gives someone who has shown up.

Evelyn Sato: "Mira. Thank you for coming early. This room will—" (she pauses, forces a smile) "—it will hold a lot tonight."

You nod. Her words land like a plank in a harbor you can climb onto. Your voice is small until you shape it.

You: "I'll make it count. Lian's projections are on my laptop; Bento's on standby. Jonah—"

The mention of Jonah pulls the air taut. Outside, the town square hums with people who have brought stakes, banners, and Polaroids. You can feel the scene stretching like a tideline between two shores.

[Visual: Cut to Boardwalk across from the town hall; protest banners hang between lamp posts; glow from phone screens like small lighthouses]
[Sound: Footsteps on wooden boards; soft chanting; the rustle of paper]
[Music: Low, steady percussion joins the strings—heartbeat tempo]

Jonah stands beneath a banner of faded blue saying SAVE THE DOCKS. He is threading a thin rope through a row of stakes, Polaroids clipped like ghostly flags—images of a dock that used to be, of nets, of Bento's hands repairing a hull. Up close, his amber eyes are raw with worry but steadied by the familiar muscle memory of organizing.

He catches your eye across the boardwalk and lifts his hand in a small, private semaphore—half-wave, half-question. The crowd parts when he moves; his presence fills the space like a familiar tide.

Jonah Reyes: "They're going to listen tonight because they have to. But they aren't the ones who fish before dawn. They don't know the feel of a good tide." (he laughs, humor brittle) "You think they can be made to see it?"

You feel the question like a weight balanced on a scale. Part of you wants to say yes because you want him to believe you. Part of you remembers the lab's sterile metrics—lines on a map that can be read as salvation or as a map of loss.

You: "We can make them see more than numbers." (you tap the tablet reflexively) "We'll show them what happens to the back-bay salinity. We'll show how berms look, and where marshes might take root afterward. Asha's plan buys time—Lian's data is cautious, but it buys time. Bento's voice buys trust."

Jonah's jaw sets. He leans closer, lowering his voice until it's private between you and him, the harbor's damp air stringing between words.

Jonah Reyes: "Then say it like that. Say it like it's not just models. Say it's people. And if they try to cut the marshes out, I'll—" (he pauses, searching for the phrase, for the promise) "—I'll stand where you point me."

You register the steadiness and the plea. His hand brushes the rope as if grounding himself. You can feel the warmth there—a human anchor amid strategy and steel.

[Scene: Town Hall — Council Chamber | Moments Later]
[Visual: Amber lamps, wooden benches, maps tacked to cork boards; acoustic panels catching the echoes of a city used to public argument]
[Sound: Chairs scrape, a gavel cough; the murmur subsides into a hush]
[Music: Strings swell gently—hopeful undercurrent]

Evelyn takes the gavel with an exhale that looks like she is letting the room know she is both machine and mother of this meeting.

Evelyn Sato: "We will call the council meeting to order. Tonight we decide not only infrastructure but what Tidehaven will feel like to wake up in. We'll hear from Dr. Zhou, then the planner, then public comments."

Your name comes last on that sequence—'planner'—but the weight is the same. As you step to the lectern, the hall smells of paper and salt; someone nearby is wearing Jonah's scarf and the scent skews toward peat and old rope. You set the tablet down, PIN it open to the GIS overlays that Asha and Lian arranged together: teal arcs of the gate, ochre patches of retreat zones, soft green for the marsh phases you insisted remain in the plan.

Dr. Lian Zhou rises from the front row. She looks calm, sleeves rolled, boots still carrying a smear of marsh sediment.

Dr. Lian Zhou: "The sea-gate offers modeled protection for several decades under current emissions scenarios. However—" (she taps a graph, the projector flicks an overlay showing salinity curves) "—salinity shifts in the back bay will accelerate, altering eelgrass zones and nursery habitats unless we pair the gate with marsh buffers and active monitoring. The gate is not a standalone solution."

Her voice is measured but not cold. You can tell she is thinking in timelines—decades—but also in species and in the quiet economies that depend on small fish populations.

You step forward, heart thudding like a tide drum. Your presentation is not a lecture; it is an invitation.

You: "The sea-gate buys time. Lian's models show that. But time without caretakers will change what we are trying to protect." (you glance at the audience—faces weathered, hopeful, tired) "A phased implementation, with enforced retreat in the most critical zones, paired with funded community pilots for marsh restoration, gives us a better chance of preserving both lives and livelihoods."

Asha Verma sits like a pillar, her slate-gray coat a silhouette of resolve. She slides her glasses down and meets your eyes. Her voice when she answers is as precise as a seal being set.

Asha Verma: "That's close to the packet. I will accept phased marsh integration so long as the gate has firm funding and a legal framework for managed retreat where we cannot guarantee safety."

You: "And I will ensure community monitoring is written into the contract. No one implements the gate and walks away."

Asha's jaw relaxes a hair. It is a small concession. You feel hope lift—practical, earned.

But the room is not unfractured. From the back, Bento's voice rises in a tone that makes you feel the room tilt toward story and memory.

Bento Morais: "We have always adapted. We plant, we mend nets, we pass down ways to read the sea. But when you tell us to leave our houses, you are asking us to bury pieces of ourselves. Make room for that in your plans."

There is a long, heavy silence—then a ripple of assent from the rows where older hands fold petition paper like prayers.

You open the floor to public comment. Kai Tan stands up, blue hair glinting under lamplight, seed packets in one hand, a rallying cry in his throat.

Kai Tan: "We want pilots to run now. Fund community crews. Let science and lived experience grow together. Don't let the gate be an excuse to cut locals out."

His words land and find purchase—young, insistent, dangerous if ignored.

You switch the slide to a scenario where the gate is paired with marsh pilots and a monitoring mandate. The projection shows not only risk lines but growth lines—cordgrass rooting, eelgrass stabilizing, fish nursery indicators recovering over years. It is not a miracle; it is work, slow and stubborn and communal.

You: "We are not choosing immediacy over people. We are choosing how to divide labor—engineers build the gate; the town restores the living shorelines; scientists and community stewards monitor the changes. This is how we turn time bought into time spent well."

Asha nods almost imperceptibly, as if acknowledging that someone else thought to translate urgency into accountability. Evelyn's fingers tap the wood of the gavel, thinking of votes and of headlines and of families on the line.

From the gallery, Jonah catches your eye again. He mouths, "Will you?" His question is an ask for permission and for partnership.

You feel the room compress into a single hinge point: urgency versus inclusion, speed versus trust, protection versus permanence. Every option bleeds into the other. Lian's models nod in three directions. Bento's stories add weight. Kai's chants hum like a promise.

You test the phrasing you will put to the vote, taste it like salt on your tongue.

You (to Evelyn, softly): "We can push for decisive funding for the gate and enforcement where necessary. We can insist on parallel community pilots tied to strict monitoring. Or we can pause to broker a phased approach with more independent study—buying time to design better integration."

Evelyn exhales, not in defeat but in the kind of tired clarity that precedes action.

Evelyn Sato: "Council, we need a recommendation."

The hall leans forward. The bell outside the hall, carried on fog, sounds like a small, hopeful knell.

Your mouth is dry. Your hand finds the compass pendant beneath your collar and rubs it as if to center. In your mind you see Jonah standing at the rail, the Polaroids fluttering like small flags against a long fight. You see Bento's hands and Lian's graphs and Asha's steady insistence on life-saving speed. The choice is not clean; it is necessary.

Your voice steadies because it must. Each word you will speak is a bridge: between science and memory, between urgency and patience.

[Visual: Tight on your face, the warm lamplight haloing the edge of your cheek; the rest of the room recedes into a soft-focused audience]
[Music: A hopeful swell that suggests doors opening, not closing]

You step back from the lectern and present the recommendation as a clear set of options for the council to consider. You do not choose for them; you hand over the compass.
