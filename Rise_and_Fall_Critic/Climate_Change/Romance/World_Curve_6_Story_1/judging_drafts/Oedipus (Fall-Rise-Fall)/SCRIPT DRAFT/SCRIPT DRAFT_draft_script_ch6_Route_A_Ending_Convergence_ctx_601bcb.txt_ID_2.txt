You stand on the sodden boardwalk, the compass at your throat a cold, familiar weight. The storm you kept watching in blue-on-black model runs has arrived with a black mouth—an appetite that does not care for intentions or petitions. Salt lashes your eyelids. The harbor is a roiling bruise under a sky the color of old concrete.

When the surge hits, it announces itself at once: a sudden, climbing voice of water pushing past its ordinary bounds. The coir logs—our soft, homemade armor—take the first blow where the current finds a seam. In some stretches they hold, bulging like tired bellies; elsewhere they tear, unspooling like bad stitches. Seedlings lift and flail, mats come loose, and the neat lines you drew in notebooks smear into the sea's messy handwriting.

You taste salt and something thicker—ash on the air where an old dock-side heater caught and smoked, or the memory of things burning. Your palms are slick on the rail, the knitted wool of your beanie soaked through. Jonah's hand finds yours before you think to reach for him; his fingers are hard and warm, but there is a tremor under the grip that maps to everything he wants to say and cannot fit into a word.

Jonah Reyes: "We did what we could. We planted every weekend—God, Mira, we worked until our knees gave out."

You want to say yes, and then explain, and then make it all right. Instead you watch a neighbor—Mrs. Leto's low yard down by the back-bay—vanish into a moving sheet of brown water as a surge eddies through the channel. The coir there wasn't enough; it never could be enough for that angle of the tide.

You imagine the two possible pasts branching somewhere behind you—whether we kept it small or brought bigger pieces in under our control—and how each decision would have turned the water a different way. The models promised different winners; the ocean accepted neither promise whole.

Jonah's voice comes again, quieter, a rasp under the wind: "Why didn't we—" He stops because the rest of the sentence would make blame a hammer. He looks at you, amber eyes flooded, a fault line you ache to cross.

You: "Because we couldn't promise the sea would respect our plans. Because we chose what we thought would keep people together and sometimes that meant taking smaller steps. Because—" your voice fails you; you pull a ragged breath in and let the rain fill the space where explanation should live.

Bento Morais stands a few yards away, wrapped in a coat that has more years in its stitches than some of the town's houses. He moves like someone who has catalogued loss a dozen times and, in that inventory, learned to carry the weight without collapsing. His hands are steady as always, but there is salt on his lower lids too.

Bento Morais: (soft, measured) "We saved a channel. We lost a back-bay. We did not lose everything. But 'not everything' is small consolation when a porch is gone."

You listen to him and feel the sentence press into your ribs. His grief is quiet, but it fills the open warehouse hollows where you had stacked tools and songs. The shipyard smells of wet wood and old rope and the iron tang of spilled saltwater.

The town rallies. That is what people do: they push at what is broken with bare hands until something gives. Some hands you recognize—Kai hauling sacks, Dr. Lian in a rainproof hood checking soil disruption—with their faces lit by the small, stubborn light that always returns after damage. There is a municipal truck that moves through the streets like a slow priest, horns low, calling out aid locations. But alongside the practical coordination, a narrative is already gathering teeth.

Jonah pulls his hand back like a scoring wave pushed against shore and speaks again, voice a saw: "We promised them safety. You presented plans, you rallied the crews—people trusted your maps, Mira. How were we supposed to know the surge would take the back-bay like that?"

You hear accusation wrapped in exhaustion. There's truth in it. There is also the fact that Jonah and so many others trusted you because you made plans, because you offered a path other than surrender. Trust is a brittle thing when saltwater is involved.

You: "I know. I know. I thought—" Your mantra of all these months cracks. "I thought local knowledge and careful work might be enough to buy time. I didn't want to make a decision that would force people out of their homes."

Jonah: (bitter laugh) "Buying time sometimes looks like delaying the tide."

He doesn't mean to be cruel. He is a man worn thin by nets that return emptier each season, by traditions that waking storms keep erasing. You could hold him and say you'll do better, but words will not stitch what the water pulled.

Bento steps between you, not placating, but offering a kind of slow gravity.

Bento Morais: "Finger-pointing helps no one tonight. We are left with what is left. We will make lists. We will get people dry. We will bury what needs burying, and sing what needs singing." He pauses, then, quieter: "Mira, you are one of us. That counts for something, even when the ledger looks cruel."

There are faces in the crowd—some look to you with fierce gratitude, others with something rawer: a blame that needs an anchor. Children wrapped in borrowed blankets look up at you as if you might conjure a safe shore out of your pockets. You feel each look like a ledger line on your skin.

The smell of peat and smoke grows stronger. Somewhere in the direction of the old kiln, something has burned through a soaked tarp and the acrid cloud adds a choking edge to the air. You cough. Your compass pendant bangs against the collar of your jacket; your mother's brass face is a small, stubborn compass in the weather.

You crouch at the edge of the shipyard where the water has claimed the lower rungs of the scaffolding. A Polaroid—soft-white border sticky with salt—lies half-buried in the muddy grit. You pick it up. The photograph is of seedlings, of hands, of Jonah smiling with dirt under his nails. That picture proved something once: that people had tried. Now it tastes like an accusation and a benediction at the same time.

Jonah: (near a whisper) "Do you regret it?"

The question lands like a stone dropped from high. You are trained to weigh variables: wave energy, fetch, sediment deposition. You are trained to speak in probabilities. Regret lodges somewhere deeper and refuses models.

You: (bare) "I regret that the water always asks more than we can give. I regret that my choices have consequences for people I love."

Jonah looks at you, then away, jaw clenched. The salt on his cheek is not rain.

Bento: (firm, gentle) "Regret is a poor thing to carry alone, Mira. Let us make a ledger of what we will do tomorrow. Tonight, hold on to that Polaroid. It will help you remember why you tried."

You let him speak, because his words keep a thin heat in your chest. You think of the faces—of Mrs. Leto at her vanished yard, of the child who would have planted seeds with tiny fists, of the rows of marsh that held in some places and failed in others. The town has been pared down; the map of Tidehaven is smaller in practice than it was on paper. Loss has become a new kind of coastline.

Night pulls in, and the town sets up makeshift centers in the community hall and the greenhouse, the latter smelling of damp soil and the faint, hopeful green of living things that remain. People speak in low, urgent threads—plans for shoring up the remaining stretches, lists for rebuilding porches, a rotating schedule for night watches. You sit on a crate by a dim lamp, compass hanging damp against your collar, and let the town speak.

You think about what you left and what you returned to. You think about whether absence made you wiser or less culpable. The answers do not arrive cleanly. They are silted with feeling, with data, with neighbors' names scrawled in ink that will never be as solid as a seawall.

Jonah comes and sits beside you. For a moment the two of you look like an old photograph—two silhouettes against the harbor's hard light. He does not reach for your hand. He doesn't need to. The space between you is full of what has been taken and what remains to be mended.

Jonah Reyes: (soft) "We can rebuild some things. Not all. Maybe the important ones."

You: (a long breath) "Maybe. Maybe home changes shape and we learn to call the new shape home."

He looks at you with a fatigue that is almost tenderness, and for a heartbeat you consider the possibility that grief could be a bridge instead of a canyon. Then he turns his face away, and you know that bridges are built plank by plank and often need the sea to be less hungry.

Bento stands, gathers his coat, and looks at you both with the tiredness of someone who has watched more tides than most.

Bento Morais: "We will plant again when the weather allows. We will teach the children to plant better lines, to set coir where it matters, and to read the tides like Scripture. We will argue at council meetings and have the same fights over and over. That is how a living place learns."

You nod because his words make a kind of map for the coming months. There will be grant applications and hand budgets and quiet, stubborn labor. There will be stories told around heaters about the night the sea learned one more secret about the town. There will also be absences—empty porches, a smaller harbor list, names shaved off a roster.

A thin, persistent ache settles behind your ribs; not all of this will be repairable. The town is smaller now, curated by what the sea would permit. You press your thumb to the compass, feeling the brass warm with your skin. Its needle wobbles and sets itself; no map can make grief tidy.

You stand, voice steadier than you feel.

You: "Tomorrow we list. We coordinate shelters. We salvage what we can. And—" you turn to Jonah, then to Bento, then to the ring of faces that have gathered—"—we remember."

Jonah and Bento exchange a look that is both resignation and agreement. It is enough for tonight. It will not be everything; that remains a fact like tidal height: verifiable and unpitying.

You tuck the Polaroid into your notebook, but there is a part of you that will always feel the sea's line on your skin. You will keep working. You will keep measuring. You will keep collecting stories and data and songs. You will accept that saving some means losing others—that the architecture of care is always imperfect and that grief will sit with the living like an extra passenger.

Bento lays a hand on your shoulder, neither absolving nor accusing.

Bento Morais: "Tidehaven will change. We will be here to greet the new shape, even if it hurts to do so."

You close your eyes for a moment and let the sound of the harbor—wet, patient, and exact—be the drum to which you set your next steps. The pendant at your throat is heavy. It will not bring everyone back. It will, perhaps, remind you every morning why you chose to stay.
