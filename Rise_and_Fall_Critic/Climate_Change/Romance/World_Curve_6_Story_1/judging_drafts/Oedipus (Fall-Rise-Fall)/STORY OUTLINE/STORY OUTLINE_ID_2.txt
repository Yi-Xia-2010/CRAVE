## Visual Novel Title
Tidehaven: Rising Addresses

---

## World View
Tidehaven is a near-future coastal town in 2042, a place where the sea is both livelihood and threat. Low limestone cliffs, tidal marshes, and a ransacked harbor form a landscape of salt-gray buildings, rust-flecked iron railings, and seaweed-slick docks. The town is a patchwork: solar canopies shimmer near derelict warehouses, living-shoreline pilots sprout reeds beside concrete pilings, and community greenhouses push saplings through corrugated glass. Politically, Tidehaven is a contested space between municipal technocrats pushing scalable infrastructure and locals advocating slow, culturally-rooted resilience. Mayor Evelyn Sato brokers funding, while Dr. Lian Zhou supplies models and GIS overlays for risk timelines. Bento Morais keeps oral histories alive; Kai Tan mobilizes seedlings and protests. The tension between top-down emergency measures (sea walls, managed retreat zones) and grassroots restoration (marsh-building, fishing co-ops) drives social conflict: livelihoods, identity, and memory are at stake. Religion and ritual are quiet—memorial gatherings at the boardwalk, blessings before launches—while class lines show in whose homes can afford elevation work and whose boats sit abandoned. The tone is melancholic but alive: fog-washed mornings, salt-scratch air, and community projects that feel like fragile vows against an encroaching sea.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mira Kestrel) return to Tidehaven as a resilience planner carrying survivor’s guilt. A sudden policy window and a grant draw Asha Verma—a decisive municipal planner—and Jonah Reyes—a community-rooted restorer—into a fraught love triangle. The story follows an Oedipus emotional arc (Fall → Rise → Fall): I fall into the crisis (personal guilt, political pressure, and rising tides), find temporary solutions that stitch science and tradition together (a brief recovery where relationships and projects flourish), but ultimately those fixes trigger larger, unavoidable trade-offs leading to loss or transformation. Branching choices let players steer which alliances I deepen—Jonah’s lived restoration, Asha’s decisive infrastructure, or a hybrid that tries to reconcile both. Outcomes vary: a warm preservation of community and love (Happy), a necessary but painful managed retreat (Bittersweet), systemic failure and loss (Sad), or a complicated “True” ending where hybrid policy is achieved but through sacrifice. Across all branches I narrate in first person, grounding technical decisions in intimacy: tidal charts become memory, marsh plantings are a form of apology, and every policy is a confession.

### Chapter 1: Homecoming Fog [Fall] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)  
* **Summary:** I return to Tidehaven after years away. The harbor is smaller, scarred—docks sag, neon from shuttered shops flickers through mist. My chest is tight with guilt over my father's lost boat and the reasons I left. Old faces—Evelyn’s warm bob; Bento’s soot-marked cap—feel like anchors and accusations. I carry my weatherproof notebook and the brass compass pendant. I walk the boardwalk in a scuffed olive field jacket and knee-high rubber boots and meet Jonah briefly: his amber-brown eyes are the same, his sweater patched, hands still callused. I feel both needed and out of place as Kai runs past with a seed packet. The Saltmarsh Research Lab looms where I used to play—glass panes streaked with salt, sediment cores like bones. There’s a grant notice taped to Town Hall and Asha Verma’s slate-gray coat is already at work in council rumors; Dr. Lian's GIS overlays hum in my head. The town is a pressure cooker; the tide is rising physically and inside me. I choose how to reinsert myself: quietly gather data, or try to stitch the town back together with my hands and heart.  
* **Visuals:** Boardwalk fog; my sea-green tipped hair in a loose knot; compass pendant; Jonah’s oilskin coat; Bento’s patched fisher’s coat.  
* **Valence:** Negative (guilt, melancholy)  
* **Arousal:** Medium

* **Next:** Chapter 2

[When I step off the bus, the fog seems to expect an apology; the harbor smells of iron and damp rope. I feel every plank under my rubber boots as if the town might tilt beneath me. I keep my head down because returning feels like being examined—not by a court, but by every salt-streaked window. Jonah appears under a sagging sign: the same crooked grin and sun-bleached hair. We trade a small, careful conversation; he mentions small restoration jobs and how Bento keeps the old launch schedule. Inside, Town Hall is a map room and a stage: Evelyn’s campaign scarf pinned to the board by a tack—an invitation and a threat. I open my notebook—tide charts, initials of projects I left unfinished—telling myself this is work, not penance. Yet the brass compass at my throat is warm with memory. The sea keeps whispering names; I retell myself the facts: models predict gradual inundation, grant windows require a pilot demonstration within months. I tell Jonah I’ll listen first. He watches me with those amber-brown eyes that ask more than words; for a second I can’t tell if I’ve come home to safety or to accountability.]
