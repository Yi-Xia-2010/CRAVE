## Visual Novel Title
Salt & Bearings

---

## World View
Tidehaven, 2042: a coastal town pinned between limestone cliffs and a harbor that remembers storms. The politics are local and practical—an uneasy town council balancing grants, developers, and the stubborn will of people who have lived by the water for generations. Culture centers on repair and ritual: communal boat mending, seed-saving in the Greenhouse Collective, and memorials at the boardwalk for lost boats and loved ones. Religion is diffuse—folk rituals, salt-blessed rites from Bento’s memories, secular town meetings that become confessional spaces. Class lines run between those who can afford elevated homes and solar canopies and the fishers who still tether boats to decaying pilings. Conflicts revolve around scale: Asha’s technocratic urgency (regional defenses, managed retreat zones, enforceable ordinances) versus Jonah’s communal, craft-based practices (small restoration projects, living shorelines). Geography is close and tactile: fog-laced boardwalks, algae-streaked warehouses, a research boathouse greenhouse and wind-burnished cliffs. The mood is melancholic, hands-on resilience—the town is neither resigned nor triumphant, always negotiating small wins against the rising sea.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Mira Kestrel: a Tidehaven-born coastal resilience planner whose return to my hometown sets the story in motion. The narrative follows an Oedipus curve—Fall → Rise → Fall—across multiple branching routes. We begin with successive collapses: a stalled council vote, fractured community trust, and the guilt of my absence when my father's boat was taken. From that low point, I pursue a temporary recovery: grassroots marsh restoration, pilot living shorelines, and a fragile collaboration with Jonah and Dr. Lian that brings hope to Tidehaven. Those successes, however, are pyrrhic. Political pressure, Asha’s regional-scale interventions, and a capricious storm reveal that our compromises traded cultural continuity for safety or safety for cultural loss. Branching choices determine which alliances I forge—Jonah’s tradition-centered projects, Asha’s hard infrastructure, or a hybrid approach—each offering a temporary victory but ultimately feeding the final fall. Endings range from bittersweet preservation and managed retreat to hard loss and a fragile "true" outcome where systemic change begins, but only after severe cost. Throughout, relationships—romantic, communal, political—mirror adaptation strategies: trust, sacrifice, and the impossible calculus of saving a place and the people who call it home.
