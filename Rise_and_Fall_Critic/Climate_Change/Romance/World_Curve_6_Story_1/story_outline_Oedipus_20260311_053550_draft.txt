## Visual Novel Title
Tidehaven Tides: The Long Low Arc

---

## World View
Tidehaven is a near-future coastal town—2042—squeezed between limestone cliffs and rising salt flats. A ransacked harbor, patchwork solar canopies, and experimental living shorelines stitch the town together. Politically, Tidehaven sits under regional emergency mandates while its small-town council—led by Mayor Evelyn Sato—juggles limited funding, tradition, and outside pressures. Culture favors communal labor: seedling exchanges in the Greenhouse Collective, elders’ sea songs at the Boardwalk, and Bento Morais’s oral histories. Economically, fishing families face dwindling catches, competing developers, and the lure of relocation. Religion and ritual are local—the harbor bell, harvest blessings, and ritual plantings—grounding decisions in memory. The conflict is clear: technocratic, scalable solutions (led by municipal planners like Asha Verma) versus slow, culturally-rooted resilience (embodied by Jonah Reyes and community builds). Geography matters: fog-laced mornings on the boardwalk, algae-green marshes, a salt-sprayed research lab, and the wind-scoured Highland Cliffs provide mood and stakes. The atmosphere is melancholic but alive with quiet resistance: weathered textures, algae-green accents, and the painterly realism of a town learning to hold ground against the sea.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I return to Tidehaven as Mira Kestrel—back from coastal engineering studies—to lead community-centered adaptation. The story begins with a moral fall: the town is close to breaking, relationships tense, and guilt from my absence hangs heavy. A major council rift forces me to choose sides: work with Jonah and craft grassroots living shorelines, ally with Asha’s decisive infrastructural plans, or attempt a hybrid data-driven reconciliation. Each choice launches a short route where we find temporary success: volunteers raise marsh berms; a pilot sea-gate holds; a regional grant arrives. These midsections are moments of rise—Hope, connection with Jonah, scientific validation with Dr. Lian Zhou—but each success carries an omission or compromise (out-of-town funders changing plans, community losses, or technical blind spots). Finally, the fall returns: a violent storm or political reversal precipitates loss—partial inundation, forced retreat, or fractured relationships—fulfilling the Oedipus curve: Fall → Rise → Fall. Across branches, intimacy and climate policy mirror one another: my guilt, Jonah’s loyalty, and Asha’s efficiency are tested, and the endings range from fragile happiness to true-but-costly institutional change to hard loss. The emotional core remains Mira’s attempt to reconcile past absence with present responsibility.
