You are standing where you stood days ago with Jonah, where the salt and the polaroid and the thin warmth of each other's hands felt like proof against the sea. Now the sea has a black mouth. The boards underfoot pitch and tremble; rain rubs at your cheeks like a thousand small accusations. You taste ash in the air—stinging, metallic—mixed with the sharper salt of surge. Light from the boardwalk lamplights cuts through sheets of water and fog in stuttered, tired arcs. In the harbor the rows of newly planted marsh blur into grey smudges, some lines ragged where coir logs hung on; other stretches are gone, stitched open by the storm into ragged wounds of mud and uprooted reed.

Your hands are sticky with peat on one hand and a ripped seed packet on the other. You remember the celebration: laughter under tarps, Bento's half-grin, Jonah saying something crooked and tender about stubborn roots. That memory feels farther than the tide.

Jonah Reyes: "You see it too. God—Mira. We held some of it. But—" (His voice cuts off on the gust. He swallows.) "Look at Marta's yard. The back channels ate it like they were thirsty."

You want to say everything—how many bundles of coir tore, which berms gave way, the exact pattern of the erosion—but you cannot. The Schroedinger rules have already taught your mouth to be careful. You let the image outline itself in more general terms: some things held, some things failed.

Mira Kestrel: "We saved pieces, Jonah. We bought time where it mattered. We—" Your words thin when you try to balance the scale between what you did and what you couldn't. "We couldn't make the sea forget its shape."

Jonah Reyes: (His laugh is a low, broken sound.) "Pieces. It's always pieces with you planners." (He doesn't say it to wound so much as to name the painful geometry of your work.) "People wanted promises, Mira. I wanted promises."

You both stand in the wind like two flags frayed at the same edge—attached to the same pole of grief.

[Scene: Old Shipyard | Dawn — After the Surge]
[Visual: Beams gone dark with sea exposure; overturned wheelbarrows; seed trays floating like pale teeth in puddles]
[Sound: Soft, sullen chatter; someone hammering broken ribs of pallet wood; gulls complaining]

By morning the shipyard is a ledger of what tide remembers. The coir logs that survived look knotted and bruised; the ones that failed are scattered rags and muddy ghosts. A narrow channel has opened where no channel was yesterday, and a dozen small yards—the ones with low fences and laundry lines—are gone or under a gray, oily silt.

Bento Morais is standing at the entrance to the shipyard like some weathered old gate he never expected to close. He wears his cap low; the lines on his face map the town's tide-lines and losses. He takes no theatrics—grief is an old craft for him, practiced and deep.

Bento Morais: "They called it a good winter for it to show itself," he says, voice flat. "Showed us how stubborn we are. Showed us what's stubborn about the tide." (He kneels and touches a half-buried wooden post.) "My father used to mark the shoreline at spring. I thought—" (He stops. The rest of the sentence is a boat bobbing out to sea.)

You kneel to the washed-up roots with him, your knees pressed into the cold, grainy grit. You remember Bento teaching Jonah to splice line; you remember him pressing a small piece of rope into your hand and telling you the town needed plans that honored stories. Now Bento's grief is not his alone; it fills the hollowed decks and makes the workbench a pulpit of shared loss.

Mira Kestrel: "We built what we could," you say, the words brittle like salt glass. "We tried to listen—to the land, to people's needs. It wasn't enough."

Bento Morais looks at you with a calm like a tide pool: still on the surface while currents rumble underneath.

Bento Morais: "It was never enough in the sense of saving everything. That's not the way storms work, child. We pick who we can carry, and we carry them as far as our hands will go." (He lays a hand on your shoulder, and it is neither accusation nor absolution—only weight.) "You carried people. That's real. It's not small."

You imagine the ledger of losses and the narrow column labeled "real." There are rows under it: Marta's yard, the low terrace on Hargreaves, the pier where Jonah used to mend nets. Your chest feels like a ledger too—entries stacked up in neat guilt columns, impossible to reconcile.

Jonah Reyes watches Bento speak, jaw tight. He walks between the scattered debris as if mapping the failures by foot. When he finally looks at you, his amber eyes are a furnace cooled by the deep weather of the town.

Jonah Reyes: "Don't tell me 'it's real' when my aunt has nothing to hang laundry on, Mira." (He says it harshly, and then softer.) "I don't want your comforting math."

You flinch at the sharpness and stifle a reflex to apologize. Words like 'sorry' have been worn thin by repetition. You want to explain the trade-offs—how keeping the project small preserved local control, how accepting the grant could have shifted water differently—but the practical explanations read like excused calculus when someone's home is gone.

Mira Kestrel: "I know. I know what it looks like. I am—" you stop because your throat constricts. "I am here. We are here. We'll rebuild what we can. We'll—"

Jonah Reyes interrupts with a laugh that is almost a sob.

Jonah Reyes: "Rebuild? Every time we patch, the sea writes another page. I'm tired of reading the margins of my father's maps and finding myself in the footnotes."

Bento Morais exhales a long, slow breath. He doesn't try to smooth Jonah's anger; instead, he lets it be a tide that will shape the shore differently.

Bento Morais: "Anger is part of the work sometimes. It tells you what's worth guarding. But when it starts to hollow people, it becomes something else."

The words hang between you like damp flags. You look at each face—a neighbor hauling a sodden child's toy, Kai squinting against rain, an old woman wrapping a blanket tighter—and understand the fracture lines not as blame assigned to a single strategist but as a communal wound needing tending.

As the town gathers, voices form into two kinds of story: the ones that name your name in gratitude and the ones that name your name in accusation. Both are true in their way. People bring donations—old fishing nets, hot soup—and someone leaves a Polaroid of the pre-storm marsh on a crate like an offering. You touch the corner and find the salt has already begun to erode paper and memory.

Evelyn would say to weigh these stories in council. Bento's presence calms the worst edges. Jonah's anger sharpens, and yet he does not leave. He refuses to abandon the harbor even as he contends with how much of it is gone.

Jonah Reyes: (He comes to you later, quieter.) "I don't know how to go on. Not like this." (He looks at you like a man measuring a rope for a knot he's never trusted before.) "I need to know if you still think this—this way of doing things—is the thing to do. Or if you'll stand aside and watch them come in with glossy plans."

You remember the meetings, the pamphlets, the choices: refuse branding; accept NGO with local governance; or push a rigorous smaller pilot. The past converges here as burned stakes.

Mira Kestrel: "I don't have a perfect answer," you say honestly. "I made choices that cost people. I thought—" (Your voice cracks.) "I thought local ownership would keep us honest, keep us engaged. Maybe I misread how strong the tide would be or how fast it would move."

Jonah Reyes's mouth tightens. "And Asha?" he asks, the name like a wedge. "Would she have saved more? Would she have taken the houses we couldn't save?" There's no accusation in the question so much as desperate searching.

You cannot answer the what-ifs without betraying the plural pasts that might be true. Instead, you fold the possibilities into something you can live with.

Mira Kestrel: "Maybe some things would have been different. Maybe not. What I can say is—" (You meet his eyes, bracing for the strike.) "—I stayed. I tried with what I had. I'm not asking anyone to forgive me. I'm asking to be allowed to keep working, to keep learning, and to carry what we lost so it shapes our next choices."

Jonah Reyes's silence is long and complicated. His hands fumble with a frayed rope; he ties an absent knot and then undoes it. Finally, he looks up.

Jonah Reyes: "That's small comfort," he says. "But it's something. I'm angry, Mira. I'm sad. I need you to be here—if for no other reason than to be angry with me when the town is unfair to itself."

You breathe out something that might be acceptance. It is not peace. It is a worn agreement: to keep showing up, even when showing up means carrying blame and tending loss. Bento watches this exchange with a soft hardness in his gaze, and for a moment the three of you—one young, one middle, one old—are the town's spine.

Mira Kestrel [Internal]: "We saved pieces. We lost pieces. Maybe that's how resilience reads—an unlovely arithmetic. But I will keep adding columns. I will keep asking the hard questions. I will stay."

Bento Morais: "We'll plant again in spring. We'll ask better questions, and we'll make our losses count. That's what we owe them—the living, the gone."

Jonah Reyes: "Stay," he says simply. "Stay with me."
