[Scene: Embankment | Morning — overcast, low tide]
[Background:"ch9_3be532_1"]
[Music: Sparse strings, low and steady]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a slow, hollow thud as a piece of board settles in wet sand]

You arrive with salt in your hair and the taste of iron at the back of your tongue. The auditors' report is folded into your pocket like a stone you keep pressing with your thumb. You did something yesterday — a step that stalled a machine of policy and money — and now the consequences are unspooling in front of you.

The embankment is quieter than you expect. Where men and machines once hummed, there are only small, accusing breaks: a half-anchored sandbag, a seam of poured mortar gone soft, streaks of rust where bolts should have held. Someone has set a tarpaulin over a sunk segment; under it you can hear the water breathing shallowly.

Your compass is heavy at your wrist. You feel its old weight as an accusation and as a tether. The tidal line is a gray bruise across the sand; the spot where the structure finally failed is raw and bright, as if the shore has been flayed.

Ivo Calder is already there, sleeves rolled to his elbows, hands coated in a patchwork of mud and concrete dust. He looks up when you step into the hollow between two broken pylons; his face is a map of too many long days — and something tauter under the skin.

Ivo Calder: "You made the right call to slow them, Alys." (He pauses, as if searching for the sentence that will comfort you.) "If we hadn't checked, more people would have been in the houses that were damaged."

Alys Maren: "They still lost roofs, Ivo. Families are moving out of their trailers today. We slowed them — maybe we saved some lives — but we also stopped a wall that people were hoping would be finished."

Ivo Calder leans on the railing, the metal cool beneath his palm. He doesn't offer the easy optimism he sometimes does; instead, his voice narrows.

Ivo Calder: "Hope can't be a glossy ribbon when the tide's hungry. People needed the feel of something finished. You know that."

You let the wind take his words. They don't land cleanly. There is gratitude under the edge — he keeps saying the word 'we' — and something else you can't quite hold onto.

[Scene: Harrow Hall | Afternoon — council hearing]
[Background:"ch9_3be532_2"]
[Sound: Microphone feedback, soft murmurs; a radiator ticking somewhere behind the wood paneling]
[Music: Dissonant piano, sparse]

The chamber smells of varnish and wet wool, of coffee gone cold. The auditors are here: two people in plain suits with a laptop and a stack of neatly stamped pages that look like verdicts. Their findings are clinical, merciless in their clean typography.

A woman from the audit team speaks into the microphone first, voice even.

Audit Lead: "Contract deviations were confirmed. Materials specified in the municipal contract were substituted with alternatives lacking required tensile strength. Several bolts were not installed to spec. We have documentation of oversight lapses during the third and fourth weeks of construction."

The room reacts like a body sensing a fever. Gasps, a rustle of papers, an old man coughing at the back. Celine Harrow's face is a practiced mask — composed, controlled. There is something harder behind her eyes, like the light behind a stormcloud.

Celine Harrow: "This is an unacceptable breach. We will hold those accountable." (She looks at you and then at Ivo with an almost maternal firmness.) "We will also ensure immediate aid for those displaced."

Alys Maren: "The audit confirms what we feared. The substitutions weakened the structure. That weakness — combined with the high tide last night — is what caused the partial failure."

Celine Harrow inclines her head slightly, the motion a practiced balance of condolence and distance.

Celine Harrow: "And yet, Alys, you made calls that halted work and raised public alarm. There are those who will say that delay increased their exposure." (Her voice is smooth; the implication sharpens.) "How do you respond to that?"

You can feel every microphone pickup as if it's prodding at a bruise. You breathe slowly and choose the truth you can own.

Alys Maren: "I pushed for scrutiny because the town deserves structures that meet the specs we've paid for. If that looks like obstruction, then so be it. I'd rather disrupt the illusion of security than sell people a wall that won't hold."

The murmurs spike; someone shouts from the back about jobs and timelines. Ronan stands up, face red, hands clenching his cap like a talisman.

Ronan: "People needed the work to keep the food on the table! You stalled them, Alys!"

Alys Maren: "Ronan, the workers put in hours that were unsafe on top of materials that weren't right. If something goes wrong during a storm — if someone dies because bolts were skipped — does 'work' mean anything then?"

Oba Kofi rises from his seat, his cane tapping the floor like punctuation.

Oba Kofi: "We have lived with the sea longer than any ledger says. To mend is to be honest. Truth is a kind of repair, too, child."

Celine Harrow nods to the auditors.

Celine Harrow: "We will commission a full remediation plan and an independent oversight committee. We will also convene a panel to review contracting processes. Accountability starts now."

The language is formal, but faces in the hall register different translations: for some, 'accountability' reads like vindication; for others, a bureaucratic shuffle that buys time while the tide keeps coming. Maya squeezes your hand under the bench — small, fierce contact — and you let it anchor you.

[Scene: Harbor | Dusk — after the hearings]
[Background:"ch9_3be532_3"]
[Sound: The tide pulling, a child's laugh turned brittle; the distant hum of a generator powering temporary lights]
[Music: Slow cello, low and aching]

The hearing has broken into smaller storms: neighbors whispering accusations, volunteers setting up tarpaulins, a line of council vans rolling by like quiet judge's carriages. The harbor smells of diesel and frying oil, of wet canvas and something that might be hope if you peer hard enough.

You walk the pier alone at first, feet finding the familiar give of planks. The town's losses are catalogued in small gestures: a toddler's shoe pinned against a crate like a forgotten relic; a fishing net draped across a fence, its floats sunk and empty. You touch each thing as if you could map the loss by feeling.

Ivo Calder finds you near a boat with its keel up, the crew laundering gear with surplice care. He doesn't approach; he simply stands a few feet away and lets the silence be a third presence.

Ivo Calder: "You did what you thought was right." (He looks at the water, then back at you.) "You know I believe in protecting people now. Sometimes that means moving faster than my head can catch up to my hands."

Alys Maren: "Sometimes it means stopping a machine that will finish wrong. The two aren't exclusive, but right now it sure feels like they are."

Ivo Calder laughs once, short and incredulous.

Ivo Calder: "Isn't that always the story? You build something to keep the sea out, and the sea becomes the only thing people talk about. Meanwhile the people who needed a paycheck or a fix — they get the impatient version of grief."

Alys Maren: "And the impatient version is loud."

He studies you, hawkish and gentle at once.

Ivo Calder: "You look tired, Alys. You look like someone who slept all night and still woke up in the storm."

You look at him and for a moment you imagine a life where the two of you find a rhythm that holds both repair and restraint. You imagine it like a boat balanced on a long tidal swell. The image makes something ache.

Alys Maren: "I don't know if I'm tired. Maybe I'm just carrying the weight I asked for."

Ivo Calder's face hardens in a way that hurts to watch.

Ivo Calder: "You asked for the weight, sure. But you expect to lift it alone. That was never the bargain, was it?"

There is no simple reconciliation in the space between you. He is both consolation and accuser; you are both shelter and stone. Around you, families pack what they can into trucks. A mother tucks a child's stuffed seal into a box and kisses it as if blessing is something you can fold.

Maya arrives with Oba trailing slower, his cane marking small, steady progress. Maya's hands are ink-stained from forms and list-making; her apron is flecked with sand. She wraps an arm around you and doesn't ask for explanations.

Maya: "We're staying, you know. Some of us are staying. But there are houses that can't be fixed. We already found spots inland where folks can take shelter."

Alys Maren: "How many?"

Maya lowers her eyes, and for a second it's like the harbor drops into a quiet well.

Maya: "Too many. Not enough. Some won't come back."

The confession hangs between the gulls and the generator. Oba clears his throat.

Oba Kofi: "You tried to make the right work, child. The sea is an old teacher. It does not explain itself kindly."

You think of the auditors' pages in your pocket — the neat, factual language that translates in the town to livelihoods and thresholds. You think of the embankment's teeth — broken metal — and of a family you saw leaving with a framed photograph wrapped in newspaper.

Ivo Calder takes a step closer, his voice low.

Ivo Calder: "We saved some. Not everyone. People will look for someone to blame for the hurt people see. They'll look for a face that matches their anger."

You feel the weight of that truth, how public grief turns precise and hungry. You have already felt the shape of it in Harrow Hall. You have already been a face in a crowd of faces that need an answer.

Alys Maren: "I'll take the blame if it means they see what's wrong. If someone has to carry the cost, let it be me that stands and says: we asked questions."

Ivo Calder searches you like a map without clear roads.

Ivo Calder: "I don't know if that's a good plan. I don't know if that's even possible without the town tearing itself apart."

You don't answer. There is nothing left to say that will stitch what was torn.

[Scene: Harbor — Night]
[Background:"ch9_3be532_4"]
[Music: A single sustained note, then silence]
[Sound: A remote radio spitting static; the intermittent splash of a rope easing under tension]

You sit on a crate and open your notebook. Your handwriting is small, a list of names and places to check, people to contact, promises to keep. Under the list you write, in a hand steadier than you feel: "Repair — Rectify — Rebuild — Remember." The words look like a map to somewhere you cannot yet see.

Maya leans her head on your shoulder for a moment and then gets up to help a neighbor with a stubborn tarp. Oba walks off toward the cliffs, cane marking a cadence you can't rival.

Ivo Calder kneels to tie a frayed rope on a boat and for a second the world feels like it's held together by small knots and stubborn hands. He looks up at you once — eyes tired and hazel, and you want to tell him everything will be okay. You don't. The sentence would be a lie as big as the embankment once promised to be.

Ivo Calder: "When this settles — whatever settlement that is — will you still be in Saltwick?"

Alys Maren: (You close the notebook and let the pen rest on the page.) "I am here now. My work is here. My family is here."

Ivo Calder: "And us?"

The word is an opening, raw and dangerous. You taste salt and the metal tang of policy and regret.

Alys Maren: "I don't know what 'us' will be after all of this. Maybe allies. Maybe nothing. I don't know how to keep both the town and what we are from breaking."

Ivo Calder takes in a breath that trembles.

Ivo Calder: "I thought the work could hold us together. Maybe I thought that because work is where I find meaning. Maybe I wanted an easier way out of saying I love you."

You give him neither assent nor denial. You don't owe him that clarity, not tonight. The town has become a place where answers are weapons and where silence is armor.

Around you, people are deciding in small ways — whether to stay in makeshift shelters, whether to take a job across town, whether to kiss a neighbor goodnight and say nothing of the future. The harbor's lights reflect like bruises on the water; the tide is a constant reminder that what you build is temporary, and so is the security you thought you'd established.

You think of the embankment's broken teeth again and the auditors with their neat paper. You think of Celine Harrow's hand on the dossier and of Ronan's bright, indignant face. You think of Oba's patient cadence. You think of Maya, and how fiercely she clutches small things to her chest.

In the end, the town will keep living — because it always has — but the ground beneath it is cracked in ways that will take years to heal. Some will leave because leaving is easier than rebuilding. Others will stay because the place is blood and memory and salt and cannot be walked away from.

You stand and feel the cold bite into your calves. You are tired and unmade, but there is a stubbornness in you like a root. You'll keep mapping what broke and what can be mended. You'll sit in hearings and in kitchens and in the mud, and you will hold the lists of names as if they are oaths.

The public record will tell the facts: deviations confirmed, partial failure, displacement, pockets of lives saved because someone asked for proof. The private ledger will tell the cost — the lines of blame, the friendships stretched to breaking, the quiet departures at dawn. The town will be altered. You will be altered.

You look out at the black water and allow the tide to pull at your thoughts until they smooth.
