## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Sunrise

---

## World View
Saltwick is a near-future coastal town reshaped by rising seas and seasonal superstorms. Once a fishing hub, it now balances community-led restoration and municipal modernization. The political center is Harrow Hall, where Mayor Celine Harrow brokers investment and infrastructure; civic decisions are livelong legacies. Economically, Saltwick is brittle: local livelihoods depend on short seasons and boatyards, while outside developers promise stability through top-down projects. Socially, there is a dense informal layer—volunteer rescue squads, elders who remember old tides, and families who have lived through multiple relocations. Technology is advanced but uneven: community labs, salvaged sensors on breakwaters, and retrofitted shipping-container research spaces coexist with faded high-visibility vests and hand-stitched rubber boots. Nature is both antagonist and partner—saltwind, seagrass beds, and the Glass Marsh hold memory and fragility. Culture centers on reciprocity and practical ritual: seasonal shoreline cleans, seedling swaps in the lighthouse greenhouse, and Oba Kofi’s tide-knowledge ceremonies. Conflicts center on time horizons: immediate protective measures versus patient ecological restoration. Religion and formal doctrine are rare—moral authority comes from elders and the science that narrates the town’s future. Atmosphere: melancholic but verdant, where every sunset is tinged by storm afterglow and every community meeting feels like a tide turning.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Alys Maren, a marine ecologist returned to Saltwick to lead seagrass and shoreline restoration. The story follows an Icarus arc: an intense, rapid ascent in influence and local success, peaking when a high-profile hybrid pilot combines Ivo’s immediate-protection engineering with your restorative science. Your dark chestnut hair is often pinned back against saltwind; your gray-green eyes watch tides and faces with equal intensity. The rise begins as your plans seed hope: community nurseries at the Old Lighthouse Greenhouse, rapid seawall patches co-designed with Ivo, and transparent data from the Muri Research Lab that wins local media. Success catapults you into difficult scrutiny—Celine offers a binding development contract with outside capital in the name of “resiliency.” The fall starts when a storm season coincides with political compromises and a critical structural failure: short-term defenses undermine newly planted seagrass, a badly timed contract displaces families, and a catastrophic storm exposes compromised infrastructure. Relationships fracture: Ivo and you clash over priorities; Maya, Oba, and Ronan demand accountability. The endings vary—ranging from a hopeful, though battered, hybrid victory to bitter displacement, regulatory exposure and moral reckoning, or a true but costly ethical victory—each concluding a rapid rise followed by a hard, consequential fall.

### Chapter 1: Returning Tide [Rise] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)  
* **Summary:** I arrive back in Saltwick, walking the harbor in my patched rubber boots, reacquainting myself with the salt wind, the scuff of rusted docks and the sight of seagrass plugs in trays at Muri Research Lab. I reconnect with Maya and set plans in motion. The initial momentum of hope builds as old relationships and small practical wins return a sense of agency.  
* **Visuals:** Painterly dusk at Saltwick Harbor: sea-foam greens and rust; I, with dark chestnut hair pinned back, olive-softshell jacket, brass compass at my neck, inspecting seedlings under brassy lamp light.  
* **Valence:** Positive—hopeful  
* **Arousal:** Medium—energized  
* **Branch:** Common  
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I step off the bus and the wind brings back the sound of lines slapping against masts. My hair, still damp from the wind, is pinned back; flyaways sting my cheek. I carry my field notebook in the inside pocket of a softshell that smells faintly of diesel and old seaweed. Maya meets me at the old boatyard—paint-splattered apron, laugh like it hasn't been stretched thin—and she leads me past nets and patched sails to the Muri Research Lab’s shipping-container windows. Inside, LED lamps make the seagrass trays glimmer like tiny green tides. Oba Kofi's cane rat-taps as he tells me old tidal stories in a low voice that smells of cedar smoke. Already, there is a rhythm to it: assess the damage, catalog the seedlings, set volunteer rotations. I show them my plan: scaled nursery expansions, synchronized planting windows, data-sharing with Dr. Leyna Ortiz. The harbor feels small and fragile and full of possibility. For the first time since the storm that took my childhood door off its hinges, the town doesn't feel like it will fold under the next rain. I allow myself to believe we can nudge the tide.
