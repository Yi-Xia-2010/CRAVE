## Visual Novel Title
Saltwind: When the Tide Came In

---

## World View
Saltwick is a near-future coastal town reshaped by the 2040s' rising seas and seasonal superstorms. Once a tight-knit fishing hub, it now lives between makeshift seawalls, nascent green infrastructure projects, and the arrival of climate refugees. Governance is contested: a municipal council led by Mayor Celine Harrow pursues top-down investment and rapid infrastructural fixes; community organizers and volunteers — led by Ivo Calder and elder voices like Oba Kofi — favor local, phased resilience. Science is present but uneven: field labs and retrofit research sites (the Muri Research Lab and the Old Lighthouse Greenhouse) coexist with salt-scorched fields and half-built harbor defenses. Culture is austerely practical and sentimental — braided fishing lines as jewelry, community potlucks beside sandbags, and ritualized sea-blessing ceremonies before storms. Religion is informal: reverence for the sea and tacit rules of reciprocity. Class tension is subtle: long-time residents, boatyard workers, and renters face displacement threats from outside investors encouraged by Celine’s modernization plan. Geography centers on the harbor, the Glass Marsh, the North Beach Ridge, and the lighthouse greenhouse — places where weather, memory, and policy collide. Atmosphere: melancholic but verdant; weather is a character, painting every dawn and breakdown with salt and light.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Alys Maren) return to Saltwick to lead a seagrass and shoreline restoration project intended to shield the town from storm surge and rebuild fisheries. The story is an Icarus arc: a fast, exhilarating ascent of community optimism and apparent success followed by a catastrophic collapse that forces everyone to reckon with the limits of plans, politics, and love.

Act I (Common Route) — I arrive, reconnect with Maya, Oba Kofi, and Ivo. I secure initial funding and technical support with Dr. Leyna Ortiz; small pilot restorations show dramatic early results. Trust builds with Ivo; the town begins to rally.

Act II (Branches A–C; Rise continues) — Different strategies diverge: community-first restoration (A), immediate protective infrastructure championed by Ivo (B), or political compromise with Celine's large-scale investors (C). Each route carries a distinct shape of success: scientific breakthrough, rapid construction, or political legitimacy.

Act III (Fall) — The Icarus fall comes fast: a catastrophic storm, a corporate betrayal, or systemic failure undermines prior gains. Outcomes vary: some endings are morally complex (True), some painfully sad, some bittersweet where ecology survives but personal relationships fracture. Every path crescendos into ruin that forces Saltwick into a new, uncertain era.

### Chapter 1: "Returning Tide" [Rise] (Branch: Common)

* **Entry Condition:** None (Start)

I encounter Maya painting a salvaged board, Oba Kofi watching the tide, and Ivo fixing a net. I feel the old responsibility and a surge of optimism: the pilot seagrass plots have started to take. I lace my olive-softshell closed against the saltwind and promise to rebuild — scientifically and socially.
* **Visuals:** Golden-hour harbor light on weathered docks; I (dark chestnut hair pinned back, gray-green eyes catching amber) stepping from a rain-streaked truck; Maya in paint-spattered apron; Ivo in faded high-vis vest, canvas cap; Oba’s carved cane glinting.
* **Valence:** Positive
* **Arousal:** Medium-High
* **Branch:** Common
* **Next:** Chapter 2

I set my boots into Saltwick’s familiar sand and gravel and realize how much the town’s contours map onto the flaw lines in me. My jacket tastes of salt; my notebook presses against my ribs like a promise. Maya waves, hair braided, sun-browned skin bright against a canvas of gray docks. Oba Kofi leans on his fish-carved cane, saying old tide names as if they’re liturgies. Ivo approaches with an easy grin — hazel eyes softened under a sun-bleached cap, tool belt at his hip. He jokes about my city-slick boots, but his hands are honest. We walk the crescent of the harbor: patches of bright seagrass (pilot plots), nets slung like exclamation marks, rusted cranes brightened by algae. Dr. Leyna Ortiz’s compact field lab hums on raised stilts, LEDs flickering. There’s an electricity in the air, not just weather: people whisper of grant approvals, of community crews ready to plant. My excitement is careful and immediate; I feel the tilt of momentum. I tuck my field notebook into my jacket and promise myself I’ll keep both the science and the people together. That evening the wind tastes like possibility.

The day we planted the first full swath of seagrass felt like a sacrament. I stood ankle-deep in salted sand, hair pinned back with sea-salted flyaways, the compass at my chest turning with each step. ### Chapter 6: "When the Sky Broke" [Fall] (Branch: Route A — Ending: Sad)

- **Entry Condition:** From Chapter 5, Option A
* **Summary:** We chose community-first restoration and delayed larger armored protections. For a while, success seemed confirmed: seagrass spread, feeders returned, and local markets thrummed. Then an unprecedented storm season hit: multiple superstorms in quick succession. The pilot beds, still young, were ripped; sand shifted, vents overwhelmed, and the fragile harbor configurations failed. Homes on North Beach Ridge were lost; a rescue boat capsized. I get called from the greenhouse by Ronan; I run in my mud-streaked cargo trousers as the wind erases the horizon. The fall is catastrophic: the town's defenses break, and the emotional aftermath is devastation and blame.
* **Visuals:** Storm-blue skies, pewter harbor, uprooted seagrass washing like dark ribbons; my weathered jacket flapping, hair loose and streaked with rain; Ivo trying to rally volunteers, face stung by salt.
* **Valence:** Negative
* **Arousal:** Very High
* **Branch:** Route A — End
* **Next:** Ending — Sad

The storm arrived not like a single roar but like a sequence of shoves. Rain drove sideways; the harbor flattened into a sheet of pewter. I remember Leyna’s face in the lab light, the data that said "probability" and the way numbers can be eaten by the ocean. We had pride and we had patience; we had not had time. I ran barefoot across the ridge, boots left behind, the brass compass cold against my sternum. Ivo shouted orders, his hazel eyes eclipsed by exhaustion; Ronan clung to a lifeline with oil-smeared hands. We watched as the newest seedlings — our children — tore loose and swirled away like small black banners. The seawall proposals we refused now felt like a phantom promise. Houses buckled, fences unmoored, and in the chaos someone whispered that Celine’s crews had already started erecting emergency berms — with contractors in tow. In the aftermath, ash-gray dawn revealed the geometry of what we’d lost: areas that would never reconfigure in the same way. People looked at me like I had promised them a shield and failed. The sound of the ocean that morning was a cleaving.

The fall is painful: visible protection exposed as a partial, sometimes cruel, fix.

* **Valence:** Negative
* **Arousal:** Very High

The seawall gleamed like a promise until the promise met the sea’s intelligence. During the storms, the armored edges funneled fury; places beyond the wall washed away with a force that felt like betrayal. Fish that once pooled in shallow nursery beds thinned, and nets hauled in emptier song. The town’s applause curdled into argument. Ivo and I stood for hours in the hollow of a shelter, the wind riffing through the supports. He blamed the council; I blamed compromise; both of us blamed ourselves. People who’d accepted our visible protections now faced long-term losses: looser livelihoods, changed harvest schedules, lost cultural rhythms. Leyna’s tablet showed shifts in sediment that read like a slow hemorrhage. I sleep in fits, nightmares of seedlings ripped from their hold, and wake to the smell of diesel and damp tarps. The harbor remains a monument — to our capacity to mobilize — but also a tropicalized wound. The arc of our success gave way to a different kind of failure; the cost is measured not only in property but in the erosion of how we were together.

### Chapter 12: "Contracts and Collapse" [Fall] (Branch: Route C — Ending: Bittersweet / Sad blend)

Some neighborhoods survive; others are devastated. The restoration lines survive in patches. * **Visuals:** Engineered shoreline under storm-clouds; Celine’s immaculate silhouette in crisis rooms; my hollowed gray-green eyes staring at a smudged ledger.
* **Valence:** Mixed-Negative
* **Arousal:** Very High
* **Branch:** Route C — End
* **Next:** Ending — Bittersweet/Sad hybrid

At first, the hybrid was a victory lap: townsfolk on the seawall, scientists and laborers working side by side, a green tongue of restored marsh between concrete ribs. We were lauded in regional papers. But the ledger taught us the hidden language of compromise: a contractor’s clause here, a maintenance deferral there. The multinational’s lawyers read fine print like weather patterns and decided margins mattered more than memory. When the compound storm came — a monstrous swell that tested every seam — some of the constructed protections held, and in those pockets our seagrass and oyster reefs sustained partial shelter. Elsewhere, houses buckled and the harvests failed. People who had trusted our judgement looked at me with the geometry of blame. Celine, in crisis mode, brokered emergency relocations; she moved fast and looked like a savior to some and a grim figure to others. I stand on a stretch of shore where seedlings still cling and families mourn, knowing we preserved some living things but at a cost: broken promises and displaced trust. The town endures, but we paid dearly.
