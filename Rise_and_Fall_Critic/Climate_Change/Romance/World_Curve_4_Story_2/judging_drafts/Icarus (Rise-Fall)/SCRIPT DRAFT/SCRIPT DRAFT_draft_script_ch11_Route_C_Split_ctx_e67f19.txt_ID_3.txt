[Scene: Glass Marsh | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of glassy pools; pilot seagrass mats punctuate the shallow water, filaments catching the first lean light]
[Music: Gentle, rising strings — tentative optimism]
[Sound: Soft gull calls, distant harbor bell; water lapping against mudflats]

You are kneeling at the marsh edge where the water is the color of old coins. Your knees sink a little into the cold sediment; the smell of brine and wet peat fills your nose. In the live feed on your tablet the pilot mats undulate like slow, deliberate breathing—brown, slimed, and improbably alive. A tiny flash of silver — a juvenile fish — darts between stems, and the smallness of that motion makes something in your chest unclench.

Your hand hovers over the screen; the braiding of the cord around your wrist presses like an answered promise. The lab lights from the Muri containers will warm soon, but here the day is thin and honest. You think of the three-line lead you drafted: "Seed. Shelter. Return." The words taste like work and like bread.

[Visual: Close-up on your notebook in your jacket pocket; the brass compass is a cold weight against your ribs]
[Sound: A single text ping — Maya: "Neighbors asking about volunteer slots. Want to join the planting day. Bring bread?"]

You laugh under your breath. Bread, you decide, and a thermos of something hot. Community muscle is a recipe: people, tools, and a reason to show up that feels larger than fear.

You pull a small clump of silt through your fingers, feeling the texture: gritty, alive with detritus and the faint perfume of seaweed. It smells like possibility.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Morning]
[Visual: The greenhouse is a warm, humid box of light perched on the cliff; seed trays and hydroponic rigs line the panes]
[Music: A warm cello line threads through the strings — forward-moving, generous]
[Sound: Wind smacking against the lighthouse exterior, distant surf; inside, the low hum of pumps and the rustle of leaves]

You climb the narrow stair, boots scuffing, and the warmth hits you like a hand on your shoulder. Leyna is already there, tablet in hand, her breath fogging in the humid air. Seedlings bend toward the light like a small congregation.

Leyna: "You're right on time. The flux on the mats looks better than the morning scans predicted. Growth velocity's trending up."

You: (softly) "How much up?"

Leyna: (tapping the tablet) "Enough that with scaled volunteer efforts we could—" (she pauses, recalibrating) "—we could double coverage in six months if we secure steady funding and keep seed survival percentages where they are."

You feel that number settle in your chest. Six months of scale. It is both a lamp and a pressure gauge.

Maya arrives, apron already smudged with potting mix, carrying a ceramic tin of homemade rolls. Her eyes light when she sees you.

Maya: "Bread intact. Also—Oba sent his blessing and said he'll bring three helpers. He says the tide charts favor the high window next week."

You: "Tell him thank you. And tell him we'll bring the big tarps."

Maya: (with a crooked grin) "And the thermos."

The greenhouse door opens and a different air slips in: colder, formal. Celine Harrow steps through, the hem of her coat skimming the trays. Her presence is a quiet incoming tide; she smells faintly of citrus and municipal paper.

Celine: "Good morning, Alys. Leyna. Maya." (She inclines her head.) "Thank you for making space. I saw the data. Impressive work."

You notice her hands—steady, well-manicured—clasped around a slim tablet and a leather dossier. There is a practiced softness to her smile that keeps its edge.

You: "Mayor Harrow. You're welcome. It's been a team effort."

Celine: (turning to Leyna) "The town needs that team everywhere. My office has been following the pilot closely. We convened a finance working group. There are investors willing to bridge capital for a larger municipal rollout."

Leyna: "Investors?"

Celine: "Public-private partnerships. Funding for scaled mat production, a trained crew, and hard infrastructure to protect areas still vulnerable." (She lays the dossier on a crate, flipping to maps.) "But—" (she meets your eyes) "—and I know this is important to you—those funds would be contingent on ecological covenants. Standards, monitoring, and an oversight clause that I can guarantee will shield the town from exploitative development."

Your mouth goes dry for a second; the word covenant lands with the authority of a gavel. There's relief braided with alarm — funding is a river you can steer, but covenants and oversight are steady hands that might not be yours.

Ivo appears at the greenhouse threshold, workshirt still dusted with dry sand. He steps in with Ronan not far behind; his presence rearranges the air into something more immediate.

Ivo: (to you, half-smile) "Heard there were rolls. Also heard there were promises."

You: (dryly) "Promised nothing I can't eat."

Ivo's gaze flicks to Celine, friendly but guarded.

Ivo: "Mayor. If there's money, we could get seawalls reinforced where people need them now. Shelters, stronger pilings. People need visible defense before the next season's shops get hammered."

Celine: "Exactly. Which is why the hybrid approach—restoration plus targeted hardened measures—makes the most sense."

Leyna: (wearing the scientist's cautious smile) "The data supports layered approaches. Immediate protective measures reduce acute loss while restoration increases long-term resilience. But the devil's in the management details. Oversight creates bureaucracy; bureaucracy can slow adaptive responses during a storm."

You: "And covenants?"

Celine: (leaning forward, quietly) "They bind the investors to ecological standards and give the town legal recourse if someone tries to gut protections for profit. I'm offering a clause that puts local oversight in a joint municipal panel."

Oba Kofi arrives then, cane tapping softly on the greenhouse floor. He smells of tar and sun and old boats. He moves slower than everyone else but his presence makes the conversation feel anchored.

Oba Kofi: (to you, quietly) "We don't want outsiders telling us which fish to keep. But we also cannot carry all the nets ourselves forever."

You feel the balance of his words — a plea and a warning. They land like sea stones.

Maya: (soft, urgent) "If the town can get more mats in the water sooner, more of our kids will learn to steward them. But if funding comes with strings and developers start talking property lines, people will be angry. They'll be scared."

Ivo: "Then we make the strings tug the right way. Mandate community seats on the governance board. Mandate labor contracts that hire locals. If Celine will write that into the deal, it's less of a sell-out and more of a lever."

Celine: (eyes narrowing just the barest degree) "I'm prepared to take political heat. But I won't approve anything that becomes a paper tiger. We need to move water and we need to move it fast."

You: (internal) The argument folds into two simple truths: speed saves houses; integrity saves a town's soul. Can both be true at once? It feels like asking the sea to do two tides.

Leyna taps her tablet and projects a visualization onto a hanging sheet — a banded graph and coastal maps that bloom with overlays: projected mat coverage, surge reduction curves, emergency shelter placements. The greenhouse fills with the pale blue wash of possibility.

Leyna: "If we accept municipal capital under strict covenants, we can scale coverage in months rather than years. If we refuse, we keep full community control but accept slower growth and greater exposure in the interim. Or—" (she glances at Ivo and then at you) "—we could attempt a hybrid crafted by both of you: immediate protections in the most vulnerable spots while crowdsourced restoration expands the living buffer."

You: (out loud) "A combined plan requires coordination and trust. We all know how fragile both can be under pressure."

Ivo: (quiet, steady) "I'm willing to coordinate. If we build seawalls where families will be saved now and seed the marsh around them, you're not choosing one over the other."

Celine: (raising a palm) "I'm willing to sign covenants that enshrine ecological standards, including periodic audits with public reporting."

Oba Kofi: (slowly) "Words are good. But we will need hands."

Maya: "And bread," she grins, raising the tin like a flag.

The conversation ricochets — proposals, caveats, soft promises intersecting like tidal eddies. You feel something like a rising current pull under your feet: opportunities cresting into possibility. The tone in the greenhouse is hopeful, energized, but also careful; everyone here is trying not to force a solution that will fracture later.

You take a breath. The greenhouse smells of damp soil and heated possibility; outside the sea grumbles. There is a rhythm here—data, people, politics—in which the pilot mats have made themselves a lever. The town needs to choose how to use that lever.

Celine's expression becomes, for a fraction of a second, complex, like a window with frost patterns you can't quite see through.

Celine: "I don't ask you to surrender authority, Alys. I ask you to share power in a way that gives us the resources to protect the most vulnerable while we restore the whole shoreline."

You: "And if the oversight board becomes a tool for outside interests?"

Celine: (meeting your gaze) "Then we bind the board to transparent processes and give community seats—real seats with votes, not token chairs. Let the covenants be legally enforceable. Let the town have vetoes on incompatible projects."

Leyna: (quiet, with a practiced impatience) "Covenants plus community seats plus a joint science panel could work, but we have to specify decision pathways for emergencies. Bureaucracy is useful until a storm hits."

Ivo: (leaning in) "Then we write those pathways together. Immediate triage, then restoration follow-through. No surprises."

You: (internal) Your mind enumerates the costs: the moral hazard of compromise, the relief of saved roofs, the time lost waiting for legal drafts, the urgency of a coming season. Underneath them all is the steady hum you felt back at the marsh: life is returning. That hum insists that you choose action that preserves both the living seagrass and the human communities that tend it.

Oba Kofi puts his hand on the wooden crate between you and Celine. His fingers are callused, sure.

Oba Kofi: "Whatever you decide, make sure the people can touch it. Paper will not hold the nets. Hands will."

The greenhouse quiets for a breath. Outside, a gull cries and the light spills warmer across the seedlings. The feeling you carry is not a problem to be solved so much as an invitation: to shape this thing together, or to let it shape you.

You look at each of them — Leyna's careful insistence, Ivo's practical heat, Celine's smooth offer, Maya's steady faith, Oba's anchor — and you feel the tide of potential. The decision will set the arc of work for months, maybe years. It will steer how neighbors sleep during storms. It will shape what is preserved and what is risked.

You reach for your notebook. The paper takes your pen with a soft scratch.

You: "We have three paths in front of us." (You let the sentence hang.) "One keeps the approach community-first and slower. One accepts municipal hybrid funds with strict covenants. One attempts a combined plan now — protective measures layered with restoration across the same timeline."

Celine: (softly) "Those are the choices."

Ivo: "No shame in choosing speed with guardrails. No shame in choosing integrity and patience either. This is about what kind of town Saltwick wants to be."

Maya: "And whether our neighbors feel like partners or like something being decided for them."

Leyna: "Scientifically, all three are viable in different ways. The difference is social coordination and political will."

You: (internal) You feel the rise, the pull toward possibility. There is faith in the room: faith that people will show up, that work adds up, that the pilot mats can become a shoreline. The air tastes of that faith.

[Music: Strings swell into a hopeful, upward phrase — not conclusory, but encouraging]

You look at the faces again and, with the steadying breath you've learned to take before big things, prepare to ask them to commit.
