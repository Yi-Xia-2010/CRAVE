[Scene: Seawall Construction Site | Morning]
[Visual: The new stretch of seawall catching the low sun; workers in bright vests moving like a practiced tide]
[Sound: The rasp of shovels against sand, the hollow thud of piled stones settling, gulls complaining overhead]
[Music: Warm, ascending strings — a sense of careful progress]

You breathe in the seawind and taste iron and salt; it feels like progress on your tongue. The section you helped plan — staggered rock, woven geotextile, pockets for planted shoots — holds the water back in the quiet hour between tides. The damp wood of the temporary scaffold leaves a dark print on your palm when you rest your hand there.

Ivo stands at the curve of the wall, shoulders forward, sleeves rolled to the elbow. His eyes track the volunteers like someone counting stars. When he sees you, his grin is the kind that carries news: small, exact, comforting.

Ivo: "Look at that. We did that — not a contractor sign in sight."
You: "We did. And it held through the last gusts."
Ivo: (laughs) "You said we'd be up against the tide. You still have that stubborn tide of yours."
You: "Someone has to be stubborn with a town that keeps its doors open to the sea."

You let the humor sit between you and him. The joke belies the hours — the sieve of permits, the late-night measurements, Ronan's awkward but earnest hauling of sacks. You feel the warmth of it: the momentum you and the town have built is real, granular as the sand that keeps shifting underfoot.

Ronan sweeps by, a streak of younger energy, salt in his hair, a bright smear of paint on his cheek. He shouts something about the next shipment of coir rolls that makes a half-dozen people laugh.

Maya pulls you aside, hands smelling of tar and rosemary (she rinsed her hands in the herb tubs after lunch). Her expression is small and fierce at once.

Maya: "Do you know what Mrs. Kade said? She hasn't stopped smiling since she saw the wall hold the storm last week."
You: "She said the wall made her feel like her house could breathe again."
Maya: "You did that. We did." (she squeezes your forearm)

Your chest loosens. You carry the memory of a different winter, of broken windows and water in basements. That ache was a map to this morning; it made this victory necessary and luminous.

The workers around you move with a kind of easy choreography now. What was improvisation last month is craft this week. Leyna's sensor buoys hummed last night showing reduced undercutting. You feel those numbers in your bones, not just on a tablet: the town is learning the sea's language.

[Scene: North Beach Ridge | Late Morning]
[Visual: The ridge where improvised sandbags sit beside newly planted dune grass; sunlight strikes puddles left in the hollows]
[Sound: Cloth fraying as volunteers tie bags; distant machinery like a slow heartbeat]
[Music: Piano motifs — steady, hopeful]

You walk the ridge with Ivo and Ronan behind you, boots sinking into the sand where green shoots fight through. There are bright places of plastic and old netting tangled in the drift, but there are also shoots of sea grass and small clusters of dune-binding plantings you designed. Wind slides salt across your neck.

Ivo: "People are showing up earlier now. They camped out last night to guard the seed beds."
You: "That's commitment, not just curiosity."
Ivo: "It's contagious. Your plan's contagious."

You want to tell him how much you want to hand him the credit for keeping the crews fed, for knowing which neighbor could be convinced by a hot meal more than a presentation. Instead you say:

You: "We made something people can touch. That changes the conversation."
Ivo: (nods) "And it gives us leverage."

The word settles between you. Leverage is what Celine wears across the council table like a pin. She's also what the municipal budget talks sound like, what the whispered offers and suits represent.

Ronan: (punches the air) "We could lay down another strip by tonight if we keep the rhythm!"
You: (smiling) "Then let's keep the rhythm. But not at the expense of the planting beds — they need staggered placement."

Ronan's grin is easy. There's a generational pulse here: urgency tuned with patience. You find yourself admiring that balance; it is quietly like the shipwright's craft youvoiced to Ivo in your first meeting.

[Scene: Harbor | Noon]
[Visual: The quayside is a collage — bright vests, rusted bollards, a municipal SUV at the far end, and the warm glow of the fish-lamp vendor packing up]
[Sound: Seagulls, low-engine hum of a service boat, soft murmur of gathered voices]
[Music: Cello undertones, hopeful but with a diplomatic edge]

You feel more confident than you did in the first week you arrived — and for good reason. People are seeing the wall's effect. They are seeing what coordinated care can do. That confidence ripples outward and draws attention. Celine Harrow arrives the way she always does: composed, quietly assured, a civic presence that notifies the town's pulse. She moves with the kind of formalism that suggests meetings and memos, but the shadow of the motorcade behind her is small, humbly municipal rather than corporate ostentation.

Celine: (smiling, hands folded) "Alys. I had to see it for myself."
You: "It held a storm. It kept water from half a dozen cellars."
Celine: "Impressive. Truly."

Her praise is careful, measured. Her eyes slide over the wall, then to the volunteers, then to you. The look is hard to read — complex, private — a mixture of appraisal and calculation.

Celine: "Momentum is valuable. If the council can accelerate this with proper resources — contractors, machinery — we could finish the main ridge before the next predicted swell."
You: (exhale) "Finish faster certainly has appeal."
Celine: "And it's about livelihoods. Speed saves harvests, saves homes. We can bring the town the security it needs now."

You hear the logic: money, speed, visible results. You hear the pressure threaded through it like a current. It hums in the ropes, in the clank of tools. You are not opposed to scaled help — you led a team that leaned on expertise last winter — but the word "contractors" carries consequences: external agendas, standard-issue solutions, and the erosion of the communal labor that stitched the town back together.

Ivo steps forward, eyes flicking between your face and Celine's.

Ivo: "We appreciate the offer, Celine, really. But these walls are more than masonry. They're a civic memory now. Folks here have hands in the work. We can't outsource that."
Celine: (softly) "Then perhaps the municipality could fund crews that work alongside them. Professional teams that can be managed with oversight."

The word "oversight" rests in the air like a question. You imagine municipal contracts, clauses written in ivory offices, deliverables that don't account for tide moods. You imagine volunteers edged out, pride traded for efficiency.

You: "What does 'oversight' mean in practice? Who signs the contracts? Who decides the materials?"
Celine: (tilts her head, the smile steady) "Standard municipal terms. We would ensure compliance with safety and speed. Contracts are the tool of trust when scale is required."

Her tone is smooth. Her hands are small punctuation. You respond not with immediate refusal but with a practical counterweight.

You: "If outside crews are on the ridge, we need guarantees: community oversight, transparent procurement — clear penalties if ecological protocols are ignored."
Celine: (a flicker across her face, unreadable) "We could draft terms that include community liaisons."

Ivo: (leaning in) "And who chooses those liaisons? Who enforces them? We will not be token representatives."

Celine: (measured) "I want a solution that preserves families and the town's economy. I believe the municipality can be that partner."

Her voice has the cadence of someone used to being bought into plans. There's a sincerity in the words, but around it — the thing the town has already begun to do for itself — sits a risk. Accepting help could accelerate work and protect more homes sooner. Refusing could embolden local pride, maintain autonomy, but leave more to the weather's mercy in the immediate term. There is no small moral calculus here, only a weight you are now invited to hold.

Ronan: (quietly, to you) "The crews could move faster. We could learn, too. But what if they bulldoze the sowing areas?"
You: "Then we'd be teaching them to see what we see. Or we'd walk away before the seeds are sown."

Maya: (soft, urgent) "People are tired, Alys. They'll clap for a finished wall and forget the seedlings. I don't want to pick between dinner and the coast."

Her words land like a stone in a shallow pool. Ripples expand. You think of the faces you've seen this morning — Mrs. Kade's relieved smile, Ronan's ridiculous grin, Ivo's steady hands — and you think of what the town's labor has become: not only protection, but identity.

[Sound: A distant bell rings — the harbor bell — like a counting of breaths]
[Music: Strings swell, bright and forward]

You open your notebook but don't write. You take the brass compass from your pocket, the metal warm from your hand; the needle trembles and then finds an axis. It is only a small comfort, a familiar weight. Still, it grounds you. Decisions are not abstract; they are the shape of people's evenings, the timbre of their pride, the literal placement of seedlings.

You look at Celine. Her posture is impeccable. Her expression is — as always in public — composed and complex.

Celine: "Whatever you choose, I want it to be for Saltwick's long-term viability."
You: "So do I."

Her eyes search yours for a long moment. You sense the calculus behind her patience: budget cycles, donors, potential reputational wins. You sense your own calculus: trust, ownership, and the fragile apprenticeship of community science.

You step back, letting the sun find the curve of the seawall, and for a moment the town holds its breath with you. The volunteers cluster, waiting not for orders but for a signal that the day's work matters. That waiting is its own kind of answer.

You feel the rise — hope threaded through tension. Whatever you choose will shape both the physical future of the ridge and the social future of Saltwick. There is momentum already; you can feel it as a literal strain on the rope you can smell in the air — salt, oil, new plantings. You know that this is an inflection point, not an end.

You close the conversation for now with a practical note, because your temperament is to make decisions in small, accountable steps.

You: "We are willing to consider municipal support, but with binding community oversight and transparent procurement. No hidden clauses. No silent trades."
Celine: (tilts her chin) "I'll have my office draft proposals. I think we can accommodate your terms."
You: (softly) "We'll need them in writing — and time to consult the community."

Celine's reply is a nod and that same composed, private expression. You hear the possibilities folding into the day like new light across the water.

[Music: A hopeful chord, lingering]
[Visual: The camera widens to show the wall, the volunteers, the municipal SUV, and the town gathered in a single frame — a town at a hinge]

You breathe in the salt air again. The morning has been good — achievements, handshakes, arguments that are blunt but not broken. Momentum has weight now; it is heavy with potential. Your chest tightens with the usefulness of it.

You feel the decision forming as one you must own publicly, but you also know it will be carried by many hands.

[Scene: Seawall Construction Site | Afternoon — Transition]
[Visual: Soft golden-hour light bathing the wet stones; shadows lengthen]
[Music: Gentle, ascending — a final lift of optimism]

You walk the length of the wall one last time before the town meeting. Volunteers scatter their tools; children run along the top of the lower bank with a kind of ceremonial joy. The sound is like small bells. You place your palm on the rock and feel that this — this collective labor — is both fragile and sturdy in equal measure.

Your notebook is in your jacket. Your compass rests against the metal of the rail. You feel the town's attention like a tide pulling at your bones — practical, expectant, hopeful.
