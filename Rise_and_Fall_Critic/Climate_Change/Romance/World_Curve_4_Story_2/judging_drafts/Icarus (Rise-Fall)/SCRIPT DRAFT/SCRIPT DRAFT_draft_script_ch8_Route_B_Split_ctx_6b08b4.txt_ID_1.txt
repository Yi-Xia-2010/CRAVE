[Scene: New Embankment | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the freshly poured embankment under a washed-out blue sky; bunting flutters; workers move like careful insects]
[Music: Light, buoyant strings — a cautious uplift]
[Sound: The distant slap of waves, the clink of tools, faint applause from a ribbon-cutting crowd]

You stand at the lip of the new embankment with the crowd thinning behind you — a ribbon already coiled in a staging box, lanterns still hung like promises along the harbor edge. Salt and fresh concrete hang together in the air: the bright, mineral tang of cured cement tangled with the old, oily sweetness of seaweed. Your boots sink slightly into a salt-softened grit and you taste that small, sharp satisfaction people get when something important is finished.

Around you, townspeople speak in low, relieved tones. Ronan jokes with a crew foreman about the speed of the pour. Maya snaps photos with an old film camera, cheeks flushed from the sea breeze. Oba stands with his hands behind his back, eyes tracking the horizon as if reading tides the way others read faces. Leyna moves through the crowd with tablet in hand, cheeks lit by data and day. Celine, immaculate even here, takes a polite step toward you — the mayor’s practiced smile in place, the dossier tucked under her arm like a talisman.

You let the swell of the moment pass through you. The embankment is visible proof — people sleep easier tonight because something visible now stands between them and the sea. That feeling is honest and bright. You made a case, you found partners, and money moved into action. That matters.

Celine: "Alys, congratulations. You did what you said you'd do." (She inclines her head; the words are smooth.)

You: "We did it." Your voice is steadier than the tremor under your ribs. You find yourself looking at the seam between the new barrier and the old shoreline — a thin line where engineering meets tide. "This is a start."

Celine: "Start is precisely what people need. A visible wall, the community can see it." (Her smile widens; it doesn't reach her eyes entirely.)

Leyna arrives beside you, breathless with both exertion and the kind of giddy exhaustion scientists get when plans materialize. "The planting windows are scheduled. The breakwaters will be seeded next month." She taps her tablet, angles the screen toward you. "We've got monitoring lined up."

You study the animation on her tablet: a green lattice along the harbor's edge, slowly filling with seagrass swathes. The image calms you the way a tide chart calms a sailor: patterns made legible.

Ivo appears from the dock with a tool belt slung over his shoulder. His cap is crooked with the wind; his face is sun-softened, but his smile when he sees you is immediate and—complex. (He looks at you, and there is that look you know: gratitude plus a thousand small practical assessments.)

Ivo: "Not bad for a week's worth of horrid weather and an over-enthusiastic foreman." (He claps you on the shoulder; his hand is callused and warm.) "You holding up?"

You: "Holding." That word carries more than you mean. Holding is translation, measurement, endurance. "How's the compaction on the eastern seam?"

Ivo: "Passed visual, passed compaction readings the crew logged." He hesitates and his gaze flicks to the contractors on the far side, where a truck idles and two men argue about tarp placement. "But there were shortcuts in the joints. They used a different sealant near the marsh fingers. Saved them two days, maybe—"

Your stomach tilts at the word. Shortcuts and marsh fingers is a bad syntax in your mind. You step down closer to the seam, and the wind lifts the ribbon of plastic from a pile of discarded forms. Your eyes pick up the sheen at the joint where concrete meets older stone — a faint, darker gloss that isn't the same as the rest.

You: (You crouch, fingers hovering but not touching. You don't want to cross a line by contaminating a potential sample.) "Leyna, can you run the site logs for the last twelve hours? Compaction reports, materials receipts, sealant batch IDs?"

Leyna's smile tightens. "I already—" She checks the tablet again, thumb brushing the edge. "There are notes flagged as 'field adjustment' in the contractor's log. I'll need a full pull from Harrow procurement to match the material manifest."

Celine, overhearing, shifts closer with an effortless diplomatic pivot. "If there's anything — anything at all — that needs reviewing, we'll handle it through the proper channels. No reason to sound false alarms on ribbon day, Alys."

Her tone is careful, an amber warning lamp that doesn't sound like accusation. There is tension in it, though — the kind that says politics is a thin glass and someone might drop it.

Maya, who has been hovering with a thermos and half a sandwich, steps forward with a hard, earnest look. "If the contractors cut corners, someone needs to answer. People didn't come back to watch them patch things cheap."

You feel the warm knot that is always there when family speaks plainly — it focuses you. Maya's eyes find yours and for a second, the crowd and the speeches and the media feeds blur into a single requirement: this is about people's homes, not headlines.

Oba sighs, a quiet sound that feels like a stone settling. "We build walls for tomorrow," he says slowly, "but the sea remembers how the walls were built. If the foundations are loose, the sea will find the loose place first."

You: "Then we check." The sentence is both order and wish. You run your tongue over the inside of your cheek. The reflex to control, to bureaucratically anchor the rising anxiety, kicks in.

You choose neither reflexively. Instead, you let the rise in your chest be a metronome: assess, conserve, decide. Being methodical is not a refusal—it is how you hold people in your care. But method is also slow, and the town just watched a ribbon become a promise. People expect motion now.

Cut to Harrow Hall: a warm, civic interior where the new embankment's contract copies are filed in a leather-bound municipal ledger. The sun through the tall windows hits the dust in slanting shafts, turning the council chamber into a cathedral of documents. Celine's office is open; the dossier she carried before is now a neat stack on her desk. She motions you in with the same practiced ease that gets her through audits and photo ops.

Celine: "I thought you'd want to see the final procurement summary. Transparency is good for the town." (Her hands are open; the statement lands like a coin.)

You sit across from her, Leyna beside you. Her tablet has been replaced by hard copies, stamps, signatures. You open to the procurement table and begin matching batch codes to sealant types, thinking in lists and tolerances the way a musician thinks in scales.

Leyna: "Here—look at this." She points at an entry: the field log lists 'Type C polymer blend' in the seam fills, but the delivery manifest shows 'Type B flexcoat' for the same line item.

You: (Your mouth goes dry. In your head, thresholds light up: B vs. C, two different tolerances, two different life expectancies.) "Is that a mislabel or a substitution?"

Leyna: "Either a substitution without approval, or a clerical error. But there are also notes about 'accelerated cure' and 'adjusted mix' in the contractor's sub-log." She looks at you — hope and worry braided. "On paper, the embankment meets specs. In the field logs, they've documented deviations."

Your internal monologue folds into a narrow, precise calculation: a substitution that shaves days could shave expected lifespan. On the other hand, the embankment functions now; children will sleep with less fear. The choice spreads out into others: trust the system that got the money flowing and let it stand, or force the grinding teeth of audits and risk losing public faith.

Celine reaches forward, fingers steepling. "Audits will delay completion certificates and could spook the funders, Alys. We want the town to feel safe. Do you want to be the person who interrupts that feeling with process?"

Her sentence is not unkind. It is tactical, polished. In the space between you, history seems to hiss — deals signed, margins cut, and the town's trust in visible protection versus invisible resilience.

Ivo: (He has followed you up from the harbor without fanfare.) "If the embankment can hold a season, we can shore up the weak spots with monitored fixes. We don't need to make a show of holding everything up for paperwork."

You: "And if the sealant fails early, those fixes will be reactive and expensive—by then, people's homes could be at risk." Your words come out fine but carrying weight.

Ivo: "I know. I also know that when people see sirens and hearings, they panic. They leave. We keep people here if they feel the barrier outside their windows." He looks down; something softer tugs at his jaw. "We don't have the luxury of long timelines."

There is a hush. Leyna leans forward, elbows on the table. "There is another route," she says. "We can flag a targeted audit on the questionable batches. That pauses a narrow path for investigation without full stop. It's not perfect, but it's measured."

You feel the prickle of possibility — precise pressure applied in the right place. Hope, in this chapter, smells like the lemon oil of well-used tools and the clean paper of plans that are not yet ruined.

Maya: "If you go quiet, they'll keep building and nobody will know until it fails. If you scream, they'll hate you for it." Her voice cracks, then steadies. "Either way, Alys, people need you to tell them the truth."

Oba remains quiet, then folds his hands around his cane. "There are ways to ask the sea to stay without angering the folks who put the first stones. Honesty is a medicine, but it can be bitter. You must pick the bitterness that heals."

The room hums with small, human friction: loyalties, arguments, practicalities. The town sits on the edge between two kinds of salvation — immediate safety and long-term viability. You feel, more keenly than before, the way love and duty can tug in different directions: you love this place, but duty asks you to be a steward of its future.

You breathe, feeling the swell of the day again: the embankment glints outside the hall windows, a physical thing, yes, but now a mirror reflecting every choice you make. Your instinct to protect the town’s people now clashes with the longer arc you were sent to shepherd — the living shoreline, the seagrass, the steady work that outlives headlines.

You think of the greenhouse where seedlings pressed their pale tabs toward light; you think of Oba's stories about tides remembered and of Leyna's quiet, furious faith in data. You think of Maya and the children, of Ivo's hands that build what is necessary now. You think about what it would mean to be the person who either delays or completes the promise.

There is courage in every route. There is also compromise. You have always feared that compromise could read as betrayal — but perhaps there is a version of compromise that becomes stewardship rather than surrender.

The documents sit between you like a map waiting for a name. You feel the rise of something that is not panic but the exacting energy of decision — the kind that pulls at every careful plan and rearranges the weeks ahead.

You steady yourself. This is not a moment to be small.

You look up at the faces around you. Each one is complex: Celine's professional composure, Ivo’s practical impatience, Leyna's scientific fervor, Maya's familial steadiness, Oba’s watchful patience. None of them are a single note. Each is a chord, and you must choose which chord to strike.

You breathe in the cool, book-scented air and decide to lay the facts plain. "There are documented deviations," you say. "We can authorize the completion as-is, we can demand a targeted audit and pause, or we can bring the discrepancies to the light and force public oversight."

Celine's fingers curl slightly around the dossier. Her face goes unreadable for a second, then smooths. Ivo's jaw tightens. Leyna exhales like someone about to sprint.

You feel the weight of the moment — not as doom, but as responsibility. The embankment is an achievement; the next steps will determine whether it is a pillar or a patch.
