[Scene: Seawall Construction Site | Dawn]
[Visual: Pale gold light skimming the top of the new wall; volunteers' breath fogging in the cool air]
[Music: Quiet, buoyant strings — a steady upward motion]
[Sound: Dull thump of mallets, surf lapping beyond the rocks, the soft clank of a radio]
You wake with sand still under your nails and the compass against your thigh like a small, private insistence. Overnight work left the seawall taller in places, buttressed in others — rough geometry kissed by salt and wet mortar. The horizon is a thin band of steel and rose; gulls wheel where the tide kisses the rebuilt stone. Momentum has weight, you think, and it sits in your chest like a hand you can lean on.

You breathe in. The smell of curing concrete mixes with kelp and diesel — the town's odd, honest perfume. Leyna's numbers hummed in your head last night; Ronan's laugh did too. You close your fingers around the leather strap of your notebook in your jacket and feel the braided cord on your wrist: practical talismans for a day that already promises labor.

Ivo is there before most of the others, sleeves rolled, eyes on a seam where two poured sections meet. He looks up when you approach, the half-smile that often softens him for you already in place.

Ivo: "You look like you slept with a tide chart under your pillow. How are you feeling?"
You: "Less like a shipwreck and more like a boat that might keep floating." (You shrug; it feels foolish and true.)
Ivo: "That's the best kind of honesty." (He taps the seam.) "Ronan says we saved half a dozen gardens last night. Maya's folks passed by with coffee and a blessing."

Ronan, already patched with mud and grinning, lifts a thermos in acknowledgement. Maya appears at the edge of the worksite, a smear of paint on her cheek, a look that is all family and town rolled into one.

Maya: "You convinced them to come. They trusted you."
You: "They trusted everyone who showed up." (You force the credit away from yourself because it sits too heavy otherwise.)
Maya: "Still — your plan held. That's a thing." (She presses a hand to your arm with quick affection, then steps back to check a row of sandbags.)

[Visual: Close-up on your compass in your hand; light cups the brass]
Your inner voice is insistent and careful. Momentum is not victory yet — the seawall buys time, not absolution — but it is something to move forward with. It changes negotiations, changing the leverage in ways that could help or complicate what comes next. You think of Celine, of budgets and contracts and the weight of her calm voice. You think of the volunteers' faces and the houses only a dune away.

[Scene: North Beach Ridge | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Windlifted grasses, the new seawall curving like a protective spine; beyond, low houses crouch behind dunes]
[Sound: Wind over reeds, the distant call of a foghorn]
You walk the length of the wall, feeling it beneath your palm. Each stone has a story — nights of hauling, Ronan's stubborn laughter, your own sleepless calculation about root structures and eelgrass planting. The town is quieter here; even sound seems held by the new shape of the shore.

A car pulls up on the harbor road and the sound of its engine is like a formal punctuation. Celine steps out, coat buttoned despite the warmth of the sun, her hair the precise bob you have watched in many council meetings. She stands at the lip of the ridge and watches the wall as if reading a map.

Celine: "Alys." (Her voice is measured; she looks at the wall and then at the people below as an investor looks at a ledger.) "This is a good day for Saltwick."
You: "We kept the tide outside the yards for one cycle. It was enough to keep a dozen families from flooding." (You measure your tone — grateful, careful.)
Celine: "Then we've earned a position to talk about next steps."

Ivo steps forward, shoulders square as a boat's prow. He is protective without announcing it.

Ivo: "Meaning?" (He does not look at her with ire; his voice is steady as timbers.)
Celine: "Municipal funding, contractors with heavy equipment, an accelerated schedule. We can shore the whole north side before the next storm season if we move quickly. I can authorize—" (Her hand brushes a palm-sized tablet she carries like an extra argument.) "—full funding and procurement."

Your chest tilts: an offer that could finish what the seawall started. It carries the scent of diesel and cement, and a neatness that might smooth over months of labor and months of worry. It also carries unknown clauses, contractors whose priority is deadlines, not eelgrass, and trade-offs that might be hard to undo.

Ivo: "Contracts like that come with conditions, Celine. And contractors bring heavy gear into delicate places. They'll flatten more than they fix if we don't watch them."
Celine: (She smiles, but the smile does not reach her eyes.) "That's what oversight is for. The municipal team will set standards."

Maya cuts between them, voice sharp with the urgency of kin.

Maya: "People want roofs that stay over their heads. They want fish in the nets. Don't make us choose between their safety and some—" (She searches for the word.) "—ideal."

Celine's face goes complex — the expression that means she is balancing empathy against politics. You cannot tell whether it leans toward bargain or true listening; you settle instead on the fact that she has leverage now that the wall exists.

Your mind catalogs outcomes like inventory: rapid completion could secure houses and economies, but risk ecological shortcuts; community-led work will hold values but move slower; a hybrid might be a middle ground — if enforceable.

The pause is intentional. You feel the hopeful tension of it — a chance to channel momentum into a shape that reflects the town's body as much as its budget. Celine's offer hangs there like a tide, predictable and capable of sweeping a lot away if you don't choose carefully.

[Scene: Harbor | Midday — Makeshift Meeting Near the Wharf]
[Visual: Volunteers sitting on upturned crates, a map spread on a folding table; the seawall visible in the distance]
[Music: Rising piano motif — careful optimism]
[Sound: Distant seagulls, the creak of rope, murmured conversation]

You call the meeting with the authority the wall has given you. People come — Ronan, mud on his knees and bright with plans; Maya, carrying a stack of flyers and people's names; older hands like Oba Kofi's, steady and watchful at the edge; neighbors you know by voice. They gather because something is changing and your role has become a hinge.

You lay out the options plainly, because clarity is a kind of honesty the town can use.

You: "We can keep going as we are — community-led, slower but rooted in our priorities. We can take Celine's offer for municipal funding and contractors and finish fast. Or we can demand hybrid oversight: money and muscle, but with legally enforceable community enforcement and transparency clauses."

Murals of faces — skeptical, tired, ready — watch you. Ivo sits close, leaning forward, fingers interlaced.

Ivo: "Fast completion saves weeks, maybe months. But if contractors cut corners, we lose more than time." (He meets your eyes.) "We need something that protects houses and habitats."

Ronan: "People want to feel like the tide won't take their life's work. If we can show progress, they'll breathe easier." (His optimism is practical: visible results convince the wary.)

Celine: (She steps forward as the crowd hums.) "I don't do deals that undermine the town. My proposal can be framed to include oversight; it's standard procurement. We can move now and make it visible. The funding won't wait forever."

You study everyone's faces. The wall is proof that the town can act. The wall is also proof that action changes the negotiation. You feel the rising chord of possibility — that momentum can be steered toward something both effective and principled.

Your internal monologue sharpens into a single question: how do you ensure that speed does not erase agency? How do you hold onto what you and the town have built while opening space to scale impact?

You picture the future you want: houses that hold in storms, eelgrass stitched back into shallows, children learning to plant rather than scramble to save possessions. You taste the salt and the sweet of small victories. This is the rise you have been pushing for — not easy, but buoyant.

Celine folds her hands, watchful, her expression a place where negotiation resides. Ivo's jaw tightens — not spitefully, but with the weight of someone who will bear the consequences if the town is hurried into a solution that betrays its ecology.

You have to decide how to use the momentum.

You speak to the crowd, voice steady.

You: "This is not just about a wall. It's about who builds the town's future. We can refuse contractors and keep it community-led. We can accept Celine's funding and contractors under municipal terms. Or we can demand hybrid oversight — accept funds only with community enforcement."

The words hang like buoys, each option a shape. You feel the tide of hope and pressure both; your leadership is the narrow path between them.
