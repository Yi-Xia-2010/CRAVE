[Scene: Cliff-top | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of the coastline — low clouds breaking like torn paper; engineered terraces zigzag down the slope]
[Music: Low, somber strings; a wind-swept piano motif underneath]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the soft slap of residual waves against breakwaters; grass whispering]

You stand with your palms on the cold, wind-polished railing, the braided cord at your wrist catching the last of the night's salt. From here the terraces look like a pale spine stitched into the cliff — concrete revetments softened by planted mats of seagrass and clumps of marsh rushes. Where the engineering meets the living, the edges are ragged and earnest, proof of compromise: mortar and root braided together.

The acceptance of Celine's hybrid funding plays back in your head not like a single decision but as a chorus of small, necessary concessions. The covenants sit in the dossier now — legal teeth wrapped with scientific obligations. For weeks they felt like promise. For weeks they also felt like a leash: someone else’s timeline, someone else's ledger, someone else's auditors.

You inhale. Salt and peat and the thin heat of the sun on stone. You can taste the meeting room air still on your tongue: formal paper, Clery ink, Celine's careful smile.

Oba Kofi approaches from the path, his cane tapping a steady counterpoint to the wind. He pauses beside you, the cliff rising and falling like his shoulders.

Oba Kofi: "You chose the middle road, Alys. Not clean, not pretty. But roads are often like this — full of ruts and new growth."

You: "That was the idea — to stitch what we know to what we need. To make sure money comes with rules this time."

Oba Kofi: (He studies the terraces, then you) "Rules are good. But rules need hands that will keep them. Paper will not stop the sea."

You feel that like a small sting beneath the sternum. He is right. The covenants will only be as strong as the people who enforce them.

Oba Kofi: "We plant. We build. We watch. The tide will tell us who kept their eyes open."

You nod and keep looking at the terraces until the paler blue of horizon blurs with light.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Morning]
[Visual: Glass panes banded with salt; inside, rows of seedlings glow copper-gold in morning light]
[Sound: Low hum of fans, the small drip of condensation, Leyna's soft keyboard taps]
[Smell: Wet earth, sea-salt, the faint sweetness of sprouting shoots]

The greenhouse reminds you why you took the hybrid path. Seedlings, once experimental, now stand in tidy rows — dwarf eelgrass shoots lucent and resilient. Dr. Leyna Ortiz crouches over a tray, fingers stained with sediment. Maya fusses with a watering rig, humming a tuneless beat that lifts the room.

Dr. Leyna: "The pilot sites that followed the covenants — the ones with proper monitoring and maintenance plans — show higher survival. The data is clear: combined approaches amplify resilience."

You: "And where the covenants were thin or ignored?"

Dr. Leyna: (Her smile is small) "Patchy. Where contractors prioritized quick containment — poured concrete, fewer plantings — we have structural holds but no living buffer. Those sections are colder in the models when the surge tests run."

Maya: (She wipes her hands on her apron) "A lot of folks don't understand what 'covenant' means until their house is on the wrong side of it. Then they know bills, audits, and 'oh, we didn't mean to' don't fix a leaking wall."

Ivo arrives with his cap in his hands, the canvas strap damp.

Ivo: "Storm watch came in last night. Satellite fed us a line of cells out of the southeast. We battened most of the shelters, but Ronan says the volunteer teams are stretched thin."

You: "How's the west cove — the low-income row?"

Ivo: (He exhales, something like air against a damp sail) "They used a temporary berm instead of the full base. The contractor shortcut — said they'd finish after the season — but the permit was stamped, and oversight was light. If the surge hits there hard, they'll be the first to fall."

There's a hardness in his voice, not anger at you exactly, but at the world that makes choices like this feel inevitable. Your chest tightens with the knowledge that your acceptance of the hybrid was supposed to prevent exactly this; the covenants were meant to close the gaps, but enforcement is municipal will and manpower and follow-through.

Dr. Leyna: "We can reroute some emergency teams to reinforce the berms, but that takes equipment and time. The legal teams are still drafting the fines and compliance language."

You meet Ivo's eyes. He looks at you as though measuring the distance between promise and practice.

You: "We pull the volunteers. We shore the berms. We make calls. Ivo — tell Ronan to take the crew."

Ivo: (He hesitates, voice low) "You can tell them, Alys. They'll listen to you."

The pause speaks a language neither of you are fluent in right now: of trust, of who leads when orders are practical and when they are political.

[Scene: Engineered Terraces | Midday — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Close, gritty view of terraces where concrete lips meet living mats; tide a darker slate below]
[Sound: The sea has become a percussion section — rhythm of hits and rebounds; wind a blade]
[Touch: Rain begins to thud; the air is heavy and metallic]

You drive along the service road with Leyna's tablet in the passenger seat, watching the storm swell like a living thing. Volunteers crouch behind sandbag walls, faces set. You see where the covenant-bound sections hold: straps tightened, monitoring buoys anchored, plantings woven into sediment. They bend, but they do not break. In other places, corners were cut: compacted gutters, unfinished anchors, fewer plant plugs.

An emergency call comes through — the west cove berm has begun to scour under an accelerating surge. Your stomach tightens into a knot you know too well.

At the berm you find families braced against the wind, children curled into blankets, belongings stacked on high points. The temporary berm groans under the weight and the water. Ivo elbows forward, directing people into the shelter trucks he and Ronan have lined up.

Ivo: "We need to get these people higher. Now. Two trucks, the old haul, and then the church hall. Leyna, can you patch the sensors? If we know the worst, we can prioritize."

Dr. Leyna: (Shouting over the wind) "I'm on it. But if the concrete toe goes, the energy will funnel straight into the lot. This is exactly what I'm talking about — structural fixes without living buffers fail differently. They fail violently."

Celine arrives, coat flapping, a municipal tablet in one hand and a microphone stuck to her collar by a liaison. Her composed face reads like a pressed ledger; her voice is practiced calm.

Celine Harrow: "We have emergency funds activated. The council will convene to release more resources for immediate defense and post-storm relief."

You: (You say quietly) "The covenants require inspections and maintenance — local oversight. Did the enforcement team follow through on the permits for this berm?"

Celine Harrow: (She looks at you, the expression unreadable) "We had audits scheduled. There were procurement delays. We also released contingency funds as the first responders called them in."

Her words are precise; there is comfort in the procedure, but precision doesn't carry people up onto high ground.

You: "That delay cost the west cove their margin. The covenant isn't retrospective, Celine. It doesn't fix what was never enforced."

Celine Harrow: (A pause. Then) "No single policy can be perfect. We bound the town legally. That buys us leverage. That is governance."

Ivo: (Anger under his breath) "It buys us leverage for those who read and can pay. It's weak where someone's got to be watching mud slide into a foundation at two in the morning."

The exchange sharpens the edges of what you all already knew: law is a scaffolding; enforcement is the hands on the beams. The storm will reveal which hands were there.

You find yourself shouting instructions, hugging a child whose name you learn at the shelter, her hair smelling of sea and soap. You check the tablet: sensors in covenanted sites show expected dampening trends; non-covenanted ones spike. It is small solace.

[Scene: Harbor | Dawn — The Morning After the Break]
[Visual: The harbor is a tableau of aftermath: half-submerged crates, a tilted lamp post, buoyant tangles of netting; between wreckage, stubborn green shoots and intact covenanted terraces hold a quieter line]
[Music: Sparse piano, low cello — a slow elegy]
[Sound: Water sluicing, distant hammers, a single gull that keeps calling]
[Smell: Wet wood, diesel, and the sharp, clean scent of newly exposed silt]

You walk the harbor's edge at first light. The town is waking into a new geometry of losses and holds. Volunteers move with quiet concentration, hauling, patching, counting. Leyna kneels by a monitoring buoy, fingers blue and steady. Maya hands out steaming cups from a battered urn, her face ash-streaked but stubborn.

Dr. Leyna: "Where covenants were followed, the biotic layers reduced wave energy measurably. Those homes saved by that margin face repairs, not relocation. Where enforcement lapsed, the damage is deeper."

You: "How many households did the covenants spare?"

Dr. Leyna: (She taps the tablet, the numbers like small pricks of light) "Enough that we'll quantify a legal precedent. Not enough to mend everything."

Maya sits beside you on a salt-crusted step, offering a cup. She looks younger today, a strip of paint across one cheek.

Maya: "You did what you could, Alys. You pushed for covenants because you wanted both the money and the care. But people got hurt."

You hold the cup and feel its heat. Your hands smell of salt and damp cloth.

Oba Kofi joins, his cane leaving neat impressions in the wet sand. He looks out, not at the water, but at the faces around him. He speaks softly.

Oba Kofi: "You chose to make law our ally, girl. Law is a weapon when wielded by everyone, but a toy if only a few hold it."

A hush falls. You think of the harbor bell: how it tolled for storms and for weddings and for the odd victory. Today it tolls for both grief and endurance.

Ivo approaches, his cap damp, the line of his jaw set like a cleat. He watches the volunteers, watches the shoreline, then you. His expression is complex — not simply anger or consolation, but a restiveness that sweeps between both.

Ivo: "We saved some. We lost some. We did what we could with what we had."

You: "Do you think it was the right path? The hybrid?"

Ivo: (He looks away, at the terraces holding their line) "Right isn't a single moment. It's a row of them. But I wanted easier edges, Alys. I wanted folks to see a wall and feel safer. You wanted a living shore. We —" He pauses, searching for an answer that isn't a concession or a triumph.

His silence says more than any argument could. You think of the sunsets you've shared and the nights of spreadsheets and the nights of laughter. You think of how the hybrid plan held in some places and failed in others, how the law was a rope thrown across a chasm that some people couldn't reach.

Celine emerges from the town square with her team, her face composed in the way of those who must speak with constituency and camera. She stops at the edge of the repair crews, surveying the panorama of work.

Celine Harrow: "We'll commission a full review. There will be reparations and targeted rebuilding funds. The covenants will be strengthened and enforcement will be prioritized."

You want to ask: prioritized by whom? You want to say: enforcement means people on the ground, not just paper. But you also know that political language is the lever that moves money and attention. You hear the ringing in your own head: the tug between an ideal and a practical clause.

You walk the pier, watching a child climb on a slipped buoy, the child laughing despite the fog. Small things endure.

Later, as the sun pushes higher, you stand at the waterline and let the wind press the dampness into your jacket. The town will rebuild. The law will be revised; the science will be refined. The covenants will become living tools, contested and fragile as any community resource. Some will be spared by this new architecture; others will pay the price because the scaffolding failed in place and time.

You think of the seasons ahead: slow plantings, months of monitoring, audits that will have teeth, and the social labor of making fairness as important as speed. You think of Ivo, and the lines between you that have shifted but are not erased. You think of Oba's cane marks in sand, of Maya's paint-streaked hands, Leyna's clipped scientific optimism, and Celine's careful, precise political will.

There is no neat, clean victory. There is an ending shaped by trade-offs: legal structures that can be improved, restoration techniques that show promise, enforced processes that must not forget the people they're meant to protect.

You breathe in the harbor air, tasting the salt and peat and iron of broken metal. The sunrise is neither triumphant nor cruel — merely honest, peeling light back over a town that is, stubbornly, still here.

You turn once more to look at the terraces and the lines of crews working, at the row of houses braced by volunteers, at the children splashing near a patch of eelgrass that survived. You let the weight of the morning sit with you: accountability will come in audits and in courtroom fights and in Monday meetings; restoration will come in seedlings and in patient hands; the town will remember who watched, who helped, and who left the gaps.

You put your hand over the brass compass at your chest. It is warm, and steady.

You step back from the water, and for a moment you allow yourself a small, private admission: this is not the ending you dreamed when the greenhouse first bloomed. It is not wholly defeat, either. It is a battered middle — a place for repair and for argued justice. You will work to make the laws hold people, not just paperwork. You will keep proving the science. You will, somehow, keep asking for the human trade-offs to be counted.

You are tired. You are resolute. You are not certain where the line between love and duty will lie tomorrow. But you are still here, and Saltwick is still here — neither ruined nor fully saved, but alive enough to continue.
