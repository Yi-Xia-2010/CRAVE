[Scene: New Embankment | Morning]

You stand at the edge of the crowd with the ribbon fluttering at your knees, the weight of the last week's signatures still warm in your palm. The embankment leans into the harbor like a promise made visible — salt-streaked, engineered, and strange against the old dunes. Nearby, children press faces to temporary barriers; someone hands out coffee in paper cups, steam curling like a small benediction.

The smell of the sea is sharp and honest: iodine, algae, the faint burnt tang of diesel from the workboats. Your jacket catches the wind; the braided fishing-line cord around your wrist bites familiar into your skin. You hear Ivo's laugh across the line and the flash of his high-visibility vest is a bright, human color in the crowd.

Celine Harrow steps forward to the microphone, her voice carrying with practiced cadence. You notice the digital cuff at her wrist catching the sunlight.

Celine Harrow: "This is what leadership looks like. We made hard choices, and today they begin to protect our town."

You: "We made them together." (You catch your breath and let your words ride out over the crowd.) "Not as concessions, but as the first steps in a longer work."

Celine's smile is polished; the applause rises like a tide rolling in polite applause. You feel the warmth of people's gratitude press into your ribs — relief, yes, but something steadier: a small, collective exhale.

Ivo squeezes your shoulder as you step down from the temporary platform, his grip warm, an anchored joke already on his tongue.

Ivo: "You sound like Oba's tide charts when they're finally right. Everyone's ready to sleep through the next surge."

You: (quiet) "I want them to."

Ivo: (half-laugh) "Then let's make sure that desire is backed by more than speeches."

Leyna appears beside you, tablet in hand, her face bright with that scientist's lightness you have learned to read as hope plus calculation.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "Preliminary readings from the test piers look good. The living modules are already reducing observable scour in the pilot zones."

Her tablet shows smooth lines, optimism turned into graphs that don't lie. You let yourself hold the graph like a talisman: data that matches the plan.

[Scene: New Embankment — Inspection | Midday]
[Visual: A closer, handheld-camera style shot of you walking the edge, boots on fresh sand and compacted gravel]
[Sound: Low gravel crunch underfoot, gulls complaining overhead]
[Music: Warm piano motifs — steady, curious]

You choose — or the day chooses for you — to walk the length of the newly poured works. Contractors linger in clusters, hands shaded against the sun, voices low. A stamped change-order binder sits propped on a pallet: not a secret, but not an invitation either. You do not assume its contents; you approach with the same methodical care you bring to a survey transect.

You trace the embankment's engineered seams with your eyes. Leyna joins you, leaning in close enough to look at the same spots, fingers folded over her tablet.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "We asked for silt curtains at every outflow during construction. I want to see the deployment notes."

You: "And the mattress layering? The compaction rates?"

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "On the plans, yes. On the surface notes... there are discrepancies."

You feel the word like a stone dropped into a quiet harbor. Discrepancies. Not yet accusations, but an unsteady ripple.

From the harbor, a foreman calls a greeting that is more habit than warmth. You step closer; a weathered contractor wipes his hands on his vest and gestures at a seam.

Contractor: "We had to shift a few things to hold the schedule. Tides moved in earlier than expected."

You: (careful) "Shifted what?"

Contractor: (shrugging) "Alternative materials in some armor units. Different bedding. We boxed it up in change orders."

Your hands itch for the binder. You resist the leap to judgment; instead you press for traceable facts.

You: "Show me the change orders."

He flips the pages with a casual professionalism, as if this is routine. His list contains stamps, initials, signatures — a paper trail. But when your finger follows the line items you notice that some of the mitigation measures you and Leyna prioritized are marked as 'deferred' or 'field-adjusted'.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: (soft, with a scientist's pained clarity) "Field-adjusted can mean many things. Sometimes it's smart adaptation. Sometimes it's compromise under pressure."

Ivo crosses his arms, jaw working. He has the practical mind of someone who measures risk in nights of sleep and calls to neighbors at two a.m. His eyes flick from the binder to the embankment and then to you, the question folded into a glance.

Ivo: "If it's a cosmetic change, fine. If it's cutting corners where the sea will test it — we can't afford that."

You: "We can't afford to hide it either."

Ivo: "So what do you want to do?"

You taste the word that has been with you since you signed the contract: responsibility. It hums through the braided cord on your wrist.

[Scene: Harrow Hall | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The council chamber's long table, papers spread like tides; late sun slants through high windows]
[Music: A hopeful swell — strings and soft brass]
[Sound: The distant harbor bell, a clerk's page turning]

The Harrow Hall meeting room smells faintly of coffee and varnish, of decisions that have been made and will be made again. You carry the binder like evidence and like a burden, placing it on the polished wood between you and the mayor's seat. The community's faces are not all here; only a few representatives — Maya, Oba, and two councilors — but it feels enough: a small, decisive audience.

Celine sits opposite you, hands folded, her expression smooth and, for reasons you have learned to name carefully, unreadable. She listens as though weighing sound and consequence on the same pair of scales.

Celine Harrow: (measured) "You brought this instead of the celebration. That says something about priorities."

You: "It says we're still accountable. This embankment was meant to be a model of how we protect people without hollowing out the habitat we rely on."

Celine Harrow: (soft) "I agree with the balance. We all wanted this finished before the season. But we also needed contracts that made the work possible."

There is an ache in the word "possible" — the lives and livelihoods compressed into bureaucratic realities. You do not assume Celine's motives; her face holds something complex, something you've seen in other leaders under pressure: a mix of conviction and compromise.

Oba Kofi: (voice like weathered wood) "When I look at the line between land and sea, I think of many hands. If corners were cut, tell us so we can mend. If it was necessary, tell us why it was necessary."

Maya: (defiant, younger and quick to the point) "We didn't come back to watch our home turned into a headline and then disappear under another tide."

You: "I don't want to undermine the protection we have in place. I want to know the degree of risk — either to ask for repair or to ask for oversight."

Leyna lays a slim tablet between you and Celine, numbers glowing softly.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "Some deviations are benign. Some increase long-term scour by a margin that matters to seagrass regrowth. I can't certify these as equivalent to the specs without auditing records and some quick in-situ tests."

Celine's eyes flick to the tablet, then back to you. Her reaction is careful and complex; you leave the exact reading of it unread in your own mind, because the town's future can't hinge on supposition.

Celine Harrow: "We can bring auditors in," she says slowly. "But the financing schedules are tight. Pausing could mean losing contractors and public confidence."

Ivo's jaw tightens. He speaks from the place of immediate, visible protection.

Ivo: "If pausing now means people feel abandoned while water keeps creeping in, we'll lose more than trust. We'll lose houses."

You: (pressing) "If we let unknown deviations stand, we may be offering people a false sense of safety. That's a different kind of betrayal."

The room holds those two words — "false sense of safety" — like a current between them.

Celine Harrow: "What outcome do you want, Alys? Delay and audits, quicker close with oversight later, or a public airing that could become political?"

Your chest is a ledger of obligations, each entry written in a different ink: your science, your love for this place, the faces of families sleeping easier now.

Leyna: "I can expedite an audit, but it will still take time. We can also do conditional certifications — verify critical points and leave non-critical work to finish under supervision."

Maya: "Time is hands and fuel. If this slows everything, we pay in different ways. But if it's unsafe, we could be paying forever."

Oba: "A safe town should feel like a safe town. People need to trust what they can see and sleep because of it."

Ivo: (to you, low) "You know how the town will read any misstep. But you also know what we sleep on when the storms come."

Your internal monologue turns inward, tracing the braided line of what could be gained and what could be lost. You imagine the ribbon, the scissors, the children's faces — all the small certainties that have been stitched together to reach this day. The mood around you is not accusation but a call: steward this, or watch the stewardship unravel.

[Visual: The chamber window frames a sky alive with the returning pink you saw when you first came home; lanterns blink to life along the harbor]

The day's light pools and softens. Outside, the harbor breathes its own rhythm: boats bob, lanterns wink, and the embankment stands like a newly learned phrase in a community's language. Inside, the conversation stiffens into a single unignorable hinge: verify — finish — or reveal. Each path will demand different kinds of courage.

You imagine what each choice will ask: the audits that force bureaucratic patience, the completed embankment that secures sleep now, the leak that could open a painful but necessary public reckoning. You remember why you returned: not to be right, but to keep people and place in a balance that honors both.

You feel the current of the town in the room — in Oba's steady gaze, in Maya's fire, in Leyna's careful insistence, in Ivo's blunt urgency, and in Celine's complex composure. Your responsibility folds across each of them like a map.

Your hand finds your notebook and your compass. The braided cord at your wrist is steady. The sea beyond the window is not waiting; it will test whatever you choose.
