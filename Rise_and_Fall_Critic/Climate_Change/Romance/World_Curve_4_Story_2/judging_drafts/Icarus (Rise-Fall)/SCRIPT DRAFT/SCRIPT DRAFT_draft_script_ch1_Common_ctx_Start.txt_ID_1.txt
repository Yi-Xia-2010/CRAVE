[Scene: Crescent Docks | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the crescent-shaped harbor as a low sun slices between cloud banks; weathered docks lead into brassy light]
[Music: Warm, tentative strings with a hint of oceanic percussion]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the creak of rope, a low tide gurgle against pilings]

You step off the bus with the weight of years folded into the pack at your shoulder and the compact field notebook already warm from being clutched. The air hits you first — a salt-and-silt slap that smells like old rope, diesel, and something green pushing up from the shallows. Your boots map the dock's familiar give; the wood still remembers the names you grew up saying.

The harbor is both the same and subtly different. Nets hang patched and bright with new cord. A stack of salvaged oyster cages bears a fresh coat of paint. Between the warehouses, patches of rehabilitated seagrass glitter where sunlight meets water — pale, tentative, like the first teeth of a new spring. You feel a small, steady bloom of something in your chest: relief, or maybe the beginning of hope.

You tuck your field notebook deeper into your jacket and breathe it in: the harbor, the work, the possibility. This is why you came back.

Maya is at the end of the dock, sleeve dusted with primer, painting a mural of a long green frond curling across a warehouse door. She looks up when she hears you approach; the smile that breaks across her face makes your throat seize the same way it used to when you were twelve and she stole the last slice of pie.

Maya: "Hey, stranger. You look like the kind of person who slept with a compass under their pillow."

You: "Only when storms were forecast. Old habits die slow." (You rub the braid at the nape of your neck, feeling the weight of the braided cord bracelet there — a minor, grounding ritual.)

Maya laughs, a bright, rasping sound. She steps closer and takes you in with quick, practical eyes. The mural is more than paint; it's the town's story in pigment — nets, a lighthouse, a green ribbon representing the seagrass.

Maya: "We saved you a corner. Figured you'd want to sign the stewardship pledge in paint."

You: "Stewardship in mural form — I can do that." (You glance at the horizon where the Glass Marsh catches the sun and turns the sea into a sheet of pewter that somehow reflects gold.) "How's Oba? And the volunteers? Did the council finally agree to fund the seed plots?"

Maya's expression flattens for a beat, then rearranges into the steady optimism she reserves for you. She shrugs, half apology, half challenge.

Maya: "Oba's as stubborn as the tide. Volunteers are coming back — kids who left and then came back with better lunchboxes. Council… Celine's been talking to people. She's moving money around, making promises. You know how she does."

At the name, your shoulders tighten just a fraction. Celine Harrow — a woman in a coat that always seems to be ironed by political winds — has been a presence on Saltwick's edges since the last elections. You haven't yet met her in person, but in the months of phone calls and grant drafts she has loomed: precise, pragmatic, making efficient-sounding bargains.

You don't respond directly. Instead, you let your gaze travel across the harbor where Ivo is working with Ronan, measuring a length of replacement piling. He looks up as you speak and for a second the world narrows to the set of his jaw and the way his hazel eyes soften. He runs a hand through his hair and tucks a stray strand back beneath the canvas cap.

Ivo: "Alys. You kept the compass."

You: "It would be difficult to do the work without it." (It's half true. More than the brass and dent, it's a promise — to your father, to the place.)

Ivo walks over, boots slapping the dock, carrying a coil of planed wood like it's nothing. He sets it down between you and the edge of the harbor and leans in as if resuming a conversation that never really paused.

Ivo: "We've got a crew for the weekend. Ronan's already rallied a few folks from the boatyard. Oba sent word that he'd bring old tide markers; he's proposing an oral-mapping walk tomorrow."

You feel the pragmatic rhythm of his words: immediate, action-first. It comforts and irritates you in the same heartbeat.

You: "Good. We'll need the mapping. But the seed plots need careful placement — too shallow and the shoots fry in summer heat, too deep and they don't anchor. If we edge the restoration with living breakwaters, we can buy time."

Ivo: (He smiles, a quick, boyish thing that betrays the weight beneath his hands.) "Living breakwaters—sounds poetic. Also expensive. We can shore some houses this season with quick barriers if that's what it takes to keep families from losing a harvest."

The exchange opens onto familiar territory: your future-focused science, his present-focused protection. Neither of you draws a line in the sand. Both of you know the shore has already moved.

Oba Kofi arrives then — bent, cane tapping a rhythm against the boards. His face is a map: tide-lines of laughter and storms. People give him a little room as he passes; respect is automatic here.

Oba Kofi: "Alys." (He says your name like a tide calling its child home.) "The sea remembers you."

You: "So do I, Oba." (You step forward enough to let him catch your forearm; his grip is surprising for its strength.) "Will you help with the mapping?"

Oba Kofi: "I will show you where the eelgrass used to run thick enough to hide a child's head." (He squints toward the Glass Marsh.) "We will listen to the mud. The town will listen if you tell it the sea's true story."

There is a hush in the conversation when Celine Harrow steps out from between the warehouses. Even from a distance her presence trims the air — navy coat, pearl lapel pin catching the last sun like a small moon. She is not loitering; this is a visit with intent. She offers a crisp smile that doesn't reach her eyes, and the crowd parts just enough.

Celine Harrow: "Alys Maren." (Her voice is measured.) "Welcome home. The council requested we coordinate on restoration efforts. Funds are being reallocated to emergency protections and, pending environmental assessments, to longer-term projects."

You sense the diplomacy — the praise and the caveat wrapped in the same sentence.

You: "Celine. Thank you. I think the town needs both: protective works to get through the season and living systems that last."

Celine's eyes flick to Ivo, then to Oba, calculating. There's a tightness around her mouth.

Celine Harrow: "We have to be pragmatic. People need roofs and harvests now. We can't beg nature for time we don't have."

Ivo's expression tightens at that; you can smell the tension like static.

Ivo: "And we'll make sure they get them. But we also have to make sure those roofs last. Band-aids won't stop the next storm."

Celine: (A small laugh.) "Of course. That's why coordination is important. We can fund a hybrid pilot — quick defenses adjacent to funded seagrass beds. It keeps both lines of work visible and accountable."

You register the offer as both an olive branch and a leash. It could be a useful compromise or a way to pace the restoration so slowly it never gains traction. But her presence here, in public, also signals something else: the town is watching. The political machinery is here and, for now, listening.

You: "A pilot would be welcome. We can design it so the short-term barriers support the long-term beds. I want the data to show the trade-offs."

Celine: "Excellent. Dr. Ortiz will coordinate the environmental assessment with your team." (She inclines her head politely.) "We can make an announcement at the hall next week."

Oba snorts softly, amused and skeptical. Maya's jaw tightens. Ivo watches Celine with a careful neutrality that smells like a man measuring wind.

You: (Quietly, to Ivo) "She moves fast."

Ivo: (Softly) "She has to. Or Saltwick disappears."

There is a rhythm in the words — urgency measured against the longer patience you champion. The harbor warms around you with the low hum of people trading tools and small gossip. The small, important things are back in motion.

[Scene: Harbor Walkway toward Old Lighthouse | Golden Hour]
[Visual: The path narrows; the Glass Marsh is a silvery smear in the near-distance. The sun tilts, casting warm edges.]
[Music: Solo piano, hopeful uplift]
[Sound: The rustle of reeds, the creak of a greenhouse hinge in the wind]

You peel off from the hub and follow Ivo along the harbor toward the lighthouse. Conversation threads between you tighten into something more private, but the noise of the town — laughter, shouted coordinates, Ronan's whoop — remains a comforting background.

Ivo: "We set up the first nursery trays at the rooftop greenhouse. Leyna sent starter nutrient mixes from the lab. You should see the seedlings."

You: (A small, almost childish thrill.) "Seedlings. Those letters are my favorite kind of census."

Ivo grins and steals a small poke at your shoulder. That small closeness — the familiarity — feels like stepping into a warm room after being out in the cold. You haven't given the town your whole plan yet. But here, with sawdust on his palms and a smudge of dried mud along his wrist, you see what you fought to protect: people who will work, who will risk ended afternoons of fishing to learn to plant.

As you climb the lighthouse path, the wind shifts. The Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse is a box of glass perched above the cliffs — its panes stained with salt but shining with life inside. Warmth pours around the door. The smell changes instantly from wet harbor to something like greenhouse breath: damp earth, a sweet hint of seedlings, and the citrus of potting compost.

[Visual: Interior of the Rooftop Greenhouse — rows of seedlings in trays under glass; the light is golden, the air humid]
[Sound: Soft drip of condensation and the faint hum of a compact fan]

Maya brushes your sleeve as she passes, carrying a tray of excavated shell fragments for a substrate mix. Ronan is excitedly comparing notes with a volunteer about planting depth. Oba moves slowly along the racks, fingers hovering over individual shoots as if recounting names.

Oba Kofi: (Softly) "They are small. They will be strong."

You kneel beside a tray, fingertips finding a damp ridge in the soil. The seedlings are fragile, luminous — roots like pale scripts. You feel a surge of tender, fierce protectiveness. This is work you can hold in your hands; the timelines you can measure in inches and weeks instead of political quarters.

You: "If we stagger the plots, we can hedge against storm windows. Some beds deeper, some closer to the reef lines. Leyna's hydrodynamic models will help us pick the sweet spots."

Maya: "And we train people — school kids, fishers, anyone with willing hands. It's easier to trust what you can touch."

Ronan: (Bursting in) "And we can get volunteers to map trash lines on the beach — keep the new beds clean. I trained a couple of kids to use the app."

The team chatter pulls taut with optimism. You hear in it not just action but a kind of collective belief: that restoration is a shared language.

Ivo watches you as you speak, his gaze steady, half measuring, half marveling. There's something unspoken between you — a tether formed by shared work. You can feel the question in his eyes: will you keep the town's future as a shared thing or turn it into a ledger of responsibilities?

You: "We make this a community project. We measure, teach, iterate. Fast wins won't stick unless people own them."

Ivo: "I like that. Ownership beats promises any day. But some houses won't wait for ownership. We'll need temporary walls at North Beach Ridge, and your beds by the reef."

You: (A small smile.) "Then we build both."

Celine's voice floats up from below — a snippet of a conversation with a councilwoman about grant logistics. It's distant but present; a reminder that there are layers to this work. You don't like the idea of someone rewriting the plan for expediency, but you also know that any funding she secures could mean the difference between months of survival and another season of vanished harvests.

The greenhouse buzzes with cooperative energy. You move among people, offering small corrections and big encouragements. There is pride here — not the single-minded kind, but the communal kind that comes from shared struggle.

Ivo finds you near a tray of older shoots, brushing a scab of algae from a leaf. He leans beside you, yet keeps his hands occupied. There's no grand confession, only the steady hand of someone who knows your rhythms.

Ivo: "Do you remember when we tried to plant those winter beds and half of them floated off?"

You: (A laugh, thin with memory.) "We learned about buoyancy the exhausting way."

Ivo: "We learned together." (He taps a finger to the tray, a private punctuation.)

You feel something like a promise in that small gesture. The town is fragile and the stakes are high, but little victories are stacking: seedlings in trays, a weekend crew assembling, Oba's knowledge returning to maps, Celine's funds — however conditional — flowing into the system.

[Music swells slightly — hopeful, like the beginning of a tide]

You stand in the warm humidity of the greenhouse and trace with your mind the next few weeks: a mapping walk with Oba, a pilot placement for living breakwaters, Leyna's assessments, a council announcement. It's a chain of small acts that could push Saltwick away from the edge.

For the first time since you stepped off the bus, the weight in your chest eases into work-shaped purpose. Saltwick is not healed, but it is healing. The town is putting its hands to it.

You open your notebook and write a single line at the top of the page: "Seed — Measure — Teach — Protect." It feels less like a plan and more like a promise.

The sun finally breaks through the cloud bank in a narrow band, lighting the greenhouse panes with a fevered gold. The sea beyond the glass is steel, but along the horizon there is a ribbon of pink that reads — unmistakably — like the first light of a day that could be different.

You look at your team, at Ivo, at Maya, at Oba. They meet your eyes and there is an answering warmth — not certainty, but commitment.

It is enough to start.

[Sound: The wind picks up; somewhere, the town's bell rings once — a calling bell for the next shift]

You feel the chapter of return closing yet understand another is opening — one of small triumphs, tests, and the slow building of resilience.
