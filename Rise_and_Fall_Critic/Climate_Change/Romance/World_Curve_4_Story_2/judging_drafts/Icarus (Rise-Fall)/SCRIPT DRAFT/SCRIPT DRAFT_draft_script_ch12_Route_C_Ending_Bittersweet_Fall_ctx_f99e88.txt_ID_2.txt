[Scene: Glass Marsh | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of the marsh—mirror-smooth pools now churned into brown cloud; a ragged line of foam stains the reeds]
[Music: Sparse strings, low and hollow]
[Sound: Gulls calling frantic, the soft slap of water against wooden pilings; somewhere, a distant diesel thrum]

You come up onto the boardwalk and the air hits you like a bruise. What should have been the soft, saline breath of morning smells of rot and silt — a wet, metallic tang that sits at the back of your throat. The marsh looks wrong; the refraction of light has been drowned in suspended grey. Thin filaments of seagrass, ragged and coated in a gritty paste, cling to half-submerged pilings like the last stubborn flags of a surrendered town.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz waits with a tablet held in both hands, her jaw set in a line you know means numbers are worse than words. Ronan paces, sleeves rolled, mud flecks on his knuckles. Maya is quiet, hands balled into the pockets of her paint-splattered jacket, eyes tracking the water as if it might offer up a reason.

You step forward. Your boots sink into the boardwalk ; the boards are slick with salt and silt and the scrape of a boot sounds louder than it should.

Dr. Leyna: "The turbidity spike—ten times the normal reading. Look." (She turns the tablet toward you. The graph is a harsh, jagged cliff.) "Dredge plumes. They hit the beds last night."

You swallow the thought that it was worse than you feared. You had mapped the vulnerability. You had argued for buffer zones. You had marked the places the dredge routes could not cross. Those maps feel suddenly theoretical, a clean thing that had no answer for the churn of a mechanical scoop in the dark.

You: "Who authorized the pass? Who—" Your voice tightens. It's not just science on the line; it's everything that keeps the town steady. Nets. Nurseries. The memory of summers when you and Maya ran along these edges barefoot.

Ronan: (spitting out a curse) "They said it was a clearance for channel maintenance. The crew—some of them worked with Ivo's volunteers last summer." (He rubs his face with mud-slick fingers.) "I don't know if they're contractors or municipal crews."

Dr. Leyna: (slow) "The plume came from the outer channel. Sediment settled across the pilot mats and the seed banks. Mortality's already showing in the deeper roots." (She taps the screen until layers of sea-vegetation models ripple.) "We can try to suction and replant, but not if the turbidity stays elevated. The rhizomes suffocated."

You press your palm against the railing. The brass compass at your chest feels like an accusation.

You: "We start triage. We document. We get samples to the lab. We—" Your words arrive in a jagged line. The plan you had—that patient, community-first scaling, the living breakwaters and slow accrual of habitat—looks like it has been smothered while the town slept.

Maya: (quiet) "Neighbors are already on the message boards. People think the dredging was to 'clear' for some incoming deal. They want names. They want someone to pay." Her fingers tangle in the braid at the edge of her sleeve. "Dad used to say you don't fight nature with shortcuts, Aly. He said you build with it. People remember that."

Ivo arrives then, boots heavy, face set. He doesn't look at you with accusation, not exactly; it's something more complicated—relief wrapped in strained patience, like a rope pulled taut.

Ivo: "We can cordon this. Pumps, temporary silt curtains. Ronan, get the spare suction unit. Leyna, we run assays; we need to know if the seed viability is dead or dormant."

You note that Ivo's voice is already moving toward solutions. Fast, practical — the way it always had been. Your chest tightens because part of you has been asking, for weeks, for time, for careful measures that honor the biology. Part of you hears, too, the immediate protective muscle in his response. That muscle is why people follow him when storms come.

You: (low) "Curtains might hold a while. But this is extensive. If the beds are compromised, the nursery collapse will follow in months."

Ivo: (measured) "Then we buy months. We do what we can now, and we figure the rest out. We don't let the town panic."

Ronan: "Buy months how, though? Volunteers can stand waist-deep for a while, but we don't have the equipment for wide-scale suction. Not without funding."

Dr. Leyna: "And even with suction, if silt is wet and compacted, the rhizomes could be severed below the point of regrowth."

The conversation unspools into the cold morning. Multiturn exchanges — arguments of methods and implications — thread through your mind like barbed wire. You try to hold to calm, to parse the science from the politics, but the community is already mobilizing rumor and grief. The Harbor's message boards light up with photos, with people accusing in capital letters. Someone posts a video of a private dredge cutter slicing through night water. The mayor's office releases a statement that sounds practiced and thin.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Midday]
[Visual: Close shots of volunteer crews assembling silt curtains; pamphlets flap on a table; a line of faces waiting for info]
[Music: Dissonant piano, restrained but insistent]
[Sound: Murmur of voices, the slap of tarpaulin, the distant thump of a tracking boat]

By noon the marsh is ringed with people—some in vests, some in the plain clothes of those who have lost a season's work. Maya organizes a rota for the volunteers with a calm no one else quite reproduces; she speaks like someone making art of crisis. You make lists on the fly: sample chains, who will call the press, who will hold town meetings. There is a kernel of your old control that flickers alive when you are given work.

A man you don't know stands at the edge of the crowd and shouts that the town council approved an exemption. Someone else retorts that the exemption was for harbor clearance, not ecological disruption. Accusations scatter like gulls.

A small knot of elders gathers—Oba Kofi among them in your memory though he is not present now—and you imagine their faces: the way generations catch and hold blame when the sea moves.

Ronan: (to you) "Some of the fishermen are saying it's deliberate. That the dredging path was shifted. If that's true, this isn't negligence. It's a decision."

You: (softly) "We document. If it's a decision, we need proof. We can't save anything with vendettas. Not today."

Ronan: (bitter laugh) "You can say that because you're not the one who loses nets this fall."

The line between method and justice narrows into a rut that no bridge seems wide enough to cross. You can feel the town's grief hardening into something angrier.

[Scene: Harbor | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The harbor under a low, gunmetal sky; the water is a flat pewter sheet dotted with boats riding lower in the water]
[Music: Slow cello, a steady, descending motif]
[Sound: Wharf bells, distant engines cutting out, the whisper of ropes]

You follow the thread of complaint to the docks and the conversation cracks open into something raw. Families whose harvests feed the market stand beside engineers in vests. The air tastes like rain and complaint.

Ivo works the line between hands and talkfulness, doing his best to calm a woman who says, through tears, that her child's allowance money comes from shrimp sales that might not return. You step beside him, and for a moment your shoulders find a rhythm together. Then he says, quietly, to you:

Ivo: "We can't let this become a blame feast. If people fight each other, no one will fix the beds."

You: (brittle) "And if we don't find the cause and hold it accountable, we let the damage repeat."

He looks at you, and the expression is unreadable. There is fatigue there, and something else—defensiveness? resignation? You cannot tell, and you do not want to presume. The lack of clarity sits between you like a damp blanket.

Dr. Leyna: (approaching, voice exhausted) "Listen to the data. We have to triage based on viability, not on who to blame first. We need to conserve oxygen in the water column, clear the worst of the silt, and start replanting in pockets that still have living rhizomes."

Maya: (interrupting, sharp) "While we do that, what about people's rent? What about the shop on Harbor Lane that will close if they don't sell fish this season? People need a plan that is not just biological on paper."

The tension splits into threads: ecological, economic, social. All of them tug at you. You see Ronan moving between groups, trying to ferry people toward practical tasks, but his energy is fraying.

You: (to the crowd) "We will do a coordinated mobilization. Leyna — give me the list of priority zones. Ivo — we need equipment. Ronan — you coordinate volunteers. Maya — community liaisons. I'll talk to the council and press for emergency relief."

A woman in the back shouts, "And if the council was complicit?" The question thrums. Someone else calls for a halt to all dredging until proper oversight occurs. A few nod. A few glare.

Ivo steps forward and raises a hand, not to silence but to steady. "We need to keep channels open for safety," he says. "We can't shut them down without a contingency for boats in storms. But we can demand oversight on methods and routes."

You: (to Ivo, quietly) "And what if oversight is too slow?"

Ivo: (hard) "Then we force speed. We get the monitors out, put crews on site. We don't let the sea take everything because we argued about process."

There is a kernel of truth in his words. Immediate responses can save lives. But immediate fixes can also entrench poor policy.

[Scene: Glass Marsh — Edge of Night]
[Visual: The marsh in low blue twilight; a few LED lamps cast tired cones of light; a line of volunteers replanting by headlamp]
[Music: A low sustained note with occasional, small harp glints—hope threaded through sorrow]
[Sound: The scrape of rakes, soft murmurs, occasional sobs held in the dark]

Night comes early, and the work goes on. You go back to the lab to run the final assays. Leyna's face is pinched; the results mirror what you saw in the water. The decline is deep; the models show partial recovery in pockets but a wholesale loss in others.

Dr. Leyna: (softly) "This will take years. We can rebuild patches, but the connectivity is gone. Larval settlement will be reduced. The shore will be more exposed for a long time."

You: "And the people who rely on the shore will have to get by until the beds come back. Some won't weather it."

Leyna: "We can increase survival with targeted planting and shading to reduce silt resuspension. But we need funding, and we need community buy-in to do this at scale."

You: (turning the idea over) "We have volunteer muscle. We do not have enough money."

Leyna: "Then the fight will be political, too. Science isn't the only lever."

You think of Celine's offers and the municipal powers that promised infrastructure. You think of the night routes of dredges, of messages that slid through midnight council channels. You think of the maps you made and how they were still, somehow, not enough.

Ivo comes into the lab doorway and doesn't speak for a long moment. His hands smell of diesel and wet rope. He looks at the slides on Leyna's screen like a man reading a ledger that lists people rather than numbers.

Ivo: "We can rebuild what we can. We can fill the gaps with artificial reefs if we have to. But someone needs to protect the people in the meantime."

You: "Protection for houses or for the ecosystem? We can't have one without risking the other."

Ivo: (bitter smile) "Isn't that always the question? It's what keeps us awake."

The conversation circles and circles, each phrase a stone cast into a pool whose edges have been whittled away. You feel the town pulling into factions: those who want hard barriers and money now; those who swear by living systems and patient repair. Somewhere between them your relationships tense like wire.

Maya finds you after midnight on the lab steps. She sits on the lip of the platform and tucks her knees to her chest.

Maya: "Do you think we did enough?" Her voice is small — not for the town, but for herself.

You: (after a pause) "We did everything we could with what we had when we knew about it. Before. After... I don't know." You feel the weight of the "if" in your chest. If only we'd pushed harder. If only we'd been loud enough. If only.

Maya: "People are saying it's the mayor's fault. Some of the younger ones say it's time to break the system."

You imagine Oba's face, the long memory of the elders, and realize the fracture is not just ecological but generational and political. The marsh is a wound and the town is pressing at it with fingers that now sting.

[Scene: Harbor — Dawn Two Days Later]
[Visual: A low, bruised sunrise over a scarred harbor; a few small clumps of green stand out like stubborn teeth against mud]
[Music: A single, mournful violin; steady, controlled]
[Sound: The hum of portable pumps, soft voices, the distant sound of a council gavel in an audio feed]

The town holds a meeting by the ferry shed. People stand packed close, mouths hard, hands cupped around coffee cups. The council livestream flickers and then steadies. You are asked to speak.

You step forward and the boards creak under your weight. The brass compass at your chest feels unusually heavy.

You: (voice steady, because you have to be) "We have loss. We have data that shows siltation from last night's dredging affected large swaths of seed beds. We are mobilizing to triage, to replant, and to protect the people who will be harmed this season."

A few in the crowd clap, some scowl. Someone calls for resignations. Someone else applauds the clarity of the plan.

You take a breath and, for a moment, lay the science out like a map of harm and possible recovery. Leyna stands beside you, adding specifics. Ronan hands out lists of volunteer shifts. Maya speaks of relief for families.

Then someone calls out: "And what about accountability? Who made the call to let them run the cutter through the channel that night?"

You feel the room tilt. This is the question that will not be answered in one meeting.

You finish, knowing words will not be enough. The plan is set; the work will begin. But the fabric of trust in the town has been rubbed raw.

After the meeting, people disperse into conversations that will not be easily mended. You stand among them, and Ivo joins you. There is no dramatic reconciliation—only two people who have always operated on different clocks, now both slowed by the same fatigue.

Ivo: (low) "We did what we could. We kept people safe tonight, didn't we?"

You: "We kept some people safe. We couldn't save every bed."

Ivo: (looking past you toward the marsh) "Some in the harbor think we should have pushed harder earlier. Others think we shouldn't have let any dredging happen at all."

You: "And who gets to decide the right time to act? Who pays the cost of being right on principle when people's roofs come off?"

Ivo: (exhales) "When do we start picking a side? Today, people are picking. It's not just about practices anymore."

The look he gives you is unreadable, layered; you do not reach for a simple reading. You both know that this moment will separate more than it will unify. The town needs both protection and restoration, and the price will be more than plans can count.

You turn back to the marsh one last time. In a shallow pool, an almost-imperceptible sprig of green pushes through a film of silt. It is small, ridiculous, stubborn.

You think about the beds that broke and the people who will break with them if the work becomes only a ledger. You think about promises you made—Seed — Measure — Teach — Protect—and how promises look to people when they are not enough.

Night after night, you will walk this shoreline and plant where you can. You will write the reports and hold the meetings and hand out schedules and tell people that there is a future worth working for. But there will also be absences: some nets will not be mended in time; some livelihoods will be lost; some relationships will fray under the calculus of triage.

You breathe in the salt and the silt and let the grief sit. There is no neat victory in this dusk. You can only keep moving, keep making the small interventions that become the only way forward.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand in the mud, thumb pressed into a small, green shoot]
[Music: A single, lingering minor chord]

You stand at the edge of the work, at the edge of politics, at the edge of a relationship stretched by differing answers to an impossible question: what do you protect first when everything is at risk?

You look at the town — at Leyna's tired persistence, Ronan's grim hands, Maya's steadying presence, Ivo's complex gaze — and you feel the fracture both in the marsh and in the community.

You cannot make it whole with a single motion.

The night the beds broke has left a hush in the town, a silence full of decisions that will be made in rooms where not everyone is listening. The work you had promised yourself — and the people — is now both rescue and reckoning.
