[Scene: Cliff-top | Dawn]
[Visual: A wide shot of the coastline — engineered terraces step down the slope like a careful staircase; beyond them, the sea is a washed pewter. Low sun leaks gold across damp sand and torn nets. Salt-sprayed grass glints with frost of recent rain.]

You stand at the lip of the cliff with the brass compass warm against your sternum. The decision you made in Harrow Hall — to accept Celine's municipal funds tied with ecological covenants — is a muscle you have had to use every day since. It looks different at a distance: terraces that hold a tide, rows of planted mats where seagrass can take root, concrete buttresses scored with community names. Some of it works. Some of it didn't.

Salt and diesel smoke ride the cold air together. The engineered terraces below are scarred, some edges chewed by storm surge; other sections are generous green, stitched with new blades of seagrass that sway in slow choreography. You smell damp peat, wet rope, and the faint, metallic tang of municipal paint.

You let your hand fall to the braided cord at your wrist, feeling the worn loop of memory and promise. The cord is the same resistance as the covenant you signed: a binding, both comforting and heavy.

Oba Kofi comes up behind you with less hurry than the gulls but with the same inevitability. He leans on his carved cane and breathes the sea in like a benediction.

Oba Kofi: "The terraces hold where the stone sits true. The plants grow where the earth is given a chance. But not everywhere, eh?"

You: "Not everywhere. We had to pick — and someone else picked where the money went. Covenant language kept the living mats in the estuary, but the contract allowed for emergency hardening in the harbor, too. Some people took that as permission to privatize whatever shore they could." (You look at him; your voice is small and precise, like a scalpel.)

Oba Kofi: (He folds his hands, eyes on the terraces.) "The law is a net. Sometimes it catches fish. Sometimes it lets the crabs slip through. It will take years for the net to mend what it breaks."

There is tenderness in his words, but also a warning. His face is a map of storms and patience. You watch a group below — Ronan among them, sleeves rolled, hauling new mats into shallow water. They move with stubborn grace.

[Visual: Cut to close-up on a patch of seagrass where a juvenile fish darts; the camera lingers on the pulse of small life]
[Music: Strings soften, then hold]

You think of the houses on North Beach Ridge — the block where Maya grew up — and the ones that aren't there anymore because someone misread covenant clauses and built a private bulkhead that starved the next property of sediment. You think of Celine's smile the night the funds were agreed to and how it folded into a different expression when enforcement files lagged in municipal hands.

You breathe. The day will demand more than counting what worked.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Morning]

Dr. Leyna Ortiz stands beside a rack of seedlings, tablet in hand. Her eyes are bright, but the lines at their corners are tired from data, from nights cross-referencing tide models and budget spreadsheets. Celine is here, too — immaculate even in the greenhouse, sleeves rolled only to a careful degree. Ivo arrives with the faint smell of tar and rope, carrying a coil of new line and the weather-scraped camera he never quite puts down.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "The models show the terraces reduced peak surge energy by twenty-seven percent last season. That translated to saved foundations in two neighborhoods. That's not nothing."

You: "It's not nothing. It's actually everything for those two neighborhoods." (You feel the relief raw in your chest, and you let it out over the plants.) "But the covenants required oversight of private shoreline works. The town's enforcement team was understaffed — they prioritized immediate utility repairs. Some contractors took advantage."

Celine Harrow: (Her hands folded, the cuff of her sleeve a steady line. Her voice is measured.) "We moved funding through the emergency stabilization line so we could stop immediate collapse. Enforcement lag is a municipal failure — not a contractual one. We will allocate more to inspections."

You: (There's a coldness that edges your words.) "When you said 'we will allocate more' in Harrow Hall, it meant a lot. For people who lost their storehouses because a neighbor built a bulkhead a foot higher than the covenant allowed, 'allocating more' doesn't come back fast enough."

Ivo Calder: (He props his shoulder against a table, watching you.) "You're right to be angry. Those families lost months of revenue and some of the driftwood that paid for repairs. We fixed docks when we could, but some decisions we couldn't undo. I pushed for onsite oversight; the mayor's office pushed funding velocity. Velocity won."

Celine Harrow: (Her gaze drifts to you, and for a heartbeat something unreadable flashes.) "I never promised perfection, Alys. I promised action to stop immediate collapse. You knew that when you chose to work with me."

You: (It stings, the bluntness of it. You wanted a partner, not a reminder.) "I knew. I also believed in covenants that would make your action ethical, and in people who would hold the line. That belief is what I signed for."

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "There are legal audits queued. I can speed the data release so the community knows where money went and where enforcement slipped. Transparency will help — if people see the facts."

Ivo Calder: (He steps forward. His voice is quieter than you'd expect.) "And we have to be honest about relocation. Some places cannot be saved practically. [Scene: Engineered Terraces | Late Morning]
[Visual: Volunteers knee-deep in water, cinching mats and knotting lines. A placard reads: "Saltwick Living Shorelines — Community Planting Day." Children watch from shore, damp boots and hopeful faces.]
[Sound: The slap of boots in water, laughter, the rasp of nets; the distant thunder of a new weather cell brewing]
[Music: A hopeful motif thins into a minor-pulse rhythm; hope tempered by worry]

You wade into the shallow, boots filling with cold. Maya is ankle-deep, sleeves rolled, hands raw with work. She grins at you, salt in her hair.

Maya Maren: "Bread?" (She holds up a cloth-wrapped loaf, flour on her cheek as if it were war paint.)

You: (You smile, and something unclenches.) "Always. I told them you'd bring it."

The group moves like a wound that is learning to close. Leyna instructs where to secure the mats by waterline; Ronan passes tools with an energetic focus; older volunteers retie lines with ritual patience. The terraces catch wave and soften it. You can see the difference: where sand would once leech into the sea, now sediment stays put, sheltering the planted rhizomes.

Ivo works a pulley that leans a heavy mat into place. He looks up at you in the pressure between gusts.

Ivo Calder: "You made a call that saved those docks over there." (He jerks his chin toward a jagged group of storage sheds, patched and smoking faintly from repairs.) "I'll always be grateful for the practical help. But I don't forgive the places we lost."

You: (Your hands ache good hurt. "I don't either." You mean it — the loss sits in your ribs like a splinter. "We should have dragged inspectors out with us earlier. We should have been louder. I thought the covenant would have teeth.")

Ivo Calder: (A half-smile, then blunt.) "It did have teeth — in the legal sense. In everyday politics, people bite their tongues for bread. We fed mouths this season. Some mouths will never be the same."

Oba Kofi: (He moves through the crowd like a slow tide, placing a palm on a volunteer's shoulder.) "You grafted life to metal and law together, Alys. The graft takes. But the tree will always scar."

Your phone vibrates: a message from a volunteer about a private seawall that breached an adjacent marsh. The breach was due to a contractor's shortcut — a violation of covenant terms, and yet enforcement was delayed because inspections were scheduled weeks after the work. You feel the old tightness in your jaw.

[Scene: Harbor | Sunset]
[Visual: The harbor is a patchwork: new bulkheads gleam beside repaired warehouses; a half-sunken dinghy is being winched; strings of community lights catch the last of the sun. Families gather on the quay with hot tins of meal; the air has that particular sweetness of cooked bread and wet tar.]

A public meeting has been called at the edge of the pier. Celine stands at a makeshift podium; she reads the names of the new hires, of the oversight measures enacted, of the restitutions the town will try to make. Her voice carries the authority of office and the fatigue of compromise.

Celine Harrow: "We do not erase loss. We must account for it and learn. The audit will be public tomorrow. We've funded immediate inspections and an additional emergency relief line. Contracts will be rewritten with clearer enforcement clauses."

You watch faces in the crowd: someone nods cautiously; someone seethes; someone simply holds a cup and listens. Maya stands at your shoulder, paint on her apron, bread crumbs in the corner of her mouth. Ivo is near the pier rail, camera down, eyes on the water. Oba sits a little apart, steady.

A woman from North Beach Ridge steps forward — a concise, worn face you have seen in the markets. Her voice shakes only a little when she speaks.

Woman: "We lost the boathouse. They told us the bulkhead would be fine. The inspector came late. Will there be justice? Will we get help?"

Celine Harrow: (Carefully.) "We will extend relief to displaced families. We will prioritize North Beach for reconstruction funds. I will ensure the audit includes timelines of inspection and funding decisions."

You: (You think of the children who used to fish from that boathouse. "Justice isn't always numbers," you tell the woman, and then you address the crowd.) "We agreed to this hybrid because our seas were rising faster than our resolve. That choice brought money we needed and obligations we wanted. Where those obligations failed — in enforcement or in timing — we have to say we failed each other. Saying that is the start of making it right."

Ivo Calder: (He meets your gaze, expression unguarded.) "You should have been less alone in that council room. I should have leaned harder on the mayor's office. We thought binding funds would be enough. We were naive about how quickly people would take advantage of gaps."

You: (The admission tastes like iron and relief.) "I'm sorry for when my faith in the covenants translated into believing systems would behave like neighbors. I thought laws could stand in for the work of care."

Ivo Calder: (He comes closer, voice low.) "We both thought different things would save this place. I still believe people's hands make the real difference. But I can't pretend the money didn't make those hands stronger."

There is a long, charged silence between you. The tide knocks against pilings. A gull cries like a small accusation.

Oba Kofi: (He stands and walks to the edge, as if to set the world right in small increments.) "Saltwick saved some, lost some. That is the truth of the sea. The important thing is what we intend to do with the part we have left. Will we rebuild only for some, or will we make room for all the ones who remain?"

Maya Maren: (She speaks simply.) "We can do both. We have to keep doing both."

You look across the harbor at the terraces that kept the worst at bay and at the eroded lot where a family once kept a store. The hybrid plan is a strange animal — equal parts lifeline and compromise. It is alive, imperfect, and now governed by audits, by oversight teams, by human frailty.

Celine Harrow: (Her voice is softer than before.) "I did what I thought would save the town from collapse. I accept that my methods caused collateral damage. I will open the books. We will be judged — and we will answer."

You: (You meet her eyes. They are complicated. "Thank you," you say, because saying it moves something.) "We will hold you to it."

Celine nods. There is no flourish of reconciliation — only transaction and a slender promise.

Ivo Calder: (He takes your hand for a moment, callused palm to callused palm; the contact is brief and steady.) "Whatever comes next, we rebuild with our hands. If the law backs it, good. If not, we will still show up. I want to be here for that."

You: (You feel the pull between him and the work like a tide-line. "I want that too." It's not more; it's everything. You let yourself rest there for a breath.)

The sun dips. The sky flushes like an old bruise, then clears into a fragile band of orange. You hold the moment in your chest — small and finite.

You do not have sweeping victory. You have audits and oversight and new hires and families who will not come home. You have seagrass that will, with fierce patience, bind sediment and cradle larval fish. You have legal lines that must be watched and hearts that must be mended.

Oba Kofi: (He sits back on his cane and looks at you with a small, wry smile.) "You carried this town in your hands for a time, Alys. Now you carry it with others. That is heavier, but it is not yours alone."

You: (You let the weight shift. It is not a lightness that banishes sorrow; it's a rearrangement that allows breathing. "I will keep bearing it," you say. "Not alone." )

Ivo Calder: (He squeezes your hand once, and there is no promise, only intent.) "Let's go check the south terrace tomorrow. They say the rhizomes took better than anyone expected."

You: (You nod.) "Tomorrow, then."
