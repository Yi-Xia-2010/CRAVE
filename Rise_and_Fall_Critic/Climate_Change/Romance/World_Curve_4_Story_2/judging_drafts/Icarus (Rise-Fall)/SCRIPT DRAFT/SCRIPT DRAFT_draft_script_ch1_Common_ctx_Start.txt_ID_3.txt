You step off the bus and the first thing is the salt — not just a smell but a memory: a brine that seems to pull the town back into your bones. The air is sharp and bright, carrying the scent of kelp and varnish and the faint sweet rot of seaweed that speaks of life and decay braided together. Your boots meet the dock with the familiar give of worn wood; the dented brass compass at your throat glints against your chest where the sun finds it.

Saltwick looks like a painting unfinished in the best places: netted boats tied to pilings, lamp posts casting long, patient shadows, benches with names carved into them. Beyond the harbor, the Glass Marsh is a sheet of quiet—mirrors of the sky, reeds standing like a nervous audience. Closer, patches of bright green in the shallows catch your eye: early clumps of rehabilitated seagrass, stubborn and surprisingly bright against the pewter water.

You have been away for years, leaving this place folded into notebooks and thesis pages. Now the town unfolds toward you in human rhythms you recognize.

Maya spots you first. She's on a ladder, painting a sign for the new volunteer shed — paint on her forearms, braid catching the wind like a banner. Her grin when she sees you is the kind that drops the years away; the sound of it is a small, delighted thing that makes your throat ache with something like relief.

Maya Maren: "Alys! You look like the same stubborn scientist, only with better boots." (She hops down, wiping her hands on an apron with a splash of teal paint.) "Gods, you smell like bus leather and possibilities."

You: "And you smell like paint and salt and every decision I've procrastinated on." (You drop your bag and the field notebook thumps against your hip.) "How long've you been up there?"

Maya: "Long enough to gossip to Ronan, to scold two volunteers, and to plan a surprise welcome. Sit, sit—you're sunburning the past, come help me screw the signboard in." (She nudges you; there's mischief and an anchorive warmth in her push.)

You close your eyes for a beat and let the motion of it ground you — the ladder's rattle, the spray of a boat in the channel, the way sunlight threads through fishing nets like a promise. Home feels like this: stubborn, a little ragged, ready to be fixed.

You: "I left them notes and a compass. Both probably needed translation." (You reach for the rag and he pulls it back with a grin.) "How's the seawall project coming?"

Ivo: "Ronan's optimistic, the volunteers are loud, and the seawall is three quarters stubborn and one quarter science. You know how it goes." (He leans back, looking at you like he's checking the weather in your face.) "We kept your notebook on the table at Maya's. Thought you might like a ceremonial dusting-off."

You open your mouth and close it, feeling every year laid out in a single line between you — the drafts, the conferences, the nights of data, and then the practical smell of rope and engine that is Ivo's constant. There's history here: shared nights hauling nets after storms, evenings at the old boatyard, the easy banter that sometimes hid more charged pauses.

You: "I'd like to see what stayed alive in the plans." (You tuck a strand of chestnut hair behind your ear; the wind is doing its best to unpin everything.) "And I'd like to see how we can knit the seagrass plan into what you and the team are building."

Ivo: (his expression softens) "Knitting, huh? Fancy words. You're gonna make me sound like I'm not just a man with a hammer." (He waits, then adds) "Look, Aly—" (he stops, the familiarity faltering for a half-breath) "—let's make sure people don't lose their roofs while we regrow the ocean. Can we do both?"

You: "We have to." (The word is a small vow. You picture the seagrass beds holding sediments, the larval nurseries returning, the slow mathematics of resilience. But you also see a family's porch lifted by water.) "That's the point. Immediate shelter, long-term resilience. They shouldn't be a trade-off."

Ivo: "Good. Because if anyone's going to drag this town into a future where kids can fish again, it's gonna be you and me doing something the town understands." (He nudges you with a shoulder, light and certain.) "And don't let Celine's smooth tie scare you off."

You: (a small bite of irritation) "Is she at the hall already?"

Ivo: "She's been at every meeting like a lullaby, promising rain of funds. She does it smile-first, then bureaucracy." (He tilts his head; there's a question in his eyes that isn't just municipal politics.) "How are you with her?"

You: "Complex." (You remember the Schrödinger quiet of power — performative concern, real money, possible strings.) "She may talk big about seagrass as a line item. I need plans, not press releases."

Ivo: "Then let's give her plans she can't clip the headline out of." (He grins.) "You lead the science; I'll keep the town dry enough for your seedlings to take root."

The sunlight picks out the bronze on his utility belt; there's a weight in the gesture that feels like an offer — not heavy, but substantial, practical commitment that smells of rope and coffee.

Oba Kofi leans on his cane and regards you with a smile like an old tide chart — slow, sure, and full of markers.

Oba Kofi: "Alys Maren, sea-son of Saltwick." (He sounds the name like a blessing.) "Look at you. The town's been waiting."

You: "You sound like a harbor bell." (You step closer, feeling the soft give of the dock boards.) "How is the marsh?"

Oba Kofi: "Tired, but learning to breathe again. The old beds remember the right murmur. We just need the right hands to teach them and the patience to let them remember." (He taps his cane, which is carved with fish and moons.) "Your father used to say the same thing: you can't hurry the sea, but you can listen. You listen well."

You feel the weight of the compliment in your ribs, the echo of your father's voice — the dented compass a cold weight against your sternum. Oba's presence steadies the conversation like a current under a bright surface.

Oba Kofi: "The town will listen if you speak plainly. They trust hands more than graphs. Show them seedlings, show them sediment tests, and show them how a seagrass meadow holds a shoreline like a thousand small hands."

You: "We can show them. Leyna and I — and volunteers — we have preliminary transects, carbon estimates, the restoration protocol. We need boots, roots, and a town's patience."

Oba Kofi: (a slow chuckle) "And plenty of tea for the waiting. Bring people, not only data."

You nod. It feels right: the combination of technique and tenderness, of numbers and storytelling. The plan starts to unspool, tidy in your head, like a knot you can teach others to loosen.

You climb the spiral stairs to the rooftop greenhouse and the air changes — warmer, humid, fragrant with soil and young leaves. Inside is a small theater of green: trays of eelgrass seedlings, tangled roots like miniature histories. The glass panes blur the sea beyond into a painting, and inside the plants hold the light like secrets.

Maya sets a tray on the central table; Ronan is sketching layout plans in the corner. Ivo brings over two muddy mugs of tea and hands one to you with a flourish that is nothing if not affectionate.

Ivo: "Civically brewed tea. For seedling morale."

You: "It smells like home." (You inhale through it, letting the steam fog your glasses for a half-second.) "Okay. This is where the plan becomes a body of work, not just a slide deck."

Maya: "Ronan's got volunteers lined up for the digging and the planting schedule. Oba's agreed to give a talk—'Why seagrass is a town's second harbor.'" (She giggles, then grows earnest.) "Celine wanted someone to show her the greenhouse. She said—" (Maya's eyes flick over to the greenhouse door where, as if conjured, Celine Harrow stands framed in the doorway like sculpture.)

Celine Harrow: "Agreed. We'll frame it around stewardship. We'll make it matter to people's livelihoods. And—" (she hesitates a fraction, then adds) "—I'll be transparent about funding sources. I owe that to you and to Saltwick."

Oba Kofi: (softly) "Words are light. Actions are ballast."

Celine's reply is a tilt, a compact diplomacy: she nods as if to the weather itself.

You sense a fold in the afternoon when the possibilities align: seedlings in trays, a mayor promising accountability, volunteers who will show up at dawn, Ivo with a hammer and a kindness that is quietly fierce. Your chest loosens in a way that feels like breathing after a long held note.

You sit at the table, the field notebook open, pen poised. The plan spreads out in front of you like a small map of hope: seagrass beds plotted against houses, timelines stitched to tide charts, volunteer rosters—and, beneath each item, the human things: who will plant, who will watch, who will tell the story.

Maya: "We have a schedule for planting the nursery batch. We can get boots on the marsh next week if Oba gives the blessing." (She looks at you, bright and eager.) "You ready to lead them?"

You: (your hand hovers over the page; the compass feels heavy and good) "I am." (The word lands like a promise to yourself as much as to them.) Ivo: "I'll coordinate the shore crew." (He meets your eyes; the agreement is both professional and personal.) "We'll make this visible. People need to see change to believe it."

Oba Kofi: "And I will tell them the old songs to remember how to tend it." (He taps his cane twice, decisive.)

Celine Harrow: "I'll take this to the council and secure the first tranche. But I want transparent reporting from you, Alys. Monthly estimates, community updates, measurable wins."
