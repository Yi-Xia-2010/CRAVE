[Scene: North Beach Ridge | Night — Storm Approaches]
[Visual: Low-angle wide shot of the ridge; improvised berms and sandbags silhouette against a bruised sky]
[Music: Distant, mournful strings with a slow timpani undercurrent]
[Sound: Wind building into a keening, gulls calling intermittently; the harbor bell tolls once and is swallowed]

You stand where the sand meets the work you've made. The berms — a crooked, stubborn line of sand, woven grass mats, and plywood — look less like an engineered solution and more like the town's prayer cast into the tide. Volunteers move along the line like hands smoothing a wound: Ronan tucking a tarp, Maya passing down armfuls of wet sand, Oba Kofi murmuring a counting cadence that steadies bodies more than numbers.

Your chest is a ledger of small calculations — seepage rates, overtopping thresholds, how many hours the pumps will hold — and underneath that the private arithmetic of risk: who sleeps where tonight, which houses you can save if the surge slants this way. The choice the town made — to build itself rather than hand the shore to contractors — hangs around your shoulders like a wet coat. It's a decision you still live inside; you taste it, salt and resolute.

Ivo moves beside you, hands raw from sand and rope, his high-visibility vest muddied to an indistinguishable gray. He speaks and the words are practical, clipped against the wind.

Ivo: "Storm surge models were right on the high bound. If it comes like the NOAA spread said, we've got maybe three hours before the first overtopping. We lock pumps now, then rotate crews every forty-five."

You: "And the overflow channels? Leyna's nets?" (You keep the questions thin and exact; facts are anchors.)

Ivo: (a quick exhale) "They're set where you wanted. Ronan and I secured the northern splice. But the western end — the gully by the old jetty — it's weaker. We held that stretch for the fishery houses, but if the surge rides left..."

Ronan: "It'll try."

Oba Kofi walks up, cane tapping a measured count on the sand. He smells of brine and cedar smoke, the universe between him and the sea distilled into slow certainty.

Oba Kofi: "When I was a boy, the sea took a whole row of houses in a night like this. We put sand where we could, but the tide does what it wants. Still, hands matter. We make the place less hungry."

You: "We make it less hungry, not full. I know."

Oba's eyes rest on you, unreadable and steady. His presence is less advice than a map of what endures; you take the map and hold it.

[Scene: North Beach Ridge | Night — The Surge Arrives]
[Visual: A closer shot; rain begins as a thin, stinging curtain. Headlamps cut through sheets of water; spray winds sideways]
[Music: Low brass and electronics; a differential pulse mimics a speeding heart]
[Sound: A metallic howl as the wind finds seams; the distant hammer of waves against the ridge]

It hits like a hand. For a breath you think the ocean has decided to speak in a single voice — furious, enormous — and you're less a leader than a witness. Water scours the face of the berms in black, freighted sheets. The sand answers, graining into the wind and disappearing. You feel each shove on your boots through the soles, the sea sneaking under, through. Your jacket is soaked to the waist; the fabric drags like a second skin.

Volunteers shout names. Orders loop back and forth; the cadence of command becomes a kind of gospel. You watch a wave rear up and fold; you watch sandbags take the brunt until a seam gives and a slick curtain of saltwater streams through into a low yard.

Maya is next to you, hair plastered to her forehead, paint-streaked apron turned into a sodden marker of who she is: your sister, smaller than you remember, louder in the mouth now because she cannot afford silence.

Maya: (shouting over wind) "We got a breach at the jetty end! Old Harris' place — she's waving a lantern!"

You: "Ronan, with me — now. Ivo, hold the north splice."

Ivo: (there's an urgency under his even tone) "I'm on it. You go. I'll call for the crew to reroute two pumps."

Ronan: "Got you, Alys."

The three of you run. Your lungs burn. At Harris' house the tide is already kissing the porch steps. You and Ronan throw your weight into pushable sandbags; you can feel them bite into your palms, the fibers rasping. Harris takes you both by the collar for a half-second, her eyes wet, a laugh like a broken bell.

Harris: "Bless yous. You always did come back."

You: (the syllables are small and private between the roar) "We try."

You can see, with a clarity that is almost cruel, the limits of what a volunteer town can hold. The seawall takes the first storm, but the second surge — a backward, cavernous swell — climbs a foot higher than your calculations. At certain angles, physics is a thief; momentum rips into the sand and pulls.

[Scene: Muri Research Lab | Night — Generator Room & Seedling Bay]
[Visual: Interior of retrofit shipping container lab; rain drums on corrugated steel. LED displays blink; seedlings under tempered light hum]
[Music: Sparse piano with a thread of howling wind sample underneath]
[Sound: The lab's generator stutters, then finds a rhythm; water splashes in a shallow tray]

If you went to the lab, the lights feel like hands closing against the dark. Leyna's tanks breathe; there is a clean, chlorophyll smell under the electrically charged air, a smell that becomes a kind of grief the moment power dips. You check the generator, thumbs on levers, palms smeared with diesel and salt. You tally fuel and calculations: how many hours of warmth can the seedlings have, how many pumps must a lab power if a pump fails at the ridge.

Ivo arrives seconds later — it doesn't matter whether he was supposed to or not; he is there, the two of you in the narrow glow, and there's a pause that has the weight of things left unsaid.

Ivo: "Data's red for three of the northern plots. Overflow fouled them." (He gestures and his voice frays at the edges.) "We saved the nursery beds, Alys. But the outplant lines — the cables — they're salted. Leyna's got pages of notes; she'll salvage what she can."

You: "We can replace lines. We can splice. Tell me where to put hands."

Ivo: (a small, tired smile) "You always say 'we' like it's a math problem with an answer. It's not. But it's where you start."

You: (you want to be everything — the scientist, the sister, the leader; you try to be practical) "We'll triage. Priority to intact genetics, then root systems, then whatever seeds slept through this hour."

Ivo: "And people?" (He looks at your face — not at the seedlings.) "What do you think we do for houses that flooded? For the families who'll need roofs for winter?"

You: (You taste the salt of the sea on your tongue and the metallic tang of your own fear.) "We make shelters. We feed them. We map relocation options. We do the jobs Leyna can't mend with graphs."

There is a pause. Between you is a kind of debt — unspoken promises from the nights you stitched the town together, and the nights you did not.

[Scene: Harbor | Dawn Aftermath]
[Visual: A bruised sunrise — not gold but a thin brass; the harbor is a field of splintered wood and blackened tarpaulin]
[Music: A low, mournful cello; sparse, single notes that hang]
[Sound: People collect in clusters; the town bell rings a somber toll; the gulls pick through washed-up debris]

The morning is quieter than the night, but it carries a different weight: countable loss. Berms held in places; houses that faced perpendicular to the surge are saturated and scarred, but standing. In other places, entire porches are gone, porches that had been the centerlines of neighborly evenings. A few roofs collapsed entirely. A handful of families — the ones on the lowest forks, the ones who could not afford redundancy — move their things into battered trucks and buses, faces pinched and defeated in ways you recognize because you loved them once.

Oba Kofi moves like he always does: slow, hands steady on the cane, looking at the sea and not puzzling over blame. He has a list in his head that is not the same as yours; it is a map of which stories to tell the next time a child asks why their house isn't the same.

Oba Kofi: "We saved a row. We lost a row. That is the world's math sometimes. It does not mean we were wrong to try."

Maya finds you at the quay and hits you with the kind of question that is its own grief.

Maya: "Why did the gully give? We built stronger. We did everything you told us."

You: "The surge found a throat. The seams in the sand aren't perfect. We made them better than before, and that's true. But storms are not promises — they're tests."

Maya: (quiet, wounded) "People are tired, Aly. They kept faith with you. They stayed because you asked them to."

You feel the weight of that as an ache. Your leadership sits as a physical thing — tender, bruised. You think of the families whose boxes are being loaded into trucks, the children holding stuffed animals tight, the elders who move with a dignity that makes your hands want to do something you can't quite name. You promise things you know you might not be able to keep on timescales people want.

[Scene: Harbor — Conversation Between You and Ivo]
[Visual: Medium close-up of two figures against broken boats; morning light cuts the salt from the air]
[Music: Sparse strings, fragile harmonies]
[Sound: A distant hammer; voices that lift and fall like small waves]

Ivo: (his hands are ink-stained, callused; he gestures toward the line of departing trucks) "They're taking the Lower Fork. My cousin's family is packed. They'll go inland until the council can secure some cottages."

You: "We can set up a resettlement hub. We can apply emergency grants for modular homes."

Ivo: "And who pays while the council waits on budgets? Who sleeps in the halls? You know people can't just keep living in a waiting room."

You: (You hear the accusation even when it isn't there; or maybe it is, a quiet, accusing thing.) "I'm trying to build a system that doesn't keep them in waiting rooms forever. That was the point of community-led planning."

Ivo: (his voice tightens) "It feels a bit academic when someone's bed is ruined, Alys. People wanted visible action. They needed roofs tonight."

You: "And that is why we held the berms where we could. Those roofs that remained — we kept people under them."

Ivo: "But not all of them." (He looks at you; the look is simple and complicated.) "You don't see them in the same way I do. You look for permanency; I look for breathing room."

You: (you bring up an image of the greenhouse, seedlings under glass — small, stubborn things that can make the difference in years to come) "Breathing room for what? For survival, yes. For living beyond survival, too. If we only patch and fortify, we keep building atop the same mistakes. We won't fix what eats the shore."

Ivo: "And if we only plant and wait, people will sleep rough. They'll be hungry, they'll leave, they'll lose the place they're supposed to call home."

The exchange is not a fight so much as two axes grinding against the same stone. You both shoulder grief for different timescales; both of you exact love as a form of action. The town hangs between you — the thing you want to save and the thing that saves you.

For a moment Ivo's face softens, and you remember that warmth that won you over in quiet, practical ways — his hands mending a hull, his laugh when a plan worked. But under that softness is fatigue, a kind of protracted grief that makes him meaner and braver at once.

Ivo: (lowered voice) "I stayed for this place. I fixed roofs. I pulled people from the water. I want to keep them here. I want that now."

You: "I stayed because I thought I could keep them alive and make it better after. I thought I could stretch the now into later. Maybe I got arrogant about how much time we had."

Silence falls, not empty but full of small, necessary admissions. You both look at the harbor, at the crowd that is trying to be whole again. There is a private fracture between you that will not be patched by the same sandbags.

Ivo: (quietly) "Maybe—maybe we'll work the same line again. Maybe not. I'm tired, Aly. I don't know if I can keep being the stopgap for everything."

You: (you taste a future where strategy and immediacy don't reconcile easily; you feel it as a cold thing sliding under your ribs) "I don't know either. But I will keep trying. For the town. For—" (You let the sentence hang. You cannot promise what you can't deliver, and you know it.)

Ivo: (he nods once, a small, almost childlike motion) "Then keep trying. But don't ask people to live in waiting forever."

You: "I won't."

The words feel both like a balm and a razor. You stand there with Ivo, both of you soaked in storm water and age-old conviction, and for the first time in days you feel the ache of being limited. It is a human pain: love that cannot hold all the things you want it to, duty that erodes the tender spaces between you.

By nightfall, a small, grim inventory is complete. Supplies have been drained: food stocks low, fuel rationed, critical lumber used for emergency repairs. The seed bank in Muri Lab is partially intact; Leyna reports a seventy-percent salvage of established beds and a fragile hope for replanting come late fall. Oba organizes storytelling circles to hold the town's nerve. Ronan runs shifts with a gravity that makes him look older than he is.

Some families pack their lives into trucks and head inland. You help Maya load a box of her paintings — bright seascapes, stubborn as defiance — into the back of a borrowed van. She presses your hand and for a second you both stand without speaking; the moment is not reconciliation, only recognition.

Maya: "You did everything you could, Aly. So did they."

You: "I know."

The harbor is quieter now. The sea roars at a distance as if still testing the land's generosity. You walk the line of what remains and what is gone like someone counting breath.

Oba Kofi: (he slips his hand into yours for a moment — an old, human gesture that says what words don't) "You chose Saltwick's hands over outside coin. That choice cost. It also bought us respect. Some things you cannot measure on a ledger, my child."

You: "We have a lot to fix."

Oba Kofi: "Then we fix. Slowly, stubbornly. With people."

You fold the sentence into your muscles: fix. Slowly. It is not the triumphant finish you once imagined; it is quieter, less adoring. It is steady.

[Scene: Muri Research Lab | Night — You Alone]
[Visual: Interior, late; you sit on a crate under the lab's dim lights. Seedlings glow faintly in the racks like a small constellation]
[Music: Very soft piano, a minor key that resolves into a tentative cadence]
[Sound: A far-off generator hum; rain reduced to a slow tapping on steel]

You are tired in a way that feels structural, like a building settling after a quake. You open your field notebook and write in your cramped hand: "Assess — Triage — Rebuild — Shelter." Words are small scaffolds. You underline them because underlining helps when some things feel unbearable.

You think about Ivo and the wedge that has formed. You think about the families who left, the ones who stayed. You think about the seedlings you can still plant and the years it will take for them to make a difference. There is a heaviness here that is not resignation but a kind of mature sorrow: a recognition that doing the right thing sometimes looks like losing the thing you wanted most.

You touch the bracelet at your wrist — braided line worn thin — and trace the memory of promises that were both you and the town. The wind outside has moved on to some other place, but Saltwick's wounds are slow.

Maya's voice comes from the doorway; she leans in, eyes red, but with that fierce, reparative energy only kin can have.

Maya: "Come home. There's stew. And Ronan's trying to make coffee without setting anything on fire."

You: (you let out a laugh that tastes like relief and grief) "Tell him the lab needs him sober tomorrow."

Maya: "Tell him that yourself. He likes it better when you boss him."

You stand. The night is cold, but inside your chest there's a small, steady ember that refuses to go out. It is not triumph. It is not even full hope. It is commitment: to the town, to the science, to the people who will not be saved by the quick fix that once tempted you.

You step outside into the salt air. Dawn will come. Maybe it will be gentle. Maybe it will be bitter. You cannot say. What you can say, with your hands and your life and your stubbornness, is this: you will be here for the work. You will shape the slow architecture of recovery as best you can. You will mourn what was lost and steward what remains.

Across the harbor, Ivo stands by a broken boat. Your eyes meet, and the expression between you is complex — not warmth or anger alone, but the layered thing of people who have shared too much to be strangers and too little to be whole.

He lifts his hand in a small, unsteady gesture that might be an apology, might be a promise, might be both. You lift your hand back. The world is large enough for both grief and care, and for now — stubborn, aching, human — that is the shape of your answer.
