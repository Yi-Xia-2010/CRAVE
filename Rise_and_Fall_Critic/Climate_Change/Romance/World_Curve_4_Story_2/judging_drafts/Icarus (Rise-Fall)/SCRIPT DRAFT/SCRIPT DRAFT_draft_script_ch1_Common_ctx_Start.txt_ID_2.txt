[Scene: Crescent Docks | Morning]
[Visual: Salt-scraped docks stretching under a pale, windwashed sky]
[Music: Quiet, hopeful strings with distant gull calls]
[Sound: Water lapping against wooden pilings; a gull's cry; the creak of rope]

You step off the small ferry and the first thing that hits you is salt — not just the taste at the back of your tongue but the dry grit on your jacket, the way it brushes your hair into a halo of flyaways. The town smells like old nets and frying oil and something green trying very hard to grow. For a second you let yourself be nothing but a traveler come home: a pair of boots finding familiar boards, a compass in your palm that still hums with the memory of your father's hands.

You: "It's smaller than I—" (you trail off; the harbor swallows the rest of the sentence)

Maya is waiting at the end of the pier, paint on her forearms and that impossible grin you remember from childhood. She crosses the boards in two long strides and wraps you in a hug that smells like citrus cleaner and sea wind. The reunion is immediate and ordinary and perfect.

Maya: "You actually came back. You always say 'I'll visit' and then scientific urgency eats the calendar." (she teases, but her eyes are bright; there’s relief there)
You: "I scheduled the ferry. Figured I could stand three hours of council emails for this." (you say it lightly; underneath the joke is the truth — you've been counting days)

Her hands don't let go. For a heartbeat you both stand in the harbor's noise: gulls, a far engine, the murmur of voices on the quay. Maya's laughter loosens something in you that the city had tightened. This place still fits you.

Maya: "Oba's down by the old net shed. He wanted to bless your boots or whatever he does." (she winks) "Also—" (she glances toward the warehouses) "—Ivo said he'd meet us. Classic show-up-late-but-always-fix-it."

You: "Ivo?" (the name pulls at the memory the same way tide pulls at sand: familiar, steady)

Before Maya can answer, a shadow steps from behind a stack of lobster crates — Ivo, hands stained with grease, work shirt rolled at the sleeves, his cap tilted back like he does when he's trying not to look overly pleased at something he's built. He leans against a post and smiles in that crooked, warm way that always makes you untangle a dozen arguments into a single forgiving sentence.

Ivo: "Look at you. You brought the field notebook. That's commitment." (he nods at the dented brass compass you keep tucked away)
You: "Couldn't leave it behind. It keeps my bad decisions grounded." (you laugh; it's an easy sound you didn't expect to hear from yourself so soon)
Ivo: (softly) "Glad you decided to come home, Alys." (he corrects himself) "—I mean, glad you decided to come back."

His voice catches on the second sentence; he doesn't finish whatever the rest of it could be. His expression is open but there is a gravity to it — practical and honest. You notice the silvered band on his leather thong, warm against the sun. There's a lot the two of you don't say aloud, and the harbor handles all the unsaid like it handles rope: it knots, frays, then gets re-coiled by hands that have done it before.

Ivo: "We got volunteers down on North Ridge. Ronan's already starting to stack bags. If you're ready, we can walk the line, see what we have to work with." (he gestures toward the mile of mangled dunes and makes the plan sound like a sail to catch)

You: "Yes. We should catalog the surviving beds, mark the sediment pockets, coordinate with Leyna about salinity runs. If we seed in the wrong current—" (you cut yourself off; your training surfaces like a tide)
Ivo: "Then we'll figure the currents. Together." (he says it like a promise and like a fact; you believe both)

[Sound: A distant vehicle, sharp and formal — the municipal van that means someone in a suit arrives]

You notice the municipal van idling by the Harrow Hall wall — its paint too clean for this weather. A woman steps out with the kind of coat that resists the sea: silver-blonde bob, measured gait, a dossier held like a shield. Celine Harrow moves like someone who has rehearsed every entrance.

You watch, and a little tension threads through the harbor's warm fabric. Celine's expression is the political equivalent of a lighthouse: steady, designed to be visible. She catches sight of you and inclines her head in that precise way she uses in council meetings. The motion doesn't tell you everything; it's the outline of intent without the interior.

Celine Harrow: (calling, voice carrying) "Ms. Maren, welcome back. The town owes you a debt for what you've done before—there's much to discuss." (her tone is smooth, public; the words are courteous but framed for an audience)

You: (you step forward) "Mayor Harrow. Thank you. We don't have time to rest on past work — we need plans that protect people and habitat." (you feel the warmth in your chest widen; your voice is steady)

Celine: (tilting her head) "Of course. Short-term protections and long-term sustainability — they aren't mutually exclusive." (the sentence is polished; you sense an offer contained in it)

The exchange is courteous, informative, and not a collision. It leaves room for alliance and challenge; it leaves room for negotiation. That possibility feels like light.

[Scene: Harbor Walkway | Late Morning]
[Visual: The Glass Marsh glimmers in the distance, mist lifting; reeds outline a horizon]
[Music: Layered piano with a steady heartbeat of percussion]
[Sound: Soft wind through reeds, distant children playing, a metal gate closing]

You walk the boardwalk toward the marsh with Ivo and Maya, cataloguing out loud as you go. The Glass Marsh sits like a bowl of sky — shallow pools catching clouds, patches of green trying to stitch themselves back together. Your fingers trace the edges of your field notebook; the pages are waiting for you like seedbeds.

You: (muttering as you scan) "If we can secure a one-kilometer buffer with seeded rhizomes in the first season, it should reduce hydrodynamic energy along this stretch by measurable amounts." (you enjoy the sound of numbers bound to a purpose)
Ivo: "And if we do that, we buy time for the community to lift gardens and shorelines where we can. We keep nets, keep boats moored." (he adds the human map to your ecological calculus)
Maya: "And we get Ronan and the volunteers excited because they'll see something change fast. Visible wins, Alys. People need them."

The three of you pause by a patch where someone has already started planting trial clusters. Tiny blades of seagrass nod in the current like green Morse code. Leyna's lab sticker is on a sampling jar nearby; her data will tell you whether these clusters will take. Your chest lifts. Data and hands and neighbors — the three things you need to stitch change together — are here.

Oba Kofi arrives on his cane, slow and sure. He smells of smoked fish and cedar and the particular clean scent of someone who's lived with wind for decades. He crouches and touches the soil with a reverence that reads like a prayer.

Oba Kofi: "When the tide remembers us, the shore answers." (his voice is gravel and ocean; he looks at you with something like approval) "You returned. Good."

You: "I couldn't leave it alone. Not again." (you say it low; the words are both private and public)

Oba Kofi: (he hums) "Salt cannot be hurried. But the hands you bring make it quicker than waiting. We will teach our children to tend." (his eyes crinkle; there's a promise in his certainty)

The elder's blessing expands the circle. Volunteers pass by with buckets and laughter; even the harbor seems to hum with possibility. It isn't the end of the work, but it is a beginning, and beginnings have weight.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm amber light filtering through salt-flecked panes; seedlings in trays glint like jewels]
[Music: Warm strings and a low hopeful hum]
[Sound: Condensation pattering on glass; distant crash of surf; the soft click of tools]

The greenhouse is a warm, humid pocket suspended above the cliff: a deliberate inversion of the town's exposed edges. Inside, rows of seedlings sway under panels of sun. Hydroponic rigs hum softly, and the air smells of damp earth and citrus. You breathe it in like medicine.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz isn't here, but her tablet is: charts of salinity trends, projected growth curves, and a column labeled "Pilot Protocols." You set the tablet down and begin to outline a plan aloud, voice steady. Ivo pulls out a battered pocket camera and flips through photos of the ridge; Maya makes checklists in bright marker.

You: "We start with twenty pilot plots in the most sheltered bays, rotate planting according to tidal windows, and use the biodegradable anchors to hold the rhizomes until the roots take." (you sketch arcs in the air; it's kinetic, and you can almost see the pattern)
Ivo: "We can have Ronan and five volunteers deploy the first anchors this weekend, run shallow dives with safety lines. Leyna will do baseline salinity tomorrow if we can shuttle her to the lab." (he's already thinking logistics)

The conversation becomes choreography — roles, dates, materials — and with each practical detail your anxiety recedes. The plan is not a dream; it's a schedule. It can be measured, repeated, improved. That makes it real.

Maya: "And the community workshop? If we show people how to plant and why it works, they're invested. They stop being spectators." (she underlines a note)
You: (softly) "Teaching is part of stewardship. If we leave the knowledge here, the project grows without me."

Ivo watches you while you speak. For a moment his eyes search yours for a hidden condition, then he nods.

Ivo: "You're not doing this alone. If you're worried about delegating, start with Ronan. He does what we tell him and asks later." (he smiles) "I can coach him."

You: (you want to say more — about old habits of shouldering things, about kindness you haven't accepted — but instead you write the first meeting date into your notebook) "Okay. Saturday morning, tide slack at 0900. Meet at the warehouse. Bring gloves, water, patience."

There is an electric modesty to the moment: a small plan that bends policy and politics toward action. It feels like pushing a door that has been too long closed; the door opens with a soft groan and sun spills through.

Celine's name appears again in a message on your tablet — a tentative invitation to a town forum, full of official language and promises of funding. You feel the pull of large-scale resources and the tug of grassroots momentum. Both are necessary, and both demand attention.

Oba Kofi: (from the doorway) "Keep both hands on the problem, child. One hand for the sea, one for the people." (the line lands like a benediction)

You sit on a bench surrounded by trays of green and think about what home means: a place that takes you back and asks you to carry it forward. The harbor is neither fixed nor broken; it's work and ritual and stubborn love. You feel the energy of a dozen hands, a dozen plans aligning. The day has weight, but it's a warm one.

Ivo: (standing beside you, quiet) "You always planned with pencil lines and patience. It's good to see the town answer them." (he nods toward the greenhouse)
You: "And you're good at building walls that keep people safe while the plants do their work." (you smile; it's acknowledgment and a peace offering)
Ivo: "Then we make both. Seagrass to slow the sea; seawalls — temporary, tactical — to buy us time to grow something better." (he says it like he's bridging two maps)

Your chest lifts at the image. A hybrid plan — immediate protection with a growing root system beneath it — is exactly the compromise your town will need. It's not simple. It's not easy. But the possibility of it unfurls like tender leaves.

[Sound: Distant bell — the harbor's old signal for town gatherings]
[Music: Crescendo into an airy, uplifting chord]

You stand up, dust salt from your trousers, the day compressed into a handful of actionable steps and people who will take them. The greenhouse's warm light feels like a promise.

You: (to yourself) "We begin."

You walk back down toward the harbor with Maya and Ivo at your sides and volunteers already forming like a small constellation. Celine's van is set against the stone of Harrow Hall, a reminder that the work will be both local and political. You are ready to start threading them together.

A moment comes as you reach the pier: the first public meeting is scheduled for tomorrow night. The notice is tentative — half official, half a community call. Your notebook sits open in your hand, and beneath the meeting line you've written, in smaller script, one word: stewardship.

You breathe the salt, the hope, the weight of what you've promised tonight. The wind carries the sound of the town — voices, metal, the sea — and you feel it answer back, a rising chorus.

You hold the compass in your palm, its brass warm. The needle doesn't tell you everything, but it points. You follow.
