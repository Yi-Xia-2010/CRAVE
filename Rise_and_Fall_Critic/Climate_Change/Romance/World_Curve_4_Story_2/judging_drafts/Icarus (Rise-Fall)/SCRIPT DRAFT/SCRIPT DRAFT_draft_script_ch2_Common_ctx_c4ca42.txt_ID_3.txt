You open the greenhouse door and the humid warmth folds around you like a familiar cloak. The notebook lies where you left it, that line underlined: Seed — Measure — Teach — Protect. For a moment you let the words settle — not as a command but as something you and the town can do together, stitch by small stitch.

Leyna is crouched by a tray, tablet balanced on her knee, tapping through graphs with a thumb still speckled green from algae. Ronan is on the compost bench, pulling apart a clump of fibrous mat to show a cluster of tiny roots. Their faces are bright in the sun slanting through the glass; you trace that brightness like a tide line under your skin.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "The mats we seeded two weeks ago — look." (She swipes the tablet, a scatter of points blooming into a clearer trend.) "Biomass is up, establishment rate is better than our pilot estimate. Salinity spikes didn't knock them back like we feared."

You crouch, hand hovering over a mat. The seedlings are small, bristling like newborns, the green a brash, defiant color against the salt-sick brown of the old shoots. You press your thumb to a softened edge; it leaves a faint smudge, warm and alive.

You: "That's better than 'not failing.' That's the kind of small accomplishment that keeps us alive."

Ronan grins, his energy translating into restless movement.

Ronan Pike: "And the crabs are already using the mats as cover. Saw three juveniles playing tag under one. It's like they noticed a new playground."

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: (laughing) "Ecosystem applause. We'll take that as a positive indicator."

The laughter loosens something in you — a ribbon of ease you hadn't realized you'd been holding tight. Yet underneath it, the practical mind that is yours catalogues implications: more volunteers to coordinate, crates to secure for new plantings, salt probes to swap out more often.

You: "Leyna, the next batch of hydroponic seedlings — do we move them to the raised racks today, or wait until after the harbor run?"

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "Move them. The marginal benefit of earlier acclimation outweighs transport stress. Also, showing the racks full at the harbor demonstration will help."

You consider the porch where volunteers will gather later: Ivo and Oba and the handful of faces that have already started to return to public work. You imagine the swell of the town's opinion tipping just a little more toward trust because of a single tray of bright seedlings.

[Scene: Shallow Harbor | Late Morning]
[Visual: A long crescent of weathered docks; patched buoys and new seeded mats anchored in the shallows]
[Sound: The slap of boats against pilings; volunteers talking; a low mechanical hum from the buoy rigging]
[Smell: Brine and warm rope oil; cut kelp]
[Music: Quiet percussion — a steady, hopeful rhythm]

The harbor is a choreography of hands and rope. Ivo stands at the edge of a skiff, sleeves rolled, toolbelt glinting. Oba, knees bent against a crate, issues directions with the economy of someone who has read tides his whole life. You move through the small crowd like a conductor — but rather than waving a baton, you hand out gloves and water bottles.

Ivo Calder: "We secured the mats to the old groynes where the current will keep sediment from scouring them out. If they take, this stretch'll start holding sand again in a season."

You watch as they ease a mat into the shallow water. It floats for a beat, then settles, the seedlings kissing the surface and the edges taking hold with a soft sucking sound. Tiny fish dart through the stems; a volunteer laughs, the sound buoyant and surprised.

Oba Kofi: (steady, voice like wind over a hull) "Nature remembers how to hold itself if you give it the place to do so. Your work gives the place."

You: "We owe a lot to the way you remember, Oba. We map, we measure, we try things — but the memory's who shows us where to begin."

Ivo gives you a look that is all warmth and pragmatism — fingers working, jaw set.

Ivo Calder: "You led this. You did the science, you pulled in the team. People want to trust what's here because it's visible. That's on you, Alys."

The compliment lands heavy and soft. You feel pride, but there is also the old habit of checking the ledger in your head: how many mats, how many volunteers, how many days until the next storm. You breathe and let the harbor noise fill the spaces.

A young volunteer lugs an extra sandbag, nearly toppling into a dock post. You reach out to steady her and find your hand brushing Ivo's forearm as he leans past. There's a quiet, unexpected current in that contact — a reminder of small promises already forming between you.

[Scene: Muri Research Lab | Afternoon]
[Visual: Shipping-container lab on a raised platform; LED panels, glass tanks of seagrass, and a wall of monitors]
[Sound: Quiet beeps of sensors, murmured conversation]
[Smell: Sterile ozone undercut by wet earth]
[Music: Soft piano theme — hopeful curiosity]

Back at the lab, Leyna walks you through the data dashboards. Charts blossom across the glass wall: establishment curves, turbidity declines, a slow uptick in juvenile fish detections. Every metric is a small, stubborn victory.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "If we keep this trajectory for another month, we'll have a statistically significant improvement in edge stability. The community's living shell is showing measurable effect."

You: "That's the headline we need. Not flashy investment, but real numbers that we can point to — to the council, to the press, to people who decide whether to keep their boats in the water."

Leyna pauses, tapping a sequence of readings, then looks at you with a scientist's urgency softened by human care.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "There's also a social metric we can track. Attendance at the harbor day tripled, volunteers stayed longer, and donation jars—"

She trails off. The lab door opens, and Celine Harrow steps into the fluorescent light like a ship's prow cutting into harbor fog. Her coat is immaculate; her smile is practiced but kind. Behind her, a municipal aide carries a slim envelope and an official-looking folder.

Celine Harrow: "Hello, Alys. Leyna. The work looks... alive." (She gestures, taking in the tanks with a careful eye.) "I thought I'd see for myself before council. The town deserves to know their money — and their choices — are in steady hands."

You feel the room narrow for a beat. Her presence is a formal current running under the workaday rhythm of the lab. You check your internal ledger again: past aid, political tradeoffs, compromises whispered in council corridors. But today the charts and the harbor's small applause give you a steadier footing.

You: "Mayor Harrow. Thank you for coming. The results have been promising — biological establishment, community engagement. We've been cautious about releasing preliminary numbers, but the trends are consistent."

Celine studies the graphs, then looks at you with the evenness she has worn like armor.

Celine Harrow: "Consistent is what this town needs. You should be proud. There are meetings next week; I want your findings included in the briefing. We have investors and partners asking for clear evidence of impact."

There's an undertone to the word 'partners' that you recognize: potential outside interests, quick fixes in neat contracts. You keep your voice neutral but don't let the opportunity to press for transparency pass.

You: "We'll present the methodology. If any partners want access to our protocols or datasets, it should be through community oversight. This isn't just data — it's people's livelihoods."

Celine's eyes flick for the briefest moment — a crack of calculation that you notice only because you're made to notice things like that. When she responds, her smile regains its polish.

Celine Harrow: "Community oversight is reasonable. We all want the same thing: Saltwick safe and thriving. I trust we can find a path that preserves the science and scales up protection."

The conversation hums with subtext: her offer is an opening, not yet an anchor. You feel gratitude for attention and a small, persistent wariness about the strings that come with rapid investment.

Ivo stands by the doorframe, hands crossed. His posture is open but guarded.

Ivo Calder: (to Celine) "We appreciate the support, Mayor. Just make sure it doesn't become a story about who profits from this town. People here aren't assets to be monetized."

Celine: (inclining her head) "I wouldn't dream of it. We will be careful."

The words hang between you all, a weathered promise waiting to be tested. You notice Oba's face in the lab doorway — he has been listening from the side — his expression is unreadable, lined with memory.

Oba Kofi: (softly) "Careful words do not always mean careful hands. But they are a start. We must watch and keep our maps."

You think of the maps you and Oba will carry to the council — not just lines of topography, but stitched with the living, breathing data of seedlings and fish, of people's returns to the docks. The pilot successes have given you a language to speak in a room where words can change policy.

[Scene: Old Lighthouse Rooftop Greenhouse | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Golden hour deepening; seedlings glow like tiny promises]
[Sound: Wind picking up; the bell of the harbor ringing in the distance]
[Music: Swells into a warm, hopeful chord]

You return to the greenhouse as the sun leans low. The day has been one of many small triumphs stitched together: mats anchored, hydroponic racks populated, data that sings in favor of what you've been trying to prove. Volunteers cleaned up, laughter trailing behind them like a wake. The town's mood has shifted — not a revolution, but a tide moving inland.

Ivo joins you on the narrow path between benches, shoulder brushing yours in a casual, grounding way.

Ivo Calder: "You did good today. The town showed up. That's half the battle."

You: "We did good today," you correct, because the truth is a net you both held. "But council night will be the real measure. We need to be clear, unwavering, and honest."

Ivo: (half-smiling) "When has being honest been a problem here? It's the politics in between that mess things up. But whatever comes, we do it together. If the council wants drama, we'll give them science and people who can hold the line."

You let the warmth in his words echo. There's the beginning of something tender in the spaces between work and promise — small, cautious, like the seedlings that insist on life despite storms. You are aware of the choice ahead: how to present the pilot's success without being co-opted; how to hold community oversight while accepting the scaffolding a mayor can provide.

Leyna appears with two mugs of tea, damp hair tucked behind her ears.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "You should rest, both of you. Strength for the council. And bring these graphs — they're clean and honest. People respond to clarity."

You close your notebook, the pen's mark still fresh. Outside, the wind carries a scent of the sea — salt and something green. The town's bell tolls again, smaller now, steady. You stand on the greenhouse roof, looking down at the harbor where nets hang like a reminder of livelihoods and at the lab where monitors blink like small constellations.

There is a sense of momentum: small data points converging into a believable trend, people's faces tilting towards hope, even Celine's calibrated attention edging into something usable. The landscape of decisions ahead feels less like a chasm and more like a series of fords you can cross if you coordinate.

You imagine the council chamber — Harrow Hall — with its heavy benches and the smell of old wood, and you know the next week will demand clarity and courage. You tuck the pen into your notebook and let your fingers rest over the underlined promise.

A long breath. Then another.

You are tired, but not worn through. The town is showing you that things can change.
