[Scene: Harrow Hall | Morning]
[Visual: The council chamber's high windows catch a pallid, washed sunlight; dust motes drift through a shaft of light that feels warmer than the air]
[Music: Light ascending strings — tentative, hopeful]
[Sound: A distant carriage of tools, a muffled hammering from the docks, a single gull]

You step into Harrow Hall with the weight of yesterday's vote still humming in your chest. Taking Celine's offer wasn't an end — it was a lever. Today is about turning that lever without crushing the delicate things that make Saltwick a place worth saving.

The chamber smells faintly of old wood and lemon oil — the mayor's attempt at civility. Your notebook is closed in your jacket; the brass compass at your throat is cool against your skin like a small, stubborn promise.

Celine Harrow stands by the long table, dossier under one arm, the calm plane of her face a practiced shore against any storm of emotion. When she sees you she tilts her head with a small, precise smile.

Celine Harrow: "Alys. Thank you for meeting so early. The council wanted momentum. The funds are ready to disburse if we can agree on the terms."

You: "Momentum is what the town asked for. I'm here to make sure it's momentum we can live with."

Celine's smile widens, eyes sharp as polished steel.

Celine Harrow: "Agreed. Rapid repairs, retrofits, embankments. Visible, fast, and—" (she taps the dossier) "—accountable. The investors want certainty. The municipal bond frees the money, but there are conditions. Oversight, timelines, benchmarks."

You feel your breath catch in a measured way — not panic, but the precise recognition of trade-offs. The word "conditions" tastes like salt that will not wash out.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz arrives moments later, tablet in hand, data pulsing across the screen like an impatient tide. She sets it between you and Celine and leans forward, the scientist's intensity lighting her face.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "If we sequence the embankments with targeted restoration pockets, we can minimize habitat loss. We need a clause for environmental monitoring — real-time metrics for seagrass health and turbidity."

Celine's fingers hover over the dossier.

Celine Harrow: "We're open to monitoring. Investors want to see progress, not process. Their timeline is tight. They want sealed contracts for procurement to guarantee contractors' performance."

You: "Sealed contracts can be written to include obligating language — environmental milestones that must be met for disbursement."

Celine: (a soft, calculating laugh) "Of course. But the harder the obligations, the slower the money flows. The council doesn't want to lose the window where public confidence still leans toward action."

You imagine the faces outside these windows — Maya, Oba, the row of fishers who needed their gear secured before the rains come. You imagine their relief if the machines begin moving tomorrow.

You: "We need both: immediate protection that preserves lives and living systems that make protection durable. Can we write adaptive clauses? Short-term draws for emergency measures and conditional draws for ecological benchmarks?"

Celine studies you. For a beat her expression is unreadable — the Schrodinger glare of someone with both admiration and a political ledger. Then she leans in, voice lowering to the kind of diplomacy that could turn a tide.

Celine Harrow: "Adaptive language is negotiable. But be aware — some clauses will bind procurement to certain firms with proven delivery records. They expect standardized materials. That may mean engineered seawalls in certain sectors rather than living breakwaters. We can pilot living systems in designated sites, but the majority of early funding targets immediate reinforcement."

You feel a small, honest grief — the compromise between your vision and what can be built quickly. Yet even grief, in this room, is shaded with possibility: you can make those pilots count.

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "If we accept that, we must ensure those pilot sites are sited for maximal ecological return. And we must have the right to pause disbursements if turbidity—"

Celine Harrow: "I'll allow the right to pause for cause, yes. But pausing has political cost. The council will be pressed by constituents who will see halted front-line work as a failure."

You lean on the lectern, feeling the grain under your palm, the tiny splinters like reminders of work not yet finished.

You: "Let's be explicit then. We accept an initial tranche for coastal reinforcements with a defined percentage reserved for immediate homeowner protection and emergency shelters. The remaining funding will be contingent upon phased environmental benchmarks, independently audited, with a neutral arbitrator named."

Celine's eyes flick toward Leyna, who gives a short nod. For a breath, you both hold a precarious bridge across two urgencies.

Celine Harrow: "Naming an arbitrator is acceptable. I propose the municipal inspectorate and a third-party engineering firm. They'll ensure bid compliance. I can have my legal team draft provisional language today; council can fast-track it."

You: "Fast-track with sunset clauses that allow us to renegotiate if the ecological pilots show a better cost-benefit. We need escape hatches for innovation."

Celine: (a faint, almost imperceptible softening) "Alys, you used the right word at the hearing: stewardship. I've always said we need stable finances. If stewardship becomes demonstrably more efficient, the investors will listen. But we must prove we can deliver, in numbers, on schedule."

The meeting ripples through a dozen small exchanges — timelines, deliverable definitions, oversight panels. Each concession is a tide moving sand in a new direction. You keep returning to the ledger in your head: lives saved now versus habitat protected for the future.

[Sound: A soft scrape as someone pushes a chair back in the hall; the gulls are louder now]

Dr. Leyna Ortiz: "I'll draft the monitoring protocol that locks in turbidity and seagrass coverage thresholds. If the politicians accept independent rollout criteria, we can protect both people and the ecosystem."

Celine: "Then we have a path. I can ask the council for an emergency ordinance that authorizes immediate disbursement upon signature. The first crews can break ground within forty-eight hours."

Something in the room shifts — a small, electric lift. This is what momentum looks like on paper and in people's faces. You imagine the cranes at the harbor, the sandbags replaced by engineered supports bolstered by the town's money. You feel a warm prickle of something like victory.

You: (quietly) "Forty-eight hours is life-changing to some families here."

Celine Harrow: "Exactly my point. Action begets trust."

Your chest eases. For the first time since you returned to Saltwick, the future feels like a thing that can be shaped by hands instead of merely survived.

[Scene: Saltwick Harbor | Late Morning]

You step onto the dock. The light is crisp; the seawall you dreamed of is, in its first honest form, beginning to exist. The sight of machines — ugly, hulking, practical — moving to protect people's houses makes your chest feel taut and full in a way that is almost religious.

Ivo meets you halfway down the dock, a grease-streaked grin under his cap. He looks like a man who has waited a long time to see a machine in this position.

Ivo: "They started early. Figured the council would want boots in the sand. How'd you do it, chief negotiator?"

Ivo: "Good. People are already hauling up sandbags to where the crew's staging. You: "That's the plan. Pay scale's been split. Immediate protections for homes; pilots for living systems. We'll watch the environmental triggers."

Ivo: (sighs) "Alright. If the town sees results in days, they'll rally. If not, they blame someone. You know how it is."

Ivo: "Promise me you won't let them cut every corner for optics."

You: "I won't. Not while I can stop it."

He nods, and the two of you fall into a companionable silence punctuated by the clatter of work. The harbor feels less like a place of loss and more like a place of repair; the sound of industry is almost musical now, a rhythm of resistance.
