[Scene: Embankment | Early Morning]
[Visual: Pale, oily dawn; rows of prefabricated concrete tetrapods stack like teeth along the shore. Steam rises from the diesel engines of cranes. A new, raw seam of concrete curves along where dunes once hummocked. Salt-spray beads on your jacket.]
[Sound: Metal on metal; distant gulls crying; the repetitive thump of a pile driver. Under it, the low murmur of a crowd and a mayoral PA testing its clarity.]

You keep your hands in your pockets because there is a cord of cold in the February air that goes straight to the bones. Your notebook is in the left pocket; the compass beneath the fabric is a drum of cool brass against your ribs. You told the council yes. You told them the town needed a wall that night, and Celine gave it — funded, contracted, and scheduled with a speed that felt like salvation.

Now the speed has a sound.

You walk the line of the embankment slow enough to read all the small things the machines will erase: a spray of dune grass still clinging to a root, a child's plastic bucket half-buried, the crooked mooring post with the carved initials you used to trace when you were thirteen. The pile driver thumps and you feel the echoes in your stomach like someone pounding a stake into the memory you keep.

Maya finds you first, paint-splattered apron and a thermos pressed to her hands. She stands a few feet away, face flushed against the wind, eyes scanning the wall where workers fit a final seam.

Maya: "You can see how quick they are. It's... impressive."
You: "Quick for what, though? To stop the tide this season, yes. To stop what we mourn forever, no."
Maya: (her voice tight) "I know why you stood at the lectern. I know you wanted oversight. But there were mouths to feed. We voted."

You want to argue that the vote didn't read the fine print. You want to say the clauses were insertion after insertion: emergency eminent-domain clauses thin as rice paper; concessions that let private companies lay claim to slips and storage yards; a 'maintenance fee' for access that turned a communal dock into a toll booth. But you also know exactly how those votes landed. You said yes because Celine's spreadsheet showed numbers that read like answers to people who'd lost roofs and seasons.

Ivo arrives with his workshirt dusted white from cement, his high-visibility vest at his arm. He stands between you and the sea like a small, familiar lighthouse — warm, blunt, unafraid of confronting engines.

Ivo: (soft) "You okay?"
You: "Do you want the truth or the apology version?"
Ivo: (a half-smile that doesn't quite land) "Both. Start with the truth."

The conversation tilts slowly, everyone swapping sentences like stones until they land with weight.

Ivo: "They stopped the last surge last month. Kids slept through the storm. Ronan's parents didn't have to row out with flashlights. That's real."
You: "It's real, and it's not everything. The wall's a bandage if it severs access or lets corporations lock the harbor gates. The seagrass beds are being bulldozed in the name of 'clearance' for the wall's foundation. You saw Leyna's note — we lose nursery grounds if they finish the revetment this way."
Ivo: "Leyna's note will get us mitigations. People needed a wall tonight, Alys. You saw their faces."

There is love in the way he says your name and fury in the way you can't let it be the final word. The conversation lengthens — an argument so careful it reads like conversation — because neither of you are monsters. You try to stitch meaning from shared priorities.

You: "I asked for clauses, for community liaison, for an inspection fund. The contracts came back with 'municipal maintenance' spelled into private hands. It's a legal cage wrapped in concrete."
Ivo: (after a long exhale) "Maybe we didn't write the law fast enough. Maybe we didn't convince enough people to trust the slower fix."
You: "Or maybe trust was traded for speed we couldn't foresee. We chose, and that choice narrows the landscape for everything that follows."

The foreman — or the company's designated liaison, a woman with a corporate smile and a windproof coat — repeats the talking points with the exact cadence of a rehearsed speech. Celine arrives behind her like an author at the opening of her chapter: immaculate coat, a ribbon glinting in the sun, PR flanked by officials and camera rigs. She surveys the wall as if admiring a new city seam.

Celine: (smiling, official) "This is what decisive leadership looks like. Fast, effective, and funded. Saltwick will be safer."
You: (to Celine) "Safer by what measure? People will be safer inside their homes, but when access becomes privatized, who defines 'safety'?"
Celine: (compassionate, sharp) "Who pays for it determines who has access. Without funds, ideals become slogans. We bought protection. There will be oversight panels."

Her 'will be' sits in your ears like an unpaid debt. Oversight panels, when convened, are gloved in municipal process; they can be helpful, but they can also be placations: structures of control that drain urgency.

[Sound: A ribbon-cutting cheer rises; cameras click. The pile driver keeps thumping in a percussion that steadies the scene and drowns some words.]

You try to swallow the bitterness, an internal sourness that feels like the salt in your mouth after a storm. You have proof of the clauses now — the dotted lines that will change who owns the harbor slips, the easements that allow contractor-controlled maintenance. Ronan comes bounding up, face bright with the triumph of the finished shore.

Ronan: "Look! They did the patch near the old boathouse. It's like a ramp now. We can haul the skiff without waist-deep muck."
You: (kneeling, touching the sand) "That's good work. It helps people. But this—" (you gesture toward the marked-off marsh beyond the wall) "—the nursery meadows were bulldozed for a foundation. That doesn't come back in a season."

Your voice frays. Around you, people cheer because roofs didn't fly off and barns are still in place. Practical relief has a sound: laughter, the clink of cups, the relief of warm hands in gloves. Your internal voice knows the town needed breathing room; it also knows the breathing room was bought on terms that were not simply financial.

[Scene: Harbor | Midday]
[Visual: The new seawall juts like a fresh scar. A makeshift sign hangs on a gate: PRIVATE MAINTENANCE ACCESS. Below it, smaller stencil letters: PAYMENT REQUIRED FOR COMMERCIAL HOIST ACCESS. Fishermen cluster near the mouth, their faces shaded and wary.]
[Sound: The chatter is more muted now; the harbor's mechanical gull calls replace seabirds as cranes hiss. The scent of fresh concrete and diesel overlays the salt.]

You organize the protest because you cannot not do something. You never were one to let injustice pass in silence. You write flyers in chipped letters, and Ronan helps paste them. You stand at the edge of the privatized boatyard and speak into a borrowed megaphone until your throat raw, not for the cameras but for the people who will be priced out of the slips their families built.

Alys (you): "This wall protects homes. It doesn't have to take our sea. It doesn't have to sell our harbor!"
Neighbor: (from the crowd) "Then what do you want, Alys? Another plan that takes three years? We needed roofs on nights like last winter."
You: "We want both. We wanted a plan that didn't put our harbor up for auction."

Celine watches from a small, elevated platform. She claps at the right moments and smiles so the cameras have something tidy to catch. When a reporter pushes a mic forward, she leans in with practiced cadence.

Celine: "We will negotiate community access. Contracts are living documents. We will ensure Saltwick's heritage is respected while securing infrastructure that safeguards lives."

The phrase lands like a promise. But the contracts are already clear. Contracts are not living in the rhetorical sense; they are living in the legal ones, with clauses that shift liability and ownership in subtle legalese. You hand a copy to Ronan; he reads the fine print and goes pale.

Ronan: "This 'maintenance fee'... If they charge per haul, my parents can't afford to keep the skiff tied up here in summer. They'll work the tides away from Saltwick."
You: (quiet) "We already did the math in my head. We knew some would be priced out. We told ourselves it was the cost of safety."

Ivo stands just off the picket line, arms folded, watching you. His jaw works. He approaches when the crowd starts to thin.

Ivo: (low) "You planned a rally without telling me?"
You: "You knew I would. You knew I'd try. You told me once that speed was protection. Now I'm asking where speed ended and sale began."
Ivo: (pained) "I didn't sign this with joy. But it kept roofs on heads. We saved people."
You: "At what horizon? At the cost of a harbor we all built? At the cost of seagrass nurseries that feed next year's catch?"

There is a silence full of history between you — of shared riverbeds where you once splashed as children, of nights on the harbor making lists, of two people who said they'd shoulder the town together. He reaches for your hand but stops short, as if some distance has been measured and now sits between you.

[Scene: Privatized Boatyard | Dusk]
[Visual: An orange sodium light pools over a new metal gate. A bright company logo has been painted on the boatyard wall. Boats that were once painted with hand-lettered names now wear registration stickers and a barcoded access tag. A small crowd has thinned to a handful of stubborn faces.]
[Sound: A lone radio plays a sea shanty softened by distance. The air carries the faint chemical tang from curing concrete. The wind in your hair is colder; the tide lifts, indifferent.]

You walk the perimeter as the sun goes down. The wall's shadow is long and in the shadow the shapes you know feel different. Oba Kofi is there, leaning on his carved cane, watching the boats like someone watching children leave for a strange future.

Oba Kofi: (soft) "They have anchored the shore like it was a coin. We keep what we can."
You: (kneeling beside him) "Is it still our shore if someone else controls the gate?"
Oba Kofi: (his eyes, small and ancient) "It is until the gate has a key that is not shared. Remember the old songs. They teach us how to name the water so it remembers us."

You want to chant the names, to anchor the harbor with words. You put your hand on the carved wood of Oba's cane and feel the grooves where other hands laid theirs. You remember that protection without belonging alters something irreparably.

Celine's photograph will be in the papers the next day: the wall; the smiling mayor; a ribbon. The seawall will be touted as evidence that Saltwick can be saved with money and decisiveness. And—practical, unglorious truth—roofs stayed down that night when the storm came in December. That real, immediate shelter will hum under the propaganda.

But the town's coastline, the way it's held and handed down, has been altered.

You write in your notebook in the shelter of your coat: "We chose speed. We bought shelter. We sold access."

Your breath fogs in the light, and you feel the weight of that sentence. Not the legal weight — the moral one that sits in the chest and doesn't let you sleep.

Maya finds you at the gate again. She slides a small carved token into your palm: a strip of old rope braided with sea-glass beads.

Maya: "My mom made these. For remembering. For when everything else feels like it gets repainted."
You: (closing your fingers around the beads) "I will keep it with the compass."

You look once more at the seawall — uniform, unyielding, and efficient. You can see already how the new geometry channels tides differently; Leyna's sketches about salinity shifts crowd your mind. The nursery flats will be silted. The small algal patches that clung to the old dunes will be gone. Some of the town's language of living with the sea — of building with its rhythms instead of fighting them — will be harder to teach to the next generation.

Ivo comes up, his face open and exhausted. He does not touch you. He speaks like someone offering an imperfect truce.

Ivo: "We kept people dry. I'm proud of that."
You: "I am too. I'm proud of that and I am grieving others. Can't both things be true?"
Ivo: (quiet) "Yeah. Maybe both can be."

You realize grief doesn't always mean bitterness. Sometimes it is a slow, patient thing that sits beneath the real. The town will hum with the practical wins and the invisible losses. Celine will be praised. Developers will call this a model. Families will sleep through storms. Some fishermen will be forced to move their boats. Some slips will be rented for prices locals can't meet.

You close your notebook and slide the braided rope against the brass of the compass.

You want to speak — to keep protesting, to push for reversal, to litigate, to draw Leyna's data into a public record that can't be quietly overridden. You want to hold onto something that feels like the town's soul. You want to reach for Ivo and articulate a future where the two of you keep building, together.

But the day is done. The wall is in place. The ribbon was cut. The contracts have already been waved through the municipal line as necessary. There will be hearings and committees and maybe a lawsuit or two, but the seawall sits now like a truth everyone has to live beside.

You stand at the water's edge until the light goes soft and the cranes hum into the background like the steady heartbeat of a town that made a fast, costly choice.

You lift your compass and look at the small needle — not because you need direction, but because it is the artifact of a man who believed in charting a course with patience. The needle trembles, then settles.

You tuck the compass back into your pocket, the rope bead warm in your palm. You write one last line in the margins of your notebook, the handwriting small and precise: "Remember why."

[Visual: The camera pulls back to show the seawall snaking along the coast, people moving like small figures in its shadow. The harbor light blinks. The sign at the boathouse reads PRIVATE ACCESS. The tide moves, indifferent and continuing.]
[Music: A minor, slow chord—low strings carrying a resigned, sorrowing melody. No triumph, only the honest ache of loss and the stubbornness to endure.]

You breathe and the breath leaves you like a small rebellion against being resigned.

You do not know whether the town will remember you as the one who saved roofs or the one who stood up to sell the harbor. Both are true. Both will be part of how Saltwick tells itself stories about this winter five years from now.

You fold the notebook into your coat and take one last look at the harbor. The seagrass beds are gone near the new foundation — but farther out, where the wall cannot reach, a thin fringe of green stirs. It will be harder to nurture. It will take longer. It may survive.

You set your shoulders against the wind and walk away from the privatized gate, toward the town that stitched itself with a seam that costs more than concrete.

[Scene: Quiet Street Outside Boatyard | Night]
[Visual: Streetlamps halo in puddles. A single lamplight catches the silver in your hair where saltwater lightened a strand.]
[Sound: The hush of town nights; a dog barks distant. Somewhere, the harbor bell rolls once — a long, steady note.]

You think of the people who will read your notes, who will inherit both the wall and the leash of its clauses. You think of Ronan hauling skiffs that might soon be kept at other harbors. You think of Celine's smile in the papers and of Oba's quiet chant.

You cannot undo the wall. You can only keep insisting on the parts that still can be held: public oversight, emergency funds, legal challenges, community gardens, seed banks. You can teach the children the old names for the tides. You can sew the torn edges of belonging back together in ways that won't fully undo the damage but that might make the town remember how to be a town, not a commodity.

You stop at the top of the lane, set the notebook beside the compass, and let the wind take the salt from your face. The town hums below, alive and altered. The sense of duty that brought you back has hardened into something like resolve — bruised but persistent.

You breathe in, and out. You close your eyes for a moment to feel the solid, honest weight of the cold air in your lungs.

You tuck the rope bead against your palm, feel its edges, and then slip the compass back into the pocket where its brass presses against your ribs.

The seawall will stand. The town will adapt around it. You will keep working. You will keep asking for the measures that make protection mean belonging.

You begin to walk home.
