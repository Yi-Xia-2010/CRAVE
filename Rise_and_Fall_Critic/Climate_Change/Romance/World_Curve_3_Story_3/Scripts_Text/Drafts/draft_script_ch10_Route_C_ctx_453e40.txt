### DRAFT
# Route: Route C — Sabotage


[Scene: Dunes — After the Decision | Dusk]
[Visual: Wide shot — the barricade frays at the edges; gulls wheel in an oil-slate sky]
[Sound: Wind combing over sandbags; a distant horn from a fishing trawler]
[Music: Low, weathered cello; a steady, minor-key ache]

Narration:
You chose the path no one asked for aloud. You chose to be the hand that draws the line under the town's maps and says which neighborhoods will stay, which will be let go, and how people will breathe after they move. The choice sits like a stone in your throat—heavy, necessary, and cold.

Finn is beside you, quiet in a way that makes the world shrink to the size of his shoulders and the wind between them. Elias is halfway between the barricade and the dunes, lips pursed against the wind. Noah stands a little apart, hands hidden, as if the math of compromise were something he keeps in his pockets. You feel all of them like heat and pressure on the same membrane.

You fold the page of your notebook back over the ink you wrote earlier—schemes, contingencies, supply chains. The compass at your throat presses, warm against the cord. The red thread around your wrist tugs at the promise you made and the weight you have now accepted.

[Scene: High Tide Laboratory | Night]
[Visual: Sodium lamps throw orange pools across wet steel; instrument panels blink like small constellations]
[Sound: The constant slap of ocean against hull; faint beeps of tide sensors]
[Music: A taut single violin — tension held just before release]

Narration:
The lab smells of ozone and seaweed and the kind of coffee that has been reheated twice too many times. Dr. Hana Park moves through the light with small, efficient gestures, as if she can arrange data the way one arranges anchors. She sets a tablet down and taps; a projected map blooms over the metal table, tide-lines braided into layers like tree rings.

Dr. Hana Park: "You came alone," (she says, not accusation, more a cataloging of fact) "and you brought conviction."

You: "I brought a plan," you answer, more steady than you feel. "Or at least, the start of one."

Dr. Hana Park: (leaning in, fingers hovering over the projection) "Tell me what you mean by 'plan.' Speak technically. I need the scaffolding under the rhetoric."

Narration:
There it is—her request for scaffolding. The scientist in her wants the bones. The leader in you wants the warmth of people to soften them. You inhale the sterile air and lay out the bones anyway: phased relocation, living shorelines where possible, formalized buyouts for the most at-risk blocks, a Rooftop Sanctuary relay to hold people and supplies during peak tides, and floating caches that double as community pantries.

You watch Hana's face through the orange light—eyes shadowed, jaw working as she sorts the ethical weight.

Dr. Hana Park: "Phased relocation is the cleanest way to reduce mortal risk. But—" (she taps an area of the map and the buildings there bloom red) "—sacrificing historic blocks is not just urban planning. It's cultural amputation."

You: "I know," you whisper. "People's memories are there. Lives folded into porches and family rooms. But if we don't move some lines voluntarily, the water will decide for us and we'll lose more than houses."

Dr. Hana Park: (quiet; she sets her hand flat on the table) "There's a moral calculus here. Compulsory buyouts, staged evacuations—where you draw the line will define who survives and who remembers. Are you ready for that authority?"

Narration:
The question isn't rhetorical. You feel its teeth. Leadership has suddenly become a ledger and a weapon. Part of you wants to step back and let the slow politics bleed the edges of the crisis; another part, the part threaded with the neighbor you lost, will not let that be an option.

Dr. Hana Park: "If we go this way, we have to be surgical. Evacuation windows, redundancy in supply lines, real shelter capacity—not promises. The Rooftop Sanctuaries can be linked with solar pumps and desal units, but they'll need formal agreements with the building owners and a legal framing for temporary land use."

You: "Rosa can mobilize café networks. Finn can—" (you stop, because naming people turns planning into risk) "—people will come if they can see a plan that keeps their kids and their dogs alive and gives them a place to sleep that isn't a wet school gym for months."

Dr. Hana Park: (a small, almost fond smile) "You always think in people, Maya. It's why you'll be a good—but lonely—leader."

Narration:
Her words fall like a recorded tide—predictable and inevitable. You grimace, feel the loneliness like cold steel at your ribs.

Narration:
Hana watches the exchange—whether you revealed scaffolding or warmed it with story, her expression is the same: measured, slightly distant, as if she is also calculating how much of you is left after the giving.

Dr. Hana Park: "If you release the map to the town all at once," she says, "expect a moral hurricane. People will feel betrayed and relieved in the same breath. If you withhold details, expect rumors. Either way, your name will be on it."

You: (a laugh that tastes like iron) "I know. I know that. That's why I'm here with you."

Dr. Hana Park: (firm) "Then we make redundancies. We stagger release windows. We make sure evacs are voluntary but incentivized where necessary. And we design living shorelines where feasible—marsh plantings, oyster reefs, permeable breakwaters—to buy us time in the places we can save."

Narration:
Her fingers sweep through the projected map, weaving data into rope: tide probabilities, habitation density, social vulnerability indices. You watch numbers become a lifeline.

[Scene: Rooftop Sanctuary (Reclaimed Mall Garden) | Dawn]
[Visual: The garden glows with dew; solar fabric sags slightly with condensation; bees are already at work]
[Sound: Soft rustle of leaves, distant murmur of morning traffic]
[Music: Soft, low piano—tender but laced with melancholy]

Narration:
At dawn, the Rooftop Sanctuary smells of wet soil and basil, a green island above the cold glass bones of a repurposed mall. Rosa is already there, apron pulled over a rain sweater, hands plunged into a tote of seed packets and thermos flasks. Her smile lights the space like a fragile lamp.

Rosa Alvarez: "You're insane," she says by way of greeting, but it's warm. "You're also terrifyingly right."

You: "We need relay points," you tell her, laying out a hand-drawn map. "Rooftop gardens that can host people for twelve to forty-eight hours during tidal surges, linked by boat and by community volunteers. Solar pumps, desal units—"

Rosa Alvarez: (interrupting, practical as always) "And coffee. Do not forget the coffee. People will panic less if they have coffee."

Narration:
She laughs and your chest loosens a notch. Small things anchor people; caffeine is, inconveniently, one of them. Rosa's warmth is contagious. It spreads into the sketches, smooths some of the edges.

You: "We start mapping tonight. Finn can help with boat logistics. Rosa, you handle community outreach and food. Hana will secure the scientific angles. I—" (you stop, aware that listing roles is a way of assigning blame and burden) "—I will coordinate and—"

Elias Novak: (arriving with coat askew, eyes wide) "God, Maya. This is brave. This is you at your most human. But you're isolating yourself. You're building an island of decisions and putting everyone else's future in the center of it."

You: "Isn't that what leaders do sometimes? Make the hard choice?"

Elias Novak: (stepping forward, voice soft) "Leaders also listen enough to keep people walking with them. You can't be the only one holding the map."

Narration:
He reaches as if to take the tablet, but instead he lays a hand on your shoulder—brief, grounding. The contact is warmth and warning. You feel both grateful and exposed.

Narration:
No choice feels clean. You can imagine the consequences like cracks forming in wet wood; each load-bearing decision shifts the structure of trust.

Finn arrives with rope and a small, stubborn grin. "I'll sleep on the boats," he says. "I'll check pumps at night. You tell me when and I'm there."

You: (soft) "You're not just a volunteer, Finn. You're my brother. If I ask something of you, you should be able to say no."

Finn: "Try me." (He grins, but the edges of his mouth are tired.) "I can do tired."

Narration:
You watch him go, the way he moves through the garden like a boy who knows every crack in the old pier. You feel gratitude and guilt braided together.

[Scene: A Construction Site Near the Boardwalk | Midday]
[Visual: Steel frames and cranes silhouette against a low, steely sun; Kestrel Marine signage flutters]
[Sound: The drone of machinery; a radio tinny with instructions]
[Music: Dissonant chords; a slow, grinding rhythm]

Narration:
Word spreads as words do in small towns—loud through doors and quiet through the back alleys. By midday someone has pinned a copy of your schematic to the café's corkboard: a rough map, a handful of bullet points, your name at the bottom in careful ink. Some people have circled the blocks slated for buyouts with red ink; others have crossed lines out.

You find Noah at the edge of a Kestrel worksite, sleeves rolled as if to keep his hands from trembling. He is an island of composure with a coffee that steams in the cold air.

Noah Kestrel: (softly) "You're shaking the whole scaffolding, Maya."

You: "That was the point."

Noah Kestrel: (hesitates; then) "There is a technical thing you might not have considered—if we stage temporary bulkheads at certain points, we can redirect surge flow and reduce immediate risk to intermediate blocks while we process buyouts. It's not perfect, but it buys time."

You: (surprised) "You mean let Kestrel design temporary barriers?"

Noah Kestrel: "I mean—" (he looks suddenly smaller) "—if you can't cooperate with firms, sometimes you can use their tools without their banners. There's a legal window where temporary defense structures can be placed under emergency ordinances. They require less funding up front, more labor. They also draw corporate eyes, which is dangerous."

Narration:
He looks at you, end of a sentence left in the air. Noah's suggestion is a quiet bridge; it implies cooperation and theft in the same breath. The possibility tastes of compromise and of betrayal.

You: (measured) "If I accept that, I hand them a door to say I agree to their larger plans. If I refuse, they will use the delay of negotiation to push through something worse."

Noah Kestrel: (softly) "I didn't come here to lecture. I came to say: there are small, technical moves that could save lives now without signing away the town. I'm offering one. Take it or don't."

Narration:
He steps back, the offer given and withdrawn like a delicate tool. You want to ask more—about funding, legalese, who he'd have to tell—but the look on his face tells you he already knows the consequence of crossing a company line.

Noah Kestrel: "Whatever you do," he says finally, "do it with the most defensible record you can make. You'll need it when they come for you."

Narration:
His words hang. He hands you a folded schematic—no branding, only angles and measurements—and walks away, the corporate logo on his cuff catching the weak sun. The town's reaction is already animating: hand-lettered signs, coffees shared in hushed groups, some windows turned away from you like faces in a crowd.

[Scene: Saltbridge Boardwalk — Evening]
[Visual: Rain-slick planks glinting lamplight; someone has painted 'Protect the Old Town' on plywood]
[Sound: The ripple of conversation; an undercurrent of argument]
[Music: Minor harmony grows louder, more insistent]

Narration:
By evening your phone vibrates with three kinds of voices: praise, fury, and pleading. Rosa keeps the café open late; people come in, faces raw. A woman you grew up with calls you "ruthless." A young teacher calls you "visionary." Someone pins a flyer calling you a traitor.

You walk the boardwalk like a person under glass, every reflection a different verdict. People cross to the other side of the street when they see you, or they approach with a Bible or a bottle of wine and ask if there's anything they can do. Being both leader and pariah is a new shirt—stiff, scratchy, impossible to get comfortable in.

Elias finds you leaning on the railing, looking out at the line where sea meets sky, and does not offer a speech. He simply stands.

Elias Novak: (quiet) "You did what you said you'd do."

You: "I did what I thought would keep people alive."

Elias Novak: (looking at you, amber eyes hard) "You're making choices that will change people's relationship to this town forever. Do you want to be the one who holds their grief for them?"

Narration:
His question lands like a wave on rock. You want to tell him that grief is not yours to hold alone; that you have always tried to share the burden and been refused. Instead you say something sharper.

You: "If not me, then who? The mayor? Corporations? People who will make choices based on balance sheets?"

Elias Novak: (soft laugh, crumpled) "None of them will do it like you. But none of them will forgive you the hard ones either."

Narration:
He steps back, the space between you widening into all the things unsaid. There is admiration in his eyes and a warning: bravery without companionship can become martyrdom.

[Scene: Your Small Apartment Above the Café | Night]
[Visual: The compass necklace catches lamplight; your boots still damp on the floor]
[Sound: The café's sink clinking below; the distant scream of gulls]
[Music: Sparse piano, a single, sustained note of loneliness]

Narration:
You sit with a mug gone cold and the plans spread around you like a constellation of obligations. Some windows of your life are closing as you map others open for different people. Your notebook is a litany: checklists for shelters, names of volunteers, legal contacts. You can feel the ledger growing under your hands.

There is a knock at the door. Finn peeks in, eyes tired but steady.

Finn: (soft) "You don't have to carry it all tonight."

You: "I know."

Finn: "You look like you're holding a funeral for something you still haven't lost."

Narration:
He catches your gaze; his presence a small, constant thing. Your thread-tied wrist rubs against the mug's handle. You realize that you are lonely in a new way: surrounded by people who need you, but separated by the decisions only you are willing to make.

You think of the town—of porch lights and salt-scarred paint, of the neighbor who drowned in a storm, of mornings in Rosa's café. The plan you keep to yourself is no longer only a set of maps and buyouts; it is a promise to prevent another grief like that one. But promises can burn.

Narration:
You breathe in, and the air tastes like metal. Your phone glows: messages—some with offers of support, some spitting accusations. The tide is both friend and enemy. You have pushed a stone into motion; now you must choose how to direct the avalanche.

[Music: The violin returns, lower, with more insistence—an approaching horizon of consequence]

Narration:
It all narrows to a single hinge again—the moment when plan becomes action and action becomes history. There are routes forward, each dangerous in its own way. You know the options and their costs. You also know that hesitation is an action in itself.
