[Scene: Saltbridge Boardwalk | Dawn]
[Visual: Soft watercolor dawn; wet wooden planks reflecting a pale, slate sky; salt streaks on railings]
[Sound: Low tidal hush, gulls in the distance, a lone bicycle bell faintly ringing]
[Music: Quiet, somber piano with restrained strings]

You press the dented compass against your sternum the way you used to in storms — as if its cool metal could steady your chest the way a hand sometimes steadies a child. The metal edge is warm from being tucked under your jacket; the small knot of red thread around your wrist rubs at the back of your hand like a question you haven't answered out loud.

The boardwalk is damp and smells of brine and frying oil. Every footfall throws up a soft, salt-clouded spray from the warped planks; the wood gives a tired, familiar creak that is almost a language you remember from childhood. You breathe it in — salt, seaweed, the faint iron tang of rust from old chains. A tide light enough that you can still see the boards, high enough that the sand has tried to claim the lower steps like an old argument.

Your notebook is under your arm, pages rough with tide logs and sketches of proposed berms and rain gardens. You thumb the edge. The handwriting there is small, practical: dates, predicted surge levels, names to call. Planning has always been good at quieting the panic — until today, when your chest is a kettle on the verge of whistling.

Rosa's café is the first light on the walk. Her window is a fragile lamp in the gray — warm honey spilling over salt-streak glass. The steam on the inside catches the light and makes the whole place feel smaller and safer than it has any right to be. You know that light; it has soothed council meetings and fed late-night volunteers and wrapped itself around arguments like a blanket.

Rosa is at the counter, apron tied, hair escaping its scarf in small curls. She sees you and grins, a crease forming that softens the salt-worn line of her face.

Rosa: "You look like you haven't slept. Or you slept badly with your notebook as a pillow again."

You attempt a smirk and fail. Your boots leave wet prints on the mat as you cross the threshold.

You: "Planner's insomnia. Also, the tide schedule and a very persuasive Moleskine."

Rosa: (low, teasing) "And the red thread — still wearing it like someone who won't let promises dissolve."

You reach to the thread without thinking; your fingers brush the tiny knot. It tightens in your memory: Finn's laugh when you both tied childish bracelets on the pier; your mother's voice telling you to keep it safe. The motion steadies and frays you in equal measure.

Rosa fills a cup without asking and slides it across. The smell — fried dough, coffee, something citrusy — sinks into you like an inheritance.

Rosa: "On the house. You look skittish, Maya. What's wrong?"

You pause with the cup at your lips. The town is waking up, but there's a different kind of stirring: little clusters of people on their porches, the mayoral seal blinking on a municipal post you scrolled through before leaving, a string of texts you haven't read. You could trace every rumor back to a single line item: funding. There was supposed to be federal match funding to unlock the modular sea-sill pilot. It was a hinge for the whole plan.

You set the cup down. The coffee is too hot for thought; it slices through the fog for a moment and leaves a bitter aftertaste you can almost taste like fear.

You: "They're delaying the match. The state's holding off on approval. I only got the bulletin this morning."

Rosa folds her hands on the counter, the vibration of the espresso machine humming beneath her palms.

Rosa: "Delay? Again? That'll gum up the contractors and the Mayor's office will seize the chorus to stall. People'll start choosing sides."

You watch the cafe window where a poster for the upcoming community forum flutters. The ink is salted, the edges soft with humidity. Consensus has always been a fragile construction in Saltbridge; delays are the termites.

You almost call Finn then, the way the habit forms itself in crises: one knot to another, brother to sister. Instead, the boardwalk door opens and Finn steps in, hair damp from the dawn and his jacket patched where the net tore last week. He smells like algae and rope and something that used to be the whole town: robust, unpolished, unpanicable.

Finn: (grinning) "Morning, Sis. You'd think the sea's the only one keeping predictable hours."

You: (soft) "Finn."

He drops a coil of rope on the table, knots already half-done. He lets you look at them — practiced, precise — like prayers he learned to whisper into twine.

Finn: "Heard about the delay from Rosa. Saw some folks back on the pier arguing with a pointing finger that looked like Mayor Lillian. They had papers. Your face is more thunder than cup, though."

You laugh, too sharp.

You: "Mayor Cho's office posted the notice. Something about 'further environmental assessments and budget recalibration.'"

Finn's face goes careful for a second, then bright again with that stubborn, practical optimism that knits up your heart even when it pinches.

Finn: (matter-of-fact) "So we do tests ourselves. We don't wait for get-rich-beside-the-seawall developers to tell us the tide's coming. We put out more sensors, map the hotspots. You make the plans, I'll make the knots. Simple."

His ease is both balm and blame. It reminds you of what you want to be — decisive, action-first — and what you keep being: someone who holds too many plans like plates and expects them not to wobble.

Rosa: "You're both going to need rest. And allies. Not just rope."

You look at Finn, at Rosa, at the smeared poster of last summer's volunteers. The cracks in the town's conversation are small now: grumbling about developers, whispers about 'resettlement' that older folks dread, younger people waving banners. You catalogue them like symptoms. The delay will do more than pause a project; it will give time for rumours, for private interests, for fear.

Your phone buzzes in your pocket. You stare at the screen. Elias' name lights it up — his amber eyes, that tendency to leap. You haven't yet decided whether you want a firebrand now or a careful, papered counterproposal.

You press the call.

[Sound: Phone connecting; wind tapping at the window]
[Visual: Close-up on your hand holding the phone; the compass pendant glinting faintly]

Elias: (bright, urgent) "Maya! I just saw — are they actually delaying the match? That's a call for action, not a delay. People need to know. We can get a crowd, a sit-in at the pier, something dramatic to make the Mayor look bad if she keeps stalling."

You feel his breathless energy through the line. It's like sunlight: aggressive and necessary. It makes your jaw ache with possibility and with the risk of burning what you want to protect.

You: (slow) "I know your playbook, Elias. But a sit-in could give the Mayor a pretext to clamp down. We need the match to unlock funds. If we blow up a demonstration before the assessment reports are public, we hand the narrative to the developers."

Elias: "Maya, we don't have time for waiting rooms. Waiting has been the policy for decades. If we don't pull people together, they'll let the mayor repackage a corporate plan as 'community safety' and the town is gone. We need pressure now — loud pressure."

You blink because it is true and because you have felt that impatient flame before. He has this way of making urgency feel like a moral imperative, which it sometimes is. But you've also seen what happens when good intention meets poor logistics: tents as liabilities, people hurt on the steps.

You: (quietly, heavier) "I don't want people hurt. We've built safety buffers into the pilot. If we lose the funding, those buffers don't happen. I can draft a targeted counterproposal that answers the assessment concerns, gives the council a face-saving route, and keeps the community voice central."

Elias: (a beat, then softer) "You always draft the maps, Maya. You always make the scaffolding for others' courage. I'm not saying stop — I'm saying I'll amplify. I'll bring the people. But you have to promise not to drift into committees when we need a front."

There's a familiar ache in his voice now: loyalty edged with fear. He almost always sounds like he's trying to get the town to stand up. You want to be that person too, but you also hear Finn's rope-and-nets pragmatism, Rosa's careful tending, the Mayor's slippery phrasing. Each route demands a different kind of courage.

You: (conflicted) "I can't be everywhere at once, Elias. I don't want to be the scaffolding for someone else's fire if I can't ensure it's safe. And I don't know if I should push you to lead public pressure or if I should write the counterproposal that could buy us time and keep people safe."

Elias: (forceful, then vulnerable) "Then let me lead the pressure. Let you be the brains. But don't go quietly into committees and let them drown us in procedure while we still have breath."

You both are quiet on the line for a long moment; the sea outside the café keeps whispering as if it has time to be patient for both of you.

You: (to yourself, a whisper) "I don't know how to ask for help without feeling like I'm giving up control."

Elias: (soft) "You don't have to carry it alone. Let someone else hold a corner."

He says it like it's a salvation; you hear it like a suggestion you aren't allowed to take without consequences.

You end the call with a promise you aren't sure you can keep: to consider leading the drafting while he organizes the pressure. But the weight of the compass seems heavier now, as if it's counting debts you've already accumulated.

The café's door swings as a regular comes in, the ordinary motion of Saltbridge refusing to be defined solely by headlines. You stand, the knot in your stomach mapped onto the wood grain under your boots.

You walk back to the boardwalk, notebook under your arm, each note you make echoing like a ledger: assessment items, points to counter in the Mayor's projected response, the names of community stewards. The town looks like a model just before it's dropped: tilted, fragile, and full of dangling pieces.

You pass the poster for the forum again. In your mind you can already hear the room: murmurs, a single microphone squealing feedback, the Mayor's practiced calm, and you — with a stack of pages that could either soothe or inflame.

You stop at the railing. The horizon is a thin, trembling line. The sea holds its breath. So do you.

Your hands go to the compass at your throat and then to the red thread at your wrist. Planning, for months, has been an answer. Now planning feels like a thin raft on a wider sea. Memory rises — the neighbor lost in the storm, the house that folded like wet paper, Finn's small, stubborn laugh as he teaches knot after knot. The compass is heavier with that image.

You could call Elias back and push him to mobilize right away. You could walk through the forum in your head and draft a counterproposal that addresses the assessment concerns before the Mayor frames anything. You could ask Rosa to host an emergency meeting, or you could go home and let the tide decide.

You: (internal, steadying) "What matters most? Safety, equity, continuity." The words fall like pebbles into the same pool and ripple into different shapes. Each would mean a different future for Saltbridge.

You do not choose yet. The options sit like stones at your feet, each a different weight and shape. The boardwalk winds on toward the town hall and toward the dunes where sandbags lean like sentinels. The day lightens marginally; the horizon does not promise mercy.

Your notebook is open on your knee. A blank page waits like an invitation and a dare. The dented compass lies heavy, and the red thread burns at your skin like a promise you have been carrying alone.

You fold your hands over the notebook and feel the tremor again — not the tremor of fear alone, but of something larger pressing in. The problem may be bigger than your plans. You know, in the marrow where all the quiet honesty lives, that this is true.

A gull cries and the town stirs more distinctly: a door slammed, the murmur of voices from the pier, someone hammering in the distance. The day is asking for an answer.

A small breath catches in your throat. You imagine the forum, the Mayor's calm voice, Finn's rope in your hands, Elias' urgency in your ears, Rosa's lamped window beside you. The choice is not yet made, and that slowness becomes unbearable.

You rise, notebook closed but heavy, and walk toward the Community Hall where the forum will be held — toward the next conversation that will either widen cracks or begin to mend them.
