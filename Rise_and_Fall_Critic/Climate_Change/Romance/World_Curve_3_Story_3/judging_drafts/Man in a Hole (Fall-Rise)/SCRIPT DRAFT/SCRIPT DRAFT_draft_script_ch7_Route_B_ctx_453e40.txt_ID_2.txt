Narration: The model hums. Buildings rise and recede on the screen like a breathing organism. Numbers march in neat columns: expected lives saved, economic loss mitigated, guaranteed uptime. The math is clean. It settles into your chest like a cold, efficient answer.

You: "And what's the trade?" (You don't say the words 'who loses' aloud; you don't have to.)

Noah Kestrel: "Buyouts in a few historic blocks where costs to retrofit are prohibitive. Narration: His use of your name is steady, professional. Each clause is a lever. You can hear the offer shaping itself: speed in exchange for control and territory.

Noah Kestrel: (He doesn't flinch.) "We have packages. Relocation assistance, job guarantees in the construction phases, stipends. It's not perfect, but it's measurable. Delay increases risk and cost — and decreases funding appetite from state levels."

Narration: He speaks in probabilities and contingency plans. It's intoxicating: a certainty disguised as a forecast.

You: "And the maintenance? Handing infrastructure over to a firm—"

Noah Kestrel: "—means professional, scheduled care, not patchwork repairs. There will be contracts. Legal frameworks. Accountability." (His eyes are steady, then briefly, something unreadable crosses his face before he masks it with a practiced smile.) "And the town keeps seats at the table."

Narration: The 'seats at the table' line is both promise and hedge. Your internal ledger ticks: lives potentially saved versus spaces permanently altered. Your chest tightens.
