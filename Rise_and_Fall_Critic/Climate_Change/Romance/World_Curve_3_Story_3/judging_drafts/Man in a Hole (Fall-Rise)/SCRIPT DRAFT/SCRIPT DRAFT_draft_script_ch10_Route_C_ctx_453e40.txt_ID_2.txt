[Scene: High Tide Laboratory | Dusk]
[Visual: Steel gangway slick with spray; sodium lamps pool orange light across instrument racks]
[Sound: Low thrum of generators, a buoy's faint ping off in the fog]
[Music: Sparse cello undercurrent — drawn, patient]

Narration:
You chose this path. You signed the line nobody wanted to sign and walked away from halting compromises and polished promises. The decision sits under your skin like a stone — hard, cold, impossible to ignore. The High Tide Laboratory smells of salt and ozone; your jacket is damp where the fog has braided into the fabric. Your compass necklace is quiet against your sternum, like a heartbeat you keep checking.

Dr. Hana Park is already there, sleeves rolled, a tablet balanced on a crate and printouts spread across a metal table. She looks up when you step into the orange circle of light: the way she does always — measured, as if cataloguing the exact weight of a problem before deciding whether to move it.

Dr. Hana Park: "You're late by three minutes and ten seconds," (she says, but there's relief under the reprimand). "Good. I wanted time alone with the models before you made them yours."

Narration:
Her voice carries through the lab like a thermometer — it measures temperature, then tells you what that temperature means. You set your wet boots on the grated floor and let the rusted railing stead you. Around you, instruments cast pale reflections on standing puddles; a paper coffee cup trembles in the corner, more from the generator's hum than from your hands.

You: "I needed to be sure I wasn't walking into a promise I couldn't keep." (You try for lightness; it frays at the edges.)

Dr. Hana Park: "No one promised you anything easy." (A beat.) "Show me what your 'third path' looks like on paper."

Narration:
You slide your notebook across the table. The pages catch the sodium light; hand-drawn maps mingle with lists of shelters, annotated tide lines, sketches of floatation caches. Hana leans over, fingertip tapping nodes: Rooftop Sanctuary relays, phasing thresholds, living shoreline buffer zones. Each tap leaves a faint salt print.

Dr. Hana Park: "Phased relocation, coupled with a distributed shelter network — it's scientifically defensible. You can buy time with living shorelines while you move people out of the most exposed blocks."

You: "Buy time, yes. But buyouts require capital. Staged evacuations require trust. And 'moving people' — it's not just logistics, Hana. It's ethics." (Your voice tightens.) "We ask people to give up homes built by grandparents. We write away places they call a childhood. How do we justify that?"

Dr. Hana Park: (She folds her hands, the light catching the silver thread at her temple.) "We justify it the same way we justify keeping people alive. There's moral clarity in triage that no politician ever wants to admit. But clarity doesn't make it easier."

Narration:
You listen to the lab's low language — beeps, fan blades sighing, the distant slap of the harbor against pylons. Hana's words are ropes you can climb or sink with.

Dr. Hana Park: "This will be politically explosive. 'Managed retreat' is a phrase people brand like a slur. But your plan — distributed shelters using the Rooftop Sanctuary network, floating supply caches, voluntary buyouts with community control — it's the model that avoids corporate capture. It keeps decision-making local." (She taps a line on the map.) "It formalizes sacrifice of some historic blocks to save others."

You: "Sacrifice." (The word is small, but it reaches the cracks.) "You mean telling an aunt her house is on the list and watching her shrink into the words 'acceptable loss.'"

Dr. Hana Park: "I mean telling her we can give her a roof that won't chew her up in the next surge." (She doesn't look away.) "You asked for a plan that refuses half-measures."

Narration:
You breathe in the lab's dry, metallic air. The plan sprawls in front of you like a map of compromises you haven't fully counted. Each dot is a life; each line is a promise you may not be able to keep.

[Scene: Rooftop Sanctuary (Reclaimed Mall Garden) | Night — Later]
[Visual: Warm golden lamps stitch shadows across raised beds; dew beads on netting]
[Sound: Bees asleep in small hives; distant muffled voices from the street]
[Music: Low piano, minor chord progressions]

Narration:
You climb the rusted fire stairs with a crate of printed maps under your arm. The Rooftop Sanctuary smells of damp soil and rosemary; the air is thick with the small, honest life of plants. Rosa is there, sleeves smeared with peat, handing out mugs of something hot that tastes like cardamom and goodwill.

Rosa Alvarez: "You look like you carried the whole ocean up here with you." (She smiles, but it doesn't reach the corners of her eyes.) "I made lists and a batch of empanadas. Volunteers are waiting."

You: "Thank you." (You take the mug like a talisman.) "We need a relay map. Rooftop to rooftop — people with mobility limitations get priority, then families with kids, then—"

Rosa Alvarez: (She interrupts gently.) "We all know what comes next, Maya. How do we convince folks their past matters less than their future?"

Narration:
Finn is at the edge of a planter, fingers stained, watching the town like someone learning to read weather charts. He says nothing for a moment, then offers, quietly:

Finn: "Make the plan certificated by the people who'd be moved. Let them sign the terms. No 'we know better' from you." (His jaw is set; he's older when he speaks of process.)

You: "We have to make it participatory." (You look at the relay points you've drawn — a chain of green dots like a lifeline across rooftops.) "And we have to staff the caches. Boats. Solar chargers. Food dry-stores in watertight bins."

Narration:
The crowd swells slowly as word spreads. Volunteers show up with bolt cutters, spare tarps, knitting for warmth, someone with a dinghy. You begin to assign tasks and your voice becomes a machine for making order out of chaos, but each command costs you a sliver of something — friends who will later become critics, neighbors who will talk late into the night about how you decided their fate.

Narration:
Elias appears in the doorway like an exhale. He steps into the lamp glow, film camera slung. His smile is the sort that gathers people, but tonight it doesn't chase away the shadow in your chest.

Elias Novak: "It's a beautiful plan on ugly ground." (He pokes at a sheet, genuine admiration in his voice.) "You're asking Saltbridge to do something brave."

You: "Or foolish." (You can't hide the edge.) "People will call it surrender, or radical, or both."

Elias Novak: (He looks at you for longer than the plan.) "You always wanted to save people without asking them to leave everything behind. This—" (He gestures toward the maps) "—is the first time I've seen you choose survival over a symbol."

Narration:
His words, warm as they are, are also a mirror: the difference between preserving a village and preserving what it means to be a village. There is no easy middle.

Elias Novak: "But I worry about how alone it'll make you. Leading looks like carrying. And you already do too much alone, Maya."

You: (You realize with a small, cold lurch that he's right.) "I know. And I know what happens when people stand on the outside and throw stones while we build bridges."

Elias Novak: "Then let people help build the bridges with you. Not just the hands — the stories. If you take the stories away, you'll be right: you'll have the shelter, but not the people who call it home."

Narration:
His plea rests against your ribs. You want to believe him, but the plan's architecture depends on tight schedules and a degree of secrecy to prevent panic. You can almost hear the headlines that would come if every detail leaked: "Town Spirals into Chaos" or "Heroine or Villain?"

[Scene: Construction Sites & Kestrel Marine Development Office — Dawn (brief cutaway)]
[Visual: Half-built pylons, cranes silhouetted; Kestrel logo glinting on blueprints]
[Sound: Distant jackhammers, a radio chatter clipped and efficient]
[Music: A single dissonant viola note]

Narration:
Noah Kestrel's presence is quieter than Elias's flame; he arrives with a measured shrug and a hard, professional face. He stands at the edge of your maps for a long time before he speaks.

Noah Kestrel: "You're coordinating evacuation routes in ways I wouldn't have expected." (He hands you a small schematic.) "If you're committed to floating caches, you should redesign the tethering system — use a variable buoyancy array. Less maintenance in surge conditions."

You: (Relief blinds you for a second.) "That's—" (You stop, because relief tastes like betrayal to some and salvation to others.) "Thank you."

Noah Kestrel: (He shrugs, eyes dark.) "Technically, it's sound. But you're asking people to accept loss on a scale that will be political dynamite." (He pauses.) "I can give you technical suggestions, but I won't be the face of it."

Narration:
He steps back, offers neither heroism nor condemnation. His withdrawal is a soft thing, a hinge closing. There's a quiet calculus in it: help that keeps its distance. You find yourself both grateful and oddly bereft at the neutral space he leaves behind.

Noah Kestrel: "You asked for a plan that refuses corporate takeover. Make sure you keep the procurement channels honest — community-controlled escrow, transparent audits. Otherwise, someone will turn this into profit."

You: "I will." (Your voice carries a new hardness.)

Narration:
Word spreads before you can contain it. A neighbor leaves a flyer at Rosa's café calling you "brave" in one breath and "reckless" in another. Someone posts an anonymous forum accusing you of "engineering displacement" for political gain. At the same town meeting where three volunteers sign up, two people shout that you're the reason neighborhood values are collapsing.

Dr. Hana Park: (Later, in the lab, when the lab lights are dimmer.) "People will polarize. That's inevitable. The question is whether the polarization preserves lives or preserves nostalgia."

You: "I didn't come back to be loved." (You say it like an accusation and a confession in one.) "I came back to make sure Finn and everyone like him stay alive."

Narration:
The loneliness of command creeps in like the tide — slow, inexorable. Leading means carrying accusations on your shoulders until they become calluses. It means making choices that make you enemies of your friends. You are both the plan's architect and its most visible target.

At the edge of the Rooftop, you stand looking over the town sleeping under a quilt of sodium lamps and neon. Each light is a conviction, a history, a possible future. Your plan feels like a scalpel — precise and necessary and cold.

Elias's admiration, Noah's brief technical mercy, Rosa's warmth, Finn's steadiness, Hana's pragmatic tenderness — they orbit you like moons, each pulling in different directions. Some call you heroic in the daytime and ruinous by dusk. The press of opposing voices has a geography: friends on one side, furious neighbors on another. Your name becomes a battleground.

Narration:
You trace the map one more time with a finger. The path forward will mean buyouts, coffers of money you have to raise and guard; it will mean telling people the cost in the exact tone that doesn't make them feel coerced. It will mean organizing a relocation that honors memory and keeps bodies safe. It will mean choosing between exposure and secrecy, between mass civil disobedience and technical subterfuge, between negotiating and breaking the machines that may not have people's lives in mind.

Narration:
You are exhausted in a way that no single hour can fix. The lab's instruments blink like small, indifferent constellations. Outside, a buoy pings and the water answers. Inside, the plan waits like a tide—not yet turned, but gathering.
