[Scene: Saltbridge Community Hall | Evening]
[Visual: The hall's brick facade soaked in drizzle; string lights halo the entrance. A hand-lettered sign reads "Saltbridge Together — 7 PM".]
[Music: Low, tense piano underscoring; swells at moments of argument]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the steady drip of rain from eaves, the soft scuff of rubber boots on linoleum]

You push open the hall door and the warmth of the room hits you like a soft current — hot air, coffee steam, the leathery tang of old paper. Fluorescent bulbs buzz overhead, throwing honeyed light across stain-ringed folding chairs and maps thumbtacked to the walls. The smell of Rosa's coffee follows you in, bitter and familiar, anchoring you like a hand on your shoulder.

Your notebook is under your arm, its cover creased where your compass rests against your ribs. The red thread at your wrist feels taut; somewhere inside it you can still feel the ropey memory of rain tearing a porch light out of the night. You breathe and try, in that slow way you learned from counting tide lines, to steady the rhythm of your chest.

Rosa stands behind a chipped service table, apron dusted with flour, her smile bright but tired. Finn is at the back, fingers worrying the hem of his jacket as if knots in cloth could undo old losses. Dr. Hana Park is already unpacking a small field tablet, the screen a cool pool of data. People cluster in patchwork groups: elders with salt-stiff coats, young parents with soaked strollers, volunteer teams with high-visibility vests folded into their laps.

Maya (You): "Good evening. Thank you all for coming despite—" (you pause; the room hums like a held breath) "—the fog and the alerts. I want us to start by hearing where everyone stands. This is about our homes."

Rosa: "We need to hear what happens to people who can't move. If they put up a wall but raise the price of living—what then? Who pays for that? Who decides who gets to stay?" (Her voice is sharp with worry and coffee-sweetened fatigue.)

You: "Exactly. Equity is non-negotiable. Any plan that leaves our neighbors to sink won't be a plan at all." (You set the notebook on the table and open it to the page you started on the boardwalk — lists, tide logs, names that matter. Your thumb traces the ink like a compass needle.)

Dr. Hana Park: (tapping the tablet) "The latest model shows a narrowing window for intervention at certain hotspots along the inlet. We can stage modular defenses now that buy time. The physics are clear on sea-level projections, but the models don't decide policy." (She looks at you, eyes steady.) "We need choices that fit both the data and the social map."

Finn: (voice low, raw) "The last time it was a 'window,' we lost Mrs. Alvarez's porch light. We lost someone. I don't want to hear 'buy time' if it means betting on chance." (He looks at you, then away, and the room tightens.) "We keep saying 'community,' but sometimes words puff up like sand and wash away."

An old man in the third row murmurs, and someone starts a soft ripple of agreement. The meeting begins in warmth — shared histories, small jokes about Rosa's new pastry — then the edges of it fray as the stakes press in.

[Visual: Close-up of a large map of the coast mounted on the wall; areas shaded in different colors, pins marking homes and businesses.]
[Sound: The tablet's soft pings as Hana cycles through model layers]

Dr. Hana Park: "There are options. Living shorelines, small-scale breakwaters, raised walkways. They differ in cost, in time to implement, and in the protection they provide. The desktop studies we handed the council were clear about community-driven measures, but funding—" (her jaw tightens) "—funding is the bottleneck."

Mayor Lillian Cho's representative steps up to the microphone. He is careful with his words, the practiced cadence of someone carrying municipal restraint.

Mayor's Representative: "The mayor appreciates the community's engagement. However, there is a projected funding gap of thirty to forty percent for the town's coastal protection initiatives." (He glances at a tablet.) "There is a conditional offer from a single firm—Kestrel Marine Development—to provide a comprehensive seawall with an accelerated timeline, contingent on a contract that centralizes management."

A hush drops. The word Kestrel lands like a stone into water already roiling. You can feel the temperature in the room change, a slow chill that has nothing to do with the wet night outside.

Rosa: (bitter) "Centralized management? So the town gets a wall, and they get to decide who comes back? Who makes sure rents don't spike? Who keeps our seniors from being priced out?"

Mayor's Representative: "We are exploring all safeguards. The administration is committed to equity." (The phrase is polished, almost automatic. You file it mentally under 'familiar municipal language.' It can mean many things.)

The choice you make ripples through the room. People look to you as if you are a pivot; you feel the weight of that role like cold iron on your sternum. You remember the boardwalk, the phone in your hand, the way the compass had warmed against your skin.

The door opens with a small gust of fog and Elias slips in, sandy hair plastered by the drizzle, an indrawn laugh on his lips like he thinks it can bind things together. For a heartbeat your breath catches — not because of what he is, but because his arrival changes the shape of the air.

Elias: (grinning despite the damp) "Sorry I'm late. The fog held the ferry and a half-dozen neighbors. I was on my way here with a list of contacts and—" (he presses a damp hand to his scarf, eyes bright) "—people are already asking how to make their own defenses. We can't wait for 'conditional offers.' We make our own muscle."

You: (measured) "Elias, we need both muscle and maps. We need to keep the town's voice in the room, not just the volume on a megaphone."

Elias: "I know. I'm not saying we ignore policy. I'm saying the town won't be gaslit by a shiny corporate wall that exports our costs and leaves our neighbors behind. Mobilize the people; we've got experience with rapid response and mutual aid."

His speech gathers people like wind gathers flags. Some faces in the room light with eager fire. Others, the ones who have to balance budgets and lives, pin their lips in worry. Elias's charisma is a tide; it moves people. It also risks carrying them past careful shoals.

Noah Kestrel passes through like a slip of sea-glass: crisp navy coat, polished boots, tablet in one hand. He offers an interview to a camera someone has propped on a tripod in the corner. His words are tempered, efficiently shaped.

Noah Kestrel: "Efficiency reduces long-term costs. A comprehensive, engineered seawall prevents recurring damage and preserves infrastructure. It will require tough decisions, yes, but it minimizes uncertainty for businesses and services essential to daily life."

You: (quiet, thinking of your brother, your neighbor) "Who decides which blocks are 'essential'?"

Noah Kestrel: (calm) "That's the negotiation. I'm open to oversight, to co-management. But incremental measures without a unified system risk leaving gaps that are exploited by the sea."

Elias: (sharp) "Or exploited by profit. You're telling people to trust a system that has historically prioritized balance sheets."

Noah: (unruffled) "I'm telling people to weigh risk. We can make this humane and systematic, but we need the scale of engineering to stand against the physics."

The room tightens into two overlapping circles: one pulsing with grassroots urgency, the other a cold geometry of timelines and models. You stand between them like a small, necessary fulcrum, and your old habits — the ones that made you shoulder too much, that taught you to be the axis around which people balanced — press on your ribs until it hurts.

The committee idea tastes prudent; the mobilize-first option tastes like heat and immediate action and the risk of being steamrollered by legal consequences. Either choice would change the texture of the fight. For a second you imagine the neat ledger of pros and cons in your notebook rearranging itself like tide marks.

Dr. Hana Park closes her tablet and meets your eyes, patient but blunt.

Dr. Hana Park: "All of these routes have trade-offs. Community-led initiatives can be nimble and equitable but are limited in scale without outside capital. Corporate-led engineered responses scale fast but risk centralization and displacement. There is no purely 'clean' option in the data. There is only trade-off."

Finn: (voice breaking) "We need to choose people over profit. People we knew are gone because someone chose profit."

Rosa: (soft, fierce) "Then we choose the people we can still save. We make a plan that doesn't ask them to move in their shoeless state if we can help it."

The mayor's representative returns to the microphone, the lines on his face sharper now.

Mayor's Representative: "We will circulate the Kestrel proposal for public review tomorrow. The administration requests a statement from the council by the end of the week."

A week. It feels like a long tide. You imagine the sea having all the time in the world.

Elias catches your eye, his gaze intense and complex — a look you cannot read neatly. He steps forward.

Elias: "If the town is given a week to respond, I say we spend that week showing what community solutions can do. We organize work parties, we present evidence of capacity, we make clear that any contract must include enforceable equity clauses."

Noah Kestrel: (interjecting gently) "And while you demonstrate capacity, allow engineers to model the integrated solution. We can run scenarios that marry living shorelines with structural defenses."

The back-and-forth grows heated but not yet bitter. Voices overlap, questions pile on questions, and you find yourself listening to both the rational calculus and the aching cry of loss. Each argument drags up a memory; each memory makes the current choices heavier.

[Sound: The murmur of the crowd dimming as the meeting reaches its close]

When the meeting finally breaks, people disperse into the wet night like flocks breaking off after a storm. Rosa folds chairs and hands you a paper cup of coffee and a pastry so you will eat; Finn walks with you to the door and pauses, looking at the map on the wall as if seeing it for the first time after a blow. Dr. Hana nods to you with a gravity that could have been a benediction. The mayor's representative gathers his packet of documents and leaves with careful steps. Elias and Noah exchange a measured look before moving in opposite directions — both present in your life like currents that pull you different ways.

Outside, the boardwalk glistens. The town's neon signs reflect in puddles, fractured and duplicated. The sea beyond the rail is a flat, black thing swallowing light slowly.

You walk back to a quiet corner of the hall, where the last string light hangs. You sit, unzip your jacket, and pull out the notebook. Your pen trembles not from the cold but from the pressure of having to decide what shape the fight will take.

You write a single line.

decide a direction

The ink is slightly smeared where your thumb drags across it; it tastes of metal in your mouth when you put your finger to the page, the residual tang of storms and stubborn promises. The thread on your wrist rubs raw against the notebook's cover.

For a moment you let your head rest against the chair, the smallness of your chest a contrast to the vastness of the map you just tried to hold. The night presses at the windows with a wet insistence, and you can hear the muted beating of the town's life continuing outside — generators humming, someone locking up, the distant lapping of water.

Page-turn moment: The single line in your notebook is both a beginning and an indictment. You can feel the town's future unspooling in a dozen directions, and each one demands a different kind of courage and cost. Your muscles are tired from holding so much. A decision waits like a tideline: once you step into it, the water will carry you toward one shore and away from others.
