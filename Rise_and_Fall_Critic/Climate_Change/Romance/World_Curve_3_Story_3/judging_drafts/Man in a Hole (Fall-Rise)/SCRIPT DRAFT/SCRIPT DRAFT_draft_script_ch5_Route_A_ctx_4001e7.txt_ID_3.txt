[Scene: Boardwalk Holding Cell | Night]

Narration:
The bracelet they slide over your wrist is inked — a number that flattens you. Cold metal meets raw skin. It is humbling in the way a disease can be: small, efficient, unromantic. You: (quiet, to yourself) "I wrote plans."

Narration:
You take out the notebook with hands that won't steady. You flip to a page with margins full of times, names, and checklists that now look fragile — like dried seaweed. You do not write so much as press the pen into the paper and let the ink pull out what you were before the kettle closed. The list grows, but the numbers on the bracelet keep pulling the breath from your throat.

Elias is in the next room, separated by a thin glass and a steel door. He meets your gaze through the slit and his face is all angles and tiredness, but when his eyes rest on you they are a kind of damage dressed as intensity. He wants an argument to be won and also, somewhere behind that want, to make sure you are still there.

Elias Novak: (loud enough to be heard through the glass) "Hey. You okay?"

You: "No. Not really." (you taste the salt again, not from the sea now but from your own fatigue) "You?"

Elias: (close to breaking) "I am furious. And I'm sorry." (he swallows) "I thought — I thought if we could force them to see us, they'd have to answer."

You: "We forced enough to be seen. We didn't force the supports."

Elias: (his voice cracks) "I know. I know."

Narration:
This exchange is not a reconciliation. It is a pair of wounded things trading facts like bandages. His hands curl over the edge of the bench. For a moment he gestures as if he could climb the small wall and press his palm against your glass. Instead he mumbles something you barely hear — an apology, perhaps, or a plan you don't trust.

You hold your compass between thumb and forefinger until the metal grows warm. The red thread at your wrist rubs raw beneath the bracelet. You think, with a cold clarity, of the neighbor whose porch light went out in a storm and the way you promised then to be better. You promised with the full arrogance of the unscathed.
