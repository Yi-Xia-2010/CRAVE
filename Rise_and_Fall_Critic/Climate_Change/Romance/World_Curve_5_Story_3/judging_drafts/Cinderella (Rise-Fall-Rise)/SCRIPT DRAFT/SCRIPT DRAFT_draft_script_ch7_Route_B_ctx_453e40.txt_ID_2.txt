Jun tweaks a sensor on a tripod and grins like this is the best joke they've ever heard.

Aria Navarro: "Keep the roots splayed—don't bury the crown. Think like a fisherman, not a bricklayer."

You say it and the line lands. Someone repeats it with a smile; the cadence becomes part of the day. You move from person to person—knee-deep in mud, jacket hem slick with sea sheen—letting your hands show what argument you made in the council chamber. The plan is no longer slides and graphs; it is a thousand small gestures of patience.

Sora: "You're right to be here, Aria. Words on paper only do so much if we're not willing to put our hands where the sea will test them."

Sora's voice is warm and raw. They move with a brisk, musical energy—planting a sapling and turning to wave at an elderly volunteer. Their optimism has always felt like sunlight after a storm. Today it smells like wet steam and paint.

You feel the town watching you, measuring your conviction not by your talk at the council but by the mud on your knees. It is a weight and a comfort.

[Scene: Mangrove Reclamation Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Clouds have layered; a glassy band of low light shimmers on the water. The saplings stand like careful promises.]
[Sound: A distant rumble—more felt than heard—like a chain dragged under water. Volunteers share nervous glances.]
[Music: Minor key cello, slow heartbeat tempo.]

The calm frays. Jun's face tightens as they route a cable. You see it before you feel it: the waterline doesn't just rise, it reaches—hungry and sudden—across a low swath of the planting bed. Your stomach flips with a recognition you've catalogued a thousand times in your notes but never wanted to meet in the body.

Jun: "Sensors—there's a spike. Sudden surge from the channel. Not predicted in the forecast models."

You move toward the worst of the rows and your hands go numb in a different way. Where you expected saplings to be anchored, there are torn roots and leaning stems, clumps of mud gone. The tidy rows look like they've been scraped by an invisible hand.

You taste metallic panic. Volunteers stare at their hands, at the mud between their fingers, as if the ground has betrayed them.

Sora rushes past you, boots knocking against planks, and drops to their knees among a ruined line. They gather a sapling like a talisman and hold it up to you—mud dripping from the roots.

Sora: "This—this is not how it ends. Not like this."

Sora's voice is frayed; for a beat their fierce brightness looks small, like a candle bent by wind. Then they stand, face a mask of stubborn refusal. Their energy becomes a kind of blunt armor against despair.

You want to say the right thing. The right thing is to explain tidal anomalies, to show the sensors, to point at what could have been done differently. But the people around you need something else: they need a leader who is not only clever, but steady.

Her words are simple and devastating. You feel the crowd's edges loosen—people walking away not in anger but in the quiet way grief thins attendance. A grant administrator's name pops into your head, and with it the memory of a polite, patient email: 'We need demonstrable early wins.' Demonstrable. Early. [Visual: Lanterns bob over patchy water. The quay is a knot of wet wood and umbrellas. Mayor Isla Cortez stands beneath an awning, her blazer damp at the hem, a municipal tablet clutched in one hand; she looks at the ruined beds with eyes that do not give away an immediate verdict.]
[Sound: Tapping of the mayor's tablet; murmured conversations; a faint wind that carries the scent of upturned earth and diesel exhaust.]
[Music: Tense low strings; a single sustained note that implies pressure.]

Mayor Isla Cortez: "Tell me what happened—plainly. The council needs an explanation before the funding committee meets tomorrow."

Her voice is even. There is a public cadence to it that both hides and reveals: a leader taught to hold the room. When she looks at you her expression is unreadable—complex. It could be anger, it could be concern, it could be the fatigue of a long political life. You cannot unpack it into neat parts.

Aria Navarro: "We planted during a slack tide window. We used community teams and—" (You stop, the words tasting inadequate.) "—we used locally sourced seedlings. There was an unexpected surge from the inlet. It scoured swathes; it uprooted nursery beds."

Mayor Isla Cortez: "Unexpected to who? To the donors? To the insurers?"

Her questions are not accusatory in tone, but they carry consequence. Each administrative word translates into budgets, into paused checks, into pages in a ledger that decide whether the town will keep this plan or pivot to something with more immediate shelter.

Sora: "We cannot fold when the sea tests us. That's always been the point—to learn with the place, not to flee at the first bruise."

Sora steps forward. Their posture is a kind of challenge wrapped in plea. The mayor's jaw tightens.

Mayor Isla Cortez: "Learning takes time—and money. My hands are tied until I can justify this to the council and to the insurers. If we cannot show mitigation in the short term, the grants may be reallocated."

Jun: (quiet, urgent) "We have partial logs that show the timing and magnitude of the surge, but there are gaps—some nodes went offline under load. If we can pull a clean data set, we can argue for the surge being anomalous and request a pause rather than a withdrawal."

You feel the room closing in with the two realities stacked: the ecological patience this work demands, and the municipal hunger for certainty. The volunteers who remain gather near the rail, faces pinched and wet. You see Elena stand behind you, arms folded, the expression of someone who has watched coastlines change by decades rather than budgets.

Elena: "People will ask why the town trusted you. They'll ask what you promised. You cannot hide from that, niña."

She is right. You also know that the town is not homogeneous—some will stand with you because they see the long arc, some will look at their flooded kitchen and want a wall yesterday.

You move through the room, trading between technical plea and the rawness of loss. Conversations loop; words are said and amended; hope and responsibility bump against one another until friction sparks.

Mayor Isla Cortez: "I want a remediation plan on my desk by dawn. Show me the data, and show me the staffing plan for replanting—and I will take it to the council. But if I must be blunt: show me that continuing this way won't cost the town its immediate shelters."

Her terms are clear even in their softness. The choice you make now will mark more than the staff reports; it will mark who you are when the town asks you to carry them.

Sora takes your hand in theirs for a breath—just a brush, a gesture—then lets go. The motion is small and speaks complicated things your past decisions might have already shaped. Whatever it means, you feel its weight against your palm.

You stand at the intersection of faith and mandate. The hurt of the ruined beds is a physical thing; the threat to funding is a different kind of wound—administrative and moral. Volunteers look to you to decide whether to ask for more time, to accept temporary engineered protections, or to escalate politically.
