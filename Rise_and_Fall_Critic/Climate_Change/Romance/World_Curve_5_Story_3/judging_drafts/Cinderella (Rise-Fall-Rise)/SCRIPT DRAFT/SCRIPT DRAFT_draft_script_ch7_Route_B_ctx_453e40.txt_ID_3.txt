You remember the first morning—how the work felt like a chorus instead of a chore. Today begins the same way, but the chorus has an edge. Your hands know the rhythm: dig, plant, tamp, tie a marker. The tide is a slow metronome, retreating in silver and coming back in thicker when it pleases.

Sora is there at your side, sleeves rolled and wind tugging a paint-streaked anorak. They move with the kind of fierce joy that makes strangers hand over seeds and neighbors unpack extra gloves. When they laugh, the sound is full and unguarded—contagious enough that even the more tired volunteers let their shoulders loosen.

Sora Lin: "Look at that row, Aria. It already looks like something. Like the coast might remember us."

You: "We keep it like this, they'll remember us longer." (You press a sapling's roots into the mud, feel the give—wet, cold, living.)

Jun comes by with a crate of biodegradable markers, breathing out a cloud of warm exhaustion and grease. Their grin is quick, brittle on the edges.

Jun: "Sensors are feeding fine. Soil moisture's higher than I wanted—maybe the tidal flats are holding a little more water, which is good and bad. Also, the donors loved the livestream. Lots of hearts."

You: "Hearts don't plant roots." (You say it sharply and then soften.) "But they help. We need both."

The morning stretches toward noon. You walk the lines, cataloguing the saplings like a mother counting children: this one needs more peat, this one is shaded too much, that one has been trampled by a curious dog. Elena appears at the perimeter with a thermos and wrapped food—her presence is heat against the cool damp.

Elena Navarro: "You did good, niña." (She presses the thermos into your hands without waiting for permission.) "Don't let the council scare you with numbers. Roots remember."

You: "We need the council to see the roots before they see budgets." (You try for gentle, but the words tremble.)

Sora is already talking to a small cluster of teenagers, painting a mural on an upturned crate—bright, defiant strokes of green and cerulean that make the mud beside them look less like loss and more like canvas.

Sora Lin: "We can do nightly vigils. People can come plant and paint and tell stories. It makes the work sacred instead of scary."

You watch them. You want to believe in the sacred. You want to believe in time.

[Visual: Clouds gather at the edge of the horizon; the air smells thicker, like before rain but with a hint of iron.]

By late afternoon, the tide schedule slips for reasons Jun can't quite explain over the radio: a pulse of current came earlier than the charts predicted. You are at the far end of the newly planted bed when the first surge arrives—nothing cinematic, just a sly, insistent lift of water that pulls at the saplings' mouths of soil.

Sound warps: the gulls' cries sharpen; boots slap faster; someone shouts but the cadence is wrong. You run. The mud gives in a darker way, and where saplings were, there are sudden, small gaps—knuckled root balls rising like wounded things and then slumping back into a sea that won't keep them.

Sora is already knee-deep, hands working, tearing at the current with something close to fury. They look up at you once—face streaked with mud and salt and something raw—and their expression is unreadable in that heavy, intimate way you've learned to translate only by feeling.

Sora Lin: "Hold the line! Get the rope! We anchor from that driftwood—now!"

You: "Jun, markers, now—no, the bigger anchors!" (You bark orders, your scientific voice and community voice braided. It works in fits and starts. You pull. You feel the saplings slip like a slow hemorrhage.)

[Sound: A high wind begins to thread through the young shoots; rain starts as a cold, stinging sheet. Tools clatter. A volunteer collapses onto their haunches and begins to cry.]

A volunteer's cry bends the work into a different shape. Faces fold inward; the livestream chat fills with frantic hearts and a few cruel, panicked messages. Elena stands at the perimeter, arms crossed not in judgment but in a blockade against panic.

Elena Navarro: "Breathe, niña. You've done the right thing. They can't take the whole of it if we don't stop them here."

You: "We can hold—" (You begin, but the sentence is eaten by the sea as another small wave finds a planted row and drags it like a thread.)

By dusk, the rain has stopped and the damage is counted. More saplings are uprooted than were planted that morning. Some have been saved, but the rows look ragged—patchy proof where there should have been a stitch. Phones buzz with terse messages: a small municipal grant will be delayed while the mayor "assesses risk"; a private donor wants to reallocate funds to "hard infrastructure" unless proofs of long-term success appear quickly.

Jun slumps on a rock, habitually tinkering with a sensor that now reads off-range.

Jun: "We can replant, but it takes weeks for nursery stock. The tide windows are tighter. And—" (They bite the end off.) "And the livestream numbers spiked for a while, but people want wins, Aria."

You: "Wins are not always immediate." (You say this as if teaching and pleading at once. The volunteer beside you looks at you with a small, broken hope.)

The tide is not the only pressure. A municipal official from the mayor's office arrives, a trench of official gray against the muddy green. Mayor Isla Cortez does not walk the site like a visitor; she moves as someone carrying the weight of a town between decisions. Her face is careful, political, and something else—tired.

Mayor Isla Cortez: "I won't pretend I don't understand the symbolism here, Aria. Mangroves are powerful. But I also can't promise the community an open ledger while private funds retreat. People have roofs to protect."

You: "We asked for time. Ecology—" (You feel the word like a delicate thing.) "—is not immediate. It builds. The benefits compound. We can show modeled outcomes for five years and twenty years, but I know that's not the same as a levee tonight."

Mayor Cortez: "No, it's not. And the council will not approve open-ended trials if they think they will lose homes. Someone will use that against us. I need a plan that balances both—something measurable by next quarter, or I risk a vote that swings away from this entirely." (She looks at you with an almost parental impatience.) "You chose this plan, Aria. Be ready for the consequence."

Her words land with the force of weather. It would be easy to feel accused by the town that raised you—guilty for asking for a nontraditional timeline. You want to answer with conviction; you want to stand in the slow science of roots. But the faces in the crowd are not abstractions: a child with damp hair, a fisherman who just fixed his net, Elena's hands floured with preparation for the next day's market.

Sora steps forward, the paint on their hands smudged into the cuff of their sleeve.

Sora Lin: "If we compromise now with the language of fear, we'll lose what makes this ours. If we let engineers take the stage, what do we have left? A wall and a name on a plaque? We planted with people's stories, not plastic molds."

Mayor Cortez: (softening, then firm) "I don't want to take anything from the community. I want to protect it. Help me make that possible without promising miracles I can't deliver. Funders want certainty. They want guarantees. I need to know you can give them something to count."

The conversation frays into a tangle of idealism and accountability. Sora's voice tightens, then softens into something persuasive and almost pleading. You feel the tug in the middle like a bruise.

Jun's hands shake as they take a call—someone on the other end, a funder, asking whether the town will "pivot" to a safer, more marketable storefront: levees, reinforced quays, immediate visible action. You can hear the rising current of opinion because money is a loud tide.

Elena places a hand over your palm, grounding you in a steadier reality.

Elena Navarro: "You promised them a voice, not a monument. But promises are heavy, niña. Remember the fishermen who lost nets last year—they don't have years to wait for roots to grow."

You think of the nights you have spent drafting language that could bridge the gap—technical diagrams and community testimony braided like rope. You think of Sora painting murals and leading children's stories about mangrove mothers. You feel the literal and figurative mud between your toes—impossible to scrub clean.

You are exhausted in a way that goes beyond sleep. You are tired the way a coastline is tired: patient, then suddenly raw.

The howling need for resolution presses at your chest. There are paths through this breach, each with a cost. You imagine the outcomes as weather systems: one that asks for more time and hopes the town holds, one that fastens temporary armor and risks losing the soul of the effort, one that stages a public reclamation to force commitments with all the danger that entails.

You realize that your next words will redirect momentum. The volunteers look toward you, not because you demanded it but because you stood up first.

You inhale, tasting salt, rain, and the faint copper of anxiety. You think of the mangrove saplings, stubby and surviving in between your fingers, and you think of the people who came to plant them—how they will read your choice as a promise kept or broken.

There are three paths laid before you, each a verdict of character and consequence.
