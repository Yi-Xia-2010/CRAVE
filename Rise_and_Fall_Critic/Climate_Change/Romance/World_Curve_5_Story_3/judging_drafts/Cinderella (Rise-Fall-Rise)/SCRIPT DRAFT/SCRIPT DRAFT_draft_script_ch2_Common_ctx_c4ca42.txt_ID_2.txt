You read it again, letting the words settle like a small lighthouse in your chest. They guide everything you will say tonight.

Jun arrives with a borrowed toolkit and a grin too wide for someone who hasn't slept. They drop a spool of sensor cable and a thermos of bitter coffee between the planters.

Jun: "Morning. You look like someone who could use more than citrus and good intentions."

Jun: "Packed. Also two dozen stickers. Outreach is stickers, you know that."

Jun: "People respond to stories. Start with a single house, one family—then zoom out. Tie the tech to that house. Let them see what happens if we don't act."

You: "Right. A narrative arc. Show the human before the model."

Jun: "And maybe a sticker that says 'We Built This Together.'"

You: "Yes. We built this together."

[Scene: Tidal Research Lab | Late Morning]

Narration:
The lab smells of ozone and algae. Charts hang like banners; a whiteboard is half-covered in equations and another half in quickly sketched community meeting talking points. Dr. Kavir Singh moves through the space with the calm of someone who has built habits to hold anxiety at bay.

Dr. Kavir Singh: "The latest model shows the seasonal surge window tightening. If we miss this funding cycle, deployment timelines slip by months."

You: "We can prioritize low-cost sensor nodes in the highest-risk corridors and push for the hybrid pilot as a proof of concept."

Dr. Kavir Singh: (nodding) "Exactly. But Mayor Cortez will want fiscal numbers and visible impact in the short term. Your presentation must translate our uncertainty into actionable confidence."

You: "We have Jun's community mapping and Elena's oral histories. We have salinity trends and sedimentation rates. If I can show how a mangrove corridor reduces wave energy by X percent and pair that with modular seawall placement where livelihood assets are concentrated, it's harder to say no."

Dr. Kavir Singh: "And your framing? How you present trade-offs will matter. People are tired of being told to sacrifice without being offered agency."

You: "That's the point we'll hover on—agency. Not a choice imposed, but options built with the community."

You stop. You notice how your sentence stumbles when you try to speak of Mateo and Sora in the same breath. The thought twists into something both tender and precarious. You decide, for now, not to categorize the warmth that blooms in your chest. Labels can come later; tonight is about the town.
