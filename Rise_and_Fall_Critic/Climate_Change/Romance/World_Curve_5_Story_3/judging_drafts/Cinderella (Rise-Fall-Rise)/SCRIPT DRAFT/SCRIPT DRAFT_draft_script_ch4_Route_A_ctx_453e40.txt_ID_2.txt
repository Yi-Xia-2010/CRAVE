You can feel the decision you made thudding in your chest like a tool on a pile-driver. The stacked modules crowd the horizon: concrete ribs, engineered to slot and lock, a promise in gray. Sweat beads at your hairline despite the chill the rising wind brings; the barometer on your diver's watch ticks a little faster than it did this morning. Jun's radio chatters, Mateo's boots clank on wet scaffolding, and somewhere behind the site a generator coughs its lonely rhythm.

Aria Navarro: "Status report. We have the third tier set—Jun, are the seals holding?"

Jun Park: "They're in compression, but the sensor on the seam is reading micro-permeations. Nothing catastrophic—yet. I'm recalibrating now."

Mateo Hale: "We test real loads anyway. We don't guess with tide pressure." (He leans over the plans, fingertips smudged with concrete dust.) "If this holds, homes on the quay get breathing room this season."

You read the blueprints again with a scientist's hunger—lines, tolerances, fail-safes—but your shoulders hold the weight of more than data. Elena's face flickers in your head, the markets under strings of lanterns, the corner where children used to chase crabs. You promised them a margin of safety. You promised them action.

You nod because the wall is your current truth, but something in your chest tightens when Sora's face drifts into memory—paint-smeared, eyes bright with ceremonial mangrove seedlings. You had seen Sora at the meeting, voice a rising tide for roots, for soil, for slower, living defenses. The plan you chose in council made Sora's jaw clench. Their lips were a margin you didn't cross.

Jun Park: "Wind's picking up more than forecast. Local buoys show a pressure trough swinging in faster."

You choose to walk the line. The concrete is a slick, living thing under your palm; salt stings your lips. You move from block to block, eyes flicking for hairline fractures, for seams that might betray you. At the edge where the structure meets water you catch a whisper of something—an undercurrent, a different rhythm than the usual tide. The sky deepens, the light folding into purple.

[Sound: A roar arrives—at first distant, then as if the ocean itself has leaned forward]
[Visual: The sea lifts; a black curve swells beyond the horizon]

Mateo's voice snaps: "Back! All hands—secure loose kit!" He is already on the crane, eyes tracking the swell. The generator coughs and then keels into silence under the wind's demand. Sheets of rain begin to fall like thrown pebbles, pricking skin cold.

You relay orders with the practiced calm that keeps others moving. Mateo moves like a man who has rehearsed danger—a machine-and-hand paired with stubborn care. Jun's hands are steady as he straps the sensor cases. You bark directions you never thought you'd have to give at your age, and the crew answers because they've chosen to trust you.

Then the sea reaches its arm further than anyone expected.

[Sound: A terrible, hydraulic tearing; the slam of water against concrete]
[Visual: A modular block, near the south end, lists and then—a moment like glass breaking—the seam opens. Water gouges through a joint with fist-like force]

A wall you helped endorse gives way.

You don't register the gasp as external at first; it's a physical convulsion from you and everyone nearby. The wave that follows is not graceful; it is a knuckled fist that crowns and lands on the quay like a verdict. It finds low doorways, the market stalls, the alley where old wiring snakes along boards. The sound of it entering the town is a chorus of clattering wood, the thud of floating crates, a child's cry cut thin and sharp.

Mateo yells something—your name and another sharp thing that could be an order or a curse. He dives toward the south end with Jun at his heels; their silhouettes are quick and stubborn against the falling night. You run after them, water soaking the cuffs of your jacket, the smell of diesel and fish and mud punching your nose.

[Scene Transition: Old Quay | Dusk — Flooded Bazaar]
[Visual: Lanterns bob above ankle-deep and then knee-deep water; tarps flap like tired flags]
[Music: Sparse piano stabs under the wind]

You reach the Old Quay to find chaos lived-in: people hauling crates that float like small islands, a vendor with a plastic sheet draped over her head, children clinging to pilings. Elena stands waist-deep, arms steady as she passes wrapped food and blankets, her voice a flat, sharp instrument of practicality.

Elena Navarro: "Move that table! Take that tarp! Aria—check the north alley, there's a gas smell."

You move because Elena doesn't leave space for hesitation. Sora is there too, up to their knees in mud and artful rage, hauling bundles of saplings and what looks like rope—maybe for reinforcing the bank. Their face is streaked with rain and salt; they meets your gaze with something you can't read.

Sora Lin: (complex) "They said concrete would buy us time—didn't say whose time." (They thrust a bundle at a volunteer.) "Get those kids to the roof greenhouse. Now."

Their voice is a blade wrapped in velvet: fierce, urgent, laced with accusation. You feel it land on you the way weather lands on exposed skin—raw, unblunted.

Aria Navarro: "Sora—there are injured—"

Sora Lin: "I know. You're not the only one who can triage. I'll handle volunteers; you handle logistics."

You want to argue, to name the breach as design failure or unforeseeable stress, but the sirens of needs are louder than rhetoric. A child wails from a half-submerged kiosk. A man curses, hauling a soaking generator. Mateo is a knot of motion across the water, barking into his radio, the copper compass at his throat flashing in the dim.

Mateo Hale: "Aria! We need manual pumps on the south passages. If the panels short, we lose our power chain. Jun—redirect the portable battery packs."

Jun Park: "On it. I'm sealing the nodes, but the telemetry's gone noisy—sand and silt's fouling the lines."

You split your attention: bearings of engineering, the immediate human need, the politics that will bloom once the water recedes. You feel the old, itchy guilt: you chose the wall. That choice is flooding the bazaar now.

Aria Navarro: "Then we keep it safe." (Your voice is thin with the cold, but it shakes the truth free: you mean it.)

Jun curses and then laughs once, a short, ridiculous sound. A volunteer pulls a soaked child into a dry corner; Elena watches with a priestess's focus, doling out warmth like absolution. But behind the collected effort, you feel the city breathing around a new scar.

Sora approaches, hands dripping with mud. For all their theatrics you'd hoped they'd be furious with you—accusatory, explosive. Instead, their face is complex: anger braided with exhaustion, a line of fear at the mouth.

Sora Lin: (complex) "You built a wall. Walls are honest in their limits. They hold or they don't. Right now—" (They look at the flooded stalls.) "—we hold what we can."

Their words are not concession nor conviction; they are the watch of someone who refuses to let the moment simplify into a slogan. It feels like a test: for the wall, for you, for ideas you represent.

You talk in brief, necessary sentences: where to set pumps, who to move to higher ground, which families need evacuation. You think about the mayor's face from that meeting—concise, hungry for a headline that calmed investors. You think of the scientists who told you probabilities, and of the fishermen who told you the sea had begun to change its grammar. All of it folds into the present: action, and consequence.
