### DRAFT
# Route: Route B


[Scene: Mangrove Reclamation Site | Dawn — Two Weeks After the Vote]
[Visual: Rows of saplings in soft green light; the muddy shore dotted with bright shirts and spades. String lights still damp with salt hang between posts.]
[Sound: Low conversation, the soft rasp of boots in mud, a gull calling, distant engines idling on the quay.]
[Music: Sparse, low piano with filtered synth—melancholic but steady]

Narration:
You remember the first morning like an afterimage—hands raw from peat, laughter slipping over the water, Sora painting protest banners with sapling leaves as stencils. There was a ceremonial clapping when the hundredth mangrove went in, as if the earth itself had been invited back to the table. People who'd never dug a hole in their lives came with gloves on and eyes that shone. The tide felt like an ally then; even the gulls seemed to watch, curious.

You smell wet soil and citrus from Elena's packed sandwiches. The taste of salt and coffee is constant at the edges of everything. Your notebook, water-streaked at the corners, sits heavy with measurements and small sketches of root systems. You run a thumb over your diver's watch—its barometer reads calm—and allow yourself the small, private permission to feel hopeful.

Sora stands knee-deep in mud a few meters down, hands smeared with green and brown. They hum while pushing each sapling in, humming a cadence you think they made up on the spot. Their expression is complex—hope braided with the tightness of someone who knows loss by habit but refuses to be shaped by it. You don't press them for what that complexity means; it's enough that they are here.

Elena catches your eye and gives the half-wink she always does—one that says keep going, niña—without the weight of explanation.

Jun tinkers at the edge of the line, checking a string of low-cost moisture sensors clamped to bamboo stakes. He looks up as you approach and grins, small, guilty: "They actually stayed upright overnight. Technology and ceremony, huh?"

You laugh—an honest sound—and the day stretches open.

Aria Navarro: "Let's make sure the root collars are below tide reach this time. We did this last bed shallower than the paper says—less compaction. If we do a staggered planting depth along the contour, they'll anchor better."

Jun: "I'll rebias the water sensors to record the surge peaks. If we get a clear tide log, Kavir can show the council. Numbers make people less nervous."

Sora: (wiping hands) "Numbers are good, but songs are louder. We'll paint the stakes with names. People remember names longer than graphs."

You say nothing for a beat, because it's true in a way that still hurts: names, hands, songs—this is how memory becomes obligation.

Narration:
The day folds into a steady rhythm. You coordinate, joke, record, and plant. Mayor Cortez stops by with a thermos and a curt, thin smile—the kind she uses when she wants to be close but must remain a certain distance. She lays a hand briefly on Elena's shoulder and says quietly to you, Aria: "Good work." Her approval is like a coin dropped into a jar; useful and finite.

At dusk, the site feels like a promise kept. Rows of young mangroves bend into the dying light like an audience bowing after a show.

[Scene Transition: Old Quay | Night]
[Visual: Lanterns bob on the water, reflecting neon salvage; the quay creaks. Clouds have thickened into bruised swathes across the sky.]
[Sound: Wind picking up, the low rumble of a distant motor, conversation quieter now.]
[Music: Minor string swell—the mood deepening toward something unsettled]

Narration:
Three nights later, you are at the quay rechecking sensor data when the tide turns on you. The weather apps say a routine king tide; the lab's models say two meters above mean. You have seen king tides before. You have not, until this night, seen one with the ocean's teeth bared like this.

The first sign is sound: a sudden, wrong wind that pushes along the harbor like a hand. The water edge that had been a thoughtful distance from the young roots slips forward in a bright, angry line. A frothed surge—higher than forecasted—rips into the planting beds. You can feel it in your teeth.

[Sound: A sharp crack of something giving, a collective gasp, the slap of water on wooden piles]

Jun: "Aria—look!"

Narration:
You run. Mud sucks at your boots. Your hands go for the stakes, for the saplings, for anything that might keep a root in the ground. You find Sora at the front line, hands thrown forward like a ritual net. They shout something—part instruction, part mantra—but the water is faster. The first row of saplings tips, clutches of them uprooted and carried off like bread crusts.

You see bodies—neighbors and volunteers—racing along the line, torches waving, calling to each other. You see Elena's hand flatten against her face, fingers gone white. The smell, immediate and cutting, is not just salt: it's the sharp metallic tang that always follows something broken.

Sora: (yelling over the surge) "Hold the line! Tie 'em down! Names—call the names!"

You act on muscle memory. You tie, wedge, push. One sapling you had planted yourself snaps free and, for a reckless second, you imagine following it into the water—following a small, grieving thing all the way out to sea.

Narration:
When the water slackens, what remains is a ragged stubble where a hopeful green had been. The stakes carved with names lie scattered like lost teeth. Jun kneels in the mud and starts cataloging—the only defense you have against the unknowable is measurement. He reads off numbers in a voice gone small: 63 planted, 19 salvaged, 44 gone. The way he says them is a kind of small arithmetic of grief.

Elena kneels too, picking up a stake painted by a little girl's hand. She runs a thumb over the painted swirl and then nods at you, something like an oath and a question wrapped in her movement.

Elena Navarro: (soft) "This is bad. We'll be all right—if we do this right. But we need plans that make sense for times like this."

Sora: (breathing heavily) "We were careless in spots. I—" (they stop, a hard set to their jaw) "We didn't expect that surge. Maybe—" (they bite the rest off) "Maybe we didn't plant deep enough in the exposed beds."

Narration:
The lab messages arrive like small, icy notes. A regional funder calls: "We saw the images. We need a mitigation plan before more funds are released." The municipal liaison leaves a terse email: "Council is worried. We need something tangible to show by the end of the week." Mayor Cortez, when you finally catch her on the dock, presses her palms together and says, "We need momentum, Aria. People need to feel safe."

Your chest tightens; there's history in that tightening. You remember the council room—the expectant faces, the way you promised patience and care. You promised the town more than an immediate fix. But promises do not stop the water.

Jun: (looking at you) "We can replant. We can change techniques—anchor lines, deeper collars, sacrificial bundles. But it's more work. Volunteers are getting tired."

Sora: (quiet, fierce) "We can hold them. We can teach the techniques. We don't need their levees. We need time, not steel."

Mayor Isla Cortez: (hands on her tablet) "I can argue for a pause in certain disbursals until you show a revised plan. But my hands are tied—there are people losing houses out there. I'm hearing that the council will favor temporary measures unless we deliver quick wins."

Narration:
The community's energy cracks thinly. That evening the Old Quay smells of diesel and fear. Someone posts a photo of uprooted green and it goes around the network in minutes; comments pile up like high water lines—some sympathetic, others impatient. The broadband buzz in your ear becomes the town's rising rumor.

You sit on a salt-stained crate and let the tide of faces pass: volunteers who showed up with mud on their knees, youth who stayed silent and watched, old fishers who fold their hands as if praying and then, pragmatically, begin to tally loss. The choices ahead feel like cliffs: one misstep and the view changes irrevocably.

[Sound: Distant, muffled voices; the hush of the harbor at night falling like a lid]

Aria Navarro [Thinking]:
So much of leadership, you have learned, is choosing which risks to take and which to refuse. You can ask for patience and ask the town to gamble on slow growth. You can bend and accept temporary engineering that will protect people in the short term while the roots learn to hold. Or you can turn the moment into a show of collective will—fast, visible, and dangerous—to force political commitment.

Each path asks different things of you. Each asks something of Sora, of Elena, of Jun, of Mayor Cortez. Of the town.

You feel Sora beside you; they are quiet, hands cupped around hot tea. Their eyes are bright in a way that looks like resolve and grief braided together. They catch your gaze and, for a second, the complexity there reads like invitation—or a dare.

Jun taps his notepad, voice low: "If the funders pull, we can't pay the stipends for community coordinators. People will be stretched thin."

Elena: "We don't want to be beholden to something that strips us of the right to choose what comes ashore. But neither do we want to watch people lose roofs while roots find their feet."

Mayor Isla Cortez: "I can call an emergency session. I can agitate the council. But I can't promise money without a path that shows when safety is restored."

Narration:
The sky is a bruise above you, and the harbor holds its breath. You can taste the salt and the metallic tang of decisions. Your notebook is open in your lap and the pen in your hand is suddenly heavy.

Narration:
You hear the town's heartbeat at the edges of your skin—sometimes a slow tremor of endurance, sometimes a quick, frightened flutter. The decision is not only technical. It is moral, political, and intensely personal. Whatever you choose will recalibrate trust, funding, and the way neighbors look at one another across the water.

You think of the kids who painted stakes with their mothers, of Elena's hands mapping the shoreline in her head like a cartography of lived losses. You think of Sora's complex face and Jun's steady practicality, and of Mayor Cortez balancing in a place that demands both courage and worry.

The night's air cools. Somewhere, a radio static crackles and settles into the heavier notes of a storm warning—less a forecast than a punctuation. You hand closes around your pen like an anchor.

The choice looms; the town waits with a thousand small breaths.
