The town unwinds in ways you didn't expect. The apprentices you trained are still working — but now many have contracts with a glossy firm, paid better for the hours they give but tied to metrics that never mention the taste of smoked fish or the lullaby your grandmother hummed while mending nets. You read Rafi's follow-up — a long piece that begins with celebration and ends with an accounting of loss. He interviewed Etta before she left and saved the question about the dock for the end. Rafi Soto: "You know I didn't want to hurt anyone. But silence felt like complicity." (He looks at you with tired eyes.) "I thought exposing the mechanics might stop it, or at least warn other towns."

Rafi Soto: "It made people see the fine print. It also made investors pivot their PR. I'm sorry." (He folds his hands in on himself like a mollusk retreating.)

You think of the little victories that once built toward something larger: a seed planted, a dock mended, a child shown how to feel the tide. You think of how those victories were translated into barcodes and billable hours and a model that promised uplift only when the uplift could be monetized.

Silent, you let the harbor be its own indictment — not of weather this time, but of hubris. You had wanted the town to survive; you had wanted people to keep living the ways that made Hearthbend Hearthbend. Your plan moved fast enough to be noticed. Faster still, it was repackaged.

You close your notebook and let your breath fog in the salt air. There are names that left the town, names you tried to hold, names that were sold in glossy brochures. The ledger is full of entries now, some in ink that you can still trace and some in a typeface you don't recognize.
