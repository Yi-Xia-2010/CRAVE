[Scene: Skyhub Pavilion | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The Skyhub's glass skin throws back the harbor like a mirror. Cool LEDs trace the frame of the pavilion; a panoramic view of Hearthbend knits the town into the skyline.]
[Music: Quiet, hopeful strings with an undercurrent of steady percussion]
[Sound: Rain tapping the raincoat; distant gulls; a soft hum of ventilation]

You step into the pavilion with your field jacket under a borrowed raincoat, the familiar weight of your notebook against your ribs. The salt that lives in your hair and the patch-smell of oilskin seem a world away from the pavilion’s clinical light, but the harbor is still there below the glass — muffled, patient. Your palms are warm where they press the carved coral pendant; the leather of your boots remembers mud.

Seren is already in the center of the room, her platinum bob catching the overhead light in shards. Her posture is the same clean line you remember from a hundred presentations: poised, efficient, the kind of person who designs a plan and then redraws the world to fit it. You feel a small, old resistance rise in your throat, the reflex that kept you on the seawall during the last surge. Then you remember why you came.

Seren: "Maya. Thank you for coming. I know meetings aren't your favorite habitat." (an almost-smile)

You: "They aren't, when the ocean's more honest than the agenda." (you fold the coat back from your notebook) "But they're necessary."

Seren inclines her head, and the light edges off her jaw like a promise. She gestures to a translucent table where a three-dimensional model of the town floats — raised streets, ribboned wetlands, and a bright, linear seawall slicing the harbor. The model hums with potential; engineers’ neat lines make your chest both lighter and tighter.

Seren: "We can fast-track the raised wall and start engineered wetland construction this season. Federal bridging funds are already queued if the council signs on to a phased relocation for the lowest-lying blocks. It’s decisive. It buys time."

Your mouth tastes of iron when she says 'phased relocation' — the phrase that names people moving, boats left in the mud, houses sometimes never fully returned to. But she’s not offering eradication; her screen blinks with clauses and an annotated legal language you don't love but understand.

You (careful): "What does 'phased' mean, exactly? Who decides which streets move first? What guarantees do communities have that cultural sites — the mangrove patch, the old boatyard, Etta's smoking house — won't be lost in the shuffle?"

Seren watches you—steel-gray eyes that measure and then, for the first time, soften. It's a small thing, but it disarms you.

Seren: "We include cultural sanctuaries in the funding envelope. We codify co-governance on selection committees. You chair the first triage panel: two community seats, one independent ecologist, one municipal liaison. We commit engineers to build to specifications we jointly approve."

The words land like stepping stones. You lean back against the glass and look out at the harbor for a second, listening to the rain as if it will tell you which stone is true. The idea of being at the table — your hands shaping policy instead of watching it imposed — warms something that’s been brittle for years.

Etta's voice arrives in your memory: I won't bless a plan that buries the sea's memory. You wait for the part where you imagine her refusal.

Etta appears at the doorway, small and aproned, salt on her shawl. The door chimes soft and then closes. She takes you in with those narrow, seaworn eyes.

Etta Muir: "You make sure you keep the mangrove. Promise me, child."

You: "We make spaces that hold both the tide and the town. That's the promise I have on the table."

Etta studies Seren like she studies fish scales, slow and exacting.

Etta Muir: "If she puts it in writing, I'll take her mark."

Seren: (a quick hand over a tablet) "That's fair. We'll draft language that explicitly preserves designated cultural nodes and creates long-term maintenance funding. And—" (she glances at you) "—we'll bind the committee to enforce it."

Mayor Ansel waits a step behind Seren, tweed damp at the shoulders. There’s the bureaucratic smell of paper and old decisions in him. He clears his throat.

Mayor Ansel: "The region wants stability. They want to see the town take ownership of the plan. Having Maya at the helm of a co-governance panel makes this tenable politically."

You feel the pressure of his words as external wind pushing the model forward. It's the kind of leverage you can use. You also feel the internal ledger — memories of loss, the sibling's name that feels like a current under everything — and you set them in the notebook anyway, so they don't sweep you under.

Ilias arrives wearing mud streaks in the seams of his jeans, hair knotted back like an island of calm. He catches your eye and doesn't smile; instead, he gives you a look like a promise—less polished than Seren's, but whole.

Ilias: (soft) "If this gives the eelgrass a fighting chance, Maya, I'll throw myself at it. We'll prove it's worth keeping the rest."

You: "That's the plan. Keep the living parts alive. Make sure money doesn't become an excuse for erasing memory."

Seren: "We don't want erasure. We want survivability. That’s why the engineered wetlands accelerate recruitment and the seawall buys neighborhoods time while relocation is arranged with dignity."

The negotiation becomes a tango: offers and counteroffers, technique folded into tenderness for the town. You ask for co-governance. She tightens the clause. You suggest a mapping of 'cultural nodes' to be community-defined. She nods and asks for a legally binding escrow fund to guarantee maintenance. You press for independent ecological review. She accepts, but asks for a timeline clause — "three-year reviews, measurable benchmarks" — to ensure funding isn't siphoned by politics.

Seren: "We do the wall. You get a living program whose results we monitor together. We escrow the funds for sanctuaries and set the review schedule. We pilot relocation with full household allowances and support services."

You: "And the mangrove? And Etta's boatyard?"

Seren: (quiet) "Designate them as sanctuaries. They cannot be liquidated for development. They will be wedged into the preservation plan."

You press your thumb into the coral pendant and feel its carved warmth. It's not a signature, but it steadies you like one.

Seren: "I won't pretend this is easy. But it's a plan that keeps people and place together. Work with us, Maya. You make it legitimate. We'll provide the muscle."

You respond not with a legal formula but with a practical outline — community triage, cultural-node mapping sessions, immediate stabilization measures for fragile houses, and clear outreach for families who will face relocation. Your voice is granular with detail; it is the language you were trained in, the language that has kept people alive before. Seren answers in timelines and resources. The meeting becomes choreography: your specificity meets her machinery, and a shape begins to form that might hold.

Ilias watches, sometimes nodding, sometimes frowning when a technical clause reads too dryly against a human map you keep pulling from your notebook. At one point he interrupts, reaching for an example.

Ilias: "Show me—if we seed eelgrass beds with this technique, with the engineered wetlands taking the brunt of the surge, can we really bring back fish nurseries within a season?"

Dr. Hana Sato would have given a measured "maybe." Seren gives you a corporate-calibrated confidence.

Seren: "With staggered wetland construction and targeted eelgrass transplantation, modeled on the region's trials, you could see measurable recruitment in eighteen months to two years. It's faster than natural succession alone."

You feel the relief like heat under rain. Faster. Not instant, but not endless either. The sound of hope is small, practical: measured benchmarks, escrow accounts, community outreach. You find that hope is easier to carry when it has a map.

Mayor Ansel signs his name with a hand that still trembles. Etta, after a long look, places her hand on yours once — not a seal, but a benediction.

Etta Muir: "You take care, child. Don't let them pave the heart out of a plan."

You: "I won't. We'll make places that hold the stories and the living."

Seren lifts a stylus, and with the ceremonial precision of someone who knows the weight of signatures, she locks the draft into the system. It's not a final law yet — it's a commitment in ink and bytes and human words. The pavilion seems to breathe.

[Direction Cue: A brief pause. The city through the glass looks smaller and more fixable than the swell in your chest suggests.]
[Music: The strings swell gently into a brighter motif]

Ilias stays behind as the others file out. He looks at you, mud still on his knees like a badge, and his ocean-blue eyes shine with a kind of stubborn faith.

Ilias: "Come to the Saltgarden. I need you to see the wetland prototype with your own eyes. It will help when you talk to folks who need proof, not promises."

You nod. The pact here is fragile and easily undermined by rumor and fear; you know that seeing is believing for people who make homes with their hands. You follow him out into the rain.

[Scene: Saltgarden Research Lab | Early Evening]
[Visual: A converted warehouse with greenhouse domes; the interior glows warm and green. Seed trays glisten with condensation, and racks of trial plots hum with misting systems.]
[Music: Soft, hopeful piano with birdlike trills]
[Sound: Water drip, the rustle of young fronds, and quiet field chatter]

You smell wet soil and a sharp, sweet tang of kelp as you step into the Saltgarden. The air is alive with green work — a human attempt to teach an ecosystem how to recover faster than grief would let it. It feels less like a lab and more like an offering.

Ilias moves through the beds like someone remembering an old map. He kneels by a tray of eelgrass shoots and lifts one up between thumb and forefinger, watching how the light passes through it.

Ilias: "We stagger substrate types here. Shell hash, soft sediment — the engineered wetlands create sheltered gradients. The seedlings anchor faster when the wave energy is diffused. We can scale this along the sanctuaries Seren committed to fund."

You crouch beside him, breath misting in the cool air. Up close, the eelgrass is absurdly fragile and absurdly defiant.

You: "If these beds take, fish return. Young fishermen have a place to set their nets again and it's not just about stock — it's about rituals, about the boy who learns to gut a mackerel in the same spot his grandfather did."

Ilias: (softly) "I want that too. For my family. For yours."

You both look at each other in the humid lamp-light, and for a sweet, brief second the town doesn't feel like an argument. It feels like a set of hands passing on a craft.

Ilias walks you through the pilot diagrams — substrate placement, flow-diffusing berms, and plant density heuristics. He points out where the engineered wetland connects to the existing marsh ribbon and where the sanctuaries could anchor communities’ memories: the boatyard, the mangrove stand, the old festival green.

Ilias: "This isn't just ecology. It's social engineering, in the best sense — building a scaffolding the town can use to keep itself alive."

You feel your shoulders drop, the shape of a plan resting on them that finally seems carryable. For the first time since the flood that changed everything, you can imagine a way to say both 'we will move some things' and 'we will hold what matters.' The compromise is messy, but it gives you a map.

You think of the ledger again — the one you used to keep and to hide behind — and you make a different kind of mark in your notebook: a list of first steps, named people who will have seats on committees, dates for mapping sessions, a sketch of the mangrove sanctuary boundary. Each line steadies you.

Ilias watches you write, his expression unreadable and then, finally, clear.

Ilias: "You did good in there. That was hard."

You let the gratitude sit on your tongue. It's not mine alone, you want to say, but the words don't need to be sharper than the feeling.

You stand, the lab's warm humidity clinging to your coat. The plan feels decisive, efficient, and clean in the way a well-stitched net looks: utilitarian and capable. Your stomach knots with relief and anticipatory grief both — relief that there is infrastructure to point at, and grief for the streets that will be phased out. Still, worry and love can be companions; they've always been.

You close the notebook and press the coral pendant through the fabric of your jacket, like placing a small talisman on the edge of a new map. The partnership feels real: Seren's muscle, your legitimacy, Ilias’s living science, Etta's blessing, Ansel's hesitant authority. It's imperfect. It's a start.

You breathe in the kelp-sweet air and for the first time in a long while let yourself believe that the town might not only survive — it might hold its stories, in places set aside and in living beds that feed both fish and memory.

The rain softens into a steady patter outside. You think of the people who will need to see these beds, who will need coaxing and evidence and time. You think of the sound the sea makes when it changes shape. You think of the ledger, and you close it with a new line written beneath the old debts: Begin.
