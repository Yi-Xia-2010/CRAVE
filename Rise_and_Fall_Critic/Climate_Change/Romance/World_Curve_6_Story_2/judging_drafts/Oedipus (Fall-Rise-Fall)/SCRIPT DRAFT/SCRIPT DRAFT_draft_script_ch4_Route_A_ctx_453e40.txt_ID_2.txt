You taste salt and sawdust and something sharper — the metallic tang that comes with anger turned deliberate. Your field jacket is worn at the elbows where the fabric was patched years ago; the brass compass charms at your collar clink as you move. They sound small and steady, like a heart finding rhythm.

Rafi's camera catches everything. He bobs through the crowd with hungry eyes and a lens that never rests, framing Etta like a relic and a promise at once. He smiles at you with that crooked, exhausted grin of his and raises the camera as if to say: now, Maya. Now is the photograph that will not be forgotten.

You step forward onto the boardwalk and the town breathes with you. Neighbors — boatwrights, schoolteachers, kids with saplings sticking out of tote bags — have braided rope and tarpaulins into banners. Someone hands you a length of twine. Your planner's pen, usually tucked inside the battered notebook, has already left faint ink smudges across your palm.

Etta Muir stands near the center, small and wind-creased, holding an old fishing net like a prayer shawl. Her shawl smells of smoke and salt; the net is patched with years of mendings, some stitches visible as if to say: we repair what we love. She looks up at you, and the look is not simple praise or accusation; it's a weathered permission.

Etta Muir: "You did what you had to, child. You called us to make noise."

You: (You want to say it's not enough; you want to say everything and nothing. Instead, you let the boardwalk's wind shape your words.) "We make the noise together. Not to drown plans, but to shape them."

Etta's mouth twitches into something like a smile, and she nods once as if sealing the phrase into the air.

[Scene: Hearthbend Harborfront | Midday]
[Visual: Makeshift tables under tarps; a small kelp-propagation float lab is being assembled; Ilias kneels knee-deep in mud]
[Sound: The slap of boots in puddles, muffled laughter, an occasional instruction shouted and softened by distance]

Ilias stands shoulder-deep in Harborfront mud, hair tied back with a stray kelp frond sticking to his cheek. His tide-watch bracelet threads small sun reflections as he moves. He has already carved little numbered stakes and filled trays with suspended rope cuttings: a low-tech propagation station pulled together from donated mesh, recycled floats, and back-burner science.

Ilias Navarro: "This will buy us time and habitat — kelp forest slows swell energy, supports fisheries. It’s not instant, but it scales sideways with people."

You watch him, fingers tracing the edge of your notebook. There is mud under his nails, and on the inside of your ribs the old guilt flickers — a shadow you carry like ballast. But today, his smile is an anchor.

You: "We need people to believe it works fast enough to matter in the council's eyes. They'll listen to numbers. They'll listen to Rafi's footage, too."

Ilias: (tilting his head; earnest) "We'll show them a replicated patch. A dozen ropes here, a hundred next month. We can make it undeniable."

You: (thinking of budgets, of committees, of the thinness of time) "Undeniable is good. But we also make it human. We pair each propagation with a neighbor who will guard it."

Ilias laughs — a soft sound that carries genuine hope. "Kelp watchdogs. I'll make you a badge."

Rafi wanders in, breathless, hands full of flyers and a roll of wet posters. He shows you a photograph he's taken: Etta with her net, the sun cutting through mist like a blade.

Rafi Soto: "This is the shot, Maya. If we get this in the paper, people won't be able to call you rabble without hearing an old woman's hands."

You: (You feel a twitch of protective possessiveness for the image, for the town's portrait.) "Then we use it — but not to shame. To invite. To show what's already here."

Etta overhears and folds a small sprig of dried kelp into Rafi's hand as if to bless the circulation.

Etta Muir: "We hold the story. Let it travel with truth."

You return to your notebook and discover that the margins have become a script. Bullet points have widened into a petition header. Sketches of stepped berms push into a schematic of distributed berms — low, earthen, reversible — that can be built block-by-block rather than by a single, displacing wall. Your handwriting is a little messier, not because you are careless but because your idea has started to ask for other hands.

Neighbors you once inspected for mooring rights show up with seedlings and tarpaulins. An old boatwright you used to write permits for carries a sack of dune grass. A group of teenagers haul planters made from recycled plastic. They grin at you as if it were a festival and also like a beginning of work, all the same.

Seren's Skyhub buses circle in the distance, immaculate and gliding like gulls that won't land. The buses' windows reflect the banners and the legs of the folks who held them. You can feel her presence in that gloss — not yet approaching, but visible; a geometrical inevitability waiting for the town to decide its angle.

Mayor Ansel returns, now more visibly hemmed in. He holds an envelope that smells faintly of the municipal office — ink, paper, the faint ozone of ending conversations. He catches you at the Harborfront edge, and there is a pause long enough for salt to collect in the folds of his collar.

Mayor Ansel: "You understand the risk of going fully public against the regional plan — funds can be withheld. Investors don't like messy ballots."

You: "They don't like messy ballots because messy ballots let people keep voice. They also don't like being reminded that money can't buy back what the sea takes."

Mayor Ansel: (sighing) "And yet if we scare off funding, we can't fund anything at all. My hands are tied between two better-thans."

You: "Then untie them with votes, with signatures, with a plan that people can point to and say: this is ours."

He meets your gaze, and the air between you is not only political; it is personal, fragile with responsibility.

Later, the crowd gathers. Someone brings an old PA system, someone else a stack of clipboards. You step up — whether you had planned to or not — and what spills out of you is an organizer's cadence grown from heartbreak and practicalities.

You: "We will not let our town be the place that learns the hard way alone. We will plant, we will build low berms together, we will make living barriers and demand jurisdictional funds for them. We will ask Seren to fund not walls but shared stewardship. If they won't, we will find the resources ourselves."

A cheer rises — ragged, earnest. Hands clap, boots stamp, a child lets out a high, delighted yelp. The town is noisy and messy and human and it feels like progress unfolding in real time.

Ilias pulls you aside, eyes bright but careful.

Ilias Navarro: "This will make enemies in the short term. But it also makes allies. We can show function and also soul."

You: "That's the only map I can hold. Function stitched to people."

He takes your hand for a moment — a quick, warm contact that says more than words. You do not analyze it; you tuck it into the margin of the day like a note that reads: keep going.

That night, you sleep with your notebook under your pillow. Your heartbeat is steadier than it has been in a long time, a steady metronome replacing the quick, flinty pacing of grief that used to live in your chest. For now, the warmth of the group is a salve. The climb ahead — months of planting, petitions, counters to engineered proposals — feels possible because of the hands you can see.

You dream of kelp forests, of young roots weaving sandbox berms into living walls. You dream of signatures inked in coffee-stained hands. You dream of a town that looks like itself when the morning rises.
