When the applause dies down, the other sound ripples through: quiet conversations about offers. An older man in a flat cap receives a sealed envelope at the market stall. He opens it with trembling hands and looks at the numbers. They are tidy, impossible to the bones of the harbor but undeniable on paper: a lump sum that could pay off a mortgage and set his granddaughter up in the city.

Etta Muir arrives slow as tide-weathered rope. She has heard. Her hands are steadier than yours when she reaches out and takes the envelope from the man, reads the neat font that speaks of corporate courtesy more than community care. Her face tightens in a way that makes your stomach drop.

Etta Muir: "They come with smooth words and quick sums. They buy the place clean, then put up glass where children once hung nets."

Maya Kestrel: "They can't—" (You want to say 'can't' and mean the sanctuaries will hold, but the clauses, dense and precise, are fences with gates where a determined wind can find a seam.)

Dialogue continues:
