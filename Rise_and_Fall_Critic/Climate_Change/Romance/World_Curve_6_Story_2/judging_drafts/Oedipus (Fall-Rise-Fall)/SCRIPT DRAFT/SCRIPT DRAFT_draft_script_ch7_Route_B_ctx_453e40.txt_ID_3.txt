You step inside with the salt still in the seams of your jacket, the borrowed raincoat clinging damply to your shoulders. The field jacket underneath—patched at the elbows, carrying the smell of kelp and old rope—feels like proof that you belong in two worlds at once. Your notebook is under your arm, pages full of sketches and names and ribs of coastline you refuse to lose.

Seren: "Maya." (Her voice is even. Not cold, exactly—curious as a measured tide.) "Thank you for coming. I know this isn't easy."

You can see the way the lights skim across her jaw, the disciplined line of her coat. The pavilion's glass reflects the town back at you in fragments—boards and boats and banners—then smooths them into a single, efficient plane.

Maya Kestrel: "Seren." (You cross the floor slowly, because momentum matters less than the exact shape of what you're asking for.) "I'm not here to be polite. I'm here so Hearthbend's ledger isn't written without its owners."

Seren sets a tablet on the table between you. On the screen, a rendered cross-section of the coast rises and falls in blues and concrete grays. A raised wall arches like a drawn blade; marshlands are modeled in engineered hexes.

Seren: "Expedited funding: a raised seawall along the most exposed stretch, built to a three-meter standard. Engineered wetlands as living buffers, seeded and monitored. In exchange: we phase relocation for the most at-risk streets. It's controlled, staggered, funded. It keeps the economy intact."

You watch Ilias across from you—mud-splattered shirt, tide-brace still on his boots. He leans forward, eyes bright and unguarded, like he wants to pluck the plan straight from Seren's tablet and hold it to the town's chest.

Ilias Navarro: "It could buy time for the kelp beds too. If we get the wetlands working faster—"

Maya Kestrel: "You mean by accelerating eelgrass recruitment." (You nod at the schematic. You know the language; you learned it in the mangroves.) "But that relocation clause—people's homes, fishwatches, the lowest alleys—it's not an abstraction for us."

Mayor Ansel Reed stands near the holo bank, hands in his coat pockets, looking like he was built for speeches and not for harbor salt. Etta is there too—short, wind-creased, shawl wrapped tight. The old fisherwoman's eyes are flint; the ledger of the town is written in the lines at her mouth.

Etta Muir: (She speaks without preface.) "You'd better name what you keep. Don't hand us a pretty map and call it a promise."

Seren's gaze slides to Etta—an exchange weighted by history. For a beat you can see the calculus in Seren's features: PR, risk, governance. Then something like concession loosens the set of her mouth.

Seren: (Quietly) "We can include designated cultural sanctuaries—legally protected sites where relocation will not be compulsory. Co-governance clauses, with a community board having veto rights on management of those sanctuaries. Funding will be ring-fenced for restoration projects led by local teams."

A small exhale escapes you; it sounds almost like a laugh that isn't sharp with disbelief. Inside the thorax of your chest the ledger shifts—lines redrawn with names now included.

Maya Kestrel: "Co-governance has to be real. Not a headline. Etta's voice has to be on the board, and there must be clear enforcement if the ring-fence is threatened."

Etta Muir: "I'll take whatever seat you give me—if it keeps the mangroves from being turned into ash."

Seren: "We will write the enforcement into the contract language. Third-party arbitration. Transparency audits every six months."

There is friction in the room—policy rubbing against memory, engineering against fish-spots and festival docks—but the turns are not all knives. Your credibility matters here; your notes, the maps you've drawn of cultural nodes and the mangrove patch you grew up in, are more than sentiment. They are evidence. Seren responds to evidence because it's predictable, and to Etta because she is Hearthbend's memory.

Mayor Ansel: "We need guarantees. If we accept relocation in any form, can you assure me that fiscal support goes to the families first, not to contractors?"

Seren: (A small, practiced smile.) "We will place restrictions on contract awards and require local hiring quotas. The firm I work with will be audited and must publish payroll records for the project."

Ilias's hand moves toward the tablet and stops, as if reaching would break the surface tension of the negotiation.

Ilias Navarro: (Hesitating) "If the wetlands and wall are built now, we can run parallel restoration—seed the eelgrass, monitor salinity, and give fishers alternative harvests during transition. It could be a model."

Seren: "That's the point. A replicable model. Show it succeeds here; scale it."

You feel the shape of the deal settle into your ribs: a raised wall, wetlands engineered to hold the shore, a phased relocation that spares certain cultural nodes, legal co-governance. It's pragmatic; it's fast. It has the cold efficiency you have fought. And somewhere beneath that efficiency is the warm, human thing you protect—Etta's hands in the mangrove mud, the fishermen's nets.

Your stomach tightens with anticipatory grief—because saving a place sometimes means letting go of parts of it—but the chord under the tension is hope. This is the first time in many meetings that the town could point to something that both protected and preserved.

Seren: (She leans back, hands steepled.) "We will draft the clauses. You will represent the community board in the drafting process. Your signatures—Etta's approval—will be required before the town accepts the relocation phases."

Maya Kestrel: "And oversight? Local hires? Enforcement?"

Seren: "Included. Audits. Open procurement. A fund to subsidize displaced households."

You close your hand on your notebook and feel the carved coral warm against your palm—an old heirloom that is suddenly a talisman for a different kind of defense.

Ilias: (He looks at you; there is a question in his face.) "Do you want to see the Saltgarden? I—there are tests we can run that would mean the wetlands could support eelgrass in months, not years."

Ilias leads you between racks of glass and steel, fingers brushing a tray of seedlings. The lab smells like a promise: wet earth, nutrient mixes, the faint chemical tang of hopeful science.

Ilias Navarro: (He is close enough now that you can see salt under his nails.) "We ran a trial—substrate mixes, flow rates, microtopography in the marsh cells. If we seed eelgrass with this hydrodynamic pattern, recruitment skyrockets. We can transplant clonal mats into the engineered wetland modules Seren's team designs."

Maya Kestrel: "And you trust their construction won't destroy the nursery grounds while they build?"

Ilias: "That's where co-governance matters. If we have oversight, we can mandate best practices during construction. No cut-and-run. If the tech is coupled with local stewardship, it's not just a wall—it's an ecosystem."

Maya Kestrel: (The admission surfaces before you can shape it into caution.) "We did what we had to. But listening isn't the same as keeping. We'll still fight. We'll still watch for clauses that let them pawn off responsibility when the money dries up."

Ilias: "Then we watch together."

The plan is no fairy-tale; there will be committee meetings and angry letters and nights you spend mapping floodlines until the coffee goes cold. Ilias: (He reaches for your hand—a small, awkward thing—and squeezes.) "We can make this work, Maya. We'll be tired. We'll be stubborn. But this... this could hold."

You turn the eelgrass over in your palm and feel the damp roots like a map of what might still be saved.
