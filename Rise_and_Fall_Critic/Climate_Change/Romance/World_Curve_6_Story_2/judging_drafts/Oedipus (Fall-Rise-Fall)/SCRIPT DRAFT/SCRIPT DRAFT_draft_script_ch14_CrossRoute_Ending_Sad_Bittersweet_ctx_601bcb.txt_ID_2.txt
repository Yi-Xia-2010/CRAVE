Seren Blake: "Decisions are lonely when they're heavy, Maya. I wanted a solution that moved us forward before the next storm. I didn't expect it to feel like this when we finished."

You walk the old planks. They still remember how to sing under a careful foot. Etta Muir: (pulling her shawl tighter) "You did what you thought would keep us breathing, child." (Her voice is like well-worn rope.) "It kept the air in our lungs, but it's not the same breath."

Maya Kestrel: "I know." (You want to say it more strongly, to deny the hollow behind her words. But the shipyard smells of things that were once busy — hemp, tar, varnish — and the hollowness hums through them.)

You think of Ilias consoling a kelp specimen in your lab like it was a patient; you think of his hope as a working tool he had to sharpen against realities. The cooperative's funding stabilized taxes, reopened shipping lanes under new regulation, and kept the lights on. It also changed how the town breathed.

You find Ilias on the far end of the yard, leaning on an upturned crate, a tide-watch bracelet wound around his wrist. Salt has taken the bright edge off his optimism; his camera is quiet at his side.

Ilias Navarro: (voice low) "They gave the cooperative the regional grant. It's what they wanted, Maya." (He straightens when you look at him.) "We argued for room for migration, for flexible zones, but when it came to the money, they wanted hard lines."

Maya Kestrel: "You advocated for the living margins," (you say) "you argued for kelp corridors and soft banks."

Ilias Navarro: "I did." (He rubs his hand across his face.) "There are pilot funds for research, but not the wide-open access I wanted. They need metrics you can sign. They need predictability. Maya Kestrel: (dry) "When did you decide you would leave?"

You wander the market, tasting the new normal. The pulled-together banners and sanctioned vendors feel like a memorial service dressed as a marketplace. The fishmongers operate under licensing and quotas; the nets that used to pile behind boathouses are gone or reconfigured.

Etta Muir: (handing you a small paper-wrapped bundle) "Eat." (She doesn't ask; she insists. Her eyes are wet but steady.) "You did what you could. Some things were chosen and some things were taken. People need to remember both."

Etta sets her jaw. For a beat you think she will answer in the small, deflective jokes she favors. Instead, she takes both your hands in hers and squeezes like someone who knows how to hold through pain.

You let yourself hear the gristle in her voice and you feel a familiar ache — not only for what was lost, but for what you could not keep while trying to salvage more.

Maya Kestrel: (to yourself) "We made a life-preserver, rust-stained and honest. It saved many. We also built a museum for some of the things it couldn't keep."
