[Scene: Saltgarden Research Lab | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Glass conservatory panes rimed with salt; trays of kelp shimmer under diffused light]
[Music: Low, mournful cello with distant gull calls]
[Sound: Slow drip from a condensation trough; soft rustle of lab jacket against a wet bench]

You still have the eelgrass cutting in the clear sleeve. The damp paper leaves a ring of cold against your palm that feels more like a promise than a sample. Behind you, the lab smells of iodine and wet soil, of iron and the faint sweetness of decomposing kelp — the honest, stubborn smells of things that want to live.

Ilias stands at a long metal table, sleeves rolled to his elbows, fingers stained with brine and ink. The loose knot at the back of his head trembles when he reaches for a pipette. His sun-bleached tips are a small concession to the light; he looks at you with an earnestness that is almost painfully human.

Ilias Navarro: "You kept it," he says quietly. (He smiles; a little apology and a little triumph.)

You: "I kept it." Your voice comes out smaller than you intend. There is safety in saying the obvious, in naming the thing you and he might still grow.

Ilias: "Good." (He sets the pipette down and looks up, really looks up.) "Maya — I wanted to tell you in person. The regional lab offered me a seat. Full access. Long-term samples, funding… equipment I couldn't dream of hauling across tide days."

Your chest thuds against your sternum. The world narrows to the sound of Ilias' words and the slow drip of water. The offer is a bright coin dropped into Hearthbend's muddy hands.

You: "On the condition you validate Seren's wetlands design." Your tongue tastes metal when you say her name; it has been a lightning rod in town meetings, a neat geometry against everything ragged and human here.

Ilias' fingers curl, unconsciously returning to the eelgrass sleeve. He looks at the tray in front of him as if the plants might hide an answer.

Ilias Navarro: "Not only that. They want case studies — places that accept engineered wetlands as a primary adaptation strategy. They'll fund pilot deployments, rapid stabilization. They want numbers, growth rates, before-and-after hydrodynamics."

You: "And what do you want, Ilias?" The question is soft but edged. You need to know what you're bargaining for when the town's shore is the currency.

Ilias Navarro: "I want to restore kelp and eelgrass at scale — community hands, not only machines. I want labs that teach, not replace. If I can get their microscopes and their models, we could show a hybrid path. Or —" He breathes, the rest of the sentence unfinished, hanging like fog.

You: "Or you become their proof that Hearthbend can be repackaged." Your notebook presses into your side like a second skin. You can feel the carved coral warm against your throat when you swallow.

Ilias' face changes — a crease of guilt, then determination.

Ilias Navarro: "I hate that it's a choice." (He huffs, then chuckles, brittle.) "You and I both know it's not pure. Seren's team has scale. Their wetlands move water differently. But—I keep thinking if we can bring community practices into the pilot design, we could protect fishers' access, preserve the nursery patches. We could make them listen."

You: "Listen, or borrow the language until it's their language." You want to say more — to enumerates losses that can't be measured in cubic meters — but the lab's white walls make words brittle.

Ilias Navarro: "Maya, I—" He reaches for you, then stops, as if measuring how far safety can stretch before it snaps. "I don't want to leave Hearthbend's heart unattended. Not you, not the docks, not Etta's beds."

You notice the way his voice trembles when he says Etta's name. It's not just professional. It's personal.

You: "If you accept, you'll be the town's bridge, or the town's compromise. The story the lab tells might be the one the funders read." You fold your notebook open and tap a pencil flat against the paper, the sound a metronome for decisions.

Ilias Navarro: "Then help me design it." (He leans in, earnest.) "Help me put community metrics first. Help me write the protocols so they're led by people who fish and seed, not only by engineers. I know how much you mistrust Seren. But if we can force the timeline to match the biology—if we can stage the engineering around restoration—maybe we buy time without erasing anyone's life."

His words are a net thrown over the gap: tempting, dangerous, and possibly sufficient.

You: "Or we refuse them entirely and make our own slow proof." Your heart tightens at the thought. Slow work feeds your guilt and your hope in equal measure.

Ilias' jaw sets. He lets out a breath that fogs the glass between you for a heartbeat.

Ilias Navarro: "Either way, I need you. I want you in the lab, Maya. Not as a token, but because you know this place in your bones."

You feel exposed, as if the eelgrass sleeve were peeled open. Your hands drum on your waterproof notebook — the carved coral a hot weight at your throat.

You: "Read it." Your voice is steady, but the phone feels heavier than it should.

Ilias: (He slides his thumb over the message, eyes narrowing.) "Rafi says Seren's firm is already funding relocation pilots in Meridian Cove." (He lets the name drop like a stone.) "They've got a pilot there—whole blocks moved inland, compensation packages, signature contracts."

You taste iron. Relocation pilots mean moving people, not just structures. It means the tidy erasure of messy histories.

You: "So they're not only building wetlands — they're building exits." The sentence is sharper than you mean it to be.

Ilias Navarro: "Maybe. Or maybe they're using relocation as leverage to push through infrastructure quickly." He rubs his temple, exasperated and helpless at once. "Which is why I think we need to be inside. If we're out, then their narrative wins unopposed."

You look at the trays of green, at the slow patient work of life regenerating in buckets and racks. The lab feels simultaneously like sanctuary and a theater where stakes are tallied.

You: "I want to trust you. I want to trust the plants." The confession tastes like salt. "But part of me wants to stand in the town square and yell until everyone's ears bleed."

Ilias Navarro: "Then yell. And then bring data." He gives you a small, crooked smile. "Use your planner's ledger like a drum. Make them feel it and then prove it."

The sun begins to thin, and the conservatory's light turns a little more brittle. You step back from the table as if the plants will recoil otherwise.

[Scene: Mangrove Patch / Abandoned Shipyard | Early Evening]
[Visual: Tangled roots and half-sunken hulls, shafts of late-afternoon sun; the air is richer here, warmer with peat and rot]
[Sound: Soft slosh of mud under boots; a gull's lonely cry]
[Smell: Acidic tang of mangrove tannins, smoke from a distant heater]

Etta is waiting by a rotted piling, her shawl wrapped tight against the damp. The wrinkles around her eyes are maps of every winter, every storm. When she sees you, relief flickers across her face, sharpened immediately by worry.

Etta Muir: "You're late." (Her voice is a rasp that disguises concern as scolding.) "Not that this patch cares for our schedules." (She coughs, then looks at your hands.) "What happened in the lab, child?"

You show her the eelgrass cutting. Her hands hover for a long beat before she takes it, feeling the damp and the faint pulse of life.

Etta Muir: (She presses the cutting between her palms like a blessing.) "They grow back from scraps, these beds. Greed can't choke them entirely." (Her laugh is thin and a little fierce.) "But the world is changing faster than my knees can count tides."

You: "Ilias might take the regional offer to validate Seren's design. He wants to bring us in."

Etta's face hardens in a way that makes your throat tighten. She steps forward and finds your fingers. Her skin is rough, dry as old rope; her palm presses into yours with surprising strength.

Etta Muir: "You must choose, Maya Kestrel. You must pick whose hands the town will trust." (She stares at you as if piercing beyond rhetorical covers.) "If you help them turn our history into a checkbox, they'll call it preservation and move us out as if we were a thought experiment. If you stand with the people, you'll be unpopular with the ones who hand out checks, but you'll keep our songs where they belong."

Her grip is an accusation and a benediction all at once.

You: "If I stand with the people and refuse funding, what then? Do we stitch walls out of our good intentions?" The words are sharper now; the fear is a physical thing behind your ribs.

Etta Muir: "We stitch with our hands, with our boats, with every person who knows the tide by heartbeat." (She sighs, older than the sky.) "There will be loss either way, child. But the scapegoat we choose matters. If we pick efficiency, then someone else's ledger writes us out."

Her voice drops to something almost tender. She presses her thumb along the line where the carved coral pendant rests against your throat.

Etta Muir: "Remember why you took this job. Not for papers and plans. For names. For rites. For the people who braid their living into the shoreline."

You feel the weight of the town as if it were a physical thing — a sack of stories on your shoulder. The truth in Etta's words cuts through the lab's sterile logic and the seductive genius of models.

Rafi's message arrives as you stand among the roots, a small, clinical intrusion.

Rafi (Text): "Seren's crew just closed a deal in Meridian Cove. Banners up, relocation incentives. Mayor there says it's 'orderly.' Locals say it's eviction with pamphlets. I'm heading out to talk to someone. Want me to look into the contract language?"

You: "Yes." The word is automatic. You think of families in Hearthbend who will face choices they don't deserve. You think of your sibling's face — the memory like a lighthouse that has never ceased to blink.

Etta Muir: "Do it, then," she says carefully. "But know — contracts can be read two ways. We need people who can read them aloud in a square and make them understandable."

You swallow. The sky above the mangroves is bruised; low clouds draw a dark lip across the sun. You can feel the weather leaning into the decision like a judge waiting for testimony.

Etta Muir: (She presses her other hand to your shoulder.) "Whatever you choose, remember the mouths that will be fed or empty. You're not only choosing tactics. You're naming whose losses are acceptable."

The words settle in your bones and refuse to be shaken out. You think of the eelgrass in its sleeve, of Ilias' plea, of the lab's instruments, and of Seren's bright, efficient plans that smell of commerce and clean geometry.

[Visual: The tide claws at the hull of a half-sunken boat nearby; muddy water slaps the hull with small, relentless hands.]
[Music: A single, sustained minor chord; thunder mutters in the distance]

You close your notebook. The carved coral pendant warms against your throat as if sensing the decision rattling toward you.

Everything that comes after will be shaped by this choice — who aligns with you, who feels betrayed, which stories get told and which are smoothed over.

You feel the town's heartbeat in your palm: small, fragile, stubborn.

The fork is before you.

[Decision crystallization — internal monologue:]
Naming this is naming loss. Saying "we'll choose community" means forgoing quick protection money that could shore a dozen roofs. Saying "we'll negotiate" risks a sanitized future that reads like progress but smells like erasure. Saying "we'll test quietly" buys rigor and time, but may surrender political momentum to Seren's narrative. Each path shapes whose children learn to tie nets at these docks and whose names will be kept in the lantern-lit obituaries of future storms.

Your fingers drum again, faster. You taste salt and iron. The pendant is a small, hot anchor against your throat.

You are not ready to close the book on anyone; the town will reckon nonetheless.
