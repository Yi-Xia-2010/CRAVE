Etta Muir stands in her dwindling shop, knitting as if each stitch could bind the town back together. Her hands are steady; the nets take shape beneath her fingers like small, determined boats.

Etta Muir: "They said they'd give sanctuaries. They did. They gave us sanctuaries and then asked the rest of us to price-compare our lives."

You step inside. The lamp smells like beeswax and salted wool. Etta doesn't look up from the loom; she only lifts a cheeked smile that is both approval and accusation.

Etta Muir: "You brought frames and a bit of steel where there used to be only rope. That's something. But people's houses are gone, Maya. Some of them left before the framed sanctuaries were even finished."

Maya Kestrel: "We made sanctuaries for the fisheries and saved the heritage pier," you reply. (You feel the list of things you managed rattling like coins in an open hand.) "We kept what we could; we fought for community clauses that limit buyouts in sanctuary zones."

You taste the salt of something like failure. It is sharper than the sea.

Maya Kestrel: "Buying regional resilience isn't the same as keeping people in their homes." (Your voice is lower than the tide.) "Some of the buyout terms skirt the cultural ties we fought to protect."

Seren Blake: "Scaling demands efficiency. The market asks for clarity. Compromise is the only way to avoid paralysis. You see losses; I see an expedited path out of risk for many."

The three of you circle the same fact like objects on a tide-table: truth shifts with the moon. Investors call the tune from a distance you cannot see.
