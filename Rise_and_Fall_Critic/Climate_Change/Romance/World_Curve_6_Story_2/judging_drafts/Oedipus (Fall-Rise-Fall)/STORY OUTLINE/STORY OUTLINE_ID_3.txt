## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
Hearthbend is a near-future temperate coastal town reeling against steady sea-level rise, harsher storm surges, and altered fisheries. Its political life is small-scale but entangled with regional agencies and private adaptation firms. Local culture blends a working-class maritime ethic, long-lived fishing families, and grassroots science: community rooftop gardens, patchwork sea defenses, and volunteer kelp restoration projects sit beside newly proposed engineered seawalls and managed-relocation plans. Religion and ritual appear as folk rites around the harbor — offerings for safe passage, story circles in Etta Muir’s kitchen — while a pragmatic, secular stewardship ethic animates much of the town.

Power is dispersed but pressured: Mayor Ansel Reed carries municipal authority yet lacks decisive resources; Seren Blake leads a well-funded regional adaptation initiative backed by a green-infrastructure firm and sleek tech; Dr. Hana Sato and Ilias Navarro represent applied science rooted in local practice; Rafi Soto controls narrative sway through freelance reporting. Class tensions underpin choices: small-boat fishermen and market vendors worry about corporate job promises; young activists favor autonomous restoration but fear being outmaneuvered by engineering. The atmosphere is tactile and intimate — salt-crusted ropes, kelp-stained boots, damp notebooks — with cinematic weather effects that make every decision feel immediate and consequential.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Kestrel, born and raised in Hearthbend. The narrative follows a Fall→Rise→Fall (Oedipus) emotional arc: we fall into crisis as accelerating climate impacts and political offers press the town; we find temporary elevations — small-scale restoration wins, temporary funding, romantic connections — that feel like solutions; then the curve drops again into a larger collapse driven by systemic forces, trade-offs, and unintended consequences. Branching choices alter the character of the temporary recovery and who stands nearest to me by its peak (Ilias, Seren, or the cooperative), but every route culminates in a hard decline that tests loyalty, love, and the meaning of stewardship. The romance elements are interwoven with policy choices: intimate moments occur in the lab beside kelp trays, on rain-damped boardwalks, and in glass pavilions above the harbor. The story forces trade-offs between community autonomy and the seductive efficiency of large-scale adaptations, and asks whether love is a refuge, a compromise, or a responsibility under climate duress.

I wake with the harbor still in my lungs. The gulls wheel like questions over tide-dark water, and the market stalls yaw under damp awnings. My boots sink a little into the pier's grain; there is salt in the seams of my jacket and salt in the pocket where my coral pendant knocks against brass compass charms. Ilias appears at the end of the dock with a clear sleeve of kelp specimens, hair frayed with sun at the tips, and he offers a shy, hopeful smile that crinkles the corners of his deep ocean-blue eyes. We walk side by side toward the hall where Seren Blake will speak — platinum bob, sharp steel-gray eyes, the kind of blazer that glints with a solar pin. I remember the flash storm that took my sister; I carry the planner's notebook like a talisman and a ledger of things I failed to do. The mayor sent an urgent notice. Tonight, everyone will ask whether Hearthbend sells its shoreline to speed or keeps fighting for small, stubborn recovery. I ready my notes, feeling old guilt like a hard stone in my throat.

We think we can be clever enough to stop a machine; we are not. The breach runs like a wound, swallowing streets and the memories tied to them. People clutch what little they can. I stand on a sodden slope watching Etta's house slid like a toy into mud. The police and contractors and the company lawyers converge, and the town's resistance turns into emergency directives. Ilias gets a message about an offered position elsewhere; he says he must go to keep working on the science that could help other towns. We kiss under a torn banner, and it feels like a hundred goodbyes in one. I am proud and ashamed in equal measure. The fall is absolute: the model we tried to force into place becomes a ruin we cannot quite explain to ourselves.
