You remember the surge of triumph — the first shipment of Hearthbend kits stacked like a promise in the back alley of the lab, the apprentices' laughter when their paychecks arrived on time, the way the community garden hummed with new hands learning to bind eelgrass to frames. The grant read like a lifeline. You traced its clauses with the same careful hand you use on cartographic margins, convinced you could mark out safe space within its lines.

Now those same margins are a ledger of nicked corners and footnotes that smell faintly of printer ink and courtrooms. Your notebook sits open on the workbench beside Ilias Navarro's tide-watch bracelet, pages threaded with memos and photographs: logos placed where a thankful credit should have been, redlines that replace "open license" with "licensed use." Your handwriting tilts downward where your pen caught on the word "royalty."

Maya Kestrel: "We built this to be shared — to keep communities from having to invent the wheel again. That was the whole point."

Ilias Navarro: "I know. We did it right. We really did." (He sinks onto a lab stool, fingers tracing a damp ring on the table.) "But look — look at what the grant's legal team did. They took the schematic and wrote their brand across it. They bundled the apprentices into contract terms that read like quotas."

He says the words as if naming something can fix it. You hear the fatigue under his voice, the same tired optimism that once made you both laugh in the rain. You want to be harsher; you want to spit out accusations that would scar them as deep as they've scarred you. Instead you fold the edges of the photograph of a community member teaching a child to weave kelp, like you are smoothing the memory so it won't crack.

Ilias Navarro [soft]: "What if I take a role with them? I get a seat at the table where they make the scale decisions. I could steer the deployments, try to keep the rent clauses out, protect apprentices—"

The possibility is a rope thrown from a cliff: plausible, dangerous. You know why he says it. You know why his chest tightens when he imagines standing inside the corporate rooms, translating ethics into line items. You also know what it means for Hearthbend if he leaves — for the hands that learned at Saltgarden, for Etta when her dock sits half-empty, for the market's fragile web.

Maya Kestrel: "You think they won't use you. They'll use your name like a seal and keep the clauses." (There is no anger left in your voice; only the dry ache that comes after hope has been folded flat.) "If you go, what happens when they start pricing our apprenticeships back at the town as 'franchise fees'?"

Ilias Navarro: "Then I go with a list. I go with our prototypes and our counter-proposals. I go because staying here while they repurpose us feels like watching a tide take a house apart, and not trying to reach it."

He says it with that clumsy, heartfelt bravery that has always been his — a choice to act rather than to be acted upon. You should be proud. You should be steadied by the courage of a friend who won't let the ship drift without anchors. Instead you feel the pendant at your throat suddenly heavy, a small carved coral pressing cold against your sternum like something you could bury in the wet mud and forget.

You feel the motion of the town shifting beneath the same tide. There is anger. There is righteous betrayal. But anger is not always enough to undo contracts signed with lawyers who drink better coffee than you ever will.

The scent of smoked fish and oilskin wraps around her like a shawl. Etta Muir: "They offered a little," she says. "For the dock. A sum that could keep me comfortable for a spell."

Maya Kestrel: "Etta—" You don't know the sequence of sentences that could make this right. There isn't one. Your throat is a dry shell. "Why sell it?"

Etta Muir [flat]: "Because I won't take rent that comes with strings. I won't be told when and how I can fish for a living by a company that puts 'efficiency' over people. I won't have them call my grandchildren 'labor credits.'"

Her jaw tightens. The cardboard boxes hold a few small things: a carved spool of twine, an old brass lighter, a photograph of a younger Etta and a man leaning on a pier. You reach out and touch the boat's paint, felt-smooth from years of hands.

Etta Muir [softer]: "I have friends who need help buying boats, Maya. I can send them money. But I'm not staying to watch our water turned into ordinance and schedules. If this was meant to keep people safe and in place, then why does it start with renting out their ability to breathe?"

Etta Muir: "Take the petition, child," she says. "Take the signatures while I find a place where no one will measure me in quotas."

She hands you a folded paper. It is a small, stubborn thing in your palm: names in ink, the looped handwriting of neighbors. You fold it into your notebook like a pressed leaf.

Ilias Navarro: "They wanted me in the global office," he says, not asking and not apologizing. "I can be a voice there."

Maya Kestrel: "Promise me one thing," you say. "If they start taking what's ours — our names, our formats, our labor — you do not become the stamp they use to sell it."

Others leave. The town's skyline — its patched roofs and handmade boats — becomes an image in glossy brochures used to sell the very replication that hollowed it out.

There is proof that you did something that mattered. There is proof that your model saved lives in the immediate months it was deployed. That truth hums like a lamp behind the more painful fact: what you made available was taken and reshaped into rents and quotas that make livelihood into line items.

Ilias Navarro [quiet]: "Write what you must. Fight with everything you have here." (He pauses, then forces a half-smile.) "And call me if you find a way to make them listen."

When the plane disappears, the town doesn't stop existing. It reshapes. People adapt. Some find dignity in new forms; others do not. Seren Blake's firm reports "successful scaling" to shareholders; an internal memo — leaked by someone who still sleeps badly at night — uses Hearthbend as a showcase.

You keep your notebook closed sometimes, because seeing the memos is a wound you have to open again. Other times you open it and add names of those who left, dates stamped like small tombstones. You catalog what you can: contracts, photographer credits, apprentice testimonies. You teach a workshop on legal literacy and show a room full of neighbors how to find a clause and mark it.

And still the pendant feels heavy.

There is no neat closure. The rise that came fast left a different kind of erosion — one born of your own choices and the seductive optimism that a model could be both open and resilient under the pressures of capital. You feel culpable in ways that a storm's randomness would never ask of you: culpable because you signed, because you believed, because you trusted systems to be better than they were.

You map the losses: Etta's dock sold; apprentices bound by rent-like clauses; the market with fewer fisherfolk and more managers; Ilias gone to a world that needs him but will not feel exactly the same as Hearthbend. Yet irrigation of small, stubborn life continues — neighbors garden on rooftops, someone reinstalls a hand-painted sign, a child learns to tie a knot the old way because you taught it in a legal-literacy class.

You are tired. You are furious. You are grieving. But you do not stop.

Maya Kestrel [to no one in particular]: "We did this so others wouldn't have to start from nothing." (Your voice cracks, then steadies.) "We were right to try. We were wrong to trust them with everything."

The pendant rests at your throat, heavier than when the chapter began but still a thing you can carry. You fold your notebook closed and tuck it under your arm like an artifact that must be preserved, not because it will heal everything, but because the record matters. You will keep teaching, keep drafting counter-licenses, keep naming the harm when it comes. That is how you honor what you have and what you lost.

You walk back into Hearthbend, into the damp light, carrying both the proof of your work and the stain of its extraction. The town's contours remain — altered, sometimes hollowed, sometimes stubbornly alive. You light a small candle at the harbor memorial and watch the flame shiver in the salt wind; you think of a ledger you once tried to balance on your own and realize the balance is never perfect. It will never be perfect. But people are still here.
