### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Ending


[Scene: Seawall | Morning]
[Visual: Fresh concrete glistens where the tide has nicked it; work lights fold into dawn like low suns. Steel forms stand like new teeth against the shore. Rain streaks the sky in a hush.]
[Music: A sparse, downward-leaning string motif — patient, brittle]
[Sound: The steady clank of a crane; distant gulls screaming]

You stand with your hands jammed into the pockets of your field jacket and the coral pendant warm against your sternum, watching a length of wall being lowered into place. For weeks you have watched that machine move as if it could re-teach the sea how to be quiet. For weeks your chest has grown lighter in a way that felt dangerous to trust.

You remember the boardroom, but it comes to you in edges and echoes rather than a full scene — lawyers leaning like overhangs, Seren's jaw set flatter than in sunlight, the signature line finally filled with ink. You forced them to sign the governance guarantees; you turned the promise into paper. You know what that paper can look like when it is both weapon and shield.

[Visual: Cutaway flash — the boardroom table, a scatter of legal pads and a pen dragged through a clause; Seren's profile framed against glass]
[Sound: Muffled voices, the scratch of signatures]

Seren: "These covenants will be encoded into the project charter. Co-governance, Maya — shared oversight. We will not—"
Maya Kestrel: "You will put it in the charter, and into enforceable subclauses. No 'subject to' and no 'at discretion.' If there's a loophole, we close it now."
Seren: (quiet, measured) "We both want a stable town, Maya. Different paths, same horizon."

The exchange had been longer; it had been painful and exacting and legalese-stiff. The lawyers had extracted phrases like teeth and inked them into dense protections — clauses stacked like armor. You felt, then, the cool steadiness of a plan bending under a thousand small compromises.

But the armor sits on a body that still breathes.

[Visual: Back to present — the wet concrete; workers pulling up orange safety vests; the first seedlings of engineered wetlands in trays along the berm]
[Sound: Applause rises from a pocket of people gathered near the pilot plots]

When the late-season swell hits, it arrives like a memory of worse winters: the surf claws at the pilings and foams up the new promenade. The seawall takes the first blows. Where you had hoped for spectacle, you feel a private, thin terror — and then the pilot sectors hold. The planted frames bend and breathe as designed; the kelp trays flex instead of snapping. People begin to clap because clapping is what you do when fear turns into something you can name.

Ilias is at your elbow. Salt has dried in the rim of his hair; there's kelp still clinging to his boots. He looks at the water with that soft, analytical attention that never entirely loses its awe.

Ilias Navarro: "The frames redistributed the swell better than the models suggested. The eelgrass plugs are settling. We bought time — for seedlings, for people."
You: (inward) You let the word 'bought' sit a moment. Time is a currency you can spend and lose.

Ilias: (leaning sideways, voice thick with relief) "You did it. We did it. We—"
Maya Kestrel: "You kept showing up," you say aloud, because the truth needs witnesses.
Ilias: (smiling, a little wobbly) "We showed up together."

The choice you make is small, a taste of agency in a day full of structural decisions. Whatever you choose, the proximity between you and Ilias becomes its own current. After the crowd thins, the two of you crouch by a tray of kelp, tracing a finger along the glistening fronds.

Ilias: (softly) "I kept the first patch alive in the tank because I needed to know this could be real. Seeing it like this —"
Maya Kestrel: "— makes it less theoretical," you finish.
Ilias: (he takes a breath, then takes your hand with a quiet steadiness) "You taught me how to carry belief as protocol."

He leans forward, and the kiss is slow and honest and unornamented: salt on skin, the smell of wet earth, the thud of concrete nearby translating into a private percussion. For a moment it feels like a small, crucial victory — flesh acknowledging the human warmth left in a world of engineered solutions.

[Scene: Harborfront | Afternoon]
[Visual: The market stalls are quieter now; new pilings cast longer shadows. Boathouse doors show new locks and glossy "SOLD" tags in neat print.]
[Music: A minor piano motif with a rain-swollen undertone]
[Sound: The low murmur of negotiation, the crumple of paper currency]

Weeks thread into months. The wall continues to rise in segments; the engineered wetlands march in carts and pallets. Volunteers trade stories of blisters and small triumphs. The pilot's survival becomes a line item in municipal reports and in Rafi's articles. You can feel the town's pulse move from fear to a fragile, organized hope.

And yet, beneath that pulse, something else is moving: offers in neat white envelopes carried to people who had weathered every storm because they had no other roof. Corporations — not the Skyhub delegation necessarily, but adjacent investors and development fronts — begin to buy out leases outside the sanctuaries. They offer lump sums with fast signatures, attractive packaging: a new car, a settled mortgage, a signed release.

You watch an older couple at the far end of the quay take a thin envelope and walk away with their hands trembling. You know their ledger: a mortgage that smelled of salt and rust, a clinic visit last winter, a grandson who needs steady schooling. You understand why people accept what appears to be salvation.

Maya Kestrel: (to yourself) You could have been kinder in your expectations. You could have expected the market to follow the safest margins.

[Visual: Etta Muir's boathouse — a wooden structure with a faded nameplate — has a new metal placard hammered into its post. Two men in corporate jackets stand with clipboards; behind them, movers ease out crates.]
[Sound: The dull scrape of a crowbar; the small, private sound of Etta’s breath.]

Etta stands watching, hands tucked into the pockets of her oilskin apron. The shoreline has written lines on her face deeper than most — creases that keep stories safe. She looks smaller somehow, as if the sale shaved a chunk from her familiar horizon.

Etta Muir: (voice thin) "They promised to keep the... common tethered, but then they used clauses. They used 'transfer of interest' to leap paragraphs."
Maya Kestrel: "We built the clauses to be thick, Etta. We put co-governance into charter."
Etta: (a brittle laugh) "Legal blankets don't keep out the rain of coin, girl. Folks get offers like a warm coat, and they take it."

You want to unspool the entire legal fabric to show her where the loopholes were threaded through — where an 'in perpetuity' line can be made to feel like 'until further notice.' You want to tell Etta that you fought for this, that the sanctuaries exist, that some places will be saved. But the existence of sanctuaries does not stop the market from redrawing the rest. She watches as her favoured docking post — the one she named after her mother — is gutted and listed for refurbishment into a boutique storage for luxury charters.

Etta: (turning to you, eyes unreadable in their grief) "It was supposed to be ours — for our nets and our stories. Now it's a portfolio asset on a quarterly report."

Her face folds inward in ways you cannot uncrumple. You remember pressing Seren's boardroom, the cold certainty that signatures could tie hands. You remember the night you thought co-governance would be a refuge. You did not, at the time, account for the way money would speak to necessity and make loneliness sound like relief.

You choose words and small interventions until your voice wears raw against the bureaucracy of property and profit. Seren receives you with the same gaze you have seen before — steel gray, unflinching — and there is an exchange that will not leave you the same.

Seren: (hands folded, earnest in her own frame) "Maya, we wrote co-governance because we respect the social fabric. But I can't legally stop a private sale in every corner. I can press for contingency funds, for first right of refusal in certain districts, but not across the board."
Maya Kestrel: "You promised enforceable subclauses — and yet the architecture of the deals was designed to make this sort of piecemeal erasure possible."
Seren: (softening, then firm) "You wanted guarantees. I pressed them into the charter. Markets will respond. I won't pretend I can fix every heartbreak. But what we've built can protect core cultural sites."
Maya Kestrel: "Core sites become islands," you say. "And islands can be surrounded."

There is a silence between you both that tastes like metal.

[Scene: Harborfront | Dusk]
[Visual: The sun slants low and orange across slick timbers; a smudge of steel-blue wraps the horizon. Lights from boats and new development blink like a distant constellation.]
[Music: A single cello line, slow and resigned]
[Sound: The tide breathing, soft and inexorable]

You sit on the shadowed edge of the quay, your notebook open across your knees. The sanctuary clauses you fought for are there in black — tightly worded, legally dense, marked with the lawyer's initials and the date Seren signed her assent. The ink is not a victory banner; it's an argument.

You flip through your own notes, the maps with shields drawn around kelp beds and eelgrass patches, the schedules of who waters which plots, the lists of volunteers who promised through frost and heat. You trace the corner of a clause that, in theory, should have prevented some of the erosion you have watched in people's lives. In practice, the clause has teeth enough to bite at some bad actors but not to hold back the slow, polite theft of neighborhoods.

You taste the sea and the taste is different now — metallic at the back of your mouth. It is the flavor of statutes and settlements, of cash sliding across palms and the way it can be labeled salvation.

Ilias finds you there, the light catching in his eyes in a way that makes you feel foolish for expecting everything to resolve. He kneels by you, careful not to crowd the small geometry of your grief.

Ilias: "We preserved some things," he says, as if tallying comfort might make it take root. "We saved the pilot; we saved the beds; we kept Etta's stories from being entirely washed away."
Maya Kestrel: "We saved them on a map," you correct gently. "We couldn't stop the rest from being sold to convenience."

He reaches for your hand and you take it — a small, stubborn anchor. For a breath, being held like this is its own kind of policy, a protocol of comfort you both can administer.

Ilias: "Do you regret holding them to the clauses?"
You: (you say the truth not to him but to the water) "I don't regret trying. I regret thinking it would be enough."

Etta crosses the quay with an unsteady gait. She sits a short distance away, not wanting to intrude on your conversation and yet not able to stop herself from being present. She looks at the notebook, then at the harbor, then back at you. Her mouth makes a small, almost private shape.

Etta Muir: "You kept us from being bulldozed out in a single sweep," she says. "That's weight enough to carry with your back. But don't think for a second that weight won't break parts of you along the way."
Maya Kestrel: "You taught me how to argue with the sea," you say. "You taught me that our stories belong in the ledger."
Etta: (smiles, small and jagged) "Stories in the ledger get footnotes. People read the footnotes when they're paid enough."

She looks at you, and for a heartbeat you see the old fisherwoman who would haul you aboard when the weather shifted, who taught you to read wind by the angle of a gull. Now she looks like the rest of the town: older, quieter, rearranged.

You close the notebook and feel the weight of the clauses fold into your palms like a map of both accomplishment and failure. The sanctuaries stand and they will stand because of the work you all did; the seawall holds in the places you said it would. But outside that architecture, the town has shifted into lines and transactions that no guarantee could fully reweave.

You thought co-governance would be a refuge. It is a refuge, in places — a sheltering ledge in a long storm — but it is not the whole shore.

[Visual: The tide laps gently against new concrete; farther down, a deck light from a recently refurbished boathouse glows with a different promise.]
[Music: The strings resolve into a low, reflective chord]

You rise, your boots slick with harbor water, and walk the length of the quay once more. Each step presses the memory of signatures into the soles of your feet. You have given the town legal armor — thick, tested, and imperfect. You have given your own heart a proof of what it means to risk compromise for survival.

You do not have a triumphant closing line. You have the resigned knowledge that fights won on paper can still leave the heart of the place rearranged.

You stand at the edge, the notebook closed, the sanctuary clauses folded between your fingers like a small, brittle talisman. The metallic tang of the sea is still in your mouth when you look up at the horizon, where the engineered line meets the wild water.

[Visual: Fade to the harbor lights; the sound of the tide lingers like a postponed answer]
[Music: Fade Out]
