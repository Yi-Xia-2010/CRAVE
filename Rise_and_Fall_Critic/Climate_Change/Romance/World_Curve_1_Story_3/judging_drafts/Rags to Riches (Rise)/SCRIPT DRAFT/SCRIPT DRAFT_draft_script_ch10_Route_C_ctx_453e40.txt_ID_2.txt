[Scene: Mayor's Office | Late Morning]
[Visual: The mayoral conference room — a semicircle of worn oak chairs, a long map-strewn table, a sunstriped window overlooking a strip of raised walkways and distant harbor cranes]
[Music: Gentle, ascending strings — cautious but bright]
[Sound: A paper cup rolling softly against a glass as someone sets it down; faint gulls and distant traffic through the window]

You arrive with the hybrid plan folded into your notebook, the potted cutting tucked against your ribs. The choice you made at the council — to stitch community work to targeted tech — still hums in your joints like a promise. It feels like stepping onto a bridge you've just drawn in chalk and asking everyone to cross with you.

Mayor Lila sits at the head of the table, laminated schedule folded like a talisman. Hana stands near the window, arms crossed but not closed; her AR monocle glints as it skims a feed. Elias leans against the wall, shoulders loose but watchful — his expression is open, and something taut runs beneath it. Dr. Jun is to your left, hands folded, eyes tracking the sunlight on the map. Teo sits farther along the table, fingers worrying a strap of his bag.

You set your notebook down and breathe in the room: warm paper, the metallic tang of a city near the sea, the faint scent of the cutting's soil. You begin.

You: (quiet, measured) "Mayor Lila — thank you. What I'm proposing is not a stopgap. It's a pilot model the city can scale: community-built living seawalls where it's socially and ecologically appropriate, paired with targeted Aegis pump nodes in the most hydraulically vulnerable corridors, and a municipal maintenance fund to ensure the community keeps agency over how both are used."

Mayor Lila: "Specifics, Ms. Rivera. The council will want to know exactly how this avoids the usual traps — funds diverted, communities sidelined, tech put in with strings attached."

You watch the mayor's pen hover over the map. You can feel the room leaning for a single, clear line of action.

You: "We commit—on paper and in practice—to three guarantees. One: all physical work on living seawalls will be community-led, with municipal grants covering materials and training. Two: Aegis-style pumps are only deployed after community consultation and only where they reduce immediate flood risk in a way living systems can't. Three: a maintenance fund under municipal oversight will pay living wages for local maintenance crews, with enforced transparency and a rotating oversight board drawn from neighborhood representatives."

Hana: (softly) "And the metrics? If pumps are deployed, we need criteria. Efficiency, failure modes, maintenance schedules. We can model the hydraulics and show where pumps reduce peak loads and where they might create dependency."

Dr. Jun: "The models are here." (he nudges a tablet across the table; algae-green graphs ripple under your thumb) "Simulations show that a hybrid approach, applied in specific topographies, reduces modeled inundation by twenty to forty percent without requiring the displacement of the shoreline communities we've been trying to protect."

Elias: (leaning forward, voice warm and raw) "My concern is governance. We've all seen how 'community oversight' can be a label that erases real power. How do we stop a corporation from sliding in and turning our neighbors into maintenance subcontractors on terms they didn't choose?"

You: "By writing the oversight into the agreement. By giving the community real seats and real veto power over where pumps go and who maintains the seawalls. By tying municipal maintenance funds to living wages and training programs, not to corporate contractors by default."

Teo: (brisk, practical) "And by making sure the parts we buy are repairable here. No sealed modules that only a certified tech from downtown can fix."

Hana looks at you. There's something in her face that's both relieved and carefully calculating — the two of you have always argued numbers and values until you reached their common edge. Now those edges are being joined.

Hana: "I can get Aegis to open a sandbox contract for a limited number of pumps. They'll want performance clauses, of course. But if we can guarantee community-led installation and a maintenance fund that pays for public technicians — technicians hired through community channels — then we can close the loop on your governance concerns."

Elias: (a half-grin) "Are you promising me this, Hana? Or promising the room?"

Hana: (a small, complex smile) "I'm promising to draft terms that make promises enforceable. Leon will negotiate the cost. You will negotiate the language. You two can both distrust me and still read the contract."

You: "Yes. We can have a pilot proposal within two weeks. Dr. Jun will finalize the hydraulic mapping; Teo and I will coordinate community canvassing and the oversight roster; Hana will work with Aegis legal to draft a sandbox MOU; and the Mayor's office can structure the municipal maintenance fund as a line item in the emergency services budget."

You feel the first real, tactile lift of momentum — not the rush of victory, but the steady raise of a tide being turned by many hands. People start to move: Dr. Jun taps at his tablet; Teo pulls you aside to run a logistics list; Hana and you trade a few low, technical sentences about sand filters and pump capacity.

You: (to Teo, quietly) "Can we get the community reps ready? A rotating board, at least five names."

Teo: (grinning) "Already spoken to Sofia and three others. They want to be on it. They'll be blunt and stubborn, but in a good way."

Elias: (catching your eye) "I'll help run the canvass. Make sure the message doesn't sound like a sales pitch."

You: "No sales pitch. Transparency and power."

Elias: (soft) "And Asha — if this works, we do more than defend homes. We show a model people can copy."

There's a heat in his voice that goes beyond policy. It's hope. It's dangerous and beautiful.

You ride the tower elevator with Hana and Dr. Jun. The city unfolds beneath glass panels: rooftop gardens stitched between reflected buildings, the harbor glinting like scattered coins. At the top, you enter a conference room where a slim delegation from Aegis waits — legal pads, a cleanly branded holo-board, and an air of practiced receptivity.

You take a breath and let the city's smell — wet earth and citrus from the markets, and the ozone sense of sea on the breeze — stay with you. You are inside the corporation's polished geometry, but the cutting at your side feels as much like an anchor as it did on the walkways.

Leon Voss is not in the room — a deliberate absence that makes the meeting feel both lighter and more precarious. An Aegis liaison introduces themselves and gestures to the holo-model: a stretch of shoreline where living seawalls and pump nodes are overlaid in neon.

Aegis Liaison: "We've run a preliminary cost model. We can offer pumps on a limited hardware-open license, with a maintenance training stipend. Leon will review the final MOU, but our legal team can start drafting."

Hana: (calm) "The license must include local diagnostics and a clause preventing unilateral firmware locks. We also want a clause that guarantees parts availability at local prices."

Aegis Liaison: (nodding) "We can structure an open-hardware clause. You will need an enforcement mechanism."

You: "Municipal maintenance fund, plus an oversight board with veto power. That enforcement mechanism is the board's remit."

Dr. Jun: "And we will instrument the pilot sites with independent sensors — open data, public dashboards. No proprietary black boxes."

Aegis Liaison: "Public dashboards reduce vendor defensibility, but increase public trust. That could be a strong selling point."

You and Hana exchange a look; both of you are negotiating not just technology but narrative — how this story will be told if it succeeds.

You: (internal) There it is: the promise that requires you to be visible and unyielding. The fear — that compromises will split you from what you love — sits in your gut like a stone. And next to it, something brighter: the possibility that careful stitchwork can keep both people and place intact.

You: (breathes in) "We have the outline. We have two weeks. Let's make sure we don't lose what matters while we're fixing what's broken."

Elias: (taking your arm briefly — a small, complex contact) "We won't. We'll make it ours."

You feel the weave take shape under your hands.
