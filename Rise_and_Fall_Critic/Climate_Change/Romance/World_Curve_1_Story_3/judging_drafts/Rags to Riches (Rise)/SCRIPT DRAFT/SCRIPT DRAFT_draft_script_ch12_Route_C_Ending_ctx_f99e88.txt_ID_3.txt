You came up before the market woke, covenant folded in the pocket of your jacket like a small, legal-shaped talisman. Mayor Lila's call still smells of heat and brass in your memory — a promise and a hinge: "Bring me the covenant and a clear ask." You did. You and Hana tightened the language around community veto, cooperatives, and escrows until the edges were elbow-greased proof against spin.

Now the rooftop smells of damp soil and citrus peel. Jun is bent over a laptop, calibrating the first batch of open-source sensors — the little gray nodes that will speak the Barrio's language to anyone who listens. Teo is two steps away, chewing the end of a spanner like it's a thought he's trying on.

Dr. Jun: "Readings look good, Asha. The humidity compensation is set. If we run those thresholds publicly, the network will flag anything outside community-agreed tolerances."

You: "Good. Make the thresholds visible — not buried. If people don't see how decisions are being made, they won't trust the process."

Dr. Jun: (nods) "Transparency is the only way to make a covenant real. The data has to belong to the people who live with the water."

Teo: (grins) "And if anyone tries to mess with it, we tag them with bandaid patches and throw tomatoes. Just kidding — mostly."

You can't help a half-laugh. Teo's humor is always the sturdiest thing in the room.

[Scene: Skyward Market | Noon]
[Visual: Tiered glass domes glitter as the midday sun slices through condensation; stalls unfurl under striped awnings]
[Music: Bright, communal rhythms; hand drums and a soft brass in the background]

The rollout looks like a neighborhood fair, because in every important way, it is. Tables laden with seed packets sit next to interactive displays streaming live sensor feeds. The covenant sits under a clear dome at the front table, surrounded by signatures waiting for ink — the mayor's, a community elder's, a representative from Aegis, and yours.

Mayor Lila: "This is the kind of partnership I want to bring to council. Legally binding, community-enforced, and with a built-in pathway for local economies."

You step up to the microphone and your voice skims across the crowd like a boat over glass.

You: "We aren't asking to be saved. We're asking to be trusted to steward our place. This covenant is a tool — but the real thing is you: the people. Keep the sensors. Teach your neighbors. Vote with your feet and your hands. Hold us to it."

Elias appears at the edge of the crowd like a sunrise — quick, bright, and on the verge of something. You both have carried the weight of falling out; the heat between you is not only political but deeply, stubbornly personal.

Elias: "You did it. I wasn't sure you'd get them to sign anything that didn't try to bury our say."

You: (careful) "It wasn't easy. Hana and I argued until dawn over wording for community veto."

Elias: (raises an eyebrow) "Hana? I pictured you storming the council with banners, not balancing clauses with a corporate engineer."

You: "The point wasn't to make nice. The point was to make sure our people aren't moved for a marketing brochure."

Elias: (sits on the low wall nearby, voice rough with history) "And what about people who want nothing to do with Aegis? What about—"

Hana: (interrupting, hands open) "We built exits. The cooperative is independent. The escrow requires audited outcomes. If Aegis walks, the funding reverts to community control."

Elias studies her, then you. For a long beat, the market noise recedes; only the low hum of the sensor grid punctuates the air.

Elias: "I used to think technology made people cold. Now I can see it can also carry our memory, if we make it carry the right values."

You: (feeling heat along your spine) "Values are not data points unless we fight for them. We have to teach people how to read the data, to contest it."

Elias: (half laugh, half sob) "Teach me first, then. I missed the workshop."

You: "You always were late for meetings."

Elias: (grins) "And you were always too good at forgiving people their timing."

Hana watches you exchange that old ping of banter, the small orbit of familiarity between you and Elias. It is complicated, layered with what you were and what you've needed to become. You feel a tenderness for both — for Elias's raw, pulsing belief and for Hana's way of building scaffolds where others would shout. The market becomes a place to stitch those different threads together.

[Scene: La Marisma | Evening]
[Visual: Golden hour bleaching into a soft violet; walkways glow under lantern light; the sea is a distant bruise]
[Music: Tender strings returning; a slow, hopeful swell]
[Sound: The tide sighs, distant music from someone's radio, muffled cheers fading into conversation]

Back in La Marisma, the rollout feels less ceremonial and more true. People stop you on the walkway to ask how to read a graph; others offer to host sensor workshops in their homes. The cooperative's fund has been activated and seeded; the first maintenance schedule is chalked on a community board next to lost-and-found keys.

You walk with Hana along a familiar stretch of raised planks. The potted cutting at your belt — the same stubborn little thing you carried through the campaign — is greener than it has any right to be.

Hana: "I… didn't expect the board to agree to the veto clause as written. I thought legal would push back."

You: "They almost did. But Jun's data and the way we wrote transfer language… it made the oversight meaningful."

Hana: (pauses) "Asha, thank you. You kept the center of this work human."

You: (turning to her) "You kept the center precise. Without both, we would've spun in circles."

Hana looks at the slice of water visible between houses — the sea a living mirror — and lets out a breath that sounds like relief. For a long time the two of you were two sides of the same coin: she, the exacting engineer; you, the stubborn steward. Now the coin looks like something you could build a bridge with.

Hana: "I want to teach the cooperative the technical side. I want to help them debug the sensors. And — if you'll let me — continue writing the oversight language as it evolves."

You: (smiling) "I'd like that. And I want the cooperative to teach us how to be better neighbors when the sea doesn't read our plans."

She laughs, tiny and bright. Elias appears at the end of the boardwalk, carrying two cups of something steaming and citrus-sweet.

Elias: "For the long watch," he says, handing one to you and to Hana.

You: "To the long watch," you echo, and the three of you stand looking over the neighborhood, the market lights like constellations below.

Elias: (softly) "You kept faith in ways I didn't always understand. I'm sorry I made you prove it."

You: "You pushed us when pushing was needed. I'm sorry I hid behind caution."

Hana watches you both — the old ache in her eyes has smoothed into something more like hope. The three of you are not a tidy reconciliation, but that is the point: stewardship is never tidy. It is messy, iterative, driven by people who argue and make food together, who fix pumps at dawn and argue codes at night.

You let yourself feel the rising light inside your chest — not fireworks, but something like a tide returning. The model you helped craft is breathing: open-source sensors humming, legal teeth preventing easy withdrawal, a funded cooperative that owns not only tools but training and authority. Jun's algorithms will be copied, forked, improved. Mayor Lila's council will hear more neighborhood voices because the market's success made political courage easy to sell.

Dr. Jun: (from a nearby bench) "We already have messages from two other districts asking for our blueprint."

You: "Then we document everything. Workshops, templates, the plain-language covenant alongside the legal code. We make it replicable and impossible to hoard."

Teo: (wiping his hands) "And we throw a better launch party next year."

You laugh, and the sound is warm and light and real.

You: (inward) You think of the people whose homes were once washed away, of every small ritual you made to keep a place called home — a seed-swap table, a repaired water pump, a mural repainting that stitched grief into color. This is not the end of storms or of loss. It is the start of a new map: one that is made by the people who live on it and defended by lines written into law and into neighborly practice alike.

Hana steps closer, fingers brushing against yours as if to anchor the moment.

Hana: "Stay."

You: (you don't answer with words, because words have a way of flattening. Instead you take her hand. It is small, warm, a pragmatic something that promises perseverance.)

You look out at the neighborhood lit by lanterns, at the tiny green cutting safe in its sling. The covenant sits folded in your jacket, a seed of paper that will grow only because people water it every day with attendance and argument and repair.
