Mayor Lila Ortega: "Asha. The council will want specifics. If you're asking us to commit municipal funds and oversight, we need to understand the risks and the returns."

You slide the weathered notebook to the center of the table and lay out three panels — sketches, quick calculations, and the routes for community-led construction. Asha Rivera: "Mayor, what I'm proposing is deliberate pairing. Community-built living seawalls — mangrove cofferdams, oyster-reef modules, reinforced vegetative berms — combined in targeted locations with Aegis-designed pumps and a municipal maintenance fund. Hana watches the model, expression folded inward, eyes reflective and unreadable. Mayor Lila: "If the hybrid keeps people in place and lowers long-term costs, I want to know: who does what? And how do we guard against displacement under the guise of 'efficiency'?"

You had rehearsed this question a dozen times with Jun during long nights of data-wrangling and with Teo while fixing a jammed pump. You don't dodge.

Asha Rivera: "The mayor's office signs a stewardship covenant that funds maintenance and seats community representatives on the repair board. If Aegis technology is used, it's on conditional trial contracts — data-sharing, transparency clauses, and a sunset review. The community co-ops manage construction and long-term stewardship; the city provides a dedicated maintenance line so the burden isn't borne by residents alone."

Dr. Jun: "The model brings measurable reductions in overtopping events when the biological structures are seeded adjacent to mechanical surge buffers," he adds, patient and soft. "The simulations show a twenty to forty percent reduction in peak stress on the seawall when both strategies are used in tandem at calibrated intervals."

Mayor Lila: (leans back, steepling her fingers) "Those numbers are persuasive. But the council will ask if the community can deliver on construction scale and technical compliance."

Elias steps forward, voice low but bright with the old conviction that once knit neighborhoods could do anything.

Elias Hart: "We can build. We've done it before. But Asha — they always bring strings. If the contract gives them access to data in ways that outmaneuver residents, you hand us surveillance under the label of 'efficiency.' I don't want our gardens to become telemetry fields."

You meet Elias's gaze. He is not worse than himself; he is simply what he has always been: impatient with hierarchy, quick to see threat in compromise. You can feel the history between you folding into the room like a second shadow. You choose language that will not erase that shadow but let light fall around it.

Asha Rivera: "Then we write the oversight into the contract. Community representatives hold veto power over data usage that affects tenancy or displacement. The pumps operate only under pre-agreed thresholds; any remote access requires dual authorization with a community delegate. Transparency is part of the infrastructure."

Hana's mouth tightens; she tilts her head, measuring the wording. Her face is a clean slate of controlled resolve, but you catch a softness at the corner of her eyes that complicates the silhouette of someone who left the neighborhood for lab keys and corporate doors.

Hana: "Conditional trials with fail-safes. I can work with that, provided the technical teams have clear SLAs. The pumps must be reliably maintainable on a municipal budget. If they're not, the model collapses under its own maintenance costs."

Asha Rivera: "We model for maintenance in Year 1 and Year 5, with line items in the municipal fund and a public audit clause yearly. And we train local technicians — Teo here will run a training curriculum."

Teo looks up, surprised into a grin, and rattles off a quick list of tools and contacts he's already lined up. The room smiles at his contagious certainty. You let yourself absorb it; optimism is a muscle you have cultivated like any other.
