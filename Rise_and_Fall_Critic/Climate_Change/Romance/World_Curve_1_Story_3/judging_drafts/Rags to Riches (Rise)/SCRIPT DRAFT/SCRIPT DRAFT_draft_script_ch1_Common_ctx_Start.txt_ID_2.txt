For a moment the city narrows to this tiny living thing and the reasons you keep going: roots, hands, names.

Your notebook hangs open on the table, pages full of sketches for living seawalls and notes for the meeting with Hana. The pages smell faintly of coffee and seawater. You look at the list — Tideworks schedule, the negotiation with Hana at ten, a site visit to the rooftop membranes — and feel that particular lift of purpose: there is a plan, and there are people who will follow it.

Teo: "You leaving or are you tending seedlings and writing treaties with the sea all morning?"

Teo: (grinning) "Speak for yourself. I left a wrench on the staircase for the stormgate, if you want to live dangerously."
You: (smiling despite yourself) "Which staircase? The one with the missing slat or the one with the better view?"

You sling your bag over your shoulder, strap the pot into its patched cloth, and step onto the raised walkway. Boards sigh beneath your boots; algae makes the edges shimmer. The city opens in sections: a sequence of courtyard gardens, patched rooftops, the occasional solar sail. The smells shift — wet wood, baking bread, diesel from a repair boat — but there is green everywhere, stubborn and tended. Children weave between legs like currents.

You push through a cluster of neighbors inspecting a newly woven sea-fence. Someone calls out a question about anchor points; you answer, more to test the plan in a voice than because they need instructions. Your words land, and the fence gets shifted half an inch, then half an inch better. It is small, but it is work done in public — and it matters.

Down below, beyond the market hum, you see Elias before he sees you — a bright patch of canvas, his patchwork jacket catching the light. He moves like someone who thinks in gestures: handing out flyers one moment, checking an elderly neighbor's sandbag the next. When his eyes find you, there's a quick, almost involuntary lift in his face, then the practiced organizer's focus settles back over it.

Elias: (approaching with two cups and a grin) "Coffee? I brought you a diplomatic salvation brew. It may convert conservatives."
You: "Do it. I need convincing today."
Elias hands you the cup; the steam fogs the air between you. His fingers brush yours — routine, nothing and everything at once.

Elias: "You look... centered. Ready for Hana?"
You: (you take a slow drink; the coffee is bitter, grounding) "As ready as I can be. She's bringing resources, and that changes the tenor. It's not just about tech versus people—it's always been about how resources shift power. You know that."
Elias: (nods, eyes searching) "I know. I just worry 'resources' gets translated into 'we hand over control and hope for the best.' That's not enough. You don't have to be the only person in the room making sure folks keep their say."
You: (you feel the pull of both arguments — Hana's order, Elias's fire) "I don't want to hand anything over blindly. But I also know what happens when we lack pumps in a storm. There are trade-offs. I want a seat at the table where the trade-offs get honest names."
Elias: (softening) "That's why I trust you in there. You name the costs, Asha. You don't let them hide behind glossy prototypes."

You both fall into a rhythm of planning — routes for public comment, which neighbors to bring, whether to ask Dr. Park to prepare a short data brief on expected tide surges. The conversation circles outward, gathering other voices: Sofia who will testify about market loss, Teo who will bring pump logs, and the quiet person from Block Seven who still hasn't decided if they'll trust the city.

Elias: (after a beat, quieter) "And... after? We need to ensure whatever they build keeps the community's hands on the levers. Not in a token way. We mean it — shared maintenance, training, transparency. No white boxes."
You: "Agreed. If we craft terms, they have to include training modules, local oversight, and open-source schematics. The work shouldn't vanish behind a corporate lock."
Elias: "Good. Then we go in speaking of power — literal and political."

There is an ease in this exchange that warms you. Elias's optimism rubs against your pragmatism and leaves both smoother; you both temper each other. The tension isn't erased, but it becomes useful — a fuel for action rather than a wedge between you.

Teo appears at your elbow, waving like someone who has just finished a repair and wants approval. He's lugging a coil of tubing and a grin.

Teo: "They finally let me mount that valve I dreamed of. Also, someone owes me three empanadas."

Elias tucks a flyer into your open notebook — a clean, earnest layout calling for a community assembly after the negotiation. On the back, a rough list of speakers someone scrawled in the margins. Your heart stirs at the sight of it: plans born on paper are the first line of defense against chaos.

You move through the market with purpose: pick up a spool of bio-filament for a Tideworks sensor, borrow a loaned ladder to reach a high greenhouse panel, exchange a line about soil composition with Sofia. Each small interaction knits you into the web that keeps La Marisma functioning.
