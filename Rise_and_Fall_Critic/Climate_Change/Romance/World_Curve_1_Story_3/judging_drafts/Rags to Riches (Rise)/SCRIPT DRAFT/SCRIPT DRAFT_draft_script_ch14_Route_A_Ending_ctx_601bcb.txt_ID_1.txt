You stand where the boardwalk widens into the estuary's elbow, the place where the barrio's maps end and the water begins. Your palms still smell faintly of salt and coffee; the cutting in your pack gives a small, green pulse against your ribs when you shift. The choice you made in Chapter 5 — to open the door to Hana, quietly, rather than shut it against the world — sits behind your teeth like a steady heartbeat.

You watch the tide breathe. You think of Elias' hands — quick, scarred from rope and lift — and of Teo emptying toolkits at all hours; you think of Sofia's stall, its stack of citrus like a small, sunlit promise. These faces are not just reasons; they're the scaffolding of your resolve.

[Visual: Hana arrives from the boardwalk's shadow, her silhouette precise against the dawn; the AR monocle catches the light]
[Music: A single, hopeful piano note]

Hana: "You wanted the meeting to be subtle. I appreciate that," she says, voice careful as a measured equilibrium. (She folds a thin roll of plans into the palm of her hand.)

You: "Subtle, yes — but not secret. People need to be able to look at what we build and know they belong to it." (You keep your voice low; the gulls shouldn't be the only things listening.)

Hana: "I agree. Corporate involvement has to be accountable. I wrote a clause for community oversight — Jun's lab will model load tolerances, and we'll log every pump cycle to an open ledger." (She taps the edge of the plans as if testing the paper's honesty.) "But I need the ground truth — builders who know the estuary — to validate adaptions on the fly."

You: "That's exactly it. Technology that listens rather than replaces." (You find yourself impressed by the clarity of her phrasing. Surprised, too; she used to argue in equations where you used to speak in soil.) "We can set up a steering team: two community coordinators, Jun, and one of your engineers. No proprietary black boxes. Everything audited."

Hana hesitates, something like relief passing through her features. She steps closer; the smell of her blazer — metallic clean, a faint ozone undercurrent — meshes with algae and wood smoke. For a second your chest tightens in a way that has nothing to do with policy.

Hana: "If we do this, we do it on a pilot stretch — the living-seawall prototype plus targeted pumps and a small desal microgrid. If it scales, it scales with the community's rules. If it fails, we dismantle. No strings beyond that."

You: (You let the permission sit in the air. You imagine the kids on those walkways running rope and laughter through the modules; you imagine Jun sighing with the kind of scientific satisfaction that makes his eyes wet.) "We make the rules public. We schedule teachbacks. We train the crew. We guarantee the labor — the barrio builds the wall; your firm supplies specific engineered composites for the mesh and funds for the pumps."

Hana: (She looks at you — long, like she is cataloguing the little changes time carved into your face.) "You know this will mean compromises. I've spent years translating community needs into deliverable specs. I won't sign away crew safety, and you won't let this become a trophy project."

You: (Your reply is a small, steady thing.) "I won't. And you won't let it be a PR ribbon." (You both let a soft laugh out; it's the kind of laugh that patches old fissures.)

Hana: "Lead, Asha. I'll follow the plan." (She smiles, small and almost private.) "And after we set the parameters, we can present it to Mayor Ortega as a mutually owned pilot."

[Scene Transition: Rooftop Greenhouse (Skyward Market) | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Dew on greenhouse panes, hydroponic beds hummingly alive; vendors arranging produce under a canopy of woven reed]
[Sound: Murmurs of conversation, the clink of ceramic cups, a distant municipal drone mapping the skyline]

Sofia: (She meets you halfway, hands dusted with soil, a grin like sun on her face.) "You brought the engineer," she says, teasing, then softer: "Is this the thing that'll stop the water from taking my stall?"

You: "It's the thing we hope will keep your stall, and the stalls down the line." (You offer Hana a nod of introduction; the two women exchange a formal, quick assessment.)

Elias: (Arriving, jacket flapping, eyes narrowed not with anger but the shark focus of someone who carries a hundred plans in his head.) "You brought Hana in?" He speaks with the bluntness of someone who mistrusts polished sleeves, but there's no edge of betrayal in his tone — only concern. "Does she come with handcuffs made of corporate policy?"

You: "No handcuffs. Terms, oversight, and public accountability." (You lay the idea plain on the near table, tracing the pilot's footprint with a damp fingertip on a spare sheet of cardboard.) "We build the living seawall — community-funded labor — and Hana's firm provides specific tech components and pay for pumps, on the condition that they're open-source to our maintenance teams."

Elias: (He studies the cardboard map, then you. His jaw works.) "And what about Leon? He won't like words such as 'open-source' or 'community veto.' He likes narratives, trophies. He likes to keep the shine." (There's a pause where he looks at Hana with a challenge, then half a laugh.) "So why should I trust a plan with a suit in it?"

Hana: (She answers without flinching.) "Because I won't let it be a trophy. Because this pilot is a controlled risk. Because we can run modeling cycles in collaboration with Jun and the barrio to minimize exposure — and because the pumps will be maintained by locals with paid shifts."

Elias: "Paid shifts." (He repeats it like a litany.) "Okay. You'll have to prove follow-through on that. Teo — what do you think?"

Teo: (Hands full of bolts, voice sandpaper-soft.) "I think we need the mesh and the living plants. I think we need the pumps when the tide goes wild. I think we need work we can get paid for, and I think this will get us a foot in the door." (He shrugs; his practical optimism is an anchor.)

Sofia: "If it keeps my daughters' feet dry in their first flood, I'll hand you all the citrus." (She grins; the group laughs, the tension easing like a rope slackening.)

[Scene Transition: Living Seawall Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Half-assembled modules sit in the shallow water; wooden forms stained with tide; seedlings bundled in recycled mesh]
[Sound: Hammers, calls across the water, the faint whirr of a rented pump; gulls circling low]
[Music: Warm orchestral swell, brass a soft glint of triumph]

You stand on a ladder, boots muddy, hands half-full of rope. Around you, neighbors and volunteers — faces you know by the cadence of their laughter or the way they tie a knot — pass materials, raise frames, weave live vegetation into the wall. The work is ordinary and holy at once: callused hands and patient instruction, the small miracles of salt-tolerant roots taking hold.

Hana is beside you, sleeves rolled, an unusual smudge of mud on her lapel. She leans in, close enough that the scent of her—clean, faintly metallic—mixes with wet wood and chlorophyll. Her fingers brush yours briefly while tying a knot; the contact is warm and electric.

Hana: "You choose the species, and I'll calibrate the mesh tension," she says softly, as if continuing a conversation that's been going on longer than either of you remember.

You: "Mangrove cuttings on the inner face, saltwort as a binding plant. We stagger root density for flexibility." (You feel Jun's voice in the back of your mind — models and numbers — but the soil under your nails is the real test.) "If the pumps go down, the living parts hold longer if we orient them this way."

Hana: "Understood." (There's an ease now that wasn't there before; a shorthand built from shared objectives.) "We schedule weekly maintenance shifts and log them publicly. We allocate a fund for spare parts. We also agree: if the tech ever jeopardizes the living components, we shut it down."

You: (You look out at the stretch of water and people, and for the first time in a long while your chest is uncomplicatedly light. The pilot is happening in the most reliable way — by work, by presence.) "That's the clause I want written in ink."

Hana dips her head, then looks back at you with a steadiness that goes beyond negotiation. "And what about us?" she asks, quiet in a way that feels like a question with no bureaucratic checklist.

You: (Your pulse quickens. You know enough to be careful — romance in the seams of fragile projects can become a distraction or a comfort. But all the careful spreadsheets in the world couldn't predict the softness under her hand.) "We keep it simple. We trust the work to be our bridge. We'll be careful in public, honest in private."

Hana: (She smiles — small, and entirely true.) "Careful and honest. I can do those things."

[Visual: The sun lowers; the seawall's first assembled modules catch light like a promise]
[Music: A gentle chorus, lifting]

Teo: (From across the shallow water.) "Hey, Asha! Elena from the north stretch says the mayor's office called — they want to look at the model before the week is out."

You: (You steady yourself on the ladder. The legal memo is drafted; the community-approved pilot parameters are nearly complete. All that remains is the final presentation — the formal handover to the mayor, the signatures that would turn this pilot from an agreement into an operative program. It's procedural ceremony, but ceremonial moments change the course of things.)

Hana: (She offers you a small sealed packet — the edited memorandum with the oversight clauses highlighted.) "Take it," she says. "Read it before we go. Your voice needs to be the one that opens the door."

You: (You lift the packet, its paper weight oddly reassuring. Your fingers trace the edge. The noise of the site — the low conversation, the slap of boards — becomes background to your own blood in your ears. You imagine the mayor's face, Jun's careful smile, Elias' reluctant nod. You imagine the barrio's children running along a raised walkway that will one day be drier because of this day.)

[Direction Cue: The camera closes on your hand as it reaches for the pen laid beside the packet — a simple ballpoint, its clip bent; the tide glints behind you like a horizon of decisions]
[Music: The strings hold a sustained, hopeful chord]

You inhale, tasting salt and dust and something like possibility. Your thumb rests on the pen. The moment feels both small and huge: a bridge between the messy work you've always done and the institutional validation that could protect it. Hana's hand finds yours, steady.

You consider the clause that gives the community veto power. You consider the clause that guarantees paid maintenance shifts. You think of Jun's models, Elias' crews, Teo's practical wisdom, Sofia's steady markets. You think of the pilot, fragile and determined, rising on the back of this single act.

You place the pen over the line — not yet touching paper, just hovering — and feel the collective breath of La Marisma in the pause.
