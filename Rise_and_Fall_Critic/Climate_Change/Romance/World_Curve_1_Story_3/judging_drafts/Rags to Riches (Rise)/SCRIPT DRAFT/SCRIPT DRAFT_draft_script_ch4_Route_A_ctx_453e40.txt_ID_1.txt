Elias: "You did it. That was—" (he searches for the word, then shrugs) "—brave. The council won't forget the photo-op, but I know you didn't do it for the camera."

You (Asha): "I did it because people needed a clear call. Because half-measures will drown someone eventually. We need momentum, and we need people to feel they're part of making it."

Elias leans in, eyes bright. There is relief there, and a quick flash of the old tether between you — late-night meetings, shared damp sandwiches, the thing that used to be simple trust.

Elias: "We got volunteers already — more than I expected. Teo did a round of the eastern alleys. Sofia's bringing empanadas. Jun texted he'll swing by with the salinity meters after the morning run."

You watch the volunteers arrive: hands callused and ungloved, teenagers with patched jackets carrying burlap and native marsh plugs, elders steady with baskets of cuttings. [Scene: Living Seawall Site | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: A half-built living seawall: woven coir mats, young marsh grasses, wooden posts sun-bleached and scored by tide]
[Music: Rhythmic strumming, warm and forward-moving]

You take a breath and step into the center, tools at your belt. People quiet down; training begins not because you have to assert authority but because everyone wants this to work.

You: "Okay. First thing — posts driven close enough to hold the mats against storm surge, but not so tight that the roots can't breathe. Think of them like ribs supporting a living lung."

A volunteer raises a hand; her voice is shaky with adrenaline more than doubt.

Volunteer: "How deep do we sink the plugs? What if the water's too salty?"

You (Asha): "We'll stagger plantings in micro-zones. Salt levels vary with the tide and with runoff. Jun will help us pick species tolerant to each band. We don't need perfection; we need resilience. We plant the conditions that can adapt."

Jun arrives with a battered field kit and a calmness that steadies the edges of the morning.

Dr. Jun: "Salinity's within expected range. We'll do a second reading at high tide. Asha, if we place cordgrass in the first band and sedges behind, it'll trap sediment faster. The science lines up with what you're building here."

Your chest loosens a fraction. Science and hands braided together — the thing you came home for.

Elias steps closer, passing you a spool of twine and a coil of coir.

Elias: "You want me to set up the east quadrant. You take west with Teo?"

You (Asha): "We'll tag-team. I'll show the volunteers the first weave, then move to the seedlings. Teo — can you check the supply drop in the alley? We need more coir if the tide runs high."

Teo appears from behind a stack of pallets, grinning despite the dark circles under his eyes.

Teo: "Got a cargo biked crate and a skeptical neighbor who thinks this is 'too pretty to be useful.' Also — we lost two bundles of coir to damp rot in storage. Not enough if we go full-tilt."

A small silence follows, not heavy but focused. Resource concerns have a way of sharpening everyone’s voice.

Student: "We made this for the march — can we hang it along the walk? Also, can we learn how to weave coir? My grandma says she's proud."

[Scene: The Boardwalk at Dusk | Low Light]
[Visual: Weathered planks silvered by the setting sun; the new sections of seawall silhouette against a calm, reflective water]
[Music: Warm cello with light chimes; atmosphere soft and swelling]

Later, with most of the day's heavy lifting done, a small circle remains on the boardwalk. Lanterns hang from thrifted poles; their light makes the faces of your neighbors softer, more human than committee minutes ever do. You stand beside Elias. Your hands are raw but steady.

Elias: "Look at what we did. Not perfect, but—" (he breathes it in) "—look at it."

You (Asha): "This is hope that can be touched. Not some glossy plan on a screen. People can come here, plant, and see it hold. That's different."

He watches you, and the thing between you feels complicated — shared history, past sharp words, an easy ferocity. But tonight it’s an ember rather than a blaze.

Elias: "We still need more hands for the northern stretch. The council will make noise. Corporations will promise money with conditions. But tomorrow — we teach another crew to weave. We bring Jun's data to the mayor. We make the living seawall legible to policy."

You think of Mayor Ortega's lamination, of Leon Voss's inscrutable smile, of the thin line between being pragmatic and being sold. You also think of the faces behind you — the people who will sleep easier if the next storm comes and the new bank of cordgrass holds. Your fear of betraying the community softens into something more precise: a responsibility to keep them informed, safe, and empowered.

Dr. Jun steps close, passing you a small tablet with the day's data curves glowing blue.

Dr. Jun: "If we log wave attenuation across these sections, we can show measurable benefit within weeks. That will give the mayor something that isn't just a speech. It will be numbers tied to names."

You take the tablet, the screen cold against your palm. Numbers and names braided together — that is the work of translating this gathering into policy.

Sofia offers you a folded piece of paper — a list of neighbors who need door-to-door reinforcement next week. Your thumb traces the inked names.

Sofia: "We won't leave anyone behind, Asha. Not if we can help it."

You feel the warmth of the barrio settle into your bones. Momentum is not just a word anymore; it's a line of people moving in the same direction. Even the resource worries that shadowed the morning have softened into actionable tasks: a petition for emergency funding, a logistics rota, a rota for teach-the-teacher sessions.

Elias: "Tomorrow's going to be a different kind of pressure. The council will ask for deliverables, and Leon might float a corporate offer — 'scaled' protection — and we have to show that our work can be part of a city's system, not just a charity project. Are you with me?"

You look out at the seawall you helped start, at the hands that planted, at the faces softened by lantern light. Hope coalesces not as naïveté but as a plan in motion.

You (Asha): "I'm with you. But we show how this works on our terms. We document; we demand oversight; we make sure people don't lose their rights for a glossy fix."

Elias nods, the outline of a plan already forming in his jaw. The tide sighs against the new living ribs you've set into the water.
