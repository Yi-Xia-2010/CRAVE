[Scene: Living Seawall Site | Dawn]
[Visual: Morning mist threading between anchored modules; ropes, netting, and green shoots of planted marsh grass]
[Sound: Water lapping softly, distant hammering, a gull giving a single keen]
[Music: Warm, buoyant strings—an ascending motif]

Narration:
You arrive before the sun has climbed, boots dark with yesterday's mud. The living seawall creaks and breathes around you—sections of recycled timber, woven geotextile, and wriggling mats of cordgrass held in place by the hands of the barrio. Lanterns still wink in the rafters where neighbors packed up; a coffee thermos steams on a crate like a small promise.

Your fingers find the seam of your jacket where the weathered notebook sits. Habit is anchor here: notes, sketches, a list of parts and names. You listen first—because what the site needs isn't always the loudest demand. Today it is a quiet, precise thing: a bottleneck in materials and a shortage of reliable pumps. The seawall has skeletons where muscle should be, and the tide is patient but persistent.

You kneel beside a half-secured module, feeling the grain of salt-streaked wood under your palm. The spray of brine is cool on your wrist; basil and algae mingle on the air. You let the noises of the site bloom around you—Teo's low whistle as he inventories supplies, Elias's voice barking a laugh as he hands out nails, the muted vibration of a municipal drone passing overhead. The day already smells of oil, fresh-cut rope, and wet earth.

You flip open your notebook and scan the tally: composite mesh—low. Bilge pumps—two functional, four out for maintenance. Fasteners plentiful enough for one more bay. The map in your pocket shows pinch points that could be mitigated, if only you could thread the missing resources into place.

You close the book and stand. There are three obvious paths forward that have been forming in your head since last night: mobilize more hands and requisition materials directly; build airtight, politically persuasive data with Dr. Jun to unlock public funding; or reach out quietly to Hana for conditional technical aid. Each feels like a different faith—faith in the barrio's muscle, faith in institutions, faith in an old friendship now complicated.

[Music: Strings lift, hopeful and steady]

You breathe in and move toward the Supply Depot, where Teo is already behind a makeshift counter of crates and canvas.

(he shrugs, then softens) "We can jury-rig, but pumps... pumps are tricky. Without steady pumps the lower modules are vulnerable when the next storm hits."

You study the board where supplies are pinned: a scatter of sticky notes, a hand-drawn timeline, a line of names signing to volunteer days. Your gut tightens, but the tightness is familiar—a tool more than a threat.

You: "If we keep building at this pace, we can seal three more bays in a week with what we have. But that's more hands, not more pumps. Without pumping cycles we're just making nicer dams for a future tide."

Elias (from across the depot): "Then we take what's needed and make it ours." (his voice is quick and bracing) "There are unused municipal warehouses on the eastern pier. You: (you feel the old rhythm of his certainty, the way it calls to something stubborn inside you) "And the legal bit? The media? The mayor's office calling it 'unauthorized requisition'?"

Elias: (shrugs, a half-grin) "Then we make it an issue people can't ignore. We film the kids watering plants instead of pumping, we show what we're losing while they polish their grants."

Teo: (frowns) "Fast gain, sure. But Leon and the city will have mouths to feed tomorrow—political mouths. If we burn those bridges, might be hard to get them to notice the Barrio's requests next month."

[Scene: Tideworks Lab (Makeshift) | Noon]
[Visual: A low warehouse alcove with grow-lights and a table scattered with algae cultures and a laptop showing tidal graphs]
[Sound: Hum of an improvised pump, the soft clack of a keyboard]
[Music: Quiet, analytical piano—rising in optimism]

[Direction: Dr. Jun arrives with data printouts; his presence is steady and quietly encouraging]

Dr. Jun: "You found me in the stacks." (he smiles, eyes soft behind round glasses) "I ran the models again with the updated sediment transport numbers you sent last night."

You: (you pour over the graphs, the lines and shading a language you trust) "What's the headline?"

Dr. Jun: "If we can secure funding for targeted pump arrays and stabilize the sediment in two critical bays, we reduce overwash risk by thirty-seven percent on medium-intensity storms. The numbers are... persuasive." (he taps the page) "But the council needs longitudinal certainty. They want two seasons of monitored data and a clear maintenance plan."

You feel the tilt of the problem: convincing bureaucracy requires time. Time is a commodity the seawall doesn't have in abundance, even as the neighborhood's energy hums like a transformer.

You: "Two seasons is... long."

Dr. Jun: "It is. But funding that comes attached to oversight and maintenance is durable. What you do with it after they hand it over matters—community-managed maintenance can be written into the contract. It's slower, yes, but it could secure the pumps and mesh for years, not days."

You: (your mind stretches to map steps—data collection rosters, sensor deployment, volunteer logs, a maintenance fund) "We have the volunteers. We can get sensors online faster if we prioritize them, but it pulls manpower away from building."

Dr. Jun: "Exactly. But if you can present a concise study—forty-five days of pre-implementation observations, a control site, and a projected maintenance schedule—I've got colleagues who will vouch. I can testify. It's not instant. It is firm."

You nod, tuning your attention to the cadence Jun suggests. It is methodical comfort—data as muscle memory for a city that favors numbers.

You walk the boardwalk alone for a few minutes—an old habit of yours when decisions press. The wood underfoot smells of salt and varnish; the sky is a watercolor wash that promises gentle weather but doesn't promise much else. This is where choices settle into you, like small stones placed in a pocket to weigh the day.

You replay the conversations: Teo's practical caution, Elias's white-hot insistence, Jun's patient scaffolding of time. Then there's Hana—her voice as precise as a calibrating device, her solutions elegant and clean. You haven't spoken to her in a while; not for lack of wanting. The professional distance between you has been a negotiation of its own.

You imagine calling her—quiet, conditional: "Can your team spare a few prototype pumps, if we agree to oversight and community control?"—and you imagine the possible faces of that ask. You imagine Elias's jaw tightening at the thought of turning to a corporate engineer. You imagine Jun's measured nod if the terms protect the community.

The estuary wind tangles the single braided strand in your hair and you feel the strange lift that comes before choices that matter. Whatever you decide will shape not just bolts and hoses but trust.

You sit, hands folded, sketching the scene in your mind as if it were a plan on a page. Each option pulses with consequence and possibility.

You think about what you owe the barrio—speed, safety, and the keeping of dignity. You think about what you owe yourself—the clarity to choose a path that won't leave you with regrets that outweigh the repairs.

You stand and head back toward the site, a small, steady determination building.
