## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Us

---

## World View
The year is 2038. Coastal metropolises have reshaped themselves into hybrid ecologies: reclaimed wetlands woven through glass towers, rooftop farms clinging to seawalls, and neighborhoods raised on stilts and patched walkways. Political power is split between municipal offices trying to steward scarce resources and corporate R&D towers promising fast technological salvation. Class fractures are visible in skyline contrasts — Aegis Tower’s clean chrome against La Marisma’s corrugated warmth — but so are grassroots networks: markets that double as food hubs, improvised clinics, and living seawall workshops.

Culture is pragmatic and intimate: rituals of seed-exchange, storm-prep drills, and memorial lantern releases coexist with AR data feeds and corporate sponsorship banners. Religion is diffuse — practical faith in community and science both vie for reverence. The central conflict is a policy and ethics fight over survival strategy: do you scale high-tech solutions from above, or embed resilient, people-led ecological systems from below? Romance and past loyalties bleed into the political: choices about pumps and pilot projects become choices about neighbors, lovers, and what we will refuse to give up.

The mood is soft cyberpunk — neon-emerald glow in rain, watercolor textures, and eco-noir lighting that keeps both warmth and clinical cool in the frame.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Asha Rivera) return to La Marisma after years of training in coastal ecology to shepherd a desperate community through another storm season. With my weathered olive jacket, braided sea-glass strand, and potted cutting, I navigate neighborhood demands, a past love-turned-rival (Elias), and an old friend turned corporate engineer (Hana). My nonprofit’s living seawalls compete with Hana’s firm’s automated barriers, and the mayor must choose which program to fund.

The structure is a “rise” arc: each path presents challenges but pushes me toward increasing competence, influence, and a real, scalable victory — different flavors of success rather than failure. The common route establishes stakes and relationships; at our first major split I choose an approach: grassroots mobilization (Elias), engineered partnership (Hana), or an ambitious hybrid broker. Each route branches again into tactical decisions about demonstrations, pilot programs, or policy bargains. Every ending is a form of progress: a community-protected neighborhood with renewed love; a policy victory that preserves culture at a cost; a replicable stewardship model combining data and neighbor knowledge. Romance threads through these choices: reconciling with Elias, rebuilding trust with Hana, or crafting an imperfect but enduring partnership. The story culminates in hard-won, different kinds of success across routes.
