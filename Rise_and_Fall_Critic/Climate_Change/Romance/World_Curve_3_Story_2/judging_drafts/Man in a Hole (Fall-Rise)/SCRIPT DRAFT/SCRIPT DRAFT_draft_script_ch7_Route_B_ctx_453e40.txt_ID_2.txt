[Scene: Solace Harbor Promenade | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm, muted light under a patchwork tarpaulin; banners ripple in a wet breeze]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the low rumble of a tide engine, market calls turned quiet by the crowd]
[Music: Minor, steady cello undercurrent; a thin patter of rain on canvas]

You arrive already half inside the movement, the weight of your notebook and the compass at your throat like tools that have been waiting for this moment. The market around you blurs into a wash of color and noise until Nova's voice cuts through — direct, metallic, and impossibly calm for someone standing in a circle that hums with anger.

Nova Duarte: "We stop them here. We stop them now. They can keep their contracts; we keep our homes."

Her platinum undercut glints under the tarpaulin. She doesn't wait for applause. She pins a schematic to a makeshift board—hand-sketched marshlines, arrows to show water paths, neighborhoods circled in quick, furious ink. The plan she lays across the board is blunt: tidal wetlands, buffer zones, targeted managed retreat where the city will not act. It's less engineering report, more moral ledger.

You stand close enough to smell the citrus of Samir's thermos and the wet fabric of Nova's protest vest. Your fingers find the spine of your notebook; the leather is cold and slightly sticky from salt. Your heart is loud in your ears, the thud arranging itself into resolve.

Samir steps up with a stack of clippings and thin, weathered testimonies folded inside a plastic sleeve. He lays them down in front of you like evidence.

Samir Reyes: "These are the letters from Murray Row. Names, dates, kids. They said 'we'll rebuild' until the sky took their roof. They need a way out they choose, Mara—if you ask me."

You take the sleeve. The paper is cold. You thumb the edges, tracing inked lines that map onto your memory of streets and lost porches. Your pen hovers over a corner of your notebook; you map and unmap the shapes as if erasing the middleman's language of compromise will reveal something truer.

Nova watches you as you work; she taps the board with a finger, punctuating the air.

Nova Duarte: "We won't let the mayor's timeline paper over people's losses. We make the city account, or we make the city stop."

You respond, your voice measured despite the heat in your chest.

You: "We make them account. We make relocation voluntary, but funded. We make the wetlands legally protected. We—"

Nova cuts you off with a small breath, not unkind. She wants action, not theory.

Nova Duarte: "You talk like someone who still believes they'll listen because you did the math. Sometimes the math isn't the thing that moves the city; the people are. We move them."

Her words land with a small, hard certainty. You nod because you know it's true and because you're tired of translation between the technical and the human.

The crowd's murmur swells into something like a chant; pavement becomes rhythm. Nova's megaphone crackles when she takes it. The first words are a scratch, then a roar: "The sea took our roofs—this city will not take our rights!" Hands clap, feet stamp; the sound is a small tidal pulse.

Your phone vibrates — a thin interruption. Elias's name blooms across the screen in a clean municipal font you've seen in other lights. You don't read the message aloud; you don't need to. The single-line text is the kind of reasoned plea that sits in your chest like a stone.

(Phone Text — Elias Kade): "Mara — please. There are contracts, mitigation funds. This blockade will trigger injunctions. Let me help secure relocation funds through permits. Don't make this a fight with the city."

You look at the screen, then at Nova. Neither message answers the ache in you. Elias offers a path of procedure, a narrowing that has saved cities before. Nova offers a fracture that might save people now. Both paths bear weight; both risk tearing what you are.

You feel the old reflex — the one that makes you parse tradeoffs as if they were components on a board. But there is no single ledger today. There is only people, rain, and the rising shape of a bulldozer on the horizon.

Nova steps close enough that you can see the fine lines around her eyes, the way the rain makes her undercut darker at the roots. She doesn't soften; she steels the room.

Nova Duarte: "If the city won't choose us, we'll choose ourselves. We will have a living shoreline — but not if they build a wall that cuts communities off, or if they coerce relocation without money, without dignity. We are blocking that machine."

Samir pulls his hoodie tight, voice low and tentative for the first time.

Samir Reyes: "Legally, they'll say 'public works'. They'll have builders and bonds. They won't hesitate to call us trespassers."

Nova's jaw tightens.

Nova Duarte: "Then we'll be witnesses. Then we'll make every bondholder know the names of children who will lose their schools. We'll make it expensive to ignore the human cost."

You feel your optimism harden into a kind of deliberate fury — not blind rage but a plan turned sharp. You map elevations with a borrowed tablet, your fingers cold, ink staining skin where you press too hard. The contours you draw are honest: homes that cannot be saved without displacing families, low banks where water has already begun to whisper through floorboards.

A volunteer runs up, breathless: a municipal lawyer is at the promenade entrance. An email has gone public — Mayor Chen's office claims any obstruction of the Sea Barrier project will be met with 'all available legal remedies.' The city's tone is practised; you can hear the smile in it through the speaker.

You: "They'll threaten us with permits. They'll threaten us with injunctions. They'll paint us as obstructionists."

Nova looks at the crowd, then at you.

Nova Duarte: "Let them threaten. We make them explain why they're building a wall that pushes people into higher ground but not into safety. We make them explain why profit comes before porches."

The promenade's market noise bends into a chant that's equal parts grief and accusation. Someone shouts, and the sound takes shape into an action plan: a blockade at the Sea Barrier Construction Site. The name of the place tastes like iron in your mouth.

[Scene Transition: The group folds into a column, walking toward the southeast highway as clouds thicken]
[Music: Drumbeat rises, syncopated; the sky darkens]

[Scene: Sea Barrier Construction Site | Storm Afternoon]
[Visual: Cranes loom like black skeletons against a pewter sky; orange safety vests signal clusters of movement; mud hills glisten]
[Sound: Heavy rain on tarpaulin, the thud of boots in mud, distant sirens that might be precaution or threat]
[Music: Low, dissonant strings; a single timpani like a heartbeat]

You stand at the edge of machinery and purpose. The smell here is diesel and saltwater and something metallic you can't name. The site feels like the city's intention made physical: a project enormous enough to be believed, a promise of protection that doubles as erasure.

Volunteers link arms. People in orange vests—your neighbors, your childhood math teacher, a barista who brought you coffee the morning after the last storm—brace against the rain. The barricade is human and fragile.

A foreman yells orders through a bullhorn. A security van idles, hazard lights pulsing. Your notebook is slick with rain, pages feathering. Your compass pendant is cold enough to bite.

You and Nova stand shoulder to shoulder. For a breath, the memory of the atrium intrudes — chair-rows of reasoned debate, Elias's measured appeals, the mayor's deadlines. Here, reason is a different act. It is bodies, it is testimony shouted into the roar of machines.

Nova Duarte: "When they move the cranes forward, we'll be the human stop. We'll file witness reports. We'll make the press stay."

You swallow and speak, the words coming out smaller than you meant.

You: "And if they call the police? If they call injunctions? If they force arrests?"

Nova's smile is brief, a flash that does not reach softness.

Nova Duarte: "Then we are arrested with our names in the record. We make it costly for them to pretend there were no choices. We will be the thing that forces a public reckoning."

A text blinks again. Elias: "This will escalate quickly. There are legal channels. Please — at least let me try before you risk arrests."

You tuck the phone back into your pocket. The words both plead and warn; in the back of your mind, you catalog the ways the city has already failed. Each failure is a ledger line against a life. Each threat from the mayor's office, each lawyer's email, a reminder that the municipal machine is designed to grind down rawness.

Samir squeezes your arm. His face is lit with rain and irons in the sky; he looks like someone who has chosen to be frightened for a reason.

Samir Reyes: "We are good people. We are tired of being last in their budget. We do this right, and we might save someone."

You close your fingers around the compass pendant, feeling it press cool into your palm. The storm is louder now; the first shouts from the foremen coalesce into the sound of movement. Your stubborn optimism—always the quiet engine—has turned into strategy and a kind of righteous terror you barely recognize.

You breathe in diesel and rain and the thick, taut hope of people who will not be papered over.

You think of Elias and his contracts. You think of Mayor Chen and her polished smile. You think of Nova's grief and Samir's steady hands. You think of the houses you've marked on the tablet, the names you have written down.

The rain cuts into the chant, and the world feels narrowed to earth and water, to human bodies against gears. The decision you made in the atrium is now a body of people, cold and loud and unwilling to be moved. The cost opens like a wound.

You hold your breath as the bulldozer idles into view and the foreman's voice tightens into an order.

A figure from the city's legal team steps forward, umbrella like a thin shield. He holds a sheaf of papers high enough to catch the light.

City Lawyer (over bullhorn): "By order of the mayor—cease and desist. Disperse now or be subject to arrest and civil action."

The words hang in the rain. Around you, feet shift, someone curses softly, a child cries once and is hushed. Nova hands you the megaphone. You feel the weight of its cold plastic in your hand, hear the static when you bring it close to your mouth.

You could speak to call people back. You could call out legal counsel. You could let Elias' last plea fold into the record.

You remember the day the last storm took part of your neighborhood—how the water came in politely at first and then arrogantly—and you know why you're here. You also remember why you were trained to make plans and find budgets. The two things inside you are pulling like tides.

You lift the megaphone to your lips, the strings of your compass warm under the cord at your throat. The rain is a sheet; the bulldozer teeth glint like a threat.

For a second, everything feels like the moment before the wave breaks: the air full of suspended choices, the crowd pressed to a single edge, the municipal machine grinding and grinning as it readies its gears.

You taste salt and mud and the small iron of fear at the back of your tongue.

Page-turn: You open your mouth.
