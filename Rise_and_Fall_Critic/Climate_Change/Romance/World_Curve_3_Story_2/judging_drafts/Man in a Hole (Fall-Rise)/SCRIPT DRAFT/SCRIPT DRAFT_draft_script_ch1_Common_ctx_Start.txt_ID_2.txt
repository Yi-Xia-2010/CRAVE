[Scene: Solace Harbor Promenade | Late Afternoon, Low Tide]
[Visual: Golden-hour light softens wet cobbles; market stalls line the promenade with frayed banners. Distant skyline silhouettes under a low ragged cloud bank.]
[Music: Low, melancholic piano with distant, slow percussion — a fall-tinged motif]
[Sound: The scrape of wet cobbles underfoot, gulls calling, fabric flapping in a steady wind]

You stand with the tide at its lowest and the memory of the high-water line still a bruise under your skin. Wind tugs at the hem of your recycled-sail windbreaker; the brass compass at your throat is cold and familiar, a tiny promise you touch without thinking. The market smells of frying fish and brine, the citrus of a vendor's crate, a sour note of pickled things. The smell brings you back to a different rain — to someone calling over a roar you couldn't make out — and your chest tightens, the way it always does the first day you come back.

You walk the stalls because habit is a map. Fingers trace frayed banners, thumb over stitching, a memory of hands that sewed those banners the same way they patched roofs. Your notebook is damp in the inner pocket of the jacket; when you slide it out the pages flutter, water-dark halos pooling around inked cross-sections and rough berm sketches. The lines look like islands.

[Visual: Close-up on the notebook: truncated seawall cross-sections, quick sketches of garden swales, notes in compact, exact handwriting]
[Sound: A vendor's cry—"Fresh sea-lace pickles! Best for the storm!"—fades under the wind]

Samir is exactly where he always is: a stall wedged between a tarp of secondhand fishing gear and a woman selling preserves. He flags you with a grin you remember but don't always deserve. Sun has kissed his skin into the same warm bronze as the rope on his wrist. His dreadlocks are tied back; his movements are easy.

Samir: "Look who finally made it back to the promenade. You carry the ocean with you, Mara — or you let it sit in your pockets like dimes."

You: (You force a smile, fingers still on the compass) "It's only been a few years. Not enough to forget how to argue with the tide."

Samir: (He hands you a small jar, condensation beading on the glass) "Pickled sea beans. Thought you might need—" (he shrugs, half-joking) "—something salty that isn't people saying 'we'll be fine.'"

You reach for the jar. The glass is warm from the sun; the lid gives with a soft metallic click. You smell vinegar and green sea, and something like belonging nudges at the ache under your ribs.

You: "Thanks. I'll put it with the others." (You tuck the jar carefully into your bag, between taped maps and a rubber band of pens.) "How's the east block holding?"

Samir: (He sobers.) "Holding in ways that make me tired. The new sandbags helped by the promenade, but folks up on Alder say the ground's different. They're patching, cursing, patching. They want you to see. They want—" (he waves, searching for the word) "—someone who knows numbers and not just optimism."

You feel the word 'want' like a small physical weight. Want and need are different things in this town; you've learned that. Your fingers find the compass and rub the brass as if rubbing sense into a design.

You step away from Samir because the promenade moves like a tide itself, bringing faces you know and faces that know what you took and what you left. A cluster of residents has gathered near a stall that sells tide maps and wool hats; their faces are like old photographs with edges softened by weather. Familiar and unforgiving. One of them — a woman with a scar along her jaw you remember from the storm shelters — meets your eyes and doesn't blink.

Resident 1: "You left town when the water came. You told us progress would come through labs and projects. Now you're back with papers."

Resident 2: (He folds his arms.) "We lost roofs, child-care centers. We lost sleep. Tell us why we should trust another plan."

You: (You inhale, the damp air filling your lungs) "You don't have to trust me. Trust the facts." (You pull the damp notebook out and hold it like a shield, then lower it. Numbers on paper feel insolent—clean solutions for messy grief.) "There are measures we can take that won't just hold the line; they'll buy time while we build wetlands behind the wall. Berms, community swales—worked where people were involved."

Resident 3: (A man with paint under his nails) "Berms are nice words when you can afford to build them. What about the rent hikes after the city 'stabilizes' the waterfront? What about the people pushed inland to make way for some glossy promenade?"

You: (You meet his gaze fully. Your voice narrows into the exactness you trust.) "We design with residents. We prioritize local housing stock when we propose engineering. I'm not talking about a top-down wall that feeds developers. I'm talking about layered defenses—smaller investments first, with co-op agreements so displacement is minimized. We can map the weakest foundations, prioritize retrofits."

Resident 1: "Words. We heard words and watched the water take our neighbor's house."

You feel the accusation like a physical slap. The memory surfaces abruptly — the thunder of wind, the high whistle of the water, the metal stench of overturned engines. You had been a teenager hiding behind a mattress; you had mapped currents the next week while your street was still a line of ruined trees. Guilt isn't a clean instrument you can use to cut policy with. It is a wet stone lodged in your throat.

You: (Your sentences become smaller, precise: you have learned this is how promises keep weight when people need them.) "I'll help set up a community survey this week. I can bring Professor Bhat in for a soil and hydraulics consult. We'll make a prioritized, itemized list — emergency retrofits, short-term berms, community gardens that double as absorption zones. No flashy promises. Just numbers, timelines, and names on the tasks."

Resident 2: (There is a long pause as he considers your tone — the exactness; the refusal to sentiment without structure.) "If you do that, and if you actually put names on tasks... maybe we'll let you."

Samir: (He watches, then steps forward) "Mara pulls the numbers tight. She doesn't hand out hope like confetti. She hands plans."

You want to say more — that you remember, that you are sorry in ways that rearrange your bones — but apology without action is a tide that recedes into noise. So you let precision stand in for confession.

[Visual: The promenade light shifts; clouds crawl in from the west and the air cools. Shadows lengthen over the stalls.]
[Sound: A low rumble, like distant machinery or a storm thinking about arriving]

Your notebook feels heavier in your hands. The sketches look vulnerable: dashed lines and arrows that could be read as salvation or as an invitation to bulldozers, depending on who has the budget. You think of rooftop gardens, of Samir's volunteer crews, of the woman with the scar who spoke for everyone. You feel the old itch—the insistence to fix, to carry away guilt in a new project that can be measured and checked off.

But the harbor light is both forgiving and unforgiving. It paints the cobbles gold and exposes the salt stains. It flatters nothing; it tells the truth of surfaces and leaves the depths in shadow.

You fold the notebook, sealing in the damp sketches. You feel the weight of choices — engineering versus restoration, municipal permits versus volunteer labor, short-term fixes versus long-term retreat — balancing like a scale you cannot steady with courage alone. The scale needs data, noise from meetings, the clatter of budget lines, the groan of a printer spitting out simulations.

You cannot do everything at once. The options lie like raw materials: each will alter what you can build, and whom you'll hurt or help in the process. You watch the residents fold back into their routines slowly, the market's daily life resuming as if storms are only events and not conditions.

You close your eyes for a moment, imagining a scale of decisions stretching out: retrofit timelines, community ownership agreements, the long political slog at City Hall. Guilt urges you toward grand gestures; training and patience push back with their quiet numbers. Both are true, and the co-existence of truths doesn't make you stronger—it makes you weary.

[Visual: A gull wheels overhead; the last gold of sunset slips away. The clouds over the western skyline deepen to slate.]
[Music: Piano motif falls to a single low unresolved chord]

You feel the magnetic tug of the compass under your shirt as if it insists on direction and not just orientation. Where you go next will be the first formal step of this return. It will call up meetings, municipal noise, and the small, stubborn labor of convincing people that your plans will not be another tide that erases them.

You lace your fingers over the notebook and let the market sounds slide around you, the laughter, the vendor calls, the clink of jars. Behind them, beneath them, there is the sound you will need to face: other people's calculations, other people's grief, and the city's indifferent math.

You draw a breath, and the harbor gives you a small, salt-sweet sting in the back of your throat. Hope without a plan is useless; action without consent is cruel. You have both a hunger to repair and the knowledge that any repair will scrape someone else. The scale tilts, and you are the fulcrum.

A line of lightning-gray cloud edges the horizon. Your thumb rubs the compass. You fold your notebook again, step toward the nearest lamppost to clear your thoughts, and—

You think of Professor Bhat's insistence on numbers, of Samir's steady hands, of the residents' faces that will not be mollified by plans without names. You think of what it will mean to bring facts into a room that mistrusts them, and of how facts alone will not soothe the scars the water left behind.

You tuck the compass back beneath your collar. You take one last look at the promenade — alive, flawed, waiting — and you set your shoulders to the work.
