[Scene: Tideward Rooftop Garden | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: Dappled, gray light through weatherproof awnings; a half-circle of sleeping bodies on tarpaulins; Nova seated where the planters meet the railing, silhouette sharp against the pale sea]
[Sound: Low murmur of a dozen people breathing; distant tide clinking against pilings; a megaphone zipped to Nova's satchel, silent but present]
[Music: Sparse piano, slow, minor]

You climb the narrow service stair with your coat pulled tight against the drizzle. The city smells of wet soil and old tar—greenhouse breath and the sour tang of last night’s fuel. Your notebook is a weight at your hip, the spine taped and soft where your fingers find the place you kept sketching clauses and neighbor names. It feels ridiculous and honest at once.

Nova looks up before you reach the ledge. Her face is a map of the last few weeks: jaw set, eyes rimmed with the faint red of spent outrage. When she stands, the rooftop seems to hold its breath; everyone watching you two like tide-watchers before a swell.

Nova Duarte: "You came. Good. We were starting to think the city had you in its pocket already."

You: "I came because I owe them answers. Not excuses."

Nova’s smile isn't a smile. It's a split of expectation and accusation.

Nova Duarte: (soft) "Did you put teeth in it? Or did you hand them a pretty plan and let them file it under 'safe'?"

You: (hands finding the compass at your throat) "I negotiated clauses. I—" Your voice catches, smaller than you intend. "I inserted oversight language, Nova. But the contractor wants a clean sign-off by morning. The mayor's people are calling for assurances of 'stability.'"

Nova's eyes narrow. The rooftop air has the sweet, metallic bite of a storm that hasn't decided whether to come.

Nova Duarte: "Inserted how? As a wish, or as law?"

You: "Professor Anika read the models. She said the design will hold—if oversight is legally binding. Without it, it's a monument." You hear the word in your own mouth and it tastes like rust.

Nova Duarte: "Then make it law. Make them sign with community eyes on the budget, on the materials, on who gets relocated."

The crowd shifts—someone laughs softly, someone sniffles. Their trust is visible: petitions folded like talismans, ketchup-stained census forms, hand-knitted lifejackets. You think of the block you lost and how real and small those losses feel when you touch them.

Nova waits through both your speech and your silence with the same unreadable line of her mouth. When you try to answer something practical, she snaps back—less at you and more at the shape of the compromise you've already helped birth.

Nova Duarte: "People will die if this is window dressing. Promises that look good in a press packet do not hold water."

You: "I know that. I know what it costs." You stare past her to the horizon, where the seawall's silhouette blurs into rain. "I don't want that to be me."

Nova Duarte: (bitter laugh) "Then stop being afraid of being wrong. Stop placating."

You: "What do you want me to do? Walk into City Hall and light the contract on fire?"

Nova Duarte: "Sometimes yes. Sometimes you have to burn the paper to show the city it's not just ink."

Silence settles like a pressure. You pick at the tape of your notebook as if you can peel the whole problem off the page.

[Scene Transition: You descend from the rooftop, rain now earnest, leading you back toward the market.]

[Scene: Solace Harbor Promenade Market | Morning]
[Visual: Wet cobbles reflecting strings of lanterns; vendors huddle under tarps; Samir at his stall, hands busy netting small bundles]
[Sound: The sizzle of frying oil, the squawk of a gull struggling with a wrapper, Samir's mutter to a customer]
[Music: Low, droning cello]

Samir’s face folds as you approach—no theatrics, just the raw, brittle look of someone who hasn't decided whether to be angry at you or at the weather.

Samir Reyes: "Did we trade our past for a promise?" He doesn't wait for the answer. The question lands like a hook.

You: "I tried to make the promise safer. I pressed for oversight." Your words are quick, pragmatic. It's hard not to be practical in the market where people barter for tomorrow's bread.

Samir Reyes: "You pressed where? On paper? On the margin? Mara—" He stops himself, jaw working. He always does that—tries to call you by the name that isn't a title. "You were our voice in that room. Now the city's contractors brag about 'efficiency' and no one tells us what 'efficiency' means for the low blocks."

You: "They promised housing clauses—relocation plans, priority jobs during construction. I put names in. I made them a condition to release funds."

Samir Reyes: (bitter) "You think names are enough? People need roofs, Mara. They need to sleep without water seeping into their ribs. Contracts don't fix fear."

You: (soft) "I know. I know that better than anyone." The confession almost feels obscene; your chest tightens. "But if I don't get the wall built fast, the nearest storm will make all those plans moot."

Samir glares at you like you could be reasoned into a different climate. The market smells hotter, fish frying like an alarm.

Samir Reyes: "So what's the plan? You and the city put a bandage on a bleeding artery and call it a win? Or you actually fight for the people who won't be able to fight for themselves after the water comes?"

You: "I can't do everything at once. I tried to wedge oversight in. Professor Anika says the models need legal oversight to ensure maintenance and community audits. Without that, I'm signing a blank check."

Samir Reyes: "Then don't give them the pen."

You feel the words like a small stone dropped into a deep well. They echo back—Mara, don't give them the pen.

Samir doesn't shout. He never did. His anger is a slow burn, the kind that burns bridges by neglect, not by spectacle. You watch him walk a few paces, then look back like a man who left his door unlocked.

[Scene Transition: Your phone buzzes—Professor Anika calling. The ringtone is a short, practiced chime you equate to lab lights flickering on.]

[Scene: You step into a narrow alley beside the Maris Institute — the sky a low sheet of slate]
[Visual: Condensation beading on your notebook; the Maris Institute windows fogged, monitors glowing blue inside]
[Sound: Professor Anika's voice in your ear; the faint thump of a distant generator]
[Music: Tension builds with a staccato violin]

Professor Anika: "Mara. I won't sugarcoat it. The structural models predict stress concentrations at the eastern pier if maintenance isn't consistent." Her tone is brisk, clinical, but there's heat under the syllables. "Legal oversight won't just appease activists; it's an engineering safety net. You bind oversight into the contract, you create a mechanism for adaptive response."

You: "If I demand that now, the contractor threatens to pull funding. The mayor—" You taste the edge of panic. "She wants the optics fixed before the council meeting."

Professor Anika: "Then bind it with a schedule. Make oversight actionable: quarterly audits, community representatives with enforcement power. Don't let them bury the requirement in broad language. Make it specific. Make it a stop-work clause."

You: "Stop-work could delay completion. A delay now could expose low blocks during the next storm."

Professor Anika: (sharp) "And so would a half-built barrier that fails because no one watches its upkeep. Mara, you're not choosing between the wall and the community. You're choosing how the city remembers the people who build it."

The line goes quiet for a breath. Her words are a scalpel.

Professor Anika: "You have leverage. Use it. If you don't push, those clauses will be performative—nice language that allows the city to smile in photos and then forget."

You: "I need political cover."

Professor Anika: "Then create it. Bring in neighborhood councils as co-signers. Offer phased funding contingent on verified audits. It's messy—but it preserves lives and holds the municipality to a standard."

You close your eyes and picture the contracts spread across your table at home, clauses underlined in red. Legal language tastes like metal.

[Scene Transition: Afternoon — Sea Barrier Construction Site]
[Scene: Sea Barrier Construction Site | Dusk]
[Visual: Rain-bleared headlamps; cranes like tired beasts; a half-built wall dark and slick; men in high-vis moving like ants]
[Sound: Heavy boots on mud, the whine of a generator, the urgent chirp of radio static]
[Music: Low brass, ominous]

You stand at the edge of the site with an aide from Mayor Chen—a woman whose smile is a blade polished thin. The contractor's foreman—a broad-shouldered man with grease under his nails—has his ledger open. Rain pings your shoulders, cold and precise.

Mayor Chen: (over comms, clipped) "We need a signature by tomorrow morning. The council wants to announce progress. The city's image is stabilizing credit lines. Delays aren't an option."

You: "Stability for who?" You don't mean to make it snide, but the question tastes like the truth. "If oversight isn't enforceable, this structure is a brittle showpiece."

Mayor Chen: "You negotiated the insertion of oversight language. You will not sabotage the project's timeline." The tone is not a threat; it's a presumption.

Contractor Foreman: "We can accelerate with a clean sign-off. Any on-site delays spike costs. We don't have wiggle room after the suppliers' schedules."

You: "Professor Anika recommends a stop-work clause tied to quarterly audits by community reps. If maintenance falls, the contract provides a legal remedy."

Mayor Chen: "That's legal theater. Tell me how a clause prevents a storm. We need bricks in the water, not theater for the press."

You: "It prevents political amnesia. It keeps money and oversight where they're supposed to be. It demands accountability."

The foreman rubs his forehead. He is a practical man; he knows pumps fail and silt shifts. He also knows that delays mean invoices unpaid and unions restless.

Contractor Foreman: "If you drag this out, you might have to tear up current procurement. Suppliers withdraw, timelines slip. We build half and get blamed when weather takes it down."

You: "If we sign without oversight, and it fails, people die. How do you sleep with that on your conscience?"

Mayor Chen: "By doing what's possible and avoiding chaos. Sometimes you compromise and accept calculated risk."

You: "Calculated by who?" The words come out hard. "By people who will never sleep in the low blocks?"

A silence like a tide holds between you. Rain drums a steady, accusatory beat. You can feel the city's expectation pressing against the hollow in your chest you keep for the people you lost.

Samir's question ghosts back: Did we trade our past for a promise?

Nova's look on the rooftop returns—steel-gray, disappointed, but also asking you to be more than a negotiator.

Professor Anika's voice in your head hums with lab-light exactness: Make oversight actionable.

Mayor Chen's assistant buzzes in your ear with the schedule: morning, signature, press.

Everything narrows to a single edge: sign and protect the city now but risk betrayal, or bind oversight and risk delay—and perhaps, very possibly, worse immediate harm.

You can feel your fingers curl around the pen the contractor slides toward you, its metal cold and authoritative. Your skin prickles with the memory of salt on the night the water took the block—the gust, the light, the terrible, unstoppable movement.

You consider the options, each a different distribution of risk and guilt, each a promise that will comfort someone and wound another.
