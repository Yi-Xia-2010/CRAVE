[Scene: Sea Barrier Construction Site | Evening — Rain]
[Visual: Overcast, cranes silhouette against iron sky; orange safety vests glow in the downpour]
[Sound: Rain hard on plywood; diesel engines idling; a distant police radio]
[Music: Dissonant horns; tense percussive rain]

The road to the site smells of diesel and wet wood; puddles catch the flashing safety lights and split them into vibrating shards. Steel towers loom, skeletal as the promises they represent. Contractors move like animals bred to a different weather; their radios jab out clipped orders.

A municipal van parks with deliberate, slow swagger. Someone in a pressed trench coat steps out—an official, a municipal lawyer, a notation of law made flesh. A laminated permit clings to their clipboard like a talisman.

City Official (Lawyer): "This access is restricted. You're interfering with a city-sanctioned construction site. If you do not disperse, we will arrest for trespass and obstructing essential services."

Nova laughs, not soft.

Nova Duarte: "Essential services? Tell that to the families on the ridge who can't afford your 'essential' anymore."

The lawyer's smile is thin; officialdom breathes a cold reassurance. Behind him, a contractor barks into a radio and two security contractors in hard hats crane their necks toward the waterline, faces wooden, hands on batons. There's a lawyer's letter already printed in the lawyer's pocket; a permit hold-line stamped with the mayor's insignia.

You step forward. The rain beads along your hairline, runs salts down your cheeks. Your notebook is wrapped in plastic; your compass hangs heavy. Samir has linked arms with two neighbors and nods to you — a small human semaphore.

You: "We're not here to damage property. We're here to demand humane alternatives and transparency about displacement. We're here because people were left behind while you decided which neighborhoods were 'worth' a seawall."

City Official (Lawyer): "Your grievances are noted. But the city will not allow an unlawful blockade. Move back, or we will remove you."

Nova leans into the megaphone. The sound cuts through the rain like a blade, half musical, half accusation.

Nova Duarte: "We will not be erased. We will not let you privatize survival. If you spend money to protect the marina and let Lutkin Alley drown, you'll answer to the people. Today you're choosing which lives matter."

A handful of cameras edge forward; a live feed blooms on a dozen phones. The contractor's foreman steps out, sleeves damp, face set to professional patience.

Foreman: "We have schedules. Delays cost people wages, subcontracts. We have authorization. This—" (gestures to the crowd) "—is illegal."

Samir: "This 'authorization' didn't ask Mrs. Ebbett if she wanted to be cooped in a high-rise for a 30-year bond payment."

Foreman: "I'm not here to arbitrate policy."

Nova: (smiles like a flare) "Then make way. You're part of the problem."

There is a hush, the kind that comes before water finds a crack. Your heart is a drumbeat in your chest. Adrenaline sharpens your fingers; the rain stings like small accusations on your uncovered skin.

A municipal notice is unfolded and read aloud—Mayor Chen's office declares the construction essential infrastructure, issues a restraining notice, threatens permit revocation for any organizers who 'interfere with emergency mitigation efforts.' Nova spits the name like it's a verdict and a dare.

Nova Duarte: "Protect public works? Protect whose public? The mayor is protecting profit lines."

The crowd answers with a raw, rising sound. Your stomach tightens. You know the municipal machine: it grinds slowly, but it grinds with teeth. Legal notices will arrive like falling shells; permits will be revoked; contractors will bring in legal muscle. The city that can build a wall can also build narratives.

Your stubborn optimism fractures under the weight of imminent showdowns. The planner in you catalogs the risks; the person who lost a street to a storm remembers exactly how fast negotiation can become obfuscation. Your strategic fury—new, sharp—rises like a tide.

Your throat constricts. The rain finds tiny hot tracks down your face. You have crossed into something raw and moral; you have stepped where the city's nice diagrams turn into human cost. The air tastes of salt, diesel, and an expected reckoning. You feel small and incandescent at once.

You take one last look at the ridge lines on the tablet, the names written in a cramped, hurried script. The rain slices the world into splinters of light and shadow. Your stubborn optimism tightens—it is no longer mere hope. It has teeth.
