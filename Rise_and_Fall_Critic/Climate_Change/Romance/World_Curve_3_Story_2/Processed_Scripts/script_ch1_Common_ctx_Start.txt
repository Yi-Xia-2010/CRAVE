# Route: Route C — Alternate Ending
# Context: Start

[Scene: Solace Harbor Promenade | Low Tide, Golden Hour]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Music: Low, minor-key guitar with distant gulls]
[Sound: Soft slap of water against stone, distant vendor calls, a child laughing once then gone]

You taste salt on your tongue the moment you step off the last wooden board and onto the cobbles. The air is cool and sharp; each breath pushes old memories into the hollow behind your ribs. The promenade looks the way you remembered—strings of lanterns, frayed banners catching the wind, a smear of market colors—but it arrives at you like a photograph left in sun: familiar, faded, brittle at the edges.

You pull your windbreaker tighter. The brass compass at your throat is cold where it rests against skin; you roll it between thumb and forefinger the way you do when a thought wants to be steadied. Your notebook is in the inside pocket, damp around the seams. When you reach the zipper and slide your hand inside you can feel paper that puckers and a handful of graphite smudges where your sketches bled in the rain—cross-sections of berms, truncated seawall details, a sketch of a pocket garden swale annotated in quick, cramped handwriting.

Samir is a bright slash of color near the second stall—his dreadlocks pulled back, sun-kissed forearms brandishing a jar as though it were treasure. He sees you and raises one hand like an island beacon.

Samir: "Mara! Thought you were going to be late. You look like you wrestled a cloud."

You let out a short, humorless laugh. The wind takes it and frays it into something smaller.

Mara Lin: "Only the usual—trains, rain, optimism. What's that?"

Samir sets the jar into your palm. The label is handwritten, the lid dented and rimmed with a crust of pickling spices. Pickled sea beans, he says without saying it. The smell is vinegary and marine, a tiny, perfect memory of a summer you spent collecting samphire after an evacuation drill.

Samir: "Keep your energy up. You look like you could use salt in another form."

You hold the jar and feel ridiculous for needing a comfort so simple. His face is easy and open. In it, for a second, the part of you that wants to belong loosens its fist.

Mara Lin: "Thanks. Tell Aya I still have those oyster shells for the rooftop plan."

Samir [grins]: "She'll put you to work for them. Don't let her talk you into free compost duty."

You both laugh, and the sound tacks you for a breath. Then Samir grows quieter, the joke folding into something heavier.

Samir: "Neighbors have been asking for you. Old Mrs. Ebbett—she's been talking about the berm idea all week. Figured you'd want to hear it straight."

Mara Lin: "I'll listen. Tell her—I'll come by this afternoon. I'll bring survey pins."

Samir studies you for a beat too long, the look full of things that don't dissolve into jokes.

Samir: "You sure you're up for it? Last time you left the city—"

You feel the sentence as if someone has shoved a scale into your palms. The scale lists events you cannot balance: the storm, the houses gone, the faces that learned how to count loss. Your optimism has a cost. You know that. You learned it the way you learned to read tide tables—by being burned by accuracy.

Mara Lin: "I'm here now." Your voice is small but controlled. "I have plans."

Samir nods, not satisfied but not angry. He presses the jar back into your hands, then gestures you onward toward the market.

The stalls are a motion of hands and fabric. Lantern light pockets into puddles. Somewhere a kettle hisses. You run your fingers along the edge of a frayed banner advertising a neighborhood repair coop; the fabric is sand-raw beneath your fingertips.

A group has gathered by the fishmonger—three men and two women you recognize by outline and unspoken history. Their faces fold like maps you've read before: familiar geography with sharp creases. They watch you the way the tide watches the shore—inevitable and not always kind.

Marta: "Mara Lin. Back to lecture us about sandbags?"

There is a laugh under the question, brittle and wary. You can smell diesel and lemon detergent, the combination of long work and home care. One of them—young, sunburned—tucks his chin like he is holding a secret steady.

You search for the right set of words. You feel your notebook thud against your ribs like a second heart. The faces around you are not waiting for poetry. They want details, not promises.

Mara Lin: "No lectures. I want practical work." Your sentences are measured, each one stripped of flourish. "First: I'll run an on-site survey for the block by Tuesday. Second: we'll map emergency exits with signposting—clear, laminated, volunteer-reactive. Third: a pilot berm in the alley east of 3rd Street to test swale runoff."

The group shifts. Some features—eyebrows, the set of a mouth—register doubt; others accept because any specificity is preferable to airy consolation.

Tomas: "Who funds this? Who's doing the digging? You coming with government planners or your own crew?"

The question lands like a small stone. It implies betrayal, it implies abandonment, all the old friction between municipal promises and neighborhood labor. You let the question sit; the air tightens with it.

Mara Lin: "Community volunteers for the pilot. I'm arranging materials through donors I know." You say it plainly. "I'll coordinate with Samir—he knows the logistics here better than anyone."

A woman with paint under her nails—Mrs. Ebbett—steps forward. She smells of salt and lavender. Her voice is quieter than the rest but carries the weight of survival.

Mrs. Ebbett: "It took the storm to make you come home, Mara. People don't forget when the tide takes their doors."

Her eyes meet yours and there's a long silence that makes the cobbles seem louder. You feel every seam of your own childhood tear open: the mattress dragged into a van, the lantern that wouldn't light, your mother's hands swollen with cold. You had promised to fix it then—promised the world—and left because you thought systems could be rebuilt from lab benches and grant proposals.

Mara Lin: "I know. I'm not asking you to forgive me." The confession feels like a private thing that suddenly belongs to the whole group. "I'm asking you to let me try to make what we can."

Mrs. Ebbett [after a long beat]: "You say that, Mara. But you better show us blueprints not dreams. We won't trade our yards for a wall that fails us when the lights go out."

You nod. There is relief that she asked for drawings rather than vows—tangible measures are your language. You reach into your jacket, fingers brushing the damp paper in the pocket. The smudged graphite on the schematics looks allergic to light.

Mara Lin: "I'll bring detailed cross-sections, materials list, and a schedule. No walls that isolate. Berms and vegetative swales that direct water away from foundations and into community planter basins. I'll be precise."

The promise sits hard in the air, like a line of salt scraped across a wound. For a moment the market returns to its regular noises—calling vendors, a child stomping in a puddle—yet under it is the thrum of something larger: committees, budgets, politicians who measure risks in precincts and polls.

You look past the market, to where the lights thin and the promenade becomes a jagged silhouette against a steel-gray horizon. Storm clouds are gathering at the edge of the world like a ledger closing in ink. Your stubborn optimism tastes exactly like salt—stinging and necessary—but the scale of the choices ahead presses against your throat. You can feel the size of what must be negotiated—a city, a budget, grief that hasn't been accounted for—stacking up in impossible piles.

Samir nudges you gently.

Samir: "You heading to the roof garden later, or do you want to go square up supplies first?"

Mara Lin: "Roof. Later." You hear the lie in your voice and you also hear the truth: you need the brightness of green before you go talk to machines and models.

You tuck the jar under your arm, compass warm against the skin beneath your collarbone, and take another look at the faces around you. They are not a single thing. They are history and demand and possibility braided together.

Mara Lin: "I'll be back this afternoon," you say. "I won't bring speeches."

They watch you leave the market; the cobbles swallow your footsteps. The wind tugs at your windbreaker again, as if reminding you there is still a storm on its way, whether you fix plans or not.

The harbor light flattens—tender and merciless—and for a moment you stand between what you owe and what you can actually deliver. Your obligation feels heavier than your body.

You stand with the market noise pressing in: the sharp smell of fried fish, the metallic tang of sea-spray, a vendor calling a price that sounds like a memory. Your notebook is damp, your hands are salt-sticky, and the compass at your throat is a small, steady decision-maker. You have made practical promises in small, precise sentences because sentiment without a plan is a tide that will swallow people.

You walk away from the stalls with your shoulders set. The scale of the work tempts panic; you fold it into a plan, at least for now. Still, the harbor keeps its honest light—both forgiving in its golden warmth and unforgiving in the way it reveals ragged edges.

You realize, with a quiet that is almost a physical sensation, that the choices ahead are larger than you expected. Bigger than the city block. Bigger than your compass.
