[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Dawn]
[Visual: Exterior shot of the reclaimed shipyard warehouse at first light; polycarbonate panes beaded with condensation]
[Music: Low, hollow piano with distant strings — slow, minor key]
[Sound: Soft hum of fans, distant gulls, the whisper of the sea against bulkheads]

You push the warped door open and warm, humid air meets you like a hand on the back of your neck. The greenhouse smells of wet soil, sea-salt, and the thin, bitter tang of cheap coffee — the kind saved from last week’s shift and heated again because there’s no time for nicer rituals. Your breath fogs in front of you and hangs, then blends with the white sheen on the panels.

[Visual: Close-up — droplets run down polycarbonate, blurring the harbor beyond]
You pad between tables of seedlings under strings of LED grow lights. Trays tilt at odd angles where metal racks are patched with a weld and a prayer. Some stems stand mute and pale, leaves cupped but resilient. Others are brown skeletons — a few too many nights above eighty degrees after the surge warmed the town’s cold snap into rot.

Your hands move on muscle memory: checking stems, loosening compacted soil, tucking a fragile seedling under a makeshift cloche. Each touch is small and urgent — there’s comfort in the work, but it comes damp and exhausted.

[Visual: Close-up of your braided leather bracelet]
You bring your fingers to the braided bracelet on your wrist. The rope-turned-leather carries salt and a memory: the pier festival — lanterns, laughter, a pier that heaved like a heart and then folded into the tide. The metal ring tucked under the braid is cool against your skin; you feel the ache there like a tide line beneath your ribs.

You check the battered tablet in your apron pocket. The projection graphs blink up with slow, clinical authority. Sea-level curves. Flood-frequency bands. The numbers line up in ways you’ve seen before and still feel like betrayals.

You swallow.

Tom’s voice comes from the lane outside before you see him — the familiar thunk of a wrench, boots on cobble. He shouldered the greenhouse door open, grease on his knuckles and salt in the creases of his laugh. He looks at the tables, at the trays, and his face settles into the practical worry of someone who’s spent his life fixing what the tide breaks.

Tom: "Mornin', Iris. You alright? Looks rough in here."

You: "Mornin'. Some of it made it. Some of it didn't." (You point to a tray where pale basil clings to life.) "Heat spikes after the surge did a number on the shallow trays."

Tom crouches, eyes scanning the stems, fingers hovering but not touching. He’s always careful with the things you plan for.

Tom: "We could rig the shade panels tighter. Etta’s been talking about extra compost to cushion the roots. But Ravi called last night — said funding’s getting tighter. Wants 'prioritization,' he says."

You: "Prioritization." The word tastes like a ledger. (You fold your hands around the tablet.) "Prioritization when everything feels critical."

Tom exhales. His shoulders are wide enough for more than his share.

Tom: "Ravi’s under pressure. You know how that goes. Fingers in a thousand pies, and the bigger pies win."

[Visual: Etta appears in the doorway carrying a box of seed packets; the light frames her small stooped shape]
Etta’s smile is small and weathered, like a garden fork that’s been polished by use. She sets the box down and the sheltering warmth of the greenhouse gathers around her.

Etta: "You look like you carried the sea in your hair, child." (She brushes a stray tuft back with dirt-smudged fingers.) "We’ll wrangle it. We've done it before."

You: "We did lose the pier, Etta." The memory presses against your throat first, then your mouth. "I keep thinking if—" Your voice tightens. "If the data had been heard earlier."

Etta's eyes go distant, the garden elder pausing where old grief and new resolve meet.

Etta: "Old losses are heavy, love. But the soil remembers too. Planting is an act of defiance. Remember that." (She moves to a shelf and produces a small packet of seeds, tapping it against your palm.) "These came from Mrs. Kelley's last year. She wanted them in the commons."

Her hands·—·small, worn, rooted—·close around yours for a heartbeat. It is a steadiness that feels like a promise and a plea.

The choice evaporates as you do both, because plans are gestures and reality takes what time it needs. You record soil moisture, log the trays that need replanting, and mark the compromised batches with sticky notes — little flags of triage.

Etta watches you work, head tilted.

Etta: "You’re wearing that look. That concentrated look. The one that means you’ll speak tonight. Are you ready for a room full of teeth, Iris?"

You: "I have numbers. I have samples." (You lift a vial of soil, the grain clinging to the glass.) "I have stories to tell. But numbers don't convince everyone, and stories don't hold up budgets."

Etta: "Ah." She laughs, low and rueful. "Politics isn't soil, child. It dries out if you stare at it too long."

Tom: "Marin's coming to town, I heard. Supposed to meet with council. Big talk about emergency allocations."

You feel that: a new current under the usual tides. Marin. Their name tugs at the memory of the pier — thin, complicated threads woven through childhood summers, later blurred into municipal maps and meeting minutes.

You: "Marin's meeting with council?" Your voice is smaller than you intend. "Do we know why?"

Tom: "Officially? Emergency funding. Unofficially? People say Marin’s trying to broker regional resources. Heard Ravi’s cut lines back — now Marin's got to try and stretch whatever's left."

Etta folds her hands around the seed packet again, thoughtful.

Etta: "Marin’s faces is inscrutable as an old map. They care what stays, but they also think of legacies—large things. Sometimes that helps. Sometimes it—" She taps her temple. "Minds make plans."

Your chest tightens. Marin's pragmatism has a way of smoothing over small human textures for the sake of greater systems. You trust them professionally, but trust in private kinships is always more rutted, filled with potholes and saltwater.

You tuck another sample into the tablet’s case, fingers still smelling of loam.

Tom: "And there’s talk in the market. A developer's been sniffing at the promenade — fancy renderings, promises of 'consolidated access points' and 'private maintenance.' People are nervous. Means someone could buy the promenade and tuck it behind fees."

Your stomach curls. The promenade is more than a strip of pavement; it's where kids learn to skip stones, where vendors set up battered tables, where the festival used to spill into the night. Selling it off would be like selling the town’s spine.

You: "If they privatize the promenade, long-term resilience gets bought as a luxury, not a right." (You think of Etta's packet, of Tom’s hands on a hull, of the pier.) "We lose place as much as we lose land."

Tom: "Then the fight’s as much about memory as it is engineering."

Etta: "And memory has teeth. You just have to show where it bites." (She smiles, hardly a victory but not a surrender.)

You take a sip of the coffee left on a near table — too hot, thin, thrift-store bitterness. The brine at the back of your tongue sharpens the memory of the pier again. The presentation for tonight sits in your head like a stone you’ve shaped and reshapd. It needs edges that can cut through fatigue and grief.

You breathe, steadying. The choice is not just rhetorical; it's tactical. You can make the room see numbers or feel a loss. Maybe you can do both. You will have to; tonight will not be a single tactic.

[Visual: Montage — your hands preparing a slide, sticky notes on the wall, Etta sorting seed packets, Tom tightening a rack brace]
You prepare the materials, each movement small and decisive: photographs of the promenade with hand-scrawled annotations, soil-sample vials lined up, a concise timeline of projected inundation. You practice the opening line until it doesn't catch in your throat.

Tom lingers at the door.

Tom: "Whatever you do, don't let them make it sound like there's no choice. People give up when it feels inevitable. Show them what we can keep, not just what we can't."

You: "I know. That's the hardest part. Showing both."

Tom: "And hey — if Marin's there? They can be stubborn, but they also know how to pull strings. Try not to fight them in public. Save that for after."

You: "I'll keep that in mind." (You mean it, though your fingers curl around the tablet with an old, reflexive defensiveness.)

Etta: "And remember to breathe, dear. People hear conviction when you're breathing. They can tell when you carry the sea instead of letting it sit at your feet."

Outside, the harbor breathes a colder breath. The polycarbonate distorts the horizon; the pier is a silhouette of missing planks and a memory sharpened by wind. You look at the photograph of last year’s festival pinned to the greenhouse wall — paper curled at the edges, lanterns caught mid-swing. The image is a small, stubborn light.

[Sound: A distant municipal siren, then the regular drip of condensation]
Ravi's message had been terse: tightening lines, prioritize. Marin's visit is the variable you hadn't planned for but must now. The rumor about the promenade sits like a bruise.

You gather your things. Your jacket still smells of salt and mildew and the faint, persistent scent of work. Your hair is wind-tousled; the sun-bleached streaks at your temples feel like a ledger of seasons. The forum tonight will be full of faces that have been hardened by storms and losses. You have data; you have soil; you have a story threaded through your veins.

You touch the braided bracelet once more, feeling the cool ring beneath, and let yourself be steady for a moment.

[Visual: Wide shot — you step out of the greenhouse into a gray morning where the promenade vanishes into mist]
[Music: Strings swell slightly — low, unresolved]
The sea is already whispering. It feels less like a distant element and more like an impatient neighbor, waiting for decisions to be made.

You take the tablet under your arm, the seed packet in your pocket, and whatever courage you can scrounge. Tonight, the community forum will begin the work of choosing which things to save.

[Visual: Close-up on your face — resolve etched, but worry present]
[Music: A single piano note hangs and fades]

You walk toward Municipal Hall, toward the layered, bureaucratic heart where Ravi tightens lines and Marin will argue for allocations. Each step feels like walking toward a tide line you cannot ignore.
