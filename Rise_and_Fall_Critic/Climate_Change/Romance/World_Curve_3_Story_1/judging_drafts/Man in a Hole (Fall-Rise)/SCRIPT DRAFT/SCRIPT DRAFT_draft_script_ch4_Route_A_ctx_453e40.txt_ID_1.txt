[Scene: Tom's Shop | Morning — Overcast, wind off the harbor]
[Visual: Interior cluttered with toolboxes, hull planks leaned against racks; the shop's single skylight streaked with salt]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a steady, percussive rhythm as someone hammers in the street outside]
[Music: Low cello, a slow, insistent tempo]

You push the warped door open and the warm, oily air swallows you—tangible as a hand on your shoulder. Tom is at his bench, elbows deep in a hydraulic fitting, his face lit by a single hanging lamp that smells like old coffee and machine oil. Your apron already feels like a second skin; the braided bracelet knocks once against the tablet in your pocket when you move.

Kai is there before you expect him, grinning in a way that looks almost insolent against the weather, like sunlight pretending it can outmatch a storm. He tugs open his windbreaker and with a small flourish unfolds something wrapped in oilcloth: a prototype module, all rivets and clever joints, edges sheened with salt.

Kai: "Look at this — snaps together, buoyant core, can be towed into place. I ran the buoyancy tests last night. Lightweight, cheap panels, and—" (he pauses, the grin softening) "—it won't need a crane."

You feel the familiar tug: a childhood memory of him on the pier, daring you to climb higher. There's also the planner in you that wants proof and permutations and a hundred failure modes listed on a single, honest page.

You: "It looks good on paper. How does it handle a shearing seam? The last thing we need is a joint that flexes under surf and tears itself open."

Kai flinches at the word "last," because memory threads through both of you. He leans the prototype on the bench, tracing a finger along a seam.

Kai: "That's the hinge. I reinforced it here — composite flange, doubled fasteners. It flexes a bit, sure, but it should distribute load. We can bolt a temporary strapping system until we refine the molds."

Tom wipes his hands on a rag and watches you both, steady as weathered timber.

Tom: "You two can tinker in the shop all you like, but we need numbers the town understands. How many volunteers for the first run? How many pallets of salvage? If this works, we're hauling waterlogged boards and old tires for months."

You look at the prototype and then at the ledger pinned behind Tom's bench—names, a tally, and a line where Etta signed with a shaky, emphatic hand.

You (thinking): You chose this because the town moves when the town moves together. You chose this because waiting for perfect paperwork is another word for leaving things to break. Your throat tightens. The habit in you—taking responsibility, filling gaps—sets itself like a brace in your ribs.

Etta appears at the doorway then—small, stooped, hands full of seed packets and a small tin of nails.

Etta: "Seeds won't stop the sea, Iris, but they give folk something to tend while they work. And they'll hold soil. They'll remember what we're saving."

You reach for the volunteer roster. Your fingers are still galvanized with that impulse to fix, to organize, to list and name every person who can help.

[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Afternoon — Humid, the greenhouse smells of wet earth]
[Visual: Warm, saturated greens under strings of amber grow-lights; windows rimmed with salty condensation]
[Sound: Murmur of volunteers, the rustle of plant leaves, footsteps on packed soil]
[Music: Quiet, strained violin]

The greenhouse is a shelter in more ways than one. You sleep there sometimes when the wind gnaws harder than your apartment's shutters can bear; tonight it's full of people: Etta humming a cadence as she arranges seed packets, Lina leaning against a planter with arms crossed and a face like a cloud before sun, and volunteers shifting pallets into neat stacks.

Lina: "Dock space isn't charity. You'll use my berth because it's a path to the forklifts. You break something there, you answer to me."

Her tone says tradeoffs and ledgers, not malice—practicality braided with threat. You know Lina's harbor like a book with creased edges; she has always viewed the town's survival alongside her own livelihood.

You: "We won't be reckless. You'll have a seat in planning and a voice at the table."

Lina snorts, but there's a small give in her posture. She crosses her arms tighter and tosses a folded map onto the table.

Lina: "Good. Then make it fast. If you don't, the developers will be lining up with permits and promises."

You (thinking): You feel the weight of asking favors. Each concession carries the echo of something sold. But the volunteers arrive—hands that know plaster and wire, teenagers with blunt enthusiasm and retired electricians with patient smiles. For a while, the air is electric: laughter between tasks, the clank of tools, the smell of wet timber and rising bread from the bakery around the corner.

You draft a patchwork liability waiver because old habits mean you tidy risks into forms before you sleep. The words are ugly and legal, but people sign because they trust you, because Etta stands beside you and because Tom hands them a hot thermos afterward.

Kai watches you fill the waiver with a kind of puzzled distrust.

Kai: "You know we shouldn't make this a paperwork trap. People come because they believe. If you start putting fences between hands and tools, some won't show."

You: "And if one of them gets hurt and we haven't documented safety? Then what, Kai? We crash everything because we were too romantic about 'belief'?"

Kai: "It's not romance—it's speed. You think I don't see what's at stake? I'm tired of meetings that talk to contractors. We can iterate faster than any council can drag a file through committee."

Your exchange lengthens into a back-and-forth where neither of you yields easily, where laughter from the room dims and the air thickens. The old ease between you tastes sharp now—familiarity against pressure—and there's friction where there used to be consonance.

[Scene: Pier — Early Build Days | Late afternoon, low tide]
[Visual: Modular sections lined on the shoreline; the sea is a pewter slab, horizon hard as steel]
[Sound: Hammers, the slap of rope, the distant groan of a breakwater]
[Music: Dissonant strings, a low undercurrent]

The first assemblies are beautiful in their battered honesty: panels clamped by hands streaked with salt and grease, bolts tightened until knuckles ached. Kids dart between legs, carrying rope like ceremonial streamers. Etta sings old work-songs under her breath and Tom jokes about charging admission.

You move from station to station, correcting a misaligned flange here, teaching a better knot there. You sleep less—nights in the greenhouse under fogged panes become your bracing—but the work sings a sort of salvation. For a brief, luminous stretch, the town looks like it might not only survive but remake itself by its own hands.

Then the inspector shows up.

[Visual: A municipal van parked at the end of the pier; an official clipboard in hand]
[Sound: Footsteps slowing; the quiet that marks when a room stops breathing]
[Music: A single sustained note, then silence]

The inspector—thin, uniform neat, badge catching the light—reads through your makeshift permits with a face like folded paper. They speak in polite syllables that weigh like anchors.

Inspector (Marin): "These structures aren't certified for public defense. Liability issues aside, without municipal approval you can't prevent developers from declaring this an unauthorized structure. The town could be fined. Materials might need to be logged. For everyone's safety—"

You watch the room tighten. Volunteers glance at one another. Kai's jaw hardens in a way you know: it's the look of someone who hears a bureaucracy threaten the spontaneity that keeps motion alive.

Kai: "We didn't ask for permission because permission moves like a glacier. People need walls tonight."

Inspector (Marin): "And I understand urgency. But the law is less understanding of good intentions. You need provisional certification or you risk invalidating any future municipal claim to responsibility. I recommend you pause—"

You feel the old habit—your own default—to carry the burden. You already have a stack of contingency emails drafted: liability waivers, a salvage inventory, a plea to Ravi to fast-track a provisional. You have also begun sleeping with the greenhouse door unlocked because someone might need you at three in the morning.

You: "If we pause now, we lose momentum. If we keep going without the paperwork, we risk legal exposure. No choice leaves us clean."

Marin's eyes flick to you—calculated, complex. They fold politeness around something like sympathy and something like duty.

Marin: "I can push for provisional review, but I will need documented structural analyses and a chain of custody for materials. I need you to present a plan."

Kai cuts in, voice shorter than it should be.

Kai: "You heard her. How long? Days? Weeks? We can't hold sand back with waiting rooms."

Marin: "We do not want to see you or anyone else hurt. Neither do I. But the town's liability would be absolute if we ignore procedures."

The conversation frays into a dozen smaller arguments—over timelines, over who gets named on papers, over whether the townspeople's will can outrun the letter of municipal code. Voices rise and fall like the weather.

Tom: "If the inspector says we can't, we'll make it a community thing. We'll sign statements. We'll take responsibility."

Marin: (softening) "That only goes so far in a court of law, Tom. I know what it is to want to protect a place. I also know how a single incident can drain a budget the town depends on."

You feel your chest tighten in a way that is physical—the old guilt again, a pressure that compresses your breath. Your hands shake just slightly when you tighten a bolt. The tide pulls at the new structure, testing seams with each returning swell.

Night brings a roar like distant freight. You and several volunteers stand as the first storm surge licks at the base of the barrier. Your fingers find Kai's shoulder for a second—both to steady him and because human motion still insists on seeking other motion.

Kai: "Hold this seam. If we can secure the flange with the temporary strapping, we might get through the night."

You: "Okay. Now. Clip here, pull—"

The barrier holds for a time. Water hammers the panels in a percussion you can feel in your teeth. Then, at a seam you thought reinforced enough, the joint gives. A terrible, splintered sound—sharp as someone tearing paper—cuts the air. Saltwater surges through the breach in a quick, gleaming arc.

Sound: A collective gasp, then frantic shouts. Someone curses. Another person tries to plug the seam with an armful of sandbags, but the current takes them like a hand off a rope.

For a long second you simply stand, breath collapsing into the salt wind.

Volunteers: (murmur) "We did our best..." "We held as long as we could..." "We'll rebuild..."

People clap—because that is how grief and pride sometimes announce themselves in this town—but the applause is thin around the edges and brittle as a wave-bleached board. Your chest aches. The failure presses into the hollow beneath your sternum and fills it.

You (thinking): The town applauds our courage; your chest anchors the sting of what we couldn't hold. Each clap is a ledger entry: intent acknowledged, result noted, loss recorded. You feel every shoulder in the crowd as if it were your own to bear.

Kai traces the break with a gloved finger, then looks up at you. His face is a map of impatience and something else—regret, maybe, or the rawness of disappointment.

Kai: "We iterate. We learn. We make it better."

You want to believe him with the quick, soft faith you once had when you were nine and he promised not to let the tide take your kite. Instead, the habit claws: you draft waivers in your head, think of municipal channels, of nights without sleep, of a greenhouse mattress that smells perpetually of damp soil.

[Music: A low, aching chord]

You stand in the drizzle as the town disperses, lamps winking on like distant constellations. The pier looks smaller, somehow, frayed at the edges by tide and expectation.

You know, in a way that is thick in your bones, that the next move will define not just technique but temperament: double down and iterate faster, or pull back and seek provisional municipal cover, or scale outward and ask strangers to shoulder some of this weight. None of them feels like a guarantee.
