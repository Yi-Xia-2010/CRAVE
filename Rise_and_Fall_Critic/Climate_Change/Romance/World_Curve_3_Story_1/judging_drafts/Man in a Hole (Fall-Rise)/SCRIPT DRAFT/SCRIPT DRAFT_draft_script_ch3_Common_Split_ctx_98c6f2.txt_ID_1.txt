[Scene: Broken Pier | Morning]
[Visual: The jagged silhouette of the pier against a churned gray horizon; seawater sluicing between exposed beams]
[Sound: A steady, hollow thrum of surf; gulls arguing in short, sharp cries]
[Music: Low cello, slow and unresolved]

You stand on the edge where last night's storm finished its work. The pier is a jag of wet wood and exposed nails — a map of things that used to hold together. Salt grinds in the air; each breath tastes like metal and old rope. Your fingers find the braided bracelet at your wrist without thinking. It is warm from your skin, a small circle of tether in a town that feels temporarily untethered.

You watch a swell drag a plank loose and then return it, indifferent. The morning light is thin and sickly, as if even the sun had learned to be cautious here. You think of the festival lot, of kids with paper boats, of old Mrs. Hargrove's rhubarb pies at the pier fair. You think of deadlines and grant windows and how an appetite for immediate fixes can hollow out the very places you mean to save.

There is a pulse at the base of your throat — not just adrenaline, but the weight of obligation. You could stand here until your feet go numb, cataloguing loss like evidence. Or you could move.

You (You): "How bad? Can we stabilize them today?"

Tom: (half-laugh, half-gravely) "We can stabilize a lot, but not everything. I can patch wood, rig braces, but it takes time and muscle. And those brackets—if we cannibalize a few hulls, it's a bandage, not a rebuild."

You: "Money? Materials?"

Tom: "We scrounge. But if Marin's plan gets the funding, that changes things. If not..." He doesn't finish. He Leans back, the word heavy between you.

You watch him, feeling the old impulse — take the responsibility; fix the immediate. Your fingers tighten on the bracelet.

[Scene: Lina Harrow's Marina | Late Morning]
[Visual: Rows of boats listing, tarps flapping; Lina under a high-visibility vest, fists on her hips]
[Sound: The metallic snap of cleats, someone slamming a hatch in the wind]
[Smell: Diesel, wet wood, boiled seaweed]

Lina counts losses like an accountant loses balance. Her voice is more bark than apology.

Lina: "This was the worst we could afford not to foresee. People are asking me what happens to payroll, to the moorings. If the town takes the festival lot, where does my dockhouse go? Where do kids learn to tie a knot?"

You (You): "We don't have that answer yet. But we do have options — municipal funding, community retrofits, hybrid models. I can explain the numbers."

Lina: (snorts) "Numbers don't buy fish. They don't feed mouths unless they're turned into checks. And checks take signatures from people who don't live here."

Kai steps up from behind a stack of crates, camera swinging against his chest. He moves with a kind of energy that makes the air inside your chest feel briefly less dense.

Kai: "We can train people to build modular seawalls. Open-sourced, low-cost designs. If we mobilize the crews, we can protect L-Block and the docks in weeks."

Lina looks at Kai, then at you, tired lines sharpening.

Lina: "And if the storm comes before 'weeks'?"

Kai: (bits of impatience in the cadence) "Then we work faster. We get everyone—Tom, volunteers, shop owners—on scaffolds. We don't wait for permission to keep the water out of people's basements."

You (You): "Kai's right that speed matters. But I'm worried about coverage and durability. Patchwork can become a false safety."

Lina: (stares at you) "And if you side with someone who negotiates with the council, will you still fight for us? Or will we be a compromise on a map?"

The question lands between you like a stone. You feel, very clearly, the weight of choosing a side. Your data supports something closer to the seam: targeted municipal fixes where they buy stability, grassroots where community ownership protects culture. Saying that out loud in the presence of Lina risks sounding like fence-sitting. The marina smells of diesel and loss; it presses on you.

Kai watches you the whole time; his expression is earnest, but there's a tightness at the corner of his mouth when he speaks about 'open-source' as if the word is both a shield and a prayer.

Kai: "We can get people to care if they build it themselves. Ownership isn't just civic poetry — it's survival."

You (You): "Ownership is protection. But it's also labor and time. And not everyone can afford to work for the public good and skip rent."

Kai: (defensive) "Then we find stipends. We crowdfund. We bootstrap."

You: "Bootstrap is not a plan if the tide's already in the basement."

Kai's face shifts — he looks at you, uncertain, the quick smile faltering.

You: (softly) "And I don't mean we should hand everything to you either."

[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Midday — Community Forum]
[Visual: Interior warm and humid; polycarbonate panes ripple with reflected water; chairs arrayed around reclaimed pallets]
[Sound: A thorny chorus of voices; a dry cough; a child's foot thumping on the wood]
[Smell: Soil, citrus from a pitcher of water; the faint tang of wet wool coats]

The Commons is full. Strings of LED lights make the plants glow, but they do little to lighten the mood. The community has come dressed in every uniform of concern: shopkeepers, teenagers, elders with seed packets in their pockets. The air hums with the small noises of people trying to be loud enough to be heard.

Ravi takes a moment up front, a procedural clearing of throat, and then he gestures. "We have two concrete proposals on the table, and a third that attempts to bridge them. Ms. Alvarez, Mr. Nakamura, Marin Voss will speak. Speak plainly."

Marin stands with their tablet like a prop; their gray eyes flick to the crowd and then to you. Their expression reads as complex, unreadable — a map you don't want to misinterpret. They begin with numbers, grant windows, the blunt arithmetic of funding.

Marin: "Regional funds will open if we submit a redevelopment proposal that secures vulnerable blocks. We can buy a credible mitigation package now, including permanent hulls and reinforced seawalls. The trade-off is some low-lying parcels must be rezoned for adaptive infrastructure—commercial leases will be renegotiated; some businesses may be relocated."

A murmur moves like tide under the words. Someone shouts a question about the festival lot. An elder asks about the old bakery. Marin tilts their head, listening, then answers with precise, slow sentences that sound rehearsed.

Marin: "We will prioritize cultural heritage as part of the plan. I'm asking for mandate authority to negotiate protections on those parcels."

You can feel the room splitting along invisible fault lines. Marin's plan offers money and durability but also maps out removal and reclassification — a necessary calculus for many bureaucrats, a sacrilege for those who measure the town in memories rather than line items.

Kai stands up, voice rising: "If we wait for the municipal ledger to decide, we lose agency. We can deploy modules — affordable, replicable, community-built defenses — and hold the shoreline while we continue to press for bigger funds. We have volunteers; we have designs."

Marin: (interrupting, tight) "And if those modules fail during the next binding storm, who will cover the liability? The town cannot be exposed to the risk of piecemeal fixes that create more damages."

Kai: "We design for redundancy. We commit to shared maintenance. Liability is a problem when people don't trust one another — not when we build trust through work."

A woman from the back stands up, voice cracking: "We don't have time for trust-building exercises. My basement flooded last night. I want to know if my house is safe."

You feel every gaze shift; some land on you because of what you represent — a project leader, a person with data and an apron and a history. The temperature in the room feels like a glass that could crack.

Ravi: "Ms. Alvarez, you have the floor."

Your mouth is dry. Data on your tablet feels both crisp and cowardly — numbers that can be read until they mean nothing if someone can't pay their rent. You consider the hybrid you have been drafting in your head over the last two days: securing immediate funding for the most vulnerable infrastructure (promenade reinforcements and the festival lot's perimeter), while organizing community-built modular buffers for residential blocks and supporting rooftop gardens as flood-capacity mitigation. It threads municipal horsepower with grassroots agency. It is also complicated and would require you to reconcile people who mistrust each other.

Inside, your mind runs the lists: projected costs, volunteer hours, maintenance schedules, risk matrices. Each figure is a small, cold lighthouse. Your chest tightens; you can feel the braid of the bracelet warm against your skin as if it, too, understands the friction.

You (You): "I think—" You stop because the room leans in; saying anything will move the tide.

Marin listens with an expression that gives nothing away. Kai's jaw is set, a wire pulled tight. Lina folds her arms; Tom watches quietly, hands stained dark. Etta sits in the second row, small and immovable, her fingers absently brushing soil that fell from a packet in her lap.

Marin: (even) "Ms. Alvarez, if you propose a hybrid, be explicit about where funding comes from, who will manage liability, and how rapid deployments will be coordinated. We cannot present a vague middle-ground to the region."

Kai: (sharp) "And if the 'explicit funding' depends on deferring to processes that take months?"

Ravi: "We need a prioritized plan today. Immediate measures, a short-term financing motion, and a community statement to prevent private bidders from taking advantage of panic."

The forum is a knot of practicalities and grief. You have three directions searing bright in your field of vision: grassroots rapid action, municipal secured funding with redevelopment, and the hybrid that stitches both. Each path brings different costs: political capital, cultural continuity, and personal trust.

Your heart thuds, a drum you can't quiet. The room is waiting — eyes pointed like compasses at you. You think of Tom's shop, of Lina's payroll, of the children who learned to cast in the festival lot. You think of how easy it would be to be brave on principle or expedient for safety. You think of Kai's conviction and Marin's clear, cold competence.

You: (internal) You could side with the crowd that believes in collective build and the fierce, messy hope of community action. You could accept Marin's conditional plan and its hard trade-offs for faster funding. Or you could attempt to pull both halves together and risk pleasing no one while carrying the emotional labor of mediation.

Your palms sweat, the tablet in your hand suddenly too thin to hold all those futures.
