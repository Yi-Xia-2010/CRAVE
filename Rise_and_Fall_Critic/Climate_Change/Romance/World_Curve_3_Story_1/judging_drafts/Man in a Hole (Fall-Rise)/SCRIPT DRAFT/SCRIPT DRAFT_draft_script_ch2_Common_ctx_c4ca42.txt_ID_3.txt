You sit in the back row of the planning office, the cheap chair folding under you with a soft creak. The room smells like wet wool and printer toner—familiar, bureaucratic, clinical. The map of Mariner's Reach pinned on the board across from you looks smaller than it should: inked streets and labeled parcels reduced to schematic symbols beneath a hard, fluorescent light. The greenhouse's warmth is a memory; here everything is measured in line items and timelines.

Your tablet sits face-down on your lap. Your thumb finds the braided bracelet at your wrist and kneads the rope as if it could steady the tremor in your chest. In the front, Ravi's shoulders are a ridge against his seat—his eyes are tired but fixed. You can hear the whisper of other councilmembers muttering, the low friction of political interest preparing to grind.

[Visual: The door opens. Marin enters in a slate-gray trench coat, collar up against rain. They close the door with a slow, exacting motion.]
[Sound: Soft scuff of shoes on linoleum]
[Visual: Marin's eyes sweep the room; pale gray, unreadable; they fold their hands over a slim tablet case.]

Marin moves with the kind of economy that looks like control. You have the odd, immediate impression that the room rearranges itself to accommodate their presence—the maps sit straighter, the air cools. They nod once at Ravi, then take a reserved seat near the front, their coat shedding rain in a small, neat halo.

A councilmember calls the meeting to order. You stand when your name is called; the floor feels cold beneath your boots. Your notes are precise: rooftop gardens as storm buffers, plots serving as green sponges; community-owned modular seawalls built to be assembled and maintained by residents, with open-source blueprints so other towns can replicate them. You practiced the first paragraph until it fit your ribs, but the sound of your voice in the room is a different animal.

You clear your throat and begin.

You: "We don't have to choose between preserving our promenade and protecting our homes. Rooftop gardens reduce runoff and buffer storm surges at the edges, while modular seawalls—designed to be repaired by the community—lower costs and keep control in our hands."

[Sound: A rustle; someone writes a note. The rain hammers the windows in time with the sudden quickening of your pulse.]

You pull up photos—the rooftop lab in action, seeded with hardy sedges; a prototype module Kai built, sketched and annotated. Kai sits a few rows ahead, ink on his sleeves, fingers twined in his hair. He gives you a small, encouraging smile, the kind that is equal parts belief and nervousness.

Ravi leans forward, palms flat. Ravi: "The analysis shows benefits, Iris. But we still need a legally sound path to bond financing. The region will want guarantees."

You feel the room shrink as people trade fiscal shorthand. Marin raises a hand, not to interrupt but to call attention the way someone points at a map.

Marin: "Iris, the goals line up with what I'm being asked to secure—resilience that is scalable. But bonds require risk mitigation and clear liability. Community ownership is admirable; it complicates the underwriting."

You: (quietly) "It complicates the paperwork, yes, but it reduces long-term cost and increases upkeep. People who care for something, keep it. We can write maintenance clauses—"

Marin: (measured) "—And who signs if there's a failure? Who repays if a module is damaged beyond repair in a storm? Regional auditors will want a single responsible party."

Your stomach tightens. The practical questions are not new; they've always been the teeth at the edge of your idea. You push on, trading technical detail for lived image.

You: "If we lose the promenade to privatization, we'll have given away the very heart of what binds people here. The promenade isn't just property—it's memory, festivals, the boatwrights that taught my brother to handle a cleat. If we monetize it, the town's character becomes a balance sheet."

The council murmurs. A voice from the far side of the room—sharp, lubricated by private interests—sails over the hum.

Developer Voice (Councillor Jensen): "If the town is serious about protection, we should consider a developer-led model. We can fund robust seawalls and refurbish the promenade if we open it to private stewardship. Public-private partnerships reduce immediate fiscal strain."

The words land like stones. The idea is simple and poisonous: protect and privatize. Give the promenade to a developer who will build a paid promenade—restaurants with higher rents, skywalks with private access, and entry points gated behind maintenance fees. The plan glitters in the way that money does; it solves short-term budget lines but leaves a hollow where the town used to be.

You: (voice rising) "That's not protection—it's displacement. You call it stewardship, but it's commodification. Who are we saving the town for if not for the people who live here?"

Someone coughs; a few councilmembers shift in their seats. Marin's pale eyes find yours. For a breath they are dispassionate, then just slightly warmer—like a window cracked open. You're not sure if it's sympathy or a professional invitation to negotiate.

Marin: "We need to be honest about trade-offs. The developer proposal could free capital for immediate protection. But its terms must be examined—permanent leases, easements, and the potential for restricted access are red flags. I can negotiate stricter covenants, but that will raise the cost and reduce interest."

You feel the old tangent swell—guilt and the reflex to take it on single-handedly. You are quick to shoulder burdens that aren't only yours. Your chest tightens; the fluorescent lights sharpen the edges of your pulse.

You: "We need covenants that guarantee continued public access and community oversight. If Marin can secure binding clauses, then maybe—maybe we avert the worst. But I will not stand for a promenade that the town can't walk at evening."

Marin studies you, their face an even field. The look they give you is complex—part assessment, part something that might be invitation. It unsettles you because you can't tell if it's a professional appraisal or a personal reach.

Marin: (slow) "You will have to help structure that oversight. My office can draft language, but we need local mechanisms to enforce it. That requires cooperative governance and, frankly, proof of long-term management."

Ravi nods. Ravi: "We can pilot the rooftop program as proof of management capacity. If the cooperative demonstrates stewardship over, say, two years, that improves our negotiating position."

Kai leans in, fingers clasping his knee. Kai: "I can have a working module ready to demonstrate within a week—low-cost joints, buoyant core. Show them it works, show them the community can assemble and repair it."

You: (to Kai, quietly) "That would help. But a demo won't erase the politics."

Kai: (earnest) "No. But it gives you something concrete to point at when they weigh the developer's glossy promises."

The room moves into subcommittees—legal, fiscal, community oversight. The conversation turns granular. People speak of covenants, of easements, of bond terms and insurance. The developer's proposal gleams like a quick fix in a way that makes your teeth ache.

When the formal part of the meeting concludes, you feel hollowed. You pack your notes with a careful thumb; the rain has blurred your vision at the edges. You file out into the corridor where the fluorescent gives way to a sullen light. Outside, the rain has thickened to a steady curtain.

[Scene: Damp Alley behind Municipal Hall | Night]
[Visual: Narrow alley glistening; tarps flap; steam rises from a drain]
[Sound: Rain in a steady rhythm; distant sirens beginning to thread the evening]

Kai corners you under an overhang, ink still dark on his sleeves, eyes wide with an energy that is equal parts excitement and fear. He holds a small hand-sketched model wrapped in a plastic bag like an offering.

Kai: "Iris—listen. The module—it's modular in the literal sense. Snap-locks, community-mounting points, adaptable heights. If we get one installed on a test roof and the maintenance logs show commitment, the legal team can't say we don't have stewardship in practice."

You: (weary) "You think a rooftop demo can sway people who are looking at a ledger?"

Kai: (half-smile) "It might. It shows agency. It shows grit, not promises on paper. It shows us—shows them—that we're not waiting for permission to act."

You look at him. The alley smells of wet wood and hot metal. A car passes on the promenade, spraying a wake that stings like salt on your ankles. Kai's enthusiasm is a warmth against the rain, but anxiety kinks his voice.

You: "What if they take our pilot and spin it into a vendor's checklist? What if they cage it within developer contracts and co-opt the design?"

Kai: (ruffles his hair) "Then we make every module open-source with clear licensing. We let the town own the blueprint. We make it impossible to privatize the knowledge."

You can see the earnestness in his face—he wants to fight with wrenches and dogged optimism. You want to believe him. You also know how "legal" can gnaw at the edges of good intentions.

Kai: (after a beat) "Whatever we do, we do it together. I'll get the parts. You keep pushing in the meetings. Marin said they'd draft language—Ravi said a pilot could help. It's not nothing."

You want to believe it's not nothing. The alley smells of oil and rain and the kind of resilience that comes from people glued together by necessity. But the sky gives a sudden, monstrous peel of thunder and somewhere along the promenade a siren screams sharper.

[Visual: You both turn; beyond the alley the promenade lights flicker and go out in a band.]

The storm picks up like something accelerated. The rain lashes at your coat, a stinging sheet that hammers the edges of your vision. A municipal alert chimes on your tablet—automatic, clipped, immediate.

[Sound: Emergency alert tone; a short, staccato message plays low on your device]

You slide your thumb across the screen. The damage report is terse and brutal: critical sections of the pier and the lower promenade have been compromised—structural supports washed out, decking split, several storefronts with broken windows. Photographs auto-attach: slabs of wood jutting into the sea, ropes snapping like small screams, lampposts leaning.

You feel the air leaving you in a slow, deliberate exhale. The options that had once been lines on a map have narrowed to the thinness of a tide line.

Ravi: (voice over the phone, clipped) "Iris, get down to the pier. We need an assessment team. Kai—if you can salvage anything for a demo, do it safely. Marin—I'm patching in the regional office. This is a response and a political test."

Marin's reply is immediate, professional, a wire pulled tight: "Coordinate with Ravi. Secure immediate safety perimeters. We'll need a temporary emergency ordinance to fast-track repairs."

You stand in the alley, rain flattening your hair, your notes saturated at the corners. Sirens start to weave closer. The developer's proposal from earlier seems like fair-weather thinking now—small, polished and obscene beside splintered planks and salt-water intrusions.

You look at Kai. He looks back at you with eyes that hold both the stubbornness of someone who builds things and the fragility of someone who hasn't lost a town before.

Your chest is a small, hot ache. The room of negotiation has shrunk to the length of a pier.

You pull your tablet into your palm again, the screen a cold shard; you think of covenants and pilots and the promise that people will keep what they help build. You think of festivals, of your brother's laugh, of the greenhouse heat. You think of Marin's complex look and the invitation—if that's what it was—to collaborate, perhaps even to trust.

The rain is louder now; each drop is a small, insistent tally against a clock you cannot stop.
