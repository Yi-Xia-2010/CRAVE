[Scene: Broken Pier | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the fractured pier; jagged planks jut into a churned, gray horizon. Wet ropes coil like exhausted snakes. Seabirds wheel low.]
[Music: Distant, minor-key strings; low swell of percussion like a heartbeat]
[Sound: Wind pushing cold salt; distant gulls; the creak of stressed wood]

You arrive while the town is still waking, but the sea has already been awake for hours. The pier—what was once a place for summer laughter and festival lights—leans into the morning like a wound. Boards splinter into teeth. Water licks at pilings that used to be anchored in certainty. Every piece of broken timber seems to memorize a name you can't let go of.

Rain the night before has left a gloss on everything. The air tastes of iron and kelp. Your boots take the wet boards with the practiced care of someone who grew up negotiating slippery steps. Your fingers find the braided bracelet at your wrist without intending to; the leather is warm from your skin. Memory and metric mingle: the map you saw in the emergency room, the torn edge you've been running your thumb along, the data points that say where the town is most vulnerable.

You crouch and run a hand along a shard of cedar, feeling the grain—salt-grimed, splintered. It is small, ridiculous, to want to mend wood with hands when what you need is policy and funding. But that desire is real, too. You breathe in the cold, and a gull cries as if to answer you.

You don't linger longer than you must. There are calls to be made, people counting losses, decisions waiting in the hours ahead. The dockside phone in your bag vibrates with names—Tom, Lina, Ravi—each a different pressure on your chest. You choose Tom first.

[Scene: Tom's Shop | Mid-morning]
[Visual: Interior of a cramped boat repair shop; rags, oil cans, and the smell of solder and old wood hang thickly. Light from a single high window cuts the dust into visible lines.]
[Sound: Shop radio low, the thump of a toolbox sliding shut]

Tom looks up from a hull he can probably fix in his sleep and doesn't. His face is a map of the last twenty years of Mariner's Reach: sun-creased skin, a jaw that tightens when he hears the town's name in the wrong company. When he sees you, his relief is immediate but complicated—like the way a tide sometimes pulls the shore clear and then takes a step back.

Tom: "Iris. Thought you'd be at the Hall again. How bad is it down there?"

You explain—concise, the way a field report needs to be. He nods, hands never idle, wiping his palms on a rag.

You: "Pier's fractured farther than last assessment. Tide took the festival lot shoreline. We'll need temporary bracing and an argument for priority funding."

Tom: (gruff) "Temporary bracing won't keep Lina from asking for compensation. She's already had boats drag at her slips. People are thinking about bills—insurance doesn't move fast."

You: "I know. That's why we have to be deliberate. We can shore what we can and make the ask stronger—show where community labor reduces cost."

Tom pauses, then glances toward the window where you both can see the shoreline. He lets out a breath like he's deciding something important.

Tom: "You think folks will turn up for long labor? This storm's cracked more than wood. It's cracking nerves."

You: "They showed up for Etta's rooftop last year when her coop needed reinforcement. It's not just labor—it's about ownership. If they build it, they'll fight to keep it."

Tom: (softening) "Then don't go convincing them with platitudes. Give 'em a plan that makes sense to a man who reads tide tables for Sunday breakfast."

You nod. He reaches into a drawer, produces a tape measure and thumps it into your hand like a benediction.

Tom: "And Iris? Don't let a politician dress you down. You know how to make people listen."

You're about to answer when your phone buzzes again—a call from Lina.

[Scene: Lina Harrow's Marina | Late Morning]
[Visual: Rows of moored boats at odd angles; tarpaulins flapping. Lina stands with a clipboard, knees salted, hair cropped short and stubborn as a mussel shell.]
[Sound: A generator coughs in the background; the slap of a loose tarp]

Lina's voice arrives like a thrown rope—fast and tight.

Lina: "Iris. You see the west slips? Five down. Insurance says 'act fast' or they'll refuse claims. We can't be waiting on the council forever."

You: "I'm here. We're compiling assessments now. I know the numbers will look bad, but there's also a list of things we can do immediately that hold until funding arrives."

Lina: (bitter laugh) "Until funding arrives. There's always an until. If your 'immediate' doesn't translate to cash, my bills sit on the desk with a number that doesn't care about community gardens."

You: "I hear you. I'm trying to make a case that will bring both cash and local work. We don't have to choose one or the other—"

Lina: "You don't get to say that if the town asks me to cede canals to a redevelopment plan. My marina is my living. You can promise gardens all you want, Iris, but not everyone has the patience for seeds."

She ends the call with a sound that is almost a laugh and almost not. The harbor smells of diesel and fear; your hands smell like sealant.

You close your eyes for a moment and remember a summer when Lina helped Tom haul nets off the pier. Memory is a heavier thing than any map; it reframes stakes in faces. You pocket the phone and head for the one place that, for now, still feels grown from the community's hands.

[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Noon]
[Visual: Interior warm with humid breath; suspended planters, strings of LED light, and a crowd of folding chairs arranged in a rough semicircle. Papers, seed packets, and half-empty thermoses clutter tables.]
[Music: Low, insistent strings; under them, the murmur of worried voices]
[Sound: Occasional clink of a cup; distant thud as someone sets down a box of pamphlets]

The Commons hums with a pressure that feels like a held breath. Etta sits at the front, a small presence that somehow commands the room; soil under her nails. People come with grief in their hands—photos on phones, receipts, memories. For some, the town is a ledger of losses. For others, it's a hearth they refuse to let go of.

Etta: "We won't shame each other today. We will name what is broken and what we can do together."

Applause is small, brittle, then real. Marin is at a table with Ravi—tablet open, municipal seal glinting. Marin's presence is composed and controlled; their voice carries the official weight of someone who has to make budgets behave. On the other side, Kai moves through the crowd like light, animated, palms stained with ink and a patchwork sensor tied to his belt.

Marin stands when it's their turn. They unfold a slim projection and sweep a hand over the crowd.

Marin: "Regional authorities will offer conditional funding for durable reinforcement. The funds are substantial, but they require a plan that includes redevelopment of certain low-lying blocks. That means some business relocations and the rerouting of the festival lot."

A ripple moves through the Commons—at first disbelief, then anger. Faces harden.

You feel the old impulse thrumming again: to speak, to take responsibility. Your throat tightens. Data is practice—maps, projections, models. But here, in a warm room smelling of compost and wet wood, the numbers become names. You know what 'low-lying blocks' can mean for Lina and for the neighbors who keep the corner shop alive.

Kai moves forward as soon as Marin finishes, voice bright but edged.

Kai: "We can do modular defenses ourselves. Open-source designs, community-built modules, rooftop buffers. It'll be slower, but keeps ownership where it belongs. We don't need to cede half the promenade to developers."

Marin: (calm, edges sharp) "Kai, grassroots effort is admirable, but funding timelines and regulatory requirements won't wait for prototypes and volunteer labor. If we miss the funding window, the town loses leverage."

Kai: (leaning in) "And if we hand everything to bureaucrats, we lose our town to someone else's plan."

Two forces in the room pull like counterweights. Voices rise—some for Marin's promise of fast, if brutal, money; others for Kai's insistence on community agency.

You stand in the space between them. Your data—annotated maps, predicted stress points, a model that shows how targeted reinforcement plus distributed buffers reduces both cost and risk—sits in your tablet like a small, practical gospel. It argues for a hybrid: a municipal backbone where the risk is highest, and a living, community-built margin where people retain agency and identity.

Your throat tightens. Saying that aloud will not be neutral. It will place you under both flags—Marin's and Kai's—or under neither.

You choose the approach that feels truer to you, both strategist and neighbor. The projection fills a wall. The hybrid model blooms in muted seafoam and rusted ochres—targeted pilings and reinforced breakwaters marked alongside rooftop gardens and staggered modules for neighborhoods. The room goes quiet in a way that is not relief; it is expectant.

You: "What I'm looking at is a way to split the load. We ask the region to fund reinforcement at key infrastructure points—the harbor mouth, bridge pilings, the most vulnerable promenade stretches. Simultaneously, we mobilize residents to build modular buffers and rooftop gardens that reduce runoff and provide social resilience. It's cost-efficient and preserves neighborhood ownership where it matters."

Kai: (quick, suspicious) "So you're saying the town takes money from the region and… what, lets them tell us how to change our blocks?"

You: "No. It means accepting money for specific, non-negotiable infrastructure—places that, if lost, will cascade damage. It also keeps the micro-level work—gardens, modules, open-source designs—in residents' hands."

Marin watches you without moving much, face an exercise in restrained calculation. You find their expression hard to read—neutral, engaged, maybe wary. It is complex in the way complex things are: you cannot tell whether it's approval or the lining up of counterarguments.

Marin: "Hybrid plans are appealing on paper. Practically, they demand rigorous oversight and a coordination mechanism that can slow deployment. Who will be the accountable party for each part? Who signs for the municipal funds?"

You: "A joint task force. Municipal oversight for the reinforced points, community cooperatives for the modular builds. We can define responsibilities and escrow funds so no one side acts unilaterally."

Marin: (long pause) "Escrow is fair. But politically, I need guarantees that we can meet the regional compliance metrics. I can't promise money without them."

Kai: (voice low, then rising) "And I can't promise my people will risk blood and sweat if this looks like handing over control. We need openness—plans, files, local procurement. No backdoor tenders."

The room crowds in closer. Tom watches you like a man waiting to see if a bridge will hold. Lina's jaw is tight; she looks as though a town's plan might chop off something she cannot rebuild. Etta's hands twist a rag in her lap as if turning the earth itself.

You feel, acutely, how thin the line is between coalition and fracture. Your chest is a drumbeat—each breath colder than the last. This is not only policy; it's a moral accounting. Your hybrid is a fragile architecture of trust.

You can see the stakes in faces: people who have already lost too much and fear losing ownership, and people who are terrified of losing everything overnight.

Marin's eyes return to you, unreadable—complex, as if weighing more than the room's applause.

Marin: "If you present a hybrid, Ms. Alvarez, the council will need a concise plan with deliverables tied to funding tranches. We will not get regional money on faith."

Kai: "And we won't labor if the town keeps making deals that put our neighborhoods up for sale."

Tom: (softly) "People will work if they believe you're not selling them out."

Lina: "You think a promise is enough to keep my marina afloat? I need concrete guarantees."

Your finger taps the tablet screen, and the map blinks with a thousand little possibilities and consequences. You are the one in the middle of that map, braided bracelet warm against the skin, a pocket with a sliver of splintered wood, a lifetime of memory stacked against models and timetables. Saying nothing is an option if you want to keep everyone pleased; saying anything risks divides.

The room holds its breath with you.

You feel the drop—the town's choices narrowing like an incoming tide. Your shoulders ache. You know the decision will splinter alliances and shape futures.

Now you must choose.
