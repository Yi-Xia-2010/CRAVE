[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Dawn]
[Visual: Fogged polycarbonate panes filter pale, bruised light]
[Sound: Low hum of fans, distant gulls; a drip from an overfull gutter]
[Music: Sparse piano underscored by a slow synth swell]

You push the patched door open and warm, humid air meets you like a wet blanket — the smell of loam and stale salt wrapping around the collar of your jacket. Your breath fogs for a second against the polycarbonate as you step into the greenhouse; droplets bead and run in slow, nervous rivers down the panels. The grow lights throw soft ambers, but the outside light is thin, silvered. Dawn here is apologetic.

On the nearest bench, trays of seedlings lie in uneven ranks. Some lean, stunted; others have been split by the late spring surge: their stems blackened, their leaves brown at the edges like tiny burnt flags. You run your gloved thumb across damp soil, the grit audible as a whisper.

You pull out the battered tablet from the apron pocket — its screen haloed with salt crust where your palm has caught seawater — and scroll through the latest projections. The numbers are flat and unyielding: mean sea-level rise, storm surge probability, projected maintenance costs. They sit there like a ledger of losses. You already know them; the tablet is proof to translate grief into a plan. Yet knowing never quite softens the ache.

You touch the braided leather on your wrist without thinking. The bracelet is rough under your fingertips, rope fibers that smelled of tar and sun the first time you tied the knot to it — your grandmother's voice murmuring about threads that outlast storms. Your mind slides, inevitable and sharp, to the pier festival you still catch in half-remembered frames: lanterns gone bobbing in the surf, the band pulled out from under the rain, the laughter that dissolved into shouted directions and the decision to cut loose the wooden parts before they could take more houses. The image is small, bright — then it tears.

Your chest tightens the way it does when you have to speak in front of people who once lost everything to a single night. You square your shoulders. There is work to do.

[Visual: Close-up of seedling trays; condensation drops like tiny moons]
[Sound: The soft clack of the tablet as you set it down; a kettle somewhere in the back clicks]

You inventory methodically. Fingers into soil to check moisture, an eyedropper of nutrient solution, labels re-affixed with trembling hand. You record sample pH and salinity into the tablet's log, voice-noting annotations because your hands are muddied. The taste of brine and thrift-store coffee lingers at the back of your mouth — the kind of coffee that keeps you moving at dawn-shift prices — and you almost smile at the absurdity of mixing sea and old beans into the morning ritual.

Etta is already in the corner, bent like a small harbor buoy over a crate of heirloom tomato seedlings. She hums when she works, a nasal tune that sounds like a prayer half-remembered. Her scarves are bright against the damp air, and her knuckles are embedded with soil. When she looks up, the lines around her eyes fold in sympathy before she says anything.

Etta: "Ah, dawn and trouble together. You always pick the dramatic hours, Iris."

You: "There's never a good hour for damage, Etta. Just choices afterward." (You move to the misted window to wipe a patch clear.)

Etta: "Choices — and folks to stand behind them. You look like you slept standing up." (Her voice is sharp with fondness.) "How bad?"

You: "A third of the seedlings are shock-stressed. Some are salvageable with shade and tonic. Coastal plots will need fresh topsoil — the surge pushed salt farther than expected. I logged samples." (You hand her the tablet; the screen shows rows of readings and an anxious set of annotations.)

Etta studies the numbers, mouth pursed. When she speaks there's a pile of history behind each word.

Etta: "Ravi called yesterday, you know? Tightening lines. Says the coffers are drying, that grant cycles shifted. He sounded—" (she pauses, searching for the right tone) "—he sounded like a man folding a map and pretending the coast wasn't there."

You: "That means approvals will be slower. Funding strings tighter. The forum tonight is going to be... charged."

Etta: "Charged? It'll be a thunderstorm made of people's memories. Best to bring facts. Best to bring bread. People soften for bread."

You almost laugh but it collapses into a breath. Etta reaches out, palms the bracelet on your wrist with a dirt-stained care that feels like blessing.

Etta: "Don't carry it all alone. The town remembers what you carry, but it remembers more the faces who keep showing up."

Her words settle like a warm seed, but then a damp chill slides under the warmth: the rumor you heard on the way in — the consolidation plan. A private developer with glossy proposals, promises to "revitalize the promenade" with upscale units and flood-insurance guarantees. It reads like salvation to people scared of another storm; to you, it reads like erasure.

Before you can answer, the greenhouse door swings again. Tom stands in the doorway, grease-smudged and broader than the doorframe allows. He smells of diesel and sea and a lunch that involved too much salt. He drops a battered thermos on a table and leans on the doorway like an old mast.

Tom: "You should have called me in — we could've saved a couple trays before the surge." (He looks at the seedlings, then at you.)

You: "The surge surprised everyone. We did what we could. It's not a blame thing, Tom."

Tom: "I know. Just—" He rubs the back of his neck. "Lina's been wiring up the marina, talking about 'development as protection.' Heard it from her yesterday — says if a developer takes over that stretch, they'll put in a hardened promenade and hotels, flood-proofing the town 'for a new economy.' She thinks she’s offering a lifeline."

You feel the marrow of the town tighten. A hardened promenade might protect infrastructure, but it would cost the market stalls, the festival grounds, the places where your grandmother taught you to mend nets. You recall Etta's garden plots lining the promenade on summer afternoons — so many small claims to a coastline that would be smoothed away by poured concrete.

Tom: "Ravi's got the budget in knots, Etta says. And Marin — Marin's headed to Municipal Hall today to try and secure something from above. If Marin can't bend the line... well."

You: "Marin's negotiating?"

Tom: "They're negotiating. Which means it's urgent. Which means we need everyone's voices tonight." (He searches your face.) "You heading to the forum?"

You: "I'm presenting the community adaptation plan — the rooftop pilot, modular barriers, the cost-benefit. Data plus the stories; we need both." (You set down another tray, fingers trembling slightly.) "But Ravi's cuts and a developer's promise of safety will change the way people hear us."

Tom: "People listen to the sound of money louder than the smell of sea sometimes." (He sounds bitter, and then ashamed for sounding bitter. He sighs.) "You shouldn't have to carry more than plans. I'm helping. I'll talk to the crew, get salvage tools on standby."

The conversation lurches, not ending, but folding into practicalities. You go over the presentation in your head while you syringe saline into a tray and label samples more carefully than before. Every number you read on the tablet takes on the weight of faces you love: Etta's scarred hands, Tom's creased forehead, the kid who used to kite-surf in back of the pier. The greenhouse becomes a workshop and a chapel and a battleground, all at once.

[Visual: You step out onto the wet wooden planks of the Promenade; gulls wheel along the horizon]
[Sound: The sea sighs softly; a distant metallic creak from the pier]
[Music: The synth swells deepen with a low, unresolved chord]

The pier's planks are slick. Where the festival once unfurled tables and bunting, there are now boards patched with mismatched wood and a hand-lettered sign: "REMEMBER." The sign looks small against the vast water, but you remember each name scrawled in the past on similar slips. The town's collective memory has a fragile architecture.

You stand at the end of the promenade and watch the sea. It does not roar; it whispers intending things. You think of Marin moving through municipal corridors — their gray coat visible in your mind like a map folded along the edges. They are a planner, a problem-solver in a suit that keeps its own weather. The news that Marin is to visit Municipal Hall to negotiate emergency funding is a double-edged promise: if they succeed, there will be money for durable solutions; if they fail, private money could take the stage and rewrite who decides what is worth saving.

Back inside the greenhouse, Etta hands you a small paper cup of coffee — thin, too-sugared, and somehow the strongest thing close by.

Etta: "Don't fasten your courage to one idea. People need the facts, yes, but they need to be invited into the making of them. Your words give people maps. Make sure you give them the pencils to redraw when the tide comes."

You: "I will. I'll show them the pilot's numbers and the cost of doing nothing. I have case studies, models, and an ask. I can make the data humane — show what is preserved and what is at risk."

Etta: "And if words aren't enough, bring bread."

You laugh in spite of the pressure, and the sound feels like glass. Tom claps you on the shoulder in a way that argues for stubbornness over despair.

Tom: "Whatever happens, we're not letting this town sell its seafront without a fight. Count me in. Count the crew. Count on me to be loud."

You feel fortified by their trust, but there's a dull horn in the distance — budget cuts, developers, municipal corridors filled with polite wrists and ledger books. It presses at the edges.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand on the rail, the braided bracelet catching a bead of spray]
[Sound: The sea whispers, then seems to recede, a brief silence like a held breath]

You return to the tablet, rearrange slides, and craft the one story you must tell: if the promenade is sold for immediate guarantees, what will be lost beyond market value? You make space in the deck for images of the pier festival, for Etta's garden, for statistics. You underline places where community labor lowers costs. You write notes in the margin—lines you rehearse aloud, steady and dry.

And yet the thought of Marin, at a glass table across from Ravi and others, negotiating line items that will decide whether the town keeps its promenade or signs it away, settles in your stomach like cold metal. You cannot be in two rooms at once.

You fold your notes into the tablet like a map you refuse to let tear. The forum tonight is no longer a meeting; it's a hinge.

[Sound: Wet footsteps returning to the greenhouse. The humidity seems heavier, as if the place itself is listening.]

Etta packs a small satchel of seedlings to hand out at the forum — a symbolic gesture she insists on. Tom pulls a roll of waterproof tarps from under a bench with a grunt like an offering. You tie a knot on your bracelet and feel the rope catch on the calluses on your palm — an anchor in a sea of appointments and rumors.

You know the data. You know how to translate models into cost estimates, how to calculate amortization of modular barriers against living-wage job losses and cultural erosion. You know the math of resilience. But you also know that numbers do not hold hands, do not hand over a ticket to the memory of a pier. Tonight, you will have to make them feel both the algebra and the ache.

[Visual: The greenhouse light dims as clouds thicken outside]
[Music: The piano drops to a single low note, unresolved]

You step back from the benches, shoulders tight, and imagine two rooms: one full of neighbors with faces mapped by loss; one full of municipal planners with spreadsheets and neutral expressions. Between them is a bridge you will try to build in the light of a single presentation.

The whisper from the sea grows louder — not a threat yet, only a reminder that time moves as tide does: slow, relentless, impartial. You inhale the brine and stale coffee and exhale a line of resolve.

For a moment you let yourself picture success: community labor, pilots taking root, the promenade safe and whole. The image is fragile; it trembles in the same way as the sprigs under your care. Then another image surfaces: polished developers' signage, a promenade leveled into a broad, fashionable sea wall with no room for bunting or booths. The two outcomes hang like weather choices.

You pick up the tablet once more, fingers steady now, and scroll to the last slide — the one with the ask: a modest call for emergency allocation, community-matched funds, and a public covenant to protect cultural spaces. You set it to present mode and rehearse the opening line in your head until it no longer feels like a script but like a vow.

Outside, rain starts: small beads at first, then a steady thread against the polycarbonate. It turns the world gray, then grayer. The greenhouse's warmth feels both shelter and pressure cooker. You realize, with a cold, sinking clarity, that tonight's forum will decide whether the town negotiates from a place of strength or sells out of fear. The stakes are no longer just seedlings.

You close the tablet. The room smells of wet earth, coffee, and the metallic tang of old salt. You press your palm to the bracelet and listen to the town's memory in the patter of rain.

A page turns in your chest, half hope, half dread. You take a breath and step toward Municipal Hall.
