[Scene: Tom's Shop | Morning]
[Visual: Sun-weak through dusty windows; tools and hull scraps stacked in layered horizontals]
[Sound: Distant gulls, a radio murmuring an old sea shanty low in the background]
[Music: Low, restless strings]

You set your bag down on a battered workbench and feel the familiar give of sawdust under your palms. Tom's shop smells like a vow: oil and old wood and the warm, honest sweat of people who fix things because they must. Your utility apron is already smudged—the first coffee stain a dark crescent on the chest pocket—proof that momentum has started before you finished dressing for it.

Kai drops into the stool across from you with the energy of a person who believes it will all work if they can only make the plans loud enough. He grins, a sudden flare of sunlight through low cloud, and pushes his windbreaker aside. From beneath, he produces a folded prototype like a small secret: a corrugated polymer panel with knuckle-sized brackets and a hand-sketched hinge.

Kai: "Look at this. Lightweight, locks together like Lego, and—" (he taps a corner) "—it flexes where it needs to, holds where it shouldn't slip."

You take it, fingers tracing the seam. The plastic is cool, the joint rough where he hand-filed tolerances. It feels possible. It feels dangerously, gloriously possible.

You: "It looks good. Weight, ballast points, how much tidal force did you test it for?"

Kai's smile softens, the kind that used to mean nothing more than plans for kites and rope swings when you were kids. Now it holds calculations and sleepless nights.

Kai: "Enough to slow a surge like yesterday's. Not enough to stop a wall of water. But if we build rows and interlock them—distributed failure, not catastrophic—people can still breathe between fixes."

You: "Distributed failure is a philosophy I can stomach—if we can organize the hands."

The bell over the door clanks and Etta blooms in, hands full of seed envelopes and that small, steady authority she wears like a shawl. She sets the packets down as if planting them right into the scrap wood of the bench.

Etta: "Seeds, yes. And sandwiches. Someone's got to keep you lot fed while you bang and swear. You remember the festival pies? We used leftover dough and good will."

Tom emerges from behind a hull, wiping his hands on a rag, eyes already running plans as he looks at the prototype and then at you.

Tom: "You two done theorizing? If we're going to slap up panels by the pier, we need bolts, scaffolding, and someone who actually keeps a head about them when the tide kicks."

Lina lumbers in shortly after—wet vest still on, the smell of diesel clinging to her. She grunts, half grin, half complaint.

Lina: "I'll give you dock space. Platform storage for panels while you assemble off-site. But don't get it into your heads to start cutting into my berth without asking."

You feel the ledger of obligations shift toward you: the map in your head—who will bring what, who has schedule flexibility, who will babysit liability when the inspector asks for paper instead of handshakes. Your braid rubs against the back of your neck; you reach for the bent ring in your pocket the way people reach for small talismans in storms.

The tension in the room recalibrates toward action. You and Kai map a work plan on the back of an old invoice: assembly lines under tarps, salvage runs to the shipbreaking yard, a roster for volunteers — mornings for heavy lifting, afternoons for calibration and instruction.

Tom: "We can use the old scaff legs from the east yard. I can torch out faulty bolts and wrench the rest. But we need a sign-up board. We need someone to actually call people."

You: "I'll take sign-ups. Etta can run training on the plant barriers and community gardens—teach folks soil retention and why green edges matter. Kai, you run the assembly protocols. Tom, you run the salvage."

Kai bumps your shoulder, a quick, affectionate jolt that still contains the map of childhood. It should be easy, organizing friends into a weekend of sweat. Instead, every plan you make carries the weight of possible failure like a wet cloth round your shoulders.

[Scene: Greenhouse Commons | Afternoon]
[Visual: Glass replaced with recycled polycarbonate glowing amber; steam hangs in beams of late light]
[Sound: Drip of condensation, soft chatter of volunteers]
[Music: Sparse piano, a steady undercurrent]

You move through the Greenhouse Commons with a clipboard and a hot thermos clutched between cold fingers. Volunteers fold like tides into the space: old men with calloused hands, teenagers with enthusiasm and no fear of getting wet, shopkeepers who bring plywood scraps and sandwiches. The air is humid with compost and the faint, homey scent of Etta's soup from the back table.

Etta pulls you close for a moment, dirt in the lines of her palms.

Etta: "Listen. People need something to do. They need direction and a place to put their grief into. Give them that, and they'll make more than you expect."

You: "They also need safety. A bolt placed wrong can become a coffin in a flood. We do this carefully."

Etta studies you, her eyes kind but unblinking.

Etta: "Care is what keeps you up, Iris. But don't let it keep you from asking for help."

You scribble notes, draft a volunteer waiver from memory, and then rework it when Kai barrels in with a small stack of printouts—diagrams, assembly sequences, and a rough liability checklist.

Kai: "Redundant straps on seams. Add the cross-latch. If we teach the team the pattern, we can speed up assembly."

You: "Teach, then test. Full-scale stress test before we bring it to the pier."

Kai's impatience flares. He paces, hands restless.

Kai: "Every hour we test is an hour the tide has to keep smashing parts of the promenade. People want this now."

You: "I know. I feel that push too. But if a panel blows and someone gets hurt, we'll have less than anger—we'll have a legal hand to shut us down."

Kai: "Then we take the risk. We adapt on the fly. We iterate."

You: "Iterate only if it doesn't cost lives. We can iterate in controlled cycles."

The exchange stalls; the room is full of others who have their own stakes in the debate. Tom watches both of you, arms folded.

Tom: "You two sound like the town arguing with itself. Decide or we lose hands to bickering."

You swallow, the taste of metal fear, and make a decision to stay up the next two nights at the greenhouse. Sleep loses shape: short rests, damp blankets, the intermittent lullaby of wind against polycarbonate. You draft liability waivers in the margins of scrap paper, printouts, and quick legalese you copy from an online template and then tailor with Etta's plain-speech edits.

[Scene: Pier — Build Days | Morning into Dusk]
[Visual: Panel assemblies under tarps, volunteers lined like a moving seam; salt spatter on faces]
[Sound: Hammers, the slap of tarps in wind, laughter threaded with the grunt of exertion]
[Music: Percussion that mimics heartbeat, tense]

The first build days are electric in the way hope sometimes is—bright and slightly dangerous. Salt-streaked hands pass brackets along a line. You watch neighbors who've never seen each other twice in their lives laugh while fitting bolts. The promenade smells of hot metal and sunblock and the yawn of the sea.

Kai moves like a conductor, calling adjustments and shouting encouragement. He touches your forearm to get your attention, the gesture small and old as the days you both fished from the pier.

Kai: "Clamp there. Turn the latch. Good—keep it steady."

You: "Check the ballast line—Tom, five extra sandbags at the north spike."

Tom: "On it. Lina, can you spare a hand with the crane?"

Lina: "I run a tight ship. You're lucky I showed up. Help me keep it that way."

The municipal inspector arrives mid-afternoon, umbrella a formal, dark shape against the bright, practical chaos. They step with a measured, official weight. When the inspector lifts their head and looks at you, there's a civics-shaped coolness; the expression is readable only as professional concern. You know Marin's presence could mean enforcement or negotiation—the look is complex.

Marin: "This is commendable energy. But I'm required to inform you—unapproved structural work in a hazard zone requires certification. Liability is not a suggestion."

You feel the room exhale like someone holding their breath too long. Kai bristles.

Kai: "We have volunteers and tested prototypes. We can't wait for a permit cycle while the town washes away."

Marin: "Permits exist because people die when they're ignored. I can expedite a provisional review, but I need formal drawings, a list of materials, and a staging plan that shows safety protocols."

You: "We'll provide that. We just need flexibility—an interim allowance while we certify."

Marin: (pausing) "I can bring that forward to Ravi. But I cannot promise legal protection from developers who might argue you obstruct access, nor can I guarantee the indemnity you want without council approval."

A murmur ripples through the volunteers. Someone jokes that the town meetings are slower than tide; another says that bureaucracy smells like bureaucracy — but beneath the jokes is the sharp tang of reality: without formal cover, your walls are gestures rather than defenses.

Kai steps closer, voice low.

Kai: "So we keep building and risk getting shut down. Or we stop and risk being washed away. Which is it?"

You: "It's both. It's always both."

Marin studies you like a map, then looks away.

Marin: "You need to document everything you do. Photos, materials logs, witness statements. If you can present that, I have better ground to argue provisional measures."

Tom: "We can log it. We'll get Tom's ledger—"

Tom's levity drops; the ledger is real, salted and patched like a boat. You sense the municipal machinery, slow and heavy, trying to mesh with the nimble, improvised work of neighbors.

[Sound: A lowing swell in the distance builds; the volunteer chatter is a thin thread]
[Music: Dissonant chords undercut celebration]

On the second night after the inspection, a storm surge arrives with a sharp, implacable appetite. You watch from the promenade as the sea presses into the town like a claim. The barrier you and sixty volunteers built stands, stubborn and imperfect, a jagged line of human intent against a vast machine. For a while it holds. People cheer; you taste salt and pride like a cheap coin on your tongue.

Then, at a seam where two panels interlock, the compromise you made in favor of speed shows itself at its worst. A bracket shears under stress—a small metallic scream—and water finds the seam like a needle through cloth. The panel twists. Momentum magnifies the tiny failure and the seam opens like an argument.

You move faster than thought. Your hands are wet and numb, fingers slipping on scaled metal. Volunteers shout, some rush to tie the failing seam with straps, others try to wedge spare timber. The world narrows to one catastrophic, stubbornly precise series of motions: find bolt, lock hinge, hold.

You: "Hold the strap—on three. Now! Pull!"

The strap takes, then gives, then slides. The panel buckles and the water wins that small theater. Cold cascades across the promenade. Cheers curdle into a stunned silence, then polite applause out of habit, and you feel the eyes of the town like small pebbles hitting your chest.

Kai: (voice breaking) "We almost had it—"

Kai's breath catches, not at the sea but in the way he looks at you—an old ease straining against a new stress. There is something complex in his expression that you cannot claim as affection or accusation; it is salt and history and impatience braided together.

Marin is there in the aftermath, hands folded, the civic coolness returning.

Marin: "We need a full report. Injuries? Structural failures? Photographs. This will be examined."

You: "Nobody was hurt. We can shore the seam tonight—"

Marin: "You might have delayed the inevitable—unapproved structures create both risk and political pressure. The council will want explanations."

The applause is small; the town's gratitude for effort is real, but it smacks faintly of consolation. You stand, drenched, the failure a dense, hot weight in your chest that presses down on every breath.

Your first instinct is the same old habit: to take responsibility, to shoulder the blame and carry it like ballast until it slows you down. You begin drafting incident notes in your head—what failed, why, who did what—because cataloging is a way to control chaos. You find your hands already reaching for paper and a pen.

Kai: "We fix it. We iterate. We make that seam unbreakable."

You: "We learn. Weeredge out what's practical and what's reckless. We don't let people get hurt."

Kai: "People want fixes now. We need momentum."

You feel the town's eyes—some forgiving, some incredulous—on your shoulders. The municipal files and the scrap bins both nudge for your choice. The promise you made to protect the town is now a question: do you lean into rapid do-it-yourself iteration, halt and seek legal cover, or spread the blueprints farther to invite help from beyond?

You stand at the seam and breathe out, the breath shaking. The map of possible next moves fans in your mind, each path carrying its own cost. The choice burns silver and cold and refuses to answer itself.
