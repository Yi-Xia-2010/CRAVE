[Scene: Harborfall Coves | Dawn]
[Visual: Long shot of low-lying coves; half-light reveals churned mud and a ribbon of machinery tracks]
[Sound: Distant diesel thrumming, gulls keening, a soft, constant surf]
[Music: Low, mournful cello with a thin high note that trembles like a bell]

You walk the path you used to take with Tomas when the tide was kinder. The salt in the air is heavy as a promise unkept—metallic, damp, threaded with diesel and crushed shells. Your boots sink into a mixture of sand and clay where tire marks cut across eelgrass. Small things catch your eye and then lodge in your chest: a faded buoy snagged in mangrove roots, a length of rope threaded with barnacle, netting half-buried in wet sand.

In one memory the machines moved with an efficiency you admire and resent: blades biting earth where Tomas once dropped his lines. In another, scaffolds stand half-finished like bones beneath a pale sky, gates waiting for bolts that never came. Elsewhere a jetty you helped steady after the last storm sits in pieces, wood splintered where the sea found a seam. You do not know which memory is yours and which belongs to the town—only that all of them smell the same: salt, oil, and the faint iron tang of panic.

Tomas stands on the rocks, hands folded into a waxed jacket, his face a map of years with the sea. He does not come to meet you. He watches, the way a man watches a weather front: patient, implacable, not surprised but not reconciled either.

Tomas: "They cut through the inlet like it's paper," he says when you are close enough to hear—voice dry and slow, the syllables rubbed by sea wind. "Not for the fish. For the guarantee in their contracts."

You feel the watch against your wrist—your brother's dive watch—cold and small under your palm. It ticks with the same steady indifference though the world around it has become less so. The watch is a loop you keep tightening until it sings.

[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Midday]
[Visual: The promenade is crowded—people in raincoats, pamphlets in hand; banners half-folded in the wind]
[Sound: Murmured arguments, a megaphone coughing, the slap of pamphlets against palms]
[Music: Sparse piano chords, a low rumble like distant thunder]

The town is a collage of faces. Hana's diner door is propped open; her apron is blotched with mud and coffee and something that looks too much like salt-stained tears. Her smile arrives first—automatic—then falters, a mechanical thing trying to keep functioning with a hairline fracture.

Hana: "You did what you thought would save people," she says before you can speak. Her voice is small but steady, a practiced reconciliation. "But this—" She gestures at the promenade, at the people clustered in tight knots—"this has hurt them."

Her eyes catch yours, and for a heartbeat you see the tenants you helped find shelter, the meals you arranged, the nights you stayed up writing emergency plans. Then you remember Hana's tenant—a small corner flat of mismatched furniture—now water-streaked and trembling. The sight of that home, half-empty, spirals in your mind.

Kaito finds you at the edge of the crowd like someone who has been searching for a keel to brace against. His sensor pendant blinks, a faint heartbeat against his chest. When his hands come up they are not steady; he cups them together the way someone might try to catch a falling bird.

Kaito: "You said we'd think through the impacts—metrics, thresholds. We would monitor and adapt." His voice is even but the tremor in it threads the words. "What happened? You made a decision, Maya. People are wet and cold and angry."

You want to tell him the calculus of it—the clauses, the phone calls at three in the morning, the sleeplessness. You want to tell him you balanced lives and livelihoods and felt every one like a coin in your palm that slipped away and broke. But the words feel too big, clumsy as helicopter blades.

You: "I—" Your voice catches. "I thought I had a way through. I thought—"

Kaito: "You thought," he repeats, softer, and the accusation is careful but it lands like a stone. "You thought for all of us, and now we're counting what we lost."

His hands tremble. He looks at you as if searching the lines of your face for the place the decision was made, as if a verdict might be visible like a scar.

Kaito: "We can measure what broke," he says finally. "But that doesn't fix the boats Tomas can't use, or Hana's roof, or the trust out there." He points toward the crowd—toward Tomas, toward people whose lines read 'We trusted you' without saying it aloud.

You remember a night years ago—the lamp of the lighthouse swinging as the storm took your brother. You remember the breath that came out of you like ocean foam. You remember promising yourself you would never be the person who let that happen again. Now the promise tastes like sand.

[Scene: Forum Backstage (Municipal Hall) | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The municipal hall smells of damp coats and old wood; folding chairs are stacked like folded cliffs]
[Sound: The scrape of a chair, distant coughing, a child's small laugh edged with fear]
[Music: Single violin sustaining a thin, raw note]

You wait in a corridor that feels too narrow for the weight of what it's about to hold. Papers rustle in volunteers' hands; a woman hums a chant under her breath; a man wipes his face with both palms as if trying to erase the sea from his skin. The town has come to the forum like a storm front: dense, expectant, and unforgiving.

Mayor Elena paces with the patient exhaustion of someone who cannot keep every promise. Lux walks past, her coat immaculate, rain beads beading off the fabric as if the ocean itself will not claim her. She moves with the efficiency of a machine designed to perform, and when her eyes find you they are not warm—but they are not cruel either. They are assessing, sliding across the room like a surveyor.

Lux: "Loss is awful," she says in that low, precise tone that sounds like an engineered apology. "We built protection. We aimed to prevent what happened. But engineering is not magic. There are trade-offs."

You want to throw the contract at her feet, leave ink and signatures and all the razor-edged words that convinced you—convince them—convince yourself. You also want to tell her that whoever drafted those trade-offs did not ask Tomas where his nets ran, did not ask the kids who grew up on that jetty where they learned to walk.

Lux: "We can adjust the plan," she continues, a practiced cadence. "We can restore what we can. We have resources—"

Tomas shuffles in, slow and deliberate. He does not meet Lux. He stops near you, draws himself up to his full, salt-worn height, and when he speaks there is no performative fury—only the kind of anger that is older than his bones.

Tomas: "We asked for protection. Not permission to erase the places we love." His voice is a low bruise. "You came with your machines. You came with contracts. The tide found our seam."

Hana stands beside him now, apron askew. Her hands are scratched and she smells of soup and salt and the kind of tiredness that is rooted in service. Her smile is broken like the edges of a storm-tossed shell.

Hana: "There are people with nowhere to go tonight," she says, not accusing you but also unable to hide the fissure that runs under her words. "We did what we could, Maya."

You feel everyone's sight like a single tide line climbing your body—starting at your feet and moving upward. You try to name where the blame belongs and feel it slip through your fingers like water.

Kaito steps forward again—closer, pleading now rather than accusing. His voice is smaller; the incense of science and the human need to be held have collided.

Kaito: "This isn't about assigning guilt," he says. "It's about what we do next. But we can't move if we don't trust one another."

You: "I—I tried to balance it." Your voice is thinner than you want. "Every day I slept with the idea that one decision could save lives. Maybe I misread the scale. Maybe I misread the town."

Kaito stares at you as if trying to read a map written in the tremor of your voice. "You made a decision that looks like security," he says, "but to some it looks like betrayal. To others, it looks like cowardice. To me—" He swallows. "To me it looks like you thought it would end the counting."

A rustle runs through the crowd like a wind shifting. Someone calls a name; someone else laughs in a sound too sharp to be comfortable. The hall breathes with accusation and grief and the tight starched togetherness of a community trying to hold itself after being pulled.

You press your fingers onto the cold face of the dive watch. The metal is an anchor and an indictment both. It is the smallest relic of loss that you can touch and still believe in.

[Music: The cello drops to a single low note; the rest fades to near silence]

Lux approaches you then, close enough that the smell of her parka is a clean, metallic contrast to the salt on your skin. Her eyes are level.

Lux: "You asked for clauses," she says quietly. "You asked for oversight. You tried to make the work slower, more local. I gave you that compromise, because I thought it was the right play. Then the tide took the places we couldn't finish in time."

There is no rancor in her voice. There is only the factual cadence of someone listing the cost of a trade.

Lux: "We can redeploy resources. We can try to repair what we've taken. But politics will be messy. The investors—" She looks past you to the crowd—"—they'll watch every move."

Kaito's jaw tightens. He steps between you and Lux like a shifting buoy.

Kaito: "We can make this right," he insists, though his voice betrays the strain. "We can adapt the systems, protect the habitats Lux's plan touched, and—"

A man from the back interjects, the voice harsh from the rafters: "Adaptation doesn't replace these coves," he says. "We lost a home today."

A child's small cry ricochets like a dropped stone.

You feel the forum tilt from debate into reckoning. The list of what must be repaired is not only physical: trust, livelihoods, faith in the idea that someone will stand between the sea and the people who live by it. It is easy to write down engineering requirements. It is nearly impossible to write down the shape of a town's forgiveness.

You fold the watch into the palm of your hand and close your fingers. The room seems to focus inward on you as if the sea itself has gathered to ask—what now, Maya?

You inhale. The air tastes of congealed rain and old coffee and the sweet, corrosive undercurrent of fear.

You: "The forum tomorrow—" Your voice is smaller than the room demands. "It isn't about options anymore."

Someone laughs, the sound thin and brittle.

You feel it in your bones: the forum will not be a negotiation of plans. It will be a ledger of blame.
