[Scene: Market Square | Late Morning — Low Cloud]
[Visual: Wet wooden stalls glisten; banners from LuxCorp ripple against a slate sky. A small crowd clusters around a demonstration table covered with jars and a blinking sensor array.]
[Music: Low strings, restrained; a single piano note repeats like a heartbeat.]
[Sound: Gulls, distant engine coughs, vendor calls softened by drizzle; a faint electronic ping from the sensor array.]

You step out of Kaito's lab with the smell of damp metal still clinging to your jacket—lamp oil and the sharp algae-cold that always smells like the inside of a storm. Your brother's dive watch rests heavy under your sleeve; it keeps time as if measuring not hours but obligations.

The square is smaller than it looks on the brochure LuxCorp handed out last week. It smells of fried fish, wet rope, and the diesel tang from Tomas's old boat, which is tied to the market-side pier like a sleeping beast. Tomas leans on the rail, hands folded over knotted fingers, and a cluster of long-time fishers hangs near him, faces mottled by sun and salt and a lifetime of reading tides.

Kaito waits by the demonstration table, sleeves rolled up despite the chill, field camera slung to his side. His fingers hover over the sensor pendant; the little LEDs blink in patient sequences. Around you, locals murmur—curiosity braided with the brittle edge of fear. Across the square, LuxCorp's pop-up kiosk glows with neat simulations and a PR woman whose smile has been calibrated to the angle of the corporate logo.

You can feel the town's weight like incoming tide. It presses at you from every direction—Tomas's wary squint, Hana's hopeful glance from the diner doorway, the tight line at Mayor Elena's mouth. The choice you make here will rearrange loyalties, livelihoods, and the literal shoreline.

Tomas clears his throat and turns to you first, voice low enough that only the people near him hear.

Tomas: "You know these waters, Maya. The old markers don't read the way they used to. If those sensors are what you say, we need to know they mean what you say before we start moving boats and families."

You: "We won't ask anyone to move on a guess. Kaito's been tracking salinity—how it spikes before parts of the marsh give way. The sensors can warn us early, not act like a wall. They give time to shore nets, move crops, pull boats."

Kaito: (softly) "Show them the reading?" (His smile is almost a private thing; there's a jitter under it you can hear in his voice.)

You watch him set the pendant on the wooden board. The screen beside it swells with a strip of data—a line of numbers that looks like a coastline redrawn in code. He taps out a rhythm of explanation, fingers patient and practiced.

Kaito: "Here—see this trough? Salinity spikes two tides before the scarp starts to undercut the roots. If we get a reliable lead time, the town doesn't have to choose between hasty engineering and losing everything."

A murmur ripples—some hopeful, some skeptical. An older fisher frowns.

Fisher: "How certain you are? Machines broke the last time we trusted ’em."

You feel the old knot tighten in your chest. The metal taste rises; it's always there before a big ask. Your brother's watch catches the light when you reach to adjust your sleeve; it ticks like an accusation or a benediction—sometimes it's hard to tell which.

Kaito watches how you choose, reading your hands the way he reads data. He doesn't comment out loud, but his shoulders shift in a small relief.

You choose words—either numbers or memories—and whichever path you pick, a few faces in the crowd change. Data soothes the younger tech-leaning crowd. Stories pull at older fishermen who still remember the shoreline before the berms.

Mayor Elena arrives with a purposeful gait, her municipal pin catching a gray slant of light. She listens from the edge of the crowd with the professional patience of someone who carries other people's elections in one pocket and grant applications in the other.

Mayor Elena: "Maya. Kaito. This is...promising. But I need measurable certainty. You know the budget calculations; I know what happens if I reallocate funds and the board turns on me. LuxCorp has a funding line ready—predictable, immediate. I need reason to choose uncertainty over a signed ledger."

You: "We can give you reason, Elena. The sensors are not a gamble—they're a lens. We just need municipal seed money to expand trials to the marsh, to collect the lead time data you can sign off on."

Mayor Elena: (sighing) "Seed money is not just a line on the budget. It's votes. It's the town that will ask why the mayor diverted money from repairs to experiments."

LuxCorp's PR woman smiles like a ship's beacon and steps forward with an ease that smells faintly of sanitizer and rehearsed empathy.

Lux PR: "Mayor Rossi, what our wall offers is immediate security and a clear funding plan. We can start construction within months. We understand the politics—this is a turnkey solution."

You notice the investor glancing over Lux's shoulder, the kind of person who could make or unmake a municipal project with a single red pen and a phone call. His presence reduces the square's warmth by a few degrees; you can feel the market recalibrate.

Kaito: (quiet, almost a confession) "We don't make promises we can't test. But the data—it's the kind that scales. If we can prove lead time across the estuaries, we give the town options instead of a single, irreversible fix."

Tomas: (to the Mayor) "And if the permit don't come? We got a season of storms coming. We can argue tills the tide takes strangers' patience. If the sensors could tell us—if they give us two tides to move nets and raise beds—how many people stay?"

Mayor Elena folds her hands, the expression of a woman balancing a ledger against a human ledger. It's not an answer, only a weighing.

A LuxCorp rep, overhearing, leans in with a tone designed to be helpful.

Lux Rep: "We respect local traditions, but we also know a disaster response when we see one. The longer you wait to accept a proven barrier, the more you risk emergency measures that cost more and leave less of the town intact."

You hear the subtext loud and clear: accept our wall and you keep what you can; refuse and you risk both political ruin and a storm that will be used to justify whatever comes next.

Kaito meets your eyes. There is a flash of that nervous, playful softness he hides behind; then it tightens into something fierce.

Kaito: "If we ask for money publicly, we'll get attention—good and bad. If we keep doing tests quietly, we might refine the tech but give Lux a month to frame the narrative. If we deploy now, across vulnerable coves, we could save a lot—if it works."

Your chest tightens; decisions compress around your ribs. Every choice seems to carry its own kind of failure. You remember your brother and the siren-slice of the storm that took him. Memory is a cold instrument in the face of risk.

Silence pools, punctuated only by the sensor's soft pings and the distant slap of water against weathered boats. Your brother's watch ticks hollowly; the iron taste in your mouth blooms again, old as guilt, new as decision.

Kaito steps closer, lowering his voice so only you can hear.

Kaito: (whisper) "Whatever you choose, I've got the models to back it. But models—like people—need time. And time is the thing we're short on."

You look at the faces around you: Tomas's lined certainty, Mayor Elena's political worry, the fisher who thinks in tides and not budgets, the investor who counts everything in return on capital. LuxCorp's banner flaps like a promise and a threat at once. Kaito's hands rest on the demo table, fingers still marked with soil and solder.

You can feel the town leaning, minute by minute, toward one story or another. You taste iron. Your brother's watch clicks in your ear as if to mark a verdict you haven't pronounced.

You inhale. The rain becomes a finer, more insistent thread. The square grows smaller, as if the sky is pulling the world tight.

Kaito looks at you with that soft humor that masks fear. Tomas gives you a look that is both plea and dare. Mayor Elena watches you like an election on a tightrope.

You are the hinge. The forum tomorrow will hear your voice—publicly, quietly, or in the rush of immediate action. The town waits, and whatever you pick will define not only the pilot's fate but the shape of Harborfall itself.
