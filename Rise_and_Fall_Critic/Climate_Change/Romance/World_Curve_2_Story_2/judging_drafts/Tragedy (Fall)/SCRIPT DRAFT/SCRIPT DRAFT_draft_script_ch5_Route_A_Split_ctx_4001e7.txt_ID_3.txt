[Scene: Hana's Diner | Late Evening]
[Visual: Warm, low-lit interior; steam rising from a chipped enamel pot; rain-threaded light at the window. Outside, the promenade lights blur through sheets of drizzle.]
[Sound: Rain tapping the awning, the low hiss of the grill, distant gulls muted by weather]
[Music: Sparse piano, minor key; a violin sustained beneath]

You push the door open and the bell tinkles like a small apology. The diner smells of strong coffee, frying onions, and the sea—an undercurrent of salt that clings to the collar of your jacket. Your hands are damp; the draft contract in your bag has absorbed a smear of the morning's fog. You pull it out now, and the pages tremble like thin bark.

Hana looks up from behind the counter as if she has been expecting you for hours. Her apron has a new stain near the hem; she smiles anyway, tired and fierce in the way she always is when the town needs a place to land.

Hana: "About time. You look like you carried weather in with you."

You: "I brought weather and words," you say. Your voice is low. You fold the packet open on the counter; coffee rings darken the corner of the first page. "Lux sent the draft."

Hana reaches over and presses a damp hand to the margin of the contract, as if she could feel the paper's temperature. The diner lights catch the edge of the ink; some clauses are printed in the small, legal geometry of certainty.

Hana: "Read it out," she says, but it's not that simple. She nods toward the stools where the regulars cluster—Tomas with his hands half-bent over a net, the lines of his face carved by years of weather. He does not look up, but his presence makes the room quieter.

[Visual: Tomas at the far stool, hands steady, mending a net; the twine whispers]
[Sound: The rasp of twine, Hana's spoon against ceramic]

Tomas: (quietly) "It's all language until it becomes land."

You feel the watch on your wrist—your brother's watch—like a small, constant pulse. It ticks a measure of time that has nothing to do with lawyers or investors. You think of nets left to dry, of cove mouths that remember names, of houses that sit low and stubborn against the shore. Each clause in that packet maps to a place that could be lost.

Hana takes the contract after a moment when you slide it to her. She reads quickly, lips moving. The sentences are beautiful in an engineered way—exact, cold, hungry for certainty. You know Lux writes certainty for a living; it is how she builds walls and investor decks. The language promises speed and protection. It also promises rights transfers and easements with the same breath.

Kaito comes in then, late enough to make the room shift. His jacket is flecked with salt and solder; he smells of damp metal and algae. He drops his bag on the floor and leans forward, his sea-green eyes finding yours with an urgency that sighs unspoken.

Kaito: "I heard you met her," he says, voice careful. "How badly did she—?"

You: "She sent a draft. It's... thorough." You force a smile. "And not generous in the places I care about."

Kaito's hand finds the counter near yours; for a moment it rests there, warm and steady. You notice the small sensor pendant blinking against his chest, its soft pulse like someone trying to be polite in the dark.

Kaito: "Read me the bits that matter," he says. He doesn't say 'we' yet—he's letting you lead. There's a map in his face: the engineer's search for a problem to fix, paired with something softer that wants the solution to be shared.

You: (quiet) "There's a clause that allows the company to ‘reassess habitation zones’ after construction. Another that gives control of shoreline access points to the contractor for fifty years."

Kaito's mouth thins. He flips through the paper as if trying to feel numbers in his fingertips.

Kaito: "That could displace fishermen—re-zoning can make a cove commercial or closed. Tomas—"

You: "—will get a letter someday saying the ebb makes his dock a hazard." Your voice tightens. "And then he will be offered relocation. A stipend. But not what's owed. Not the history."

Hana slams a saucer down with a soft thud—an anxious punctuation.

Hana: "This isn't just about living. It's about belonging. They'll sterilize the shoreline into tidy zones and call it safety."

Tomas finally looks up. His hands, the hands that have always known the sea's mood before anyone else, are raw at the knuckles.

Tomas: "They'll tell you it saves people. They tell us everything saves people. But it doesn't save the things I can't replace with concrete."

Kaito: "We can model the impact," he says. "I can run simulations, show trade-offs. We can propose hybrid designs—nature-based reinforcements, targeted engineering. It won't be as fast, but it can keep access."

You look at him. The weight of expectation sits on both of you. His optimism is not naive; it's calibrated, and yet tonight it feels like a small lamp under a storm-sky.

You: "She wants a yes for funding. If I stall, she threatens withdrawal. If I accept, we get immediate protection but at the cost of places that don't have a 'market value.'"

Kaito: "Then don't let her write the story alone. Push for legal clauses that guarantee local oversight—binding boards, veto rights, relocation guarantees that include cultural restitution."

Hana: "Get it in writing, Maya. Not promises—paper."

You: You hear the ring of certainty in both their voices, and the ring feels like a trap. Promises are cheap on company letterhead; they are cheaper in storms.

The night tilts toward a kind of pressure you have come to recognize—how responsibility becomes a physical thing you can almost touch. You feel tired in that way that is more than bone-deep: it's a fatigue of decisions deferred and costs counted twice.

Kaito watches you. He reaches for the counter again, then withdraws. His voice drops.

Kaito: "Whatever you do tomorrow at the forum, remember who the clauses will let stay—and who they will make strangers of."

You: "I know." The watch on your wrist knocks a soft rhythm—ten ticks to every breath. You think of your brother, of an empty chair at the table, of a storm that stole more than boats. The image crowds the edges of your resolve like moss covering stone.

Hana pours another coffee, and the steam fogs the glass where LuxCorp's distant lights smear like a promise in the rain.

Hana: "You could sign. You could fight. You could go loud and drag them into the light."

You: "Each option wounds different people," you say. "Signing buys immediacy, but not all that we love survives. Fighting to bind them keeps us in the driver's seat but risks losing the money. Going public forces accountability but could blow the deal entirely."

Tomas: "Sometimes the shore decides for us," he says. "Sometimes it's not a choice; it's a reckoning."

You look down at the contract. The black type seems to stare back, like a map that erases names. The diner fades around the edges into a horizon you can almost see: the forum tomorrow, a room full of faces, a microphone, a town that needs you to translate complex maps into a single human yes or no.

Your chest is tight. You are tired. The salt-air taste of negotiation is bitter and familiar.

[Music: Low cello; a single, aching note]

You fold the packet slowly, each crease a decision that might become a scar. You can feel the weight of the watch, the gravity of the room, the town holding its breath with you.

You stand at the bar. The diner seems to hold you in place, the community gathered like flotsam around a single current.

[Music: Pause, then a breath of low strings]

You realize: any clause you accept tonight will become a wound in Harborfall's living memory. Tomorrow the forum will ask you to choose how to wound it best.
