The door exhales the greenhouse's heat when you push it open—warm, humid breath of compost, brackish steam, and the sharp green of crushed leaves. Your scarf, patterned with tide charts and flecked with watercolor stains, dampens against your throat. You feel the weight of the clipboard like an extra rib: names, sketches, compromises penciled in the margins.

You step in. Kaito is already there—sleeves rolled, a sensor pendant swinging against his chest like a tiny heartbeat. His camera hangs cross-body; his hands smell faintly of solder and wet peat. When his sea-green eyes find you, a small, private curve of a smile crosses his face. For a second—that small, electric second—enough is not a theoretical concept but a real possibility. It sits between you like a plant waiting for water.

Kaito: "You made it." (He hears the tiredness in you but doesn't move to fix it. He offers the smile anyway.)

You: "Wasn't going to miss this." Your voice wants to be steadier than it is. You feel your brother's watch against your wrist—the old dive watch that keeps his memory close—and the tendon near your thumb pulses, a metronome of guilt and obligation.

Dr. Noor wheels in a tablet cart, screens reflecting low graphs and tide-readouts. Her eyes skim the data, then the room, offering both clinical certainty and human worry.

Dr. Noor: "Tide anomalies are trending worse than last month. Not catastrophic yet, but the variance window narrows. If we stagger the plantings and the berm raises, survival probability improves—statistically." (She taps a datapoint. Numbers are cold, but her voice doesn't let them flatten the people in the room.)

Tomas sits at the long workbench, hands folded over pages of careful, looping notes; his fishing hat is salt-bleached and comforting. A tin of Hana's cookies sits unassumingly between his elbows—half-eaten, sugar dust on the edge of a tray—familiar ritual amid high stakes.

Tomas: "Mangroves take time. Roots that anchor lives are not curves on a grant folder." (He looks at you with that slow, sure stare—meaning more than he says.)

Hana refills a chipped mug. Her apron smells of bay leaf and broth; she moves as if to lighten the atmosphere by habit, not by optimism.

Hana: "Someone needs to be practical and kind in equal measure." (She slides the mug toward you; the steam smells like home.)

Mayor Elena arrives last, blazer crisp even under the greenhouse heat. The municipal pin at her lapel gleams. She steps in with paper in one hand and the weight of decisions on her shoulders. When she sees the assembled group, the expression in her eyes folds inward—exhaustion meeting resolve.

Mayor Elena: "I've read both briefs. LuxCorp is offering immediate funding for an engineered wall and automated sluice controls. They guarantee protection to the high-water mark." (She pauses; you hear the pause like a hinge.) "But that comes with buyouts and rezoning. It would save lives, Elena—" (She corrects herself to your name as if to ground the abstract in you.) "—but it will redraw the town's map."

Lux's projection floats in your mind—a polished animation of chrome and water, promises with clean lines. You sense her presence the way fog suggests a shoreline: not solid, but shaping the space. The greenhouse, with its patched solar panels and sticky windows, feels at once like sanctuary and battleground.

You put the clipboard down and feel the rust on the metal clip bite your palm. You map the trade-offs aloud because you are a planner and because silence would let the polished solution swallow the messy one.

You: "Berms and mangrove transplants—those are community-built, low-cost, and maintain habitat. They ask for space, years, and a lot of volunteer labor. We ask people to give up plots, to accept risk while roots take hold. LuxCorp's wall is fast. It protects property lines quickly. But it sacrifices intertidal habitats, changes currents—" (Your voice tightens.) "—and it changes us."

Mayor Elena: "We don't have years if the models are right. We need something that prevents immediate displacement." (Her fingers worry the corner of a document.) "Council's under pressure from insurers. Voting blocks are nervous."

Kaito steps forward, not to argue but to stitch: his hands animate, small diagrams cut in the air. He proposes a middle scale—pilot sensors, early-warning networks, and targeted habitat restoration that prioritizes the most vulnerable coves.

Kaito: "If we run sensor pilots at key inlets we can get lead time on storm surges. We can combine that data with targeted berms—small, modular defenses that buy us time while roots establish. It's not an either/or. We can stagger interventions so we don't lose places that matter to people's livelihoods."

You want to believe him. You want to believe that technology can be gentle—that his prototypes can be both tool and covenant. His face is earnest; his voice trembles sometimes when he tries to persuade more than to explain. You see the memory of the research institute in the way he balances optimism with a fear of causing harm.

Dr. Noor: "The data support the hybrid approach as a scenario with favorable risk distribution. But the margin is thin. If storm intensity increases beyond modeled upper bounds, even modular approaches get overwhelmed."

Tomas: "And who pays for the pilots? Who subsidizes those who lose plots to berms? We ask people to take smaller risks—but not everyone can afford smaller risks."

Hana: "We do community evenings. We teach people how to tend the berms. But that's volunteer labor, and volunteers have families."

Silence pools in the greenhouse like run-off. A beam of light warms a single planter; inside, a salt-tolerant grass trembles. Your watch ticks. You can hear it—the soft mechanical metronome as if your brother were in the room timing you with old kindness and a grayer patience.
