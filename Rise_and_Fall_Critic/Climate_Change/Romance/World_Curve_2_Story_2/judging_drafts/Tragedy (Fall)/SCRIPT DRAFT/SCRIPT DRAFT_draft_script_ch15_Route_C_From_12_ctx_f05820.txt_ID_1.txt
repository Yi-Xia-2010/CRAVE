[Scene: Flooded Streets Outside Hana's Diner | Late Afternoon — Rain, Just Slowing]
[Music: Low cello and distant percussion — a slow, hollowing rhythm]
[Sound: Water lapping against curbs, the tired creak of sagging shutters, murmured sobs]
[Visual: Wet mementos in clear plastic sacks; neon diner's sign half-submerged, steam rising from a overturned pot; ash-gray sky reflecting in puddles like bruised mirrors]

You step through the diner's door on legs that still remember running. The bell doesn't ring — the mechanism is jammed with salt and silt — but the smell finds you first: coffee gone bitter with fear, boiled cabbage, and that metallic tang of seawater folded into everything.

Hana is behind the counter, sleeves rolled up, hands working a towel into the same rhythm as her breathing. She looks up, and for a second she is all light and motion and ridiculous, indomitable warmth. Then her face tightens into the look you know when she is counting losses aloud and pretending they're small to protect you.

Hana: "You came back. Good. How—" (her voice breaks and she swallows) "—how are you holding up, Maya?"

You feel the watch cold and heavy in your palm, the metal damp from where you pressed it to your chest all morning. It has stopped; the second hand is a small accusation. You slide it back onto your wrist because leaving it in the pocket would be like leaving the grief elsewhere. Wearing it is a confession.

You: "I don't know." (You force breath into the sentence.) "I thought— we thought exposing the contracts would protect people. It did stop them from selling us, but it also left us with nothing but our hands."

Hana folds the towel over her forearm. Her eyes are wet, stubborn. She gestures toward a folding table by the window where a group of neighbors sit with wet photographs and battered toys.

Hana: "You did the right thing. People need to know. But right and safe are not the same thing, not today."

A man at the table — Javier, who used to fix the boardwalk lamps — looks up. His fingers have left salt lines on the laminated menu.

Javier: "If LuxCorp had built the wall, maybe this would've been smaller." (He laughs without humor.) "Maybe we'd still have the east channel."

You want to answer, to push back with the legal filings, the clauses, the strings pulled behind the scenes. Instead you listen as the flood's logic presses into every sentence: money walked away because the market smelled risk; bureaucrats folded like wet paper. The moral victory sits like wet cloth over the town's shoulders: heavy, damp, useless warmth.

Hana moves between tables like a lighthouse keeper running shifts. She passes you a paper cup with something thick and savory. The diner's radio trills with a municipal broadcast you do not have the heart to tune out.

Hana: "Tomas was down by the pier again. He didn't say much. He just watched the boats be what they were: too light for this water."

You feel the name like a stone in your mouth. Tomas is there, an island of old rope and older weather, the kind of man whose grief is a slow, permanent thing and therefore more dangerous. He stands outside the window, hands stuffed into a heavy wax jacket that no longer keeps the salt out.

You step outside. Rain beads on your jacket, cold and precise. The promenade is a line of things that used to be normal: benches bolted crookedly, plaques washed clean of the dates they once marked. People drift past with sacks and blank looks like driftwood.

[Scene: Promenade Entrance | Late Afternoon — Tide Receding, Silt Exposed]
[Visual: The waterline crawls higher than you remember it ever did; a pale smear of foam clings to a railing; gulls pick at the ruins]
[Sound: The distant groan of a generator, a child's muffled cry, the steady slap of water against the embankment]

Tomas meets you without greeting. He watches the water with the same careful attention he used when you were a child and wanted to learn lines on the map. For a long while there is only the two of you and the tide.

Tomas: (He speaks as if he's naming the parts of a broken machine.) "They salted the streets in town proper, but the channels were left—" (He stops.) "We had warnings. Not the kind that showed on screens. Old currents don't negotiate with paperwork."

You say nothing because what you could say would be an argument with the weather. Instead you let the ache behind your ribs rise and become something like language.

You: "I thought the referendum was the town's voice. We proved we could be heard."

Tomas: (He finally looks at you.) "You made a voice. The world answered with silence and red pens. That is not your fault, Maya. It just—" (He searches for a word.) "—it just shows how loud the wrong people can be."

He turns away then, the way an old fisherman does when something is too big to lift. The smell of diesel and kelp clings to him. He will not say more. You don't push. He doesn't need you to explain; he needs time to look at what the sea took.

You turn back toward Hana's where the light leaks out like a fragile treaty. The diner's door swings on a hinge that groans of too many late nights. Inside, the room has filled with faces you know too well; people you used to joke with now measure their losses like inventory.

Mayor Elena stands at the back, a municipal pin darkened by salt. She meets your eyes with a mixture of exhaustion and something like resolve that's been forced into the shape of resignation.

Mayor Elena: "We won the case." (She says it plainly, as if saying it will make it true.) "But the investors spooked. Pipeline funding dried up. Grants went on hold. We have emergency funds, but it's string-and-prayer level."

You: "We traded one danger for another. If we risked silence for resources, what were we really protecting?"

Mayor Elena folds her hands. Her ring glints like an inflexible axis.

Mayor Elena: "You weren't wrong to open the contracts, Maya. People deserved to know what they were signing for. But I also —" (She exhales.) "I also underestimated how quickly the money would evaporate. How fragile the system is when public trust fractures."

You and Elena argue in small circles — legal language and municipal responsibilities — until words feel like pebbles against a tide that keeps coming. The conversation loops over emergency loans, volunteer brigades, the limits of insurance. No neat answers emerge. All of them smell faintly of a stop-gap measure that will not hold a real storm.

[Scene: Hana's Diner — Doorway | Late Afternoon]
[Sound: A muted knock. The room goes still.]

The door opens and Kaito is there, rain traced through his hair like a halo of bad weather. He looks ten years older and ten minutes the same person whose soft humor used to unthread your worst assumptions.

Kaito: (His voice thin.) "Maya."

You: (You step forward before you know whether it is what you want.) "Kaito —"

He doesn't wait for a hug. He just holds the edge of the counter and looks at the room, at the sacks, at the photographs. The sensors at his shoulder — the small blinking pendant — are gone. In his hands is a small bag of equipment and a rolled tarp. He set it on Hana's chair like a pledge you both know how to read without words.

Kaito: "I'm taking the field kits and the deployables. It'll make no sense here if I leave them idle and the funding's gone. I can carry the data, Maya. I can try to turn what happened into lessons that won't just be memorials."

You want to tell him to stay, to plant his feet here and help reopen the coves, to share the burden of other people's grief. You want to tell him that leaving will feel like running away. Instead he gives you something like the most honest apology you have ever seen: not a theatrical apology, but the kind that is an imploded weather report — the facts of what he couldn't do, the compassion for the rest.

Kaito: "I'm sorry I couldn't stop this. I'm sorry the pilot — the sensors — weren't enough. I thought proof would be power. It wasn't. Not when the money left before the machines even turned."

You: "You didn't make the money walk away." (You want to say so fiercely that it's a rebuke. It only comes out as a thin hope.) "You tried to build something that could help."

Kaito: (He looks at you, eyes hollow in a way that makes your chest go cold.) "I will keep going. I won't let this be just a lesson for Harborfall. I can take the models, the readings, the failure modes. We can—" (He struggles.) "—we can try to build better protocols. But I—" (He stops.) "—I have to leave. For now."

You: "Will you—" (You don't finish. The question is always the same: stay and rebuild, or leave to fight elsewhere and maybe never come back?)

Kaito's mouth presses into a line. Then he does what he always does when the words are too big and the world too loud: he reaches out, brisk and clumsy, and pins a small sensor pendant you recognize to the inside of your jacket.

Kaito: "For you. If you need it on the berms. If you need—" (He searches again and finds something steadier.) "If you need someone to prove that these things can work, I will. But I can't do it from here alone. Not now."

He lifts his bag, shoulders it like a burden he will make into something useful. He looks at you, and for a second the storm seems to answer him, its roar a kind of reluctant benediction. He walks out into the rain.

[Scene: Flooded Neighborhood — Early Evening | Dusk Leaning Heavy]
[Visual: Homes half-submerged, curtains clinging to furniture, potted plants floating like tiny islands of hope; children's drawings plastered to a washed-out noticeboard]
[Sound: Siren fade into the distance, older radio chatter, someone's muffled sob close by]

You walk the streets you grew up in, each house a catalogue of losses: an old boat lean-to canted at a ridiculous angle, salt lines snaking up clapboard like calligraphy, an overturned bicycle caught on a fence. People sit on stoops with sheets wrapped around shoulders, faces stunned into the same palette of disbelief. There are hands to hold, names to remember, and the monotonous work of salvaging what can be salvaged.

A child presses a soggy drawing into your hand — a wobbly rainbow over a boxy house. You fold it into your pocket like contraband. It smells faintly of crayons and seawater. You think of the referendum’s language, the proud lines you read into microphones, the moral clarity you felt under the greenhouse glass. That clarity is now an ember under landfill of what you didn't foresee.

You find a small group near the promenade steps where the flood’s first wall broke through an under-reinforced channel. Tomas stands there, shoulders hunched, talking softly to no one in particular as if naming the loss will keep the shapes of things intact.

Tomas: "We can build it back. But we need hands. Not the kind that come with glossy ads. The kind that come knowing how to bend a nail without a video."

You: "I will stay." (The words rise steady and cold. They are not a vow. They are a necessity.) "I will stay and help rebuild. We will ask for aid differently. We will gather what we have."

He looks at you, and the look is not triumph. It is not even relief. It is recognition. You both know the truth: staying will not fix everything. Staying will mean nights where you count the steps of children in your head and mornings where a list of repairs is the only prayer. It will mean arguing with officials, begging for materials, bargaining with the kind of bureaucracy that runs on forms and coffee and calcified pity.

You secure your brother's watch tighter against your wrist. It is cold and stopped, but when your thumb brushes against it you feel a kind of steady weight that is not time but promise.

[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Night — The Dark Washing In]
[Music: Sparse piano, a single violin note like a sigh]
[Visual: The lighthouse, half-built scaffolding cut against lightning-breath clouds; the promenade slick and gleaming; a lone lamp flickering]

You step down to the edge where the boards meet the sea and let the dark wash over you. The sound is a plain thing — water opening and closing like a mouth. The town behind you murmurs and shelters; people gather in shifts to tend to the injured, to inventory, to sing broken lullabies.

You think of what the referendum bought: a truth, a public airing of a company's ambitions, the cleansing glare of sunlight on hidden contracts. You also think of what was lost when the market recoiled — projects that might have held a fraction of the tide, materials that never arrived, lives that were not saved.

You feel the referendum as a pyrrhic victory: morally sound and tactically catastrophic. You taste salt and shame together, indistinguishable except by degrees.

Mayor Elena's earlier words loop in your head, a kind of municipal lament: "You weren't wrong to open the contracts," she said. "People deserved to know what they were signing for." But the rest of the sentence — the part about the money leaving, the part about how fragile systems are to attention and fear — hangs like a sign you cannot take down.

You press your palm to the stopped face of your brother’s watch, the cold metal an accusation and a comfort. You make a decision with the heavy clarity of someone who has learned the high cost of both action and silence.

You will not leave Harborfall. You will not watch the town become a footnote in someone else's white paper. You will stay and organize the small, stubborn repairs that keep people warm and boats right. You will broker volunteer brigades that sew, stack, and shore up. You will talk to donors with fewer illusions and more patience. You will hold people when they need it and argue when necessary. You will let your grief become labor.

There will be court hearings to revisit, grants to wrangle, a press cycle that will move on. There will also be the smell of wet wood and the taste of raw, honest work. There will be nights you don't sleep. There will be hands that call you by your name in the dark and mean it.

You lift your face to the low cloud and let the rain taste like absolution that isn't total, a kind of grace that requires digging. The dark around you is not a blankness but a field to be planted.

You inhale. Harborfall is broken in ways that cannot be erased. It is also still here.

You will stay.
