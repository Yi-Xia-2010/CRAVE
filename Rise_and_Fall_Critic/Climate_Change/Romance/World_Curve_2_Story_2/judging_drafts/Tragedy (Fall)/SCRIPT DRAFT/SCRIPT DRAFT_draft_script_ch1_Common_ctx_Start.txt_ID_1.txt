[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Dawn]
[Visual: Pale fog rolling over wet timber; LuxCorp banners a clinical teal at the far plaza]
[Music: Low, austere piano with distant whale-bell undertone]
[Sound: Gulls calling, a slow slap of tide against piling]

You wake before dawn because the town has always woken you that way — not politely, but by habit and obligation. Your hand finds the battered dive watch without thinking; cold metal under skin, the glass scarred with a hairline fracture where you once jammed it against a fallen plank. It reads seconds like a small insistence. Your brother's name is not on the face, but the weight of his absence is an indent behind your ribs you pretend is muscle.

You twist your hair back with the other hand and feel the teal braid there, braided in a small, private ceremony last spring when you swore you'd make time mean something useful. The rope belt at your waist feels wrong until you cinch it tight — knotting is a habit, a tiny promise that nothing will slip.

Sea air fills your lungs: salt, algae, oil, and rust. The promenade boards are still slick with nighttime mist. Retrofit lamps along the rail blink faintly in their staggered rhythm; the light tastes like low battery and municipal hope. Farther down, the LuxCorp banners cut the fog with a sterile stripe of teal, a geometry of control that makes your palms itch. Their simulations will glow on a kiosk later today, whole landscapes of promised safety rendered as neat, clean lines. For now the town is uneven: patched planks, a crooked handrail, coffee cooling in paper cups beside picnic benches.

You open your waterproof notebook and run a finger down the list you've kept since last year: sandbag inspections, seed runs to the rooftop greenhouse, elder check-ins, the names of volunteers who can lift in a storm. The ink is smudged from the occasional rain; a corner bears the smoothed impression of a thumb that refuses to let go of the past.

In the quiet between gulls, you hear Kaito's voice as if through an old radio — not present, only the memory of the tone he uses when he's outlining data. Gentle, analytical, like a tool calibrating itself. "If we run an array along the berm and cross-validate with the estuary sensors, we can get a twenty-four-hour lead on surge patterns," it says in your head. You imagine the prototype sensor pendant he brought back last month, its soft blue blink a promise. He trusts numbers; he trusts designs. He trusts, it seems, that measurable things can be made to keep people safe. You want to believe him. You also remember the times when numbers met deadlines and lives fell between them.

A dull ache sits under your left rib — more than memory this morning, like a tide that won't quit. The ache steadies you and warns you both. You run through the logistics like a checklist in your head, trying to turn fear into action.

You pass Hana's window when the diner's blinds are still half-closed. Steam ghosts the glass; someone has already set a pot to simmer. The smell of onion and broth pulls you, a domestic gravity that has always anchored Harborfall. Hana appears at the counter, one hand wiping a bowl, the other already tuning toward the town's rhythm. She sees you and lifts a hand in a small wave.

Hana: "You're early, Mays. Soup's on. Thought you might be on the sandbag roster today?"

You pause at the doorframe, the bell above the diner's door muted by the fog. Inside, the light is a warm yellow against your jacket. You want the pause of soup, the smallness of a table and a window. But the list in your head jitters like a loose wire.

You: "Just passing. Need to check the west berm before the tide turns. How's the morning crowd?"

Hana (tilting her head, smile a hair too bright): "Mayor's office called — said LuxCorp folks will have a team doing measurements near the plaza. They want to show the simulation to the council this afternoon." (She lowers her voice.) "Mayor's fretting. Tomas looked like he hadn't slept."

You let a short laugh escape that tastes more like a sigh. "Of course they are."

Hana watches you, eyes quick, as if she's calculating the angle of your shoulders. She knows you well enough to know when you're bracing.

Hana: "Take soup. On me. You look like you'll eat it standing up and still roll into the tide."

You: (soft, almost conceding) "Alright. One cup."

You step in. The diner's warmth hits you like an apology. The counter is patched wood; a jar of toothpicks and a stack of mismatched mugs. Hana ladles broth into a steaming cup and slides it across with a practiced ease that feels like comfort, and also like duty—small kindness as civic infrastructure.

You take the cup anyway — whether you drink it or carry it, the act is small ritual. Hana watches you like an anchor. The town needs anchors today.

Outside, the pier is a low silhouette; Tomas stands there, the slow certainty of him like a keel in a small boat. His waxed jacket is the color of well-aged hulls; his hands, when they move, betray a lifetime of rope and muscle. He tips his chin at you.

Tomas: "Storms don't come the same as before," he says without preamble. "You feel that? The swell used to be more like a sermon; now it's a calculation. Not worse, not better — just… different. Sneaks in."

You walk toward him, boots making quiet splashes. His voice is a slow tide, and his eyes, for a flicker, go past you to something only he remembers.

You: "We have more data now. Sensors help. But the sea still decides how close we get."

Tomas (snorting softly): "Data makes a fine picture, girl, but pictures don't pull nets. They don't mend boats. You'll need both. You know that."

There's something in the way he says "you'll" that is not advisory, but a handing over. He doesn't speak your brother's name. He doesn't have to. Harborfall's losses are shared in long looks and the way someone's silence fills a doorway.

You: "I know. I just —" You stop, because the ache under your ribs speaks louder than any plan. "I don't want us to be late."

Tomas studies your face like a captain reading weather. His hands are steady on the rail.

Tomas: "None of us want to be late. That's why we keep early. That's why we mend. But don't carry the town like it's a stone in your pocket you can't set down. It will sink you before it sinks the shore."

You: "I don't have the luxury of setting it down."

Tomas: (a slow, patient exhale) "Maybe not. But you can let others hold some edges. Let Kaito's numbers run, let Hana feed us, let the council make bad calls sometimes. We patch, we learn. Harborfall's been stubborn longer than any of us."

You want to argue — that LuxCorp's neat lines will carve the town into someone else's plan, that every "let them help" came with a bill and a map that pushed families off their waterfront. But Tomas's hands are already on the rope ladder to his small skiff, and his eyes are on the water. He does not press you. Not today.

As you walk away, Kaito's imagined voice returns, softer this time. "If we integrate sensor arrays with community reports, we get situational awareness grounded in experience. Not just models, Mays — living, breathing verification."

You close your eyes for a second and feel a memory: rain hammering a roof, a younger brother laughing because he wanted to be on the water more than anything. The memory is sharp and then it blurs. You thumb the edge of the notebook where his name is not written and feel the dent of that absence.

You force your shoulders back into planning posture. Salt flakes on your jacket seam. The promenade bends toward the plaza and toward the rooftop greenhouse where you'll run a seed drop between inspections. The greenhouse sits like a glass heart half-hidden by municipal berms — a small refuge you've fought for. You think of the seedlings, of volunteers who will wait to set them in raised beds, of the children who press mittened noses to the glass after school. The city might hand over an engineered kurt wall; the plants will not stop loving the salt, they adapt, and if the town can adapt with them, maybe the loss will be less sharp.

The LuxCorp banners hang like promises and warnings both. Someone will be in the plaza with a holo by noon, showing a neat, reassuring simulation. Mayor Elena will listen. The council will ask questions that sound like policy but are really about money and legacy. You think of what to say. You think of what to do.

A gull shrieks, closer this time, a raw sound that eats the pause.

You slow at the intersection where the promenade splits — one path leads up to the public stair toward the rooftop greenhouse and municipal gardens, the other down toward the west berm and the line of sandbags that have already earned their second coats of tarpaulin. Both routes feel urgent. Both routes feel necessary. Your notebook is in your hand. Your watch ticks.

You feel the weight of the season like a tide at your chest: decisions gathering like stormclouds, sympathetic and relentless. There is a practicality to choosing first: check the berm and be certain the town's immediate defenses hold. There is also strategy: meet Kaito at the greenhouse now and get his sensors integrated before the day's council presentation; that could shift the conversation from theater to evidence. You reach a boundary where action and principle touch, and the place burns in the same color as the LuxCorp banners — teal, clinical, impossible to ignore.

You stand there, wind trying to slit at your scarf, and for a moment the town's history feels like a fan in your hands: you can try to fold it back into neatness, but you always end up with more pieces than you started.

You breathe. You inventory again: sandbags (team on call), seedlings (volunteers waiting), Kaito (sensors poised), Mayor's meeting (midday). Each item is a small claim on you. Each claim pulls equally.

You look at the greenhouse stair and then back to the berm. Your watch ticks; the gulls have moved on. The ache under your rib tightens as if understanding the question.

You are aware, with a steadiness that is almost cruel, that today will ask decisions you cannot postpone. You are small and steady and the sea is larger still.
