[Scene: Harborfall Promenade | Morning]
[Visual: Damp wooden planks, gulls circling; banners for "Harborfall Votes!" fluttering]
[Sound: Distant motor hum, gull cries, a radio playing a muted civic jingle]
[Music: Low, sparse piano with a somber undercurrent]

You taste salt on the air and the edges of last night's rain in the boards beneath your boots. The referendum is no longer a phrase in a meeting; it's a stack of wet petitions under your elbow, a line of names curling in blue ink across a page. You remind yourself—again—that this is what you chose. That choice sits in the pocket of your jacket like a stone.

Your brother's dive watch presses against your ribs through the coat. It has the faint scuff from when he laughed and caught a wave it shouldn't have; sometimes it feels like an accusation. You let your fingers close over it and feel the weight anchor you and the ache under it—old grief braided with stubborn purpose.

Hana is there by the rail, hands stuffed in her apron pockets, hair still damp from washing the coffee urn. She hands you a laminated list of streets and a thermos that smells of boiled milk and cinnamon.

Hana: "You look like you didn't sleep." (she smiles, but it doesn't reach her eyes) "Good. We need you awake. Tomas is down at the pier waiting for you to take 'the old route'—he says the fishermen won't sign if you come all polished."

You nod. Words sit in your throat—prepared arguments, urgent facts, the small, human reasons that don't fit on a pamphlet. You fold them over in your head and push them out: phrases that are honest and short.

You: "Give me Tomas's list, and Hana—keep the diner on emergency alert. If people need respite, they go there first."

Hana slaps the thermos into your hand with a laugh that trembles. "You tell me what to do and I do it. Always have. But you—" She looks at your watch, the way the light catches its scar. "—you don't have to do everything alone, Maya."

You say nothing for a beat, because saying it out loud sounds like a promise you might break.

Hana watches you choose and tucks the unused pamphlets into an organizer like a woman mending a sleeve.

You make for Tomas's boat. The pier smells of tar and old nets, diesel, and the sweet, rotten tang of seaweed. Tomas stands with a stool as steady as a moored mast, his hands folded. He scans you with that slow appraisal he reserves for storms and for people he's known since the town had more horses than cars.

Tomas: "You pick the roads, kid. But remember—every promise you make will be braided into someone's livelihood. Lux's adverts—they're loud. They paint us safe with steel and lights. People want calm roofs and dry floors, not lectures."

You: "I know. I don't think safety isn't important. I just—" You search for the balance between mortgage schedules and marshland restoration. "I think a vote gives the town agency. If we win the referendum, the change belongs to Harborfall. We make the rules."

Tomas grunts, not convinced, but he signs your petition after you remind him that the vote itself won't stop Lux from building—it's a mandate of consent, a political lever.

Tomas: "Make sure the fishermen can speak. Make sure they know what they'll lose if decisions are made with blueprints instead of nets."

You feel it: the tug of responsibility. The referendum is a commitment to the slow, messy work of democracy. It will not keep the sea from rising tomorrow.

[Scene: Hana's Diner | Midday]
[Visual: Steam fogging the glass, rows of pamphlets on the counter, a corkboard full of handwritten shifts and volunteer names]
[Sound: Chop of a spatula, clink of cups, low murmur of volunteers folding leaflets]
[Music: Quiet strings—tight, frayed]

Inside the diner, the air is warm with soup and the friction of too many bodies in a small room. The corkboard of the campaign reads like a map of community life: routes, elderly check-ins, a schedule for the loudspeaker van. Volunteers bend over stacks of flyers with ink-smudged fingers. The fluorescent light hums low and unsteady, like a circuit under strain.

Mayor Elena steps in, blazer slightly wrinkled, carrying a municipal folder that smells faintly of municipal varnish and worry.

Mayor Elena: "Maya, thank you for doing this. We need the referendum to be clean. The law is on our side if we follow procedure, but LuxCorp's pockets make their messaging loud."

You: "Will the town hall be neutral? Will the municipal site publish both sides?"

Mayor Elena: (she exhales) "I'm trying. The budget won't buy airtime, but I'll ensure equal access to facilities. I just—" She searches for a conciliatory word. "I can't promise the playing field won't tilt."

You look at her, thinking of all the times she's had to choose between budgets and people. You know why she hesitates—votes cost money; fairness sometimes costs political capital.

Kaito's voice comes over your sat-phone, clipped and anxious. The field lab's rain-splattered background is audible even through the speaker.

Kaito: "They're running geo-sim ads—rendered scenes of a concrete wall holding back a perfect storm. People respond to visuals. Facts alone won't cut it, Maya; if you don't fight visuals, you lose hearts."

You (to Kaito): "What about a counter-visual? A day-in-the-life of the marshkeepers?"

Kaito: (pauses) "We can try. But Lux has a creative studio. They hire narrative doctors. Be careful—if we make this personal, we might emotionally alienate people who need to feel safe now. If we don't, we're outshone."

You think of the greenhouse seedlings and the muddy work that saves land over decades. You think of Tomas's nets and Hana's soups. You think of your brother's watch and the image of a cliff-edge home taken by a surprise tide. The town deserves both safety and meaning, you tell yourself, but the sentence feels incomplete.

Hana squeezes your wrist; her calluses are warm and tired.

Hana: "We still have the loudspeaker van. People listen to stories at the dinner table. We can make the visuals ourselves—raw, honest."

Mayor Elena: "If you put out a narrative, be precise. Accusatory exposes could get our council sued, and the last thing the referendum needs is a legal distraction."

You close your eyes for a second and picture the town as it was when you were small: rope swings, salt-smelling blankets drying on rails, neighbors dropping by with pie. That memory is a fragile engine for what you're doing. You tell yourself that principle matters—still—but the voice in your head that knows the sound of the sea when it gets angry warns you about time.

Kaito: (softly, into the phone) "You're running on heart, and that's not bad. Just—don't let being right be the same as being effective."

You: "Kaito, what would you do if you were here?"

Kaito: "I'd split the work. Let science and story have separate lanes and coordinate their timing. But... and this is the thing—time is a currency we don't have much of. Lux is buying airtime this week. They'll flood perception before our grassroots feels complete."

You tuck Kaito's warning into the pocket of decisions that feels heavier by the minute.

[Scene: Market Square | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A temporary stage set with folding chairs, a plexiglass lectern smeared from rain; a LuxCorp digital billboard across the plaza]
[Sound: Murmuring crowds, snippets of opposing chants, a polished ad voice from the billboard]
[Music: Low percussion with an unsettled woodwind motif]

The square is split down the middle like a tide line. On one side, volunteers with hand-lettered signs and coffee-stained clipboards chant for "Local Control Now!" On the other, a projection of LuxCorp's ad loops on a tall screen—slick renderings of a shining seawall, families with dry feet, children riding bikes on protected promenades. The ad voice is smooth, practiced, practiced in safety.

Billboard Voice (LuxCorp Ad): "Harborfall deserves certainty. Our engineered shield—proven, funded, immediate. Vote yes, vote for protection."

A ripple of applause follows the clip. The crowd shifts; you feel the air thicken with competing desires. A woman in a soaked coat approaches you, eyes frantic.

Woman: "My son's job's at the boatyard. If the feds come in and change the coves—what happens to our rent? To our work?"

You: "We will make that part of the discussion. The referendum isn't a blank check. We're fighting to ensure local conditions and protections. But I need you to sign the petition first so we can get this on the ballot."

She hesitates, hands hovering over the pen like it's a key. You can see the calculation: immediate security or uncertain self-determination.

Nearby, a LuxCorp rep in a storm-blue coat watches with a catalog's smile, speaking into a slim earpiece. Their presence is a reminder of money and timelines and investors who expect returns.

Mayor Elena arrives, breathless. She moves through the crowd and finds you.

Mayor Elena: "I've arranged time for both sides at the forum," she says. "We can demand transparency from LuxCorp—financial disclosures, timeline contingencies. But Maya—you must be ready. If the referendum weakens confidence for investors, jobs could leave—and quick stability will be harder to sell."

You think about that: the way fear pushes people into promises that feel immediate. The referendum can be a sword or a shield; it can cut ties to paternalistic choices or sever sources of employment.

Kaito pulls you aside for a few words. He keeps his voice low, the field lab's rain still audible behind him through the call.

Kaito: "If the referendum goes through, it gives you legitimacy. If you win, you make the rules. If you lose, LuxCorp uses the vote as a lever—'the town chose to keep options open'—and they'll escalate investments anyway. Both outcomes carry real harm."

You: "So what do you want me to do? Let them do away with us so everyone's feet stay dry for a year?"

Kaito: (frustrated) "No. I'm saying be tactical. Your moral clarity is fierce, Maya. It's also a target. Use it, but don't let it leave you exposed."

Your voice is low, the truth of the evening settling like fog.

You: "I know there is no perfect answer. I know choosing this means sacrificing speed for consent. I know some will call that naive, and some will call it brave."

A child runs past, clutching a paper boat with a crayon mast. The boat is soaked, but the child's laugh slices the weight of everything for a second and then the weight returns heavier.

The volunteers' energy frays where LuxCorp's ads play on loop. People start to argue—not in the communal, seventies-town way you remember, but with the sharp edges of fear. A heated exchange erupts near the stage between a man shouting for immediate security and an elderly woman who refuses to hand over control to an outside firm.

You step between them, and your palms feel slick with rain and old sweat. You can manage the crowd. You cannot manage the timeline.

You walk to the lectern, the microphone cold and indifferent under your fingers. The square recoils and leans with you. The referendum is now a thing that will be argued in town halls and on slick screens and across kitchen tables.

Your mind sorts the options into neat costs and consequences, but your chest knots with the ethical contour of the choice: how to frame a civic commitment that will be sustainable, fair, and viable in the face of money.

You think of Tomas's nets, Hana's diner, the greenhouse seedlings and Kaito's sensors—everything braided together into one messy plan that might or might not hold when the next storm comes.

The night presses in. The LuxCorp billboard flickers its promise. Somewhere, a group of volunteers re-wraps a leaflet with tired hands. Your watch ticks under your wrist, each second a small, accusing drum.

You realize the referendum is both a line in the sand and a battlefield. You can shape it—anchor it in ecological stewardship, attack LuxCorp's contracts, or try to stitch a fragile compromise that might make both sides nod and both sides still grumble.

You breathe out and let all the small noises fill your ears: the hiss of the crowd, the soft exhale of the ocean, Kaito's paused call, Tomas's thin, resigned whistle from the pier.

Everything tilts because of the way you choose to present this fight. Whatever route you take will paint you—Maya Inoue—as steadfast, conciliatory, or willing to rip open the town's wound for truth.

You know you must decide how the referendum will be framed. The square waits, and your chest tightens with the falling weight of responsibility.
