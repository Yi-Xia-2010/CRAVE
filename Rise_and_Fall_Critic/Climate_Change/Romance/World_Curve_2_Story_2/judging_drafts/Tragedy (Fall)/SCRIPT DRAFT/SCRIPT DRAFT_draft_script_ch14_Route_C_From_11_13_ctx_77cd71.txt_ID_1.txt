[Scene: Mangrove Estuary | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Low amber light slants through broken clouds; the walkways arc like tired ribs over black water]
[Sound: A hollow, slow thud of distant waves; barnacles rasp as the tide pulls back]
[Music: Sparse cello, high single violin note repeating]
You step off the boardwalk into the knee-deep muck because you cannot bear to only look anymore. Salt stings the backs of your hands. The mud gives and holds around your boots, cold and obedient. Seedlings—small, green and limp where they should be upright—float like overturned promises in the shallow eddies. Their roots, once still as breath, have loosened and drifted in a late-season surge the models barely mentioned and no meeting anticipated.

You think of lists: grant applications, planting schedules, volunteers' names in your battered notebook. The lists are helpful; they are scaffolding. They do not stop the sea.

A gull arches overhead, its cry thin and sharp. You try to remember how many times Tomas told you the estuary could take a beating and still come back, but this is not a single beating. This is a pulling apart, a slow unravelling you can feel in your teeth.

Tomas appears at the end of the walkway, salt on his cuffs, hands in the pockets of a jacket that has seen worse winters than this. He watches you in silence long enough that you feel the weight of the town in his eyes.

Tomas: "They weren't set deep enough. The surge—she took 'em like a hand taking at a net."
You: "We planted on schedule. We followed the volunteers' timing."
Tomas: (soft laugh, not quite humor) "Schedules don't wrestle tides. You know that, Maya."
You: (because you are tired of swallowing words) "I know. I know the models. I know the planting windows. I also know that seedlings don't float on policy."

He studies your face as if trying to read an old map. You think of your brother—how easily the sea took what you thought was steady. The old watch on your wrist ticks at a rhythm that no longer matches the tides.

[Scene: Eelgrass Beds / Harbor Promenade | Early Evening]
[Visual: A line of gray sky reflected in flat water; engineered rocks stand like foreign teeth at the low tide mark]
[Sound: Water whispering; distant hammers still working on raised houses]
[Music: Low piano, two notes repeating like a heartbeat slowed]
You walk the promenade, salt-scorched wood underfoot, the plaques dull with rain. Where eelgrass beds used to breathe with a green, fetid life, there are ragged edges and a new trench cut at the line where the contractors piled rock and armor. The engineered placements—meant as holding anchors—have, in certain stretches, redirected the current into neighboring coves. Eelgrass that once cushioned the shore thins; shoals appear overnight; a long-fished cove has become a flat of stealing water.

Mayor Elena stands at the edge of a crowd, coat buttoned close against wind and policy. She reads numbers into the air like liturgy.

Mayor Elena: "We have an emergency relocation fund. It will help some, but not all. We are prioritizing families with immediate risk and the elderly. We will continue to apply for supplemental aid—"
You: (interrupting because speaking feels like making a place for grief) "How many is 'some'?"
Mayor Elena: (voice tight) "Enough to start. Not enough to finish. Not without more commitments." (She looks at you then, a mixture of municipal burden and human fatigue.) "Maya, we need your help to triage—who goes first, who stays."

You think of houses raised and ribboned with new pilings and of others sagging, their paint run like tears. The relocation fund helps roofs and concrete; it cannot lift someone’s life back into the shape it had.

Hana passes a tray of bowls with her shoulders hunched—there are fewer conversations at lunch now, the diner corners emptier. She meets your eyes and offers a small, tired smile.

Hana: "People keep coming to the counter but leave earlier. They say 'I'm fine' like it's a costume they can put back on tomorrow."
You: "You're not fine, Hana."
Hana: "Neither are you. Pass the salt."

[Scene: Kaito's Field Lab (Workshop) | Night]
[Visual: Fluorescent lamps cut the dark; sensor arrays dangle like caught stars; glass jars rattle faintly on a shelf]
[Sound: The low hum of refrigeration; a notification ping in the corner]
[Music: Tensioned strings, a steady undercurrent of unresolved chords]
You find Kaito hunched over a tray of sensor readouts, the glow of the display painting his face a sea-green that matches his eyes. He has been running simulations, looking for correlations between ragged eelgrass and the engineered placements. His face is drawn in a way that makes you want to soothe him—then angry because coaxing isn't the answer.

Kaito: "You pushed for the referendum to keep decisions local. You wanted the town to have ownership. That's what we fought for."
You: "So you don't think that ownership includes saying 'no' to outside offers that would have spared this—"
Kaito: (cuts you off, voice low) "It's not about sparing. It's about scale and timing. There are towns two counties over already underwater. I can't—" (he rubs his face) "I can't only look inward."

The argument coils—one of your worst fears voiced: that your principles might have been a luxury in the face of a faster, nastier sea.

You: "And you? You argued for pilots—small-scale, careful—because you wanted to prove we could do it right. Now? Now the pilot couldn't hold when the larger moves changed the seam."
Kaito: "I haven't stopped believing in the careful way. But careful takes time, and the sea has different deadlines. I thought we could do both—tethers and trust. Maybe I was naïve."

He stands, the tray of readouts forgotten, and shuts the lab light in a neat, decisive motion. For a breath, you imagine stepping forward, wrapping your hands around his shoulders. Instead, you feel the weights of many people's needs between you.

Kaito: (softly) "I'm leaving for a regional conference. I have contacts there—other towns, other teams. If we can make our lessons portable, we might prevent this elsewhere."
You: (the old ache twists) "You're leaving here—now—when we still have hearings, when Tomas needs boats—"
Kaito: "I need to do what I can, Maya. I can't be effective for everyone by staying here and being crushed. I have to try to help more people. You understand that, don't you?"

His eyes are an open ledger: earnestness, apology, a plea to be believed. You see him as both partner in problem and separate trajectory.

Kaito: "I care about Harborfall. I care about you. But I also have to act on what my work can do."

You both talk in circles until exhaustion flattens the edges. He leaves with a packed satchel. The lab door closes with a sound like a final page turning.

[Scene: Storm-broken Lighthouse | Dusk]
[Visual: The lamp sits scaffolded and quiet; lightning pinpricks the horizon; your brother's watch—stopped—catches the last glow]
[Sound: Wind like distant voices; the lighthouse stones hiss as water retreats]
[Music: Single piano note; then a hush]
You climb the lighthouse steps and feel the wind wedge into your jacket. The teal streak at your temple flips in the gust like a flag. Below, Harborfall looks smaller and again unbearably intimate: tarps, raised foundations, a perimeter where sand meets the new cuts in shoreline. Tomas's boat bobs gently at its mooring, nets hanging like impossible answers.

You take your brother's watch from your wrist. Its hands are frozen—an exact time that will never arrive again. You think about all the measures you drew up to keep time with the tides, all the meetings, all the small, loving acts of labor in mangrove beds, the late-night audits, the public forums. You hold the watch to your ear and hear nothing but wind.

Memory threads through the present. You remember his laughter over a busted tackle box, the night he taught you how to read a calm in a storm. That memory aches now in a very particular way: not only loss but the price of being the one left to count what remains.

Mayor Elena called earlier to say the relocation fund would be deployed; LuxCorp sent a brief update about adaptive contracts and conditional funding; Hanna left a thermos at the diner for you with two spoonfuls of clam chowder and a note that said, simply, "Stay. Eat." Tomas sent a text with an anchor emoji and a single line: "Nets still need mending."

You are exhausted enough that your ideals scab into practicalities. You also feel the sharper edge of responsibility: people are casting glances at you that look for anchors where you only have two hands.

You think of Kaito's retreat and the towns he might save with his conference contacts. You think of LuxCorp's ledger and the houses it can lift if it accepts more local oversight. You think of the mangrove seedlings you tried to tuck back into mud and the eelgrass you could not stitch to the shore.

You close your eyes and let the wind take a sound from your throat—half-sob, half-plan. The grief is heavy but not paralyzing. It limbers into something like intention.

You press your forehead to the wind-sanded glass of the lighthouse and let the salt find the lines of your face. To measure is human: you have been measuring loss—houses, eelgrass, trust—countering every number with another figure, trying to add up to a whole. Measurement is also a map, and every map you trace is only as true as the hands willing to follow it.

You feel the smallness of what one person can do and the stubborn, terrible stretch of what you must try to hold: community, memory, the fragile scaffold of a coastline.

A wave of tiredness goes through you like a prayer. You breathe in the cold; you taste iron on the air. Your plans—small, stubborn, imperfect—sit like stones in your jacket pocket, and you know there are more stones to place.

You are at a decision point that will shape the months to come: whether to commit every ounce of yourself to stay and rebuild Harborfall from within—continuing the slow stitching of ecological repair and civic pressure; whether to step back and try to broker larger conditional support from outside players even if that invites compromise; whether to walk away for a time and carry Harborfall's lessons into the wider world so fewer towns need to reach the brink you are facing.

You let the wind hold you while the horizon goes black and the town below glows in small, stubborn lights.
