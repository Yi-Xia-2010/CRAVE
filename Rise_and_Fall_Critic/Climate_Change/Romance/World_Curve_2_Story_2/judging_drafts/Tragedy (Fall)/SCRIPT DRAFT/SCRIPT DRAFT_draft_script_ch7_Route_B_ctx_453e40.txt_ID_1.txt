[Visual: Rain-streaked shutters, glass jars on a workbench, sensor wires draped like seaweed]
[Sound: Rain hitting corrugated metal, a distant gull, a faint electronic ping from a sensor array]

You push open the boathouse door and the smell hits you first — salt and solder, earth and oil, a damp that seems to seep into the bones of the place. Fluorescent lamps cut through the gray, making the jars on the bench look like bottled tides. A thin steam fogs your dive watch; the glass clouds, then clears again in a lazy silver ring.

Kaito is bent over a tangle of wires near the back, sleeves rolled, a strip of insulating tape held between his teeth. He looks up when you step inside; for a breath his face is just silhouette and rain. Then his sea-green eyes catch you, bright and struggling to be steady. He gestures with a gloved hand — half invitation, half apology.

Kaito: "You're late enough that Hana's probably started worrying. Come on back — I was just calibrating the north node. The reed cluster at Cove Three is still throwing noise."

You move through the clutter like it's home: jars labeled with dates and salinity numbers, a clipboard with Mayor Elena's name scrawled in a different ink, wire spools like coils of grey rope. Your jacket brushes against a coil; salt flakes off onto the floorboards. The familiar weight of responsibility sits on you, heavier in this cramped, earnest light than in the greenhouse's soft air.

You: "How bad is the noise?"

Kaito: (removing tape from his teeth) "Electrical interference mixed with biogenic hum. The reeds move more than the sensors expect after storms. I've been trying a low-pass filter, but —" He cuts himself off, rubbing the bridge of his nose. "Do you remember the prototype Noor sent for the lab? The one that dumps raw data? It's doing… okay. Not great. But it tells us things Lux's models don't."

Dr. Noor steps forward from behind a stack of notebooks, wet boots leaving soft prints. Her voice is steady but tired.

Dr. Noor: "The sensor spread is giving us high-resolution profiles of salinity gradients after the last surge. If we pair that with targeted mangrove planting in the sheltered coves, we can reduce wave energy at specific points. The datasets show localized efficacy — but the confidence intervals are wide because our sample size is small."

You can feel each word like a pebble dropped into a quiet pool. Small sample sizes. Wide confidence intervals. The statistics you trust like air-conditioning units that hum just beneath the rhetoric of certainty.

Tomas shuffles in, leaning on an old canvas pack, the smell of fish and tar clinging to him. He surveys the jars and the dangling sensors with a slow, weathered skepticism that has teeth.

Tomas: "I can take you to Cove Two at low tide. Old maps show a nursery bank there — if it's still there under the silt we can plant stumps without dragging in heavy gear. But it won't be quick, Maya. Mangroves don't like being bullied into growing."

You: "We don't want to bully them. We want them to come back on their own terms."

Kaito gives a quick, grateful smile at that. There's a tenderness in it that touches something raw in your chest and then retreats, because tenderness here must wear boots and a timeline.

Kaito reaches over and taps a patch on the bench where a sensor puck sits, its LED blinking a tired blue.

Kaito: "If we do this publicly, it's a pledge — volunteer labor, local oversight. No one else's string-pulling. But the mayor wants numbers. Dollars. And Lux… Lux wants to talk. She offers scale, and with scale comes a delivery timeline that can drown the slower stuff."

Dr. Noor folds her arms, worry line tightening at the corner of her mouth.

Dr. Noor: "From a modelling perspective, the hybrid is feasible — nature-based defenses plus sensor-driven adaptive management. But feasibility isn't the same as fundability. Small grants will pay for volunteers and rent. They won't pay for the heavy lift if we need to shore up a failing berm next winter."

Tomas: "We fix a berm with a few trucks and a lot of money, or we fix the living shoreline and wait for it to hold. Both of them have people who will get hurt while we decide which hurts less."

You have known this since you stood on the promenade the night the sea took Jonah. You know the math of loss better than most: one storm, one bad choice, a dozen people upended. You also know the truth of small, stubborn recoveries — their timelines are long and full of doubts, but they keep a town's memory intact.

You: "Have any of the nodes not rebooted after the last wash?"

Kaito: "Two did. The north float failed — probably a biofouling issue. That's fixable with a different mount. But Noor's right: we need more data, and we need people in the coves. Volunteers are stretched thin; Hana can provide meals for a week, but we can't eat grants."

Dr. Noor: (softly) "We could validate enough to convince Mayor Elena if we secure a municipal pilot acknowledgement. She needs political cover — a report that says 'likely to reduce risk at key access points.'"

Kaito: "And if we get Elena on board, we can keep Lux out of the immediate picture long enough to show proof. But —" He hesitates. "If we go to Elena and she opens negotiations with Lux anyway, we risk being co-opted. Lux will present numbers that look very final."

You feel the room tilt with all the weight of those conditional words.

A soft vibration hums from your pocket — Mayor Elena's number, persistent. You let it ring twice, then silence it. Letting the call go to voicemail feels like postponing a verdict you can't yet make.

Kaito moves toward you, voice low enough that the lamps and the rain are the only witnesses.

Kaito: "You don't have to do this alone, you know. If you want me to stand with you at the forum, I'll—"

You want to say yes and mean it. You want to stand with him and with the town and hold one clear plan up like a lantern. But even as the words hang on your tongue, you see the ledger in your mind: volunteers' hours, grant cycles, the mayor's reelection pressures, the looming meetings with LuxCorp. You see Jonah's jacket in your closet, the way the sea line wrote itself into your life's margins.

You: (quiet) "I know. I need to know — are we vulnerable because we're small, or because we're honest?"

Kaito: (after a beat) "Both. But honesty buys trust. And trust buys time, sometimes. Not always. But sometimes."

Dr. Noor: "We can make the case to Elena with field results that matter: reduced salinity spikes at key inlets, wave attenuation measurements at Cove Two. The metrics aren't sexy, but they are defensible."

Tomas bristles at the word 'metrics' like it is a slap.

Tomas: "People measure different things. My father measured whether his nets filled. You measure whether your models predict survival. There's a thing you don't quantify until it's gone — a shoreline's memory. We keep that if we can."

You rest your palm on a jar, feeling the smudged labels through the glass. The rain on the roof sounds like a clock. You can taste metal at the back of your tongue. There is a small, stubborn hope here — tender, vulnerable, and very possibly insufficient.

The room leans toward you in different voices: Noor's measured caution, Tomas's slow moral gravity, Kaito's quietly contagious optimism. Each argument carries a cost. Each argument could become the reason you fail someone later.

You think of the forum at dawn, a room full of neighbors, a microphone, the promise of numbers to a town that wants to be safe. You think of LuxCorp's glossy maps and Mayor Elena's tired face. You imagine the headlines if you present this pilot as the main plan and it collapses in a storm. You imagine the silence if you hide it until you have proof, and the missed window that decision would cause.

Outside, the marsh's low reeds sway and the sensor nodes blink in steady blues and reds, faithful little lights in the rain. Trust here, like the circuit boards, is fragile. One short, one overload, and everything shuts down.

You close your eyes for a second, feeling the watch fog against your skin, remembering Jonah's laugh, the way he used to run his fingers over tide charts like they were prayers.

You open your eyes.

You: "I have to decide whether I champion this publicly, present it as a compromise to Elena, or keep building proof before anyone else knows."

There is no applause. Only the rain, and the small mechanical heartbeat of equipment doing what it can. In the wet lamplight, their faces are unreadable in different ways — hope, fatigue, calculation — all under the same roof. You know that each path cuts a different wound through the town. You know that whatever you choose will not be purely technical; it will carry memory, debt, and the weight of other people's children.

The watch ticks. The node at the north mount pings a tiny, uncertain signal. Outside, Cove Three holds its breath under the tide.

You stand at the center of the circuit, fingers grazing a spool of wire like a seam you might close or leave open.
