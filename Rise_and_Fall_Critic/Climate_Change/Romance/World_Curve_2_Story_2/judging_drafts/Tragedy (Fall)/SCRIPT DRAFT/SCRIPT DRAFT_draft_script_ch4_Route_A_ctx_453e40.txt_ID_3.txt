[Scene: LuxCorp Coastal Plaza | Dusk]
[Visual: Wide shot of LuxCorp HQ; mirrored panels reflect a bruised sky. Rain-slick tiles ripple with LED reflections.]
[Music: Low, tense piano with distant, slow percussion]
[Sound: Soft hiss of climate-control vents; the distant gulls are muffled by glass]

The plaza feels like a different weather system—cool and controlled, as if the building exhales climate-stable air and never learned to be messy with salt and sorrow.

You step beneath an overhang and feel the difference immediately: drier hair at your temples, the faint citrus of disinfectant, the hum of concealed systems. The storm-blue coat Lux wears catches the last light and makes her silhouette precise as an instrument. She stands near a display of geometric planters—everything here is measured so it can be scaled.

Lux notices you before you reach her. Her eyes—steel-gray, sharp and calculating—map you in a heartbeat and find the patched elbows, the knit scarf, the battered clipboard. She smiles with the kind of warmth that has the edges sanded off for presentation.

Lux: "Maya. Thank you for coming. I know it's late for you."

You: "It's not that late for Harborfall, but thank you for the offer." (Your voice has the dry, low steadiness you practice in meetings.)

[Visual: Lux gestures; a holo projection unfurls between you—waves rendered in smooth arcs, a ribbon of a raised cofferdam, floodgates sliding into neat, obedient positions.]
[Sound: Soft whoosh of the holo]
[Music: A single cello notes the promise; then a thin, uneasy counterpoint]

Lux: "We have a proposal that moves quickly. A raised sea wall with automated gates, integrated flood management, and a community resilience fund to retrofit homes. We can begin construction within the season—permits accelerated, funding lined up. Fewer people displaced, fewer emergency evacuations."

You study the hologram. The wave bends away like a trick of light. It fits on the slate of her tablet.

You: "It's beautiful when it does that on a screen."

Lux's smile narrows in a way that reads like assessment, not offense.

Lux: "It's not a trick. It's engineering. The models hold. We can protect thousands of residents. Your credibility here—your voice—would help expedite acceptance. We want Harborfall as a flagship."

You feel the heat at the back of your neck, the old reflex of measuring a promise against what it costs. The dive watch on your wrist ticks; in this room each tick seems to count for votes and livelihoods and the brittle bones of dunes.

Kaito stands at the edge of the plaza, hands tucked into his vest pockets, his field camera slung low. He watches the holo with a frown you recognize—an argument in geometry and ethics that hasn't been spoken yet.

Kaito: "Scale is useful," he says when Lux pauses, voice level. "But the estuary—those models can't fully account for the biotic feedback. You flatten a shoreline and a hundred small losses compound. You can't just 'protect' people without deciding what you erase."

Lux: "We account for the estuary in the design. There are mitigations, wetland transplants, compensatory zones. We do not proceed blind."

Kaito: "Compensatory zones on a map are not the same as roots holding mud. You know that."

Lux doesn't shift her gaze from you; her tone is polished, almost conspiratorial.

Lux: "Kaito, you and your lab do excellent work with sensors. We want data—we can use it. But Harborfall doesn't have the luxury of indefinite piloting. People need protection now."

You feel the gravity in both their words, different pulls. Lux offers now—an immediate shoring up. Kaito offers method—tests that might save the ecology but take time.

Lux leans in, and for a breath you feel the meeting turn intimate—like a negotiation that has been rehearsed to feel like care.

Lux: "Maya, you know what it's like to lose someone to a sudden storm. You and I both came up with pilot ideas once, didn't we? We don't want Harborfall to be in that position again. This isn't about control—it's about saving lives at scale."

You: "We did—once. Plans that respected the shore. But I remember how plans become timelines and timelines become bulldozers."

Lux: (softly) "Then stay with me on building the plan so it doesn't feel like bulldozers."

Kaito: "How many coves are on your list, Lux? How many traditional fishing spots will be reshaped into access ramps? They may be 'compensated.' They may not be the same."

Lux: "I'm not here to steamroll tradition. I'm here to prevent the next catastrophe. We can design with cultural input."

Kaito: "Design with input is not always design in partnership. Contracts have clauses. Funding has conditions. Who holds maintenance in thirty years? What happens when the company that built the wall moves on?"

Lux's expression is unreadable for a moment. Then she reaches toward your clipboard, fingers careful, almost maternal in public.

Lux: "You have the town's trust. You can be the bridge. With you, we can anchor the plan in real needs. We provide the resources; you ensure the values are honored."

You feel the compliment press against the places you keep guarded. Someone invoking your community credibility is a way to co-opt the very thing you protect. And yet—homes reinforced, children safer. The taste of compromise gathers at the corner of your tongue.

You: "What guarantees would Harborfall have? Not just money—maintenance funds, oversight, transparency. A plan that can be audited by locals."

Lux: "Absolutely. We will set up a local stewardship board, seed the fund for operations, and commit to open data streams. Our contracts can include community oversight."

Kaito: (lifted brow) "Open data is not the same as shared decision-making."

Lux: "I don't expect community members to run turbine algorithms. But we will train and hire locally for maintenance."

You measure the words. They are enticing: jobs, training, visible care. They are also carefully shaped pieces of a larger argument that benefits from being built fast.

Lux taps the air; the holo rearranges to show a timeline—expedited, layered with milestones and phasing.

Lux: "We move fast because every season matters. The window is closing. If we wait for perfect consensus, the next storm will be the one that breaks us."

You think of Tomas's slow hands on rope, Hana's crowded diner during storms, the greenhouse where seedlings are counted like a small, stubborn prayer. Waiting is also a kind of violence when the sea is rising.

Kaito: "We can scale our pilot. Data from sensors could validate a hybrid approach. We can retrofit in phases so the estuary adapts."

Lux: (a little impatience showing) "Phases extend exposure. Phases leave people vulnerable longer."

You feel your chest tighten. You are the hinge not because you alone decide, but because your framing decides who hears what and when.

You: "If we go forward, it's not just your wall. It has to be integrated—habitat corridors, local oversight, economic provisions for displaced fishers. It has to be measured against community-defined success, not investor timelines."

Lux: (tilts her head) "That's reasonable. With your voice, we can set the criteria."

Kaito watches you. There's a softness to his expression now, a plea in the quiet of it.

Kaito: "Maya—you know me. I want to keep our shore alive in more than name. Let me help draft the integration plan."

Lux: "We welcome that. I want local expertise at the table."

For a moment the three of you exist in a careful choreography: promise, plea, and the slow compression of what can't be held all at once.

You realize you're hungry for the warmth of the rooftop greenhouse you left—the place smells of soil and sharp tea, and inside it people argue with hands full of seedlings, not charts. The thought of it feels like a bruise and a balm.

Lux: "I'll put together a formal offer by tomorrow. Terms, funding, phasing, oversight framework. You can review it with your board. If we agree, we begin."

You: "Tomorrow." The word tastes like a hinge—an opening you cannot easily close.

Kaito steps a pace closer, voice low. "Whatever you do, don't sign something that trades our shore for comfort masked as safety."

Lux: (smiles tightly) "We don't want to buy compliance; we want to buy time."

You stand with the weight of the plaza's quiet systems around you, feeling the smallness of human flesh against corporate timelines and mechanical promises. Your watch ticks; rain starts in a thin, cold lace on the plaza tiles.

You remember the greenhouse's warmth and the feel of dirt under nails. You remember a younger version of yourself, drafting plans in a stormlight, certain you could make things right.

You leave LuxCorp with a cup you've taken or a cup you haven't—whatever you chose during the small choices—and a promise lodged between business cards and the cold light of a tablet. The formal offer will come tomorrow. The rooftop greenhouse will be colder for your absence. Your hands smudge ink on the clipboard as you step back into Harborfall night.

[Visual: Transition—luxury glass fades into the film grain of Harborfall's promenade; fog and salt return.]
[Music: Low strings; a single, unresolved piano chord]

You walk the promenade with the holo's image still behind your eyes—the wall bending the sea away—and the memory of Kaito's face sharpens into the present like a warning.

You: "I will read the offer," you tell yourself, because promises have weight only when acknowledged. You turn the page in your notebook and write words that feel like a compass: oversight. stewardship. community-defined metrics.

The taste of compromise is there, metallic and sweet. It sits alongside a steadier current: the knowledge that whatever you choose to support, someone will be helped and someone will lose something irretrievable.

You pull your scarf tighter against the wind and let the rain make tracks down the fabric, as if to wash away decisions not yet made.
