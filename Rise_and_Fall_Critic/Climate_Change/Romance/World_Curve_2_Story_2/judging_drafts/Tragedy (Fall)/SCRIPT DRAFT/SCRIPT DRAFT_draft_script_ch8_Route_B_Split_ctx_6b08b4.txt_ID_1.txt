[Scene: Market Square | Early Morning — Low, Sullen Light]
[Visual: Wet cobblestones glint; vendor tarps sag with rain. LuxCorp banners visible across the harbor, crisp and polished against Harborfall’s salt-bleached facades.]
[Sound: Distant gull calls, a single radio crackling behind a coffee stall, the soft ping of a field sensor on Kaito’s belt.]

You step into the square with Kaito and Tomas at your shoulders, and the air tastes like old rope and fried fish. Market stalls huddle under tarps, steam from Hana's soup booth fogging the morning. People move with the slow precision of those who read tides in their bones—each step planned against a calendar they didn't choose.

Tomas squeezes the cartilage of his thumb against a weathered net, then offers a grin that doesn't reach his eyes.

Tomas: "You brought the lab down to the stalls, eh? Modern miracles and old coffee. We can make do with both."

You let a half-smile out of favor to the old fisherman and step toward a cluster of them—men and women whose hands map every notch of Harborfall's decline. You know the stories they'll recite: the storm that ate three cottages, the new line of barnacles on the north pier, the younger fishers leaving for the city.

Kaito sets a small tablet on a crate and flips a screen. The sensor's interface is clean, a ribbon of numbers pulsing like a heartbeat: salinity spikes, micro-tilt of the shoreline stakes, a rising trend in subsurface water movement. He taps, and a graph blooms blue then jagged—a salt spike before a recorded erosion event.

Kaito: (soft humor) "Not magic, just a stubborn correlation. Salinity up, structure down. It gives us a lead time—sometimes hours, sometimes days."

You feel it in your chest—the promise of lead time. It could be a warning enough to shift nets, move children, shore a berm. It could be the difference between a tide that scares and a tide that takes.

Fisher 1: "All this pretty squiggle-talk—how does that help my boat?"

Kaito: "It tells us where the water is starting to cut deeper under the roots. If we know that, you change the mooring, move your traps, we shore that edge in time."

The fisher narrows their eyes, fingers rough and smelling of brine.

Fisher 2: "Sounds like you need more than a tablet. Sounds like money. Mayor's got the keys to that."

Tomas: (sharp) "Mayor's got the keys, and LuxCorp’s got a forklift of PR leaflets."

You glance toward the far edge of the square where a LuxCorp rep adjusts a backdrop—a glossy rendering of a monolithic sea wall. The company’s well-dressed team smiles with the sterile cheer of people who sell inevitability.

[Visual: Close-up on your dive watch; the second hand drags, each small click a thin accusation.]
[Sound: Kaito's sensor pings again — a high, nervous note.]

You open your mouth and close it; the weight of every household that leans on your word shifts like a tide under your feet. Kaito watches you, his expression quiet. There is humor in his lips, but the skin around his eyes is taut.

Kaito: (soft) "If you want me to speak in plain terms, I will. Or—"

You know you can translate his data into the language of livelihoods. You can make graphs into promises, promises into votes. Or you can keep the tech talk contained—let the numbers grow stronger in the lab before anyone stakes a political claim.

[Scene: Reclaimed Marsh Walkway | Midday — Low Cloud Shelf]
[Visual: Stilted wooden walkway curves over black water; halophytic plants clasp the mud. The light is thin, like a coin held to the sky.]
[Sound: Footsteps on wet planks, the low creak of wood, the whisper of distant maintenance motors.]

You and Kaito lead a small procession—Tomas, two fishers you persuaded, and Mayor Elena, who arrives with a municipal umbrella and a ledger of cautious necessity. LuxCorp's PR shadow flutters somewhere beyond the berm; their ease is a clean, clattering presence against the town's ragged edges.

Mayor Elena: (measured) "We need evidence that this pilot will reduce municipal exposure. I can't justify reallocating emergency funds on potential."

Her voice is tired but not unkind; she wears the lines of compromise like armor. You can see the calculations behind her eyes—budgets, votes, lengths of tenure measured in meetings.

You bring up one of Kaito’s recent sensor logs, the one with a clear pre-erosion salinity spike. Kaito taps the screen and draws a finger along the spike.

Kaito: "We get a spike here. Then, the stake readings show the marsh losing volume the very next tide. If we install sensors and pair them with targeted berm reinforcement and plant restoration, we can act before the erosion becomes runaway."

Mayor Elena: "Targeted action is expensive. Long-term restoration even more so. LuxCorp's proposal is—to be blunt—an externally funded structure. It solves my budget equation on paper."

Tomas: (with a slow, weathered anger) "On paper—until the paper blows away. People live here. We don't want a wall that turns our coves into dead water."

Mayor Elena: "I know that. I also know the calls I get when the flood sirens go off. I need measurable certainty that this pilot gives us, frankly, more than a talking point."

A LuxCorp representative steps forward at that moment, timing a smile like a practiced curl. They hand Mayor Elena a glossy brochure.

LuxCorp PR: (smiling) "Mayor, we'd love to walk you through our modeled outcomes. Our wall is already reducing modeled flood exposure by sixty percent in comparable towns."

You feel the PR wedge—every phrase engineered to reframe urgency as inevitability. Their pamphlet's renderings show Harborfall with a clean line cut into the horizon and no boats on the foreshore. The images ache like an erasure.

You meet Elena’s eyes. The town’s ledger is in her hands. Kaito folds his arms, sensing the political weather; his humor fades into a steady, nervous focus.

Kaito: "We can give you measured certainty, if you let this pilot get municipally resourced now. A distributed sensor net, paired with small, adaptive interventions—those numbers are meaningful."

Mayor Elena: "Meaningful how? You said sometimes hours, sometimes days. I don’t work in 'sometimes'—I work in budgets that must hold when forecasts are wrong."

You feel the press of argument building—a physics you have learned: the more you push, the more the town resists or snaps.

You think of your brother’s watch, the way its hand once ran like a calm river and now feels like a metronome set to the speed of your grief. The iron tang at the back of your mouth is a memory that names risks you've vowed to never let repeat.

Tomas: (quiet, to you) "If they ask for certainty they already decided. They always need that. But sometimes certainty is what you make, not what you wait for."

Mayor Elena crosses her arms, considering, then softens.

Mayor Elena: "If you bring me a plan with cost estimates, risk metrics, and a commitment for community-led oversight, I will consider an emergency reallocation. Not because I like risk, but because I owe it to people who can't get to safety on their own."

Kaito: (a small, guarded smile) "We can do that. We can make metrics that mean something for those who go out to sea."

You want to cheer and collapse at once. Progress and the chill of politics braid together. Then a new voice cuts in—the LuxCorp rep's warm tone finds a new audience.

LuxCorp PR: "Mayor, with all respect, there's no time to dally. Models and pilots are fine when you're not under a season's storm. Our approach is ready to deploy. It is scalable, funded, and—"

Tomas: (barks) "Scaled down our coves and called it salvation."

The square of your chest tightens. Every path forward now carries a price: political cost, social trust, the possibility of technological failure in a storm. Kaito's hand brushes the strap of his sensor pack; its little pendant blinks, as if afraid.

You sense the fork forming: you can make this public now and force a political battle, you can keep refining the system quietly until the numbers are irrefutable, or you can push an immediate deployment in the face of a looming forecast and pray the prototype holds.

Kaito looks at you, the soft humor gone. His voice is small.

Kaito: (direct) "We can roll it out in stages. Or we can wait. You have to tell me which. Because I'll stand with you—either way—but I need to know you're ready to be the face of whatever happens."

You taste the salt-stiff tang of fear, and the watch ticks below your sleeve like an accusation: do you act and risk failure publicly? Do you hide the work and risk being sidelined? Do you rush and risk breaking the fragile thing you built?

[Sound: The harbor sighs; somewhere a bell tolls once and then again; the watch on your wrist clicks soft as confession.]

You let the options gather in your chest like a shoal. There is no clean choice here—only trade-offs, each one taking something. The pilot could be made public and wrestle the town's future into a contested field; it could stay dark and risk being discredited as secret tinkering; it could sprint out ahead and be judged by the worst weather throws at it.

Kaito stays beside you, steady and quiet. Tomas watches the horizon with a fisherman’s patient cruelty. Mayor Elena's ledger is a weight you can feel even when she is absent. LuxCorp’s banners rustle across the bay like a vow.

Your brother's watch keeps time as if to remind you of the debt you carry: to keep people alive, to not be stamped as the one who said yes at the wrong time. You have to choose not only a technical approach but the character you will present to Harborfall—cautious steward, bold leader, or quiet engineer.

The sea breathes; so do you.
