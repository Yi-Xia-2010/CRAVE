[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Afternoon]
[Visual: Salt-smeared glass panes; sun slanting through dust-moted air onto rows of raised planters. A stitched tide-chart scarf hangs from a clipboard.]
[Music: Low, minor strings; a distant, steady percussion like a mounting heartbeat]
[Sound: Muffled gull calls, faint city hum, the whisper of ventilation fans]

You pause with your hand on the latch, thumb brushing the cold of the metal. The greenhouse exhales warmth—humid breath of peat and compost, the faint copper scent of salt and rust. Inside, the plants steam in the sun like small, stubborn promises. Your dive watch ticks against your wrist; in your pocket a weather alert pulses amber, discreet and insistent.

You step in.

You: "—thanks for coming," you say, more because sound replaces the ache in your chest than for any rhetorical need. Your voice catches on the word and you smooth it with a breath. The clipboard rests heavy in your hands, watercolor stains catching the light like old maps.

Kaito looks up from a string of sensors he's been untangling; the pendant at his throat blinks a patient blue. There is dirt at the nailbed of his index finger where he always fidgets. For a second his sea-green eyes find you and he smiles—small, almost private—and the world narrows to that unselfconscious curve of mouth. It makes you think, dangerously, that maybe enough is still possible.

Kaito: (softly) "You made it. I—uh—left the prototypes where you asked. Dr. Noor's got the telemetry lined up for the first pass."

Dr. Noor maneuvers a wheeled cart of tablets and printouts into the sun patch; her breath fogs on the glass when she pauses. Her clipboard is bracketed with graphs that try to be hopeful.

Dr. Noor: "Tide variance models, three sensor nodes, and a worst-case projection. The nodes will give us two weeks of continuous readings if we can keep them above the spring line."

Tomas sets a battered notebook on the table—pages flap with marginalia—and beside it, Hana's plate of warm cookies anchors the paper like a truce. He rubs his knuckles together as if rubbing the memory of salt out of them.

Tomas: "You know what's worse than empty numbers? Empty coves. If we lose the moorings near the lower inlet, the rest of the run changes. The nets won't bring in the same tide. That's not just livelihood—it's the town's rhythm."

Hana: (brisk, practical) "And if your project's late, people come here hungry. They don't care about elegant solutions then. They want dry floors."

Mayor Elena arrives then, blazer precise enough to crease the light. She stands at the edge of the plant benches like someone surveying a row of appointments. In her hands is a folder stamped with the municipal seal and, beneath that, a slick brochure from LuxCorp folded into the shape of a promise.

Mayor Elena: "Maya. Thank you. We don't have much time. Lux has offered a staged wall—quick deployment, regional funding. They're asking for our decision before the investors reconvene. Their engineers say days, not years."

You taste galvanized metal at the back of your mouth. Lux's projection—sleek renderings of a clean horizon where the sea stops at chrome—already hangs in the room, a ghostlight on the far wall from a LuxCorp simulation your phone received this morning. The image feels like a cutout of the town: neat, leveled, and unfamiliar.

You set the clipboard on the potting bench. The paper trembles under your palm.

You: "The trade-offs are real," you say. You lay them out the only way you've ever been able to—practical, blunt, like trimming dead stem to save the root. "Nature-based defenses—berms, mangrove transplants, living shorelines—protect the estuary and maintain fishing coves, but they take years. They ask people to move plots, to raise foundations, to accept disruption to daily life. Lux's wall—fast, external, efficient—reduces immediate flood risk, but it will change tidal flows and shape the coast in ways we can't reverse. It will, frankly, cost us some of what makes Harborfall Harborfall."

There's a hush. The scent of brine feels heavier, like someone has closed a door.

Mayor Elena: "The council hears that. They also hear the state auditor: if we don't show mitigation, they'll reallocate funds. The people's houses—if those go under in the next surge—I'll be the one explaining why we waited."

Dr. Noor: "If we can pilot sensors in the most vulnerable sectors now, we can produce high-fidelity data in weeks—data to argue for combined measures. But—" (she taps a graph) "—that requires commitment to maintain the nodes and an initial budget for community teams."

Kaito steps forward, fingers restless on the prototype casing. He speaks with the careful cadence of someone trying to thread two ends of rope together without fraying them.

Kaito: "What if we start small? Deploy three nodes tonight, get two weeks' worth of localized feedback, and—simultaneously—begin visible habitat work in the estuary. The data helps quantify the benefit curve, and the habitat work buys us social capital. It won't be enough on its own if a storm hits next month, but it gives us leverage and time."

You can see the hope in his posture: practical optimism, the sort that refuses to be crushed by bad weather. You want to catch it and pass it along.

You: "We can do that. I can organize teams—Tomas, Hana, some of the volunteers. Dr. Noor, can your lab commit to processing the feeds weekly? Mayor—if we can show initial metrics, would the council consider a temporary moratorium on large-scale contractual commitments?"

Mayor Elena's fingers steel around the edge of the brochure. Her face flits: public servant, politician, someone who sleeps with spreadsheets on her chest.

Mayor Elena: "Maybe. But 'maybe' isn't what Lux's representatives will take to the table. They want signatures and a schedule. They're moving money. We either buy time in a way Lux accepts or we roll our dice on bargaining power."

A low beeping from your pocket matches the beat of your watch. Amber. You feel it like a friendly finger on the spine of your anxiety. You don't pull the phone out. You don't have to; the mood in the greenhouse has changed from hopeful to taut.

Kaito frowns at your guardedness, but he doesn't push. His reply is soft, an attempt to anchor where you risk drifting.

Kaito: "If you want me to, I'll take responsibility for the node maintenance schedule. I can train volunteers. I—" He hesitates, looking at your hands more than your face. "—I can be here."

His words sit in the humid air like a small pledge. You swallow.

Tomas, who has watched more storms than most can name, closes his notebook with a sound like a low tide.

Tomas: "Promises are good, but we need salt-season plans that people can act on when they aren't thinking about the next meeting. A plan is nothing if the folks on the wharves don't own it."

Hana nudges a cookie toward you; it's warm and ridiculous and somehow essential.

Hana: "You can worry and build at once, Maya. People have done both. But you have to eat, too."

You laugh—sharp, wind-burned—and it fractures into a cough. Your brother's name ghosts behind your ribs: the storm that took him, the night you swore you'd never let that happen again. The watch ticks. You feel the cadence of that loss bargaining with your present.

Your voice is quieter when you speak next.

You: "I don't want to promise what I can't deliver. If we commit to a phased pilot—nodes tonight, habitat work staged—then I will ask the council for bridging funds. I will ask Lux to delay capital deployment long enough for us to prove a hybrid approach has merit."

Mayor Elena's eyes go to the brochure and then past it, as if trying to measure the space between two futures.

Mayor Elena: "Asking Lux is politically messy. They've been clear about their timelines and their expectations. They'll spin any delay as obstruction."

You can see Lux in the room as easily as you can see the fog outside—her glossy proposals, the way her engineers smooth every edge into a marketable line. She is not here, but her footprint presses down on the conversation like a tide.

Dr. Noor folds her hands around a tablet and exhales.

Dr. Noor: "If you can get maintenance trained by my team and a municipal green-light to install tonight, I can process the first batch within seventy-two hours. The data will speak. Numbers carry weight with the council and with Lux."

Kaito leans in, urgent now.

Kaito: "But we can't do sensors and restoration without community buy-in. We need volunteers, local labor, proof that this is their work, not just another corporate demo. Tomas—can your network pull a team for the berm plots? Hana—can the diner host sign-up sheets?"

Hana: (with a wry smile) "And cookies. Sign-up sheets and cookies. Works every time."

The humor is thin and dear. You feel the room compressing around the decision like a tightening fist. You reach for the familiar rhythm—the planner's list that has kept the town alive through smaller crises.

You: "Okay. We have an operational plan we can attempt tonight. Three nodes, volunteer teams for the estuary patching, data processing on a rolling schedule. I'll ask the council for a temporary hold on outside contracts—buy us a week, at least. If the data supports it, we use it to reframe the public case."

Mayor Elena scans you and then looks away, the kind of look that means she is already doing the math of votes.

Mayor Elena: "A week is optimistic. You'll need a demonstrable action within forty-eight hours to placate investors. Lux will interpret delays harshly."

A rustle of disquiet travels across the benches. Dr. Noor's screen flickers as a new notification pushes through—more projections, more models. Tomas's grip on his notebook tightens.

Your watch ticks. The amber alert in your pocket pulses again, insistent.

You can feel the room leaning toward consequences that have names: lost coves, dry floors, investor anger, or a city saved from immediate flood but hollowed where it once was. Your brother's ghost keeps time with your fear—each tick a story that ended too soon.

You hear yourself promise things you might not be able to deliver. The promise settles in your chest like a pebble; it is small and heavy and impossible to hide. The warmth of the greenhouse presses in on you, like a small boat in a rising sea.

You glance down. The amber glow in your pocket blinks again.

You: (to the room, quieter than before) "We start tonight. Volunteers, sensors, data. We ask the council for a short hold. We present initial readings within forty-eight hours."

The words leave your mouth and land in the air between them all. For a second, the greenhouse is a held breath.

Outside, the horizon's light shifts; the fog thickens at the edges, a gray that looks like a hand closing. Inside, people move—preparing, planning, each of them carrying their own ledger of risk and loyalty.

A wind stirs the palms, and a stray leaflet from LuxCorp's brochure flutters like a white gull across the benches. It stops near the potting soil, ridiculous as a seagull on dry land.

You feel the tide of possibility and the pull of consequence at the same time.

You check your watch again. The needle makes its small, honest revolution.

The weather alert in your pocket flashes amber, then steadies.

[Music: Strings tighten; a low cello holds a falling note]

The decision hangs—sharp, inevitable, and not yet done.
