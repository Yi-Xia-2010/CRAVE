# Route: Route B
# Context: 6b08b4

[Scene: Kaito's Field Lab / Main Door | Dawn]
[Music: Low, minor strings; rain tapping a steady rhythm]
[Background:"ch8_6b08b4_1"]

You leave the lab with the notebook closed like a held breath. The rain smells of iron and seaweed—sharp and metallic on the tongue—so familiar it knits a bruise of memory across your chest. The lamp light falls wet on Kaito's knuckles as he buttons his coat; his sensor pendant blinks a steady, embarrassed green.

Kaito Sato: "You look like you haven't slept."

Maya Inoue: "I didn't, exactly." (Your voice is smaller than you expect.) "Did you—did you copy the last readouts?"

Kaito Sato: "All here." (He taps a tablet screen; graphs bloom, jagged and honest.) "Salinity spikes at Station Three, fifteen minutes before the last sluff. The pattern matches the models—only denser than we thought."

He shows you—a smear of numbers that means erosion, that means cliffs softening like old bread. His fingers hover over the screen, then curl into his pockets. There's a quick, self-deprecating smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Kaito Sato: "If we put sensors at the headlands and near the mangrove stubs, we can get a lead time. Not perfect. But it gives Tomas and the crews a window."

You taste iron again. Your brother's watch on your wrist ticks on, slow and accusing, as if it could sew time back together. The lab's fluorescent hum seems to thicken the air.

You inhale. Outside, the market square is a darker pool beneath the rain, but it's where people still meet—where gossip, anger, grief, and hope get traded in equal measure.

[Scene: Harborfall Market Square | Morning]
[Sound: Market calls muffled by rain; gulls distant; the slap of wet fabric]
[Background:"ch8_6b08b4_2"]

Tomas is already there, leaning on his cane, the gold in his beard a dull flame in the gray. Around him, three fishers you recognize—callused hands and old scarves—argue about nets like priestly disputes.

Tomas: "They'll promise the moon and hand us a contract in fine print. Trust was our currency; they want to monetize it."

You move through the smell of fried dough and wet rope, your boots leaving dark prints on the boards. The fishers look up, and the lines around their mouths tell you everything you need to know—loss, calculation, suspicion.

Maya Inoue: "We have something that can warn you earlier. Not a wall—an early warning network. It won't be pretty. It won't be instantaneous. But it will give you minutes that could matter."

Old Fisher 1: "Minutes don't mend a boat, girl. They mend a decision."

Old Fisher 2: "And who pays for the minutes when Lux brings a wall with her name on it?"

Tomas watches you with soft, slow intensity. He doesn't move to argue; he waits for you to earn it.

Tomas: "Show them the numbers, Maya. Show them the thing that says tomorrow might be different."

Kaito Sato steps forward, breath steaming in the rain. He sets a small portable sensor on the table—brass and plastic, a humble thing—and the pendant at his chest blinks like a heartbeat.

Kaito Sato: "Watch." (He gestures, and the sensor projects a transient readout into the air—salinity curves that wobble as the tide flexes.) "See that climb? That spike is saline surge. It eats at the root mass before you see the collapse. If we can catch that...we can buy time."

The fishers lean in. The projection paints their faces in cold blue. You feel, for a flicker, that the whole square could tilt with their consent.

Old Fisher 3: "You say it saves us. I say it makes us vulnerable if it fails."

Kaito Sato: "Then we don't move faster than we test. We place, we read, we respond."

His voice is steady; your chest eases. But there are other sounds—a smooth, rehearsed applause that doesn't belong to the market. LuxCorp's PR team has a portable display beneath an umbrella two stalls over: a slick animation of a wall rising like a white promise. Two uniformed staff hand out pamphlets with bullet points: "Immediate Protection," "Donor-Backed Funding," "Guaranteed Jobs."

PR Woman: (smiling too brightly) "Mayor Elena's briefing later will feature concrete models. You should come—see the future the city's counting on."

You smell the gloss of their flyers—ink and ambition. The PR woman's teeth are a precise, designed thing.

Internal monologue: The town is hungry for certainty. Lux's wall is a spoonful that looks like a meal. But it's not always the right taste. Kaito's sensors are the slow stew—less immediate, but nourishing if tended. Your brother's watch ticks louder. The weight of a possible choice presses at your shoulders like a tide.

[Outcome C: "You step toward the kiosk, voice thick. The PR woman smiles sharper; a ripple of cold air cuts the circle. Kaito grips your arm, sensing the escalation."]

The market reacts like an animal—shy and sudden. Your choice nudges people into remembering, into calculating, into bristling. Kaito watches you with a look that is both plea and apology.

Kaito Sato: "Whatever we do, be careful. Public means leverage. Secrecy means suspicion. Fast means risk. I can't...I don't want to be the one to make you lose everything because I pushed too soon."

Maya Inoue: "You won't make me lose it alone." (You mean it, and the words taste like iron and something else—determination.)

Tomas: "We don't have forever. The mayor wants figures. Lux wants a ribbon-cutting. You want to move the town. Choose a way that doesn't betray what this place is."

You stand between them—between numbers, memory, and polished promises. Rain threads your hair to your forehead. The watch at your wrist ticks. The market hums with small decisions—the exchange of coins, the folding of umbrellas. Each pocket of conversation is a micro-economy of trust.

[Scene: Reclaimed Marsh / Walkway | Late Morning]
[Background:"ch8_6b08b4_3"]
[Sound: Water sluicing, distant engine, a child's laughter cut short by a gull]

You walk the reclaimed marsh with Kaito and Tomas, sensors strapped to a pole like a fragile flag. You run a hand along the rail; salt has left a crust that flakes onto your glove. The stakes in the mud look small and stubborn—proof that people here keep trying.

Kaito Sato kneels, hands sure despite the cold, and demonstrates the probe insertion. The sensor sighs as it wakes, a soft electronic exhale. The readout blinks—then trails into a pattern for you both to read.

Kaito Sato: "Place them at the mangrove edge, by the headland, and near the old lighthouse footing. Erosion starts where root mass is compromised. We can model it. We can issue community alerts—get boats out, shift sandbags, move livestock."

Tomas: "And if the model tells the wrong thing?"

Kaito Sato: "Then we learn faster. We iterate."

There's a hard edge to that last line; an economist's optimism laced with the knowledge of failed prototypes. You hear the implied confession: prototypes fail when they haven't been tested in the open storm.

Internal monologue: A prototype is a promise not yet fulfilled. Pushing it into the mouths of the town's narrow trust is like asking them to feed on hope. You can see how Lux would use a single failed read to swallow your credibility whole.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Encourage Kaito to publish the first reads"
> Choice B: "Suggest we keep the initial reads private until refined"
[Outcome A: "You nudge him toward transparency—let the town see the errors as part of the process. Kaito glances at you, torn but relieved at your faith."]
[Outcome B: "You turn the sensor away from the path of gossip. Kaito studies the data quietly and agrees, but his jaw clenches; secrecy tastes like safety and fear in one mouthful."]

Kaito Sato's hand brushes yours as he passes the tablet back. It's a gesture that says more than the numbers: trust me, he seems to say, and I will be careful.

Before you can shape the argument further, Mayor Elena appears on the walkway, raincoat buttoned, her municipal pin a bright, tired star. She is accompanied by two aides. Behind her, through the wet, a LuxCorp representative takes notes with a smooth, predatory grace.

Mayor Elena: "Maya. Kaito. I heard a demonstration was happening."

Maya Inoue: "Mayor. We wanted to show you—this gives warning time. It could mean fewer lost boats, fewer houses flooded before we can act."

Mayor Elena: (eyes flicking to the sensor) "Evidence is welcome. But the council wants guarantees. They want predictability for the budget revision. Lux has proposed a comprehensive wall; it comes with funding lined up and a timeline. I need certainty."

Kaito Sato: "There's no certainty, only probability. But we can improve our odds with networked sensors. It gives you options—localized response rather than a razed coastline."

Mayor Elena: "Options cost money. The grant Lux offers is immediate. Constituents want to be safe now, not after a phased pilot."

Her voice softens, but the undercurrent is steel. You feel the mayor's burden—the math of votes and mortgages. Behind her eyes: the same calculation Tomas warned about. You see the temptation of a single, visible act that will look like leadership.

LuxCorp PR Man: (overly casual) "Mayor, our models show protection for the next fifty years with minimal upkeep. It's an efficient allocation."

Maya Inoue: "For whom?" (It slips out, sharper than intended.) "Your model protects property values; our approach invests in people who read the tides every day."

Mayor Elena exhales, pinching the bridge of her nose. The rain splinters on her shoulders. She looks at you—not accusing, but exhausted.

Mayor Elena: "Maya, how much would you need to push this pilot publicly? A request in the morning's council? Emergency reallocation? The window is closing."

The question is both simple and a noose. Two paths branch immediately: make it public and invite scrutiny, or keep it private and risk the slow erosion of trust—and a third, darker option, lashes at the edges of thought: deploy immediately without full testing. Each promises salvation or ruin.

Kaito Sato's face goes pale with the weight of the choices. Tomas folds his hands on his cane, a metronome of patience that trembles.

Kaito Sato: "We could—" (He stops. He looks at you for the leadership you were asked for.) "Maya. I need to know: will you make the ask? Will you stand with it publicly if I put my name next to you?"

Your brother's watch ticks. Your mouth tastes of brine and old alarms.

Internal monologue: You are the hinge between the town's need and your refusal to hand it to the highest bidder. Every option is a kind of failure if measured in absolutes. The community's love is brittle and beautiful and full of knives. You feel the pull of duty like a tide on your ankles.

[Music: String chord falls, a held, unresolved note]
[Background:"ch8_6b08b4_4"]

You look out over the marsh, at the dark line where water meets root, at the LuxCorp banners rippling like a threat. You know what each route might demand—visibility, secrecy, or immediate action—and you know equally well the cost of being wrong.

Kaito Sato waits. Tomas waits. The town's breath lengthens with yours.
