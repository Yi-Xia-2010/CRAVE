### DRAFT
# Route: Route A


[Scene: Hana's Diner | Late Evening]
[Visual: Warm, dim interior; steam rises in soft coils from chipped mugs; a neon OPEN sign flickers through the rain-streaked window]
[Music: Low, minor-key piano; a distant, restless percussion like a heartbeat]
[Sound: The scrape of stools, the low clink of spoons, a kettle sighing; the rain on the diner roof keeps time with your breathing]

You set the papers on the counter and the diner leans in around them—Hana's apron rustles, the coffee machine hushes, and for a moment the room is all attention. The contract smells faintly of printer ink and someone else's certainty. A dark ring of coffee blooms on the top page where you rested your palm too long. Your tide-chart scarf is pulled higher against the draft; the braid of teal at your temple prickles with salt in the air.

Your brother's watch is heavy against your wrist, its small tick a private metronome. You feel the weight of all the decisions that have led to this evening collecting like brine under your ribs. You brought Lux's draft back to the place that still feels like home—the diner where town arguments taste like tomato soup and the votes of old ladies are barked over pie. Here, choices become people again, and that makes them harder.

Hana lifts the pot and pours. Her smile is quick and efficient; she sets a mug in front of you before you can say anything.

Hana Kim: "You look like you need more than one cup, Maya." (she taps the papers with one flour-dusted finger) "Lux's handwriting finally on paper. How bad is it?"

You: "Meticulous," you say, and the word tastes like metal. "Hungry, too. It reads like a map with no room for our edges."

Hana exhales. The diner seems to listen—Tomas's placid silhouette is absent, but his presence is felt in the salt-worn benches and the hooks along the wall that hold nets and stories.

Hana Kim: "Sit. Tell me shape it takes in your mouth. Then we'll see whether it needs to be spit out."

You fold your hands around the warm mug, feeling the dampness of the contract under your fingers through the paper—pages softened at the corners where rain found them earlier. You read the language as if it were a tide: clauses that push and clauses that pull, parentheses that remove people from the map. Every line that promises security makes a small cut somewhere else.

Kaito arrives with the wet of the harbor clinging to his jacket—brine in the seams, a smudge of grease and solder on one cuff. He moves as if the lab he left behind has followed him to the counter; a small sensor pendant blinks against his shirt like a heartbeat that won't stop. When he sees the papers, his fingers hesitate at the edge of the page as if afraid to touch a wound.

Kaito Sato: "They sent it already?" (he asks; there is steadiness there, but you can hear the question forming into a plea) "Did you—how much time did they give you?"

You: "Until morning," you say. "Lux wants an answer at the forum tomorrow. They promise a faster build. They promise lives saved."

Kaito sets down his satchel gently as if not to jar anything fragile. He leans closer and his voice drops to the kind of soft engineers use when coaxing a stubborn prototype.

Kaito Sato: "Faster can mean safer. But faster can also mean 'our choices are removed.' Maya, read it with me—who does the wording give the right to displace? Who gets buyout funds first? Who decides what 'protected' neighborhoods mean?"

You feel the map of Harborfall rearranging under the table. His sea-green eyes search yours. There is data in them, and something more: trust. He trusts numbers, and he trusts you; sometimes he trusts the two together before either one has proven itself.

You: "If I sign, construction starts fast. If I push for control clauses, Lux will bristle—maybe withdraw. If I expose it, we invite chaos and investor threats. None of it feels clean."

Kaito presses his thumb to his lip as if testing for the right words.

Kaito Sato: "I built sensors to show where the berms actually need reinforcement, not where a line on a map says 'protect.' We can argue for a hybrid: immediate barriers where lives are on the line, and funded nature-based restoration for the rest. But that needs time and legally binding language that keeps local hands on the levers."

You look at the phrase "legally binding" and taste the battle ahead. Lux's offer already reads like a ledger where aesthetic words—'protection,' 'efficiency'—are laid over the places names that matter to you more than any metric.

Hana watches the two of you with the small, fierce calm of someone who runs a room by knowing when to throw a towel on it and when to let it simmer.

Hana Kim: "You can make a list of what you won't give, Maya. Tell me the red lines. We'll shout them at the forum til people's teeth chatter. Or you can try to keep the lawyers busy until the town decides. Or—" she looks at you over the rim of her mug, "—you can just burn the thing and wait for the storm to come. I don't recommend burning contracts."

You: (inside) The thought of burning Lux's glossy pages feels almost like a prayer. It would be loud and honest. But prayers don't stop tides.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Ask Hana to rally the regulars tonight") -> Reaction: "Hana nods and starts listing names—Tomas, the dock crew, the gardeners upstairs. Convening people will bring voices; it will also leak strain, but you like the honesty." || Choice B ("Keep it contained; call only Tomas privately") -> Reaction: "You choose restraint. Hana's eyes flick with worry, but she slides a note to you—Tomas's number. Keeping it narrow keeps leverage, but you feel the cost of the withheld truth like a stone in your pocket."]

Kaito runs a hand through his hair. He looks more tired than you feel; you know part of that is guilt—he left once, and now he carries both the tools that could help and the guilt of having been part of solutions that ignored people. He sets a small tablet on the counter and scrolls to a schematic—an overlay of the town and LuxCorp's proposed footprint. He doesn't publish specifics; he points instead to relationships between scale and displacement.

Kaito Sato: "If the wall takes the coves, those fishing moorings go. Tomas's nets won't be the same. The data doesn't lie: a footprint that big will require landward compensations. They use a phrase—'coastal realignment'—that sounds like planning-speak but means people's houses and rites will be rearranged."

You: "Tomas will mend what he can. He will not take money as a salve. He'll take a map and ask what it will mean for the bones of this place."

Kaito nods. His eyes don't blink away from the implicature—that some things are not fixable by money.

Kaito Sato: "Listen. Lux's team wants certainty. Their investors want boxable risks. They will sign on for speed if you let them own the levers. If you hold legal power here—contractual requirements for local governance—you keep the right to say no to things that erase memory. It will slow everything. Lux may threaten to pull. She may—"

You: "She may do what she says—save people." The sentence is flat, like something cut to fit a frame. "She used my name tonight like a promise. Her smile at the plaza was empty and bright at once—complex. I can't read it for what used to be there."

Kaito's expression goes unreadable for a beat. He reaches out and balances the edge of the paper with his fingertips.

Kaito Sato: "She used to be someone who... cared. Maybe still does. People change into the armor they think will protect them. Whether Lux is a shield or a blade now, I don't know. I know the timeline she gives us: fast is useful, but it's not an answer without protections."

You feel the tired harden inside your chest. The town's want is a tangible thing: faces in the greenhouse, hands that patch nets, the mayor's tired ledger of votes. You can taste the salt and the metal of compromise together—the mix is bitter.

Outside, rain begins to smear the neon sign into watercolor. The bell over the diner door tinkles and someone leaves; Harborfall is doing its small nightly rituals, oblivious to the legal architectures sliding like dark tides over tomorrow.

Hana pushes a plate of leftover stew toward you.

Hana Kim: "Eat something. Decide better on a full stomach. Then talk to Tomas. He'll say it plain. He always does."

You take a mouthful. The stew is hot and real, a small human anchor. Tomas's patched hands appear in your head—the way they fold rope, the scar on his knuckle where a spring tide once bit. You imagine the coves as places with names that live like people.

You: (inside) Any clause I accept will become a wound in the town's history. I am tired. I am tired in a way that makes my jaw ache and my patience thin. Tomorrow the forum wants a clean answer; the town will want to sleep easier. You keep thinking of the weight of that sleep—who will sleep on cement and who on sand.

Kaito leans back, the light catching his sensor pendant. He exhales slowly.

Kaito Sato: "Whatever you decide, I will be with the technical work. If you sign, I'll make the sensors report on the construction's impact. If you insist on governance clauses, I'll write them in ways that matter for enforcement. If you leak it—" he stops, searching your face, "—I'll help you frame it so it's honest and careful."

You look at him, at Hana, at the damp contract like a pale animal. The room presses in like a tide. Outside on the promenade, the town's lights blink—small beacons under a restless sky.

You rise, the chair scraping, and the small urgency of movement sharpens everything.

The forum is in the morning. The watch on your wrist ticks the thin hours away. The decision is not only legal or tactical; it is a moral incision that will be visible for years.

[Music: Piano chords descend, unresolved; the rain increases to a percussion of nails on the roof]
[Visual: Close on the contract's header; LuxCorp's logo glints, then blurs with a drop of water]

You stand at the edge of many possible harms and a single, aching desire: save people, but at what cost to the place that made them who they are?

You inhale and the air is salt and coffee and the metallic tang of worry.

You: (inside) I will have to choose. Any path will leave marks. Any path will ask me to name a loss.

You look down at the papers one last time. The town wants your answer tomorrow at the forum.
