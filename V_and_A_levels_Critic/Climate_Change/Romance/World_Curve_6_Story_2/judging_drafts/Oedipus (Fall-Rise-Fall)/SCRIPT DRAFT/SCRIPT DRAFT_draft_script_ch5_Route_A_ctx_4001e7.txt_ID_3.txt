[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Early evening — after Chapter 4]
[Visual: The converted warehouse glows with task lamps and blue LEDs; fog presses against the panes. A tangle of wires and modular barrier schematics crowd a workbench. Pinned notes flutter with each gust through the half-open door.]
[Music: Rapid, hopeful strings under a driving percussion — tempo building]
[Sound: Distant traffic of conversation, the soft churn of pumps, a notification ping from a tablet]

You step into the lab and the air wraps around you like a wet sleeve — cold, briny, threaded with the faint chemical sweetness of new adhesives and the warm, overbrewed coffee someone left on the bench. Your windbreaker still smells of the promenade: frying oil and salt, the town pressed into fabric. You press your palm to the brass compass at your throat. It bumps against your skin, ordinary and steady.

Elias is there, half-leaning over a monitor, sleeves rolled, a smear of mud on his knuckle. Nima paces near the prototype wall, tablet clutched like ballast. Two investors stand by the entrance in reflective raincoats, their voices measured, offering numbers with the confidence of people who plan spreadsheets as if they were tide charts.

Investor 1: "The pilot shows promise. Scale is straightforward if we centralize logistics and control. We'll add four more modules across the coastline in phase two — contingent on management rights for deployment and maintenance."

You feel the word "contingent" like a line tightened around your chest. The promise of modules is clean and bright the way sunlight looks through a storm: possible, tangible, and not without shadow.

Elias: (softly, to the investors) "We designed the modules to be community-installed. The point is local stewardship. We—"

Investor 2: (interrupting) "Local stewardship is a nice phrase. But for reliability at scale, uniformity matters. Our funders demand centralized oversight. We can fund everything — full deployment in eighteen months — if the council approves operational control."

Nima: (exchanging a look with Elias) "That's… a big ask. It ties up shoreline access, maintenance windows, and data rights. Investors want telemetry and remote patching."

You watch Elias's amber-brown eyes shutter for a fraction — a blink that closes like a curtain. His hand finds the leather camera at his hip and doesn't take it, as if choosing not to witness what he might have to sign.

You step closer, because you always move when things feel like they might fracture. Your voice answers before you rehearse the shape of it.

You: "The pilot's success was because people here cared enough to check sensors at dawn, to plant native grasses in the same beds as your planters. Outsourcing that to a distant operations team changes what 'success' means for Puerto Alba."

Investor 1: (without heat) "We don't mean to displace community effort. We mean to make it durable. A single team with authority reduces failure modes."

Elias: (hands spread, pleading) "Durability and displacement can be the same thing if you let them. There are models for cooperative governance — community trust funds, rotating stewardship. If we can weave that into the contract, you get both reliability and legitimacy."

The investor's smile is patient, like someone who has rehearsed objections in a mirror.

Investor 2: "Those clauses increase cost and complexity. Our board can accept oversight language — but not veto power for every minor maintenance decision. If you want faster deployment, concessions happen."

Your mouth tastes like copper; adrenaline lifts behind your ribs. This is the pivot everyone warned about. You remember Abuela Rosa's thorned hands in the marsh, how she once knelt to point at a reed, naming the season. You remember Mateo's laugh under the market awning when a sensor finally registered a calm tide. You remember the way Sofía chalked a child-sized map and labeled the spots where she wanted to keep playing.

A soft scrape at the lab door draws your head toward the lobby. Abuela Rosa arrives like a steady tide — a shawl wrapped tight, eyes clear as old glass. She doesn't speak first. She places a small envelope on the bench between you and Elias, pressing it down with a practiced hand. Inside, a ribbon of sea-grass is pressed flat and beside it a note, her handwriting small and sure.

[Visual: Close-up of the note; the words are legible: "Remember what the sea asks of us. — Abuela Rosa"]
[Sound: The lab's hum seems to dim; for a moment, only the wet rasp of the world outside exists]

You slide a finger under the ribbon and feel the faint crunch of salt-dried fronds. Words rise in your chest — the kind you carry when the town leans on you.

You: (quiet) "Abuela gave me this. She—" (you look at Elias) "—wants us to remember why we started."

Elias: (voice caught) "She always knows."

Nima: (to the investors) "We can draft community governance clauses. We can set escrow for shoreline decisions, audits, sunset provisions. But this requires time and shared oversight mechanisms."

Investor 1: "Time costs money."

Your pulse accelerates. The lab feels smaller; the screens, the wires, the very light seem to lean toward you as if waiting to see which heartbeat will set the tempo. Hope presses at the edges — the uptick of possibility that you can stitch governance into scale — but the watchword is urgency. The investors' funding could mean sensors where there were none, rapid reinforcement where erosion will bite next winter. The community governance clauses could ensure the town keeps its voice. The door narrows to either-or, but the edge you stand on is not the sharp despair of loss — it is a razor of responsibility balanced with promise.

The investors shift. One of them taps a tablet and projects a sample clause on the wall: operational control, data transfer, priority access to shoreline easements for module emplacement. The sentence hangs like a legal net.

Investor 2: "We can draft an amendment that allows a community advisory board, but it would be non-binding. You understand what 'non-binding' implies in scaling contexts."

You want to name what 'non-binding' feels like: a promise you can't keep. Instead you say what you can carry without breaking.

You: "Non-binding is not governance. It's optics. We are not a sticker on a machine."

Elias: (low) "If we accept non-binding language, it becomes something future councils can't claw back. I… I don't want to sign away the very thing the town relies on."

Nima: (stepping in) "There are hybrid legal forms. Cooperative charters. Place-based covenants that stay with land use — they're enforceable."

The investors exchange a glance, the kind that counts risk in decimal points. Behind them, the lab's blue LEDs throw their faces into something almost sympathetic. The mood isn't hostile yet — it's a negotiation of survival and values — but pressure is a current, and you can feel it tugging at decisions that will become precedent.

[Scene: Council Hall | Later — public corridor leading into the meeting chamber]
[Visual: Fluorescent lights, protest placards leaning against the lobby wall, the bell rung earlier now ringing softly as people file in. Rain taps the terrace outside like separate, impatient fingers.]
[Music: Drum hits, brass accent — tempo climbing, urgent but steady]
[Sound: Murmurs of conversation, the wooden clack of a cane, a child's shoe squeaking on wet tile]

The council chamber smells of old varnish and paper. People are gathered — volunteers, a few investors now flanked by municipal aides, Abuela Rosa at the back, Mateo scanning the crowd like a sentinel. Iris stands near the dais, the municipal lapel pin catching the light. She hasn't yet spoken to you; her posture is between politician and captain — disciplined, but not closed.

Iris: (approaching, measured) "Mara. Elias. We should talk before this becomes a vote."

Her voice is not an accusation; it's the kind of offering that weighs what it expects in return.

You: (to Iris) "We were just shaping language. The town needs safeguards."

Iris: (tilting her head) "I can broker a conditional contract. With my signatory authority and binding municipal oversight wording, we can get the investors to accept stronger conditions. But it requires that the council be a co-signer. It also means municipal authority will be involved in enforcement."

Your breath hitchs. Iris's offer is a hand extended across a churning sea. It promises leverage — the kind that could lock in protections and accelerate deployment. But it would also bring bureaucracy into neighborhoods that have long skated along on communal rules and informal reciprocity.

Elias: (searching both of you) "That's… tempting. If Iris will help make the governance binding, that could be a path to both speed and accountability."

You: (thinking of Abuela's note, of Sofía's chalked map) "Or it gives the municipality the final say on shoreline rights. Our people might be protected, but they might lose places they call theirs."

Iris: (softening) "I won't take shoreline away without community consent. But I need political cover. If the township backs it, I can hold investor demands to a minimum and negotiate binding clauses."

Nima: (whisper to Elias) "We can propose escrowed funds, independent auditors, and a rolling clause review. Investors hate uncertainty, but they respect enforceability if the political risk is contained."

You feel tension coalesce like clouds: each option offers shelter, but where the roof lands will change the house. Your chest pounds with a new energy — not panic, but an urgent, electrified hope. This is the kind of pressure that makes you sharp; you can taste the possibility of a compromise that preserves what matters and protects what could be lost.

Abuela's note thumps again quietly in your pocket, a pulse to match your own. The room leans forward; faces look to you. Your sea-green eyes lock with Elias's, and he meets you halfway across the impossible distance of decisions. You sense his idealism bracing against the practicalities of his role, his optimism tempered into something unsteady but resolute.

You step to the dais with all the small ceremonial weight of a town in your palms. Your voice will be heard across this room; it will shape the clauses, the press release, the next month of living. The arousal builds — a high, purposeful pressure — and your choices bloom into the focus you need.
