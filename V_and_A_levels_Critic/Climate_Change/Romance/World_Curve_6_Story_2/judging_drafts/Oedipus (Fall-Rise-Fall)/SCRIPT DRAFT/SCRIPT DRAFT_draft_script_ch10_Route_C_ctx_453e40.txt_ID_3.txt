You breathe as you push through the doors. The room smells of coffee and damp coats, paper and disinfectant. Cameras angle like curious birds. Every face is a small, weathered map of what Puerto Alba has already lost and what it still risks losing.

You feel the compass at your throat, a cold weight under your scarf. It was a choice you made back in the meeting room — the hybrid — and now you live inside the consequences of that choice: meetings, clauses, maps, people waiting for you to keep your promise.

Iris waits at the dais, hands folded like an anchor. Her jaw is a line. Elias stands a few seats down, sleeves rolled, a printout of sensor schematics tucked under his arm. Nima's presence is a quick current — efficient, alert, a tablet glowing with messages you do not want to see.

Iris Varela: "Mara." (the name is formal, the net casting wider than politeness) "Explain again why municipal support should hinge on—" (she holds up a sheet of paper) "—this patchwork of wetlands and volunteer labor, when we can sign a contract today and have measured barriers before the next season?"

You feel your mouth move before you can quiet the momentum in your chest. You have rehearsed this a hundred times, and rehearsal is not the same as listening to the room rearrange itself around your words.

You (Mara): "Because measured barriers are not the only thing the shoreline needs. Concrete alone shifts the problem and strips communities of stewardship. If we pair modest defenses with marsh restoration, we keep living shorelines, livelihoods, and decision-making local. The investment must be conditional — overseen by the council and the community, not handed over wholesale."

Iris: (lash of impatience) "Conditional funding becomes delay. Delay becomes exposure. How many times, Mara? How many storms before the town runs out of time?"

Elias: (leaning forward, voice warm but fast) "There's a technical middle ground. Low-footprint modular units, marsh buffers—if we integrate sensor feedback we can prioritize packages and stage construction without cutting the community out. Conditional doesn't have to mean slow. It can mean staged, accountable rollout."

Nima: (dry, precise) "Investors need milestones and KPIs. They fund outcomes. If your conditions are too diffuse, funding stalls. We can write tight conditions, but they must be measurable. Otherwise, the collective patience — and the money — evaporates."

You hear the click of a pen. Papers slide. The room feels like a drum being tuned: tightened, ready to snap or sing.

Iris: "And who coordinates this community stewardship? Who guarantees maintenance? Who backs the council's authority when some homeowners refuse to participate?"

You: "Abuela Rosa's network, youth teams led by Sofía, volunteer brigades coordinated through the marketboard — it's already starting to organize. We map plots, create cooperative agreements, and attach clear maintenance obligations to municipal support. If a homeowner opts out and later expects relief, the ledger will show participation or the lack of it."

Iris's face doesn't soften. She taps her tablet; an email preview flickers for a second like a blade.

Iris: (quiet, insistent) "Paper promises are not walls. Walls hold. People sleep easier with walls."

Elias: (gently) "Paper is a tool. It's not the same as abandoning people. If we agree on legally binding conditions that attach stewardship to funding packages, we can bind the protection to local governance."

The exchange speeds up: council members interrupt, someone shouts for clarity on liability, an investor's representative asks about return timelines. Your pulse races; the percussive loop underlines it — a soundtrack to pressure.

You find yourself steering through statistics one moment and negotiating trust the next. This is the work you chose. It is grueling in ways that the notebooks and field sketches did not prepare you for.

Sofía: (breathless, buoyant, barked orders) "Two rows here! Tag the GPS points! Don't step on the estuary tray—Mateo, your team holds that line!"

Elias: "We can stagger sensor deployment to monitor sediment accretion. If readings show good retention, we unlock the next tranche of modular supports. It's programmatic — direct feedback to investors and an automatic release mechanism."

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Evening]
[Visual: Converted warehouse, LED panels casting blue light on wet notebooks and damp boots; a whiteboard thick with conditional clauses]
[Music: Frictional electronic pulse; the tempo quickens]
[Sound: Printer whirring, urgent whispers, a notification tone that repeats like a metronome]

Back at the Labs, the procedural machinery runs at full speed. Legal drafts litter a table like a battlefield map. Nima paces, tablet in hand.

Nima: "Investors are asking for a clause: if ecological metrics lag two quarters, they're allowed to switch to accelerated structural deployment. It's a protective measure, but it undermines the conditional commitment."

Elias: (tense) "We can build guardrails — a remediation clause where the council can request technical assistance before any rapid pivot. Transparency windows that require a communal review."

You: "We need a veto of sorts — community review panels that can hold a temporary stay while experts propose alternate measures that don't abandon the marsh."

Nima: (raising an eyebrow) "A veto will spook some backers. They'll call it politicizing a technical instrument."

You: "Then we write it in as a governance mechanism, not an obstruction. Municipal oversight with a scheduled review timeline. It's still measurable and predictable."

Elias: "Also—" (he taps the tablet) "—we can build triggers that notify both investors and the town with the same data stream. No black box. If they see the same numbers, there's less room for panic."

Your energy frays at the edges. Each sentence is a negotiation between two kinds of time: ecological rhythms that take months to show results and investor time-horizons that demand returns in quarters. You trade clauses like fishermen swap nets: cautiously, precisely, with an eye to what will tear.

[Sound: A stern ringtone slices through the room. Iris's name flashes on a council line. She will call; she will push. You brace.]

Iris (on speaker): "We cannot gamble with lives because a few people prefer salt grasses to concrete. Show me the legal backbone or we pursue the contract. The town cannot be stuck."

Elias: (soft) "We are not gambling. We are building accountability into the agreement."

Iris: "Then present it. Tomorrow. And be concise."

The line clicks dead like a seal closing.

You: (internal) There is a vote scheduled — municipal support on the line. The clauses on your table must look forthright, airtight, and plausible to both a council member worried about damage and an investor worried about loss. You rewrite sentences until they scream on the page.

[Visual: Close on your eyes; sea-green rimmed with the exhaustion you feel; your fingers bruise the edges of the paper]
[Music: Crescendoing strings; tempo racing]

Word spreads through the town: maps pinned at the marketboard, youth teams texting meeting points, Abuela's planting song replayed by mothers making supper. For a time the pace is feverish but unified. People move like a single organism reacting to a moving threat. It feels, in the moment, like everything that could be done is being done.

But complexity breeds friction. An elderly homeowner resists cooperative elevation of his porch. A small business owner fears construction closures during tourist season. An investor calls for faster metrics. A council member demands a guarantee that their ward won't lose access to the marina.

The pressure stews into something heavier.

You: (to Elias, quietly, near the lab door) "Are we moving too slow for them to believe in us?"

Elias: (his voice low, the usual easy warmth threaded with sharpness) "We're moving as fast as the marsh lets us, and as fast as the law will allow. Fast enough to be credible, slow enough to be honest. That balance—" (he looks at you) "—is what will sell it."

You: "And if Iris brings the rapid-contract motion? If the emergency clause triggers and the council folds?"

Elias: (measured) "Then we show the data. We show the participation. We show that this is not an experiment but a governance solution. We get people to see the plan as both protective and sustaining."

Nima: "We need the municipal resolution to include staged release conditions, an oversight panel, and a remediation schedule. I'll draft the clauses tonight. Investors will read them in the morning. If they balk, we adjust the weighting, not the principle."
