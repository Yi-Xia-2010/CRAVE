[Scene: Seawall | Late Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of the seawall at low tide — sun blunted by a high cloud deck; gulls wheel in the distance]
[Sound: Dull slap of water on concrete, the steady scrape of metal against rock]
[Music: Sparse piano chord, slow tempo]

You arrive with the crew while the tide is still generous and forgiving. Mateo is already there, sleeves rolled, a strip of salt drying on his cheek like a pale scar. He moves with the economy of someone who has hauled too many loads to count — no flourish, only the motion that keeps things from falling apart.

Mateo: "About time. You gonna stand there sketching tides all day, or lend a pair of hands?"

You: "Both," you say, and set your notebook down where you can reach it. Your gloves smell of diesel and old rope. The barnacled concrete bites at your knuckles when you scrape it; grit presses into the pads of your fingers until they lose sensation and become all work and pain.

You work in rhythm with the others: heave, brace, push. The seawall is old and queer with age — patched plates, narrow seams, the stubborn barnacles clinging like memory. When the wind finds the cracks, it whistles through them like a voice you half-remember.

Mateo hums as he works, a tune he says his father used to whistle on the boat. It's both comfort and a dare: keep moving, keep living. You answer with quiet shifts, catching your breath in time with the pull of ropes. Your muscles remember the motions; your mind does not let them blunt the worry in your chest.

Mateo: (pausing to catch his breath) "They're saying the contractors might start as early as next week, if Iris gets it. Heard anything?"

You: "A memo came through. Short, sharp." The taste of salt is real on your tongue, but the sentence tastes like metal. You fold that information into the work, as if you could sand it down into something manageable.

Mateo: "She's not wrong about needing hands. But 'short and sharp' — doesn't mean it's right."

You: "No." The word is small, a pebble thrown into the tide of what must be said. You feel every look that drifts in your direction: neighbors, volunteers, tired children watching from the raised walkways. They are counting on steadiness you are not sure you own.

A low wind lifts the edge of your scarf. You find your fingers moving to the shell pendant at your throat, polishing the chipped curve as if you could buff away the rest of the ache. The pendant is a talisman and a small accusation: you promised to protect this place and its people; promises are heavy.

Mateo watches you choose, the corners of his mouth softening whether you move muscle or mind. Nothing about his expression reads like judgment; he trusts work in whatever form you give it.

Mateo: "Abuela's been down a few times. Left some of her maps on the bench. Says the old channels might take pressure off that eastern span if we try to redirect flow to the reeds."

You: "She always has a map for a wound." The remark comes out tender, resentful, blessed. Abuela Rosa's maps are old as the town and twice as stubborn. You think of the stories she told — of tides with personalities, of sand that remembers — and you tuck that memory away like an extra glove.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Converted warehouse, damp concrete, a scatter of sensors and prototype pylons]
[Sound: Low hum of equipment, the clack of Elias adjusting a dial]
[Music: Muted synth pad, sustaining a single melancholy note]

Elias is at the edge of a shallow test pool, wrist sensor taped to his forearm as if it were prosthetic. He moves with the careful pleasure of someone calibrating something precise and new. The sensor chirps softly as he nudges settings; the sound is almost hopeful.

Elias: (without looking up) "We can start a pilot here. Modular pylons that break wave energy, let mud settle. Low footprint, adaptable. If we place them in staggered rows, the erosion slows without sealing the coast off."

You: "And the timeline?" Your voice is lower than his optimism; the question is small but sharp. Optimism is a currency you have learned to exchange cautiously.

Elias: "Months for the prototype to be sea-worthy. Weeks to set the first modules if funding's quick. We can test the sedimentation every fortnight." He smiles, the way someone reaches for a lighthouse in fog. "If they work, we won't have to build a full wall."

You: (watching the sensor's readings) "If investors pull, if the company pushes scale... will the design hold? Or will it become a pretext for a bigger, harsher structure?"

Elias: (pauses, fingers still) "I won't let it. Nima says our backers are—" He stops himself, a small flush of reluctance. "They're interested in scaling, yes. But the collective's ethos is community-first. I want a humane rollout. I want it to be ours."

There's a hesitation in his words you can taste — sincerity laced with the fear of being broken by his own idealism. He looks at you like someone hoping you will anchor him.

You: "You're good with numbers, Elias. But numbers don't hold grief. They don't hold someone's lost home. They don't make a town a town."

Elias: "They can buy time for other things to work, though. Education, marsh restoration." He shifts, the sensor beeping anew. "Please. Trust that I'm trying to build something that doesn't overwrite what people already know."

You: "Trust has to be earned." The statement hangs between you like a flayed sail.

Elias: "Then ask me. Keep asking me. Make me prove it."

Your conversation unspools — question and answer, a scrape of honesty. He offers data; you narrow your eyes and push for context. He offers concessions; you test their edges. It's not an argument; it's a slow, necessary examination of whether technology with a human face can withstand the pressure of capital.

Elias's hand brushes yours for a second when he hands you a damp sensor calibration card. It is the smallest of intimacies: technical touch, practical and warm. Your chest tightens, not just with decision but with the small ache that has been collecting between you both for months. Words unspoken hang like fishing nets.

Nima, hovering in the doorway with an insulated mug, calls over in a business cadence that smells faintly of compromise.

Nima: "Elias, short memo from Iris. Wants the council to accept a contract — immediate deployment of corporate engineers. She says it'll be faster than proposals like this and that funding is guaranteed if the town signs."

Elias: (hand on the doorframe) "Guaranteed is a dangerous word. What are the strings?"

Nima: "They weren't listed. She said the town will have to trust leadership on discretion."

The phrase "trust leadership on discretion" tastes like a stump pulled from wet soil: soft center, rot at the roots.

[Scene: Council Hall | Dusk]
[Visual: Municipal building bathed in a gray-gold light; banners and placards rest against the railings]
[Sound: Murmured conversations, the distant cry of a protest, a single bell marking the hour]
[Music: A slow, low cello line]

By the time evening rolls in, the town's edges fray. Sofía arrives with a ragged banner and a cohort of students, breathless with the adrenaline of righteous clarity. They hand out leaflets that smell faintly of printer toner and hope.

Sofía: "You heard? They're calling for secrecy. We deserve to know the contract. We deserve to know what they plan to trade away."

You: (kissing her forehead, a small ritual) "You speak for us. But don't let them shut you down in that room. Talk facts. Ask the hard ones."

Sofía: "You should be the one to ask them." Her eyes are sharp. Her childhood is a surprise you still adjust to.

Abuela Rosa moves through the crowd with a basket of boiled fish and a folded map under her arm. Her presence grounds the town; people reach for her like a shore line. She presses a hand to your cheek the way oceans press to cliffs.

Abuela Rosa: "The old channels remember. We can slow the storm if we coax the marsh back. But we can't play with fire and expect saplings to hold a house."

You: "A hybrid approach — tests, modules, community-led reed beds, and some reinforced structure where lives are at risk."

Abuela Rosa: (nods, slow and sure) "Balance. Not surrender."

Then the public notice goes up: a single stamped poster on the municipal bulletin board, the paper rough where fingers have already rubbed over the ink.

[Direction Cue: A municipal clerk pins the notice to the board; the camera lingers on the words]
[Visual: Close-up on the notice — bold letters: COUNCIL MEETING TONIGHT. ALL RESIDENTS REQUESTED TO ATTEND.]

Iris's memo had been brief, terse as a storm: accept the contract. The town divides in the way weather divides a shoreline — some pieces erode away cleanly, others break off and tumble.

You stand in the lobby of the Council Hall, spreadsheets and tide sketches folded in a damp stack at your hip. The lab's LED smear across the glass behind you, making halos around people's heads. Your sea-green eyes feel rimed with fatigue, the color of a weathered wave.

Voices overlap: Mateo discussing manpower for repairs; Elias promising a demo if he can secure an extra hour at the podium; Sofía rallying the young; Abuela Rosa humming an old chant under her breath, as if to will the tide to remember gentler ways.

Everything presses on your shoulders — the repairs your hands can still do, the tech Elias crafts with care, Iris's offered security that demands quick faith. Each path has consequences you can already taste, and each loss feels like an erosion of something that will not return.

You polish the chipped shell pendant one last time and slide it back beneath your shirt where it rests warm against your ribs. The pendant's worn curve is a small counterweight to the town's larger tilt.

Sound narrows to the ringing in your ears. Your breath is shallow and careful, as if you might split the moment by inhaling too eagerly.

A public announcement is made. The hall will open at dusk, and everyone is urged to come. It is already dusk.

You look up at the balcony where council members will sit like a thin cord across the room, holding the town's decision in their hands. Your voice will be needed, you know this with a bone ache that runs through the rest of your body.

[Visual: The light in the hall shifts; outside, the first smear of rain begins]
[Music: A single held note resolves into a soft pause]

Page-turn thought: You breathe in the smell of wet paper and salt, and for a moment you imagine the words you will say — blunt, precise, impossible to ignore. They hover on your tongue like a bird unsure of the next wingbeat.

You stand at the doors of the Council Hall feeling pulled in three directions, the town's future balanced between hands-on repair, Elias's measured optimism, and Iris's urgent, unforgiving contract. The night will make the choice louder than the day ever could.
