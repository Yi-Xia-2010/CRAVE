You walk through the lab door with the weight of the council chamber still under your ribs. The brass compass at your throat is cool; the notebook in your windbreaker's inner pocket is damp from a thumbprint of ink. You supported Elias. You said the words the room needed to hear, and the town shifted with that sound. Now the work begins.

The air inside is warm and layered with smells that belong to experiments and lunchboxes: hot tea, oil from machinery, the faint, mineral tang of sea-salt drying on concrete. Sensors hum on benches, their LEDs blinking like patient eyes. Nima, sleeves rolled to her elbows, meets you with a smile too quick for how much she has to carry.

Nima: "You made a face in there I didn't expect. Good face?" (she waves a hand toward the council hall, then gestures to the whiteboard)
You: "A face of commitment. And a little nervousness." (you shrug, settling into the rhythm of buttons and clamps) "We need liaison outlines. Community schedules. A volunteer plan that doesn't burn people out."

Elias stands by a table littered with prototype shells and filament spools, amber eyes folded into a careful, tired smile. He wipes his palms on his overalls and pushes a stray coil of wire back into place.

Elias: "We should start with three community hubs — marketboard, the promenade kiosk, Abuela Rosa's shed. People will trust familiar places." (he looks at you) "You were persuasive. If you can lead the liaison drafting with Nima, I can keep the sensors calibrated."

You feel the tug of being needed, the familiar electrical thrill: good work to do, hands to steady. You take the whiteboard marker and draw a rough map in your looping script, plotting outreach, training shifts, and sensor clusters near eelgrass beds.

[Sound: The creak of rubber boots through the lab; distant gulls]

You assign volunteer teams. You write Sofía's name by "school outreach" because she already came to you, wide-eyed and ready, skateboard under one arm and a stack of tide posters in the other. You put Mateo on logistics — he can move boats and get things where they need to be without ceremony. Abuela Rosa is, of course, listed as "marsh guardian" despite her protestations. She punches the air in mock victory with a wooden spoon.

Abuela Rosa: "You young ones will listen. The marsh remembers what the maps forget." (she runs a palm over a tray of sprigs, salt-sweet and green) "Not everything is new. We move the grass up a ridge and it will do the remembering for us."

You: "Teach me where to plant. I'll make sure we don't crush the roots with heavy planters."

Abuela Rosa smiles, and for a moment the room loosens. Her hands smell of peat and boiling seaweed; her voice sets a steady pace to work. The plan is technical and old at once — sensors and seedlings, modular planters and stories passed from one generation to the next.

[Scene: Marsh Planting | Low Tide]
[Visual: Wide shot of the marsh — peat ridges, muddy flats, and the glint of standing water; volunteers moving like the slow choreography of a flock]
[Sound: Thud of shovels, murmured instructions, children laughing; the sea hiss of distant water]
[Music: Low, hopeful strings — steady, rising]

You get mud on your knees within an hour. Your windbreaker takes a trophy of brown and salt: splashes that make a map of the day across fabric. Activation stickers — bright orange tabs — are pressed onto planters and overalls, stickered like small medals. Sofía runs a half-circle of kids, eyes bright, explaining the tide like it's a secret they've been trusted with.

Sofía: "When the water comes high, the grass eats it first and lets it go. If we teach the grass to be higher, it can guard the shore." (she demonstrates with a stick) "And sensors tell us when the water tries to trick us."

Child: "Will the sensors cry if the water's mean?"

You crouch to tie a knot in a biodegradable tie and find yourself smiling despite salt and mud. The children giggle; their blunt, earnest questions make the work feel less like a plan and more like a living thing. You hand a child a shovel. The soil is soft and smells of rot and fecundity; when you press the seedlings in, you feel a quiet firmness in the world.

Mateo, wiping his forehead, nods toward the line of sensor beacons you've just tested.

Mateo: "They pinged clean through last night. If the tide jumped like the models said, we'd see it."

You: "And then we act. That's the point."

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Afternoon — Prep for Demo]
[Visual: Benches cleared, sensor rigs arranged like obedient soldiers; investors' rented chairs stacked near the door]
[Sound: Camera shutters from a local blogger; the soft zip of investor tablet cases]
[Music: A gentle uptempo motif — measured excitement]

Nima coordinates logistics with the serenity of someone used to translating between languages — grant-speak and fisher-speak, algorithm and anecdote.

Nima: "They arrive at three. We run the demo at high tide plus the modeled surge window. If the sensors and planters respond as expected, it's a data win."

Elias nods; his voice is low but animated, the engineer's tremor of someone about to share a hypothesis with the world.

Elias: "I tuned the humidity thresholds to account for salt aerosol. The modular planters redistributed weight as designed — they flexed rather than sheared in last night's test."

You: "If it performs, we have air for everyone who doubted. If it doesn't, we know more than before."

The investors arrive in suits that have never seen salt and in smiles practiced for a thousand boardrooms. Their shoes make clean prints on the lab's concrete. They cluster politely, phones out. A local journalist, breathless and eager, asks you for a quote. You give one: measured, honest. You feel Elias at your elbow, needing nothing and everything in the same breath.

Investor 1: "This—it's promising. Scale faster, and the mitigation effect compounds. The market is ready."

Elias: (hesitant) "Scaling only after iterative community testing. We need more seasons to validate ecological impacts."

Investor 2: "Seasons cost money. We'd like to see a pilot that scales within six months."

Nima: (a small, careful laugh) "We could explore phased scaling with covenants. Community governance clauses ensure—"

Investor 1: (interrupting, smooth) "That's legal language we can work out. The important part is deployment."

You listen, recording the cadence of priorities. The investors speak growth and timelines; Elias speaks thresholds and learning. The room, which felt like a community hub in the morning, becomes an arena of priorities in soft suits and work-scarred hands.

[Scene: Promenade — Storm Simulation Test | Dusk]
[Visual: Artificial surge machines pumping water toward the planted line; sensors blinking live data to a projector]
[Music: Quietly tense, then resolving to a clear note]

The first simulated swell comes like practice for a real grief. The planters hold; the marsh breathes; the sensor array reports values that fall within safer bands than the model's worst-case. People clap, not wildly but with the relieved breath of a town that has passed a quiz it feared.

You feel hope in the clapping — not the triumphant kind, but a clean, utilitarian relief. Elias grins, slightly astonished.

Elias: "We underpredicted resilience in the edge conditions. The planters redistributed erosive flow better than the simulations suggested."

Nima: "Data's clean. We can publish a preprint and use it to apply for expansion funds."

A few friends swallow tears. Abuela Rosa stands with her hands tucked, watching the grass stand. Sofía, cheeks flushed, explains the graphs to a small group of teenagers as if they were obvious things.

Sofía: "See here? The pink line used to spike, but now it stays low. That means the sea didn't win this hour."

You stand on the promenade with the salt breeze in your hair, realizing the relief is practical: boats will have a safer harbor for now; families won't have to move tonight. It's not victory over climate — nothing in this work is victory — but it's enough to make supper feel like a future again.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Evening — After the Demo]
[Visual: Lab lamps glow amber; the projector sleeps; leftover coffee cools in paper cups]
[Sound: Low conversations, chairs scraping; a distant radio murmurs town updates]
[Music: Warm, low tones — satisfaction edged with thought]

The town breathes a little easier. You and Elias walk the lab's perimeter in companionable silence for a while, hands scrubbing salt from equipment. His shoulder brushes yours; the gesture is small, intimate in the way of people who have shared tension.

Elias: "You did good today. You kept people listening."

You: "You did the impossible — you made the machines listen back. We did it together." (you pause) "But their timelines worried me in the hall."

Elias draws a breath, weighing his words.

Elias: "Investors want speed. I want careful. We can have both if we build constraints into contracts." (he looks at you) "Are you with me on that? The liaison framework you helped build — it's the leverage we need."

You feel your stubborn empathy settle. You want to trust him. You want to believe careful work can satisfy hungry timelines and hungry wallets.

Nima joins you, holding a tablet with messages. Her face is open but there is a list in her eyes.

Nima: "We got a cold call from a corporate group — national-level. They want to discuss scaling. Their offer includes immediate funding and a roll-out timeline. They're glossy."

You: "They want fast scale?"

Nima: "Faster than Elias' preferred schedule."

The room tilts into the moment of choice, but not in the abrupt way of drama; it's the slow pull of two magnets. You are caught between the relief of a successful test and the pressure of those who would take the pilot and run.

Mateo: (from the doorway) "We should be careful. Money with strings has bitten us before." His voice is the practical counterpoint; it keeps the tension honest.

Abuela Rosa: (quiet) "Money is wind. It can lift you or drown you. You must build the walls with hands that will remain when the wind goes quiet."

You think of Sofía's wide certainty, of the children's hands in mud, of the way the sensors sang during the test. You think of investors' clean shoes and their timelines. The balance you defended in the hall now grows small and personal: how much of this place will be reshaped by contracts inked by people who have never smelled peat?

Elias: "We have options. Phased agreements, governance holdbacks, community oversight panels. I can draft proposals."

You: "We need them. We need to make sure the community holds the stopcock. If we hand over the pilot without it, we risk losing the thing that made it work."

Nima: (nodding) "I can push for covenants. But know that some investors will pull if they can't scale to their numbers."

You fold that sentence into your chest like another compass to carry: investors can leave; the town cannot. Your windbreaker smells of diesel and salt and a thin hope. The night cools. Your hands ache pleasantly from work.

You pull your notebook free and write one line across a clean page: "Protect governance. No rush that erases stewardship." The handwriting is a small, stubborn talisman.
