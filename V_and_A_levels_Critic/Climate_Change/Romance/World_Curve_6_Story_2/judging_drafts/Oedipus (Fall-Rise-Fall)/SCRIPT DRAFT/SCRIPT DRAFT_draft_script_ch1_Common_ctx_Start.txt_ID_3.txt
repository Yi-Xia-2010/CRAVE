You wake to the smell of brine and frying dough before you open your eyes — a smell that becomes a weather report and a memory all at once. Your jaw is tight from sleeping on one side; your windbreaker lies across a chair, still damp where you left it after trudging home yesterday. The copper tips of your hair drip slow beads of salt onto the floorboard. Your fingers fumble for the notebook on the bedside table: waterproof cover flecked with yesterday’s mud.

You sit up and let the room fill in: the chipped shell pendant on its thin chain, the worn leather of your compass cord that never quite returns to stillness, the tide sketches taped to the wall like a small, stubborn map of what you owe the town. You think of your father's boat — the hull listing in the boatyard where the salt has already eaten at the paint. There is a weight there, an unfinished work that settles in your chest like sediment.

You stand. The apartment smells of sea and oil from the vendor below; a neighbor clatters pans. You button the windbreaker tight against the chill and step outside.

[Scene: Puerto Alba Marketboard | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of a narrow, raised timber boardwalk lined with soaked stalls; strings of salt-streaked lights droop against a steel-gray sky]
[Sound: Soft, steady drip of water, the slap of a loose tarp in a light wind]
[Music: A single sustained cello note under the ambient sounds]

The marketboard is quieter than it should be. Half the stalls sit on improvised stilts, tarps looped over frames like makeshift roofs. Pans steam. Vendors move with small, efficient gestures — wrapping goods, wiping salt from surfaces, tacking up warning signs. The air tastes like salt and fried dough; gulls wheel and land on a sagging sign.

Mateo is crouched in front of an old bakery stall, sleeves rolled, hands thick with a paste of mud and flour. He looks up when you approach, and for a second you see the town in his thin, sun-browned face: stubborn, tired, practical.

Mateo: "You're late this morning. I thought you were out with the team at first light."

You: "Couldn't sleep. Tide sketches kept me up. How bad is it?"

Mateo: (shrugging) "Worst of it ran through the east row. Most of the boardwalk held, but two of the fish racks are gone. Abuela's stall took in a foot of water. We got a couple of old nets out to hold things, but—" He trails off, rubbing his fingers.

You crouch and let your fingers trace the salt pattern on the stall's planks. The wood has gone a gray that looks like old bone. Your fingertips smear ink from the notebook. The motion grounds you: the physical evidence of the tide, the salt, the things you can still touch and try to fix.

You: "I'll take a look at Abuela's stall. Then I'll check the boatyard."

Mateo: (softly) "Don't overdo it, Mara. You ran yourself ragged yesterday."

You: "Says the man who slept on a pile of tarps."

Mateo grins, but the smile doesn't reach his eyes. He nods toward the far end of the boardwalk, where Abuela Rosa's stall sits wrapped in a canvas sheet.

Mateo: "She asked for you. Said you have 'hands that remember the sea.'"

The words feel like a benediction and a rebuke.

You move toward Abuela Rosa's stall either way — because Abuela always pulls you close, regardless of your route. She is there before the canvas is fully raised, apron damp, hair braided down her back. When she sees you, something in her posture softens like sunlight through cloud.

Abuela Rosa: "Ah, mi niña." (She brushes salt from your hair with a thumb that smells of lemon and smoke.) "You look like the night took a piece of you."

You: (half-laughing) "You always say that. How is the stall?"

Abuela Rosa: "The bread is wet but not gone. The herbs were spoiled, but the recipes remember. Sit."

You sit on the low bench beside her, the wood cool and still damp. She produces an old, folded map from beneath a stack of papers — the paper edges browned, ink faded into a soft brown like old tea.

[Visual: Close-up of the old map; lines showing past high-water marks and hand-scrawled notes]
[Sound: The map unfolds with a soft crackle]

Abuela Rosa: "They used to draw lines in the salt with chalk and call it prophecy. This map—my mother used it. See these marks? Storm of '82, '97, '14." She taps a faded curve with a wrinkled finger. "Quick fixes were good for a season. Good for election speeches. But the sea remembers every borrowed season."

You: (voice low) "I know. I know. But if we don't... if Iris gets the council to sign with those folks, they’ll put up walls that cut off the estuary."

Abuela Rosa: (eyes like warm, brown glass) "And if nothing is done, we lose the boardwalk. We lose houses. My sister lost her fish traps when the wall collapsed in '97. She died thinking a concrete bank would hold her life together." Her voice lowers. "History is heavy."

You fold the map into your palms and let the weight of it sit between you. The warning is not new, but it lands harder this morning because the marketboard still smells like the tide.

Abuela Rosa: "You are stubborn. But stubbornness without rest becomes brittle."

You: "I can’t just... stand by. I have volunteers who will listen. I can organize repair crews, patch the planks, teach people how to bind the marsh grass—"

Abuela Rosa: "Organization is good. But listen to the slope as much as your hunger to fix. Don't let the technical words drown out the people who remember how the land bent."

Her counsel is a gentle friction against the urgency doubling in your chest. Your perfectionism — the part of you that tries to do everything and resentably calculates how to make it enough — bristles at being told to pace. You feel that bristle like a physical thing under your sternum.

You take the notebook from your pocket — the waterproof cover slick under your palm — and flip it open. The tide sketches look like small confessions: lines of crest and trough, a practice of trying to make the sea legible. Your fingers pick up a smear of mud and black ink; the smudge feels honest.

Abuela Rosa watches you write and nods once, small and sure.

Abuela Rosa: "You will rally them, yes. But bring old maps with you. Bring stories. Make them hard to ignore."

You tuck the map beneath your arm like a flag and head toward the Saltmarsh Labs, where Elias often tests sensors and where modern proposals are pinned to whiteboards like prayers written in blue Sharpie.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Late Morning]
[Visual: Converted warehouse; task lamps glow orange against blue LED panels; experimental rigs extend toward the estuary]
[Sound: Low hum of equipment; soft footsteps on damp concrete]
[Music: A single bowed violin, quiet and sustained]

Saltmarsh Labs smells like solvent and seaweed, warmed by the low heat of equipment. Muddy boots line the entryway. Inside, the floor holds little pools where someone dipped off tide boots and forgot the sun.

Elias stands near a table strewn with modular models — small, hyper-detailed blocks of recycled composite and planter boxes. He is halfway through explaining something when you arrive, hands animated. His voice is careful, practiced.

Elias: "—and the modules link like vertebrae. They flex with wave action, capture sediment at the right tide, and the plantings take root within a season. Low footprint, community-maintainable."

You: (watching the models) "They look... smaller than I pictured. How many would cover the eastern inlet?"

Elias: "Enough that we can build in phases. Start at the fish racks, move to the boardwalk. Investors want numbers, but Nima and I think of it like stitching a wound closed. Not cutting anything away."

Elias's optimism is a soft sort of light. He lays a module on the table between the two of you, and the little model looks vulnerable and earnest, like an answer carried in two hands.

You: "I worry about scale. And about who decides what gets built. Who maintains them? Who draws the line between protection and privatization?"

Elias: (sighing) "Those are fair worries. That's why I'm bringing this to you and to Abuela. We should design something the town can learn to run. Community training. Local fabrication. No one gets locked out."

You: "And Iris? She’s taking a different tack."

Elias's jaw tightens briefly. He is good at smiling away small ruptures, but the crease around his eyes betrays strain.

Elias: "She's got council support. There's pressure to show action. Investors are nervous. They want a single, fast lift. I'm asking them to slow down and let the prototypes prove themselves first."

You: "If Iris wins the argument tomorrow, we might not get 'first' to prove. We get 'done' and finished."

Elias: (softly) "I know."

There is a pause, a small weather front between you. He reaches out as if to close it with information: he pins a small schematic to a nearby board. The diagram is neat — tide lines, attachment points — but it is also a statement of intent.

Elias: "I'll be at the council meeting at dusk. I promised I'd answer questions. Not all of us want walls. Some of us want to try a different language—engineering that listens."

You: "I'll be there. But I won't let technical language drown out Abuela's history or the fishermen's voices."

Elias: (meeting your eyes) "I don't expect you to. And I don't want to be the one who brings change to you without asking how it will feel."

The exchange is warm and precise, like two machines learning to synchronize. Yet even as you and Elias speak, the lab door opens and a municipal noticebearer steps in, breathless, handing a paper to the receptionist. In his hand is a formal notice stamped with the municipal seal.

[Sound: The soft thump of a paper being set down]
[Visual: Close-up of the notice: embossed seal, formal type]

You cross to it like a current deciding its path. The notice has the tone of full stops and capital letters.

Receptionist: (reading aloud softly) "Council Notice: Special Session at Council Hall this evening. Mayor Varela will present 'Rapid Coastal Resilience Proposal' for immediate implementation. Public comment period at dusk."

Elias's face hardens in a way that is familiar from other fronts where optimism meets urgency. You feel the room tighten.

You: "Rapid. Immediate. She means large structural intervention."

Elias: "She called it 'the only option to prevent imminent loss.' The phrasing is... final."

Receptionist: "She said she'll be there to answer questions after the presentation. Also—" the woman flips the page — "Elias Park will be present to show alternative measures and take questions."

The two names together on the notice feel like a weather map with two storms converging. The rest of the lab hums on, indifferent: a pipette, a small fan, a lamp blinking.

You tuck the notice into your notebook, where sketches and smudges compete for space. You draw a quick tide line next to the printed text — the curve fits naturally there, as if to remind you that policy and water both bend.

Your chest has a dull, persistent ache you recognize as exhaustion. It's the kind that makes questions feel thicker — not impossible, but heavy. Your stubborn empathy asks you to gather people, to patch, to teach. You know, too, that the municipal language is already shifting toward terms that can sound like inevitability: "rapid," "structural," "prevent imminent loss."

You think of your father's boat again. You think of the patchwork of community fixes that held the marketboard for generations. You feel, in the marrow of your hands, both the impulse to rally volunteers and the bone-deep knowledge that your own efforts are limited.

On the walk back to the marketboard the town moves around you in small, precise motions: a vendor rearranges salt-damp fish; a child tucks a soggy poster under a nail; someone nails up a new public notice by Council Hall with gloved hands.

You notice a small, official-looking poster plastered to the municipal bulletin: the same notice, this time in bold. The words feel louder pasted against wood.

Poster Voice (distant, municipal): "Town Meeting — DUSK — Council Hall. Mayor Varela to present Rapid Coastal Resilience Proposal. Public comment to follow. Elias Park to demonstrate modular alternatives."

The afternoon leans toward a low, leaden sky. People move with a quiet gravity. Conversations have the texture of careful stitching: interruptions, quick clarifications, the kind of talk that repairs or avoids a rupture. You feel your pulse slow and then steady — low, deliberate.

Abuela Rosa finds you again among the stalls, her eyes steady.

Abuela Rosa: "Make sure the people with the smallest voices have seats at the hall."

You: "I will."

Abuela Rosa: (squeezing your hand) "And make sure you take a moment to breathe between believing you must carry everything and letting others take their part."

You try to imagine delegating, to trust others with the labor you've claimed, but perfectionism is a muscle you have long trained. It tenses when you think of leaving anything undone. Still, you take Abuela's words and tuck them somewhere that isn't your notebook: into the space between two breathes.

You open the waterproof notebook one more time and draw the tide again. Your lines are careful, not frantic; a methodical tracing of what the water did yesterday and what it might do tomorrow. The smudge of ink on your finger looks like a badge you can't wash off.

There is a meeting at dusk. Iris will speak with urgency and policy; Elias will answer questions and show prototypes. The town will assemble — some with placards, some with hands callused from work. You feel a small, steady ache that is part guilt, part obligation, part something that might be hope if you let it breathe.

You look up at the sky and then back down at the map in your hands. The announcement hangs between the two of you: a hinge that will swing the town toward one solution or another.
