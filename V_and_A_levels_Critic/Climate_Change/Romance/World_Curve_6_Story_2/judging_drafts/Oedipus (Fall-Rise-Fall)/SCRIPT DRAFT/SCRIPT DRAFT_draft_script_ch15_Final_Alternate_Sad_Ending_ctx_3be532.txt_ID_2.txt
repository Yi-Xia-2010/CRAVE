You wake before the sun decides to commit. The lighthouse smells of brine and old paint; the air inside is cool where the stone has stored the night's damp. Your hair clings to your neck, fringe darkened with salt. The compass at your throat is warm, an odd, small insistence that something is still moving even if your chest feels heavy and still.

Abuela Rosa is already awake — not because dawn wakes her but because she always chooses to be early where endings begin. She sits on the narrow bench by the round window, hands folded around a mug that breathes steam. Her braid hangs over her shoulder like a rope of silver; her face maps the town's losses in lines that have not softened. When she sees you, she does not smile; she does something quieter: she reaches for you.

Abuela Rosa: "Mara," she says — your name in her voice is both call and reckoning. "You have been carrying water for others until your feet forgot how to stand."

You cross the wooden floor, boots making a muted rhythm on planks softened by salt. The room is full of small, patient things: a stack of tide charts, a threadbare blanket, the map remnant Abuela folds like an offering. She lifts a coil of braided rope from beside her knee and lays it in your hands. It smells of cedar and oil and that particular dust of rope kept for years.

Abuela Rosa: "Take this. My sister used to tie us to the dock with the same color. It kept us from being taken when the surge came. It is not a promise it will keep you from losing. It is a tool. Hold it like you would hold a memory."

You run your thumb along the braid. Its fibers rasp under your skin, small and honest. She produces the map remnant — a ragged corner from a larger chart, edges browned and inked with notes in a hand you remember from markets and late audits. There is a single line drawn in faded red that encircles the estuary, and a word you have said aloud in your sleep: stewardship.

You want to say something permanent, something that will stop the town from unravelling when you are off the map. The words come too big for the space you have.

Abuela Rosa: (softly) "Do not try to carry everything, niña. You think leaving is abandonment. Perhaps it is instead a different kind of tending. Your hands can move farther away if your heart remembers how we tie ourselves back together."

You look at her face and then at the rope. You think of the meetings, the compromises, the way the town's edges wore thin against the new concrete wall, the hush that followed votes. You think of Elias's face tightening when he learned what the investors wanted; you think of how you soothed and then seethed. The town stands. It is not whole.

[Scene: Cliffside Promenade | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of tempest-scarred cliffs; the sea a soft, greying sheet; a small launch tied to a halyard below]
[Music: Single quiet string; the soundscape empties to the roar of distant water]
[Sound: Sparse footsteps on gravel; a faint cough of diesel down the incline]

Elias is waiting where you suspected he'd be: on the lower path that descends to the harbor, hands jammed in the pockets of his vest, eyes fixed on the water as if the future could be coaxed from tide-lines. The air around him smells faintly of machine oil and the sensor gel he carries at his belt. He looks smaller than you remember in some frames and still steadier in others.

Elias Park: (voice tight) "I heard you went up to the light this morning."

You stop a few paces away. You can see how well the dawn has caught the rakes at the corners of his eyes — the way he holds himself as if ready to repair anything that breaks, including the words between you.

You: "I came to say goodbye."

Elias Park: (a laugh that wants to be brittle) "Goodbye? Mara. You don't just— We were supposed to finish the retrofit reports. Nima's expecting data, the contractors need—"

You cut him off with a single, small laugh that edges into the salt air. It's not cruel; it's the sound of someone naming the exact thing that will not be changed by explanation.

You: "I know. You said we'd buy time. We bought it. It is not enough. I keep seeing the fractures — not only in the models but in the way people lock their doors, in the way protests start to sound like resignation. I can't fix that from a lab bench."

Elias steps closer. He reaches out, not touching yet, as if to test permission. His fingers hover near your shoulder.

Elias Park: "So you're… leaving? To policy work? To— to some bureaucratic office that will send form letters about coastline resilience?"

You consider how to tell him that leaving is not erasing the town. That moving away is, for you, a different way to protect where you came from — to try to change the things that make every town here trade its shoreline for ice-free promises. You want to say you won't forget how to mend a net or patch a roof. You want to say you will carry the town like a compass.

You: "I'm going to work on systemic policy. On networks of towns, not just patch jobs. I can push for the funding frameworks that make community stewardship possible. It's… farther from the shore. It's not easier."

Elias Park: (a quick, pained sound) "Is this because I asked you to be patient? Because I thought we could do it together? Because I—" He swallows. "Because I don't know how to ask you not to go without sounding like I'm trying to cage you."

His mouth finds words you didn't expect. He won't say the name of what he'd lose — that would make grief a thing you could measure — but it hangs in the space between you like fog.

You: "I didn't leave because you asked me to wait. I left because I realized the scale of what has to change is bigger than us here. Staying would be like bailing while the hull is still full of salt. I'm not abandoning you. I'm… changing stations."

Elias Park: "Change stations. Like a radio?"

You both offer half-smiles at the same time that don't land fully.

Elias Park: (low) "Will you— will you write?"

You: "Yes." You mean it. The town will be the spine of your sentences. You will write the data you collect and the frameworks you shape into words Elias can take back and argue with contractors. You will send accounts of hearings and of small victories. Maybe it will be enough. Maybe it won't. But the act of reaching, of not letting the story close, feels like the only promise you can truly give.

Elias studies your face like he is scanning for a flaw in the plan — something he can engineer around. He finds nothing but resolve, and his shoulders lower in a way that admits defeat.

Elias Park: (quiet) "I wanted to promise you I'd follow. I wanted to promise you a path that kept you in town. But the truth is, Mara, I am still learning how to hold other people's losses. I am good with bolts and sensors, not with the wide, wet places where hearts are torn."

You: "You do better than you think. You made time possible for people. That's not small."

Elias Park: "It's not enough."

You and he stand close and say nothing for a long minute. The life of the town — its markets, its arguments, the way Abuela Rosa stays up late weaving — all of it compresses into that silence. It hurts like an undertow.

Elias Park: (after a breath) "Promise me one thing. If you find a way to make the policy change— if you find partners who will listen to stewardship instead of quick edges— come back. Or at least tell me how to help from here."

You: "I will tell you everything I can. And I'll keep the letters."

He lets out a laugh that tastes like salt and resignation. He pulls you to him with a hold that is gentle and long, not loud. It is not a sealing kiss and it is not a cutting farewell — it is both and neither, a memory in the making.

[Scene: Puerto Alba Marketboard | Morning]
[Visual: The market strip under a pale sun; people gather in muffled clusters, eyes lowered or raised depending on what they owe each other]
[Music: Low, single sustained note; ambient hum of the waking town]

You move through the market with your pack lightened by the map remnant and the rope coil. People notice you — Abuela Rosa's friends incline their heads, vendors stop their hands mid-sweep as if asking permission to continue. The town's gaze is a quilt of accusation and gratitude. It is not a single voice. It never was.

Abuela Rosa meets you at the edge of the main lane. She slips a folded scrap of fabric into your palm — a small, square embroidered with a boat — the color already faded. She does not offer advice. She offers an old, weathered blessing.

Abuela Rosa: "Do not think that leaving is the same as giving up. There are many ways to carry a town. If you leave with your hands empty, you have failed. If you leave with an urgent, new set of tools, you may yet come back with what we cannot grow here."

You fold the scrap into the inner pocket of your jacket. You think of Sofía's skateboard, of Mateo's steady eyes, of the kids in the raised beds who have only ever known tides like homework. You think of the wall that protects them but also hemmed them in. You think of every small kindness that has kept the town breathing.

There is no grand send-off. No speech. The market keeps moving, a slow, practical orbit of people who will be here after you have left and will have to live with their choices as you live with yours.

You step down to the harbor where the launch waits. The sky is a wash of pale gray and gold. The sea smells like a memory that hurts in the chest.

The boat rocks in soft, indifferent motion. Your hands are steady now. You have tied the rope to your pack, you have tucked the map where you will know to look for it, and you have put distance between things that will not change because of your presence.

Elias comes with you to the low gangway. He does not board. He stands on the pier and watches the small shape of you against the wide, soft edge of the sea.

Elias Park: (final, breath-thin) "Will you be all right?"

You turn back once. The town is a line of dented roofs and higher walkways. The wall Iris fought to build cuts a strict silhouette against the water. You feel every fracture in your chest. You fold it into a single, quiet thing: purpose.

You: "I will be all right. I will be tired. I will be angry. I will miss you. I will keep trying."

He searches your face for a soft place to leave a mark and finds only the leveled planes of someone who has made a hard decision.

Elias Park: (soft) "Then go. Make them listen."

You: "And you — keep tending what you can. Don't let the town forget to teach the children the old ways. They are as necessary as any sensor."

He nods. The words are a pact and a wound at once.

You climb aboard. The launch pulls away with patient, inevitable motion. The rope is coiled on your pack as if it were a part of your arm. The map remnant sits above your heart. Abuela Rosa's square is a hidden talisman. Elias's silhouette shrinks with the pier as the boat turns. Market voices thin into a background hum.

You sit very still and let the town flatten behind you into memory-colored shapes. A gull circles once, twice, then moves on. The lighthouse recedes, crouched and honest on its rock. You feel a small, immense no — a not-icing of something — and then you breathe, and the sea keeps moving.

Your hands find the notebook in your pack. You open to a blank page and write one sentence: "We will need new laws and old stories." It is not a plan; it is a belief you can return to.

You do not promise triumphant returns or certain victories. The work you are about to enter is slow and bureaucratic and full of people who will want proofs of everything but the necessary patience. It will be less cinematic than it sounds; your days will be full of meetings and margins and the small indignities of policy drafting. It will hurt like time.

And yet you leave because leaving is not defeat. It is a redistribution of responsibility. It is hope in administrative clothing — dull and crucial.
