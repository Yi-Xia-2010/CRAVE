[Sound: Distant gulls, a low creak of boards]

You wake with the taste of brine in your mouth. The mattress smells faintly of damp canvas and yesterday's rain; the pillow is cool where it pressed against your cheek. For a moment you lie very still and count the quiet: the gulls, the clock's soft tick, the distant slap of a loose board on the promenade. Your windbreaker, rolled at the foot of the bed, is still damp from yesterday's work. When you pull it on the copper tips of your hair leave wet tracks like tiny rivulets down the collar.

You rub your fingers against your temple and feel the grit of mud under your nails. The day after a high tide is always small and exact — the town catalogues its losses while the tide catalogs its edges, and you are the sort of person who tries to keep both lists balanced.

[Visual: Close-up of a waterproof notebook; tide sketches scatter across the page]
You flip open your waterproof notebook. Ink beads in the corners of your sketches, smudged by the sweat of your thumb. You draw the arc of yesterday's waterline again: a jagged crescent along the marketboard, a notch where the old boat racks held, a cross where the bakery's crates floated. Each line is a small, precise confession. Your fingers smear mud into the margins and you do not bother to clean them.

[Scene: Puerto Alba Marketboard | Morning]
[Visual: Raised timber walkways glistening; half the stalls ringed with drying tarps]
[Sound: A single radio playing low, the hiss of frying dough, conversation in soft clusters]
[Music: A low, sustained cello note under ambient wind]

The market smells like everything the town is made of: brine, frying dough, the iron tang of old rope. Hands tug at tarps; someone laughs too loudly to be a laugh. You walk along the raised walk, boots dark with salt and mud, and note the small stubborn repairs: planks lashed with rope, a stack of sandbags under a bakery stall, a hand-painted sign that reads "WE'RE OPEN — SORRY FOR THE MUD."

Mateo is near the fishmonger, sleeves rolled, a damp beanie pulled low. He moves with that quiet efficiency you have known since childhood — a body memorized to boats and tides. He looks up when he sees you and for a second the fatigue on his face is a mirror to your own.

Mateo: "Mara. Morning. Came by the docks? Saw you out there yesterday."

You: "I was at the north end, helping with the raised beds. You? The boats—"

Mateo: (shakes his head) "Some are worse. Ran a salvage line this morning. Found a crab pot wrapped round Pop's old cleat. That boat of yours — I saw it half-buried near the spit." He hesitates, searching your face. "You okay?"

You feel a squeeze behind your ribs — guilt like salt. Your father's ruined boat is not just wood; it's the reason you came back to Puerto Alba. You open your mouth to say you should have done more, but the words are a thin net that won't hold the weight of everything.

You: "I should've checked sooner. I thought—" Your voice frays. "I thought I could fix things one at a time."

Mateo: (softly) "You always try. But this tide took more than one thing. We can't blame ourselves for the sea."

You: "I know. I know," you say, and you don't know. You feel your perfectionism tightening like a rope around your chest — the old impulse to shoulder the town's needs alone — and you feel tired down to the marrow.

Abuela Rosa's call comes as a warm, deliberate presence. She stands at the edge of a stall, the fabric of her apron damp, eyes like warm brown glass as she folds a salt-stiffened map in half. She holds it out to you with an authority tempered by decades of storms.

Abuela Rosa: "Sit, niña." (She pats a crate; you oblige.) "You draw, yes. But maps remember things your sketches don't. Quick fixes have long legs and short memories."

You take the folded map. The paper smells of cedar and old ink. Her fingers linger on a coastline drawn in a time when the town was smaller and the inlets were kinder.

You: "What is it? A survey?"

Abuela Rosa: (her voice flat as a tide line) "An old marker. Before the wall proposals — before big money came to look like salvation. They put something like this up once. Promised it would last. But the sea only learns your promises when you keep them."

You: "So you think—"

Abuela Rosa: "—that we remember what we can, niña. That is my thought. Don't let a loud plan drown out the small work that keeps people fed today." She taps the map as if it were a heart. "And don't forget the boat," she adds, softer, and you feel the old ache flare.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Late Morning]
[Visual: Converted warehouse, warm task lamps, blue LEDs reflecting off puddles]
[Sound: Soft whirr of equipment, a faint echo of the estuary outside]
[Music: Single sustained violin note; contemplative]

The Saltmarsh Labs smells of wet concrete and machine oil, undercut by the green, vegetal scent of the experimental planters pushing their soft shoots into engineered soil. Your notebook is still open as you step inside; a smear of ink decorates your knuckle.

Elias is there before you register him, a casual figure against a backdrop of sensors and a half-built modular wall. He carries a low-boxed prototype — neat, efficient, ridiculous in its hope. When he sees you he pauses, the easy warmth of his smile folding into something more careful.

Elias: "Mara. I didn't mean to surprise you. How's the marketboard?"

You: "Soggy. Some stalls worse than others. We did what we could." You close the notebook with a soft slap. "What do you have there?"

Elias: (holding the prototype up) "A module. Lightweight, vegetated panels, sensor nodes that talk to each other. Low footprint, adaptive. I wanted you to see it before the council meeting. It could be deployed in sections, work with the marsh instead of against it."

You look at the box, at the neatness of the welds and the printed logo that says, simply, "HarborFold." There's a practical beauty to it that both reassures and frays the edges of your resistance.

You: "It looks…solid. But we've seen promises with pretty logos before. How invasive would this be? Where would you put it?"

Elias: (his voice softens; he leans on a table) "We can stagger it, let native grasses colonize the panels, use local crews. I'm not saying it replaces the small repairs — just that it could buy time. I want to work with you, Mara. I want this to be something the town shapes."

You want to believe him. You also want to believe the town can decide its own shape.

Elias: "I know 'buy time' sounds like a cautious phrase. But I've run numbers. I can show you the models, the failure rates. It isn't magic. It's a tool."

You: "Tools that change how people fish, how kids play on the spit — that's not just technical. It's social. It's a lot of 'what ifs' for people's everyday lives."

Elias: (a small laugh that carries worry) "You're right. And I keep thinking about the balance you talk about — community knowledge with technical aid. I don't want to steamroll anything. I just hoped—" He stops. His hands find the edge of the prototype as if anchoring himself. "I hoped you'd let me show you."

You: "Show me, then. But remember: promises that sound like help can become orders if the money behind them wants speed."

Elias nods, the air between you folded tight with things unspoken — investor pressure, the need to deliver results, your fear that compromise will mean erasing history. He looks away for a beat, and for once his optimism looks like something worn thin from rubbing against reality.

[Scene: Council Hall | Midday]
[Visual: Municipal building with damp banners; a bulletin board crowded with typed notices and handwritten pleas]
[Sound: The distant muffled hum of a PA system; paper rustling]

The notice hangs like a black tide line across the council bulletin board: an official print, crisp against the rain-speckled glass. Iris Varela's name is in the header. You feel the knot tighten every time your eyes hit it.

[Visual: Close-up of the notice]
[Sound: The scratch of your thumb along the edge of the paper]

NOTICE: COUNCIL MEETING — DUSK
Iris Varela will present a rapid structural plan for immediate deployment. Representatives from HarborFold will be present to answer technical questions.

Your chest is a small, steady drum of anxiety. Iris — decisive, hardened by past losses — moving quickly is not surprising. The speed industry and urgency have a merciless logic when lived experience has taught someone that delay costs lives. But delay also costs trust, and when trust fractures, the social fabric tears along lines that are harder to stitch.

You fold the notice into your jacket. Abuela Rosa's warning loops in your mind like a low, repeating tide: quick fixes have a history. The municipal language on the notice already smells of council room air — clipped, procedural — and you imagine voices becoming sidelined in it: the stallholders, the teenage activists, the quiet biologists who keep the marsh's small truths.

You stand in the little plaza outside the hall and let the town's sounds settle around you: a child scolding a dog, someone hammering a loose shingle, a radio counting down the day's slow news. The meeting at dusk will be the first public crack where Iris's plan and Elias's prototype must, inevitably, be named and measured by the town's grief and hope.

You think of your father's boat, half-buried and mute, a small monument to everything the sea has taken and everything you've tried to save. You think of Abuela Rosa's map and its frayed edges. You think of Elias's careful hands and the neatness of his prototype. You feel an exhaustion that is not only physical but ethical — a tiredness that comes from trying to be both guardian and negotiator, from wanting to give people certainty when certainty is impossible.

The day compresses into a narrow hour of light. The arousal that threaded the morning — quiet, persistent worry — tilts slightly upward now, a measured rise that promises nothing dramatic but promises that decisions are coming. The marketboard will need volunteers; the Labs will need scrutiny; the council will need voices that will not be drowned out.

You tuck your notebook away. The tide sketches fold in your chest like a brace.
