[Scene: Green Prototype Site | Morning]
[Visual: Overcast sky, fog settling around modular barriers; orange safety tape flutters. Close-up: your wet-gloved hand wrapped around the brass compass at your throat.]
[Music: Low, insistent drums; a high, uneasy violin line underneath]
[Sound: Distant engines, the murmur of a restless crowd, gulls fighting the wind]

You feel the compass against your skin like a pulse — familiar, stubborn, impossible to lose. The pilot rigs around you smell of wet concrete, motor oil, and the bitter tang of seaweed left to rot in puddles. Workers move with the slow, focused economy of people already stretched thin; their faces are mapped with salt-streaks and decision.

Elias stands beside you, sleeves rolled, the sensor blink on his wrist like a small, hopeful heartbeat. Nima is on the scaffolding, shouting down a list, her breath fogging in the air. They back you. The feeling of that support is both ballast and new weight — their alliance makes your refusal possible, and with it comes the loss you expected and feared.

Iris's voice is still in your ears from the council: tight, practical, a blade wrapped in concern. She promised to protect the town. You insisted on governance clauses — community vetoes, transparent accounting, a joint stewardship board — and when investors moved to demand transfer-of-rights as a condition, you closed your mouth around the brass of your compass and said no.

Murmurs circle you like birds.

Investor Rep: "You don't understand scale. Without rights, the project is nonviable. This is how infrastructure is built."

Elias: "We understand scale. We're asking for partners, not ownership. There's a difference."

Investor Rep: (sharp) "Contracts mean leverage. Without it, our investors—"

You cut in, quieter than the engine hum but harder than the loudest shouted number: Mara Serrano: "We will not give away the town to someone else's ledger."

The investor's face thins. Papers are folded, a phone is checked. You watch the withdrawal happen in slow motion: a polite nod, a turned back, three men in raincoats moving toward idling vans. Their footsteps make the ground feel hollow.

Nima climbs down, mud on her boots, and finds you. Her smile is thin; her voice tighter than it should be.

Nima: "They warned us. I told Elias the investors would balk."

Elias: (soft, almost pleading) "But we needed them to scale responsibly. We still can — we still have designs that work."

You study Elias: the way his shoulders square when he believes in something, the small, habitual avoidance in his eyes where hard human edges should be named. You want to argue that belief isn't the same as certainty, that numbers can be wrong and people will be where numbers fail.

Mara Serrano: "We keep the designs. We keep the sensors. We work slower, but the town keeps the say."

Elias: (after a long breath) "Okay. We'll do it your way. We'll make the safeguards stronger."

His agreement feels like a truce — not all the way to peace. You accept it and the way the acceptance tastes like iron.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Noon]
[Visual: Warm task lamps cut the gray; walls tacked with tide maps and hand-scrawled notes. A kettle whistles somewhere. The lab smells of damp paper and antiseptic.]
[Music: Thumping, urgent percussion; a subtle choir of dissonant strings]
[Sound: Soft clink of tools, distant laughter that sounds brittle]

You move through the labs the way you move through memory: purposeful, scanning for stability. Volunteers huddle over sensors and planters, fingers raw from work. Abuela Rosa sits at a sticky table with a notebook full of crab-walk tide marks, her hands steady as ever but her jaw set like stone.

Abuela Rosa: (in a low voice) "You did what I taught you. You put the land in people's hands."

Mateo arrives, hat soaked and grin tentative. He slaps your shoulder, the knuckles of a friend and the urgency of a first responder crumpled into one.

Mateo: "We can make up the hours. You got the clause. That's what matters."

You want to let the small relief bloom into warmth, but the lab hums with fissures. Phone calls in corridors end with a thin exhale. Some neighbors come to voice anger — they'd wanted speed; they feel betrayed. Others come to thank you with wet eyes. The town splits along lines you can see on the skin: some faces harden into suspicion; others soften into solidarity.

You oscillate between both tasks — empathy and labor — until the siren comes.

[Sound: A distant, rising alarm; the kind that scrapes the chest open]

The weather, which had been an argument in the background, sharpens into a command. Forecasts you had all read aloud in better moods are suddenly a ledger of threats: an unexpected trough, sea-level surge with battering winds. Iris's partial municipal barrier — quick, authorized, heavy on concrete and lighter on community process — is already being hailed by some as salvation. It will be tested tonight.

Elias finds you as you tighten straps on a pump.

Elias: (voice low) "We can pre-position crews along the promenade. Reinforce the weakest joints. Nima and I will calibrate sensors to give you more lead time."

You can hear the strain beneath his calm; his usual reliance on tinkering feels like not enough next to a roaring storm.

Mara Serrano: "Do it. Call Mateo's boat. Tell Abuela to get the archives to high ground. Tell everyone: no heroics unless necessary."

Elias hesitates, thumb rubbing at the sensor on his wrist — a small, human motion.

Elias: "And you? Stay with the north team."

You want to say no. You want to say you're fine whether or not you are. You want to be everything. But you also know you cannot be everywhere at once.

Mara Serrano: (quiet) "I'll be with the volunteers at the seawall."

He nods, and the nod is all the consent you need — and all the worry he refuses to name.

[Scene: Seawall / Partial Municipal Barrier | Dusk — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: The sky has gathered to a low bruise; the municipal barrier stands like a blunt tooth in the water, armored but with visible seams and hastily patched joints. Volunteers cluster, raincoats snapping in gusts. Small wildflowers along the bluff are whipped flat.]
[Music: Full strings in staccato crescendos; percussion like distant thunder]
[Sound: Wind tearing at fabric, the ocean hitting concrete with a repeated, violent clocking]

You stand at the wall's edge, gloves slick, boots planted in the choking mud. The partial barrier, Iris's emergency solution, looks grand from a distance but up close the seams tell another story: mismatched pre-cast segments bolted with visibly different hardware, gaps awkwardly filled with sandbags and prayer.

Iris approaches, her coat buttoned to her chin, eyes narrowed against the salt. She is unsurprised by everything and by nothing.

Iris Varela: (flat) "You chose self-governance. We got emergency funds. This—this saves lives tonight."

Mara Serrano: "It saves lives tonight, maybe. But the seams are wrong. This was rushed."

Iris: (a flicker of something softer) "We did what was necessary. People couldn't wait for committees."

The argument is a slow, grinding thing, and behind it all you can hear the sea: the proof you keep returning to. Community control or speed — neither is pure. Both take names. Both leave marks.

Mateo jogs up, hair plastered to his forehead, grin gone. He claps his hands once, loud, to get attention.

Mateo: "All right — team A with me on the northern seam. Team B covers the inlet. Watch the bolts; if they pop, move back, move back!"

You move with them, directing hands, bracing an arm against a wet railing while the wind wants to pull you out into the dark. Volunteers hip against the wall, sandbags stacked, ropes tied to weathered posts. For a time, the rhythm of human work steadies the panic. You feel a thread of something like hope.

Then the sea hits differently.

A gust comes that is not wind so much as a hand: it grabs the whole shore and shakes it. Waves slap the barrier in a way you have only seen on ruined footage — not high at first but precise, forcing water into seams, prying, testing the hurried craftsmanship.

Elias scans with his handheld sensor, the device whining as readings spike.

Elias: (urgent) "Pressure's spiking on Segment Seven — the anchor points are flexing. Get back two meters from the seam. Now."

You shout; volunteers scramble. Mateo yells above the roar, a laugh that is not a laugh; people work like they always have: with grit and a dangerous bravery. A pre-cast slab, already compromised by an unsealed gap, shifts. The bolt holding it in the center — an aftermarket, not spec-grade bolt — pops with a metallic scream.

Concrete grinds. A section tilts.

Mateo: (yelling) "Look out—!"

Time condenses. You lunge — not thinking, only moving — and catch the edge of Mateo's jacket as the slab tilts. Mud sprays. The slab half-collapses, pinning him between brutal material and ruthless sea. You feel the impact up your arm, the shock of bone-rattling force.

Sound explodes: a sharp, animal shout from Abuela; the crack of concrete; someone's radio falling into the water and filling the air with an inelegant, drowning static. Elias is already there, gloves scrabbling, voice breaking distance with commands and prayers tangled together.

Elias: "Hold on! Hold on, Mateo! Move your left leg — don't force it!"

Mateo groans, breath ragged, the kind of noise that says he's very afraid but he will not let it show. Blood blooms dark on his shin where a jagged bit of reinforcement sliced through canvas. The volunteers press their hands where they can, pushing with a rhythm of desperate leverage. You count and recall and reroute pain into action: a rope here, a crowbar there, a truck's hydraulic jack bellowing like an animal as someone finds it in the back of a storm-smeared pickup.

Abuela Rosa: (primal, distant) "Blessed waters, take not more than we can give."

They pull him free with a horrible slowness. The slab is too heavy to right; it has crushed more than concrete — it has crushed a quiet faith that slow, careful work would always be enough.

You lean over Mateo as he lies on the slick ground, boots caked, face pale with salt and shock. His breath stabs shallow. You touch his hand; it is hot and trembling.

Mateo: (a whisper) "I'm — oy. I'm sorry, M. I thought—"

Mara Serrano: (desperate) "Don't be. Don't say that." (to Elias) "Get Nima — tell her to bring the med kit and the rig. Tell Abuela to call the ambulance, now."

Elias moves like someone who has learned to live in the shell of certainty; now that shell cracks, his movements are brittle with fear. He kneels, fingers searching for a pulse, then away to make room for someone who knows splints.

The wound is bad. A compound fracture, and the laceration will take hours, stitches, and someone will remind you in measured, awful words of infection and permanent damage. For a beat — a long, burning beat — the adrenaline makes everyone nimble and precise. Then the weight of the cost lands.

[Sound: Rain begins, heavy and vertical; the ocean groans in regular, victorious beats]
[Music: Climactic brass, then sudden thin silence under a single, impossible violin]

Mateo is moved onto a makeshift stretcher. He squeezes your hand — hard, an anchor. His eyes find yours for a moment, and there is no bravado there, only wide, honest trust.

Mateo: (half-smile) "You always told me — don't be an idiot around bolts."

You laugh despite yourself, a sound that turns to something else when he groans. Your fingers are sticky with mud and blood and the life you are trying to keep.

Abuela Rosa: (soft, steady) "You did right by the clause, niña. You held the town's teeth in its head. But teeth can cut."

You watch as the ambulance, a blob of orange in the rain, takes Mateo away. People gather close, faces lit and raw. Some begin to cry outright. Some stand with hands balled, furious, blaming you with eyes that say: You slowed what could have been done faster. You refused the funds.

Iris stands apart from the cluster, rain on her hair like a halo. She does not gloat; there is no satisfaction on her face, only a hard grief knotted with the political calculus she has made real.

Iris Varela: (quiet, to you) "You wanted control. You have it. Now the town must carry the price."

Her words are not a barb now but a truth like a stone thrown into a still pond; concentric circles of consequence ripple through the crowd. You want to answer: But we saved our agency. But the volunteers are exhausted. But we did not expect a slab to fail. But you — But you.

You feel the weight of blame press into your chest, an iron that slides down into your stomach. Your stubborn empathy wants to own every failure; it wants to rearrange the world to keep everyone safe. It wants to punish you for not being everywhere.

Elias slides to your side, rain plastering his hair to his skull. He looks impossibly fragile and impossibly furious.

Elias: (hoarse) "I told them — we had to strengthen the anchors weeks ago. I thought — I thought we had time to do it together."

Mara Serrano: "We made a choice. We kept the power in the town. We didn't hand the coast to a balance sheet."

Elias: (shaking his head, close) "I didn't mean— I keep thinking I can fix things with bits and sensors. I thought if I worked enough, the hurts would stay small."

He reaches for you and misses by a breath, and the miss is as full as an accusation and a plea both.

Elias: (raw) "Mateo could have been safer. Maybe if I'd pushed harder, if I'd stayed—"

You press a hand to his sleeve, feel the tremor that answering his guilt will not soothe.

Mara Serrano: (brittle) "None of us are perfect, Elias. Not you, not me. We chose a longer, harder road because the town deserves it."

Elias: (voice collapsing) "Was it worth it?"

The question is small and enormous at once; it hangs in the rain like something you have to walk through. You do not have an answer that is neat or comforting. The town's autonomy is preserved; the cost is real and heavy. You must live with both.

[Scene: Aftermath — Saltmarsh Labs | Night]
[Visual: The lab is a flurry of bandages, cups of tea, and wet coats. The tide chart on the wall is smeared. Someone has draped a blanket over a chair like a small island of warmth.]
[Music: Low, mournful cello; a single piano note repeated as if learning to breathe again]
[Sound: Soft, steady rain; muted conversation; the whirr of a heater]

The town moves in circles, gathering and re-gathering around a core of collected grief. Volunteers tally damage: a collapsed segment, several flooded basements, one person with a broken leg and a deep cut that might never quite un-ache. People argue — loudly, sometimes angrily — about whether the choice was correct. Some speak like prophets of efficiency; some like saints of sovereignty.

You sit with Abuela Rosa. Her hands are folded in her lap; the lines of her face hold every storm she has ever survived.

Abuela Rosa: "You stood firm. That is a kind of courage that costs. You must forgive yourself, child, when you are able."

Mara Serrano: (soft) "How do you forgive yourself for someone getting hurt because of your choice?"

Abuela Rosa: (without hesitation) "You don't forgive yourself in one go. You forgive yourself by continuing to care — by mending what can be mended and by changing what must be changed. That is stewardship."

Elias is across the room, hollow-eyed as he reviews logs with Nima. He glances toward you and then turns away, as if a look might be a plea he can't afford to make or answer. When he does approach, the conversation is short and jagged.

Elias: "I'm sorry, Mara. I keep thinking sensors could have warned us more, or the anchor inspections—"

Mara Serrano: "We did inspections. We negotiated timelines. This was a failure of materials and haste. It is a failure I carry."

Elias: (quiet) "We both carry it."

He reaches out, careful, then sits beside you. His shoulder is warm and shaking.

Elias: "I don't know how to be better than I was tonight. I only know I want to try."

You let him be that small, broken promise. It will have to be enough for a while. You imagine long nights of engineering and town meetings, of stitches and budgets and apologies, of both of you learning to hold the world without trying to own it.

Outside, rain softens to something more like a blanket than a punishing hand. The town will be rebuilt in increments and arguments, with new vigilance and old stubbornness. The community governance clauses remain in place; the investors have gone. The partial municipal barrier holds some tide but not all of it; the scars will be visible for years like a wrong stitch in a once-beloved coat.

You close your eyes and, for a moment, let yourself feel the awful truth of the day: you made a choice because you thought it was right; rightness did not spare you the price.

[Visual: Slow fade to a dim, grainy shot of the harbor at night — lights mistaken for stars; boats bobbing, patched and patient]

You think of Mateo's squeeze of your hand in the mud, of Abuela's steady voice, of Elias's unfinished apology. You think of the town's faces — some thankful, some turned away — and you know stewardship will be a long conversation, not a single victory.

You stand, fingers on the compass at your throat, and feel the weight of tomorrow pressing at the edges of your bones. The cost is counted; the decision stands. You breathe, salty and small, and prepare to begin the long work of making this right again.
