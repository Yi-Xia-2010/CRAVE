[Scene: Green Prototype Site | Morning — two days after the council vote]

You wake to the taste of iron in your mouth — not blood, not yet. Just the metallic aftertaste of decisions signed and words said where everyone could hear them. The compass at your throat is cool, an anchor that feels suddenly too small for the weight you've asked it to hold.

The pilot's scaffolding looks fragile in the gray light, like a promise built of borrowed time. You were the one who closed the hall and made the town's governance the condition. You remember the small, decisive motion of your hand as you pressed for community clauses, the way Elias took your breath with that look — steady, hopeful. Now the hope is elastic and stretched.

Elias steps up the temporary ramp toward you, mud splashing along his boots. Nima follows, her jaw set in the kind of line engineers use to hold panic at bay.

Elias Park: "They called last night. The fund — they're pulling the transfer rights demand, but they're pulling the money too if we don't relent on scaling."

You let whatever you were going to say hang in the salt-scented air. The truth tastes worse when it gets a name.

You: "So they leave us with no cash and with the optics of, 'community couldn't scale fast enough.'"

Nima: "They won't admit it that way. Investors hate uncertainty. They've already sent a press release with a vague line about 'reassessing commitments.'"

Elias runs a hand through his hair. His prototype sensor ticks at his wrist like a second heartbeat, a small green LED blinking frantic checks against the weather feed.

Elias Park: (low) "We knew this could happen. We put the clauses in because it mattered. But—"

You interrupt, because interrupting keeps speech from curdling into helplessness.

You: "But the point is: they wanted to own the town before they would fund it. We said no."

Nima exhales, the sound sharp enough to carry. She points at the scaffolding, at the half-planted sedge in the modular planters.

Nima: "We can stretch what we have. Re-prioritize materials. Call more volunteers. I can rework the deployment schedule so the sensors get in first."

Your chest tightens with a mixture of vindication and dread. Vindication because the clauses are the boundary you drew for what Puerto Alba will be. Dread because every boundary costs muscle: money, time, nights without sleep.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands — mud-streaked, knuckles white around a twisted bolt]
[Music: Pulsing low strings; a single high violin note like a held breath]

A municipal truck halts at the site, exhaust stinging and authoritative. Iris steps out — her coat is the same hard charcoal, a municipal pin glinting like a bite of light. She crosses the gravel with everyone watching.

Iris Varela: (to the gathered crowd) "When the town's safety is at risk, the council acts. Emergency municipal funds will cover a partial barrier. It won't be everything. It will be fast."

Her voice is the authority you once trusted without question. Now it strikes you like the snap of a line being tightened.

You: "Partial? On what terms, Iris?"

Iris: (even) "Emergency protocols. The municipality assumes responsibility for certain sections. We'll prioritize the main promenade and the old wharf. It will hold for the near term."

Abuela Rosa stands at the fringe of the group, outline hunched against wind. Her hands are folded into each other like prayer, her eyes a map of tides and losses.

Abuela Rosa: (softly) "It will hold for some storms, niña. Not for all. We know this."

You move to her, needing the steadiness of roots.

You: "They'll try to spin this as us versus them."

Abuela Rosa: (looking at you) "They will try. But you stood where it mattered."

The municipal trucks unload men with machine drills. Volunteers arrive in clumps — fishermen with calloused hands, teenagers with scarves flapping, Abuela's neighbors with thermoses. The work begins with a rhythm: hammer, plank, shout. The pilot slows to a crawl as labor shifts from funded machines to human muscle.

[Visual: Montage of labor — hands pushing sandbags, sensors being wired with twine and patience]
[Sound: Tools, strained laughter; a child's voice calling a joke that lands flat]

You find Mateo at the front line, a familiar shape bent over a rope. He grins when he sees you, but the edges of his smile are raw; he's been carrying nights of watchfulness.

Mateo Reyes: (trying light) "Thought you'd sleep in and let me handle the heavy lifting, huh?"

You: (short) "Not today."

He elbows you, the motion a claim of friendship and of shared fatigue. You shoulder a sack beside him. The cold bites at your wrists; the smell of wet rope and diesel is the new normal.

You choose — not to shirk responsibility but because responsibility has multiplied into dozens of tiny, needful things. The shift is immediate: those who had favored speed feel vindicated by the municipal funding, even if it is partial. Those who favored governance stand shoulder to shoulder with you, sweat a proof of principle.

Days blur. You sleep in the confines of the Saltmarsh Labs between shifts — hard benches, the hum of refrigeration, pinned tide charts like calendars of a future you're buying with labor. The labs smell of ozone and warm tea. Elias brings you a thermos once; his hand brushes yours and stays too long, then shorter than you want.

Elias Park: (quiet, rushing) "We're doing the right thing. It's messy, but it's ours."

You: (soft) "It is ours, and it's costing us."

He looks at you, and for the first time a fissure opens in his optimism. The sensor on his wrist catches the faint change in humidity; he reads data the way some people read scripture. Now the numbers lean toward a verdict he can't wish away.

Elias Park: "We can strengthen the design. We can run a quick reinforcement test at the seawall. If the municipality's barrier has weak seams, we'll shore them. We can—"

You: "We can try. But we don't have their cranes."

Nima joins, breathless.

Nima: "Investors are sending statements. They're blaming indecision. The paper's already running a piece about 'the costs of community control.'"

The mood in town shifts: neighbors argue in doorways. Some call the council's action brave; others call it reckless. The town's moral fabric pulls at the seams as quickly as the tide pulls at the saltmarsh.

You stand near a seam where the municipal barrier meets the pilot module. The gap is smaller than your fear but still a gap. Hands of different kinds — municipal, volunteer, engineer — pass each other like cauterized truths.

Mateo clasps a bar and laughs once, a short bark that might be bravado.

Mateo Reyes: "We'll be fine. The town's got muscle."

You: "Muscle isn't the same as design, Mateo."

He glances up at you, and the grin drops a little.

Mateo Reyes: (earnest) "But design without people won't hold either. You know that."

You bite back the retort; it's true and you don't want truth to be used as a shield again.

The radio crackles. The word "unexpected" lands like a cold stone.

Radio Voice: "—quick update: sudden low-pressure system moving up faster than models predicted. Estimated arrival within the night. All crews prepare for high seas."

The lab's LED screens flash red. Elias swears under his breath; Nima's fingers move like birds across a keyboard, rerouting resources. The wind picks up. The sky bruises into something codified as "danger" by the same models you and Elias trusted.

You choose in the instant between the data and the saw-tooth courage of neighbors. The choice is cruelly binary: do you trust the numbers to save lives, or the hands that already love this town?

Whatever you choose, the storm arrives sooner than anyone expected.

[Scene: Seawall / Municipal Barrier | Night — Storm]
[Visual: Black water torn by white, the lights of the barrier stuttering; rain like thrown glass]
[Music: Violent percussion, brass slashes; tempo accelerates to a frantic pulse]
[Sound: Unrelenting surf, shouts lost in wind, a groan of stressed metal]

The first impact is a sound that isn't a sound: a pressure in your bones, a low, immense shift like a fist closed underwater. You taste salt and earth and something metallic. The municipal barrier takes the first brunt, and for a moment — a miracle — it holds. The town whooped a raw, wet cheer that lasts less than a heartbeat.

Then the seam gives where the barrier meets the pilot module. Water rams into a joint engineered without the benefit of your longer-term reinforcements. For all the muscle and municipal money, a small misalignment lets a column of froth through.

A volunteer yells. You see someone — a silhouette borne on the edge of the water — slip. Mateo is there, out where the line is thin, hands full of rope. He fights the current like a man trying to hold a door against a housefire.

You: "Mateo!"

You run. Your boots can't find purchase; the sand slides beneath you like fate. Hands grab and release, a chain of trying. Someone's scream is welded to the roar of the sea.

There's a moment — no longer than a breath — where you think you'll reach him. Your fingers close on wet rope and skin.

Mateo's face is close; his grin is not joking; it's feral and honest. He laughs, wind tearing the sound from him.

Mateo Reyes: (shouting) "Get back! Get off—"

A strain — a crack of torque — and then a sound like metal being split. The seam collapses wider. A body is caught between giving and retrieval.

You feel, with a clarity that strips all theater away, the impossible physics of small human things against systemic failure. Mateo goes down. Not gone, not gone from the world, but slammed against concrete as if the water had a mind.

You and three others are thrown back. Someone grunts and sends a spool of wet rope toward the volunteer team. Hands find Mateo and haul him up, but he is too heavy and limper than he should be. He is bleeding from a long gash along his temple; his breathing is jagged, a machine misfiring.

Nima is beside you, hands already on his neck checking for airway. Her face is pale and fierce.

Nima: (sharp) "We need a splint. Get the med kit. Now!"

Elias reaches Mateo first. For a second his optimism — the soft rounded thing that let him imagine a better future — snaps into something raw. He places his hand over Mateo's chest like a prayer and can't form the words.

Elias Park: (struggling) "Mateo, stay with me. Stay—"

You are hollowed out by a grief that is immediate and volcanic. Guilt floods you like the sea: I insisted we hold governance. I pushed for control. Did that mean this? Did choosing process over speed turn into a wound in his skull?

Abuela Rosa appears with a towel, eyes wide, hands quick. She is old and weathered but moves like a force remembered from another storm.

Abuela Rosa: (cold, fierce) "Someone—tie this. Hold his head."

You find yourself kneeling beside Mateo, his blood warm on your gloves. You press a towel to the wound as if sealing a leak. The town's voices converge: orders, curses, prayers. The storm obliterates pretense.

[Visual: Close-up on Mateo's face — one eye half-open, mouth working; rain sluices his hair]
[Music: High violin shriek with a sustained drumbeat like a heart struggling]

A rescue boat arrives, municipal flares bobbing. They pull Mateo aboard with formal care, crisp and fast — the municipal machine finally moving with its trained competence. Someone shoves a stretcher into the back of an emergency truck. They move like a practiced ballet; violence tempered by training.

Elias climbs onto the boat without thought for the danger. You try to follow and are held back by a line of hands. His hand finds yours across the wet, and his fingers close like an apology and like a plea.

Elias Park: (broken) "I'm—I'm sorry. I should have—"

You press your forehead to his knuckles, because saying anything would be like patching a gash with paper.

You: "No. Stop. Not now."

He flinches at your tone, at the word you didn't mean to carve so hard. There is an exchange between you that contains a thousand words unsaid: blame, love, the knowledge that you both did what you thought right and now the town pays the bill.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs — Triage area | Night, hours later]
[Visual: Bleary lights, damp blankets, the smell of antiseptic mixed with sea]
[Music: Slow, aching cello; muted bell tones]
[Sound: Low conversation, machines beeping, rain against metal]

Mateo is in a cot. A bandage wraps his head like a crown gone wrong. He sleeps — or is sedated — and his breaths are regular but small. The doctors talk in clipped, efficient phrases that translate to "he'll live" and "it will take time."

You sit in a chair that creaks. Your back is a map of strain. Elias is across from you, hands wrapped around a paper cup. The warmth of it does nothing to thaw the cold.

Elias Park: (voice low) "If we'd accepted the full funding, they'd have had the cranes, the prefabricated seals. This wouldn't have happened."

You look at him and see the man who wanted to believe in scale without seeing the cost. He looks like someone squeezed by the realization that optimism without reckoning can be destructive.

You: "If we'd accepted it, we might have given up governance. We would have given away the right to decide who lives where and how we protect what remains."

He closes his eyes. The room hums with machines and the long, patient tide of people keeping watch.

Elias Park: "I can't stop thinking about the models. The variables. How one seam—"

You cut him off, because numbers now feel like blame.

You: "Mateo is hurt, Elias. How many seams are there between doing the right thing and the cost we pay? What do we do with that?"

He opens his eyes and for the first time the avoidance you've seen all along is gone; he looks truly frightened.

Elias Park: (fragile) "We learn. We change. We—I'll do everything to make this right."

His mouth tenses. You want to tell him you know he means it, but the words will be heavy. You are both weathered into new shapes by the same storm.

Abuela Rosa comes and sits beside you without ceremony. She takes your hand with fingers knotted by age and tide.

Abuela Rosa: (soft, fierce) "We kept the town's rights, niña. We fought so a child of the sea would not be traded like an asset."

You: "And Mateo—"

Abuela Rosa: "He will remember who tried to save him. He will remember the hands that held him. Remember that when the night is long."

You close your eyes, and the image that presses is not the rupture but the hands — the scramble, the wet palms, the towel pressed to a wound. The cost is a fabric of small, chosen things.

[Music: Dissonant chords resolving into a minor lull]

The aftermath comes in slow measures. The municipal barrier holds for the main surge but shows the strain lines where the community's pilot met the larger structure. Repairs will take months. Mateo will recover but with a scar you will see in sunlight and in sleep. Some neighbors speak of betrayal; others speak of courage. The town divides along lines you did not intend.

You walk the perimeter the morning after. The salt air is washed clean but the ground is littered with the debris of rushed choices. You pick up a shredded survey marker — a splintered flag — and the act feels ceremonial.

Elias falls into step beside you. Neither of you reach for easy words.

Elias Park: (after a long time) "Will you... will you still walk this road with me? Even if I'm harder to carry now?"

You can feel the question like a tide reaching. Your heart wants to fold into him, to make the ache a seam you could stitch. But the ache has teeth; it has consequence.

You: (quiet) "I will walk with you. But not if we keep pretending that optimism erases the labor of consequence. We have to name the harms we caused and do more than promise."

He nods, small and fierce.

Elias Park: "Then we'll name them. We'll repair. We'll learn."

You think of Mateo in his cot and of the town gathered in the labs. You think of Abuela's steady fingers and Iris's municipal pragmatism. You think of the clauses that kept the town's voice intact.

The victory is bitter and bright. You saved the town from being sold, but the cost will be carried in scars and in relationships that will take time to sew back together. You taste saline and ash.

Abuela Rosa meets you one last time at the labs' threshold. She places a callused hand over your compass.

Abuela Rosa: "You stood for something. Don't let the grief make you small."

You: "I won't forget I chose this."

Abuela Rosa: "Good. Remember the sea. It asks for stewardship, even when stewardship is expensive."

You nod. Outside, the sky is a wash of pale steel. The town will rebuild in stitches and in heated meetings and in small, stubborn acts of repair. The pilot breathes, slower now; volunteers will be quieter, wary. Iris will press municipal power where she can; some will cheer, others will turn away. But the decision stands: community governance, held and defended, imperfectly.

You look at your hands — scarred, mud-streaked, human.

You: (to yourself) "We refused to be bought. We paid."

[Music: Low, unresolved strings; a distant, solemn trumpet]
[Sound: Waves, a town beginning to speak again]

You take one last breath of the sea. It does not forgive. It does not lessen the hurt. It is only what it is: relentless, honest, necessary.

You tuck the compass back under your scarf and feel the shape of the coming days — long and narrow and full of work.
