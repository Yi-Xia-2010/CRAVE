Abuela Rosa: (there is no hurry in her answer) "Long enough to teach us patience. Longer if we care for them. Shorter if we hurry and leave gaps."

Sofía: "Data is like a weather friend. If you listen to it, it tells you how it feels. If you ignore it, it yells."

The first storm of the season rolls in like an exhale. Not violent—yet—but the forecast calls for a rapid tide and wind. The pilot's true test arrives with ordinary inevitability. You stand between data and the sea, watching as volunteers tighten straps and clamp sensors. Abuela Rosa offers a quiet blessing and then moves to steady the planters.

Elias: (into a radio) "Begin live-feed transmission. Tier one sensors, now."

On the screen, lines rise and fall in an almost musical rhythm. The sensors hum their small electric song. You feel the lab's blue LEDs echo in your pupils.

Investor 1: (offering a thin smile) "This will be a good case study."

Investor 2: (checking a tablet) "If the metrics hold, we can brief a much larger fund tomorrow."

Nima watches the readouts, frowning and then breathing out. The curves on the screen are better than the model predicted. The marsh's planned redundancy is doing exactly the thing you and Elias argued for: damping the surge, holding the line you and the volunteers planted.

Nima: (a little relieved) "That's— That's better than expected. The overlap is working. The planters create micro-buffer zones."

You watch the volunteers cheer in small bursts, not triumphant but relieved. Children clap, oblivious to the stakes, and Sofía beams as if she has created the weather herself.

You: (internal) For a moment, the town breathes as one small organism. Hope is not loud here; it is a series of careful exhalations, a pragmatic satisfaction. You feel Elias at your side, and if there is a small electric current between you, it is the shared joy of a thing working because people tended it.

Elias: (softly) "We did this right. For now."

Investor 3: "Your pilot is impressive. We could scale these modules nationally. Imagine standardized units, fewer training hours, immediate deployment. There's a lot of interest from partners who can move quickly."

Elias: (cautiously) "Scaling is possible, but not at the cost of community governance. We need to retain local oversight."

Investor 3: "Of course. Conditional governance clauses are easy. But speed is money. Our partners will want to see a clear rollout—fast."

Nima: (quiet, private to Elias) "They're already drafting terms in the back room."

You feel the air change around you. The moment of success folds into a new pressure: attention draws in resources, but also maps of ownership. The investors are not villains. They are agents of a different urgency; where you see stewardship, they see leverage.

You: (internal) Neutrality arrives in the form of a ledger: success plus new variables. The pilot proved what you hoped it would — but proved something else, too: its value is visible. That visibility will attract more than praise.

Elias meets your eyes. His expression is steady, the way it has been when you are both elbow-deep in something that matters.

Elias: "We'll need to read any offers. Carefully."

You: "Carefully is the word for everything now."
