## Visual Novel Title
Tide Lines

---

## World View
Puerto Alba is a small coastal town in the near future, rearranged by sea-level rise and stronger storms. The town's economy is a patchwork of legacy fishing livelihoods, small artisanal shops on raised timber walkways, and new corporate-sponsored pilot projects centered on coastal defense. Political power revolves around the municipal council, local cooperatives, and outside investors who promise rapid technological solutions; religion is local and pragmatic, made of rituals around tides and seasonal blessings carried by Abuela Rosa's stories. Class divides show in who can afford raised homes and insurance versus those who rely on shared boats and communal kitchens. The culture prizes practical communal knowledge — tide lore, fishers' seamanship, grafted gardening on raised beds — while newer generations are educated in sensor networks and modular engineering. Geography is intimate: a marketboard over marshy flats, an estuary housing the Saltmarsh Labs, a cliffside promenade and a battered lighthouse where couples meet. The mood is weather-worn, cinematic: salt-stained facades, storm-gray skies punctured with golden afternoons. Conflicts center on whether to accept a fast, centralized infrastructure deal to preserve the town physically but change its character, or to pursue community-led adaptations that are slower and uncertain. Love, responsibility, and resilience are tested against climate risk and political compromise.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Mara Serrano, returned from university to steward Puerto Alba. When new engineered defenses and sensor networks arrive — championed by Elias Park, an empathetic engineer, and pushed through by Iris Varela, a decisive municipal leader — the town must choose a path. I distrust investor-driven fixes; Elias believes humane tech can help; Iris presses for rapid, centralized action after losses. The narrative follows a Fall–Rise–Fall emotional arc: at first we are overwhelmed by storms and mistrust (fall), then a hopeful hybrid plan seems to reconcile community stewardship and technology (rise), but that success leads to consequences — political compromises, design flaws, or sacrificial decisions — culminating in tragedy or durable but costly loss (final fall). Across branching routes I negotiate love, civic leadership, and the costs of compromise. Intimate scenes — repairing a seawall at dawn, storm watches atop the cliffside promenade, quiet hours inside the abandoned lighthouse — interweave with council meetings at the Hall and hands-on work at the Saltmarsh Labs. Every choice shapes whether Puerto Alba keeps its soul, who survives, and whether Elias and I build a lasting partnership.
