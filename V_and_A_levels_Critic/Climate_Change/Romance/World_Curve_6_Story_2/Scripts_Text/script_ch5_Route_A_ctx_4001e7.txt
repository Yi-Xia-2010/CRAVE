[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Dusk]
[Visual: Warm task lamps pool over wet concrete benches; LED sensor panels pulse faintly blue. Walls are papered with tide maps and sticky notes. Outside, low tide reveals mudflats reflecting a bruised sky.]
[Music: Driving strings with quickening tempo]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the metallic whisper of a projector]

You step into the lab already carrying the evening in your bones — the taste of salt, the memory of applause, the small hard weight of the brass compass in your pocket. The air smells of wet paper, solder, and strong coffee. Voices fold over one another: measured investor cadence, Elias's softer technical rhythm, Nima's clipped clarifications. The room hums like a living thing, and your pulse lifts to match it.

At the head of the table, under a strip of harsh lamp, three investors have spread glossy renderings of modular barriers across plywood. Their words are clinical and fast: "scale," "supply chain," "deployment windows," "management consolidation." The promise sits on the page like a gleam — more modules, wider coverage — and the fine print is a coastline of surrendered rights.

Elias Park stands beside them, hands half-open as if to hold both the plans and the people who will be folded into them. He looks at you, and for a second the amber of his eyes shutters, a flicker that is part apology, part calculation. His fingers fall to the leather camera at his hip and linger there two heartbeats too long.

Investor 1 [slick]: "We can double deployment this quarter if the town centralizes management. We handle logistics, guarantee uptime. It's efficient."

Investor 2 [leaning forward, smiling]: "We cover immediate modules at no extra cost if Puerto Alba signs a shoreline stewardship clause granting operational control. It's a trade: security now for streamlined stewardship later."

You feel your jaw set. Community governance — not abdication — has been your condition from the first time you sketched tide lines in your notebook. The lab smells of ozone and possibility; the investors' offer smells like a shortcut with teeth.

You clear your throat because silence would let the room tilt toward easy numbers.

Mara Serrano: "Operational control means more than uptime," you say. "It changes who decides where the sea touches the land. It changes livelihoods."

Investor 1 [slick]: "We understand local sensitivities. That's why we propose a managed transition. Local input is part of the model."

You watch Elias Park. He smiles the small, diplomatic smile he uses when reconciling code with chaos.

Elias Park: "We designed these modules to be minimally invasive. They can be reconfigured with community oversight. The sensors are open-source; data's transparent. We can—"

Investor 2 [interrupting, lightly]: "—fast-track that openness through a governance framework we administer. We have the platform. Time is the variable here. Storm season is coming."

Nima rubs the bridge of her nose, voice sharp as the lab's LED.

Nima: "Investors are pushing for centralized management because it's easier to scale operations and attract more funding. But that also gives them leverage over future decisions."

Your palms are cool on the back of a mud-streaked chair. You remember Abuela Rosa's hands, callused and sure, how she taught you to read tide lines the way some people read old letters. You slide a folded scrap from your inner pocket and unfold it: sea-grass pressed flat between paper thin as a whisper. The handwriting is Abuela's — steady as the sea.

"Remember what the sea asks of us," it reads. The note smells faintly of brine and lavender. It feels like a tether.

You lift your head. Hope doesn't quiet the edge of fear; it sharpens it. The room feels smaller — the renderings larger; your obligation heavier. Still: there is heat in your chest, the kind that tells you this is a place where you act.

Investor 1 [regaining composure]: "We appreciate the point, Mara. Governance can be codified. What the town needs is guaranteed modules in place before the next storm cycle."

You can see the calculation in Elias Park's face — the engineer balancing human cost against the cold math of lives saved. His hand brushes the camera again; this time you read indecision in the gesture. You step closer, so your voice only reaches him.

Mara Serrano: "Elias, demand the clause that keeps management local. Don't let them shift 'efficiency' into permanent control."

Elias Park [a breath]: "If I push too hard and they walk, we delay deployment. If they walk, we risk not having modules when the next swell hits. But if we accept now, there's a precedent. People will lose control piece by piece."

The conversation cracks open into a quieter, sharper argument. Nima folds her arms and lays out the investor timetable in blunt terms.

Nima: "Investors expect ROI on a schedule. They want assurances that scaling won't be run by a thousand disparate committees. They want a single point of contact with authority. That's the rub."

You want to say: committees build resilience. You say: "Local decisions right now can save what stays standing later." But your voice is not enough alone.

Investor 2 [smiling too easily]: "We can agree to transparency reports and community liaisons. But full governance parity slows us to the pace of municipal processes. The next hurricane won't wait for policy redrafting."

The urgency rises like a tide. The positive valence of potential protection presses against the negative cost of ceded control. Your optimism — stubborn, stubborn as the saplings you and the volunteers planted in reclaimed marsh — pushes you forward rather than back.

Elias Park looks at the investors, then at you. He speaks carefully, as a man assembling a fragile bridge between data and heart.

Elias Park: "We need guarantees that deployment won't become unilateral. A conditional stewardship clause—community veto during initial five-year deployment. Auditable data, shared control panels, and a phased handover. We don't want the town to be a product."

Investor 1 [cool]: "A phased handover could be acceptable. Fifty percent governance initially, decreasing as systems prove reliable."

You feel the room tighten. Fifty percent sounds like a negotiation win until you imagine weak spots where community voices wear thin. Fifty percent could mean your people sign away the parts of the shoreline they've tended for generations.

Abuela Rosa's note flutters in your palm. You breathe in; the smell steadies you.

Mara Serrano: "Phased handover is only meaningful if the town retains veto power over encroachments — the shoreline lines, the fishing grounds, cultural sites. We won't trade that away."

Investor 2 [smiling too easily]: "Veto is a blunt instrument. It can stall necessary repairs. But we're open to a jointly appointed oversight council with weighted representation."

Elias Park meets your eyes. For a moment he looks undecided — you can see his optimism wobble into avoidance. Then he straightens, voice firmer.

Elias Park: "We ask for the council in writing. We ask for an independent audits clause. And we ask that any shoreline rights remain with Puerto Alba."

Nima nods and adds: "Signed protocols for data access and community-led maintenance — not just advisory seats."

The investors exchange looks that are professional and precise. They nod, but you catch the calculation under their smiles. The lab lights feel suddenly too white.

Investor 1 [cool]: "We'll take these proposals to our board. I can't promise they'll accept every point. They do care about outcomes — about lives — but they also require safeguards."

You want to press. You want to stand at the table and insist that 'safeguards' not become euphemism. Your lungs feel tight with the urgency of it. The chance to secure more modules is real; the risk of losing the town's agency is realer.

Elias Park glances at you, and you notice the way his jaw tightens. The room is a crucible: community, technology, capital, and the fragile human calculus between them.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Ask Elias if he'll refuse unless the community keeps veto power"
> Choice B: "Tell Elias to accept phased control and fight from inside"
[Outcome A: You lean in, gaze steady. Elias swallows and says, 'I won't sign anything that strips community veto. I'll push for the clause.' Relief and fear pass across his face like weather.]
[Outcome B: You suggest a tactical acceptance with oversight. Elias looks torn but murmurs, 'If that's what keeps modules coming, we'll make sure oversight means something.' Nima's expression goes unreadable.]
[MERGE: The investors will return with terms; the timeline compresses and decisions loom.]

The investors say they'll return with an answer on terms within seventy-two hours. That time compresses like the space between lightning and thunder. Hope quickens; worry spikes. You sense momentum — the pilot's success has made the town visible, and visibility draws capital like low tide draws gulls.

[Sound: Footsteps approaching, heavier, measured]
[Visual: Door opens onto the wind-splayed terrace; the evening light slants in cold and sharp]

Iris Varela steps into the lab, her presence smoothing the room's edges like a practiced tide. The municipal lapel pin on her jacket gleams under the task lamp. She doesn't look like an investor — she looks like someone who has spent more nights steering councils than spreadsheets. Her voice is precise, the tone you've heard before in meetings with consequences.

Iris Varela [quietly]: "I hear the offers. I've been talking to both sides. There are ways to secure the modules without surrendering the town's core rights — if someone will accept political oversight in return."

You feel a dozen threads pull taut. Iris's entrance changes the dynamic: politics, power, a broker with the map of both. The hope in that is hot and immediate. If Iris can broker a conditional contract, then maybe the town keeps voice and gains hardware. But the cost — political leverage and precedent — sits heavy.

Mara Serrano: "What kind of oversight?"

Iris Varela: "A co-signed agreement. Municipal guarantees that bind investors to community clauses, and in return, the town accepts a structured institutional role in deployment. I can make the approvals move quickly. It preserves immediacy and secures rights on paper."

Elias Park listens, amber eyes slanted. He looks at Iris with something that might be gratitude or suspicion; you can't be sure.

Elias Park: "That sounds…possible. But any municipal guarantee I need to be sure doesn't erase local maintenance prerogatives."

Iris Varela [a small, tight smile]: "We'll write it. But understand this: leverage costs power. You hand me a co-sign, I hand you bureaucracy that can enforce those clauses. It shifts the battlefield from negotiation to policy."

Nima's hands, expressive even when still, lay out a cold reality.

Nima: "Investors like a municipal co-sign because it limits liability. But it also centralizes authority. If Maria (sic) — (she corrects herself softly) — if Puerto Alba signs this, the town's governance becomes entangled in investor timetables."

You press your thumb along the edge of the sea-grass note until the fibers make a quiet rasp. Abuela's writing feels like a touchstone: the sea asks patience, memory, and stewardship. But storms don't wait.

Your heart races. The room feels electric, every conversation a current. Hope is loud and bright; fear is an undertow. Your love for Elias Park — careful, stubborn, practical — pushes you to protect him from losing himself to investor timetables, even as your desire for immediate safety pushes toward compromise. These pulls make your chest ache in a way that's both terrifying and clarifying.

You meet Elias Park's eyes again. He meets yours, and in that glance a thousand tiny things are negotiated: trust, fear, the future. He clears his throat.

Elias Park: "We should draft three paths: community governance as the default; conditional scaling with investor oversight and strict audits; and a municipal-co-signed hybrid. Present them to the investors and put them on tight timelines. Let them choose which terms to accept — in writing."

Iris Varela inclines her head. Investor 1 checks his watch. Nima translates policy speak into probabilities. You feel the adrenaline of the approaching choice-rate sharpening every sense. The next words you speak will compress into the town's decisions for months to come.

The arousal that began as a low hum is now a drumbeat. Hope swells — fiercely, insistently — because there are ways forward if people stand and fight and craft terms that hold. You are not alone at this table. Elias Park’s presence beside you is a lever; Iris Varela’s offer is both trap and lifeline. Abuela Rosa's note is a compass.

You inhale, tasting salt and coffee, and prepare to negotiate.

[Scene: Council Hall | Night]
[Visual: The chamber is lit in institutional cools; posters and placards cluster near the doorway. A single spotlight tracks the table where terms will be drafted.]
[Music: Crescendoing strings, urgent but hopeful]
[Sound: Murmurs of negotiation, the rustle of paper]

The room feels like a throat — small, enclosed, and necessary. The investors have returned. Iris Varela sits at the council table like a conductor ready to cue the orchestra. Elias Park, Nima, and you fold the three possible frameworks into neat, dangerous proposals. The air vibrates with possibilities.

You place the sea-grass note on the table, face up as if to bless the documents with roots and memory. The sight of it steadies you.

Investor 2: "Your three-path approach is…organized. It gives structure. We can decide in the morning if we accept the hybrid with municipal oversight or conditional scaling."

You sense the timeline closing. Hope demands quick, decisive action; responsibility demands careful safeguarding. The emotion surges, high and bright — you're not just arguing numbers. You're arguing for a town's right to shape its future.

Elias Park leans toward you before the room dissolves into final bargaining murmurs.

Elias Park [low]: "Whatever you decide, I'll hold the line on auditability and community access to data. I owe that to the people who helped install the sensors."

You reach for his hand across the table for a moment that is quick and human — fingers brushing fabric and skin. The contact is enough to steady both your resolve and the room's tension.

Mara Serrano: "Think of Abuela's note. Remember what the sea asks of us. Not just protection, but stewardship."

Iris Varela's expression softens, just for a breath, then settles into the practiced resolve you know so well.

Iris Varela: "Protection with stewardship is my selling point. I can present a co-signed agreement that preserves legal rights and enforces oversight. But signing it hands political authority to me and this council. That gives me leverage I will use — to guard the town, but also to make the hard decisions."

The near-climax thrums in your veins. The emotional arousal curve that began as a steady rise is now at its peak: hope, pressure, romantic steadiness, institutional gravity, all aligned on a single hinge. You can feel the town leaning in with you, hearing every clipped negotiation syllable like a heartbeat.

This is the moment to choose how to negotiate: fight for community governance, accept conditional scaling with tight oversight, or invite Iris Varela to co-sign and bind the process into municipal power.

You inhale, steadied by the sea-grass and Elias Park's steady presence, and prepare to speak.
