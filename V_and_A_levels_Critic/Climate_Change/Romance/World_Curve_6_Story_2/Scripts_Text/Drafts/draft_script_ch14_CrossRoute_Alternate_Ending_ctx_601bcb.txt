# Route: Cross-Route Alternate Ending


[Scene: Community Kitchen | Morning — Two months after the agreement]
[Visual: Warm, worn benches; enamel pots steaming on a battered stove; posters advertising retraining classes pinned over a municipal noticeboard]
[Music: Sparse piano, low register, slow tempo]
[Sound: Cutlery clinking, soft chatter, kettle hiss]

Narration:
You know the map of losses by heart now. It is not just a line on a document; it is a set of small, precise absences: a dock where a boy used to tie his boat, a cove that used to hold three skiffs and now holds one, a stretch of promenade where an empty bench faces the sea and remembers a voice that no longer comes. This morning, those absences wait for you in the shape of work — hot broth to ladle, volunteer sign-in sheets to straighten, Sofía's flyers for youth seamanship scrawled in marker.

You move through the kitchen like someone walking the seams of a familiar garment that has been re-stitched. The steam on your cheeks is gentle, the smell of onion and sea-salt laced through the broth. Your hands remember what they must do before your head gives you permission to feel.

Abuela Rosa: "Mara, your hand — come, stir a little. Don't let it stick."

Narration:
Her voice is small but exacting, a weathered compass. You reach and take the ladle she offers. It is heavy and warm. The motion is simple; the rhythm steadies you in a way words cannot. You are doing the thing that keeps people fed and so, for the moment, whole.

Sofía (off-screen, breathless, proud): "We set up the first junior crew meeting for next week. They're signed on for basic safety and patchwork repair. I used your tide diagrams."

Sofía: "You should come, tia — you can tell them about Abuela's notes."

You (internal): The image floats — your notebook splayed open, tide-smeared sketches, a dozen kids crowding the doorway asking about knots and marsh plants. You taste something like hope and salt. It is fragile; you tuck it behind the steady weight of obligations.

[Scene: Berm Construction Edge | Late Morning — The new structure rises like a blunt horizon]
[Visual: Low, engineered berm of concrete and planted terraces; machines idling in shallow water; workers in orange vests speaking in soft, efficient tones]
[Music: Distant cello drone, slowly sustaining]
[Sound: Slow drip of water, a muffled generator, gulls very far off]

Narration:
You stand where land becomes policy. The berm is solid and foreign in the same breath — a rational answer to a rising sea. The surface is cool under your palm; the concrete smells faintly of metal and rain. Where coves were once accessible by foot, now ramps and sluice gates speak of efficiency and exclusion. The town is safer in inches and less free in gestures.

Iris approaches, her steps measured. There's a softness in her expression that survives council rooms and contracts — a softness you have seen when she believes she is protecting people.

Iris Varela: "We kept the channels where we could. We couldn't do everything. But we saved the harbor's main corridor. We kept the co-op's boathouse intact."

You (softly): "You did what you said you would."

Iris: (shrugs, not a concession) "I signed for political cover and I signed for the money. That wasn't the same as doing what you wanted. I know what you gave away, Mara."

Narration:
She names the thing you have been naming in private: that preservation required trade-offs you do not forget. Her voice is not triumphant; it is ledgered. The wind moves through the grasses planted on the berm, carrying the faint earthiness of engineered marsh sedge.

Elias arrives, sleeves rolled, his hands still smelling of the workshop. He studies the berm with the trained, hungry mind that has always been his defense against feeling. He looks at you, and for a moment you expect the old warmth — the easy angle of his smile that used to make the work feel like a shared secret. Today his smile is small, a mouth closing behind responsibility.

Elias Park: "They're installing the cooperative boat slip design next week. It isn't as many slips as we wanted, but the retraining modules are scheduled; Nima and I rewrote the curriculum to include engine basics and EmberNet mapping."

You: "You're doing more than engineering now."

Elias: (a short laugh, then earnest) "It felt like the practical place to be. If we can't hold the coastline the way we imagined, we can hold people's livelihoods. We can make other doors open."

Narration:
You see him in the kitchen later, checking sign-up lists, sketching rota plans in the margins of a structural diagram. His hands are steadier than his mouth. You are grateful, but the gratitude carries a weight — a tightening in your chest that is not anger so much as the ache of recognition. The person you fell toward and the person at the table of municipal deals have folded into a different geometry.

[Scene: Promenade | Late Afternoon — A lowered sun paints the changed shore in thin gold]
[Visual: The promenade stretches with new guardrails; some original benches have been moved; a section of rock and cove is now filled and terraced]
[Music: Single sustained violin note that breathes out slowly]
[Sound: Wind over the water, distant hammering that ebbs and does not return]

Narration:
The promenade keeps memory the way a cliff keeps layers of sediment. Today it keeps the sound of fewer footsteps. Tourists have not returned in numbers; instead, there are strollers of municipal workers and, once in a while, the light, quick laughter of Sofía's cohort practicing knots on an upended crate. The sea is constant and indifferent, a gray mirror that gives nothing away.

You sit on a bench that used to be someone else's favorite place. The bench is not the same as it was; it now has municipal rivets and a small plaque that reads: "For Safety and Community — Restored, 2032." The plaque is correct and also not what your hand remembers.

Abuela Rosa: (settling beside you) "You did the hard thing. There is dignity in choices that save lives."

You: "We lost more than I thought we would."

Abuela Rosa: "You saved what you could. A life is a hard count to tally and an easy thing to lose if you are not careful."

Narration:
Her words are simple. You feel them like a tide that lifts a boat enough to see its hull again. Behind them is an ache that threads the day's small victories: the new food program that keeps a family fed through the off-season; Sofía teaching kids to reef nets responsibly; cooperative engine bays where owners swap hours so a single vessel serves many hands. None of it replaces the cove, the choked inlet, the exact curve of a shoreline. They reweave life in a pattern you did not expect but must learn to live in.

Elias arrives and halts a few steps away, hands tucked in his pockets. He watches the water for a long moment before looking at you.

Elias: "I wanted to tell you — the investors lifted a clause they had about immediate scaling. They accepted the conditional plan. It means — it means the programs could actually be funded long-term."

You: (a small, inadequate laugh) "So the money turned out to listen eventually."

Elias: "It listened because Iris forced people's hands. Because you — because you agreed to bind our work to the council's authority. That was the hinge."

Narration:
He searches your face as if trying to find the shape of the person who made that choice. His gaze is not accusatory; it is cataloguing, the polite professional curiosity of someone measuring consequence. You see the distance you have both come to accept. It does not feel like abandonment, exactly; it feels like two rivers bent by the same weather into different courses.

You: "Do you resent me?"

Elias: (a long breath) "Resentment is too hot for this. I am tired, Mara. I am a person who builds bridges; sometimes bridges become barricades. I am trying to learn the difference. I'm pouring that learning into programs now. They are—" (he falters, looks away) "—they're all I can give that isn't just words."

You:
His words settle into you — practical, plain, not a balm. For a moment you are angry at the coldness of the world that demanded such a trade. For a moment you are grateful and hollow. For a moment you remember the nights welded to plans and the mornings you watched waves climb where your father's boat used to rest.

Sofía arrives, cheeks flushed, carrying folded life-jackets like small, hopeful flags.

Sofía: "Tia, the first training filled up. They want to start building a shared boat schedule. We have a slot for you to show them knotwork."

You (smile, then small): "I will.

Narration:
There is a pattern to what remains: community, altered but alive; social programs that bear the face of compromise; a coastline that is safer but reshaped. The material comforts are real — roofs that do not leak, a harbour that will not flood at the next tide — and they are bought with a silence in certain places. You feel that silence most when the promenade empties, when the gulls fly low and the lights come on over the new guardrails.

Abuela Rosa: (quietly) "You will love differently now. That is not the end of love."

Narration:
You test the truth of her sentence. You let your memory go to Elias's hand, the accidental brush at the table the night you sealed the agreement. There is a warmth there that now sits beside the pragmatic plans and the municipal forms. Love, you find, can reshape to fit new structures. It can, even when distance grows, become a thing of different work — tending programs, showing up to teach, speaking in measured tones in corridors of power.

You rise when the light softens. The promenade's new rail hums faintly under your palm. The sea's noise is a patient counting, not a shout. The town is safer in the ways that matter for mornings: roofs, food, work. It is smaller in the ways the heart marks as loss: a cove filled, a boat route altered, benches that remember more than they face.

Elias: "Come with me tomorrow morning. We'll oversee the cooperative slip layout. I need someone who remembers why we're doing it."

You (slowly): "I will come. We will make it worth the footprint they left."

Narration:
You mean it. It is not a promise of the old things — not a promise of a neat reconciliation of all that broke and all that was rebuilt. It is a pledge to the particular labor of staying: to make social programs that are not charity but structure; to teach skills that let people reclaim agency; to hold municipal power to the light when it overreaches.

Abuela Rosa: "Bring a thermos."

You: (smiling) "I will."

Narration:
The evening folds into itself. On the promenade, a group of children practice knotwork with Sofía's clipped instructions. Laughter rings — not as full as it once did, but deliberate; a practiced thing like a stitch. You watch them and you feel something that is not light, and not heavy — a careful, aching steadiness.

You (internal):
You have traded a shoreline for safety. You love someone who has retreated into work as refuge. You have bound your voice to a councilwoman who will do hard things. Between these facts you build a life that keeps other lives warming, fed, moving.

The cost is a visible map on the land, and an invisible thrum in your chest. You carry both. You learn to carry them with hands that work: ladling, teaching, signing off on rosters, checking engines in the cooperative bay. The romance between you and Elias does not end in a single grand gesture. It changes into breakfasts shared over rota plans, into him turning up with a thermos and you standing alongside him while Abuela checks the tide. It is not the ardor of the first heat; it is the slow, resilient tending of what is left.

[Visual: Wide shot of the promenade at dusk; a few lights glow; the berm silhouettes the altered horizon]
[Music: Piano note dwindles to a single, resolved tone]
[Sound: Night wind, distant gulls]

Narration (final):
You walk home on the raised walkway, the town's new contours humming beneath your boots. Somewhere a child practices a knot, somewhere a mother signs a retraining form, somewhere Iris argues a policy that will be tested a hundred times by weather and by conscience. The shoreline will never be what it was, and yet people will continue to step into morning. You have chosen the life of tending, of allocating care where the sea and the market leave gaps. It is not triumphant. It is not pure. It is necessary.

You press the compass to your chest as the light dies. It is a small, familiar weight. It does not point to a single north now; it points to a path you will walk again and again — toward roofs that hold, toward programs that teach, toward a love that has learned to be work as much as warmth.

[Music: Soft sustain fades out]
