You run your thumb along the dented edge and try to order the morning — one meeting at a time, small engineering sprints, conditional clauses to be drafted, community workshops scheduled like stitches.

You are careful now. Quiet negotiation was the choice you made: a compromise stitched out of patient maneuvering, a rota that would keep Iris present without letting her become the whole. In practice it looks like a brittle peace — agreements written gently enough to satisfy investors and blunt enough to reassure the Council. Mara Serrano: "We need the marsh to be part of the first phase. If we postpone it, the barriers do more harm than good in the long run."

Mateo comes by with a thermos, his face splintered into lines of relief. "It held," he says simply, and because he is practical and blunt, his smile is unadorned but wide.

Mara Serrano: "It held." (Your own relief is quieter. It sits like a bruise that eases but leaves a mark.)

Elias Park: "The sensor calibrations did what we expected. Nima's oversight helped. Investors will be pleased." (He looks at you, and your eyes meet in a moment of held possibility.)

But the quiet is fragile. The retrofits you won for the marsh are proceeding, but slower than you hoped. Permits, volunteer fatigue, and the calculation of risk versus return have stretched timelines. The rotating council keeps watch, but the work is human, and humans carry limits.

There is no cinematic rupture; nothing explodes. Instead there is a slow, aching unbuilding. Houses are boarded; people move like ghosts along the raised walkways. The town’s sound is low and prolonged, not sharp.

Mara Serrano: (You kneel beside Mateo, hands wet, feeling the uselessness of your compass.) "Hold on." (It is what anyone says; it is less medicine than a plea.)

Mara Serrano: "We were trying." (Your voice croaks. It doesn't fix the break.)

Elias Park: "We didn't account for the surge layering with the lunar tide curve." (He looks at you.) "Our models missed a compound variable. I missed it." (He is not asking for absolution; he is stating failure.)

Mara Serrano: (You feel a cold widening in your chest.) "We all made choices." (You don't say whose choices should have been different. The sentence would curve into blame, and you do not want that tone to define this hour.)

Her voice is a fissure where grief tries to sound like orders. You cannot tell whether her resolve will save the next week or hollow the next year.
