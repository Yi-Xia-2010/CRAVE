# Route: Final Alternate / Sad Ending
# Context: c4ca42

[Scene: Seawall | Morning]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_1"]
[Music: Low, hollow strings — slow, steady]
[Sound: Wind sighing; the scrape of metal on concrete; the distant clank of a buoy]

You wake to the taste of salt in the back of your throat and the ache in your shoulders from yesterday's carrying. The seawall looks smaller in the gray light, but the surf keeps arriving the same way — patient, indifferent. Your gloves smell of tar and old rope. Mud flakes from your boots when you climb down to the bench beside the wall.

Mateo is already there, sleeves rolled, back squared to the water. He hands you a pry bar without looking up, eyes fixed on a seam of concrete where barnacles have knit themselves into a seam. He moves with the easy economy of someone who's spent more days answering tides than calendars.

Mateo: "Morning. Tide's low enough to get the east run patched. Think you can hold the bracing while I lever?"

Mara Serrano: "I can. Keep the bar steady, and don't—" Your voice is small against the ocean. You try for practical; the muscles in your jaw unknot and retighten instead.

Mateo [Grunting as he shifts]: "You always sound like you're trying to convince the tide itself. You okay?"

Mara Serrano: "I will be. Just—" You look past him, toward where the horizon is a band of lead. "This patch won't hold the next season if the storms keep coming."

Mateo pauses, then breathes out a short laugh that isn't light. "Nothing will unless we keep doing it. One seam at a time. You know that." He jerks the pry bar; a slab gives with a wet, surgical noise. He hands you the hammer. "Hammer."

Your hands find the rhythm — brace, swing, scrape. Salt spray beads on your skin and stings at your eyes; the coarse concrete rubs like a memory. You work until your knuckles are raw and the barnacles sing beneath the blade.

[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_2"]
[Sound: Metal hitting stone; breaths; distant gulls]

Mateo talks while you work: stories of small wins — a neighbor's foundation steadied, a family who could sleep upstairs again. He peppers the practical with old jokes that fall flat and then land. Each sentence is a tether: to normalcy, to a life of small, fixable things.

Mateo: "If we keep at this, the walkways will hold through the winter. We'll not be pretty, but we'll be here."

You want to answer with every plan you have sketched in your notebook — marsh buffers, community stewardship maps, seating those old pylons in a grid — but the concrete demands simple replies. You say, "Okay," more times than you mean to.

The work is a slow, physical conversation. It keeps the panic at bay: muscle to sound, effort to outcome. But every time a slab yields, a little truth slides with it — that repairs buy time, not promise.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs | Midday]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_3"]
[Music: Quiet hum of equipment; a single, sustained piano note — contemplative]
[Sound: Soft beeping of a sensor, the click of a wrist device being secured]

Elias Park is crouched at the water-facing window, calibrating a wrist-mounted sensor. Up close, the little device looks like a flattened compass: smooth, efficient. He smiles when he notices you watching, and for a second his face is something like sunlight on wet stone — warm but thin.

Elias Park [Softly]: "Hey. You found the lab without three detours. That's progress."

Mara Serrano: "Only two detours." You move nearer, and the smell of wet metal and algae presses at you. "Show me."

He holds the sensor out; the screen ripples with tiny numbers and a scrolling graph. He taps, explaining with the sort of measured enthusiasm that used to make your notebooks look neater.

Elias Park: "If we place modular pylons in this configuration, they interrupt the erosive flow at key points. The sensors feed data back — humidity, tidal velocity, substrate shift. The pylons are designed to be low-footprint. We can iterate."

You listen, wanting to believe the neatness of his diagrams. He maps solutions like someone arranging lifelines on a page.

Mara Serrano: "Low-footprint on paper." Your voice tightens. "And what about the marsh? About the people who fish where you want to put these things?"

Elias Park's smile falters a fraction. He sets the device down and folds his hands like a plea. "We run smaller pilots first. We consult. Nima's ready to relay community feedback. I'm not trying to bulldoze anything. I swear."

The exchange lengthens — words passed back and forth that are more than technical. You ask about failure modes, about investor timeframes that have already been mentioned in town whispers. Elias Park answers with data and with caveats, and when you press about the social cost, he starts to deflect, then circles back.

Elias Park [A little quieter]: "I know you're worried about scale and speed. I worry about that, too. But I also don't want to watch the town get washed away while we argue. There's a way to do both — careful tech and community governance."

Mara Serrano: "I want both, too." The sentence comes out thin. "I'm tired of promises, Eli. I'm tired of things that look like help and end up selling off the shore."

Elias Park: "I know. I—" He stops, glances at the sensor that pulses like a heartbeat. "Let me show you the model run with the marsh restoration layered in. Not instead of people doing the hard, local stuff — with it."

You want to lean into his optimism because it feels warm and possible, but your chest remains a low, steady pressure. The tidal graphs on his screen don't erase the memory of a neighbor losing a line of mooring overnight.

[Scene: Marketboard / Outside Saltmarsh Labs | Afternoon]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_4"]
[Music: Whispered percussion; low sustained chord — uneasy]
[Sound: Murmurs, a thin bell clanging the call to public notice in the distance]

Sofía has gathered a handful of students and a couple of parents. She hands out photocopied flyers and pins one to the lab's noticeboard. The kids chant a muted rhythm: names of places they love; their voices tremble but hold. Sofía's curls frizz in the salt wind; she looks fierce and young and terrified in a way that sharpens everything around you.

Sofía: "They want a contract without showing the maps. That's not safe. We deserve to know."

You crouch to meet her eye.

Mara Serrano: "You're doing good work. Keep that list of questions. Make them specific."

Sofía: "Do you think they'll listen?"

Mara Serrano: "They will if we make them see the cost. If we show where the marsh connects to the nets. If—" Your voice frays. You stop because the nouns start to sound like demands you don't know how to meet.

An older man lingers at the edge of the crowd, reading the memo on his phone. His mouth thins when he gets to the line that makes your chest catch: 'Immediate acceptance recommended.' The word immediate is like a knife in the air. Someone hands you the phone.

[Sound: Notification ping; a paper rustle]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_5"]

Iris Varela's memo reads: "Council is advised to accept the attached contract. Engineering teams and funding will arrive within two weeks. Immediate implementation reduces risk of structural loss. — I.V."

The message is as concise as a ledger, and it lands in the crowd like a stone.

Mateo [Quiet, under his breath]: "She says immediate like it's the only thing left."

Mara Serrano: "Immediate is not the same as right." Your reply is softer than the thought it contains.

Sofía squares her shoulders and starts drafting a list of demands: transparency, independent review, community oversight. The students echo her, louder this time. A protest is a small, human machine against a decision made in cold fonts.

You look at the lab where Elias Park is packing a sensor into a padded case. His hands are steady; his jaw is drawn. There's something unreadable in his face when he catches your eye — a mix of worry and resolve.

Elias Park [Approaching]: "They're pushing the timeline. Iris Varela sent that memo just before lunch. Investors are getting impatient."

Mara Serrano: "And counsel?"

Elias Park: "Divided. Nima thinks there's room to negotiate. Iris Varela thinks fast is safer than messy."

Mara Serrano: "Which is it?"

Elias Park exhales, then lets the words come slow. "I don't know. But we can make a plan that keeps people in their houses without selling the shoreline."

Mara Serrano [Internal]: You want to press him further, want to know if his prototypes can be executed on a human timeline and on terms that keep control local. Instead you tuck your hands into your pockets — the waterproof pages of your notebook damp against your palm.

[Scene: Abuela Rosa's Porch / Cliffside Promenade | Late Afternoon]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_6"]
[Music: Quiet, plucked cello — ancient as tides]
[Sound: The slow creak of wood; gulls in the distance; faint children's voices]

Abuela Rosa watches you approach without comment. Her gaze is soft but assessing, like a tide line read at low water. When she speaks, the words have the weight of long practice.

Abuela Rosa: "You look like the sea took a piece of you and left a bruise. Sit."

You sit on the porch step. Her hands take yours for a moment, callused and warm, and the touch steadies a little of the vertigo inside you.

Mara Serrano: "She sent the memo. Immediate. They're pushing the town to accept."

Abuela Rosa [Listening]: "I know that voice. She chooses the quickest route because it is the easiest to explain to herself. But quick is not always safety."

Mara Serrano: "Iris Varela thinks this will keep people safe."

Abuela Rosa: "Perhaps. She has lost much to storms. Her hands close around solutions like they are life rafts. That doesn't mean they won't cut when you hold them too hard." She taps a small wooden box at her knee — inside, old tide maps and a list of old planting dates.

Abuela Rosa: "There is older knowing. My mother taught me to watch the eelgrass in spring. When it goes, the shore follows. If we place stones where the current can gather sediment, and plant the spartina back in places they pull away — you can make a hybrid: nature and careful iron. Test it small, learn. Don't let hunger for quick fixes drown the longer work."

Mara Serrano: "A hybrid." The word is fragile and promising at once.

Abuela Rosa: "Try the smaller wall where the netters' moorings lie. Let Elias Park's pylons be a pilot. Let people keep the right to say no to what costs them homes. If you test, you buy knowledge. If you accept blindly, you buy a promise that may not hold."

Her voice is as calm as a reef. It presses against the low panic in you and makes a small hollow where a choice can sit without immediacy.

Mara Serrano: "How do we make sure tests count? How do we keep people from being bought out with the first check?"

Abuela Rosa: "You keep records. You keep the maps. You hold the town's hand. And you teach the young ones what the tide remembers." She looks at you kindly, unafraid. "You will be tired. But you are not alone."

[Scene: Saltmarsh Labs — Damp Workbench | Dusk]
[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_7"]
[Music: Single low drone; heartbeat-like]
[Sound: The distant call of the town bell; the soft scratch of pencil on paper]

Back at the lab, you set your notebook on the bench and spread out tide sketches and spreadsheets. The ink has bled at the edges from damp fingers. You polish the chipped shell pendant, the metal cool beneath your thumb. It is an old, small talisman whose cracks remember storms you were too young to name. You rub at it not because it will change the world but because ritual steadies the hand.

You sort papers into piles labeled for different possible paths — grassroots repairs, modular pilot, negotiated contract. Each stack carries a cost in lost ground, in compromise, in human dignity. You think of Mateo's work-rough hands, of Elias Park's hopeful demonstrations, of Iris Varela's clean memo. You feel the three pulls like currents on your ribs.

Your sea-green eyes ache at the margins from looking too long, and a tiredness sits like silt in your limbs. The lab smells of ozone and salt, and the computer fan hums like distant surf. The town bell clangs again, single notes that seem to mark the passing of another small era.

You arrange your sketches, touching each line as if the ink would answer back. You whisper the names of the places at risk: The Old Netting, Rosa's Marsh, the children's climbing rock. You want to make a speech that holds all of them, but words that hold everything are rare.

Elias Park enters quietly, hands empty. He doesn't speak at once. When he does, his voice is low.

Elias Park: "They're calling a council meeting at dusk. Iris Varela will be there. I will, too. Nima says we should prepare a short presentation of the pilot and restoration overlap."

Mara Serrano: "Do you think they'll listen?"

Elias Park [Looking at your hands on the pendant]: "Some will. Some will not. But we have to show up."

Mara Serrano: "All of us?"

Elias Park shrugs, a small movement that hides more than it reveals. "If you want them to see a different path, they need to see people who will work for it. I will tell them the data. You will tell them what it means on the ground."

You consider the list of names you could call: volunteers, the students, Abuela Rosa. Each name has a weight. You can feel the low pressure in your chest sharpen into something like focus. The arousal has been quiet all day — an undercurrent. Now it picks up, not into panic but into a resolute ache.

Sofía appears in the doorway, breathless. Her flyer is damp, but her voice is steady.

Sofía: "We got the school. We'll come. We want transparency. We want to make sure no one signs their house away."

Mara Serrano: "Bring the list of questions. Bring the marsh maps. Bring the young ones who will inherit the shore."

Sofía nods, and you notice how small she still is when she breathes in and out. You put the pendant against your chest and feel the slow beat of your heart.

[Background:"ch2_c4ca42_8"]
[Sound: A low, distant murmur — people moving, preparing]

A public notice pinned to the municipal board rounds off the day: Council Hall, dusk. You read the time twice, as if a second look will alter the gravity. You feel the pull of all three directions — the hands that fix, the hands that engineer, the hands that sign.

You stand and gather the soaked papers, tucking the spreadsheets and tide sketches into a waterproof folder. The pendant swings once against your chest, a small, private metronome. You know, with a slow, certain dread, that your voice will be needed tonight. Not because you are the loudest, but because no one else has held the maps and the memories and the names in a single place.

You step outside. The evening air is colder now, and the light thins like a bruise. The town moves toward the Hall with an unsteady, deliberate pace — some with banners, some with briefcases, some with hands empty. The seaside smell — salt, frying oil, wet wood — follows you like an old argument.

You inhale. It does not clear your chest. It only fills it with the knowledge that the choice ahead is not between two tidy options, but among fractures: immediate security that might hollow the town, volunteer toil that might not be enough, and careful hybrid work that requires time the town may not have.

For a moment you close your eyes and press the pendant to your lips. It tastes of metal and memory.

You take one more slow breath, and then you move toward Council Hall.
