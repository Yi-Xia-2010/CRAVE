## Visual Novel Title
Mariselle Rising

---

## World View
Mariselle is a near-future coastal micro-region in 2038 where climate change has reconfigured everything people once took for granted. The town’s economy, identity and streets were built on fishing, promenade cafes and artisan yards; now seasonal “supertides,” intensified storms, salt-worn infrastructure and managed retreats have remade public life. Political power sits in a tense triad: municipal offices and regional development authorities pushing engineered, centralized solutions; private capital seeking profitable densification along fragile coasts; and grassroots networks fighting for stewardship, cultural survival and equitable retreat.

Everyday technology is pragmatic—solar canopies, desal hubs, crawlspace retrofits, improvised wetlands restoration—and the visual palette is salt-stained: seafoam greens, slate grays, rust, and neon emergency lights. Architecture mixes reclaimed timber and corrugated metal with municipal glass facades. Religion and ritual survive in small shrines on piers and memorial plaques for lives lost to flash floods. Class tensions thread through town meetings: legacy fishers, displaced renters, volunteer restoration crews, and contractors in polished boots. Conflict centers on what "saving Mariselle" means—top-down engineering, market-friendly relocation, or a slow, community-driven living-shoreline approach that preserves daily life and memory.

Against this network of policies, storms and grief, people must choose care versus spectacle, compromise versus confrontation, and whether intimacy can survive while the town remakes itself.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Ari Navarro, a coastal planner returned home to Mariselle to lead a community-led adaptive strategy after personal loss. The story is a Rags-to-Riches (Rise) arc: you begin beneath weight—guilt, scarcity of trust and bureaucratic resistance—and gradually build agency, coalition and emotional openness. The common route (early chapters) establishes the stakes: supertides, Lina Moreau's municipal program for engineered defenses, Ephraim Blake's developer schemes, and Sora Watanabe’s urgent organizing.

At Chapter 3 the narrative splits: one route follows a fidelity to grassroots solutions and a romantic arc with Sora; another explores a pragmatic political compromise that draws you into a complex partnership with Lina; the third tests a path of legal-political syntheses; and a later emergent route is forged from cross-route decisions that create a hybrid, “True” settlement between technocracy and community stewardship. Each route contains middle splits forcing moral choices (mitigation vs adaptation, confrontational activism vs negotiated settlement, rapid relocation vs incremental retrofit). The emotional arc rises steadily: tactical victories, repaired trust, and finally tangible improvements to Mariselle’s resilience coincide with increasingly candid intimacy. Endings vary—Happy (cooperative success and a durable romance), Bittersweet (structural wins at cultural cost), Sad (a costly compromise that fractures relationships), and True (a messy but sustainable third way)—but every route takes Ari from guilt and isolation toward growing competence, coalition, and emotional repair.
