[Scene: Living Shoreline Pilot Site | Late Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of a patchwork shoreline—newly planted cordgrass, a line of woven reed fascines, volunteers moving like careful punctuation along the edge]
[Sound: Low hum of murmured questions from reporters; the distant slap of water on old pilings; the steady click of cameras]
[Music: Tense, driving strings with a bright harmonic undertow—urgent, hopeful]

You step out from under the greenhouse tarp and the air takes you in—salty, peat-dark, spattered with the faint sweetness of marsh flowers. The sky is a washed silver, the sun trying to pry itself through mist; reflections glint like tiny promises on standing puddles. Volunteers bend and rise in a rhythm that feels both improvised and rehearsed. Someone has propped a hand-painted sign on a crate: LIVING SHORELINE / MARISELLE WORKS. The letters wobble, earnest.

Sora is already moving through the crowd—one long, charismatic current. They clap a hand on a volunteer’s knee, trade a joke with a reporter (a quick sideways grin), then fling themselves into a short, bright speech that makes even the camera operators lean in.

Sora Watanabe: "We show what we can do with hands, science, and a refusal to be washed out of our own story. This isn't just a pilot—it's a promise."

You watch them. The way they speak makes the raft of cameras feel less like accusation and more like witnesses. When their eyes find you, there is that same steady, fierce invitation that has anchored you since the boardwalk the night you said yes.

[Visual: Cut to close on Lena—sunburned, hair pinned back—threading native reeds into a taught rope. Her fingers are raw-bright, deliberate]
[Sound: The soft rasp of reed against reed; a child's excited whisper]

Lena (laughing as she ties): "There—hold that end steady. Don’t yank the roots, or they'll sulk and die."

You crouch beside a kid—brown-mud knees, too-big rain boots—who is cradling a sedge like a fragile secret. You switch from municipal metrics to small-hand instruction without thinking: levee height predictions to how to cup a root.

You think of your sibling—how a single missed instruction once unstitched everything—and the lesson cuts close. The guilt hums under your ribs like a low tide that never fully withdraws. But the current here is different: careful hands, patient voices, people naming what matters.

A volunteer nudges a tablet toward you; a council member from the nearby ward is watching, clipboard taut. A local reporter angles her microphone, bright-eyed, ready to ask about the pilot’s efficacy.

Maya Cruz (soft, running a hand along the boardwalk railing): "They're saying the footage will run tonight. People will see this. Real people, blinks-and-all."

You could—right now—read off infiltration rates, accretion speed, the expected sediment capture in month two. You could anchor the pilot in numbers and give the council a tidy column of proof. Or you could let Sora's rally turn the heat up, push attention toward action.

The reporters don't wait long. A woman with a camera lens the size of your palm sidles up, asking for a quick comment. Your words feel like currency here; you count them, spend them carefully.

Ari Navarro: "The early data suggests the fascines are already reducing nearshore scour in sampling plots A and C. We expect measurable accretion within the first three tidal cycles, but—" (you breathe, tilt toward the human) "—what matters most is that people learn to tend what will protect them. The numbers are a map, not the mapmaker."

Reporter: "Are you worried the council will try to take control—turn this into a headline, not a home?"

You let out a half-laugh, the kind that clears smoke from a throat.

Ari Navarro: "If they do, we make sure the residents who planted these reeds are the ones who keep them."

Sora moves in then, planting themselves at your elbow and letting the interview run on the warmth of their presence. They add a quick, snappy line for the camera that gets a ripple of applause from the volunteers and a laugh from the reporters.

Sora Watanabe: "And if anyone doubts we'll stand for our coast—tell them to come down here at dawn. We'll be standing with shovels."

Their eyes find yours in the brief silence that follows. They don't ask you to be performative; they ask you to be present.

[Visual: A drone hums overhead, green LED blinking]
[Sound: Drone motor; a clack from the staging yard gate in the distance]
[Music: Pulse quickens—percussion joins the strings]

From the direction of the staging yard comes a new current of noise: a low murmur of activity and the metallic creak of a gate. You know that yard—the place where Ephraim Blake's materials were stockpiled last season, where municipal trucks sit like sleeping beasts. If Sora's eyes are lighting with the electricity of escalation, it's because the yard is a symbol as much as a stockpile.

Your fitness-watch, always retrofitted with tactile alarms for barometric shifts, clicks lightly against your wrist. The barometer readout is a metronome you learned to trust: steady rise. It tugs at you in ways both professional and primal—an old reflex to measure and prepare before the sea writes its own decisions into the map.

Maya sidles up, breath smelling faintly of wet tobacco and cinnamon gum.

Maya Cruz: "Lina called earlier. She wants performance metrics on her desk by noon, and—" (she shrugs) "—a promise we won't interfere if we take municipal support."

Your stomach folds in a way that used to mean danger. Lina's call is a political advance, silk-threaded with conditions. You can imagine her nails on a tablet screen, ice-gray eyes appraising. You can imagine the funding lines that reopen because you signed a piece of paper. You can imagine the neighborhoods that would be prioritized—and the ones that might be asked to shift.

Dr. Noor appears at the edge of the group, camera slung over her shoulder like an old friend.

Dr. Noor (quiet, hands pressed together): "Data helps. Strategy helps. Trust holds the fragile center."

You walk to Dr. Noor, the small, precise scientist who once asked you whether saving numbers satisfied you if they didn't save people.

Ari Navarro: "Lina's office called too. They're asking for metrics and a non-interference guarantee if we cooperate."

Dr. Noor studies you, her face a soft map of concern.

Dr. Noor: "Promises like that buy silence. They buy time—sometimes the time we need. They can also buy us into corridors where decisions are made without us. Which problem are you trying to solve today?"

The question is blunt and necessary. You could feel the aroma of peat drying on your coat collar—comforting—and the salt sweat at your hairline—reminder. Your throat tightens with the old, unhealed ache: another failed evacuation. The image flashes—water taking a wrong turn, systems that did not listen to the people who knew the town best.

Sora finds you there, hand finding your forearm—warm, insistent. Their touch is a steadying force and a summons at once.

Sora Watanabe: "We can't wait for polite hearings when the tide keeps rising. Occupy the yard tonight. Put bodies in the way of that equipment. Make the camera lights that Lina loves point at her compromises instead."

You can hear the conviction in their voice like a drumbeat. It pushes you toward motion.

Ari Navarro: "If we occupy, we risk arrests. We risk undermining the pilot's scientific credibility if disruption looks like manipulation."

Sora tightens, not in anger but in urgency.

Sora Watanabe: "And if we don't act? We risk being ignored until it's too late. People need to see that this is about saving homes, not balance sheets."

There is a beat where the two of you stand close enough that your breath mixes—a salt-sweet, peat-smoke breath—and the world feels compressed to the size of the decision you have to make. Around you, volunteers keep working; cameras hover like beetles; Lena knots another reed rope, her grin fierce and unafraid.

Lena (calling over): "Whatever you two decide, make it quick. We need you for the demonstration demo at noon."

You almost laugh at the redundancy—"demonstration demo"—but the laugh is cut short because the stakes are bright and immediate. The council deadline sits like a horizon line on the map of today.

You can feel the emotional current building—hope braided with pressure. Positive, yes, but urgent: victory is not promised; it must be seized, argued for, defended. The town is watching you both to hold the line between credible science and uncompromising action.

Lina's voice returns, through Maya's earlier news and the slender thread of municipal power. You picture her in her precise coat, hands calm on a tablet. She will want numbers. She will want reassurance. She will want you to temper the heat.

Dr. Noor presses a palm to your shoulder, a quick, grounding gesture.

Dr. Noor: "Whatever you choose, make it defensible. Make it honest."

You think about honesty—not the charity of truth as weapon, but truth as anchor. You think about trust—how fragile it is and how quickly it can become a scaffolding for something larger.

Your watch ticks. The barometer ticks up again, a tiny, insistent crescendo against the roar of the strings in your chest. The volunteers tighten a line; someone hands out more gloves. Sora leans in and whispers one more thing—half-strategy, half-promise—that lands like a benediction.

Sora Watanabe (near-whisper): "I don't want this to break what we're starting. I want it to make it unignorable."

You breathe and feel the surge of possibility—this is work and theatre and politics braided into one. The moment is alive and humming. You realize the choice isn't simply tactics; it's about the kind of trust you want to build: the quiet, measured trust of data and process, or the galvanizing, risky trust of bodies in the street.
