You come in on the tail of the night you left — papers folded into your coat, the Moleskine warm against your ribs. The plaza smells of cheap coffee and wet wool; a salt dampness clings to every surface like a memory. Camera lights throw hard rectangles across the boardwalk. Banners flap with a tired insistence. Everything is practical and slightly frayed, even the spectacle.

Lina stands near the dais like a cut of shadow in a storm-gray trench coat, tablet-case clutched to her hip. Her profile is clean, practiced; she doesn't seem to breathe the way the rest of the crowd does, as if the air is a variable she can set and monitor. When she turns, her face is a neutral dial—professional, persuasive, contained.

Sora wedges themselves between two volunteers, paint still flecking their cargo pants. They catch your eye and grin, a quick, raw dash of color in the neutral field. The pendant at their throat swings; the shell and glass wink like a private lighthouse. They approach with the urgency of someone rehearsing a speech out loud in the shower—bright, frictioned with undercurrent.

Sora: "Ari—" (they use your name like a small plea) "we need you to speak. People listen when you explain the numbers without theatrics."

You: "I—" You feel for steady sound in your own voice. It comes out measured. "I can speak. But I want it to be clear—about risk and about care. Not just data."

Sora: "Good." (A beat; their voice softens) "Don't let them flatten the losses into a spreadsheet. Say the names. Say the neighborhoods that can't be rebuilt in place."

Their eyes are wide, earnest; there is no presumption in them, only a blunt plea. You keep your face even. Inside, guilt is a slow tide, a memory of a street you can still map by the smell of the bakery that used to be there.

Dr. Noor taps at a probability curve with careful fingers. The models tilt and refuse tidy conclusions. Where Lina offers a line of equations dressed as certainty, the graphs Dr. Noor has pinned show bands of failure—ranges where storms compound, where levees falter, where social vulnerabilities turn projections into human costs.

Dr. Noor: "These aren't contradictions, exactly. They're contingencies. We can plan for likely outcomes, and we must plan for the long tails."

Lina: "Contingency planning has a cost." (She doesn't sound unkind; it reads as leverage.) "And the council will ask about budgets and deliverables. We can't build policy on marginal scenarios the size of whispers."

You listen, the technical language settling into a shape you can hold. You imagine mapping evacuation corridors the way you used to draw tide lines—precise, patient strokes. The necessity of it feels immutable. Yet each practical line draws a scar across someone's memory of home.

Maya sidles up, wearing her fisher's coat like armor. Her knuckles are calloused and immediate.

Maya: (whisper) "They're saying Ephraim's been buying council votes. Old money with newer promises. Don't let him near the east berth."

You: "Have you—" You stop. You don't know how much is rumor and how much is a thread of truth pulled tight. You say the safe thing. "I'll be careful."

Maya's mouth flattens. Her eyes say she expects you to be more than careful.

[Scene: Council Chambers | Noon]
[Visual: Warm wooden panels, a bank of overhead lights that flatten faces; windows show the dull sea beyond]
[Sound: Murmured cross-talk, the scrape of a chair; the faint, metallic smell of rain on tarpaulin]
[Music: Low cello; a chord that settles but refuses resolution]

The chamber's air is heavy with the smell of coffee, wet coats, and a municipal building that has absorbed too many arguments. People file in—volunteers with flags, council aides with nervous tics, reporters trading quiet rumors like contraband. Ephraim's slick suit looks oddly foreign here; he moves through the crowd as if the salt might erode his edges. When he passes, there's a quick, controlled smile.

Ephraim: "Good morning, Ms. Navarro." (Polished; a net cast for something)

You: "Ephraim." You keep your greeting practical. You don't want to feed whatever machine he runs.

He eyes the Moleskine briefly—curiosity thin as an invitation. You close it a little tighter.

Dr. Noor corners you near the corkboard and points at a band on the graph where uncertainty blooms wider than the rest.

Dr. Noor: "There are thresholds here. If the council locks into a single, infrastructure-only plan without community oversight, the model shows increased displacement within twenty years. Not to be dramatic—just the data."

You: (Quietly) "This is what I've feared—the 'efficiency' that forgets place."

Dr. Noor: (A measured, tired smile) "You understand the math and the memory. Use that."

Across the room, Lina steps up to a podium and speaks to a camera with impeccable cadence. Her words are rehearsed but not hollow—she's built a public face that sounds like assurance when people want it.

Lina: "We propose a phased approach that prioritizes critical infrastructure, desalination capacity, and managed retrofit. Funding will follow measurable milestones."

Sora, from the corner, lifts their voice before the applause dies.

Sora: "Measured milestones won't buy us back the marigold alley that's already been underwater twice. People need refuge now—not distant promises."

The room tightens. Lina's jaw flexes, not aggressively, but with the economy of a trained negotiator.

Lina: "No one is suggesting otherwise. But we must avoid the chaos of uncoordinated action. There are standards, permits—"

Sora: "Sometimes standards are cover for inaction."

Their exchange sounds like two tectonic plates gliding, neither wanting fault lines but each scraping inevitable friction.

You step closer. The council expects a technical voice that can translate fear into feasibly funded plans. The community expects a voice that will say, plainly, that their streets are not expendable. Your throat narrows in an ache you have practiced hiding.

You: "If I may—" You clear your throat, the sound small. "We can build phased infrastructure, but it must be designed with the living shoreline pilots and binding community oversight so that relocation isn't the only option."

Lina: (Tilt of the head) "Binding oversight—how would you legally enforce that? And who pays for the pilots? The budget isn't infinite."

You: "We leverage public funds with conditional grants and tie maintenance to community-led cooperatives. The pilots reduce long-term costs if they are implemented in tandem with levee retrofits. It's not charity—it's risk reduction."

Lina pauses, appraising, then nods almost imperceptibly. It is not agreement; it's the calculus of political feasibility.

Maya pulls you aside, voice low as surf.

You: "We have to show cost-benefit that includes culture." Your fingers find the chipped seagull pin and trace its edge. It is a talisman of a place that was real and maps your failure as much as your duty.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: The boardwalk mirrors the city in puddles; neon work lights are small constellations in shallow water]
[Sound: Gulls calling in slow spirals; the sea's breath against pilings]
[Music: Single, sustained strings—building, but quiet]

Outside, the boardwalk looks like a map of what you're arguing for: reclaimed wetlands here, flood-scarred storefronts there. Volunteers hang hand-painted signs; someone strings fairy lights that look comically hopeful against the slate sky.

Sora finds you again, close enough that their shoulder brushes yours. There's a complex look on their face—part worry, part raw faith. You can't tell if what you share is professional solidarity or something with a softer weight; their expression is, admirably, unreadable in the ways that matter.

Sora: "Say you'll say the names. Say our losses. If you can make that technical and also tender, people will listen without panicking."

You: "I want to be precise. Panic comes from ambiguity. Clarity saves people."

Sora: (Exhales) "All right. Will you say—" They stop, seeking the right balance of phrase, "—that managed retreat isn't the only answer? That living shorelines and co-ops can hold homes? Please."

You spend a minute sketching fragments in the Moleskine: phrases you can't quite speak aloud yet. 'Bind maintenance to community cooperatives.' 'Pilot living shoreline, scale with evidence.' 'Evacuation corridors with housing guarantees.' Your hand moves with the mechanical certainty of someone drafting both a risk map and a prayer.

Dr. Noor comes up behind you and taps the margins of your notes with a knowing look.

Dr. Noor: "Keep it anchored. Names, places, thresholds. Give them the map to hold onto when the numbers feel abstract."

You: "I will."

Sora: (Soft) "We're counting on you."

Your heart beats like a tide that knows the rhythm but not the shore it will find. The motion feels mechanical: measuring tide heights, mapping evacuation corridors, balancing cost and culture. Each calculation is a small, private grief. Each proposal is a promise you might not be able to keep.

You close the Moleskine for a second and try to breathe. The press of bodies, the metallic taste of salt and coffee, the faint scent of printer toner and old wood—these are the textures of the deliberation. You feel the familiar weight settle into your shoulders: the knowledge that policy choices have bodies attached.

The council clerk clears their throat. The room focuses. The vote will be called. You can hear the slight intake of breath across the chamber. Somewhere beyond the windows, the sea turns slow nonsense beneath a cloud.

You stand there—papers folded, words lined up—knowing this is one small hinge in the town's larger arc. The urgency has been a low simmer all morning, rising not to alarm but to an intolerable, patient pressure. It is enough to make you steady and anxious at once.

A council aide approaches with a tablet; the agenda scrolls, names and bullets arranged like a machine. Your name is on the list as a potential speaker. The space in front of the dais looks suddenly narrower, the lights a little brighter, as if attention is a physical force.

You take one last breath and step forward, drawing the air into a shape that will become a sentence—or not.
