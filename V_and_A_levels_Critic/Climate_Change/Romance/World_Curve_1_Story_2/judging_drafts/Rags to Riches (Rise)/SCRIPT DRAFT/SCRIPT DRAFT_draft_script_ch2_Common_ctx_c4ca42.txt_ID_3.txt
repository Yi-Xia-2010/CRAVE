[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wide shot of the plaza, wooden panels gleaming under camera lights; protest banners snap in a damp breeze]
[Music: Low, mournful piano with distant electronic hum]
[Sound: Camera shutters; muffled reporter questions; the sea, steady and patient]

You step from the damp boardwalk into the plaza and carry the night with you like a wet coat. The lamps throw hard circles of yellow; they make the polished wood look almost cathedral-clean, which is an ugly sort of mercy. Salt flakes the seams of umbrellas. The air smells of coffee and rain-warmed asphalt, threaded with sea-brine and the faint, metallic tang of equipment cases being unlatched.

Lina stands where the municipal steps meet the plaza: trench coat buttoned tight, tablet case at her hip, silhouette cut so exact it could have been drafted. Camera lights halo her, flattening the fine animation on her face down to a precise, public geometry. She looks, to a fault, fully in control.

You remain at the back, fingers finding the chipped seagull pin at your lapel by habit. It is a tiny cold talisman against a city of decisions. You trace the nick in its enamel the way you trace tide lines—slow, cataloguing, an attempt at steadiness.

[Visual: Close on corkboard inside the council foyer; Dr. Noor’s charts are pinned with neat, severe angles]
[Sound: Low conversation hum inside the Council Chambers]

Dr. Noor’s models are pinned in the foyer like a confession. The graphs arc and dip in neat ink—then become jagged and human when she overlays uncertainty bands. The tidy lines Lina favors sit beside probabilistic failures and scenario brackets that widen into ragged possibility. You look at them until the edges blur.

Sora arrives like a small, bright complaint against the gray. Paint spatters across their cargo pants look like constellations if you let them. Their shell-and-glass pendant catches the light when they tilt their head; their eyes are wide, honest and raw—an argument in themselves.

Sora finds you before you find your voice. They plant themselves too close, breath sharp with adrenaline and the mineral tang of pressed citrus from the rally station. "Ari," they say quietly—no show, just the word urgent and soft at once—"will you speak?"

You could answer with numbers: evacuation corridors, expected surge heights, probabilistic casualty curves. You could answer with the small, exact things that keep people alive. Instead you find your mouth arranging the shape of it: "If I speak, I will say what we need to do, and why. Not slogans."

Sora blinks, the laugh at the edge of their mouth compressed into relief. "Good," they whisper. "No empty theatrics. We need someone who talks like a map people can follow."

Sora’s hand returns to the satchel at their side. They press a crumpled flyer into your palm—your name smudged in someone else’s hurried handwriting. "They're asking for speakers who know the retrofit plan," they say. "You know the retrofit plan."

"Yes." You say it once, but the word holds more than agreement. It is a ledger entry, a responsibility. Dr. Noor drifts closer, the warmth of her coat a soft armor. "Ari," she murmurs, reading you like a seismograph, "say the risk. Tell them the assumptions. Tell them where the model can't tell us."

You look at her, and it is both permission and accusation. You want to be precise. You want to make every number carry the weight of the people behind it.

Maya materializes at your other shoulder, all elbows and small, impatient energy. She smells of fish oil and tea. "They're saying Ephraim bought the second-row seats," she whispers, the tone almost a private joke that has teeth. "Rumor says his donors expect a different kind of 'resilience.'"

"Ephraim?" The name tastes like copper. You watch the slick suit across the room—impossibly wrong for salt air, perfect for televised reassurance. He speaks softly with a group of older council members; his hands measure invisible property lines between his palms.

You think of the houses that used to sit where his plush development plans now glimmer on glossy pamphlets. The memory is a low ache that refuses to be tidy. You close your Moleskine for a second and press your thumb into the spine.

[Visual: Interior, Council Chambers; rows of wooden panels glowing under camera lights]
[Sound: Murmurs of the crowd, the scrape of chairs; a distant gull]

Inside the chamber, the lighting is unforgiving—bright enough to make small faces look tired and important faces look practiced. Wooden panels duplicate themselves into a stepped auditorium. News vans cluster outside like barnacle-encrusted stones; their cables run like veins to the building.

Lina steps forward to speak to the camera with a voice made for governance: measured cadence, policy-first phrasing. She mentions public-private partnerships; she mentions 'scalable automation' and 'efficient allocation of resources.' People outside clap because the sentence is easy to repeat.

You watch her deliver tidy promises and feel the cold lift in your chest that comes with knowing precisely the scale of what those promises will cut out—streets, histories, people who cannot afford to be compacted into efficiency. The negative weight in your gut tightens into an argument you cannot keep silent about.

Sora approaches again, quieter this time. "They'll listen to you," they say. "They listen when you talk about corridors and thresholds. They believe data—it's our foot in the door."

"You want me to be the foot?" You say, voice low. The mechanics of the request make your chest ache. It is procedural, but every procedure carries lives.

"I want you," Sora insists, "to give them a map that doesn’t leave anyone behind."

Dr. Noor nods slowly. "Be honest. People will blame you for what you say—yes. But if you can make the human costs unavoidable in people's minds, their votes change. Facts framed as obligations."

Ephraim’s laugh—precise, practiced—drifts over like a surface swell. Lina smiles politely and turns back to her tablet. The cameras feast on the exchange.

Your heart is a metronome: steady, not loud. The arousal feels low—an internal pressure that keeps you moving without making you frantic. Each breath is a small, deliberate effort in rhythm with the room.

You rehearse in small ways—a breath, a phrase, the placement of your hands on the podium if you have to mount it. Each rehearsal carves away a bit of the distance between the abstract of policy and the concrete of a wet street where a child once played.

People file into the gallery. You can count the faces you know and the faces you don't. The atmosphere is low-intensity anxiety, the kind that hums rather than erupts. It is a negative charge you can wear like wool: heavy, insulating, unavoidable.

Sora catches your eye and offers a little, rueful grin. It's a plea without melodrama. "Promise me one thing," they say, and you feel a small thing unspool in your chest—hope wrapped in instruction.

"What's that?" you ask.

"That you won't let them speaking statistics be the end of it," Sora answers. "Make them see that the numbers are people."

You want to say yes in a way that isn't a vow you cannot keep. You want to say yes because your whole plan is built on that premise—policy with faces. But promises from you have weight; promises from you mean additional nights of work, additional hearings, additional confrontations.

Dr. Noor hovers, voice a soft instrument. "If you need to pivot mid-speech—if someone says something false—know your cornerstones. Evacuation windows, retrofit priorities, community ownership. Anchor in those and you can steer them back."

Ephraim moves then, cutting across an aisle with a smooth, practiced gait. He nods in your direction—smug, rehearsed civility. "Ari," he says, and his voice is inoffensive enough to make you suspicious. "Your models are helpful. I hope the council considers all options."

You watch him go. The suit smells faintly of something chemical—new leather, maybe, or the citrus of elite hand-sanitizer. You hear Maya mutter that he is “buying the optics” and you can see how easy the town is to sell when the lighting is right and the words are smoother than the water.

You close the Moleskine and breathe, slow and deliberate, tasting salt and coffee and the iron hint of decision. The meeting will begin. The room tightens, attention folding in like a tide.

You feel the familiar weight settle in your shoulders: responsibility, memory, and that same old guilt that tightens whenever policy and bodies intersect. It is a negative weight—heavy, unavoidable, necessary.

The council clerk clears her throat. Microphones lean in like curious mouths. Lina steps forward to the official lectern to open the session, her voice threaded through the room like a taut wire. Cameras tilt; the light finds her.

You taste the moment as if it were something alive under your tongue: the high, cold smell of the sea; the warmth of Sora's hand on your elbow; the quiet insistence of Dr. Noor's eyes. Everything is measured. Everything is pressing.

You fold your hands, close your mouth, and prepare to turn the page.

[Visual: Shot of the boardwalk outside through chamber windows—neon lights reflected in pooling water, gulls wheeling slow and patient]
[Sound: A single gull calls; the echo of distant chanting rises, held back by glass]

You tighten your scarf and step forward, the motion small and inexorable—a low arousal climb that opens into the quiet before you are called.

The clerk's voice formalizes the beginning of the vote session. Cameras find faces. The air tastes of expectation, strong and slightly metallic.

A final, private image lodges in your chest: the corner of your childhood street, remembered as if through a film of rain. You hold it like a proof.

You are on the brink of speaking, but you have not yet opened your mouth.

Each will be judged tonight.
