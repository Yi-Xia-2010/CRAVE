You arrive with the weight of the decision still lodged like a pebble in your throat. From Chapter Three you chose to pull the curtain back — to point at Ephraim's money and the strings tied to it. That choice folded into this afternoon: flyers, testimonies, and a slim stack of copies you could not unsee without feeling like you'd left a wound untended.

Your scarf is damp where salt has settled into the knit. You feel the roughness of the boardwalk under your boots, the chill of wind off the sea threading into your collar. Around you, paint-splattered jackets and sun-weathered faces watch with that strange, hungry patience crowds have become after years of near-misses.

Sora stands to your left, shoulder pressed close enough that you can feel the heat of them without looking. Their presence is a punctuation mark: bold, immediate, a living argument for action. They squeeze your hand once — a quick, grounding rhythm that says we'll go together.

Sora Watanabe: (low, urgent) "You ready? Say it straight. They need to hear the money trail."

You: "We lay out the facts, no flourishes. The town can read the record and judge." (You tap the sheaf of documents at your hip — preferential contracts, redacted invoices, an annotated summary you stayed up all night preparing.)

You lift the papers. Cameras angle; a reporter pushes forward, voice poised for the soundbite.

Reporter: "Ari Navarro — can you summarize the basis of these allegations?"

You inhale the salt-tangled air and answer as if speaking to a room that has to be taught to hear slowly.

You: "These documents show direct preferential contracting between Ephraim Consolidated and municipal advisors tied to proposed harbor development. Timelines and clauses indicate expedited procurement and exemptions that bypassed standard public oversight." (You let the words land. They are clinical — precise, which is how you mean them to be.)

Sora steps forward then, handing a folded stack of printed testimonies to a camera person. Their cadence is fierce but not theatrical.

Sora Watanabe: "We have fishermen, dockhands, and small-business owners who testified that promises were made — promises that never included community oversight. This isn't rumor. It's a pattern."

A cluster of volunteers — Maya among them — chants support in the back. Maya's voice carries the particular rasp of someone who spends days knee-deep in mud and logistics.

Maya Cruz: (shouting over the crowd) "We grew those seedlings. We know what the co-op's contracts look like. This is ours, not theirs!"

Sora Watanabe: (soft, fierce) "We did the right thing. They needed to know. You did right."

You: "Right doesn't remove what happens next. It only makes the consequences visible."

Sora: (short laugh) "We knew that when we came. Come on — we'll face it together."

Their tone carries both certainty and the vulnerability that keeps it humane. There's an intimacy in the shared risk that isn't theatrical; it's the quiet of two people standing on a pier while the tide tests the pilings.

[Scene: Old Harbor District | Early Evening]
[Visual: Half-submerged storefronts and faded murals; volunteers moving along raised planks with flashlights]
[Sound: Sputter of emergency lamps, shoes on wet wood, low conversation]
[Music: A subdued riff — steady, focused]

You walk the hollowed alleys with Sora and a small contingent of residents. Testimonies arrive in clotted, earnest bursts: a café owner who lost inventory to an unannounced tide, a retired dockworker who points to a concrete crack and says it widened after a 'consultant's' shorepinning last year. Sora records each voice, pressing for detail, coaxing memory into usable statements.

Resident (café owner): "They promised elevated boardwalks, but the contracts we saw had clauses that let developers choose materials. We were told to trust them."

You: "Did anyone send that promise in writing?"

Resident: "Only in emails. The drafts came from the developer's counsel."

Sora: (gently, firm) "We'll collect those emails. We'll make them part of the record."

Maya appears, muddy boots and grin, hauling a crate of plant bundles for a temporary wetland patch. Even here, logistics hum: volunteers assigning boats, inventory lists checked, the co-op's network humming.

Maya Cruz: (practical) "If this forces a legal pause, we have to keep the desal parts moving. Supply lines for filters are fragile. We pack backups today."

You: (low) "We can't let the good we do be choked by panic. Maya — keep separate manifests for emergency parts. If the injunction hits, we need them traceable."

Maya nods without dramatics. Her competence is a mild kind of salvation.

You: "I wanted a change that didn't leave people stranded while lawyers argued." (You feel the equation of justice versus practicality rearrange under your palms.)

Sora: (half-smile) "That's always the work, Ari. The messy middle. We knew it would get loud."

You hold the half-smile like a buoy.

[Scene: Municipal Offices — Back Corridor | Night]
[Visual: A narrow corridor behind the council chamber, fluorescent lights humming; the public plaza's noise muffled through glass]
[Sound: The echo of distant camera flashes; a door closes with authority]

Lina finds you in the corridor after the press ends. The cameras framed her institutionally; here her shoulders loosen in a way that the televised image would not permit.

Lina Moreau: (measured, softer) "Ari. Briefly — in private. There are legal teams asking for emergency measures, and I'm trying to thread procedural safety with the public outcry. Can you walk me through the documents?"

You: (guarded) "You distanced yourself publicly."

Lina: (a small, almost rueful smile) "Public duty has its theater. Private conversations are where we avoid theater. There's a difference."

Her face is complex — unreadable in the way someone trained to weigh probabilities becomes when watching lives in the balance. You can't tell whether her private interest is an attempt to help, to co-opt, or to mitigate the municipal risk. You file that uncertainty into your assessment like any other variable.

Lina Moreau: "If the injunction succeeds and developers' funding freezes, we could see contractors walk away from partially completed defenses. That creates gaps in coverage. I need your aid to make sure any pause doesn't become abandonment."

You: "I exposed the ties because secrecy makes danger easier for the powerful. But I'm not interested in causing a vacuum."

Lina: (leans in slightly) "Then help me craft a transparent hold — one that halts suspect transfers but preserves emergency procurement channels. Give me the technical language I can use before counsel twists it."

The offer smells like compromise. It is simultaneously an olive branch and a testing probe.

You: "I can draft terms with Noor and Maya that specify emergency continuations for life-sustaining materials, backed by community oversight. But I will insist on public disclosure of any back-channel procurement."

Lina: (nods once) "Agreed. After this, come to my office. Off the record.”

Her hand taps the municipal ID on her lapel — procedural affirmation. She turns then, the corridor swallowing her silhouette as she steps back into procedure.

[Sound: A distant siren. The crowd outside continues to hum, restless and expectant]
[Music: The music thins into a single steady pulse — tension without collapse]

You feel the air of the evening shift: the exposure has unmoored something into motion. Legal teams mobilize, injunctions ripple across accounts, supply chains threaten to hiccup, and public momentum leans toward oversight. For a sliver of time — when Sora holds your hand and the volunteers tighten their lines — the fight feels aligned and effective. There is warmth in that alignment, uncomplicated and immediate.

Then reality folds in the other direction: private security, lawyers with subpoenas, and a municipal apparatus that must appear impartial or face ruin. Press feeds cycle the images of your podium; threats appear in your inbox. The fight you started has all the right ferocity — and all the unfamiliar hazards.

Dr. Noor's voice returns to you when you check your watch.

Dr. Noor: (low, edged with professional worry) "An injunction can buy time for review, but if suppliers stall payments, pump replacements and filter shipments could be delayed. Two weeks' delay on a critical part means increased failure risk in the next storm cycle."

You swallow. Two weeks is both a breath and a countdown.

Sora squeezes your hand again, closer now. The heat from their palm steadies you.

Sora Watanabe: (soft) "We'll keep moving. We'll organize backups. We knew this would bring teeth."

You: "I don't want to be the cause of someone going without because we chose exposure over an easier silence."

Sora: (firm) "No one asked you to choose silence. You chose to try and make things accountable. We'll work the parts. You always do the planning."

You glance at Sora, at Maya, at the faces in the crowd — salt-streaked, paint-splattered, determined. For a brief, shining moment you are aligned: raw, righteous, intimate in a way that is almost sacred.

Then the first legal notices begin to stack up in your inbox and the private messages with thinly veiled threats arrive. You know the next days will ask for hard, nimble decisions: how to keep essential supplies moving without violating court orders; how to shield volunteers from private security; how to make transparency operational while preventing collapse.

Everything you set in motion is now moving fast. The town will either get steadied by the scrutiny you brought, or stumble under the friction of sudden withdrawal. You sense the arc bending toward escalation.
