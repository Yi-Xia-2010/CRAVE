Council Member Reyes: "We will now take the vote on the package as amended."

Ari Navarro: "I'm not choosing erasure. I design systems that keep people alive. We'll bind oversight—legal audits, community board seats, relocation as care not displacement. It's not perfect. It isn't supposed to be. It's accountable."

Sora's jaw works. They pull air through teeth.

Sora Watanabe: "Accountability has been a promise in this town for a hundred years. People have listened until it ends in platitudes. Will you—for us—anchor it in law?"

Lina (interjecting, quietly): "It will be in the ordinance. I will sign it. We'll build the oversight board with community nominees. We can put penalties for noncompliance in the funding clause."

Sora stares at Lina. It's a charged moment: breath charged with everything that led here—years of missed funding, Noor's whispered corrections, Ephraim's pressure, the sea's impatient tongue. Sora's expression fractures into something like betrayal and then something resembling weary calculation.

The final tally lands. Hands raise. Voices count. The motion passes: the levee, the desal center, conditional oversight, and the list of designated relocation blocks. The room surges—applause and outrage braided together. You feel your limbs vibrate with the force of it.

You move through the Old Harbor where the sea has already taken its small claims. Maya meets you with a clipboard and gloves, her face a captain's—resolute, tired, necessary.

Maya Cruz: "Moving crews ready. We'll catalog each shop, each memento. We won't leave the history for the developers to patent, Ari."

Maya's hands are constant, busy. She organizes volunteers into teams that will help families pack, label, and move—careful with photographs, with pots, with the stubborn boxes that contain the cumulative biography of a place. Her presence is practical grief.

You walk the evacuated alleys and remember your sibling's laugh in an old doorway. The salt-slick steps echo with small ghosts. You touch the flaking paint of a storefront that used to sell netting and coffee—its sign half-swallowed by sea-swell. Mourning is a physical thing here; it sits in your shoulders and hums in your bones. But threaded through that grief is a fierce satisfaction—children will have evacuated routes that work; the school will not be cut off by storm surge; the desal plant will keep pumps alive even when the grid hiccups.

Lina finds you where the light catches on an algae-brightened puddle. She approaches with an umbrella—precise, sheltering—and when the umbrella tilts, her expression is less of the politician and more of someone who keeps precise, small kindnesses in a pocket.

Lina Moreau: "You make hard bargains, Ari. I prefer to say 'necessary ones.'"

You let a laugh escape—short, relieved.

Ari Navarro: "They both sound the same in the middle of the night."

Lina: "Perhaps. But I wanted you to know: the oversight board will have binding vetoes for funding misuse. We'll publish audit results quarterly. I'm not handing the future to developers without eyes."

Her words are formally stated, but there is a tremor under her precision—a vulnerability folded into policy. She says it like she's offering you more than a political concession: she is offering an alliance.

Lina Moreau: "I would like you on the oversight board, officially. I think your voice anchors the work to people."

The invitation lands in your chest like a small, warm stone. It's a partnership—political, yes—but also intimate in how consequential it will be: late nights drafting clauses, the small tyranny of detail, the shared fatigue that builds trust. The idea of working with Lina has always felt like crossing a line from public to private: negotiation and then the quiet nights of drafting, coffee rings on maps, shoulders nearly touching over blueprints.

Ari Navarro: "I'll take it. We will make sure relocations are treated as care: relocation counselors, compensation with communal spaces guaranteed, heritage salvage teams."

Lina nods. For a heartbeat, the two of you watch a small wave feather into a gutter and pass. The town's geometry shifts in your mind—not disappearance, but rearrangement. You feel the high of something completed, the sharp joy of a plan landing where it can do the most good.
