You sign your name on a battered clipboard and roll your sleeves. The fabric of your jacket is damp faster than you'd expect; mud beads in the creases around your wrists and the scent of peat lifts with every breath—earth, salt, a faint tang of algae. Your hands smell of working things into place, which is easier somehow than cataloguing what was lost.

Sora stands at the center of the volunteers like a pivot: one side shaved, the other in a cascade of glossy waves that fall over their shoulder when they lean to knot a coir mat. Their jacket is splattered with paint; they move fast but with an economy that feels rehearsed. When they look up, the garden seems to recalibrate toward them.

Sora: "Okay—teams A through D. Ari, you take wave-overtop mapping with Maya's group. Show them the transect routine, keep it simple. We'll crowdsource the first dataset; people with phones upload time-stamped photos. Noor will overlay with her models later."

You nod and feel the familiar shape beneath your ribs—a method you can place trust in. You pull your Moleskine from the inner pocket and tuck a pencil behind your ear, then crouch beside a low boardwalk where children have left a trail of small, muddy footsteps.

The footprints prick the place behind your sternum you have learned not to name out loud. For a second you are both here and somewhere else: a smaller street you used to run down, a flash of water too fast. Then a volunteer tugs your sleeve and you turn.

Volunteer (young man): "Ari—sorry, quick—what's the threshold for an overtopping event? Like, how do we log it without freaking people out?"

Your mouth tastes of salt and peat; you find the technical words, sand them down.

You: "If the water crosses the berm and reaches the boardwalk, that's overtopping. Mark the location, take a photo from the same angle every time, note wind and approximate wave height. Keep notes short—time, place, what moved. We'll stitch it together."

Volunteer: "Right—short notes. Got it."

The channels take shape under your hands—trenches of dark water that will slow and redirect winter surges, patches of cordgrass planted in thick clumps so roots can bind the new soil. You bend over a sapling, fingers cramped around fragile stems, and talk without really planning it.

You: "Plant deep. Pinch the crown, press soil around the roots—no air gaps. If we get the spacing right, the stems will break wave energy and trap sediment."

Sora comes up from tying a coir log, breathless with three words and a grin half-hidden by paint.

Sora: "Crowdsourcing the data—people upload, we verify. You'll love it; the town becomes the sensor."

You raise an eyebrow; your watch, retrofitted with a barometric sensor, hums and gives a small vibration on your wrist. You glance down and the pressure reading ticks upward. A soft, nervous laugh escapes from both of you—half relief, half the sound of people aware things are happening on schedule.

You: "We need baselines. If everyone uploads an image with a timestamp, we can spot anomalies faster than models alone."

Sora: "And we get everyone to feel like they're doing something, not just reading about it. That's how change stays."

A shadow falls across the planting beds. Dr. Noor arrives with a wet-creased clipboard and a face that is, as usual, careful and a little concerned. They hand you a sheaf of printouts—contagion-maps revised to reflect the new channel patterns and recent storm surges.

Dr. Noor: "Adjustments to the exposure lines. Where you place these channels changes probable flooding corridors—some areas become safer, others marginally more exposed. It's not bad news; it means we can be surgical with placement. But it's also a reminder—adaptive measures shift risk, they don't erase it."

You: "Surgical is fine. Transparent is better. We'll map and share—no surprises."

Their eyes flick to the kid's footprints and then to you. For a moment their expression is something you read as complex—memory, calculation, a sympathy they don't lay out like a blanket but offer in measurements instead.

Maya comes up with a chipped enamel mug and a tin teapot wrapped in a tea towel, steam curling toward the low clouds.

Maya: "Tea. For the planner with mud on their cuffs." (she grins, earnest)

You reach for the mug.

Volunteers watch you when you stand and speak; the field jacket you habitually wear—scuffed, patched, salt-creased—seems to give you a kind of quiet authority. They ask practical questions about measuring slope and the difference between setback and managed retreat. You answer, because you're good at that—the precise angle, the cadence of instructions, the things that make plans live.

Volunteer (older woman): "You used to map this with a different team, didn't you? We—well, we heard."

You pause. How much of history do you give them? How much do you hold to yourself?

You: "This town taught me how coasts give and where we can move to keep what matters. I learned a lot messily. We patch that learning into these designs."

Sora sidles close while you sketch a simple transect in the mud with a stick. Their paint-streaked jacket brushes yours, not on purpose but in that small way that makes something else possible. They look at the diagram and then at you, eyes deep and searching—not needy, but intent on reading whether your words match how you mean them.

Sora: (soft) "You explain this like someone who has seen both sides—data and people. Is that how you feel, Ari?"

Your answer hovers. You remember the sibling under a different sky, the emergency sirens, the ache that made you study and return. Words taste like rationed truth.

You: "I try. It's why I'm here, Sora. To keep the plans tied to people—so a map isn't just a lifeline on paper."

Sora lets out a breath; volunteers drift toward other tasks. The work folds into a rhythm: dig, plant, tamp, measure, record. The music of bodies under a coordinated, human tempo. The mid-afternoon light steels itself; fog thins to reveal a slice of horizon the color of oyster shells. You flip through the notes in your Moleskine, aligning the seeds' spacing with tide tables and the barometric blips your watch keeps recording at the edge of your sleeve.

Dr. Noor: "If we overlay the crowd data this week, I can run a sensitivity scan by morning. It will show us where to prioritize community drills."

You: "Good. Prioritization beats panic."

They nod, but the crease at the corner of their mouth hints at calculations still being done. Maya returns with a tray of biscuits and the sound of children playing somewhere in the reeds—a thin, bright noise that tugs at you.

By the time you string the last lanterns, the air has cooled; paper shades cast soft pools of light on the boardwalk and the greenhouse glows amber through its windows. The volunteers move with the ease of people who have earned something together—tired, satisfied, practical in their camaraderie.

Sora lingers beside you as the final knot is tied on a lantern line. Their pendant—polished shell and reclaimed glass—catches the amber light and throws a sliver of color across their collarbone. For a moment there is only the sound of cranes somewhere offshore and the muffled laughter of a kid being scolded by an older hand.

Sora: (quiet) "Stay for the tide watch tonight? We can go up to the rooftop greenhouse—bring your watch. I want you to see the data live."

You feel the day's steady arousal settle into a point centered between curiosity and the habitual caution you've learned to keep. This is not a dramatic ask; it's an extension of the work into the watching. You notice the way they wait for your answer, not demanding, just wanting you there.

You: "Okay."

A long exhale you didn't realize you were holding slips out. The garden breathes in and out with all the small human arrangements you have stitched into it today—plants, lanterns, lists, promises.

You tuck your Moleskine into your jacket, the mud leaves a ring at the edge of the cover. You and Sora walk toward the greenhouse, shoulders close enough for occasional contact, each step setting the next little creak in the boardwalk. The town behind you hums low as it does—preparing, waiting, steady.

[Visual: The greenhouse amber grows larger as you approach; the boardwalk recedes into a corridor of light and shadow]
[Music: The chord sustains—comfortable, mid-tempo; it does not resolve but leans forward]

Page-turn thought: There's a small, steady satisfaction in building with hands—soil under nails, shared labor, a ledger of small successes. You recognize too that these small things must be kept in conversation with the models and the funding and the politics that gather beyond the lights. Tonight, you will watch tides with someone who insists the town can be both measured and held. The tide will come. The question of how you'll answer next will not be solved by lantern light alone.
