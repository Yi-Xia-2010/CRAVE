[Scene: Mariselle Bus Stop | Late Afternoon — Fog Thickening]
[Visual: A battered bus sighs open; salt streaks along its windows]
[Sound: The low hiss of brakes, distant gulls, muffled chant of a crowd]
[Music: Sparse, low-register piano — slow, reverb-heavy]

You step down from the bus with your bag heavier than it should be. The strap digs at a bruise on your shoulder you don't remember getting; the Moleskine tucked under your arm is damp at the edges, pages swollen with tide notes and shorthand you wrote in a rush on a pier that smelled like diesel and wet rope. Your fitness-watch—an old thing you retrofitted with a barometric sensor—beeps once, polite and indifferent.

The boardwalk stretches ahead: a skein of salt and patched planks, protest flyers flapping under the fog like pale flags. Someone has tied a banner to a lamp post—hand-lettered, impatient. The salt in the air tastes like old metal. Your scarf is damp; the knit pressed close to the back of your neck, holding the scent of brine and smoke.

You breathe in and the place reasserts itself the way an old bruise does—familiar, achey, easy to ignore until you touch it.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Moving Toward the Co-op]
[Visual: Close-up of scuffed boots on salt-bleached planks]
[Sound: Shoes splashing on a shallow puddle; paper snapping in the fog]
[Music: A single bowed cello, slow]

Maya is waiting under the crooked awning of the co-op, the hood of her fisher's raincoat pulled low. Her face is freckled and half-hidden; when she sees you she tilts her chin in that way she always did as kids—an invitation without words. You notice the co-op's racks of wetland plugs, their leaves dark with spray. Someone has propped mud-streaked shovels against a wall.

Maya: "About time," she says, but there's a softness to it. She chews the inside of her cheek, like she can't decide whether to let anger have the shape of a hug.
You: "Traffic," you say because it is always easier to say things that are obvious. The word comes out smaller than you feel.

Maya's hand drifts to the strap of your bag. Her fingers are quick to judge—no, that's not it; they're quick to check. She rests them a beat too long on the leather.
Maya: "You look like you slept on the bus," she teases, then drops the teasing like a stone into deeper water. "Did you bring the notes? Dr. Noor said she'd want to see you today."

You pull the Moleskine free. The corners of the cover are softened by salt and use; inside, your handwriting leans into itself—habitual shorthand for tides, velocities, sleep-deprived calculations. The pages rustle in the wet air.

You: "I brought them." Your voice surprises you—level, practiced. The truth is not the feeling: you are hollowed and heavy, the memory of a siren that never reached you lodged behind your ribs.

Maya watches your fingers on the book as if they could read what is written there through your skin.
Maya: "Sora's out at the plaza. They're starting to gather before the council meeting. Lina's doing her rounds, too—Ephraim probably already made the rounds of the donors. The whole thing's tense." She shrugs. "As if it hasn't been for years."

You: "How bad is it?" You don't mean the weather. You mean the vote. You mean the choice that could make the difference between a plan that protects lives and one that feeds private pockets.

Maya looks at the sky, not for weather—her human radar is on people—but the clouds answer her anyway: low, iron-gray, the first storm tightening like a fist beyond the hills.
Maya: "Bad enough." She taps her knuckles against a crate. "But you're here. If anyone can turn tide models into something people can hold on to, it's you."

You want to believe that. You want to fold your hands around that compliment and hold it like a buoy. Instead, your fingers find the chipped enamel seagull pin at the inside of your jacket—something you clasp absentmindedly, the metal cold and familiar.

[Scene: Passing the Tidewatch Institute | Rooftop Glowing Amber]
[Visual: The Institute's corrugated cladding blurred by fog; a warm amber light from the rooftop greenhouse leaks into the gray]
[Sound: A distant hum of filtration systems, muffled voices behind glass]
[Music: A single sustained synth note; distant, almost like a breath]

You and Maya walk past the Tidewatch Institute. Its rooftop greenhouse glows like a held ember in the cool fog; you can almost see shapes moving behind the glass—hands gathering seedlings, someone making notes under amber lights. The building smells faintly of wet soil and herbs, a human warmth you had forgotten you craved.

You: "Noor's there?" you ask, though you think you already know the answer.

Maya: "She stepped out earlier," Maya says. "Said she had models for you. Went up to the dunes after that." Her mouth, always honest, tightens. "She didn't look at the scoreboard when I asked. She only asked if you'd be coming by."

The word 'models' sits in your throat like a stone. Models are dry equations, maps, margins—things that can be folded and passed across a desk. But models are also numbers that, in their coldness, decide where people move and which neighborhoods fade into salt.

You: "Show me when you're ready."

Maya gives you a look that's equal parts solidarity and warning.
Maya: "Ready? You look like a ghost." She steps closer, voice down, the co-op's creak muffling the street noise. "Ari—" (her emphasis on your name is small, intimate, the shape of a thing not yet said) "—promise me you won't let the council make it into a thing where the kids in Flooded Row get bussed away like an inconvenience."

You swallow. The boardwalk seems suddenly narrower.

You: "I won't make promises I can't keep." A lie, perhaps. A caution. The truth is heavier: I will try. I will measure and plan until my hands hurt. I will not let the math be the only language we speak.

[Scene: The Dunes | Dr. Noor Appears — The Air Rises a Degree Colder]
[Visual: A braided crown of silver hair, silhouette against pale sky]
[Sound: The tread of boots on sand; distant murmur of a growing crowd]
[Music: Quiet piano chords, spaced further apart, like measured breaths]

Dr. Noor is waiting where the dunes slope toward the ruins of the old pier. She is tall as ever, braided hair white at the roots, and carries a folded raft of papers that look almost wrong in the fog—crisp, dry edges in the damp. When she sees you, her face un-lifts with a look that is not quite welcome and not quite relief.

Dr. Noor: "You look like you never left," she says. There is warmth, but it is small and carefully measured.

You: "Did I?" The question is rhetorical; you feel the town in your bones. The Moleskine in your hand feels like an apology.

Dr. Noor folds the raft into both of your palms. The pages are cool and faintly chalky with model dust: sediment curves, projected accretion lines, a new set of numbers you did not expect. You fan them open until a gust threatens to steal the edge of a map and have to press them down with a finger.

Dr. Noor: "The math changed," she says, quietly—the sentence carrying a weight that makes the fog sound louder. She doesn't look at you when she says it. "Our baseline pushed. Sea-surge projections are different. The old evacuation window is tighter."

You feel the sentence as if someone has tightened a strap across your chest. The physics of it is simple and brutal: less lead time, higher water in less time. The old failure—the siren that didn't reach your sibling—slides forward in your mind and sits on your tongue.

You: "How much tighter?"

Dr. Noor: (pulls a map closer) "Days, not hours. In some scenarios, hours." She meets your eyes. "I didn't want to alarm you here. But you should see it before the council meeting. People will judge you by what you do next."

The words fall into the space between you, and everything in the dunes seems to lean in. You hunt for air, and the air feels thin in a way that has nothing to do with altitude. Your palms go slick with a cold sweat that the fog won't wash away.

You: "People—" The word fractures. "They already do." You think of a child's hand pulled from yours in the rush of water, of the last evacuation run that turned into a corridor of mistakes. "Why did the numbers change?"

Dr. Noor: "New data from the southern barometer array. Sediment transport, altered by the storms last season. Ephraim's dredging reports—some of it was underestimated. Compounding variables." She pinches the bridge of her nose. "It's messy. And messy is worse for people who are already fragile."

You stare at a contour line that cleaves through a neighborhood you can name without looking. Names make things human; numbers flatten them. Your heartbeat is a slow, stubborn drum. The barometric sensor on your watch ticks once—another quiet, indifferent beat—and the readout nudges again: a fraction lower. The change is small on a device, but large in the hush.

Dr. Noor doesn't rush you. She waits, giving you—by the way she stands—the same thing you have always respected in her: time to come to your own conclusion.

You think of Sora and their crew at the plaza, the chant banners, the urgency in their eyes. You think of Lina's careful, polished smile in council briefings and Ephraim's suit sleeves. You think of the people who will be moved or left according to a matrix of cost-benefit analyses. You think of your sibling only in fragments now: a shoe caught on a step, a laugh that came before a siren. The thought is sharp and clean in the fog.

Dr. Noor: "They'll ask for an answer tonight," she says. "The council—" She stops, as if the rest of the sentence might be heavier than air. "They're voting on the levee-and-housing scheme. Lina has the funding lined up; Ephraim's donors are ready. Sora will try to make the chamber impossible to ignore."

You fold the raft of papers back into yourself like a map of where everything has already gone wrong.

You: "I need time," you say, because it is what you need and because it's what people expect you to say. But the barometer tugs at something tighter. The pressure drop on your watch is a small, steady line that feels like a verdict.

Dr. Noor: (her voice is soft, intimate in a way that takes you by surprise) "People will judge you by what you do next."

You replay the words as if they might change with repetition. Instead, they set into your bones.

The fog breathes; the distant protest sounds like a river you might have to cross. Your hands close on the Moleskine as if it were a life preserver. A quiet resolve settles in: you will try to make a plan that saves more than statistics. You will measure for people, not for paperwork.

The world around you smells of brine, wet wood, and the acrid tang of protest smoke. Your scarf is damp at the throat. In the hollow between breaths, the town waits—fragile and patient and tired.

You look at Dr. Noor. You look at the dunes. You look toward the plaza where voices rise like tide-lines.

You: "Then let's make sure the math isn't all anyone sees." It's not a speech. It's a promise to yourself. A vow to not let numbers be the only language of a town.

Dr. Noor's eyes are unreadable for a second—old grief and renewed urgency pitched in equal measure.
Dr. Noor: "Then don't let them reduce people to risks on a page. You're not alone in this. But you should be ready for how they'll test you."

You close the Moleskine. The pages have absorbed the damp and the salt; the ink is a little runny at the edges. You feel the old pressure fold over your shoulders, heavy and familiar. The storm on the forecast slides closer like a shadow at noon.

[Scene: Dunes Edge — The World Compresses into Sound and Scent]
[Visual: Fog swallowing the boardwalk; a thin strip of gray ocean]
[Sound: The crowd's distant chants weave with the hum of hidden machinery]
[Music: The piano dwindles to single, held notes]

Your hands are steady. Inside, something is hollowing out and rebuilding at the same time—quiet, deliberate. You promise yourself again that this time it will be different: this time you will make a plan that saves more than statistics. The promise is a small, fragile thing in the face of the tides.

The barometer on your wrist ticks with a small, steady, indifferent rhythm. Somewhere down at the plaza a megaphone opens and closes. The day bobs on a hinge.

You turn away from the dunes and head back toward the co-op, toward the boardwalk, toward a council chamber where votes will be cast and futures decided. Your feet find the path as if they remember it by muscle. You do not know the shape this coming fight will take. You only know the weight of what sits in your palms: the Moleskine, Noor's raft of models, the memory of the siren that never sung loud enough.

The fog closes a little. The light from the Tidewatch greenhouse is a distant ember. The sound of Sora's voice carries, then dips; someone laughs, then the laugh runs out.

You inhale brine. You taste smoke. You feel the pressure drop once more, small and inexorable.

You straighten your shoulders. You begin to plan.
