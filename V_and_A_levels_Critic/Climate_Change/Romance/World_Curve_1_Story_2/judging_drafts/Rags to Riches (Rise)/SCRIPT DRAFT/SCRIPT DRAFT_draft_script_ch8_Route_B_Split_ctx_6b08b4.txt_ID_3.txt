You are still sitting at the long bench when the lights in the lab below click out and the institute settles into the small noises of late night: the whir of a refrigeration unit, the soft clack of a distant security gate. Your hands are smudged with soil and ink. Stacks of memos and tide-model printouts form a paper canyon around your elbows. The packet you signed earlier sits under your palm like a promise with weight.

The greenhouse smells of wet earth, salt, and something green and sharp — crushed marsh grass, a hopeful smell you’ve held like a talisman since childhood. The warm light makes the pages look honest; the ink looks like it means what it says.

Your hazel eyes narrow under tired lids as you scan the latest municipal packet: levee alignments, funding contingencies, and a spreadsheet that lights up red where neighborhoods are annotated with relocation schedules. The text is technical and polite; the effect screams.

You breathe through it. You have read this kind of language enough to hear the people under it.

[Visual: Close on your hand tracing a line of text; the seagull pin catches the amber and throws a tiny, honest glint]
[Internal: You remind yourself you took this route to be inside the system where decisions happen, not outside shouting at them.]

Dr. Noor appears at the greenhouse hatch like a figment you were waiting for—braided silver hair tucked into a rain-streaked jacket, field camera slung as if at any moment she might photograph a tide line. She moves with steady purpose and sets down a thermos beside your papers without asking.

Dr. Noor: "You’re still awake. Good. I—do you have a minute?"

You: "Always. Sit."

She perches on the bench opposite you. The two of you keep a small, necessary distance; the bench is long enough for the discipline your grief requires.

Dr. Noor (softly): "I went back over the models tonight… and there’s a caveat I didn’t make clear before the briefing. The calibration for the uptick in storm surge tied to that newest estuarine feedback loop—we used a regional data patch I trusted. It compresses failure modes in neighborhoods A, B, and parts of Old Harbor."

You feel the words as if someone has put a hand on your sternum. The bench is cool beneath your palms.

You: "How much compression? Are we talking margin of error or a structural bias?"

Dr. Noor: "Bias. A conservative underestimation. In plain terms: if we take the levee alignment Lina’s team proposes and do nothing else, those margins widen. The levee protects main corridors, but the hydrology diverts and deepens flooding in lower-lying pockets. The calcs—" (she taps a spreadsheet) "—show that without relocation or extensive wetland engineering, those pockets repeatedly fail."

You close your eyes and count a breath. Your internal voice, the one that catalogs variables like lifelines, lists options: retrofit, staged relocations, additional wetlands, or lose funding. Each line is a lever you know how to pull; each pull has a human face.

Dr. Noor (more quietly): "I’m telling you because I trust you to hold the trade-offs publicly. I also know you—this will not be a number exercise for you."

You: "So we need either more money, more land set-asides for wetlands, or acceptance that people will be asked to move. And if we ask, we pick which people. The administrative part becomes intimate."

Dr. Noor: (nods) "Yes. And there’s another factor: Ephraim offered an accelerated schedule—loosen oversight, allocate the money immediate, but fewer community safeguards. If the council takes that, the levee goes up faster. But the social safety net you care about gets thinned."

You: "Which is exactly why I’m awake."

[Scene: Old Harbor District | Night]
[Visual: Flood-lit alleys; protest banners tied to lampposts sway; small groups of people cluster under emergency lamps]
[Sound: Sora’s voice—loud, cracked with emotion—cuts through the night]

You find Sora in a seam of the crowd, their silhouette lit from below by a hand-held lamp. Paint stains streak their jacket; a bandana hangs loose from their neck. They stand on a crate like an impromptu pulpit, the same charisma you’ve seen at rallies, weathered by exhaustion.

Sora: "—and we will not be moved like pieces on a developer’s board! We will not let them decide where our memories go!"

The crowd roars. You step forward and the roar reduces to a blade of focus aimed at you.

Sora’s face is a storm. When they see you, the edge sharpens into the thing you dread most: betrayal, right or wrong, made intimate.

Sora (voice breaking): "Ari. You signed. You went inside. How many of our doors do you think you can save while you sell us out?"

You: "I didn’t sign to sell people out. I signed to be inside the space where I could steer things—"

Sora: "Steer? Or slow us down while they shore up the rich blocks and leave the rest? Do you know what it felt like to stand in that room when they talked about 'phased relocations' like they were moving crates?"

Your chest tightens. You’ve been practicing answers for political audiences; nothing prepares you for the way Sora’s voice breaks the practiced tone. Their accusation is not only political; it is a personal pleading.

You: "Sora—" (you reach out; they step back)
Sora: "Don’t come at me with your calm voice. Not tonight."

A woman from the crowd—Maya—pushes through, raincoat beaded with salt. She plants herself beside Sora, palms on Sora’s shoulder like an anchor.

Maya: "We need options. Not just levees. Not just moving. Ari, you know the land. You know the science. Tell them there are other steps."

You and Sora exchange more than words—there is a history in each silence. Your methodical calm frays because Sora’s accusation lands where your guilt lives.

Sora (quieter, raw): "If you endorse that plan without the guarantees, we will lose more than buildings. We will lose stories. We will lose the seam that's kept this town whole."

You: "And I—" (your voice rough) "—I’m trying to keep people safe enough to keep those stories. I’m not asking for blind trust; I’m asking for a chance to make it better from the inside."

The exchange lengthens—the two of you circling, stabbing across and ducking into the same truths. It is not tidy. It is human and abrasive and somehow steadying. The crowd listens like a body holding its breath.

[Scene: Municipal Council Hall | Morning]
[Visual: Harsh overhead lights; warm wooden panels; banners folded into neat stacks at the back]
[Sound: Microphones clicking; murmured introductions; steps echo on hard floors]

You sit at the table with Lina two seats over and Ephraim’s representative hovering like a storm front. The press is present; someone counts on their phone. Your packet of recommendations is in front of you. Your thumb drums the edge until the rhythm becomes a metronome of decision.

Lina’s presence is exact as ever: platinum bob, a trench coat buttoned, gold hoops catching halogen light when she turns. She looks like a person shaped to give efficient warmth.

Lina (clinical, carefully gentle): "Ari, if we recommend targeted relocations for the most vulnerable pockets while we seal the levee and fund wetland corridors, federal funding unlocks immediately. We protect corridors and critical infrastructure. Lives saved."

You: "Which neighborhoods are we considering? Where do we draw the line between 'vulnerable pocket' and 'home'?"

Lina: (tilts tablet toward you; a map blooms) "The modeling shows repeated failure in the lower Old Harbor blocks, the northern row at Alder Street, and the backwater near Rook Lane. Relocations there reduce hydraulic load and allow the levee to operate at designed capacity."

Ephraim’s liaison—slick, polite—slides in with a smile that doesn’t reach his eyes.

Ephraim Rep: "Accelerated timeline is on the table. If oversight is centralized—fewer review cycles—capital moves faster. We can have the levee online one season sooner."

Your mind stutters at the math of seasons: one season sooner could mean lives saved next winter. Faster is a siren, though; it comes with strings.

Lina (softly): "Ari, recommend the phased relocations. Your assent will carry weight. You will get formal involvement in the oversight committee and a seat at the implementation table."

You feel the offer the way one feels a hand extended over a dark gap: steady, practical, but it requires stepping.

Your moral engines grind loud. The greenhouse memos, Dr. Noor’s caveat, the protest voice of Sora—everything multiplies into a single weight. You imagine the people who live in the pockets marked on Lina’s map: an old woman who teaches knotwork at the pier, a line of duplexes with hand-painted shutters, the grocery with the cracked neon sign that’s always been open late for fishermen.

Lina: "Tell us where—who—we ask to leave. The state needs names to allocate relocation funds."

You: (the words are granite-hard in your throat) "This is administrative. It is also the most intimate thing I will ever do in this position."

Dr. Noor steps forward, quietly, like a scientist delivering both data and mercy.

Dr. Noor: "There are other routes—additional wetland buffers, phased buyouts combined with community-led design—that will preserve more of the social fabric, but they require either more funds or more time. Political capital is a currency we can spend—do we spend it to buy time, or to buy concrete?"

Ephraim Rep (leaning in): "Time costs money and risk. We can front-load capital if oversight is reasonable. We can move quickly or move carefully. Those are the options."

Lina’s hand brushes the tablet; the gold hoop flashes. Her voice is precise, almost tender.

Lina: "Recommend the phased relocations, Ari. Help me pick where the phases fall. Do it with me and we protect many. Refuse and we risk losing the window of federal support."

Your methodical calm frays at the edges. Guilt curls in your throat like a living thing. It knows the faces that will be asked to leave. It knows the small joys that will be displaced.

But the Valence of this moment—despite the ache—is a strange, hard hope. This is what you wanted: levers to pull that change outcomes. The fact that the lever requires cost makes it heavier, not less meaningful.

Your pulse picks up. The music in your head reels faster: urgency layered over conviction.
