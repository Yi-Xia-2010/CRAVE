[Scene: Rooftop Greenhouse | Night]
[Visual: Warm amber light pooled over trays of marsh grass; condensation beading on polycarbonate panes]
[Sound: Low hum of ventilation, distant surf like a held breath]
[Music: Insistent strings building under a steady, rising percussion]

You stand with your palms flat on the long table, the signed packet folded beside a tray of sedge roots. The paper still smells faintly of printer ink and wet cardboard—a prosaic scent that tastes like consequence. The pilot is underway. The town has accepted the idea of a levee pilot with municipal oversight. You told yourself that signatures were a lever, not a surrender. Now the lever twists in your hands and brings new mechanisms into view.

The greenhouse is warmer than the night outside. Moist air clings to your throat; soil dust settles under your fingernails when you rub them together. Your hazel eyes narrow against a soft ache at the lids. You had planned for logistics—dashboards, nominations, audit clauses—but plans become political currency the moment someone else needs to buy them.

Dr. Noor appears at the doorway like an evening tide: patient, slow, carrying a mug that fogs in the warm air. Her braid catches a single amber light and throws a ribbon of silver.

Dr. Noor: "You look like someone who hasn't slept in three weeks."
You: "Counting shifts, not nights. How bad is it?"
Dr. Noor: (she hesitates, setting the mug down) "There's a modeling caveat we didn't flag to the council. It changes the risk surface if certain corridors are protected and others left behind."

You feel the sentence as if she let go of a rope you were holding. For a moment all the other noises—ventilation, distant waves, the town's slow machinery—compress into a single, clear tone.

You: "Caveat—how? Errors? Boundary conditions?"
Dr. Noor: "Boundaries. Our models show that a levee protecting the main thoroughfare reduces system-wide flood risk dramatically—but it also increases hydraulic pressure on adjacent lower-lying neighborhoods. The trade-off is real. The data suggest relocations would save lives downstream of the levee."

You: "Relocations."
Dr. Noor: (softly) "I wish it weren't the word we had to use. In theory, with more time and investment in distributed defenses—living shorelines, retrofits, neighborhood pumps—we could avoid displacing households. But the federal funding that's on the table is contingent on a demonstrable engineered solution. The levee unlocks that money fast."

Her hand finds your forearm, quick and steady—an old habit of hers when she wants you to listen without distraction.

Dr. Noor: "Ari, I trust your judgment. I also trust that you understand the human cost. I'm telling you because you need the full picture when the council asks for recommendations."

A high, clean chord rises in the music. You absorb the detail by degrees: the mechanical certainty of models, the moral geometry of neighborhoods shifted on a map. The greenhouse smell—peat and brine—suddenly tastes like a ledger again, but your chest tightens with a different weight now: possibility. If you advise correctly, lives may be saved.

[Visual: Close on the map pinned to the greenhouse wall; neon markers crowd the shoreline]
[Sound: A distant chant threading up from the Old Harbor, muffled but insistent]

You: "We can buy time with grants for distributed defenses—"
Dr. Noor: "Not at the speed the funding requires. At least not without other engines pushing the same cart."

That "cart" is the town's future. Your methodical calm scrapes against the jagged reality: to unlock the money fast, something—somewhere—must give.

Sora Watanabe: "You promised the town you'd keep us from becoming numbers. Now you're the person drawing the lines."

[Scene Transition: Tidewatch Institute — Council Prep Room | Late Night]
[Visual: Council table strewn with memos, coffee rings, a tablet glowing where Lina's hand had rested]
[Sound: Distant rain tapping the institute's corrugated cladding]
[Music: Full strings, rising toward a single, thrusting note]

Inside the institute's council prep room, Lina Moreau stands by the window, her silhouette precise against the gray sea. Her coat hangs immaculate even here; the halogen light slices her features. She taps a tablet; the gold hoop of her ear catches the light—small and exact.

Lina Moreau: (calm, clinical) "The federal package requires demonstrated engineered protection with a clear relocation plan for high-risk zones. Recommend phased relocations, and the funding flows quickly. Hesitate, and the window closes."

She says it without heat, the way someone explains a weather pattern. Her voice is almost an offer.

You look at the maps again. The memos Dr. Noor mentioned lie at your elbow, annotated in a dozen handwritings. Names you know—streets you walked as a child—marked with colored pins.

You: "If I recommend relocations, people's homes get listed as 'sacrificial' zones on paper. If I don't, we may lose the funding and lose different lives."

Lina: (leaning in, voice low) "Which do you want to be remembered for, Ari? Saving more people now or preserving every street at the risk of catastrophe later?"

The question isn't rhetorical. It aims like a knife into your refusal to choose. Behind her, a live feed ticks data—projected storm surges, probability bands—numbers obeying rules we once believed were neutral.

You: "I don't want to be remembered for a ledger."
Lina: (almost a smile) "Then advise in a way that keeps people alive and keeps the council honest. Recommend neighborhoods for phased relocation with strict oversight terms. Insist on transparent compensation, relocation supports, and a community oversight board."

Her hand brushes the tablet again; the gold hoop flashes. The gesture is both seduction and policy. The urgency tightens—what she offers is actionable, fast, and it comes with her political favor. It also asks you to wield administrative weight that will reshape lives.

Maya Cruz arrives with a stained raincoat and the exact steadiness of someone who has dug ditches at dawn. She sits across from you, eyes soft and blunt at once.

Maya Cruz: "You know my block better than anyone. If this goes wrong, it's my aunt's house on the line."
You: "I know. I'm trying to balance time and lives. I'm trying to make an honest recommendation."
Maya: (holding your gaze) "Make sure the people get the supports you say they'll get. Not promises. Things that happen."

Her practical insistence is a tether—reminding you that policy becomes meals, transport vans, school moves. It is both a warning and a lifeline.

Dr. Noor returns to stand with you among the plants. Her expression is quieter, more resolute.

Dr. Noor: "You have three real options before the council tomorrow. Recommend phased relocations to secure levee funding. Advocate for alternative protections and push for more time. Or reveal the memos publicly to force transparent negotiations."

You feel the air sharpen. The three phrases line up like tidal marks on a post—each a different measure of flood.

Dr. Noor: "None are perfect. Each carries losses and saves things that matter—lives, homes, dignity. But you will decide which neighbors to ask to leave, or how hard to fight for another month of mitigation funding, or whether to light the town on fire so the smoke forces change."

You: (breathe) "If I recommend the relocations, I can secure funding quickly. If I fight, I might lose the window. If I reveal the memos, we get transparency but risk political collapse."

Dr. Noor: (soft, almost a whisper) "You were never going to be spared the choosing, Ari. You were going to be given the instruments instead."

Your throat tightens. The arousal that has been building—the practical urgency, the personal stakes, the political heat—becomes a red point on the horizon. You think of the siblings you lost, of Maya's boots, of Sora's cracked voice. You think of Lina's precise hand on her tablet. None of it is clean. All of it is fixable in degrees.

You stand, the plants ticking their humid breaths against the glass. For a moment everything slows and then accelerates—the city's pulse matching your own. Hope sits under the pressure like a brittle seed, begging for water and exactness.

Dr. Noor: "Whatever you choose, make the terms bind. Make relocation dignified if it must happen. Make delays accountable if you push for more time. If you reveal memos, be prepared for fallout—but know that the town will see the truth."

You look at the memos one last time. The numbers do not comfort you. The possibility of saving many is a bright, hard shard. The knowledge that saving some requires asking others to move leaves a hollow that is not defeat—it is the shape of compromise.
