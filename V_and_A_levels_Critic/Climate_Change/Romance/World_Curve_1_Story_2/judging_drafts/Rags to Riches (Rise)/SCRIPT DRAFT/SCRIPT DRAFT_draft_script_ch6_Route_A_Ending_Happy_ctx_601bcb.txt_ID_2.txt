[Scene: Living Shoreline | Late Afternoon]

You step onto the soft boardwalk and the air hits you—salt and warm mud, the oily tang of tide pools, wet rope under your palms. The town has come out in a way you haven't seen since the summer festivals: banners tied to lamp posts, sun-faded quilts laid on sand, a tangle of bodies leaning into the same weather. Every face is lit with the frantic, hopeful energy of people who have decided to act.

Dr. Noor stands near the demonstration channel, her coat pinned open, hair braided into its usual crown. She holds up a damp chart—smudged pencil strokes, corrected lines, a corner already stained by rain. The crowd hushes; even the gulls seem to pause.

Dr. Noor: "We ran the old model again with the new field readings. The assumptions about sediment accretion were... optimistic. Roots are taking hold where we added native mats. That means overtopping slows, overland flow reduces. The error was not in the idea—it was in underweighting community observations. Those notes from the kids? They mattered."

You move closer until her voice goes through you like a pulse. You remember that soggy corner of the Moleskine; the child's ink blot where a shoe print intruded. You feel the climb—fast, bright—like wind catching sails.

Sora Watanabe climbs the crate without pretense. They don't need a megaphone; their voice breaks through anyway—sharp, theatrical, a blade tempered with warmth.

Sora Watanabe: "This isn't about being anti-development or pro-romance with engineering. It's about who gets to name safety in their own town. It’s about mothers who still wake to the press of water in their dreams. It's about us—having a voice."

A ripple of approval moves like current. Someone starts clapping; then it becomes a drum of steps and hands. You feel your heartbeat snap in time with the sound.

You: "We tested, we listened, and we fixed what the models missed. It's not perfect. It doesn't pretend to be. But it holds water, and that matters now."

Sora looks at you—no performance, only a blazing, private kind of approval that lights their whole face. Their hand taps the crate beside yours and it feels like a private agreement open to the town.

Dr. Noor: "Matched funding, phased. The pilot reduces peak overtopping significantly in our simulations using current data sets—meaning fewer emergency evacuations and lower long-term infrastructure costs."

[Scene: Council Balcony / Chamber | Sunset]
[Visual: The sun drops, washing the room in a toxic-yellow light through streaked windows. Outside, the living shoreline glows a saturated green against the gray. Inside, the chair raps the gavel.]

The vote is blunt, fast. The council clerk calls names. You count in your head like tide marks—yes, yes, no, yes, abstain—until the chair brings the gavel down.

Council Chair: "The motion passes. Conditional municipal support is granted with the following clauses: matched phased funding, legally constituted community oversight with veto on major design alterations, and a preliminary audit of model assumptions led by Tidewatch and community scientists."

For a moment the chamber hangs—then breaks. The sound that erupts is not just applause. It's a shockwave: cheers, a child's shriek, someone sobbing, camera shutters, a hundred people shouting a wet, immediate relief. You feel it as a physical thing: a pressure budge under your ribs, a bright, where the day had been taut and wired, now given a small, desperate exhale.

Sora laughs, something near tears, powerful and raw. They fall into your shoulder when the crowd surges; your arm goes around them almost without thinking. Their fingers find yours and hold—like a found thing.

Sora Watanabe: (voice low, against you) "We actually did it. Can you—can you feel it?"

You: "I feel like the tide turned for a second. We have to keep it that way."

Lina watches from above. Her ice-gray eyes have softened in a way that makes you think of fractures healed with careful gold. She approaches the railing, and for a beat the two of you share a look—politics condensed to posture. She inclines her head, the smallest of gestures. Not surrender. Not embrace. Something pragmatic that might yet mean trust.

Lina Moreau: "You built something here worth protecting. We'll make sure funding follows—on the condition that oversight is transparent and technical partners are engaged."

Sora: "We wanted input, not oversight that silenced us."

Lina Moreau: (the tiniest, almost rueful smile) "Then keep us honest. Keep yourself accountable. We'll meet that standard."

Ephraim's developers mutter. A few of them clap—obligatory, thin. The air tastes of hot metal and victory.

You let the moment hold. The town is still bruised—there will be more work, more negotiations, more storms—but it has something like scaffolding now: plans, oversight, community muscle. The pilot is not a cure; it's an armature on which a future can be built.

Sora turns and catches your gaze, and the green fleck in your left iris flashes like a small lighthouse reflected in their dark pupils. They don't say anything for a long beat. When they do, their voice is low, tender, and entirely unshowy.

Sora Watanabe: "This is how I wanted it. Not fireworks. Work. Folly. Mending nets at two in the morning. You at my side, stubborn in the right ways."

You: "I wanted the net mended. I didn't realize I'd find a reason to stay."

Sora Watanabe: (firm) "Stay. Help build it proper. Argue with me when I go theatrical. And when we screw up, help me fix it."

You: "I'll remind you when your megaphone needs tuning. And I'll be there to patch the holes you make."

You fold your hands into theirs, a quiet seal. The streets still smell of wet rope and tomorrow's plans. The town sleeps with one eye open; you sleep with your head full of lists, diagrams, and a warmth that does not feel fragile.
