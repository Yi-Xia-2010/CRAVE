You stand behind the makeshift podium with Sora at your right and Maya threading through the crowd like a practiced current. The decision you made—openly naming Ephraim and his tangled funding—rests in your throat like one precise syllable. It is present. It is not dramatic; it is consequential.

A volunteer hands you a stack of printed contracts. The paper smells faintly of ink and damp. Your fitness-watch taps once against your wrist—an incoming calendar reminder you set three nights ago, now oddly ceremonial. Around you: salt-streaked faces, paint-splattered jackets, a kid balancing a hand-painted sign against a railing. The town looks at you as if you're a hinge about to turn.

Sora leans close, voice low but steady. "You sure you're ready for this?" (their breath smells faintly of sea-salt and coffee)

You want to say yes and mean it, but a ledger of possible consequences scrolls behind your eyes—injunctions, supply disruptions, lawsuits, and the human cost that doesn't show up neatly in graphs.

Sora Watanabe: "People are counting on you. Not because you're perfect—because you care. Speak the facts. We can handle the fury after."

You: "Facts, then. No theater." (You run a thumb along the chipped enamel seagull pin and feel the familiar scrape of metal against fabric.)

Maya emerges, palms sticky with mud from plant pots, cheeks flushed from running back and forth. "I've got the witnesses ready at the desal yard—three recorded statements, two willing to go on camera. Logistics are tight but we can shuttle more people if needed." Her voice is quick and efficient, the cadence of someone used to making imperfect plans work.

Maya Cruz: "Also—if this triggers legal attachments, we'll need people to physically verify plants and materials at the wetland nursery. Documentation. Chain of custody." (She taps a damp clipboard.)

You nod and breathe through the little tightness in your ribs. This is what you chose: to pull thread from a system that had been stitched in secret.

[Scene: Mariselle Boardwalk — Press Area | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Cameras arrayed like a passing tide; reporters cluster beneath banners; the sea beyond throws a flat, silver line]
[Sound: Microphones rustle, a murmuring crowd, a distant foghorn]
[Music: The beat tightens—mid arousal, focused; undercurrent of rising anticipation]

You step up. The documents are in your hands: copies of contracts, annotated redlines, emails with timestamps, a ledger of preferential clauses. You set them on the podium as if laying sediment samples for inspection.

Sora stands beside you—fierce and composed. Maya is visible in the crowd, waving a hand to local volunteers. Your pulse is a measured drum; the world is unusually loud and close.

You: "Good afternoon. I'm Ari Navarro. I'm here to present evidence of preferential contracts between private developer Ephraim Blake's firms and decision-makers within our municipal process." (Your words are leveled, not shouted. Each clause is a stone you set in place.)

Reporter 1: "Are you accusing Mr. Blake of corruption?" (A hand shoots up; a camera leans forward.)

You: "I'm presenting documents that show deals made without transparent public process. I'm asking that these be examined and that the municipal review be reopened." (You slide a marked page forward. The cameras zoom on redlined clauses.)

A reporter asks about motives; a legal counsel off-screen begins to speak into a phone. You feel Sora's hand briefly close on your wrist—an electric, grounding squeeze.

Sora Watanabe: "We are not here for performative headlines. We're here because people have been priced out of their docks, because access was privatized, because contracts were exchanged under opaque terms." (They meet your eyes and then the crowd. Their voice carries.)

Ephraim appears at the edge of the crowd, gaunt face like a carved thing—pale under the light, smile tight. He raises a hand as if conducting the moment.

Ephraim Blake: (over a microphone passed to him by a supposed supporter) "These accusations are nothing more than theater. Our investments bring jobs. The process was legal and vetted." (His words are smooth; his posture is deliberately unflappable.)

Sora: (interrupting, sharp) "Legal doesn't mean ethical. And 'jobs' doesn't restore the docks you privatized." (They step closer; the crowd reacts—a collective inhalation.)

A lawyer in a crisp suit threads through volunteers and plants themselves near the podium; a security team in dark jackets positions along the rail. Cameras pivot between faces. Someone in the crowd shouts a question about the Old Harbor District; it lands like a question rock.

You: "We have filed for an independent audit and intend to pursue injunctive relief on projects already approved under these arrangements." (You lift the documents like a shield.) "We are asking the Development Authority to freeze any further approvals until this is resolved."

Lina Moreau steps forward with a municipal statement prepped and calm. Her voice is measured, a practiced civility.

Lina Moreau: "The municipality acknowledges the concerns raised and will cooperate with any legitimate review. However, we ask for restraint in public demonstrations that could interrupt essential services." (She does not call Ephraim by name. She does not applaud you.)

Her expression is complex, unreadable—part professionalism, part the private calculus of a politician balancing optics and obligations. The crowd consumes the words in waves.

Reporter 2: "Mayor Moreau, will the municipality halt the development approvals immediately?"

Lina Moreau: "We will evaluate. Our priority remains public safety and continuity of essential projects." (She folds her hands; the line between 'evaluate' and 'freeze' is thin.)

Sora leans in, indignation sharpened by the smell of salt and diesel, and whispers, "She's hedging."

You: "A pause to evaluate can mean the difference between mitigation and more privatization. We can't let 'continuity' be an excuse." (Your voice tightens, but you keep it composed.)

The crowd erupts—cheers, shouts, someone banging a drum. For a moment the sound swallows you. It is raw and righteous and, in its noise, clarifying. You let the current pass through you.

[Sound: The press conference noise fractures as a legal aide steps up and starts to read a statement—threatening litigation language filters outward]
[Music: Pulse steadies into a lower note; energy remains mid—engaged, not panicked]

From the periphery, a security detail moves toward volunteers. A man in a dark suit—one of Ephraim's counsel—places a hand on a volunteer's shoulder and says quiet, professional words you can't hear fully. A flier on the ground flutters with the salt wind.

Your fitness-watch buzzes again—this time multiple notifications: incoming messages, a flagged email, an urgent voicemail from Dr. Noor.

[Scene: A quiet corner near the promenade | Moments later]
[Visual: You step aside; the sea rim is a flat gray smear through the fog; your breath fogs in front of you]
[Sound: Conversation muffled; a distant crash of waves]

You answer the voicemail. Dr. Noor's voice is precise and lined with the particular worry of someone who reads risk models for breakfast.

Dr. Noor (voicemail): "Ari—there's a cascading risk if funding pipelines are severed abruptly. Dr. Noor (on call now, voice terse): "Good. Because the models show a window—if the supply chain hiccups in the next thirty-six to seventy-two hours, response capacity during the next surge will be compromised. Legal remedies are important, but anticipate the physical consequences."

You run a hand over your face. The arithmetic of responsibility is a ledger you're forced to reconcile: the ethical imperative to stop corrupt privatization versus the practical imperative to keep pumps and parts flowing.

[Scene: Old Harbor District | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Half-submerged storefronts, faded murals, boats moored where cars once parked; an injunction notice tacked to a board; the air is pearly and close]
[Sound: Soft, slapping water against derelict steps; a distant radio playing a fishermen's ballad]
[Music: Low, steady; contemplative but building a trace of tension]

A legal injunction posts on a weathered notice board near a shuttered café—typed words and an official seal. Volunteers cluster to read. The injunction freezes certain contracts and approvals; the immediate effect is clarity and confusion at the same time.

The Old Harbor District already hollowed out, breathes in the new reality. Businesses that depended on contracted work see payments delayed; a small contractor you worked with looks at you with a measured mixture of relief and fear. Someone mutters that injunctions are victory; another says they're worried about payroll.

You: "This will force transparency, but it will also reroute cash flows. We need to be proactive about relief." (You feel the practical landscape—supplies, labor, trust—shifting like sand.)

Maya: "I've already pushed for a co-op emergency fund. We can bridge wages for a short period, but we have to ask the county for rapid relief." (Her voice is a ledger of logistics—hope dressed as spreadsheets.)

Sora stands close—close enough that you sense the heat of their presence as the sun breaks through fog in a narrow, salt-yellow ribbon. They are furious—fury braided with loyalty. Their hand finds yours with a weight that is not dramatic; it is real and necessary.

Sora Watanabe: "We did the right thing. It matters. But Noor's right—this is a complicated machine. People will be hurt if we are not careful. We need contingency, now." (Their gaze is direct, unwavering.)

You: "We plan. We call for emergency payroll relief. We signal to suppliers that community oversight will prioritize continuity and safety. And we prepare for lawsuits." (Each sentence is a tool you hand to the town.)

Sora shifts closer, the conversation folding into a quieter space between you two, away from cameras and councillors.

Sora Watanabe: (soft) "When you stood up there... I could see you carrying all of it. You don't have to carry alone."

You: (quiet) "I never thought bravery would feel like an accounting ledger."

They snort, a small, disbelieving sound that becomes a smile. "That's you—romantic with spreadsheets." Their fingers find the seam of your jacket and pause, a simple, intimate gesture. For a brief moment the world is not about injunctions or supply chains; it's about the warmth of another human hand on yours.

You: "Promise me something—don't let us lose the people under the legal wrangling."

Sora: "I won't. You don't have to promise. We'll do it together." (Their voice is steady, certain.)

The intimacy is brief, practical, and honest. It threads through the rest of the afternoon like a taut rope.

You: "And transparent." (You add the second word because it is what you mean.)
