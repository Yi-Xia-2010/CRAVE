[Scene: Storm-sheened Boardwalk | Dawn — heavy, low cloud]
[Visual: Long shot of the boardwalk, salt-black planks slick with rain; protest banners snap in gusts; portable floodlights smear neon into the fog]
[Music: Driving percussion under a rising string ostinato]
[Sound: Sea on the edge of rising; distant shouts; the metallic clank of a handcart]

You wake with the taste of salt and paper on your tongue — the Moleskine still in your coat pocket, the single held note from last night folded between pages like a pressed leaf. The town is not quiet; it is arranging itself into a dozen small mobilizations. You can feel the momentum like a current underfoot.

Maya is already there, sleeves rolled, mud under her nails. She bristles at a line of temporary fencing and then grins when she sees you.

Maya Cruz: "We put up extra ropes around the Old Wharf. The security teams tried to reroute the volunteers; I told them their contractor didn't buy the town. They didn't like being told that."

You take in the fenced-off lot where Ephraim's contractors have parked a line of cages and a squat, modular office. Men in dark jackets stand with radios and polite stiffness. The desal staging yard is half-hidden behind a scrawl of plywood and plastic tarps. A hand-lettered sign someone taped under the municipal notice reads: KEEP OUR PUMPS SAFE — NOT PRIVATE LOCKUPS.

The air is a bitten thing — cold, damp, with that metallic tang the sea lays on everything before a storm. Rain beads on your scarf; when you pull it closer the soaked wool clings to your skin like resolve.

Sora arrives, breath visible, a wet bandana looped around her neck like a banner. Her eyes find you and sharpen — not angry so much as determined.

Sora Watanabe: (low, urgent) "They're blocking access to the old intake. If they control the staging yard, they control the pumps. We can't let them sit on spares and membranes when a storm is on the way."

You think of the list you made last night — procurement reroutes, volunteer rosters, legal calls — and how each item translates into a person who will either dry off because of those choices or freeze on a stoop because a valve wasn't turned.

Ari Navarro (You): "We need the pumps online, but we also need to keep the pressure on Ephraim. If they stash the parts, neighborhoods go dark."

Sora's jaw tightens. She moves among the volunteers with quick, efficient gestures — passing armloads of signs, pointing where banners should be thrown, where scaffolds can be scaled. Her energy is contagious and absolute.

Sora Watanabe: "Occupation. We put a human line around the yard. No trucks in, no secrecy. People see what they're doing. We force the issue in public."

You feel the familiar coil in your chest — the old calculus of risk and consequence. Your sibling's face, for a half-second, flashes under the rain: a small shoe at an angle, a toy bobbing. You don't let the image stay long; it is both talisman and torment.

[Scene: Desal Staging Yard Gate | Midday — closing storm, wind biting]
[Visual: Close on a chain-link gate, a laminated contractor badge fluttering; a megaphone lies on the wet ground]
[Sound: Shouts, the crackle of police radios; the pounding of rain on tarps]

Sora organizes the approach like a conductor with a stubborn score. A ring of volunteers link arms; a few climb the scaffolding to hang signs that read: COMMUNITY ACCESS NOW and HANDS OFF THE PUMPS. Security answers with a measured show of force: a few more men with ear pieces, a lock, a portable floodlight trained like an eye.

A woman in a cheap suit — Ephraim's local fixer — takes a picture of faces and hands it to a man who lifts his phone and speaks into it with the practiced cadence of escalation. You watch and catalog: who will stay, who will fall silent if the police arrive, which volunteers have medical training.

Then your phone buzzes. Anonymous. Two words, no signature: WE KNOW WHERE YOU SLEEP.

Your guts do a small, ugly flip. The arousal spikes into a white-hot thread of anger and protective clarity. Whoever sent it wants you to step back, to be afraid. The town, right now, needs you not to be small.

You wade through the crowd and find Sora, drenched at the helm, cheeks flushed.

Ari Navarro (You): "I got a message. Threat. It's nothing. Or it wants us to think it's something."

Sora's eyes go hard and then softer in a quick blink that is almost tenderness — the way she looks when she notices a cut on your finger and doesn't say it hurts.

Sora Watanabe: "We stay visible. Threats that rely on fear crumble under lights and names. If they wanted you gone, they'd come when the yard is empty. They won't. They're cowards."

Her certainty steadies you, and for the first time today, the fear thins into an instrument: not to be ignored, but to be used to sharpen the will.

Footsteps drum; a compact car pulls up and Dr. Noor steps out, hair tucked under a weatherproof hood, rain on her shoulders like a halo. Her face is tired but lit from some inner, precise flame. She moves straight toward you with a paper tube in her hand.

Dr. Noor: "I ran the numbers again on the emergency maintenance windows. There's something you need to know."

She unfolds the printout like an offering; the ink smears and then reads clear as a tide chart. Her brow creases.

Dr. Noor: "Funding reallocations tied to the injunction could trigger delays in the pump maintenance schedule. If the municipal transfers are frozen before the April window, the pumping stations might miss critical membrane swaps. That's not theoretical—it's a days-to-weeks delay that raises flood risk during high-surge events."

Your heart stutters, but you find your voice.

Ari Navarro (You): "So pulling funding or stopping contracts now could make things worse. We knew exposing Ephraim would provoke a legal tangle, but this is—"

Dr. Noor: "This is a narrower margin than we thought. But it also creates leverage. If we can get a court order that secures emergency maintenance as a protected item or push the council to temporize funding releases under an oversight clause, we can force a buy-in that preserves operations while the prosecution proceeds."

Sora's hand on the megaphone tightens. She looks like she might argue revival or collapse in the same breath.

Sora Watanabe: "So — negotiate for the pumps now, drag them into court later. You want to broker assurances, Ari? That's capitulation to me."

Dr. Noor: (soft, but firm) "Not capitulation. Containment. We can build a legal firewall around critical maintenance without erasing the case."

Your mind diagrams possibilities like a seagull rearranging its flight: escalation, negotiation, or a public blow that forces judicial oversight. Each path expedites some danger and soothes another. Each has faces attached. You can hear them: an elderly couple who runs a bed and breakfast with sandbags on every window; the volunteer who left her day job to learn how to repair valves; the small boy who delivered the hand-lettered sign through a storm; your sibling, memory like an instruction manual in your jaw.

The crowd hums louder; someone starts a chant that is less a slogan and more a procession: We are the town, we keep it alive. It moves through your chest, steady and bright.

Then a municipal vehicle arrives, discreet but official. A sheet of paper is handed to Sora: the town council has scheduled emergency hearings for the afternoon. You can practically feel the meeting's gravity land on your shoulders.

Sora Watanabe: "They howl at our feet and then invite us to their table. Typical."

Sora is furious and elated in the same inhale — the arousal here is not only anger but the high-strung hope that the town's voice will intersect policy at the exact right frequency.

[Scene: Municipal Council Hall Plaza | Afternoon — sky tearing, rain slanting sideways]
[Visual: The glass façade of the Council Hall reflecting a band of angry clouds; protest banners tied to the plaza railings]
[Sound: City PA system, murmurs swelling into a roar; rain like a drumroll]

Council security opens a narrow corridor for representatives. Cameras line the perimeter. Lina Moreau is there, immaculate even in wet fabric, her coat a precise cut against the chaos. Ephraim stands near the edge, suit dark and unmoved, a private smile like a shadow.

Inside, the chamber smells of polished wood and hot coffee. Staffers move with the careful geometry of people who have rehearsed their expressions. The hearing will be televised; your words could be ink on millions of scrolls and also the small, crucial push that keeps a pump on schedule.

You pace the public gallery, the Moleskine a soft weight in your hand. The storm presses on the windows like an unblinking audience, and you find yourself standing where you promised yourself to act differently than fear. Positive conviction thrums: you can steer this without betraying the movement.

A voice speaks from the dais — not yet decisive, but open enough to be a hinge.

Town Council Spokesperson: "We recognize the community's right to transparency. Given the allegations, we will hold an emergency hearing and discuss immediate guarantees to protect critical infrastructure."

Your mouth forms the shape of a plan. Sora's earlier words echo and then settle into something else: not simple capitulation, but negotiated defense. Dr. Noor's correction is a blueprint with an invitation: use legal tools to create temporary protective shells for maintenance while accountability proceeds.

Sora approaches you, rain whipping tiny knives across her lashes. Her hands find yours for a moment — whether to steady you or to stake a claim, you can't tell — and her voice falls to a quiet, dangerous pitch.

Sora Watanabe: "I don't want us to be the kind of people who sign away the town's dignity for short-term comfort. I need to know: do you stand with the people who risk arrest to keep everything public, or do you stand with the council that will put things back on a timetable you can trust?"

Her question is not only political. It lands in the place you have kept closed: the box where grief and duty have polygonal edges. Your sibling's face is there again, unbidden, the worst-case that always makes you choose the cautious path — except that caution isn't neutral right now. It could be complicity.

You breathe the storm in and taste the salt of a different certainty. The arousal that has built through the morning — rain, banners, threats, legal briefings — reaches a fever. Everything hums toward a single hinge: action now, or action tempered.

You picture three roads, each lit by different lamps and each with their own risk and redemption: a direct occupation that refuses compromise; a legal and municipal armistice that buys time; a public exposure that drags contracts into the light while coordinating maintenance.

The choice is crystalline and terrible and, under the roar of rain, oddly full of possibility.

You feel the town next to you — Sora at your side, Maya in the crowd, newcomers who name their streets when they chant. The moral calculus is no longer abstract; it is faces, shoes, valves, and the small things you promised you'd protect.
