[Scene: Mariselle Bus Stop | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A battered bus exhales its last passengers onto salt-scoured pavement. Fog hangs low, swallowing the horizon.]
[Music: Sparse, low-register piano — notes spaced, each one lingering thinly in the air.]
[Sound: The bus doors sigh closed; gulls cry once, distant and hollow.]

You step down with a bag of tools slung over your shoulder and a Moleskine tucked under your arm. The notebook's spine is softened by rain and a dozen thumbprints; tide sketches and shorthand crowd the last pages. Your boots hiss on wet gravel. The town smells the way it always has when the sea is in a mood: brine, wet wood, melted tar—an odor that makes memory ache around the edges.

You let the town fold itself around you like an old scar. The boardwalk is a skein of salt and patched planks, banners and protest flyers tangled in lamp posts, colors dulled by fog. A warped bench bears salt crust like barnacles. A rope strung between posts drips darkly; beyond it, the sea is a flat pewter sheet.

You pause, hands cold inside the sleeves of your field jacket, and trace the chipped enamel seagull pin at your collar with your thumb. It grounds you for a second and then the old pressure settles back on your shoulders—the weight that belonged to promises you once made and the one you failed to keep. The memory of sirens at night, the wrong evacuation route, the mouth of a river rushing where a road should've been—it's not cinematic. It's the flat, slow ache inside your chest that doesn't let you move quickly anymore.

[Scene: Co-op Front | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A spray-painted sign swinging; muddy boots lined on a threshold. Sun-freckled hands rearrange nursery trays.]
[Sound: Low conversation, a kettle shrieking faintly in the back; the slap of a wooden crate being set down.]

Maya is there, half-hidden beneath her fisher's raincoat, hair unruly at the edges, eyes like knives softened by care. She sees you and for the first beat you study the map of her face—the tiny scars from rope burns, the laugh-lines that deepen when she smiles. She opens her mouth, closes it, then lets the smile come out slow.

Maya: "Look at you. Back in one piece. You carted the whole tool shed with you or is that just show?"

You: "Just the essentials." (You try for lightness; it comes out as an economy of breath.) "How's the nursery holding up?"

Maya: (tilting her head) "Better than the council's flood map, if that's what you're asking." (She laughs, a sound that gathers and then splinters.) "But we planted three more spartina last week. Kids were in knee-deep mud, grinning like fools. Kept me from carving the mayor's name into the wood with a pocketknife."

You let a small, private smile slip and then the wind takes it. You fold the Moleskine open without thinking and flip to a page of tide cross-sections. Maya reaches out and taps one of your annotated notes, careful, as if touching a blister.

Maya: "You still writing the sea love letters?"

You: "Someone has to chronicle how it breaks us." (The words hold more than the joke allows.) "I—" (You swallow.) "—I brought the retrofit kit Dr. Noor asked for."

Maya's face shifts; the sun freckles darken to something unreadable.

Maya: "Good. We need hands. And headspace. The boardwalk crew keeps showing up—Sora's been loud enough to drown old Ephraim's commercials." (Her voice tightens.) "You know they're gearing up. The vote's in two days."

You: "I heard." (Your fingers edge the watch face. The firmware's little barometric glyph watches the sky as much as you do.)

Maya: "Your scarf looks damp." (An easy observation; it reads like concern.) "Coffee? Or do you still drink saltwater for breakfast?"

You both laugh, the sound small. It doesn't feel like a beginning. It feels like moving furniture around a house with a cracked foundation.

You stand, shoulders bending, and the two of you walk past the co-op's crate-stacked doorway toward the Tidewatch Institute. [Scene: Along the Boardwalk Toward Tidewatch | Early Evening]
[Visual: Protest flyers flapping slowly; a distant group huddled around a banner. The skyline is low and ragged.]
[Sound: Muffled chanting to the left, the occasional clack of a camera tripod being set up.]

You hear Sora before you see them: a high, clear voice wrapped in cadence and challenge. You don't approach; you stand at the edge while a crew hands out flyers. Their energy is incandescent in the way of flares—bright, necessary, and dangerous in gusty weather.

You watch as the demonstration folds into the boardwalk—people's voices rising and then sinking again. You recognize some faces from old days: volunteers, small business owners, kids with paint on their hands. You cannot tell, from here, what the demonstration will break into; you only feel the tide of it like a pressure against your ribs.

Across the bay a digital billboard cycles a polished rendering of a levee and glass towers—the promise of order and safety. The seal at the corner bears the Development Authority's mark; Ephraim's signature is on the paperwork gleaming at the base. Lina's name is on the municipal pamphlets pushed under the door of every house whose residents still bother to open them.

You think of the council chamber: warm wood, cold calculation, the loop of cameras, the muffled way decisions are made in fluorescence and applause. You think of those rendering images—clean, final—and then of the actual streets: half-submerged storefronts, the smell of oil and wet paint, a child's bicycle where a stoop used to be.

[Scene: Dune Path Near Tidewatch Institute | Twilight]
[Visual: Dune grass bowed low in wind. The Tidewatch building stands to one side, its metal skin streaked and honest.]
[Sound: The sea like a constant low drum; the gravel underfoot grinds softly as you walk.]

Dr. Noor finds you at the dune path, hands already folded around a thin stack of paper. She moves with that quiet authority of someone who taught you how to measure a shoreline but also knew how to hold a grieving hand without words. Her hair is braided, silver threaded through like tide lines in a map.

Dr. Noor: (handing over the folded models) "I didn't want to give this to you in the meeting. Too noisy. Too performative."

You take the papers. They feel like thin, brittle things—ink as weight.

You: "Sediment runs, not politics." (A joke to keep your voice even.) "Is—what's different?"

Dr. Noor: (her eyes, always kind, are sharp tonight) "The math changed." (She says it slowly, like placing a stone into still water so the ripples show.) "Subsidence rates updated, storm intensity projections—shifts you wouldn't necessarily notice until it's too late. There are feedbacks the old models didn't account for. We... I was arrogant. I apologize."

You don't know how much of her apology is for herself and how much is meant for you. The words fold quietly between you both.

You: (your fingers close around the paper until it creases) "How bad?"

Dr. Noor: "Worse than we told council last winter. Some of the maps that landed on Ephraim's desk were optimistic edits. Not lies—optimistic. The levee plan will protect certain corridors, but not neighborhoods that have been sinking longer. And the housing—if it goes up where they propose, it will put people in harm's way in ways the model didn't flag."

You imagine streetlights going out under a brown sky, sand in the air, the sound of plywood slapping against windows. You imagine routes that lead to water.

Dr. Noor: (leans in, voice low) "People will judge you by what you do next."

The sentence is a small, terrible detonator. It doesn't explode outward; it sinks into the ground like a post hammered into wet sand.

You look at the sediment maps again. The lines on paper are not just lines anymore; they are the scraped margins where lives might stop. Your watch nudges with a barometer beep—an old firmware patch you installed yourself. The glyph crawls left; the pressure is dropping.

Your hands narrow without permission. Your hazel eyes—green fleck catching the weak light—tighten as if squinting will make the data rearrange into something kinder.

Dr. Noor: "You don't have to carry this alone." (She says it as if she might be barging into a private storm.) "But you will have to decide which storms the town survives and which it doesn't. The vote—"

You cut in, because the word vote is the seam that could split everything.

You: "When is it?"

Dr. Noor: "Two days." (She exhales slowly.) "They're calling it a rescue package. It will look tidy under the cameras."

You hold the papers to your chest. The Moleskine is small and soft below your hand, the tool-bag heavy. The air tastes like iron and wet salt. Your scarf clings damply to your neck.

You walk away from the dunes the way you have always left things you love: moving slowly, as if speed might snap the fragile things into pieces. The town's lights begin to pinprick the fog—neon signs smeared, the greenhouse a steady amber glow that insists on life regardless of everything.

Your retrofit watch buzzes again, a low tone. The barometric arrow points down. You look at the skyline: a collage of banners, a billboard with glass towers, the wet wood of the boardwalk reflecting a yellow glow. Sora's voice is still a smudge of sound. Maya's hand brushes yours as you pass a crate of reeds—brief, human, and necessary.

You press your thumb to the Moleskine's corner and feel the impression of your own handwriting, a habit that grounds you. You think of your sibling's name—how quickly it can arrive without warning—and the evacuation route that failed them. The image comes like undersea pressure: slow, invisible, and then terminal.

You breathe in. Brine, wet wood, protest smoke. Your scarf is damp against your throat. You promise yourself something in the low, private way people do when the world is about to demand everything.

You: (whispering, not sure if the sea or the sky hears) "This time, I will make a plan that saves more than statistics."

It is not a vow with fireworks. It is a small, steady contraction of will. The town keeps moving around you—voices, banners, the soft clack of a camera—but inside you the air has thickened. The barometer on your wrist lowers another notch. The fog seems to sit closer to the skin.

You tuck the folded models under your arm, the weight of them equal to the weight of decision. Ahead, the municipal council's windows are dark rectangles waiting for morning. Somewhere in a café, a council packet will be annotated and signed before the light lifts. Sora's crew will print another batch of flyers. Ephraim's underwriters will make calls. Lina will polish her speech.

Everything is gathering itself for a motion.

You stand at the edge of it, a single person with tools and a notebook and a history that presses like tidewater. The approaching storm is a slow current now, not a flash: it moves ceaselessly, inexorably, the kind of danger that creeps up through miscalculation and human hope alike.
