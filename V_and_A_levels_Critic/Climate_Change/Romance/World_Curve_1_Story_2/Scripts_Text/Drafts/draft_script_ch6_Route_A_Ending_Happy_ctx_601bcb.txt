### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Ending: Happy


[Scene: Living Shoreline / Boardwalk | Late Afternoon → Evening]

[Visual: Wide shot of the Living Shoreline demonstration—muddy channels braided with living roots, boardwalks slick with salt; children in bright rubber boots splash in shallow channels; volunteers cluster around reed mats and root bundles.]
[Sound: Voices layered into a rising murmur—laughter, instructions, the slap of water, an undercurrent of chanting from the plaza.]
[Music: String quartet building into a restless, driving rhythm; percussion snapping beneath like surf on rocks.]

Narration
You felt the crowd before you saw it—an old Mariselle tide of people pulled inward from every alley and dock, folding into the staging yard and the reclaimed garden like something returned. There are faces you know too well and faces you don't, a hundred pale islands of expectation under rain-streaked banners. Your chest tightens and loosens in the same second, like a bell being struck and then held so its note can stretch.

Ari Navarro: "We built this with our hands. We mapped every inch, measured every seep and swell. This is not a stunt. It's a scaffold for the lives that remain here."

Narration
Your voice slides out over the demonstration and thins among the clatter—then finds purchase. Children kick muddy water against root mats and squeal; a volunteer points and explains, palms stained with peat. Dr. Noor stands near a dry-erase board pinned with graphs and a page torn at the corner and stained by rain—an old wound made data. She catches your eye and nods, small and steady.

Dr. Noor: (soft, precise) "Models are maps—helpful, but they were never the territory. We found where our simplifications hid risk. We corrected them. We asked questions we should have asked sooner."

Narration
Her words land like a hand on the back of your neck—practical and reconciliatory at once. You can feel the crowd leaning: not just listening, but recalibrating.

Narration
You choose, and the choice is small and bright: a private reinforcement of the public thing. Either way, the energy around you spikes—eager, razor-sharp. Sora moves through the crowd like a current, trading looks and quick hands. When they pass you, their fingers brush yours and the contact is a promise made of salt and work.

Sora Watanabe: (breathless, immediate) "They're listening. When I asked people to come, I told them we weren't here to beg—we're here to show what we already know how to do."

Narration
They climb a crate for a better view without asking permission, and the world contracts to that ascent. A hush gathers like netting tightening.

[Visual: Tight on Sora's face, hair catching the low light; their voice thrown outward is both intimate and combustible.]
[Music: Percussion snaps into a faster tempo; brass threads through the strings.]

Sora Watanabe: (shouting, then softer) "Look at them! These kids—this soil—this knowledge. You can't buy what we've learned with money. You can't shove it aside with permits and polite speeches. We are the sea's first responders, and we've been doing the work while meetings made plans."

Crowd Member: "Tell them, Sora! Tell them how to keep our homes!"

Volunteer: "Show us how to build more of these—teach us!"

Narration
They talk fast, hands chopping the air. Their voice is honest enough to cut and warm enough to glue bones together. People answer with calls—names of streets, names of grandparents, names of places the sea has already taken. The chant becomes a rhythm: evidence, memory, demand.

[Visual: Cut to Municipal Council Hall balcony—Lina Moreau stands framed by glass; her expression is unreadable, the placid plane of her face disturbed only by a tiny, almost-invisible nod.]
[Sound: A murmur of discontent near a cluster of well-dressed figures—Ephraim's developers—low, metallic voices like wind rustling over tin.]

Ephraim Developer: (murmuring) "This is theatrical. Our clients need certainty, not sentiment."

Ephraim Developer 2: "Yes, but—funding flows to spectacle sometimes. We should push for standardization."

Narration
Lina watches from above—ice-gray eyes tracking the crowd like a measurement. You don't know the private ledger of her calculations, the things she weighs that none of you get to see. Her jaw softens barely, a millimeter of concession that reads as both strategy and something like recognition.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Call up to Lina with a direct invite to tour the pilot after the vote") -> Reaction: "You shout up—your voice brittle but clear. Lina inclines her head, and for a moment you imagine the balcony as a bridge. People around you clap; Sora grins at the interruption and gives you a wink." || Choice B ("Keep your head down and let the crowd speak first; stay with the volunteers") -> Reaction: "You stay put, chestful of work. A volunteer tugs your sleeve and points to a dredged channel that held back a late tide. Staying feels like fidelity; you meet Sora's eyes and they hold your choice like a mirror." ]

Narration
The council convenes in quick, hyperbolic time—camera lights flare, press tents unfold, and arguments fly in short, scorching gusts. Dr. Noor steps forward at the podium with the calm of someone who has read and reread the wrong maps until the right ones arrive.

Dr. Noor: (firm, compassionate) "We made errors. We corrected them. That is the point of science—not perfection, but making better with what we have. This pilot shows living systems can reduce overtopping and preserve the shore's social fabric. We ask the municipality for conditional support: matched funding and oversight. We ask for a covenant—transparent governance that includes community representation."

Narration
You speak next because they look at you. Your Moleskine is a small island again; your voice is not the soft one you keep for private mourning but a now-hardened calm that carries.

Ari Navarro: (clear, unwavering) "This is not the only answer. It is, however, an answer built from the labor of this town. Give us oversight. Give us matching funds. Let the engineers and the people be partners, not opponents."

Narration
The vote is not instantaneous. It is a measured collapse into decision—murmurs, a brief exchange in the council, the rustle of pages. You feel your pulse in your throat, a drumbeat that the crowd picks up and echoes back.

[Sound: Silence—then a single authoritative knock of the gavel. The music drops to a single, high violin that threads into the wind.]

Council Chair: (resolute) "The municipal council grants conditional support to the Living Shoreline Pilot—on the terms of transparent community oversight and matched, phased engineering funding that respects living systems."

Narration
When the words land, the sound is like the first stormwave breaking on a defender that actually holds—loud, shocked, elated. People scream and clap; some weep. Sora jumps down from the crate and collapses into your arms with a laugh that has tears in it. Maya whoops and slaps you both on the back so hard salt sprays.

Sora Watanabe: (laughing, breathless) "We did it. We—God, we actually did it."

Ari Navarro: (softly) "We did it together."

Narration
Lina descends from the balcony slowly, the precise lines of her coat softened by the light. She moves through the crowd with a small entourage, and when she reaches you, there's a tiny, formal smile that doesn't erase the distance but narrows it.

Lina Moreau: (measured, almost tender) "This is a beginning. I expect the oversight committee's formation to include clear metrics. We'll craft phased funding windows and accountability checks."

Ari Navarro: (guarded, but firm) "And community seats on that committee."

Lina Moreau: (a beat) "Agreed. Transparency is required. No surprises."

Narration
Her words are a contract inked with necessity. You feel relief and caution in the same breath. The town will not be whole overnight, but the scaffolding is in place—legal, social, and human.

[Visual: Montage—volunteers laughing as they stitch reed mats; children dragging tiny wheelbarrows; Dr. Noor teaching a small group how to monitor sediment transport. The sun breaks briefly through a yellow-hazed cloud, lighting the wet grass like a promise.]
[Music: Full ensemble swells into triumphant, urgent chords.]

Narration
Night comes quick, and with it the work doesn't stop; it multiplies. Lanterns bob like tiny beacons. You and Sora take to repairing nets with practiced hands—your fingers roughened by rope and salt, moving in a rhythm that needs no speech.

Sora Watanabe: (quiet, intimate) "You were brilliant up there. You make the language of plans feel like it actually keeps kids safe."

Ari Navarro: (soft, admitting) "I learned from you—how to make people hear the risk. You taught me how to make it feel like a fight worth joining."

Sora Watanabe: (turning toward you; their voice a low flame) "We taught each other. That's the point."

Narration
Their hand finds yours in the dim light. There is no sudden, cinematic kiss—only the slow, precise fit of fingers and the way your pulse answers. The green fleck in your left iris catches a lantern, and for a suspended instant you see that green reflected in Sora's dark gaze.

Narration
The hours fold into a kind of liturgy: knot, stitch, catalog, call. Volunteers swap stories and sandwiches, and the air is full of salt and peat and hot tea. Maya hands you a thermos and grins like someone who has won twice—once for the vote, once because her wetland plants will be used properly.

Maya Cruz: (teasing) "Look at you two. Finally, people who know how to hold both a clipboard and a hand."

Narration
You laugh, which feels dangerous and necessary. The ache of past loss sits as a shadow you both acknowledge and keep at the edge. Tonight it is smaller because there are more hands holding the shore.

[Visual: Close on the living shoreline at dusk—the channels slow the tide; root mats bulge with trapped sediment. The sound of the sea is steadier now, less like a predator and more like a partner kept at bay by the town's collective effort.]
[Music: A single, sustained chord that resolves slowly into warm, consonant harmonics.]

Narration
The municipal clause is imperfect: conditional, bureaucratic, full of caveats. But it is real. The town has a lever now—a way to argue for its own future. You feel the weight of it, equal parts responsibility and relief. You let yourself imagine, cautiously, that the sibling you lost would have liked this—the stubborn, attuned kind of saving.

Sora Watanabe: (soft, sure) "We won't let them forget the nets or the names. We'll keep watch. Together."

Ari Navarro: (steady) "Together."

Narration
You stand beneath the washed oyster-gray horizon and for a long moment do not speak. Around you, Mariselle breathes with lamp light and tired laughter. It's not the end of the storm; it's the first harvest from seeds you planted in anger and devotion. The town is bruised, yes—but it's grafting new tissue. So are you.

[Visual: Fade to an intimate close—your hands, Sora's hands, the green fleck shining like a signal in the dark.]
[Music: The strings resolve into a final, warm cadence; percussion softens into the sound of distant waves.]

Narration
You stay until the last volunteer leaves, until the lanterns grow thin and the mud cools. Then you walk the boardwalk with Sora, mapping evacuation lines by flashlight, trading jokes that mean more than they sound. There is a steadiness between you now, a slow-heat kind of commitment that can survive argument and exhaustion, policy and tide.

Ari Navarro: (to Sora, quietly) "This—this is what I wanted. Not to make everyone safe by numbers alone, but to make safety something we could touch."

Sora Watanabe: (smiling) "And to make it worth fighting for."

Narration
You look at the town—at the children, at Dr. Noor with her field camera slung over her shoulder, at Lina conversing with the oversight coordinator in a voice that is, against your expectation, almost private. Ephraim's developers move away in small huddles, disgruntled but watching the new contract like creditors surprised into respect. The tangles of compromise and triumph sit together like salt and seaweed: messy but nourishing.

[Visual: The moon lifts like a coin over the water; the lights of Mariselle are scattered like stitches along a wound healing.]
[Music: A single, sustained note holds and then gently fades.]

Narration
Tonight is a harvest—of evidence, of trust, of a new governance that includes the hands that will have to fix what breaks. It will not shield you from every storm. It will not erase loss. But it stitches the town and you back into competence and connection.

You press Sora's hand—your fingers finding the familiar fit—and at the same time you press your palm to the braid of reeds used in the mats, feeling the literal fibers and the social ones binding together. The thought is quiet and enormous: you built something that might keep children here another year, another decade. You built a frame for care.

[Visual: Long shot of the shoreline—people moving like constellations; the town a work of living things and stubborn hands.]
[Music: Final chord resolves into silence.]

[ENDING]
