### SCRIPT
# Route: Route C (True Ending)
# Context: Start

[Scene: Rooftop Garden of Maia's Childhood House | Pre-dawn]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Music: Sparse, contemplative piano with distant low strings]
[Sound: Soft gull calls, a kettle clicking off somewhere below]

You wake before the town. The rooftop is cool—corrugated metal underfoot still holding the night's chill—and the rosemary breathes up the familiar, resinous scent that always catches the back of your throat and pulls a memory of your mother's hands into focus. Your hair is tied in the same messy bun it always is when you leave the house for long days: dark chestnut, salt-streaked at the tips, a crooked halo from a thousand windy walks along the bay.

Your Moleskine is heavy in the inner pocket of your jacket, pages edged with mud and margins full of shorthand tide notes. The brass compass on its chain catches the first thin light and gives a small, steady clink when you move. You run your thumb across the braided blue cord on your wrist—frayed, familiar—feeling the knot where you keep a promise to yourself. It steadies you the way a deep exhale steadies a boat in a swell.

You let the rosemary scent sink in. It tastes of sun, of your mother's laugh in the kitchen, of late afternoons stringing nets. It is small and perfect and, for a moment, the weight you carry is nothing but a ribbon tied around a wrist.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Tuck a sprig of rosemary into your jacket"
> Choice B: "Check the brass compass one more time"
[Outcome A: You press the sprig into the lapel of your field jacket. The green is warm against the fabric and for an instant your chest eases—an old, domestic ritual becoming a talisman.]
[Outcome B: You hold the compass in your palm. Its needle quivers to the bay and then stills. You let it click shut and imagine the map you'll draw today: channels, wrack lines, the margins of what remains.]
[MERGE: The morning continues and you head to the marsh.]

[Scene: Marisol Wetland Reserve | Dawn]
[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: Water whispering in tidal creeks, the soft scrape of boots on wood]
[Music: Low, rhythmic whoosh—wind through grass]

You move the way you always do through the marsh: slow enough to watch the water line, quick enough to beat the gulls to the latest wrack. Each board creaks under your boots like a statement: solid, then not. You measure loss the way other people measure time—one washed plank at a time, one nest displaced, one species' absence catalogued in a list you haven't yet learned to stop making.

Tomas's door is ajar two houses down; his voice rides the air—grainy, fond, lined by years of rain—before you see him. He stands on the low step, a figure that seems carved from the same weather the town has been made of: white hair stuck to his forehead with damp, a sweater stitched over broad shoulders.

Tomas: "Morning, Maia. You're up early as a gull."

Maia Rivera: "The gulls are wrong about dawn as much as they're right about fish, Tomas." 
You let a half-smile slip. It lands warmer than you expect.

Tomas: "Good. You'll want clear light for the creek mouths. The last storm did something ugly to the north bend."

Maia Rivera: "I'll start there. Did you—" 
Your voice pulls into the ways of the practical; you don't go further into what 'something ugly' means because you already know.

Tomas: "—I saw it. Been thinking of the boathouse, same as always." He makes a gesture toward the bay, callused fingers loose in the air. "Ain't the same, but it's—" He stops, the sentence folding into the water between you.

You don't need the rest. The memory of the boathouse—your father's cleat, the angle of the rafters as the storm took them—is a tide you pull under whenever you speak. Tomas watches you watch the bay, his face pulled into a soft kind of grief that is also a shield.

Tomas: "You're doing right, kid. Don't let the paperwork drown you this morning."

Maia Rivera: "I won't," you say, and you mean it. For now. The ledger is always waiting, but the marsh is immediate: reeds to map, creeks to measure, wrack to photograph.

[Scene: Boardwalk, further along the marsh | Moments later]
[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]
[Sound: Low chuckle, the slap of a net folding; gulls wheel, their calls punctuating the air]

Jonah Kade: "You're mapping the north bend? Thought you'd be at the old sluice."

Maia Rivera: "Sluice after the bend. The north has the most change—silt, new outflow channel. Might be where the eelgrass's edge moved." 
You tap your Moleskine, the pages protesting softly.

Jonah Kade studies the water, his sea-glass eyes narrowing against the pale light; the canvas cap shades him into thought. There's a rhythm to his face—measuring, weighing—and for a beat it's the same look your father had when deciding where to set a net.

Jonah Kade: "We lost half a string last week. Nets hung on a new snag. If the eels shift, the young won't find the channels we taught 'em."

Maia Rivera: "Then we'll map the channels and find new pathways together." 
You mean it like a plan, a thing that can be built from steps. "I'll flag the snag and make a recommendation for the cooperative."

Jonah Kade: (A small, soft exhale.) "You always do the map-and-fix thing. Works more than folks think."

Maia Rivera: "Only because I talk to enough people to know where the maps lie."

Jonah Kade: "Hm." He leans the brim of the cap back and looks at you full-on. "You been sleeping, Maia? You look like you haven't shut your eyes."

You feel the question more than hear it—less a prodding and more a careful weighing. His voice has the steady cadence of someone who knows tides and patience in equal measure. You could deflect with the professional posture you wear to meetings, but something in his look makes that feel thin.

Maia Rivera: "I've been awake. Thinking about survey schedules, grant letters—" The list spills out in the practical tone you've always used to make massive things feel like checkboxes. "And last year. The storm."

Jonah Kade: "The one that took the boathouse?" He doesn't say your name; it's not needed. "Tomas told me."

Maia Rivera: "It did." The word is small. The memory is briny and sharp. "I came back for the restoration."

Jonah Kade: "Smart." He shifts his weight, the boards creaking softly. "We'll check the lines together. Lupe's bringing coffee in town if you want." There is an offer in his tone, uncomplicated, solid. A small anchor.

Maia Rivera: "Coffee would be good." 
You let your shoulders drop an inch. The small kindness is not nothing.

Their exchange lingers; you both know the sea is a school of memory and livelihood, every conversation pulled taut between current and past.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Ask Jonah more about the snag"
> Choice B: "Accept the coffee offer and change the subject to the survey"
[Outcome A: You lean toward him and point to a shadow in the water. Jonah Kade nods, tracing a line with his finger on the wet board, and suddenly plans unspool—nets, markers, a time to pull the snag.]
[Outcome B: You say "coffee" like a treaty. Jonah Kade cracks the smallest smile, the kind that moves the stern set of his mouth, and the two of you shift into task mode: maps, markers, timeframes.]
[MERGE: You kneel to sketch a channel and continue the survey work.]

[Scene: On the boardwalk—You kneel to sketch a channel | Mid-morning]
[Background:"ch1_Start_4"]
[Sound: Pencil on paper, distant drills from a restoration staging site, gulls fading into town noises]
[Music: Sparse guitar harmonics, steady]

You catalog what you can see: the new outflow seam where last night's rain pushed fresh silt; a patch of wrack with unfamiliar debris. Your fingers are ink-stained and slightly numb from the cold. The Moleskine is a ledger of place—tide marks, species notes, shorthand for permissions and permits—each page a geography of what you've promised to guard.

Your inner voice surfaces, soft and insistent. You run it like a tape: the failed response. You remember a year that went wrong—radio silence when the storm changed course, a misread channel, a decision you made that sent volunteers out where they shouldn't have been. The guilt sits like a bruise beneath your sternum; you know its shape so precisely you can follow its edges in the dark.

Maia Rivera [Thinking]: "I should have seen the bend. I should have—" 
The sentence never finishes; guilt is not generous with absolution.

You catalog instead. The ledger is safer: it gives you control where the weather won't. If you map everything, if you bring the town the data it can use, then maybe you can prevent the next mistake. Maybe.

Your pocket buzzes. Ava. You pull your phone and see the message preview: tide gauge anomalies flagged; preliminary storm model attached. A thin line of cold slides up your spine—the same one that visits you when the sky changes color and the sea smells a certain way.

Ava (text): "Maia—early read on tide sensors. Gauge 3 spiking overnight. Sending model. Can meet at nine? This looks like a shift, not the usual swing. —A"

You feel a tightening at your wrist where the braided blue cord rests. Responsibility is a physical thing—heavy, like a wet net. You let the phone pulse in your hand for a beat before opening the file.

You open the attachment. The model is cautious, the numbers prefaced with caveats, but the trend is clear in the gentle gradient of reds along the map: an anomaly at the north bend tracking into the marsh. Your chest tightens not into panic—there's no time for that—but into that focused, surgical kind of pressure you know as work.

Maia Rivera: "On my way. Mapping north bend now. Meet at sluice in twenty?"

Ava: (She writes back in under a minute.) "See you. Bring yesterday’s sediment cores if you have them."

Maia Rivera [Thinking]: "Sediment cores. Of course."

You close your Moleskine and rub the knuckle of your thumb where the pages meet. The book is full of things that make sense when you measure them: distances, depths, recovery times. The guilt still lingers at the edges of every decision, but structure and protocol are tools that can blunt it.

You stand. The marsh is quiet, but alive with small noises that mean movement—eelgrass whispering, a crab skittering over a mud lump, the distant hum of a pickup on the harbor road. The town sleeps under a thin glaze of haze; you are the first to break its surface today.

[Scene: Boardwalk—You shoulder your bag, Moleskine tucked close | Late morning]
[Background:"ch1_Start_5"]
[Sound: The clasp of the Moleskine, Jonah Kade's soft instructions as he pulls out markers]
[Music: A gentle swell, the promise of momentum]

Jonah Kade: "If Gauge 3 keeps this up, we'll need to talk to the cooperative first thing. Lupe'll want the timeline." 
He hands you a roll of survey tape as if it is both an offering and an assignment.

Maia Rivera: "I'll get the cores and the model printed. We can do a quick community call—Mayor's office will want a heads-up." 
You make the list aloud; saying it gives it shape.

Jonah Kade: (Nods.) "Good. Tomas said he'd help flag the narrows if you need; he's been up early."

Maia Rivera: "Tell him I said thanks." 
You hesitate a fraction, then add: "And tell Lupe I'm bringing coffee."

He grunts, a small sound of warmth. "She'll lord it over you as payment."

You laugh once, short and real. The sound scatters like light over the marsh. For a moment the ledger and the weight are ordinary things again—tasks, assignments, people to call and coordinate.

But beneath that is the tightening you felt when Ava's message arrived: a slow, patient pressure that bends your spine toward action. It is not terror, not yet. It is the low, steady drum of duty that has been your compass since you came back.

You check your bracelet one last time. The braided blue cord bites into your skin. You close the Moleskine and slip it into your bag, resolve knotted in the spine like a seam.

You step off the boardwalk toward the sluice, toward the next set of measurements, toward the town that will slowly wake to what you already know. You do not yet have solutions—only work, and the people whose lives will hinge on whether you can translate that work into something they can hold.

[Music: The piano returns, low and hopeful; strings hover, patient]

You move with deliberate care. The morning stretches ahead, measured in planks and pages and coffee. The day will ask you for decisions in small, steady increments; you feel the arousal of obligation building, but it remains a practical urgency—focused, manageable, not frantic.

You take the boardwalk's last step and look back once—at the rooftop where rosemary still smells of home, at Tomas's door ajar, at Jonah Kade's silhouette against the bay. The world is not resolved. It will ask more of you. You tuck your hand into your jacket, around the Moleskine, and let the chord of resolve set: act, map, call, do.

[Page-turn thought: The tide gauge's spike is a small red smear on a screen. It will become a conversation, a meeting, possibly a dispute. For now, it is a data point and a summons. You lift your shoulders and go.]
