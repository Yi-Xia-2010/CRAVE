[Scene: Harbor and Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Weathered planks, nets sagging; gulls wheel against a silvered sky]
[Music: Distant, low cello—steady but thin]
[Sound: Boat engines idling, the slap of water against hulls, rope creaking]

You step back onto the boardwalk like a pendulum finding its arc—still, measured, carrying the weight of morning's decisions. The salt tang hangs in the air, braided with diesel and the metallic tang of bait. Nets slump on the docks like sleeping beasts, their knots holding more than rope: memory, livelihood, everything the bay has left to give.

Jonah walks ahead of you, shoulders set under a thick knit sweater pilled at the cuffs. The brass fisherman's ring on his hand catches the lamplight and goes blind in a small, private glow when he gestures. He doesn't look back until you fall into step beside him.

Jonah: "Catches been off. Not just smaller—different. Pale herring where mackerel used to be. Lupe's worried the quota math won't hold if the patterns keep shifting."

You listen. You have the data; Ava's charts live inside your tablet like a second shadow. But listening is different than solving. Jonah's voice has that low, practical cadence that translates grief into inventory.

You: "Adaptive quotas could buy the coop time—but only if Lupe's numbers line up with the samples Ava's been running. If the age classes are collapsing, we need a different fix."

Jonah: (pauses, frowning) "Different how? We ain't got time for experiments that take a season to show up. Folks need fish now, Maia."

His impatience carries more than hunger for income—there's the blunt edge of fear. He pulls a hand through his wind-rough hair as if to steady the world.

[Visual: Lupe approaches, ledger tucked under her arm; freckles, sun-leathered skin, quick smile]
[Sound: Paper shuffling, a bell over the fishmonger door]

Lupe waves the ledger like a pennant. Her voice is bright, a counterpoint to Jonah's gravity.

Lupe: "Maia! Jonah! Look—numbers are messy, but we can model rolling quotas. If we shift the harvest windows by…"

You watch her—energetic, relentless. Her optimism is practical, braided with urgency.

You: "Show me."

She flips the ledger. Ink runs into small, impatient charts. The page smells faintly of fish and old coffee.

Lupe: "If we set the quota seasonally, not yearly, we cushion the coop. It's not perfect, but it's something immediate."

Jonah: (skeptical) "Immediate's only good if it doesn't hollow us out."

You: (softly) "I know. But empty nets don't help anyone either. We'll test it. Pair it with the habitat work on the reserve—Ava's cores show recruitment pressure. If we shore up nursery grounds, the quotas won't feel like a stopgap."

Lupe brightens, a hopeful pull at her mouth.

Lupe: "That's the ticket. Tomas says we should patch nets too—fixing gear keeps smaller fish from slipping through, heh."

Ava's message buzzes at the edge of your sleeve—three new graphs, a few annotated sample IDs. Her handwriting reads like a beat: "look at 14B / temp spike?" You tap quickly, skim, and the lines on her screen make a small, private crack through the afternoon haze. The juvenile index drops half a tick in the estuary core. It's not catastrophic—yet—but it's a trending curve that will not be placated with good intentions.

You: (muttering) "Ava, run it again. Cross with salinity. If that temp spike's localized, we can adapt faster."

Ava (voice, over the tablet speaker): "Already on it. I'm out by the sluice now. Tomas dropped by—says he'll bring his logs. Also—Elias landed down at the staging area."

The name curdles the air.

[Visual: A glint of silver across the harbor; Elias Wren stands by a sleek container, trench coat clean against the mud]
[Sound: A metallic click—an AR pin blink—muffled murmurs from nearby fishermen]

You look toward the staging area. Elias Wren's trench is too clean, his hair too slick to belong to this place. The silver watch on his wrist catches the sun like a small interrogation light. He speaks with a couple of suits, gesturing at renderings on a laminated board. There is a mechanical rhythm to the way people cluster around him—equal parts curiosity and caution.

Jonah: (under his breath) "Great. The suits are back."

You: "He's got funding letters, the state's green stamp. He moves money—and he moves decisions."

Jonah: "They move 'em fast. Last time someone like that was here, they wanted us to cede a fishing lane in exchange for a flood wall. We were lucky we read the fine print."

You: (flat) "You were lucky. Luck won't keep the nets full."

The harbor narrows in your chest. Elias's presence isn't just an antagonist in a headline—it's a lever in real people's hands and futures. Mayor Rosa's office is a whisper away; you know how her balancing act teeters toward bargaining chips when funding is on the table.

Your phone buzzes—Mayor Rosa. When you see her name, the ledger of community expectations tilts again. She doesn't leave a message. She waits while you answer, which is the small courtesy of power.

Mayor Rosa: "Maia, thank you for taking this. The council wants you to present the data at tonight's meeting. People are worried—there's pressure to accept the Wren proposal."

You: (breath held) "Rosa, there's nuance to that proposal. The models—"

Mayor Rosa: (soft but firm) "We need someone who knows the bay. You know it better than most. Speak plainly. The town's looking for clarity."

She says it like a verdict and an entreaty at once. You feel the smear of a hundred petitions under your thumb; the map of the bay folds into corridors of consequence.

You: "I'll prepare. I'll pull the youngest samples and the storm runs. We need to be honest about risk and options."

Mayor Rosa: "Good. I'll see you at six. People are scared, Maia. They need a clear course."

After you hang up, Jonah's hand finds your elbow—steady, not intrusive. There's a moment where the world reduces to the pressure under his palm.

Jonah: "You can handle it. You always do."

You: (small, brittle laugh) "Somehow that's both comforting and a condemnation."

He looks away, the harbor light catching in his eyes; his voice softens like a tide pulling back.

Jonah: "My sister—Luz—she used to stand right where you're standing. She'd shout at the gulls and throw scraps to the pups. She never wanted anything fancy. Just steady work. We lost her in a storm. It—" (he breaks off, jaw tightening) "—it taught me what to keep. I'm not eager to hand that over to a drawing on a board."

You: (voice low) "I know. Loss writes itself into everything here. I'm sorry—for Luz."

Jonah: "Don't be. I get… defensive. I'm worried a shiny solution will take more than it gives."

You: "So am I. But I also see the models—Ava's spikes, the estuary cores. We need systems that don't just promise safety with strings attached."

The conversation curves, two hands feeling for purchase on an uneven ledge. You both know the bay in different dialects—his in the pull of nets, yours in tidal curves and sediment transport—but grief translates easily into suspicion.

[Sound: A low, distant rumble—meteorological servers humming; the town's weather feed updates]
[Visual: Your tablet screen flashes; a storm model ticks forward, a track line bending closer]

You pull up the storm model. The line that earlier skirted past the bay now arcs inward by several miles. It's not yet an alarm blaring red—it's a precise, clinical nudge toward urgency. Your notebook, open on your knee, fills with new lines: timelines, thresholds, community messaging points, likely infrastructure stress points. Each note you make is a small, private admission that time is less generous than you'd planned.

Tomas appears at the end of the dock, leaning on a cane carved with waves. He smells of boiled coffee and old wood. His voice is a weathered thing that carries stories the town keeps like talismans.

Tomas: "When the boathouse went, we thought the sea had taken a thing that belonged to itself. But what it takes it sometimes gives back differently. We can patch. We can hold. We also can't pretend the tide won't come higher."

You: "We won't pretend. We'll plan. We'll mobilize the nursery restorations, push for the rolling quotas, and—"

Tomas: (interrupting gently) "And you'll be pulled in three directions, child. Money from above, mouths to feed, storms to dodge. Remember the thing you can't trade away—who we are."

His words settle like a cloak that fits poorly—comforting, but weighty.

[Music: Cello deepens; a faint, insistent metronome underneath]
[Visual: The late sun lowers; shadows lengthen across the planks]

The arousal in your chest builds with a steady, inexorable pace: reports to write, a mayor to reassure, a cooperative to safeguard, a staging area across the harbor where a man with a clean trench coat smiles with proposal decks. You measure the pace of your fingers as you write the next line in your notebook. The ink beads; each word demands a response.

Lupe: "If there's a meeting, we need to show people a path that keeps the coop in control. Not just 'no'—a 'yes, and' with protections."

You: (nodding) "Agreed. Bring those points. Tomas—can you tell some of your boathouse stories at the meeting? People listen to you."

Tomas: (a small laugh) "You ask me to tell stories in my old bones? Fine. I'll tell them why we held the nets, and why we walked away from the wall that would have taken our channel."

The storm track continues to nudge inward as people around you make small, urgent motions—tying off tarps, calling relatives, checking generators. Elias walks along the edge of the staging area now, a figure that translates policy into promise. His suit jacket flashes when he glances at the harbor; his smile finds a journalist across the way. You watch him watch the town, and in the watching you feel a cold, corporate calculus—an efficiency that is not tuned to the music of the docks.

Your notebook takes another line: "Elias Wren—conflicts of interest? stipulations? conditional funding?" You circle it, the pen scratching loud in the near-silence.

Jonah: "Promise me you won't let some glossy plan undercut the people who actually work the bay."

You: (firm) "I won't. But I also won't close the door on resources that can help—if they're done right. We need safeguards, not slogans."

The sun drops closer to the horizon. The harbor's colors deepen into bruise blues and steel grays; the neon at the fishmonger begins to blink with thin, electric impatience. Your breath fogs small and white in the cooling air. The list in your head grows, and each item is a hinge: a vote, a modeling threshold, a community trust ledger you're trying desperately not to let overdraw.

You make one last note as the wind lifts lightly off the bay: timelines, talking points, an ask list for the mayor, objections to Elias's preliminary plan, a plea to the cooperative to consider rolling quotas with protections. The storm model refreshes again. The line creeps closer by a subtle margin that will feel enormous by night.

You close the notebook and feel the braid of your cord bracelet press into the inside of your wrist. For a breath, you imagine the rooftop garden back home—rosemary and the glow of sunset—and the sharp, soft ache of responsibility returns.

Page-turn thought: The meeting tonight will be the moment where politesse meets pressure. Words will have to carry not just data, but trust. You look across the harbor at Elias's clean trench coat, at Mayor Rosa's folded petitions, at Jonah's steadying presence—and you rise because there are no other hands to do the lifting.
