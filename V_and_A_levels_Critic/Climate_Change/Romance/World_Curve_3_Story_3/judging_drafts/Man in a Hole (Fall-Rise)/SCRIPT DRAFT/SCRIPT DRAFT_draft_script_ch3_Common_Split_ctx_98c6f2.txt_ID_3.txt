You step off the boardwalk of nerves and into the gym's harsh, humming light. The room smells of dust, strong coffee, and the salt you only think you can outrun. Your linen blouse is steady under the worn navy blazer; your Moleskine lies open at the podium like a placard: maps of marsh restoration mats, hand-sketched tide-lines and a column of numbers you have rehearsed until the digits taste of metal.

Faces lean in around you. Tomas's cane taps a patient, carved rhythm from the front row; Lupe balances a thick stack of the cooperative's ledgers; Dana's protest banner is folded in her arms like a wound she keeps touching; Mayor Rosa clutches petitions so tightly the edges are creased. Jonah stands near the windows, hands shoved into the pockets of his oil-stained jacket, sea-glass blue eyes hooded and unreadable. Elias Wren waits beside the projection screen—crisp, clinical, an AR schematic hovering as if the ocean could be solved with a clean line.

You feel the room as a living thing—tension like a skin stretched over bone. Ava sidles up to you and leans in, voice low enough that only you can hear. "The storm model updated," she says, voice thin with sleep. "Eight days. Track tightening along the shelf. If it shifts east, our low-lying blocks take level-three surge."

The words land in you like a fist.

[Visual: Close-up on your Moleskine—the ink smudged where your thumb has worried the paper]
[Sound: A chair scrapes loudly; that single noise pulls attention like a signal flare]

Elias: "Good evening. I know this is hard—I'll be brief," (he gestures; the projector switches to a glossy render of a seawall and an offshore renewable platform) "Our design reduces average flood heights by sixty percent in modeled scenarios. Funding is secured; construction can begin within months. This isn't about outsiders colonizing the coast—it's about preventing another—" (his tone flicks past memory to policy) "—preventing another catastrophe."

Dana: (cutting in, voice sharp) "Preventing catastrophe by erasing us. You bring machines and blueprints and call it salvation. Whose coast are you protecting if our fishing grounds are sealed off?"

Elias: (calm, practiced) "We engage the cooperative. There are workforce guarantees. We will place fish passages—"

Jonah: (leaning forward, voice quiet but carrying through the room) "Fish passages don't translate cleanly when currents shift. I run nets; I read currents. You change the bottom and you change the whole highway they use. You speak of guarantees like a promise—what you're offering is a trade. Who pays when the shoals leave town?"

Elias: (a beat; then) "Adaptive engineering has contingency. We model migration shifts and adjust—"

Jonah: "Models are paper; the sea laughs at paper."

You: (you want to interrupt, to fold the room back into order with a single clear sentence; your throat tightens) "Jonah—"

Jonah: (he looks at you, not unkind, as if they're waiting for you to anchor his warning in something the town can hold) "People need to know the price before the check is signed."

You choose composition over interruption—for now. The tide-lines glow. The room leans toward the rectangle of light as if seeking refuge in clarity. You point to restoration mats mapped along the marsh fringe, the low-tech interventions that buy time and can be built by local hands.

Maia Rivera: "These mats trap sediment; they raise the marsh edge slowly but meaningfully. They're cheaper, deployable within weeks, and they're done by people who know this land. We can buy time—"

Mayor Rosa: (raising a hand, voice flattened by exhaustion) "Time is what we need, Maia. Who pays? How many hands do we need? Does 'buy time' mean another decade of being exposed until the budget runs out?"

Lupe: (speaking fast, eager) "Local labor—coop members, young people. We can train crews. It's work that keeps the bay in our hands. Jobs are part of resilience."

Dana: "And what about scale? When the storm comes, will a mat mean anything compared to rock and concrete?"

Ava: (softly, from the side) "They don't replace structural protections. They slow erosion and preserve nursery habitat. They buy you options, but they don't erase a high-energy surge."

The debate ricochets through the room—technical counters and moral claims. Voices rise. The fluorescent lights hum louder in your ears. Tomas clears his throat and speaks, each word a small brick in an old foundation.

Tomas: "When I was your age—" (he pauses; the room hushes) "—we made things here with our hands and mouths, and when the sea took, we took each other's hands back. I'm not against science. I'm against having answers decided somewhere else."

Elias: (leaning in, unexpectedly earnest) "No one here wants control taken from Marisol. We need partnership models that respect tenure and local input. We can write that into agreements."

Dana: (bitter laugh) "Agreements that read like permission slips. How many times have we signed away our say for 'solutions' that arrive pre-packaged?"

Jonah: (to Elias, quietly fierce) "Have you walked the run of the tide with a net between your hands? Have you sat through a winter where the catch is nothing? Respect isn't a clause."

Elias: (strained) "I lost someone to a storm too. I promise you, I—"

Dana: (cutting, disbelief raw) "Then stop promising. Start listening."

[Sound: A low murmur swells; hands slap paper; some stand, others sit like stones]

Your chest feels like a bell—guilt thrumming in the hollow. This is where your failures and your duty meet: the memory of the boathouse you couldn't save; the tide gauge's red spike stubbornly present in your mind. The storm Ava flagged is a timer you cannot stop. Eight days. The room warps into urgency: policy, memory, livelihoods, and a corporate machine with tidy charts.

You can sense the energy coalescing into lines in the sand—literal and metaphorical—and you know the moment to pick which line will guide Marisol is now. But before you commit, a softer, smaller choice surfaces—how to frame your voice to the town.

You feel the metronome in your ribs—one, two, one—Ava's model, Tomas's cane, Lupe's ledger, Jonah's nets. The air tastes of iron and coffee and salt as arguments become pleas and pleas become demands. The projector hums, Glory-point images of engineered structures rotating like planets indifferent to the tiny griefs below.

Mayor Rosa: (voice weary, edge of command) "We don't have a month to wait if a storm tracks in eight days. Maia, people will look to you for a stance tonight. This body needs direction."

Elias: (softening, an attempt at alliance) "If you're open to negotiation, we can formalize protections now. We can draft—"

Dana: (almost spat) "Formalize protections the same way they formalized contracts that took our shoreline access? No. Not without teeth."

Lupe: (urgent) "If we refuse now, we might starve the town of jobs and money we need to keep people here."

Jonah: (to you, quietly) "Whatever you do—don't let them sell us hope when it's a mortgage on our coast."

You look out over the crowd—faces of your neighbors lit by projector light, the elderly, the fishermen, the young faces of kids who will inherit whatever choices are made. Your hands tremble at the podium. Your Moleskine is open to a page where a dozen small plans are written in neat, hopeful ink. But the room's atmosphere is not hopeful; it's charged, sharp, angry, and drifting toward collapse. The emotional pressure has been building—voices layered, accusations thrown like loose netting—and in your chest guilt is no longer a background note but the drumbeat driving you toward a choice that cannot be undone tonight.

Your heartbeat ratchets up: this is not a measured meeting anymore; it's a last call before a storm. You can feel the high arousal—heat in your face, sweat at your collarbone, the tilt of every listener waiting for the syllable that will pick a path.

You inhale. Your voice will decide a direction for Marisol Bay.

[Visual: The projector dims as everyone quiets for your turn; the gym seems to hold its breath]
[Sound: The hum of the projector drops, the fluorescent buzz feels louder—then a pin-drop hush]

You step forward to the microphone, knuckles whitening on the podium. Your eyes scan the crowd once—Tomas's steady gaze, Lupe's raw hope, Dana's ire, Jonah's unblinking stare, Ava's thin, determined face—and you make the decision you have been circling toward in your head.

I call for an immediate community-led restoration plan, rejecting the corporate proposal.
I open formal negotiations with Elias for a partnership, seeking concessions.
