[Visual: Fluorescent lights buzz over folding chairs; a projection screen glows with a paused slide as people crowd the aisles]
[Music: Strings scratch into a taut, urgent tremolo; percussion begins a mounting, irregular heartbeat]
[Sound: The hum of a generator through the walls, a distant gull cry swallowed by wind]

You cross the painted floor with your Moleskine folded beneath your arm like a shield. The decision you birthed back at the sluice is still warm in your bones—expose, you told yourself—so you brought the audit into the light. The braided blue cord at your wrist catches on the edge of your sleeve; your compass pendant drags cold against your sternum and steadies you like a metronome.

Ava stands at the laptop, fingers trembling only a little, and the first slide blooms on the screen: sediment maps, annotated transects, clause excerpts that smell of legal ink and old promises. The projection is clinical, ruthless. The gym smells of dust, damp coats, and too-strong coffee. Voices ricochet off the bleachers; someone laughs too loud and it falls into the same space as the generator's cough.

You place your palm flat on the podium. Your voice arrives small—thinner than you meant—before you force it into even cadence.

You: "Good—good evening. Thank you for coming. Ava and I have something the town needs to see."

Ava: "I'll cue the figures on your mark." (She doesn't look away from the screen; her jaw is set.)

You inhale and let breath shape the rest. You speak the numbers as if they were rows of plants needing tending: tidal ranges, projected inundation lines, the sediment loss the consultants didn't highlight. Each fact clicks into the room like stones into a jar.

You: "This audit—Ava's audit—shows where the firm's impact assessments omit critical migration corridors and where contractual clauses allow for relocation of fishing zones without community consent." (You feel your teeth catch on the last word. You keep speaking.)

Dana is already standing before you're finished—her arms a ribbon of motion, her voice a match struck.

Dana: "We demand a halt. We demand transparency now!" (Her words are a bell. Teens stand, phones raised like lighters at a concert.)

From the bleachers a chorus answers—some hoarse with fury, some trembling with the weight of livelihoods.

Mayor Rosa: (pale, paper petitions slipping half from her lap) "Please—please. We must be measured. There are families who depend on—"

Elias rises from the front table like a black tide. The lights pick up the tight line of his jaw, the sterile sheen of his lapel AR. He doesn't look surprised; his expression is the practiced cool of someone who shapes headlines for breakfast.

Elias Wren: "This is sabotage," he says, voice calm enough to be more dangerous. "Selective data, leaked out of context. You know the implications of misleading a regulatory review, Maia—"

You: (Your name in his mouth makes the room shrink. The compass at your throat is heavy; your chest trembles with a remembered storm.) "My name is Maia Rivera." (You let the correction land, precise and unignorable.) "The data are complete. Ava will walk through methodology after I finish the highlights."

Ava moves the slides with a practiced hand and the room leans forward. A section of contract, blown up in ugly clarity, sits between the coastline maps and the population displacement models. Clauses that permit phased access changes, indemnities that hedge corporate responsibility—words that sound like paper until people feel what they mean.

Elias doesn't retreat into rhetoric—he pivots. He brings up counter-analyses, a clean slide with smoothed projections and corporate seals. His team murmurs soft assurances about mitigation and job creation. Their language is polished enough to shine; their slides have the sterile confidence of things designed to be believed.

Elias Wren: "We offer stability. These models are peer-reviewed. The funding is immediate. Delaying risks federal withdrawal. You ask for scrutiny and you offer paralysis."

You feel the room swing. The left side—fisherfolk, older faces, Jonas's crew—gives a low, fearful sound. The right—students, activists, Dana’s cohort—cheer. Noise multiplies, a foam of overlapping urgencies. The fluorescent lights hum in time with your pulse.

Jonah moves through the crowd with the slow economy of someone who works with ropes and tides. When his sea-glass eyes find you they are wider than usual; danger sits there, and something else—fierce, brittle loyalty. He places himself half between you and Elias, not as a wall so much as a question.

Jonah Kade: (low) "You sure this is the only way?" (His voice is rough as nets.) "We stop funding now—what happens to Lupe's ledger? What happens to the guys who fix nets in the morning?"

You: (You could hear the history in his doubt—losses that taught him when to hold fast. You meet his gaze and the gym narrows until all that exists is two sets of breathing.) "I know what this costs." (You name the ledger, the nets, the hungry mornings as if listing bones.) "I also know what it costs to let promises erode the things they claim to protect."

Jonah hesitates, then nods because his trust in you is a different kind of tide—slow, returning. He doesn't applaud; he simply stands there, steady, a presence the room can read as both warning and support.

Ava: "The sampling chain is unbroken. The transects—" (She clicks, zooms, points.) "—here, here and here. You can see the migration corridor shifting northward by three hundred meters in five years. The firm's mitigation profile only contemplates artificial reefs—no wetland buffer, no community consultation schedules."

A man near the front—one of the contractors who'd been hired for the initial phase—stands abruptly, face red.

Contractor: "You want to freeze everything? People were hired! Supplies bought! We have crews on contract—"

Dana cuts across him with a voice like a bell.

Dana: "Then pay the crews through the pause. Don't sacrifice people's livelihoods to keep broken promises."

Noise rises again. Someone starts counting off impacts—short-term jobs, delayed payments, a school fundraiser that needed the grant. The arithmetic of immediate survival presses against the ethics of long-term protection. The gym is a pressure vessel that creaks under both weights.

Mayor Rosa's hands are small and quick. She smooths petitions, straightens, then looks at you with an expression that is nearly unformed—equal parts betrayal, relief, exhaustion.

Mayor Rosa: "This will be messy." (She inhales.) "Regulators will need to be notified. We may lose the first tranche of funds while they review. We may—" (Her voice shakes at the edges.) "—we may have to find interim work for people. We need a plan."

You feel the room tilt. This is the instant the world splits forward: oversight and delay, potential safety for the bay, and the immediate sting of livelihoods interrupted. The generator hiccups like a timed drumbeat; a fluorescent tube pops with a small, sputtering crack.

Elias's team is already composing statements. He leans in, voice narrower than before.

Elias Wren: "You leaked this information. You endangered timelines with an irresponsible release. If regional partners pull funding, the climate mitigation this project would provide disappears. Is that your aim?"

You: (You let the accusation hang, and it sounds quieter than his syllables warrant. You are tired of being asked to trade principle for expediency, but you also carry the image of hungry nets, of docks turned to mud.) "No." (Short, sharp. It lands.) "My aim is accountability. To make the decision about our coast with facts, not curated narratives."

A flash of camera light, somewhere in the back, makes the room blink. A reporter stands, microphone like a blade.

Reporter: "Ms. Rivera—are you suggesting the firm intentionally withheld data? Have you filed these findings with regulators already?"

You: (The leak lives now in the air—Ava's secure transfer to contacts, the set of emails you sent before you walked in.) "Yes. We alerted the state regulators and provided copies to trusted press contacts. This is civic duty."

The words rip the room open. Some people cheer—hands slap seat-backs—relieved that someone named the wrongness. Others look away, mouths working, calculating consequences. Contractors murmur about missed payroll; a woman near the door clasps a child closer.

[Sound: A sudden swell in the music—brass and percussion collide; the pace triples]
[Visual: Close-up on your hands shaking at the podium, then on Jonah's jaw clenching; dust motes spin in the florescent light like frantic planets]

You stand exposed—literal and otherwise. Your hair has come loose at the nape, a dark curtain with salt-light at the tips, and your compass swings a tiny, ridiculous circle against your sternum. The decision you made ten minutes ago stretches outward and multiplies.

A voice near the front calls your name: "Integrity," someone says, and the word threads through the crowd like a flag. A few mouths form a chorus: "Integrity. Integrity."

But the other chorus—fear, practical need—answers back with equal volume. The town is split along lines you can read in the furrows of people's faces.

Jonah steps close enough that you share breath. He doesn't argue now; he simply quiets.

Jonah Kade: (low) "You did what you had to." (There is no praise in it, only the heavy acceptance of consequences.) "I'll stand with you on this."

You feel lighter in a way you hadn't expected—like a weight lifted though the air around you buzzes as if charged. The future is uncertain; there will be legal wrangling, angry phone calls, families counting days until paychecks. There will be storms that do not wait for paperwork.

You: (You look out over the gym—at faces you know and faces you don't—and the tide of responsibility rises in a new pattern. For the first time since the boathouse, you feel an ethic lining up with action. You chose principle tonight. That choice is a compass that, whether it points to ruin or repair, points true.) "We will work on interim support for those affected. Ava, help me draft immediate outreach. Mayor—let's coordinate with the regulators on a transparent review process."

Mayor Rosa nods, slow and exacting, the kind of nod that carries municipal weight.

Mayor Rosa: "I'll call the state. We'll set up hotlines. We'll ask for bridge funding."

Elias watches this with a look that is not purely defeat; it's recalculation. He already speaks into a device with soft, efficient commands.

Elias Wren: (quietly, to himself more than to you) "Pause the contractors. Rework the press line."

You: (The pause feels enormous. For a moment the gym is only the thin layer of air between accusations and the next step.)
