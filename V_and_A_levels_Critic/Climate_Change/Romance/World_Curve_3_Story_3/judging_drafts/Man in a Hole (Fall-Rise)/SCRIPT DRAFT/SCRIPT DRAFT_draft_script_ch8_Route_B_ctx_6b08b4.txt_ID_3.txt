The Moleskine lies open between you and Jonah like a small battlefield: margins scrawled, clauses crossed out and re-scribbled until the paper feels soft and apologetic. Your finger finds the braided blue cord around your wrist and the simple knot gives a steady, private weight. Nights have become a sequence of tiny combustions — one clause, one concession, another set of redlines — and sleep keeps slipping its deadline.

Jonah's brow knits over a paragraph of dense legalese. The rooftop's copper light strips his face into planes you know by habit. He inhales and you hear the scrape of paper in the wind.

Jonah Kade: "This is one hell of a sentence. Who writes sentences this long on purpose?" (dryly)

You: (You let a small, brittle laugh out, the kind that comes when the body's tired of being brave.) "Lawyers do. Then they hide the hard parts in commas."

Jonah looks up, eyes catching the distant harbor lights where site trailers blink like an expectant crowd. He taps a line with a callused finger.

Jonah Kade: "Independent fishery studies. Seats for the co-op on the board. Audits written into the deployment schedule. You put everything people asked for in here."

You: (You feel the list in your chest — the same list that wakes you at two in the morning. It matters not because it reads well but because it will be read later, by hands that will measure your choices against tides.) "I put what we can make stick, not what we'd wish. There's a difference." (firm)

Jonah Kade: "Sticking isn't always fast," he says. "You know that. Lupe sent me the payroll numbers today. If this drags another month... people will start talking about leakage — about choosing a different line to float on."

You: (You hear the arithmetic of survival threaded through his worry. It's never abstract with Jonah; it's always a net that either holds or tears.) "I know. I also know what gets sold as 'speed' sometimes strips the harbor's guts out. That's why the seats matter." (quiet, pleading)

Jonah studies you, then the paper, then your hands. For a long beat he says nothing — an unspoken negotiation across years of shared shoreline.

Jonah Kade: "Then we make the seats harder to take. Put quorum rules in. Make audits with teeth. If you're asking the bay to trust a phased roll-out, you don't let it become a blank check."

You: (Ava's edits run through your memory — the language she'd suggested, the clauses she cross-checked until she nodded like a judge. You nod now, not because you have the energy to convince, but because the work still needs a shape.) "Quorum. Teeth. Independent audit. And—" (you hesitate, hands twitching) "—a clause that requires cooperative sign-off on any alterations to fish migration paths."

Jonah's jaw tightens. He presses his thumb to his lip, reading for more than words.

Jonah Kade: "And if they push back? If Elias hides the change behind 'efficiency' or 'safety' language?"

You: (You picture Elias's neat coat under LED light — the sleek edge of his confidence. You picture his team in the staging area, tablets glowing; you picture the contractors already whispering timetables. The pressure tightens, as if the sky itself leaned closer to listen.) "We force transparency. We name the penalties. We make the first phase contingent on benchmarks not just of build speed but of ecological signal. If those fail, the next phase cannot start."

Jonah Kade: "So you want slow to make it safe, and some speed to keep the town afloat. That's a narrow seam."

You: (You know how narrow; you have been walking its edge for months.) "It's the seam we have."

[Scene: Rooftop Garden | Late Night — Small Table, Lamp]

Ava arrives with a thermos and a sheaf of printouts so crammed with notes the edges are feathered. She moves with the exactness of someone who has chased footnotes until they surrendered.

Ava: "I ran the coastal-run scenarios again. If they agree to phased deployment with independent sensors we can at least get baseline metrics in the ground before the heavy stuff goes up." (matter-of-fact)

You: (Ava's voice steadies the math in your head; numbers translate to possible futures.) "And if they refuse sensors?"

Ava: "We have leverage if we make the first tranche purely observational. No barriers, no permanent fixtures, just monitoring platforms they're contractually obligated to fund. If the data shows adverse trends, phase two can't proceed."

You: (You circle a phrase with a pen until the paper softens under it.) "Include a clause: 'Verified signal threshold suspends advancement.' I want the trigger spelled out, not hidden in generalities."

Ava: (nods) "I'll draft thresholds. We can tie them to fishery indices and marsh inundation frequency."

You: "And the governance seats?"

Ava: "Mandate two seats for local co-ops; require a rotating chair every eighteen months. If the company balks, make sponsorship funds contingent on cooperative representation."

You: (Ava's pragmatic churn is what keeps you from romanticizing the fight.) "Good. Add language that prohibits unilateral subcontracting for ecological components. No outsourcing the hard parts to labs we've never met."

Ava: "Done." (she taps a tablet) "I've also flagged where Elias's team replaced 'shall' with 'may'. It's small but it's the hinge."

You lean forward until the lamp's circle feels almost surgical. Sleeplessness has given your thoughts a sharpness that sometimes feels useful and sometimes feels dangerous.

[Scene: Community Center Negotiation Room | Dawn — Fluorescent Lights, Folding Chairs]
[Visual: A wide shot of the gym-turned-hall; banners hang, fluorescent buzz]
[Music: High, metallic — a building crescendo]
[Sound: Rain now a box of pebbles on the roof; muffled chants from outside seeping in thinly]

Mayor Rosa sits at the head of the table like a hinge between two doors: weary, precise, hands folding petitions into smaller, urgent shapes.

Mayor Rosa: "We will be civil. We will be direct. We will ensure the town's voice is heard." (soft, unflinching)

Dana's presence is a current outside the room — hoarse voices through an open door, banners creasing in the wind. Lupe sits beside Jonah, printouts spread like a ledger of hope and need.

Lupe Kade: "Payroll shows a two-month buffer if the cooperative takes on certain maintenance. We can manage if jobs start in phase one. But if they're fronting capital on terms that bleed the coop dry in year two—"

You: (Lupe's counting is quick and clear. Her hands find the numbers the way Jonah finds the safest line through a shoal.) "We need revenue-sharing spelled out. No back-loaded compensation that assumes goodwill."

Elias Wren enters with his team in a sweep of tasteful rain-slick collars. His briefcase is a polished universe of folders. He smiles like someone delivering facts.

Elias Wren: "We've reviewed your language. There are concessions we're prepared to make. Modular deployment allows risk management and operational learning. An independent audit clause is reasonable, and we're willing to fund the first phase's monitoring."

You: (He says 'reasonable' like a measurement. You listen for what the word hides.) "We need the governance seats spelled into the contract, not an addendum."

Elias Wren: (a small nod, then cautious) "We can agree to cooperative representation on the governance board, within a framework that preserves operational integrity. Too many hands on short timelines risk efficacy."

You: (You feel the sentence — 'preserve operational integrity' — crease like a map toward concession.) "Operational integrity cannot mean erosion of local rights. You will not unilaterally determine what constitutes 'efficacy' when it directly affects migration corridors."

Elias Wren: (his tone cools with something like respect sharpened into negotiation) "We specify benchmarks. We bind the phases to performance indicators. We include an audit clause carried out by a mutually agreed third party."

You: "Mutually agreed is only as good as the process to agree. Add a procedural clause: if parties cannot agree on an auditor within fourteen days, a preselected panel of neutral scientists is empowered to appoint one."

Elias's hand hovers, then writes a note into his pad.

Elias Wren: "Fourteen days is tight in procurement terms."

You: "It's not procurement; it's people's livelihoods."

Jonah leans forward, voice low but firm, and the room feels suddenly smaller.

Jonah Kade: "Elias, we won't trade our fishing grounds for ephemeral 'efficacy.' You come here and say you're saving coasts, but if you take the bay's soul in the process, what's left to save?"

Elias Wren: (a trace of something like flicker passes — a human pause — before he masks it) "I'm asking for measured steps. Immediate, wholesale rejection risks long-term loss if we don't act quickly."

Dana's voice from the doorway pierces the room like a flag.

Dana Park: (shouting, voice raw) "We need action, Maia! We need protection now! Not slow dances with corporations!"

Mayor Rosa: (raising a hand to calm) "Dana, we hear you, but this table is to negotiate specifics."

Dana pushes both ears and eyes open in the crowd — a collective pressure the table can't ignore.

You: (Your chest tightens. The storm's rhythm in the window aligns with the cadence in your veins. Every sentence you sign will be read and problematized by history. You can feel the future folding around the present like paper.) "You offered a phased approach," you say to Elias, voice steady despite the tremor undercutting it. "Spell the phases. Spell the audit triggers. Spell the governance seats and their transfer of power. Make the first phase purely observational unless benchmarks are met. Put penalties for breach in plain language."

Elias Wren: (slowly) "We can outline modular deployment with an audit clause. Phase one observational. Benchmarks before phase two. Cooperative seats. Funding contingent on meeting metrics. We can draft—"

A contractor's voice rings faint — superimposed on the wet sky — a vanishing promise of heavy machinery arriving if the paper clears.

You: (Everything shrinks to that frame: a clause preventing unilateral subcontracting, teeth in the audit clause, penalties spelled in dollars and operation suspensions. You imagine future hands opening the folio, fingers tracing the ink. You imagine Tomas's face, carved with weather, asking if you chose the town or a tidy timeline.) "And the penalties," you press, "must be automatic, not discretionary. No 'may be' on enforcement."

Elias Wren: (a slow exhale) "We can do automatic suspensions tied to signal failure. But you must accept that some delays will occur."

You: (The word 'delay' tastes like compromise and like a chance for the storm to take advantage.) "Delays that prevent harm are not failures."

Mayor Rosa: "We need a decision today. Contractors are scheduled. The procurement window closes."

The fluorescent lights hum like a held breath. Outside, the protest's rhythm accelerates; inside, the legal language tightens into a noose of options. Your hands rest flat on the table, fingertips against a stack of pages that will decide whether the town keeps a seat at the table or trades it for a promise that glints like a contract.
