[Scene: Harbor and Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Weathered planks, nets draped like sleeping beasts; a smear of neon from the fishmonger at the frame's edge]
[Sound: Distant gulls, low diesel hum, the creak of rope against timber]

You follow Jonah along the boardwalk, the salt tang heavy on the back of your tongue. His sweater is a map of years on the water—pilled, softened at the sleeves. The brass fisherman's ring at his knuckle catches the lamplight in a dull, steady glint.

Jonah: "Catches are spiking and collapsing same week. One morning it's schools thick enough to drag nets like canvas, next it's like pulling hair." (He spits into the harbor, eyes on the water.) "Lupe ran the ledger. She's worried."

You walk with your hands in your jacket pockets because there are too many things to hold at once: the Moleskine sits behind your ribs like a steady heartbeat; the braided blue cord around your wrist feels tight, familiar. The harbor smells of diesel, fish, wet wood—comfort and warning braided together.

Lupe appears at the rail, ledger clutched to her chest, sun freckles in a constellation along her nose. She bounces on the balls of her feet, all urgency and hyperspeed competence.

Lupe: "Maia! Jonah! Quotas—we need adaptive ones. Static limits don't work with these swings. We can shift by size, season, tide. We can protect the brooders without shutting everyone down." (Her hands sketch plans in the air, or maybe they're tracing the wear in the wood.)

You: "Adaptive could work if we map with the spikes. Ava's samples show younger cohorts in the shallows—"

Lupe: "—which means the nets are where they need to be, or they were. But if migration's changing, we miss them. Or worse, we crush a recovering cohort."

Jonah folds his hands, fishing calluses worrying the ring. His voice is practical, but there's a tightness underneath.

Jonah: "Adaptive needs enforcement, too. Quotas are nothing if half the fleet can't afford downtime. Lupe—what's the cooperative looking at for compensation or rotating days?"

Lupe: "Emergency fund is threadbare." (She laughs, but it's thin.) "We can push for tariffs on larger distributors, but that's political. We can push for microgrants if Maia's numbers help prove need."

You run your thumb along the edge of the ledger; paper is a poor shield against policy, but it's where the town's lives keep score. Your head hums with calculations—spawn timing, tidal windows, economic bleed. The afternoon light is soft and diffuse, like everything has been bleached of certainty.

Lupe presses the ledger closer. Her enthusiasm is practical, fragile. She looks at you with the fierce faith of someone who needs proof to believe in tomorrow.

Lupe: "If you can show the trend—if you can show that adaptive quotas would stabilize income over seasonal cycles—Mayor will have less wiggle room."

You feel the tug of every meeting you haven't had yet: community center chairs, fluorescent lights buzzing, the tilt of petition stacks on Mayor Rosa's desk.

[Sound: Your phone buzzes — a flat, precise note. You flip it. AVA: SAMPLE UPDATE — NEW TEMPORAL PATCHING COMPLETE.]

Ava's message blooms across the screen: graphs, annotated vials, a thin, efficient note. You tap through—salinity profiles, juvenile densities, a new uptick in nearshore temperatures. The numbers aren't dramatic on their own; their implication is. You can hear the tightening in your own chest that is never quite absent these days.

You: "Ava's got a patch. The nearshore temps bumped an entire band. That explains the scatter in the catches."

Jonah: (Quiet.) "My brother used to say the sea told stories. Lately, it's been whispering threats instead." (He looks away, toward the narrows.) "We lost him to the swell. You know that."

You feel Jonah's voice flatten into a single, worn scar. It softens him—makes him human and frightened in a way the ledger never can.

You: "I remember. I—" You hesitate. Words should be a bridge; they often feel like rafts with holes. "I'm sorry, Jonah."

Jonah: "Don't. We were young and stupid in that storm. You can't fix what's already been taken. You can make sure the next one doesn't take more."

The harbor seems to hold its breath. A gull cries, a rope slaps, a distant engine coughs. Tomas's voice floats from a bench where he's been watching the tide, a slow rumble like sea glass.

Tomas: "My boathouse stood two storms longer than it should've—old nails, better luck. I kept a lantern in the rafters for years, waitin' for the boys to come home." (He chuckles, but the laugh catches.) "We learned to patch and to pass the knowledge down. But patching only goes so far when the water keeps rising."

You move closer to Tomas; the texture of his sweater is wool and salt and memory. He offers you a hand-callused palm, and you take it. His grip says: history matters; grief matters; fishing matters.

You keep moving because there are always more steps. As twilight lowers, a figure appears at the far end of the jetty—trench coat too clean for the mud, hair black and slicked back like an invoice. He is accompanied by two people carrying a case that clicks with tech in the same precise rhythm you keep for surveys and samples.

Elias Wren stands too straight for the harbor. His silver watch flashes when he checks the time; his AR lapel pin flickers with schematic lines no one in Marisol needs until they decide to. People turn, a tide of murmurs rising. There's an automatic tilt of shoulders away from his polished confidence—defensive, wary.

You watch him from a distance, cataloguing the difference: his trench coat, the hum of projection tech, the confidence of someone who believes money and models can outrun the sea.

Jonah: (Quiet, contained) "That's him. Elias Wren. Hearing he's here already makes the rounds. He brings money and a plan. That watch of his will tell him the optimal stage for his pitch. For us, it tells how fast our lives get rewritten."

Lupe's hands hover over the ledger, fingers suddenly unsure. Tomas squints, weathered face folding.

Tomas: "They bring rope and promises. They built a town like a clock and think a clock can fix tide."

Jonah: "We've had outsiders before. They talk big about efficiency. They talk small about what gets left behind."

Your phone vibrates again—Mayor Rosa. Her name blooms on the screen, and you answer with practiced calm.

[Sound: Phone pickup, muffled harbor ambience on the line]
Mayor Rosa: "Maia, darling, do you have a minute? I need you to speak at the town meeting tomorrow. People are scared and they want facts. You're the one who can make sense of Ava's graphs and Lupe's ledger together."

You hear the rustle of petition stacks even through the line; she balances petitions like a litany.

You: "Mayor Rosa, I can do that. But if Wren's pitching while the meeting's going, people will hear money before method."

Mayor Rosa: (Measured.) "I know Elias is here. He has funding offers on the table. He asked to present. I'm trying to be fair, but there's pressure—state inspectors, donors. We can't ignore options we don't understand."

You think of the ledger, the ledger's thin margins, the way Lupe's optimism looks fragile under fluorescent lights. You think of Tomas's boathouse and Jonah's brother and the way the water licks at foundations.

You: "Make the forum structured. Time limits. Panel moderators. No backroom promises under the table."

Mayor Rosa: (A soft sigh.) "I'll try. Maia—people listen to you. Bring them the data, and as always, remind them why we still live here."

You: "I will. I'll be there."

You end the call with a weight added to your shoulders—less physical, more the kind of responsibility that arranges your lungs around worry.

[Visual: The light thins; the neon flickers brighter against the salt haze]
[Music: Cello strings rise a half-step; the tempo nudges forward]

Ava pings you again, urgent this time. Her annotation shows a storm model shift—an ensemble run nudged toward your latitude; the cone pulls closer. The data is clinical, a sequence of probabilities rendered in cold colors, but your body reads it like a table of casualty lists.

You flip the Moleskine open and your pen finds its rhythm—times, names, tasks, potential allies, possible compromises. The lines multiply like tide rivulets; your handwriting gets smaller as the list becomes a map of pressure points.

Jonah watches the screen you hold as if he could read the weather off your face.

Jonah: (Soft.) "If the storm hits near the projected window, the narrows get mean. Boats will get pinched, nets tore up. We need staging plans. Evacuation for the shacks along Pier Three."

You: "We need the town hall prepped for a meeting that isn't just for show. We need defensible proposals and backup plans. And we need to make sure whatever Elias offers doesn't hollow us out."

Tomas: "You always make the room for the boats and the bones. Don't forget to make one for the kids, too. They'll inherit the tide."

The pressure rises in the way steady work piles into an avalanche. It's not panic—you're still making lists, still placing calls—but the accumulation is unmistakable. Each new line in your notebook is a decision waiting like a coiled rope.

You look up. Elias moves through the crowd with practiced charisma, silver watch flicking, AR schematics floating briefly for those who accept the projection. Faces divide: some curious, some tightly closed.

You feel Jonah's presence beside you like a buoy—stable, not complacent. He meets your eye and you both know what comes next: a town meeting that will place lines in the sand, choices that might split loyalties, money pitted against memory.

Your notebook fills another column. The storm cone's edge overlaps the town's name on the map. The light narrows; the harbor's sounds sharpen into purpose. There isn't time to soften everything into consensus.

Page-turn thought: The town hall will be a room full of breathing stakes—arguments, grief, petitions, and proposals. You can already smell the coffee and the fear. You flip the page and make another line.
