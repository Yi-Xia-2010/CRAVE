[Music: Urgent strings, brisk percussion]

Narration:
You left the town hall with your choice raw in your mouth like seawater—no corporate partnership, the room heard you and a dozen faces rearranged themselves around that refusal. This morning the decision turns from speech into soil. The Moleskine is folded against your ribs; the tide-break berm sketches scrawl beneath your thumb like a plan you both trust and fear.

You tie your dark chestnut hair higher than usual, knotting it into a tighter bun. The olive field jacket fits like a promise and a shackle—patched elbows, frayed cuffs. You breathe in: cold salt, old rope, the sweet, sour tang of wet mud. The crew waits, and so does the clock; news travels faster than the tide.

Maia Rivera: (You can still feel the echo of the town hall in your chest. Your voice is hoarse but steady; steadiness can be an anchor for other people.)

Jonah Kade: "Morning," he says, and it is neither careless nor light. It is a weathered word, scuffed by early hours. He smells of tar and salt, sleeves rolled, hands already stained. He studies the berm sketches in your hand like a captain reading charts.

You: (You need him steady. You need the cooperative steady. You need not to let your guilt make the choices for you.)

Lupe Kade: (grinning, fists on hips) "If we plant from high tide back, the cords will take quicker. I can get the kids from the school to help after breakfast—drag a few kids with sticky fingers and we get more hands."

You: "Good. We stagger the planting—breakwaters first, mats next. Jonah, you'll handle the anchors. Lupe, marshal the younger crew. Tomas—"

Tomas Rivera: (from the bench, cane leaned against his knee) "—I'll tell stories and keep the kettle hot. Can't have you lot working on an empty stomach. Reminds me of the boathouse days; we did worse in worse weather." (He chuckles, voice like gravel and comfort.)

Narration:
Tomas's laugh pulls a thread through the group. You hand out gloves and a few tattered tools. The mud is cold and patient; it takes what you give and remembers. Dana moves along the edge, brush in hand, painting a banner that will hang from the Harbor—bold letters, a coral streak of anger braided with love. Her brushstrokes are fast, deliberate, impatient with diplomacy.

Dana Park: "Signs matter," she snaps without looking up. "People show up for a reason. A banner holds a mood. A banner says we own this land."

You: "And signs don't plant cordgrass," you say, but the response is gentle; you know they are complements, not opposites. Action and attention must travel together.

Not intrusive; a steadying weight. His sea-glass eyes are worried but patient. There is more said in the pause between his fingers and your spine than in some town hall speeches.

Jonah Kade: "We can make these ridges hold," he murmurs. "If we do it right—root systems, staggered—maybe the fish come back quicker than the models say."

You: "We need a buffer—two rows, double-laid mats along the breakwater. If funding holds."

Narration:
There it is. Funding. The word scratches the air.

Lupe: (biting her lip) "Supplies are almost gone. The burlap is threadbare, the stakes split. We borrowed the netting from the cooperative. It's...tight."

You: (Your chest tightens, an old muscle memory from when a storm took the boathouse and you couldn't do more than watch.) "We stretch what we can. We prioritize the northern creek—"

The volunteers find cadence again. The berms rise like a chorus of small refusals to the sea. [Scene: Boardwalk | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: People coming and going; a hastily assembled supply table; the Harbor visible beyond]
[Music: Low brass undercurrent; heartbeat percussion]
[Sound: Distant footsteps on wood, the slap of tar against boot leather]

Narration:
Mayor Rosa arrives as if she were a traveling weather front. She walks with civic rhythm—practical heels, briefcase, petitions folded under one arm. Her face holds the balance of a swing vote and a woman who has watched these streets flood more than once.

Mayor Rosa: "Maia," she says, voice even. "You've been busy."

You: (You stand, mud-splotched, and feel suddenly very small and very large at once. This is the compressing of politics into the mud you know how to move.) "We are. We have a plan."

Mayor Rosa: "I saw the sketches. The state is prepared to offer a grant—small, but enough for the materials we need to finish the first phase without borrowing from the cooperative. There are strings, though. Reporting, oversight, an appointed liaison from the county that reviews progress."

Narration:
The words fall like a tide over coral—nourishing and dangerous. The state money would mean more berms, faster planting, less strain on volunteers. It would mean you could get the northern creek shored before the predicted squall. But "oversight" tastes like loss; it tastes like someone else deciding how your history is balanced against charts.

Jonah Kade: (his jaw tightens; he is quiet, which is louder than any outcry) "Strings," he repeats. "They'll want rights-of-way, access. It'll change who controls the nets and the docks, not just the berms."

Mayor Rosa: "Not necessarily. The oversight clause is standard—accountability for funds. I can try to secure a cooperative oversight clause, but that will take time and political capital. The county will not be thrilled to cede monitoring responsibilities. You have to decide, Maia. Do you accept conditional funding and get materials now, or do you refuse and keep complete control?"

Narration:
Her eyes look at you for more than civic leadership; there is the knowing of a neighbor, the awareness that every choice is a thing someone might never forgive you for. The rumor that Elias's firm might swoop back with another offer flutters at the edges. The storm clock ticks—forecasts tightening into a schedule that will not forgive delay.

Dana Park: (throwing down her brush, voice sharp) "We can't sign away our right to govern the shoreline. Not to some faceless office that won't feel Tomas's stories when the tide takes his steps."

Tomas Rivera: (quietly, to you) "You know what I say, girl? We do what we can. But don't you let them tell you what the sea remembers." (His voice trembles—age and fear braided into one.)

You: (Guilt is an old tide pool you keep wading into. You worry your insistence on autonomy is vanity if the berms fail and people lose roofs. But you also know that if outsiders steer the work, the cooperative could be priced out or marginalized. The math here is brutal and intimate.)

Lupe: "Moments like this make or break us. If we accept oversight, will our kids still learn how to stake a net? Will they get paid, or will contractors take the jobs? We need both hands on the wheel and both eyes on the chart."

Narration:
The air tightens. Volunteers keep planting, but their glances toward the harbor are frequent and anxious. Each shovel stroke is now measured against an invisible ledger of cost and control. You feel the scale tipping under your ribs.

Narration:
You step aside with Jonah and Lupe. The three of you lean on the rail and watch the harbor—boats idling, nets slack. The smell of diesel, fish, and rain sits heavy.

Jonah Kade: "If you take it, I won't stop you," he says slowly. "But I want a firm promise that the cooperative will maintain veto power on any access decisions. I want the fishing grounds protected from anything that would move our nets. I don't... trust 'standard clauses' to mean what they say."

You: "I don't either. But refusing could leave us with empty hands when the next squall hits. We need materials—stakes, geotextile, plants. We need to finish before the storm window closes."

Jonah Kade: "Then fight for the map, Maia. Don't let them hand you a pre-drawn course."

Narration:
His words are a gauntlet wrapped in a hand. He places his hand on your shoulder again—this time firmer, steadier. There is an unspoken question in it: can you protect them and the town? Can you keep your autonomy and still win time?

Mayor Rosa: (softly) "I want to keep accountability local, Maia. If it helps, I can propose a co-managed clause—language that binds the state to share oversight with a community-appointed board. It will take meetings, pressure, and a public record. It could slow the disbursement, but it might buy you both material and control."

Narration:
Her offer is a lifeline and a rope. Political capital is a currency you are rich in with the town—people trust you—but spending it trades on other debts. The sky has thinned to pewter; wind threads through the banner Dana is finishing, making it snap like a drum. Somewhere toward the horizon, thunder is a low promise.

You: (Your heartbeat has been slow and measured all morning; now it climbs toward a peak. Each option is pain and preservation. Your guilt whispers that choosing autonomy could be vanity; your responsibility tells you not to beg for strings you may later regret.)

Maia Rivera: (to yourself, barely a breath) "We don't have forever."

Narration:
The moment pulses. The crew looks up. Jonah's hand presses once more to your shoulder. Tomas watches, cane across his knees. Dana's banner flutters, half-painted letters catching the light. Mayor Rosa's eyes wait—politician and neighbor braided into one expectant frame.
