You told Jonah to meet you. You remember the small, decisive press of your thumb on his name—an attempt to anchor something loose—and the quick, low answer on the other end: I’ll be there.

You step onto the harbor with the smell of salt, hot metal, and fresh tar in your nose. The place that used to speak only of nets and morning coffee now speaks project timelines and contractor jargon. The retrofitted docks are efficient in a way the town never was: stainless rails, stamped placards that read inspection dates, sensors blinking like secondhand clocks. It all moves fast. It was meant to move fast.

Maia Rivera: (You steady yourself on a piling. You scan faces for the familiar, for fault lines you can still mend. Your Moleskine is zipped into your bag like a patient organ. You have reasons for what you did; you keep them close.)

Jonah Kade: "You're early." (He wipes his hands, oil on his knuckles. His voice is low, the sea-weathered quiet you used to mistake for peace.)

Jonah Kade: (He shrugs, then points to a rigging you used to joke about as "too fancy for a fisherman's hands.") "We’ve had to re-route a few channels. Nets don’t like the new pilings. I’m patching—again."

You: "I know." (You reach out but stop; measures and maps have been your answers for months. Touch is no longer a reliable verb.)

Jonah Kade: "Do you know what’s missing?" (He doesn't wait for your permission to speak. There's a rhythm to his impatience now—short, pragmatic bursts.) "The shoals are changed where my father taught me. The map on your wall would look different if you’d been born with a boat instead of a tide gauge."

You: (You take inventory—Jonah’s jaw tight, his hands steady but moving faster than needed; Lupe's ledger promises floating like a second currency; Dana's flyers have already been handed out along the boardwalk. You catalog consequences the way you used to catalog species at low tide.)

Maia Rivera: "Triage." (The word tastes clinical. You say it aloud, the justification you’ve given a dozen times. Speed was triage. It’s still triage.)

Jonah Kade: "Triage is for wounds we can dress. You picked surgery without anesthesia." (He looks at you then, searching for flare or flinching and finding neither. His eyes are not accusing—only factual, which feels worse.)

Jonah pauses, looks out over a channel that used to hold the Kade family's best nights. The noise of the harbor fills the space between you—concrete and machine and the faint thump of a social feed somewhere broadcasting a contractor livestream.

Jonah Kade: "Lupe’s doing the postings. Compensation, what’s left. She’s… she's tired, Maia. People are tired."

You: (You process the word tired like a datum. Tired is measurable: hours worked, payouts, the ratio of old boats to new pilings. You have spreadsheets. You have schedules. You do not have an instant for rewriting how people measure their lives.)

[Scene: Flooded Historic Quarter | Dusk]
[Visual: Cobblestone half-submerged, storefront windows ringed with white salt lines. Neon from a coffee shop flickers against standing water. Protest banners hang from balconies like new algae.]

Foundations are boxed in with reinforced steel; one storefront is a skeleton of scaffolding with a corporate placard; across the square, a line of protestors threads itself like a seam being pulled tight.

Dana Park steps forward with a megaphone. Her ponytail is streaked with salt and ink; she wears a field vest like a banner.

Dana Park: "They promised protection, but they took our bay as collateral!" (Her voice snaps into the crowd. People lean in; someone drums on a trash can. The live feed on a phone projects her voice wider.)

Maia Rivera: (You know Dana’s pattern—observe, amplify, demand. You also know that the pattern works to pull headlines to a hinge point. Your jaw sets. Neutral assessment: Dana's action changes public attention vectors. High attention increases risk of reputational damage; reputational damage increases political cost.)

Dana Park: "Maia—" (She looks straight at you, timing precise as any organizer.) "You signed the timelines. You chose speed. Now tell us who counts in your calculus."

Maia Rivera: (You inhale. The air tastes of salt and heated breath. Around you, people are reading compensation postings, gestures small and empty against the scale of what they mourn. Your options narrow to tactical forms: defend, deflect, acknowledge. Each has a cost metric.)

You: "I chose the option that would limit loss of life and infrastructure." (You say it plainly. The sentence is an engineered structure. Some in the crowd nod; others shout louder. The megaphone scrapes your words thin.)

Dana Park: "You chose to change our shoreline and call it triage. That sounds like a decision that didn't include us." (Her accusation is clean, efficient. The crowd feeds it with questions.)

Elias Wren: (To Maia, quietly, as if from a separate calendar.) "The infrastructure is performing. The levees held the last pulse within projected margins. We can scale this model regionally." (His voice is designed for headlines—neutral in warmth, high in conviction.)

Maia Rivera: "You measured performance. You measured margins. You measured the bay. But what about what people tether their identity to?" (You dare a question that is not about data. It hangs there—qualitative, messy.)

Elias Wren: "Identity is a variable in any socio-ecological model. It will adapt." (He means it clinically. The idea of adaptation as a dataset sits in the air like an instruction manual where a hymn might be expected.)

The pumps, which have been running non-stop, kick into a higher gear—mechanical answers to a human roar. Sensors blink; alarms, brief and controlled, chirp as they self-test. The levees, for all the arguments, whisper that they are holding.

A man with a placard thrusts forward and points at you with the single truth of his anger.

Placard Man: "You sold our shoals for a promise. Where's the shoreline you promised us?"

Maia Rivera: (You feel your pulse sprint to meet the drumbeat. Every choice you made now returns in faces and in the cadence of chants. Your words are measured—neutral in syntax—but your chest vibrates with adrenaline. You can feel the very high arousal in your throat, in the quick sound of breath against the megaphone's rumble, in the way the crowd's anger thins and then thickens into a direct line toward you.)

Jonah Kade: (He steps between you and the man, not taking your side but refusing the single-target blame.) "Hey—this is not who we are." (His attempt to mediate sounds small against the noise; still, someone in the back of the crowd quiets.)

Dana Park: "Who we are is speaking up." (A retort sharp enough to cut through.)

Elias Wren: (He raises a hand, palms outward as if to calibrate the room.) "Progress will feel like loss at first. That is true of any meaningful infrastructure."

You: (You hear the words and measure them against the ledger in Lupe's hand, against Jonah's net-stained palms, against Tomas' old stories your father told you. The crowd's energy condenses into a single moment of directed focus toward you. Cameras frame your face; a live feed ticks up viewers by the thousands. The harbor's pumps sound like a distant surfline that will not cease. Jonah’s expression folds into something unreadable—something that could be resignation or a bruise of blame saved for later.

An organizer calls for order and the drums keep beating.

You stand at the center of a rapid, complicated equation: levees that function, livelihoods altered, political capital spent, a town's cultural ledger rearranged.

You breathe in. For a moment everything narrows to that breath—the sea, the pumps, the megaphone, Jonah's silent face. You understand the contours of what you have traded. You also understand that the trade can be defended on technical grounds.

You also understand that defense will not be the same as reconciliation.

You listen to the crowd. You look at Jonah. You glance at Elias, whose camera-ready composure is as guarded as any levee. You see Lupe’s ledger, damp at the edges from the day’s sweat or the tide’s salt; you see Dana's banner, pushed but not broken.

You think: the shore is safer for now. The heart is quieter and angrier.

You think: something that was tethered to place is now a ledger entry.

You think: there will be another tide.
