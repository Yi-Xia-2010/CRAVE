The rosemary takes up the corner where your mother's chair used to sit. You press your palm to a sprig and breathe it in: resin and sun-warmed citrus, the smell a small, stubborn map of home. Your hair—dark chestnut, salt-streaked at the tips from the bay air—escapes the messy bun and tickles the nape of your neck. The brass compass pendant rests against your sternum, cool as a coin.

You reach for the Moleskine in your jacket pocket. It's heavier than yesterday: tide sketches, notes from last week's permit meeting, a pressed reed. You flip it open to a blank page and feel the same careful economy you use when you choose where to draw a new boardwalk—each line matters.

[Scene: Marisol Wetland Reserve | Dawn]
[Visual: Boardwalks slick with rain, reeds bowed, reflective puddles]
[Sound: Water lapping softly at pilings; a tired wind that smells of salt and peat]
[Music: Low, slow strings—calm, patient]

The marsh waits the same way a breath waits for the decision to be let out. Boardwalk planks gleam; algae threads along the rails are a familiar green-brown that reads like a ledger of hours. You pad along the wooden path—rubber soles whispering—Moleskine tucked against your ribs, eyes skimming the creeks.

You measure loss the way others measure time. A clump of washed-up wrack, a scar where a crab habitat used to be, a narrow channel cut deeper after last month's storm: these are entries in your mental ledger. You make careful, methodical notes—widths of creeks, salinity in the morning puddles, where the restoration mats are lifting—and you let each observation sit beside the others.

Tomas clears his throat from the porch of his bungalow two houses over. The sound is small and intimate, like the wooden click of a cane against threshold. You turn; he is there—face carved with seasons, hands the color and shape of knots. He has always moved like an old rope: reliable, weathered, threaded through the town.

Tomas: "Mornin', Maia. You come catch the light before it runs."

You: "I like to think it waits for me, Tomas." (You offer a short smile. It doesn't need to bloom into anything larger; there is history in the economy.)

Tomas: (He huffs a gentle laugh.) "Used to be your ma would have it fixed with two words and some chamomile. You keep that rooftop of hers tidy, eh?"

You: "Mostly for the rosemary." (You tuck a loose tendril back into the pot. The scent seems to steady something inside you.) "How's the knee today?"

Tomas: "Knee remembers storm days better than I do. Don't let it teach you the same lesson." (He taps his cane. A small sermon embedded in a joke.) "You out early, cataloguing the morning?"

You: "Mapping creeks. Checking the southern mats. I've got a new transect to run today."

Tomas: (He squints out at the marsh.) "You do more than maps, Maia. Remember the boathouse—" (His voice softens.) "—you kept that promise when you came back. It's why half this town sleeps easier."

You feel the familiar tightening at his words—not guilt this time, but something like a hinge catching. Tomas's praise is both comfort and weight; it reminds you of the one promise you never let yourself forget.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Morning]
[Visual: Jonah stepping onto the planks, canvas cap shading his eyes; nets and buoys in the background]
[Sound: Soft creak of the docks, a gull calling offstage]
[Music: Sparse guitar notes, low and warm]

Jonah is already there, sleeves rolled, canvas cap tucked back as if the wind has been trying to rearrange him all night. His hair is sandy and wind-tossed; one hand toys with a net line and the other rests on the rail. He looks up when he hears your steps.

Jonah: (Grunts, then a half-smile.) "You beat the sun today, Maia. Can't be good for the tide gauge."

You: "Someone has to keep an eye on it. Otherwise—" (You let that sentence hang; everyone knows the otherwise.)

Jonah: "Otherwise the sea gets two votes." (He shrugs, eyes flicking to your jacket.) "You got your ledger with you. Always the scientist."

You: "Always the scientist who remembers how to tie a decent knot." (You tap his hand where it holds the line.) "How's the cooperative faring?"

Jonah: (He shifts, the movement practical.) "Lupe's been wrangling the books like a prize on a shelf. Fish are being stubborn, though—more water, less shoaling near the inlet. We had to change routes last week. Old routes don't mean much when the sea moves."

You: "I can run a quick sonar survey this afternoon. See if the shoal has shifted or just dispersed."

Jonah: (He looks at you for a long moment—sea-glass blue eyes steady.) "That'd be useful. But don't put yourself between deadlines and the sea. We need you whole, Maia. Not burned out on meetings."

You feel the words like a gentle admonition. Jonah's concern is practical, edged with the kind of care rooted in long tides and shared work. There is something both terse and warm about the exchange—familiarity that keeps its distance.

Jonah: "Also—" (He pauses, searching for the non-awkward way to be softer.) "Tomas says hi. He says you still owe him coffee and one of those ridiculous weather stories."

You: "Tell him I'll bring the coffee if he brings an extra blanket." (You smile, letting a small ease spread.)

You walk with Jonah a pace along the boardwalk as the town behind you wakes like a slow tide. The harbor smells of wet rope and frying oil from the fishmonger. Someone on shore hauls a net, and the rhythm carries like a metronome.

Your Moleskine opens without thinking; notes go in tidy columns—salinity, wrack lines, a rough sketch where the restoration mats have pulled at an edge. Each entry feels like setting a peg into soft ground: a thing done to hold the place from sliding.

You are methodical on the surface because structure keeps old ghosts at bay. Beneath the ledgered calm is the memory of a past storm response: you were younger, more certain, and a decision you made in a flashing hour left outcomes that still visit you in the rain. You do not name the failure aloud. Naming it fills the world with blame and would make the ledger heavier than you can carry.

[Scene: Marshside Bench | Late Morning]
[Visual: Light brightening; puddles reflecting the sky like small, honest mirrors]
[Sound: A phone's soft chime from your jacket pocket]
[Music: The piano returns, a single sustained chord]

Ava's message arrives as a small vibration. You almost let it go—almost an ordinary day—but the subject line hitch-hooks your attention: "tide gauge anomalies + preliminary storm model."

You palm the phone and read her brief, clinical summary. The tide gauges along the southern inlet registered a low-but-consistent increase in high-water marks overnight; sensor drift isn't ruled out, but the early storm trajectory model shows a slight left shift—enough to change inundation in the Flooded Historic Quarter. She attaches a snapshot of a model plume and a hurried line: "Need eyes. If you're free, meet me at the south gauge in an hour."

The tightening you felt earlier—small, like a glove being laced one more notch—threads through your wrist where the braided blue cord bracelet sits. Responsibility is a physical weight. You rub the bracelet absently and feel the knot where childhood braided into something that would later keep you anchored.

Ava: (Her message tone is brisk but not alarmed.) "Maia—I double-checked sensors but wanted you to see it. Could be nothing. Could be a trend. I'm heading in with a sampler. Coffee?"

You read it twice. The rational part of you parses possibilities—sensor error, local run-off, an actual shift. The other part—the part that keeps you awake in weather—tilts forward, attentive.

You close the Moleskine with a slow, decisive motion. The pages whisper like quiet waves. Outside, the town hums its small, ordinary life, indifferent to the ledger that lines your palms.

You touch the compass pendant, then the bracelet, and stand. The plan forms in the same way your notes do: methodical, necessary. Check the gauge. Run the transect. Talk to Jonah about the cooperative's altered routes. Pull Tomas in for his stories of where the water used to be, because memory fills gaps sensors cannot.

You feel the weight, yes—but not as panic. As a responsibility you will meet in steps. For now, morning must be done: the rosemary must be watered, the Moleskine must be in your pocket, and you must be on the boardwalk before the tide does something unexpected.

A small, private voice in your chest—shaped by past failure and kept soft by morning light—says: keep the ledger honest; don't let promises become blinders.

You step toward the stairs that lead down from the rooftop, the town opening and rearranging itself around your movement. The day is asking for attention, and you plan to give it carefully.

You breathe in the rosemary one more time. Salt, herb, and a copper-pink horizon fold together into a single, steady present.

You close the rooftop door behind you, the click like a page being turned.
