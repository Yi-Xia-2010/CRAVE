[Scene: Marisol Wetland Reserve | Dawn]
[Visual: Pale light through coastal haze; boardwalks glisten with dew. Close-up on scuffed rubber boots sinking into soft mud.]
[Music: Driving strings with a clipped tempo; percussion like distant rain]
[Sound: Shovels scraping, low murmur of volunteers, gulls keening]

You cinch your hair tighter into a bun, the braid of blue cord rubbing cold against your collarbone. The Moleskine is heavy in your pocket, its pages crowded with sketches of tide-break berms and lists of who still owes you their signatures. Your voice is raw from last night—hoarse with too many small meetings and one long, necessary speech—but steady enough when people look at you and expect a plan.

Maia Rivera: (You step forward, palms flecked with mud, and call out.) "Okay—two teams. Jonah, you take the northern line; Lupe, you lead the planting grid by the inlet. Keep the mats tight—overlap by a handspan. Dana, banner later. Right now we move the living stones."

Jonah Kade: "Aye." (He exhales, palms on the rolled edge of a restoration mat; tar and salt linger in the fibers.) "You look like you could use coffee. I can run for a thermos."

You almost refuse—habit, ego—but the muscles along your jaw loosen at the offer. You nod. "One," you say. "Make it black."

Lupe Kade: (smiling, fierce) "Black and tall. You'll owe me a croissant." (She hoists a planting tray and turns, eyes bright, mud flicking her calves.) "Maia, we got three more high-school kids signing on. They want to earn community hours, and Tomas offered to teach them knotwork if they'll listen."

Tomas Rivera: (from the bench, cane planted) "Back when the boathouse was whole, we made knots you could hang a tide on. Show 'em the way, Maia—teach 'em the sea has a memory."

You watch Tomas speak—his voice a gravel-thin thread of story—and the weight of the boathouse memory presses into you like low tide. Tomas's cane taps once on the boardwalk; you feel the rhythm of the town in that tap. It steadies you and torques the guilt in your chest. You think of how the boathouse fell and how you left once, like a coward or like a student. The thought knifes briefly: Did you come back to make amends, or because you could no longer stand to watch?

[Visual: Hands passing a sack of cordgrass; dark, wet, glistening fronds folding into the soil]
[Sound: Grunts, the slap of mat against mud, breath quickening]

Dana Park: (she's near the harbor edge, brush in hand, painting a banner. Her movements are kinetic—anger braided into action.) "If we had the funds, Maia, we could triple the berms today. Not everyone wants to sit and sew a cushion while the firm draws blue lines in their glossy brochures."

You go to her. The paint smells sharp—acrid and peppering the salt air. Her jaw is set. There is no softness between you now, only the hard calculus of what this town will be allowed to decide.

Maia Rivera: "I know. I know." (You turn a sample of cordgrass over in your mud-caked fingers.) "I called for this. If we can't move fast, the next storm—" Your mouth closes on the word. You do not want to say the rest aloud.

Jonah steps close; the warmth of him feels like borrowed shelter. His hand finds the small of your back, an unspoken brace. "We can do a hell of a lot with what we have," he says. "But Maia—if the grant's on the table, we should hear it out. Rosa's not a snake. She sees Tomas every Sunday. She knows the boathouse story."

You want to trust Jonah's assessment—his steadiness is a ballast—but your chest tightens. Trust runs like tidal lines here: thin, visible, easily broken by a new current. "Come look at the berm sketch," you say instead of answering. "Tell me if the overlap's enough."

The choice hangs briefly, then dissolves into work. Whatever you pick, the arousal ratchets higher—muscles burn, voices sharpen, and the sky toward the harbor darkens with rumor and distant cloud. Volunteers move like a single organism: planting, tamping, singing a few borrowed shanties to keep the rhythm. The sound swells—breathing, wood creaking, laughter that tries to hold back the undercurrent of fear.

[Scene: Boardwalk (near Harbor) | Late Morning]
[Visual: Closer view of the harbor; Elias' sleek staging containers are a smudge on the horizon. Mayor Rosa arrives with a tablet tucked under her arm and a careful face.]
[Music: A low-pulsing ostinato; a heartbeat doubled in timpani]
[Sound: Footsteps on wood, faint gulls, the distant beep of a municipal vehicle]

Mayor Rosa: (approaching, her voice folded with civic weight) "Maia. Everyone. Tomas." (She regards the crew, then you, with something that could be pity or calculation.) "The state allocated a contingent grant for shoreline resilience. It's small—enough to get materials for a full day's expansion on your berms. It comes with reporting and a county overseer to ensure compliance."

A hush moves through the workers like a ripple in a shallow pool. Dana's brush freezes; Jonah's hand tightens on a mat. You feel words spring to the tip of your tongue—rejection, acceptance, negotiation—each one a ledger of consequence.

Dana Park: (snap) "Oversight. Of course. They'll eat our plans and spit out a narrative that sells for billboards."

Mayor Rosa: "It's not meant to—" (she rubs the bridge of her nose) "Maia, I know the community's always fought for autonomy. I also know funding dries up faster than we can build. This would let you buy more marsh mats now. You won't have to wait for volunteer cycles."

Maia Rivera: (You measure Rosa. Her eyes flick to Tomas; for a second you see the mayor as a neighbor before she puts on the office guard again.) "And the oversight—what does it actually mean?"

Mayor Rosa: "Monthly reports. Periodic inspections. A county liaison at meetings. They may require certain contractors for heavy lifts if safety codes demand it. But the grant language... it's written to allow local input. It doesn't say 'we take control.'"

Jonah Kade: "Local input that comes with a parole officer," he mutters. He doesn't hide contempt. "What's to stop them from writing 'input' as 'formal approval'?"

Mayor Rosa: (softly) "Political cover isn't perfect, Jonah. But neither is running out of lumber in a week because we couldn't buy enough. I want you to have a real choice, Maia."

You feel the pulse in your throat—faster, sharper. The storm clock is not a metronome but a drumbeat; each tick presses you from both sides: the ethical backbone of autonomy, and the practical requirement to keep the marsh alive. Guilt is a tide you know too well. It curls in on itself and spits you onto the rocks—did you fight the firm because you feared losing ground, or because you feared losing the town?

Tomas watches you as if seeing an old map fold at an uncomfortable place. He clears his throat.

Tomas Rivera: "When my boy and I built the boathouse, we used nails and argument. We patched and prayed. We didn't always agree—and we broke some things along the way—but whatever we did, we did it together. Don't let someone else tell you the rhythm of our hammers."

Dana Park: "And don't forget—what they call 'safety' is often cover for contractors who take profit and leave the scars."

Mayor Rosa: "Dana—" (she raises a hand) "I am not naive. I know you all have history. I came because I was asked. It's a conditional grant. I'm offering it because I think it will save the berm from failing before the season's out."

You swallow, the salt of your own tears indistinguishable from the spray-coated air. Jonah's gaze holds you, not demanding but present—like a seam you can lean into.

Jonah Kade: (lowering his voice) "Maia—what do you want to do? Not the safe answer for me. For you. For the marsh."

Your internal list unspools: materials bought, mats secured, kids signed on, Diego's dock unthreatened—versus monthly reports, a liaison in meetings, the fear that oversight becomes censorship. The arousal crescendos—lines of decision snapping taut.

You think of the night you watched the boathouse go. You think of the small, fierce faces planting roots at your feet. You think of Tomas’s cane tapping a cadence older than grant cycles. Your throat tightens; your hands are still muddy. You need air, and you need to give an answer that won't erase people.

Time narrows. The sky at the harbor edge bruises—darker bands rolling in from the open water. Volunteers hurry, voices high and ragged. Dana's banner hangs half-painted, the slogan a raw cut across the canvas. You feel Jonah step close; his breath is warm on your ear.

Jonah Kade: "If you need me to hold the line with the county people, I will. If you need me to keep people off the plans and on the marsh—I'll do it."

Maia Rivera: (Your answer pools in you, and it is not yet formed. It sits like a shell at your feet, a decision too heavy to pick up without both gloves.) "Jonah—" (you pause; the sandpaper in your voice betrays how much this choice matters) "I don't want wins that cost us our voice."

Jonah: (quiet) "Neither do I. But I don't want to be unprepared when the water comes. I'm not arguing you—I'm saying watch the tide with me."

Mayor Rosa: "Maia. You're the town's voice on this. Whatever you decide, the council will follow. I can push for a co-managed clause—a stipulation that keeps a community oversight board in the reporting structure—but it will take some political capital. It might be the middle ground."

Dana Park: (sarcastic) "Middle ground is where the sea drags the dead and the living both. Compromise is fine—unless it sells us out."

The arousal escalates to a near-physical ache in your chest. The decision is a sharp cliff edge. Your guilt, a recurring tide whose undertow you've felt since the boathouse fell, whispers that autonomy is vanity if the marsh collapses without funds. Your stubbornness whispers that compromise is surrender. The sky is ready to break, and you must choose what the town will be when it wakes to the next tide.

You look at the hands in the mud—some young, some old, all trembling but determined. You look at Jonah: sea-glass eyes steady, worried but loyal. You look at Mayor Rosa, a public face that has private stakes. You look at Dana, whose anger is a kind of love you respect and fear.
