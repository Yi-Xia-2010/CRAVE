The sea reflects the gray sky and, between the two, your reflection is small.

Tomas: "We don't bury what the sea took. We plant what remembers it."

Narration:
Winter arrives with a clean, cruel clarity. You work late into nights that taste of metal and tea. The town's new routines are grit-smoothed: shifts at the soup line, rotating tool crews, young people learning to splice new kinds of netting. Lupe organizes three mornings a week to teach bookkeeping and cooperative governance—practical lessons made from necessity.

Elias appears sometimes at the edge of the warehouse. He is less an antagonist now than a complicated neighbor. He moves like a man who has worked long to compress his arrogance, and who has learned, bruised, that systems need human hands to make them live.

Elias Wren: (quietly, when he stays after everyone else has gone) "I misread how fast I could move things here without breaking what matters. I thought the fix was infrastructure. I didn't see the rest of it."

Maia Rivera: (you fold a piece of reclaimed timber) "You still believe in large fixes. That's not wrong. But they have to be worked with, not over, the people who live here."

Elias Wren: (nods) "I'm learning that. I'm trying to learn that."

Narration:
He hands you a small envelope — a private gesture. Inside is a list of commitments his firm has agreed to: apprenticeship programs, hiring local contractors, a fund for cultural restitution. His eyes are steel-gray and wet with an unfamiliar humility. He says nothing about romance. He offers a kind of apology that is professional and personal—measured, and human.

Elias Wren: "You didn't ask for any of this. You asked people to trust you. You stood in the middle."

Maia Rivera: "I stood. I also failed in the language I used sometimes—too sharp, too sure. That has consequences."

Elias Wren: "And you carry them openly. That matters."

Narration:
You are not absolved. The ache of what was lost sits in your shoulders, an old weight that helps you remember. But you are not alone in carrying it now.

Narration:
The rooftop is small and stubborn, like the seedlings pushing through its soil. You kneel at a planter and find, among the ash and grit, green tips of rosemary pushing through. Your fingers press into the soil that once held a boathouse memory. It's sticky and real.

Tomas: (quietly, beside you) "Those shoots don't need speeches to be important. They need tending."

Maia Rivera: "They also need a system to keep the water right, to keep salt at bay. We can sing and plant, Tomas, and we also have to engineer."

Tomas: (a small laugh) "Then engineer and sing. I've heard worse plans."

Narration:
Jonah is there, later, sleeves rolled, helping Lupe tie new trellises. When he looks at you, something soft has settled into his sea-glass eyes: not forgiveness exactly, but a new contour of respect. He does not collapse the boundary. He accepts it as part of surviving—both of you changed by what you could not prevent.

Jonah: "You did what you could. We did what we could. Some things won't be the same, Maia. But we keep what we can."

Maia Rivera: "And mend what we can."

The final hour is crowded with small, humane actions: Lupe distributes homemade bread; Tomas reads the names of those lost; volunteers string up photographs of the old boathouse; someone cues a guitar and a child begins a clumsy chorus that the crowd improves into a song.
