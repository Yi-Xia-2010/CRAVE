[Scene: Offshore Project Site | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Sleek shipping containers line a temporary pier; LED strips trace scaffolding; an AR schematic flickers faintly on a steel pillar]
[Music: Taut strings, a quickening rhythm; percussion like distant thunder]
[Sound: Distant gulls, metallic clank of a crate, the low hum of generators]

You step off the harbor's familiar boards and into something designed to look inevitable. The air here feels engineered—sterile LED halos cutting the salt haze, a scent of oil and antiseptic where mud used to be your measure of worth. Your fingers still bear thin crescents of mud from the morning—smeared on the cuffs of your field jacket—proof that the work you believe in begins in the dirt, not in slide decks.

Elias is waiting with a leather folio that clicks open like a promise. The folio smells of new paper and economy: timelines, impact assessments, funding agreements stamped with signatures that glint like small surrender flags. His AR lapel pin projects a coastal schematic over the space between you, an elegant web of breakwaters and turbines hovering in midair.

Elias Wren: "Maia. Thank you for coming. You'll want to see the model—this is the fastest, most comprehensive plan we've put together. Protection for the whole bay, jobs, emissions reductions. We can start deployment in months."

You: You listen while the schematic spins. It is convincing in the way good salesmanship is convincing—clean numbers, glossy renderings, an urgency wrapped in competence. You feel the pull of that urgency, a tightness in your chest. "Months" is a short word; it vibrates against the long rhythms of tides and nets and the histories behind every shoreline claim.

Elias: (He leans in; his voice is calm, measured.) "We can allocate workforce training to local unions, contracts for the cooperative. We want this to be collaborative—on paper, at least."

You: You hold his gaze. There is a sheen of corporate polish that reflects back your own tired face. Your internal list—fish migration corridors, sedimentation, community governance—scrolls like a nervous eyelid. "Collaborative" is a start. It isn't a guarantee.

[Visual: Mayor Rosa standing near a glowing tablet, her fingers hovering over the signed funding agreement]
Mayor Rosa: "If we lock this down, we get federal matching and immediate work for dozens. The coffers will ease—Tomas, Lupe, families that are hanging by threads."

Tomas stands a few feet away, cane planted against the pier, the carved grain warm under his knuckles. He watches you like someone waiting for a verdict. His face is a map of years of salt, of remembering how things used to be—before seawalls and before the bay decided to forget its old contours.

Tomas: (Quiet) "You know the shoreline, Maia. Don't let them paint over our hands."

You: His words land like a rock in the water. They ripple outward: contractual language vs. lived shoreline. You step between the tablet and the schematic, fingers ghosting over the AR image as if you're feeling for seams.

Elias: (A small, controlled smile) "Of course—let's go through everything. We can overlay migration corridors. Our mitigation strategies are robust. We have independent reviews lined up."

You: "Independent reviews"—you've heard the phrase used as buffer before, as future tense that comforts committees in the present. You want to know what "robust" means in numbers and in lived seasons.

Jonah stands at the end of the pier, shoulders hunched against the wind, jacket heavy with salt. He doesn't step forward. His eyes, sea-glass and guarded, track the schematic like he is watching a weather map for the moment the line collapses. There is something in his posture—defense more than distance—that shaves warmth from the air between you.

Jonah: (Quiet, clipped) "You brought them here. You brought Elias to our water. How does that sit with folks who fish these lines every day?"

You: His question is not an accusation you can easily deflect. It's a ledger entry: livelihoods, routes, the quiet knowledge of shoals. You try to answer, but Jonah opens up before you finish, the tide of his worry pushing words free.

Jonah: "If turbines or piles reroute the fish, it's not some abstract loss. It's nets empty at dawn. Lupe sorting invoices that don't cover rent. My family passed down lines for a reason."

You: "That's exactly why I'm here—so we don't hand off our ocean and lose the things that hold people here." Your voice raises a fraction; you taste iron. "Tell me which corridors are most at risk. Tell me who signs off on it. Tell me who has veto power if the migration models are wrong."

Elias: (A faint edge to his tone) "We can build in adaptive monitoring. If the fish routes shift, we alter the deployment. This is standard in coastal engineering—"

You: "Adaptive monitoring needs enforcement language. Who audits? Who can pause construction? I need binding clauses, not a promise to 'alter' when it's already too late."

Elias: (He tilts his head, patient) "Binding clauses slow projects. They increase costs, and in the current funding window, delays could mean losing the match. We can compromise—time-limited milestones tied to observable indicators."

Mayor Rosa: (Interjecting, pressure in her voice) "We don't have the luxury of endless studies. People need help now, Maia. If the project stalls, other towns will take the funds. We need action."

You: The mayor's plea is a lever pressed against your sternum. You feel the room shrink—community need on one side, ecological safeguards on the other. The generator hum matches the pulse at your throat. You think of Tomas's stories of seasons when shrimp returned like clockwork, of Lupe's hands sorting invoices, of Jonah's steady, salt-scratched laugh. You think of the storm models Ava has been nudging you toward all week; they are not gentle.

Ava appears at your shoulder, tablet in hand, cheeks flushed from sprinting across docks. The tablet's screen blinks: a storm cone, a timetable, a packed forecast. The model's numbers overlay Elias's deployment timeline like a countdown.

Ava: (Breathless) "Maia—new run came in. If the storm veers this way, construction windows tighten. That 'months' becomes weeks of safe weather. We have to assume more instability."

You: The humidity in your mouth thickens. Every plan is suddenly fragile. The urgency becomes audible.

Dana stands outside a ribbon of safety tape where a handful of protesters have gathered—painted signs snapping in wind. She punches through the crowd and walks up the pier without waiting for an invitation, her ponytail whipping, hands stained with banner paint. Her voice is a crack like lightning.

Dana: "You're selling Marisol, Maia. This is the model of the same firms that bulldozed other coasts. You call it 'collaboration' and they call it 'efficiency.' Who benefits? Not the people who built this town."

You: The accusation lands harder than you expect. Dana's stare is not just anger; it's a mirror of every community-directed plan you've ever labored to birth and seen swallowed by a bigger appetite. You search your chest for a steadier answer.

You: "I'm not selling—we're negotiating terms. I'm trying to hold a seat at the table so the town has a say."

Dana: (Bitter) "Saying 'we' while you're in their rooms is not the same as standing in ours."

Lupe steps forward, hands busy with an old notepad full of accounting scrawl. Her energy is quick; her positions are practical.

Lupe: "We need jobs, Maia. But we also need nets that catch fish tomorrow. Is there room in this contract for cooperative oversight? To make sure the local crew gets first call on jobs and the monitoring is transparent?"

Elias: (A thin smile) "There's always room for negotiation. The quicker we move, the more leverage we have. If you tie every clause to co-management, implementation drags. We risk losing the matching funds."

You: His logic is crisp; its edge bites into your options. The room's noise coalesces into a single demanding frequency. Your Moleskine would have neat bullet points for this, if the decisions you needed fit into bullets.

Tomas: (Softly) "Maia, you remember why we used to plant cordgrass our way. We didn't wait for someone else's blueprint. We patched where we could. But we also didn't have tides like this. What I want is not ceremony—it's control."

You: Control. The word sits between you and the sea like a splinter.

Elias steps closer and offers more than charm this time—data packets, contingency budgets, a projected timeline that promises visible gains in months, not years. His steel-gray eyes flick up to yours, searching for a shortcut you might grant.

Elias: "Let us move fast. We can protect lives. We can create economic stability. History will read us as the people who acted."

You: History's ledger is written in lives and losses, in what was preserved and what was sacrificed. The arousal inside you swells—pressure converting to heat. You feel pulled from all sides: Dana's protest, Rosa's pragmatism, Jonah's guarded grief, Lupe's practical hope, Tomas's counsel, Ava's storm run.

[Visual: The AR schematic pulses; a layer flips to show a projected storm path intersecting the deployment window]
[Sound: The generator's hum rises; the wind picks up; a far-off horn warns of incoming conditions]
[Music: Crescendo—strings fray into sharp staccato]

You: Your voice is small at first, then steadier—because someone must stitch this into something the town can live with. "We have to decide how tough to be. Do we insist on hard, enforceable environmental clauses even if that means the timeline stretches—and risk losing funding? Or do we accept a faster deployment with concessions that could hollow out some local control? Or—" Your throat tightens—"—do we demand co-management and try to bind you to the cooperative through contract language that gives them oversight?"

Elias: (Measured, bordering on urgent) "Each additional constraint adds months and more cost. The funding window is narrow."

Mayor Rosa: (Exhausted) "People need relief now. If we keep pushing back, we may lose everything."

Jonah: (Low, steady) "And if we take it and lose our water and our ways? What then? Who feeds the kids?"

You: The storm warning ticks on Ava's tablet like a metronome to your thoughts. The arousal reaches an edge where thinking becomes impossible and choosing becomes a hot, painful necessity.

You: You inhale the air—salt, oil, the metallic tang of decisions—and feel every person's cost like weights on your ribs. There is no generous answer left in this room, only triage.
