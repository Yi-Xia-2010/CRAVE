You: (You feel the knot in your throat loosen and behave like a cord finally uncoiling.) "Not much. Wanted to be sure the mats were holding before I let myself rest." 

Jonah: (a brief, half-smile) "They held. You held them in place." 

He says it like a fact, like a navigation bearing. There's no flourish—just steadfastness—yet the warmth under the words is large enough to be a harbor. You want to answer with a careful, policy-shaped sentence; instead you let your shoulders drop and let something like a laugh out.

Jonah: (a dry, incredulous edge) "You draw like the tide—honest as hell. How long till those east mats are self-sustaining?"

You: "If we keep the planting schedule, three seasons. If the cooperative keeps the monitoring locals, five years for full sedimentation along the new curve."

Jonah: (he blinks, impressed) "You're always the practical miracle, Maia."

You fold the notebook shut and let your fingers stay on his hand for a beat longer than you planned. The sun cuts through cloud and lights the salt in his hair; your pulse picks up a rhythm that matches the music.

Jonah: (quiet, a little awkward, earnest) "You don't have to carry it all the time, you know."

Jonah: (leaning closer) "Good. 'Cause I was getting tired of watching you carry the whole coast on your shoulders."
