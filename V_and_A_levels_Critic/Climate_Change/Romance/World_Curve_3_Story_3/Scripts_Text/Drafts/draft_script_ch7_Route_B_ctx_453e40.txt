### DRAFT
# Route: Route D (Sad-but-Seeded Recovery Ending)


[Scene: Offshore Project Site | Late Afternoon — Storm-Banded Sky]
[Visual: Sleek shipping containers arranged in a temporary U-shape; scaffolding silhouettes against the water; an AR schematic pulses on a portable holo; rain spatters the metal like fast, tiny drumbeats]
[Music: Tense BGM — low synth thrum with a snare-like pulse building]

You step from the harbor walk onto the platform and the air changes—metallic, ozone-sweet, charged before the storm. Your fingertips are still smudged with mud; the smell of wet peat and salt hangs under the clinical odor of warm electronics. The leather of your jacket squeaks where it brushes a crate. Every sound feels amplified: the click of a tablet closing, the distant slam of a rope, the soft hum from the floating turbines beyond the scaffolding.

Elias meets you at the threshold with that measured, corporate smile that never quite reaches his eyes. He extends a leather folio like an offering. You can see the corners of timelines, the precise edges of impact assessments, signatures that promise money, manpower, speed.

Elias Wren: "Maia. Thank you for coming. We wanted you to see the plans in person—transparent as possible." (His fingers press the folio to your palm; his voice is smooth, practiced.) "We can protect the bay and bring jobs. The models show a fifty to seventy percent reduction in surge energy for the main inlet once the breakwater and offshore platforms are operational."

You take the folio, feel the weight of contracts and the cold of Tony-clipped signatures. The schematic on his lapel pin flickers—a rotating 3D of engineered reefs and sleek turbines, tidal arrows marked in bold lines. It looks like salvation from a render: clean, inevitable.

You: (You flip through the folio with a controlled hand; your chest tightens as each page resolves into charts and assurances. Numbers are persuasive; money is persuasive. Your mouth tastes of copper and obligation.) "The renderings are... impressive. But 'models' are not the bay. What about migratory corridors for the anchovy shoals? What about the nursery marshes? Those are not lines on a rendering."

Elias slides a page forward, calm as a tide that always returns.

Elias Wren: "We included migration mitigation in the latest assessment. There are simulated corridors—"
You: "Simulated corridors rely on assumptions. Real fish move in messy ways and change with each storm. I need empirical assurances: baseline surveys, adaptive management clauses, real monitoring officers from the cooperative."

Elias pauses, the smoothness briefly thinning.

Elias Wren: "We can include monitoring. We can—" (He searches for the right level of concession.) "—define an independent oversight panel. It will slow permitting, but it can be done."

The platform feels crowded with futures. Mayor Rosa stands a few paces behind Elias, hands folded over a stack of printed proposals, the lines at the corners of her eyes deeper than you remember. Lupe hovers near the gangway, chewing the inside of her cheek—paperwork and livelihoods balanced on a breath.

Mayor Rosa Santiago: (soft, worn) "This could mean jobs, Maia. The grant covers retrofits for the harbor infrastructure too. We could fix the pier we promised the cooperative last year."

You: "Jobs matter. So do the fish and the people who read tides by memory, not models." (You meet her gaze evenly.) "Rosa, if the deployment comes at the expense of migratory routes or access, those jobs will be hollow."

Lupe: "We can't afford to wait forever. Jonah's crews are short-handed already. People are scraping by—" (her voice cracks, then steadies) "—we need relief measures built into any plan."

Jonah stands back on the harbor edge outside the fenced staging area, coat heavy with salt and hours of work. He has not come onto the platform. Rain freckled his cap; a gust makes him clamp the collar of his jacket tighter. His sea-glass eyes narrow like a lens finding a fault.

Jonah Kade: (from the dock, low) "Maia. Don't let them draw lines where our nets belong."

You: (You hear Jonah as if through the thud of your own heart. His position is simple and sharp: protection must not become exclusion. You know the fishing runs like the seams of your palms; you know what a shifted migratory line can mean to a brother's rent, to Lupe's books, to Tomas's nights.) "I won't let them displace people. I won't trade our shore for an impermeable promise."

Elias: (leaning forward, voice that doesn't ruggedly rock like the sea but plots like an engineer) "We design with communities, Maia. Cooperative-led employment, local hires, revenue sharing—"

You: "Then write it in," you say. "Enforceable, with teeth. Not gestures."

Elias meets your insistence with a slow, measured nod and a look that says: you could be a valuable ally or a stubborn roadblock.

Elias Wren: "You ask for a lot. Time, regulatory leeway, governance input. We're working on national timelines. Delays cost resilience."

Your teeth feel like they might crack under the weight of what 'delay' can mean: another season of floods, another boat lost, another winter where the pier collapses. The thrum in the speakers steps up; the weather app on a nearby tablet blares the word 'approaching' in a red bar.

[Sound: Tablet alarm — low, urgent beeps increasing in tempo]
[Visual: A projection briefly flashes a storm cone over Marisol Bay; the model's lines march like a scythe]

Tomas's voice cuts through the schematic and the promises—a rough, memory-worn sound that anchors the room.

Tomas Rivera: "Speed ain't everything, Maia." (He leans on his cane, the carved wood dark and damp.) "When I was your age we built things that lasted fifty years. But we also worked with the folks who'd use them. If some man from a city comes and draws maps of our living water, we lose something you can't measure."

You: (His hand, rough and sea-stained, tightens around the cane. In him you can see what community means: continuity, ritual, a slow-threaded knowledge. You feel the guilt—old and familiar—curl in your ribs. You want to honor Tomas's memory and not break him by letting corporate scaffolds sweep culture away.)

Dana's voice slices from the cordon around the site, sharper than the rain.

Dana Park: (shouting from the protest line) "Sellout! Maia, are you selling us out for glossy turbines?"

You: (They chant; they hold signs. The drums of the protest center the town's anger like a heartbeat. Your throat goes dry. You can smell paint and rain and the hand-stitched banners that once were just cloth now heavy with accusation.) "Dana—" you call, but your voice is largely swallowed by the clatter.

Dana storms closer, damp hair plastering her forehead, eyes burning bright. She doesn't wait for introductions.

Dana Park: "You broker this, you know what it will do. You want our approval, but you're already on the inside. How many people will be displaced while the company runs their timeline?"

The accusation lands like a physical thing. For a beat you see yourself from outside: dust on your jacket, the Moleskine tucked in your bag like a concealed heart. You are the negotiator and the accused—caught mid-bridge.

You: (defensive, quick) "I'm trying to hold more than one thing—" (You hesitate, aware that 'trying' has been a thin consolation in the town hall for months.) "—I want safeguards written into contracts. I want local oversight. I want mitigation measures—before shovels hit sand."

Dana: (snapping) "Words. Words are how they buy us. You want them to 'include' things. Tell us how that'll stop a bulldozer with a corporate check."

Lupe steps between you and Dana, palms up, voice even but loaded.

Lupe: "Dana—wait. Maia is here with us, not against us. If we make hard demands now, we could actually secure something. But if we blow them off and they pull? We lose the funds."

Your mind runs the permutations like tide graphs. Each choice fans out risk into ripples that strike people you love. The projection screen shows deployment windows—six months, nine months, one year. Every delay shortens the distance between the present and a storm that could make those months count for nothing.

Elias returns to the folio, laying out a deployment timeline with an economy of words. He is efficient, practiced at adding people to boxes and boxes to timelines.

Elias Wren: "We can start mobilization in three months with an expedited permit package. We'll prioritize local hires in the first two waves. We can make the co-management board—if that's what the town demands—part of a post-deployment oversight structure."

You: "Post-deployment oversight is too late. By then the structures will be in place and the patterns set. We need an adaptive clause: if fish migration alters beyond X percent we pause construction, recalibrate. If access to traditional fishing grounds is reduced, there are compensatory measures and guaranteed alternative zones."

Elias: (a slight coldness) "That creates uncertainty investors hate. You understand how funding works: certainty of timelines and mitigation plans that are predictable."

You: (the room narrows to a wire between 'uncertainty' and 'certainty' with lives hanging) "Then make the investors understand the value of local knowledge. The cooperative should be part of the planning team, not an afterthought."

Mayor Rosa: (softly, torn) "If we refuse to cooperate fully, what if they go to another harbor? The funding may benefit others while we remain—" (she swallows) "—we can't let that happen."

Tomas: "And if we let them plow through our ground we'll be left with a shore that isn't ours. It's a different kind of abandonment."

A gust hits the open water; a roil of dark cloud pushes a low, urgent light across the bay. The tablet's storm plume bites in closer on the projection. You feel the tension like an ache in your elbows.

Your pulse spikes. The room's chatter washes into a single, loud, urgent current. You realize you are precisely where policy becomes flesh—where signatures will be read aloud at council meetings and doors may close or open forever after.

[Music: Tense BGM — tempo increases; percussion thumps like a rising tide]

You press harder, words dropping like weighted anchors.

You: "Listen. I will not sign anything that treats the bay like a risk to be fenced off. We need enforceable environmental clauses, binding local oversight, and clear mitigation triggers tied to ecological indicators."

Elias's jaw tightens. He meets you with the precise calm of someone whose job is to deliver results and avoid messy delays.

Elias Wren: "Maia, push too hard and we risk the whole package. Compromise is part of governance."

You: (your voice thin with urgency) "Compromise where it protects people and places—never where it sacrifices them."

Dana's protest chorus roars again outside. Jonah's silhouette is rigid at the edge of the harbor, a figure of salt and resistance. Lupe's fingers find yours for the smallest, human squeeze, a plea in a pressure point.

The tablet gives one more harsh chirp: the storm cone tightens. Outside, the first hard drops begin to scatter like tiny hammers on tin.

Tomas leans close, voice low and fierce.

Tomas Rivera: "You got a choice, Maia. Don't let the town be sold in a hurry because a storm's coming. But don't let fear force you into thin promises neither will keep."

Your breath comes faster. For a wild second you imagine both futures collapsing—the town either boxed by a corporate wall that steals access and reshapes lives, or left to drown because the perfect solution never arrived.

Everything contracts to the narrow rim of the present. You can feel the weight of signatures in your hands and the press of a community in your chest. The storm drums now against metal; the site lights strobe as if echoing the thump of your pulse.

[Sound: Rain intensifies — wind, metal, distant gull calls cut off by thunder]
[Music: Tense BGM — crescendos, then tightens into a single sustained note]

You inhale. The decision arrives with the gravity of an incoming tide.
