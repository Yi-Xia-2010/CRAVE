### DRAFT
# Route: Route B — Ending: Sad / Bittersweet


[Scene: Town Hall — Conference Room | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: The conference room's glass faces the harbor. Pale winter sun strips the sky to silver; the sea is a sheet of fractured light. A single potted marsh reed trembles near the door.]

[Music: Quickening strings under a steady pulse — urgent but hopeful]

You smell boiled coffee and varnish. The air carries the faint grit of salt blown in from the open window; it tastes like the edge of every decision you've made for this town.

You came because Celeste asked you to meet alone. You came because you wanted to move—fast, if necessary—but not at the cost of everything you've sewn into the Commons. You came because she promised to put relocation funds on the table, and that promise alone could keep half your neighbors from losing the roofs over their heads.

You fold your hands on your satchel. The copper bracelet at your wrist is warm from your skin; the engraved coordinates feel like an anchor against your pulse.

[Visual: Celeste Park stands by the long conference table, immaculate as ever; a slim portfolio rests open on the table. Her trench coat is draped over the back of a chair. The light catches the silver wave pin at her lapel.]

Celeste Park: "Mara. Thank you for coming on such short notice." (she hands you a glossy executive folder) "I know time is a premium for you. I won't waste it."

Her voice is even, conversational—precise, the way someone who writes briefs speaks when they want you to listen. There's a smile, measured to be disarming.

You register the layout of the table: a map of the promenade's footprint, highlighted in warm colors; a printed draft of "community benefits" with bullet points; legalese that snakes across a page. The relocation fund line sits in bold, an offer that hums like an engine in the room.

[Visual: Dr. Amina Bhatt sits at the far end, notebook open, sleeves rolled. She taps a pen thoughtfully against her knee.]

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "I asked to sit in to ensure the science is represented, Mara. But I also wanted to listen." (she inclines her head) "There are things here that could be engineered safely—if we keep the checks."

She meets your eyes and there's no theatricality: only a steady, professional concern. Amina's presence steadies the air.

You breathe. You want to start with the Commons—where seedlings, salt pans and memory occupy the same beds as grit and resolve—but Celeste's first words put the carriage of her offer forward.

Celeste Park: "We propose a full-scale promenade: a raised commercial spine, integrated floodwalls, and a continuous civic front. Funding—secured. Relocation assistance—committed. In exchange, we'd like an agreement limiting disruptive protests on the construction corridor and flexibility for certain engineering methods that ensure the project's viability."

You feel the words land like a tide against rock. It's the language of trade-offs: infrastructure for compliance, money for maneuvering room.

You let the silence stretch. It ripples across the table like a small current.

Celeste leans forward, voice dropping almost conspiratorial.

Celeste Park: "We'd also like to make Elias Navarro part of the implementation team—on paper. His name gives credibility; your endorsement gives us political buy-in. It's a compromise that respects local knowledge and expedites the work."

The mention of Elias makes you hear the word "pilot" in your head and the rooftop garden's rope ladder beneath your palms. Elias's face—calm, earnest—flashes in your memory; the way he clasped a field notebook to his chest the last time you watched sediment cores at Tidewatch Lab.

You feel a small, hot spike of something—protectiveness, jealousy, hope. The offer reads like a lifeline. But you remember the clause: "flexibility for certain engineering methods." Those words could mean innovation that preserves wetlands—or they could mean concrete boxed over the marsh.

Mara (you): "Flexibility is a broad term. Which methods are you envisioning as 'flexible'?" (you keep your voice even; the room needs it)

Celeste Park: "Adaptive pile systems, modular seawalls, possibilities that can be adjusted as monitoring data comes in. We are not promising a single cast-in-place answer." (she slides a page across the table) "We want an agreement that lets engineers adapt without being bogged in conditional approvals that risk months of delay."

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (sharply) "Adaptive systems are promising, but they require binding monitoring and trigger-points—transparent metrics. Otherwise 'flexible' becomes 'unaccountable.'"

Celeste Park: (a small, diplomatic gesture) "Amina, I respect monitoring. But the political calendar, the investors—"

Celeste's tone tightens. You can see the pivot: efficiency versus safeguards. The tension rises like low pressure before a storm—only this storm might be harnessed.

From your satchel your fingers go to a folded photo—the younger face of your sibling, the one the storm took—because you always bring that quiet weight of consequence to negotiations. You don't show it. You never do. But it informs the line of your shoulders, the set of your jaw.

Mara (you): "Relocation funds are generous on paper. But the community needs guarantees: binding covenants for the wetland buffer, legal recourse if engineering cuts corners, and real participation in monitoring. No more 'maybe we'll adapt later.'"

Celeste's eyes flick almost imperceptibly. There's calculation there—politics finding its angle.

Celeste Park: "You ask for a binding pilot—an independent oversight body, performance-based disbursements, and a legally-binding clause that opens remedies if targets aren't met. That's a slower path. Investors usually balk at strings."

You feel urgency knot into your stomach. The council's docket is scheduled; momentum matters. Delay could fray support. But so could a bad build.

Celeste listens, and for the first time you see the edges of a concession forming in her face." || Choice B ("Ask for time to talk to Elias and Amina before deciding") -> Reaction: "You ask the question out loud: time. Celeste's smile doesn't vanish, but her fingers tap a quick rhythm on the folder. Time is political capital; you're asking for a currency she wishes she'd never spend."]

Celeste Park: (after a beat) "We could structure phasing to include an initial legally-binding pilot: a contiguous two-block section with defined monitoring metrics, if it expedites permitting in exchange for that constraint." (she offers) "But the full funding package is contingent on some assurances about the promotional corridor remaining clear while we're building."

The offer is narrower than you feared and wider than you hoped. It's a hinge-awarding you leverage but demanding a kind of restraint from your allies—the very people who make the Commons a living thing.

Your phone buzzes once on the table. You don't check it. The room pulses; the decision you didn't make yesterday has arrived, dressed in leather and legalese.

Then a soft knock. Elias steps in—unannounced formally, but not unwelcome. He sheds his field jacket, face flushed from walking. He carries his notebook and a quiet patience that makes the room feel different—personal, human, not just transactional.

Elias Navarro: "You asked me to come if talks moved beyond public forums." (he glances at you, then at Celeste) "I didn't expect to be offered a seat on the team, though." (he allows himself a thin laugh)

Celeste Park: (smooth) "We thought it beneficial to have someone with local technical credibility. You'd be an invaluable liaison."

Elias's expression is an honest map of conflicting pulls. He looks to you, not to Celeste, and the air shifts. He believes engineering can be a bridge. You know his default is to fix things by building them better—never to obscure them.

Elias Navarro: (softly to you) "Mara—I want to protect Saltwren as much as you do. If joining means we guarantee monitoring and community oversight, I'm willing to take that role. But I can't promise it will be easy."

He meets your eyes with a steadiness that disarms. The offer of his participation, mediated by Celeste, feels like a ladder tossed across a gap. Your pulse quickens—hope and a spiky fear braid together.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "If Elias is willing to advocate for performance-based releases and real transparency, that's significant. But the contract language must include enforceable triggers and an independent audit by a third party agreed on by both the cooperative and the community."

Celeste Park: (a shade less composed) "An independent auditor is acceptable if they're credentialed and their scope is defined. We cannot entertain open-ended vetoes that stall the project."

The room tightens. The rising arousal becomes a thrumming energy: the investor timeline, the town's families, your late sibling's absence folding into every clause you read.

You imagine the Commons under excavation—planks uprooted, seedlings boxed and moved—if the wrong method is chosen. You imagine your neighbors with cash to move inland, their faces slack with relief. The image pushes at your chest. Hope and responsibility are not separate now; they are coiled.

Mara (you): "If Elias signs on, he'll need protections to speak openly. No gag clauses. No behind-closed-doors changes to the engineering specs." (you feel the words cut to the quick of the folder on the table) "Our community must be part of the monitoring panel, with binding authority to halt work that violates agreed-upon thresholds."

Celeste Park: (measured) "You're asking for a seat at the table with veto power. That's a big ask, Mara. But it's not impossible."

There is a beat where the world seems to hold its breath. The sunlight over the harbor slants like an omen of possibility.

You realize the choice in front of you is not binary between preservation and progress. It's three jagged roads: take the money and move fast, slow the machine with legal iron, or blow the lid off in public and trust the town to decide. The adrenaline in your veins sharpens each option into crystalline clarity.

Celeste slides the contract toward you—pages fanned like a promise. The relocation fund is there in bold figures; permissive engineering clauses are woven in; a clause about "limitations on disruptive actions" is tucked into a section that could be interpreted strictly. Elias reaches forward, fingers hovering but not touching.

Your pulse is loud in your ears; your hands brush against the warm ceramic of the coffee you accepted earlier. The room hums with positive possibility—this could be the resource that saves lives—and with pressure so intense it feels like the wind before a storm breaks in a good and terrible way.

[Music: Swells — high, urgent strings; a hopeful brass undercurrent]

You have to choose.
