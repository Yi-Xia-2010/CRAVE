# Route: Additional Route / Ending


[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of boardwalk and raised beds; morning mist lifting off tidal pools]
[Music: Soft ambient guitar with distant gulls]
[Sound: Wooden planks creak under slow footsteps; faint drip of water from a tarp]

You step onto the boardwalk with your boots half-full of last night's mud and two fingers of salt still on your lips from the walk down the tide line. The Commons wakes in small, deliberate motions: a spray of brine on your hands when you reach for the railing, the warm sour of compost when you kneel to check a bedridden seed tray, the way the mist makes everything a little softer around the edges. This is the work you know by touch before you name it—the pull of reed roots, the patience of salt-tolerant seedlings, the careful tilt of a reclaimed channel so water lingers where it must.

The reclaimed plots glint in the light. Someone has nailed new signage—fresh paint over an older slogan—and a string of paper lanterns still hangs from driftwood poles, drying stubbornly even as the sun comes up. Beyond the marsh grass the sea is a sheet of slate; the tide is going out and leaves a mirror of smeared clouds in the shallow pools. You breathe in. It smells like all the things that make Solhaven feel like home: kelp, wet wood, rain waiting somewhere up the coast.

A familiar voice breaks the quiet like a practiced oar stroke.

Tommy: "Mara. You see those mussel lines? Held through the night. Lord knows that's something."

You look up to find Tommy leaning against the post where the boardwalk bends, a thermos in one hand, the other hooked on his beanie. His face reads the weather the way you read a soil sample—old map lines and worn roads. He blinks against the light and grins, the grin more tired than celebratory.

You: "Good. That'll help with the nets next week. Any word from the northern fleet?"

Tommy: "Quiet. Better quiet than not, right? Though—" He shifts, the optimism oddly cautious. "Council's got papers in the trailer outside the Promenade. Word is a presentation later. Pretty suit boys showing maps."

You feel the word 'presentation' the way you feel a nail through wood: small and invasive. You steady your hands on the railing.

You (internal): The Promenade plans—Celeste's banners, hard lines, straight concrete—have been the shadow in every conversation these last months. You can map the tension across your own palms: living shorelines versus a hardened edge, community plots versus a paved promenade. Both promises sound like stability. Both ask us to trade something away.

Tommy: (soft) "Rae's got a flyer. Says there's a meeting tonight. She wants you there."

Before you can answer, a flicker of movement: Rae emerges from between two raised beds, paint on her fingers and a stencil rolled under her arm. She smells of aerosol and tea. She sprawls on a nearby bench as if the bench has always belonged to her.

Rae: "Morning, Mara. You're gonna love what I did with the protest placards. Or hate them. Depends on your mood." (She lifts a hand; it's smeared in teal.) "Also, who do you want me to flip to the front page? 'Grassroots' or 'Grassroots—and stubborn'?"

You: "Both. Keep the nuance."

Rae's laugh is quick and usable; it tucks a corner of the edge off the morning's tension.

Rae: "Also—Tommy says someone's at the tide line. Bringin' a tablet and polite shoes." She winks. "Looks like corporate diplomacy, but he sent me a selfie with a light windbreaker and an expression that says 'I have a plan.'"

You glance toward the tide line. A figure stands where the wet sand meets the boardwalk shadow—slim, methodical movements that betray an engineering gait more than a fisherman’s. He holds a tablet against his chest, like a small shield. The air folds itself into that familiar space of strangers with proposals: courteous distance, measured optimism.

[Visual: Tight on Elias as he walks up the damp path; light catches the edge of his jacket]
[Sound: Soft, rhythmic tapping of his shoes on wet wood]

Elias: "Good morning. I'm Elias—Elias Navarro." (He offers a hand, polite, slightly tentative.) "I work with the Coastal Cooperative. We—" He pauses, scanning the boardwalk, taking in wooden signs and seed trays the way someone reads a room. His blue-gray eyes hold a quiet that isn't blank; it's attentive, like a notebook learning to trust your handwriting. "I didn't mean to interrupt."

You feel the small heat of your palm as you take his hand. His grip is steady, neither too firm nor slack. There's something honest in his pause—an engineer learning how much to say and when to listen.

You: "You didn't. We're usually better at interrupting ourselves." (You nod toward the beds.) "Mara Lin. We run the salt beds and the marsh channels."

Elias: "I've read about Saltwren. Dr. Bhatt mentioned your planting protocol. She—" He stops again, as if measuring how public praise lands. "She said you ground things in the tide. Thought it worth seeing with my own eyes."

Rae: (elbowing you) "See? Not all visitors have power suits. Some have gadgets and glyphs, too." (She grins at Elias like she's already allies with both of you.)

Tommy: "So. Come to show us a map? Or come to sell us something?"

Elias: (a dry half-smile) "Neither. I brought a set of proposals—engineering overlays, if you will. But I'm here to listen. Really." (He gestures to his tablet.) "We want to design something that actually works for people here. I figured starting with you—Saltwren—makes sense."

You feel the tilt of that sentence. It could be earnest. It could be the beginning of negotiation theater. You keep your face even; it's what you do—let the work show you instead of promises.

You (internal): He says 'listen' and you hear 'implement.' You know engineers who mean 'do' and organizers who mean 'stay.' You want to know which he is before you let any plans cross the boardwalk.

Elias: "If there's time, I'd love to walk the tide line with you. I can show what we've modeled and get immediate feedback." (He catches your neutral look.) "Or—if you'd rather wait until the public forum tonight, that's understandable. I know community spaces are—" His voice smooths, careful not to push.

The morning breathes around that offer. The birds pick at the spilled seeds, the lanterns above you sway like watching eyes, and the low tide leaves the marsh to its quiet maintenance. The choice in front of you is small in gesture and large in implication: an early, private conversation that could tilt trust or an insistence on public process that honors the Commons' communal life.

Your pulse doesn't race. It steadies. This is part of the careful work: decisions made by hands that have felt the tide shift.

You weigh both options—meet now and start a quiet, personal exchange that could build a working relationship, or wait for the public forum and hold him accountable to the Commons' collective voice. Neither choice is dramatic. Neither is neutral. Both are the slow work of keeping place and people together.

Your chest settles around the decision like a hand.

[Visual: Close on your hands resting on the railing; the sea mirrors the sky]
[Music: The guitar softens; a single sustained note hangs]

You look back at Elias. He folds his hands in front of him, an open, unassuming posture. Rae taps her stencil against her knee with a restless energy that could become rally or camaraderie. Tommy hums as he fixes a rope, and the Commons holds its breath with you.

You are ready to choose.
