You wake to a town that is howling.

The last chord from the council chamber is still in your bones — the gavel, the bulldozers, the forwarded image of machines waiting at the promenade like teeth. Now those images are drowned by a different noise: wind like a fist, rain like small stones. Your satchel is across your shoulder, damp at the strap. Your boots make a wet slap on the pavement as you move.

You think of the Saltwren Commons: raised beds, boardwalks, your seed stores. You think of the promenade site: half-done pilings that could funnel water into neighborhoods. You think of the people who sleep with the ocean in their ears and those whose roofs will not hold. Memory is a dangerous, practical thing tonight — your sibling’s last night presses across the back of your throat like a warning.

Mara Lin: "We need triage. Now."

Your voice is small, steady only because you force it to be. Around you, the emergency coordination center in the old mill — repurposed by the council for tonight — is incandescent with activity. People shout into radios, mark maps with grease pencil, shepherd smaller hands through barricaded aisles. The Mill's rooftop garden thrums with generators and human bodies, and you move through it like a scalpel.

[Visual: The Mill's interior, fluorescent lights shaking from gusts; monitors showing tide projections and radar]
[Sound: Radio crackle: "—twelve-foot surge predicted—" "Repeat—twelve-foot—"]
[Music: Rapid strings; heartbeat percussion]

Elias stands by a battered conference table, jacket dark with rain, a tablet clasped to his side. His face is lit by the glow of a tide-forecast model — blue bars jagged into alarm. For a beat he looks up at you with something like rawness in his eyes, as if the equations had stripped him down. He does not look like a man comfortable with chaos — but tonight he looks knotted by it.

Elias Navarro: "They just pushed the surge window earlier. Tide's aligning with the storm center. If the breakwaters don't hold, the promenade will act like a channel. We can reroute pumps, shore up temporary berms, but I need crews on the north flank first."

You want to ask which houses he means. You want to tell him to pick the nursing home on Larkin Street where Mrs. Ayodele lives and keeps a porcelain dove above her bed because it makes her feel like the sea is friendlier. But the Schrödinger rule sits heavy in your chest: you can't assume what he thinks you know about local pockets or what resources exist. Instead you aim for the goal.

You: "Who do we get moving now? Official rescue, or people-powered skiffs?"

He exhales, a breath that tastes like rain on metal.

Elias Navarro: "Both. I can call for county assets, but they’re stretched. If the county can spare the coastal skiff they'll go for the largest clusters — senior centers and the promenade strip. The problem is the western alleyways; they're narrow, and the skiffs can't reach them if the water rises too fast."

You feel the surge of urgency build under your ribs. Very high. The room has narrowed to the breath between orders and lives.

Dr. Amina Bhatt materializes at your elbow, her field vest patched with instrument loops, hair pinned tight. She hands you a printout — sediment projections, clarified into bullet points.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "If the promenade breaches, scouring will redirect the surge east. Low-lying homes by the marsh will be first. Pumps buy time, not miracles. We need to prioritize evacuation points that are defensible and accessible from both road and water."

You: "Tommy? Where is Tommy?"

Tommy is at the map, hands blackened with rope grease. He looks like the town — battered, ready, unbelieving.

Tomas 'Tommy' Reyes: "I've got crews on the boats. Rae's moving supplies to the Commons for people who choose to ride it out there. But the Commons itself will flood if the tide comes up high. We can stack rakes and sandbags, but…"

His voice breaks on the last word and the rawness becomes a practical count of the cost. You think of the Saltwren boardwalk under the gold light of evening, now a strip of wood across a hungry sea.

Celeste arrives with a slick folder and a waterproofed face. Her hair is plastered to her temple; her trench coat keeps a neat line despite the weather, which irritates you more than it should. She moves with the kind of decision that used to make her persuasive in council rooms; tonight that same decisiveness scrapes.

Celeste Park: "We have a temporary policy directive. The county is authorizing prioritization by infrastructure value and maximum lives saved per asset. That’s the legal feed-in — it's efficient."

You flinch at the language. "Efficient" in Celeste's mouth is often a synonym for "calculable," a shield against the messy human things you try to hold. You don't answer immediately because the question CANNOT be answered with spreadsheets alone.

Mara Lin (internal): You don't have the luxury of idealism tonight. You never did. But that doesn't make the choices less corrosive.

Elias meets your eyes after you act. There's a tension in his mouth.

Elias Navarro: (softly) "You'll have to coordinate. If we route skiffs differently we'll leave a path exposed. The county will notice if we divert assets. They might reallocate last-minute."

You: "And if we don't?" The question isn't rhetorical. You know the possible answers too well.

Elias Navarro: "If we don't, smaller pockets will be cut off. People could be stuck in attics before the rescue gets there."

He says it like a meteorologist who also cannot shake the image of a neighbor's porch disappearing. You imagine the slivers of someone's life bobbing like flotsam.

The roof above the Mill trembles with a gust. A radio operator screams into a handset and the word "breach" cuts through like glass. Someone shouts a coordinate: Promenade, Sector B. Bulldozers at the promenade, you remember, are already queued — and now they're at the wrong place at the wrong time.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We have to decide triage priorities. We can't save everyone at once. We need defensible clusters."

Tomas: "Defend the Commons. If people won't leave, at least make it survivable for a night."

Celeste: "That is a liability. We need to protect critical infrastructure — pumps, main artery roads — so that recovery can follow. If we lose the pumps, half the town never recovers."

Your head is a thunderclap of options. The core truth sits between Celeste's clipped logic and Tommy's marrow-deep urgency. Elias shifts his weight unbearably, like a man caught between two calculations that both carry blood.

Mara Lin (internal): You can feel the air thin. Each second is a ledger. You cannot save everyone. The ledger will be written in names.

You see the way Dr. Amina watches you when you act: tired eyes that always search for the scientific constant in human chaos. There is a look she gives — part instruction, part plea.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We need to be explicit. Priority A: human life, but within that, triage by accessibility and vulnerability. Priority B: people whose loss would cascade — elderly clustered in small houses, the community food pantry. Priority C: infrastructure that will prevent future loss. It's messy. It's morally messy."

Celeste bristles, but she is also, tonight, operating within a legal framework that could bankrupt the town if ignored. You see her hands flex on the folder.

Celeste Park: "Mara, you're the community organizer. Your people's lives are your argument. Use your credibility to keep panic from cascading into bad choices."

Her words are oddly tender. They also feel like a blade. You taste salt and metal.

The radio screams again; this time more urgent. Sector C — families trapped on the promenade walkways. You remember: the promenade, half-finished, corrals the water. If that section fails, the surge could leap across to the eastern lanes where rental blocks slope down.

Mara Lin: "Tommy, what’s our ETA for shoreline crews? Rae can coordinate volunteers at the Commons, but the promenade needs professionals."

Tommy: "Two boats, fifteen minutes if the channel holds. We can jury-rig lines but —"

He stops because his voice chokes. You know what he's choking on: the people who will be beyond reach if those fifteen minutes stretch. You can almost see the tide's teeth finding their purchase.

Elias presses a hand to his temple, then, unexpectedly, takes the tablet and begins tapping commands. Technical fixes — a reroute here, a web of radio frequencies there. For a moment he acts the way he taught you in calm days: methodical, hopeful. It helps. It doesn't fix the moral torque.

Elias Navarro: "If I can get a pump diversion on the western culvert and grid a priority channel, the skiffs can get to the promenade walkway from the south side. We might pull three families per run."

You imagine those families — faces in rain, a child's stuffed animal drenched, someone clutching a photograph. Your throat tightens.

Mara Lin (internal): Every triage is a set of names you will have to live with choosing or not. You do not know which of those names will return to you in the future with gratitude or blame or silent space. You only know this: someone will stand at the door and say your name with no tone left.

A shriek of metal. A generator halts. The lights stutter and the overhead map goes dim, then a single emergency lamp flips on. The Mill answers the storm by breathing with it — quick, irregular. The room moves faster.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We're running on three supply lines now. If any go, we lose immediate communications to the northern quarter."

Celeste: "Then prioritize the northern quarter communications. If we can't coordinate, we lose control."

Tommy: "If we lose the northern quarter, we lose entire crews. There's a school there."

You feel the entropy of decisions: fix this, and that fails. Do that, and a different heartbreak opens. The arousal — the body's urgent drum, the mind's hurricane — climbs past anything you felt in the Council. This is no longer debate. This is triage, real and bitter and terribly final.

Elias turns to you, desperation threading his voice thinner.

Elias Navarro: "Mara — it comes down to deploy now or hold for coordination. If we deploy without county support, we can save people who aren’t on the official list, but we risk losing legal cover for the town. If we wait, county skiffs will take the official registry first."

You look at him. You search his face for the math of compromise he always offers. Tonight, compromise tastes like delay.

Mara Lin: "We keep making calls," you hear yourself saying, voice raw. "We do both. We move. We log. We tell county exactly what we’re sending. We don’t pretend we can save everyone."

The room bites on your words. It is as close to a command as you can make without pronouncement. People nod, set into motion. Radios click awake. Boots slap.

A thunderclap, closer now, rolls the windows. The promenade's beacon — a small construction light — snaps and tilts. Someone passes a handheld to you. The decision point is immediate, enormous, unavoidable. You can feel the weight of every life in the town like an additive pressure on your chest.

Mara Lin (internal): You want to do it all. You want to fold the world into a palm and stop the water. There is no magic. Only choices. Only triage. Only the terrible arithmetic of here and now.

For a dizzying instant, every face in the Mill is a story: Tommy's hands, Celeste's folder, Amina's quiet calculations, Rae's furious energy, the names on the community roster that you cannot see but feel. Elias's jaw is clenched to the brink of breaking.

Then the radio screams a new coordinate — closer, more urgent — and hands move faster.

[Sound: The wind seems to say the word 'now' in every gust]

You step toward the door.

[Music: Crescendo to a raw, unresolved peak; drums like a storm heartbeat]

You are only seconds from motion. The next directive — which channels will move, which neighborhoods to prioritize, who will go by skiff and who will be told to move to high ground at the Commons — will set the immediate fate of people you love and people you barely know.

Your lungs want to collapse; your fingers tremble on the doorframe. The siren on the generator blares in a single, sustained tone that feels like accusation and prayer.
