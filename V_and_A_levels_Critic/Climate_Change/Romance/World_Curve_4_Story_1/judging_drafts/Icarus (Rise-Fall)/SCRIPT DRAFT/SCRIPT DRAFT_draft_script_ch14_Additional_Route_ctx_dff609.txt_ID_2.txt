You look for Elias through the crowd. He is there—shirt sleeves damp, jaw tight, a tablet under one arm with streaming tide models. Elias: "They just updated the projection. Surge could top the promenade deck within—" (he swallows) "—thirty minutes if the wind holds. The regional command wants to prioritize the structural anchors; their plan stages rescue by pre-designated sectors."

You: "Pre-designated by who? The models or the investors?" Your voice is raw; rain pricks at the collar of your jacket.

Elias: "Both. The command's concern is logistics. If anchors fail, rescue routes from the north will be entirely cut off. You can see him balancing protocol on one palm and the town's faces on the other. The sight tightens something low in your ribs.

Tommy pushes through, coat slapping with water, the smell of old salt and diesel faint on his breath. He's breathing like someone who's been rowing hard.

Tommy: "Mara—there's a family at the southern slip, kids. One of Rae's crew says the boardwalk's gone. Their car's floating like a bathtub. I can take my skiff but—" (he looks at the chain-link where construction lights blink) "—I don't want a damn inspector on my chest later. People first."

You: "How many able hands with you? How fast can you move against the tide?"

Tommy: "Four. We know the channels. We can be there in five. We can get children off things you wouldn't expect to hold a person in this weather."

Amina steps forward from behind a bank of monitors. Her face is lit by screens and concern. She takes a breath and steadies herself to the scientific rhythm, but there is no comfort to be offered beyond cold facts.

Dr. Amina: "Surge timing is narrowing. The highest tide will coincide with the strongest gusts in under forty minutes. You: "So both places are dangerous in different ways." You say it without wanting to. Your mind reels through maps: boardwalk ladders, Ray's painted signs, the greenhouse vents at Saltwren that trap warm air—human anchors to memory.

Dr. Amina: "Exactly. We do a time-sensitive triage: save people most at imminent risk and secure zones that, if they fail, create cascading failures. But resources are thin. Even the county teams are stretched."

A radio chirps; a voice you recognize as a volunteer coordinator reports that a crew is trapped halfway along the promenade with slashed life-lines and a sprained ankle between them and shore. The volunteer's breath is audible in the speaker—fast, ragged.

You feel the room tilt into a single decision—an old thing dressed in new words. There are not enough boats, not enough rescue tech, not enough hours before the surge. Everything has to be chosen.

Elias leans forward, voice low and measured despite the weather in it.

Elias: "If the promenade anchors give, we lose the main staging area. The command will want us to pull everyone back and secure the heavy equipment. It's the system move; it protects infrastructure that saves more people later."

You: "And if we pull back, those people are still on that half-finished stretch now." Your words come as if from somewhere behind your teeth. You can smell the metallic tang of blood from the stretcher in your mind. "So 'later' might mean 'never' for them."

Elias: "I know. I'm saying—" (he searches for a phrase that won't end in a fight) "—we can try a targeted extraction with trained crews while we shore the anchors. It's risky. But the command's liability concerns mean they won't sign off on ad hoc teams."

You: "Trained by whom? County command? There's a lag. Tommy's crew knows those shoals like their hands."

Tommy: "We don't need training. We need conviction. We'll take the southern channel. I'll go with you—" (he looks at Elias) "—if you'll sign off here and now, Mara."

Elias's face flinches at the public ask, the crack in his composure like a small glass. He opens his mouth, then stops, torn between the weight of policy and his trust in people who have been his neighbors for half a year, a year—how long has it been since you first showed him the seed beds?

Elias: "I—" (he swallows) "—I can file an exception, but it complicates evacuation sequencing. If it goes wrong and the anchors fail, we'd be responsible for clearing a worse scene."

You: "If it goes wrong and we do nothing, we'd live with that, too." The sentence lands heavier than any argument about liability. It is not just about rules; it is about who you have to look at in the morning.

Celeste: "Mara, Elias, the investors want the promenade secured. If the anchors fail, we lose funding and the project collapses. We can—" (she leans in, voice soft but exact) "—coordinate a prioritized structural hold with heavy machines at the north access. That keeps evacuation lanes open for the bulk of the town."

You: "The 'bulk of the town' is an abstraction when someone's ankle is crushed on the promenade now, Celeste." Your patience cracks. "We can't let calculus erase the person under the harness."

Celeste: "This is not calculus. This is triage at scale. Decisions have to be made with the greatest net benefit." Her eyes flick to Elias, sharp and appraising, and then to you. For a blink her expression softens and becomes unreadable again. (Complex.)

Elias reaches out, fingers brushing the laminated sheet. His palm trembles for the first time—not from cold but from the weight of choosing who gets a boat.

Elias: "Mara—" (he searches) "—we don't have the resources to guarantee both. If we attempt both and the anchors fail, we risk losing both crews. If we consolidate at one, we will save fewer people now but maintain the ability to respond later."

You feel the comprehension cut you open: the calculus of survival. Not heroic in the abstract, but intimate and awful. The storm isn't a metaphor tonight; it is a mechanized, indifferent thing, and you are the person appointed to measure out mercy.
