You follow the judge's last look out to the dark, thinking the weight of his silence is an omen. The chamber empties in a press of coats and murmurs; someone says "evac" and the word snaps at you like a command. Outside, the sea has already started to climb.

You are Mara Lin. Your lungs taste of metal and the stale coffee from the hearing room. Your palms are damp. You move because motion is how you steady yourself: toward the hub where the town scrambles into order.

[Scene: Town Hall — Emergency Coordination Center | Night]
[Visual: Folding tables strewn with maps, printouts taped to kettles of lukewarm tea; fluorescent lights flicker]
[Sound: Radios loop, a generator drones; shoes scuff on linoleum]
[Music: The percussion quickens; a sharpened, anxious tempo]

Dr. Amina Bhatt meets you at the threshold, her voice a steady blade. "Mara," she says, and the name in her mouth is both an anchor and an accusation. "Surge models moved up an hour. We’ve got flash inundation and—" She doesn't finish; the map she slaps down on the table says it all: the half-finished promenade is a target zone; the low-lying terraces by Saltwren are already under seven inches.

You look at Elias across the table. He is in the center of a cluster: his tablet open, graphs alight in the storm-blue glow. He is calm in the way of someone used to translating panic into plans, but his hands are not still. He fingers the border of a photo on his device — a neighbor's porch, a child's toy half-buried in silt. You have watched him wear policy like armor; now you see the invisible seam where that armor pinches.

Elias: "We need to move shelter points inland. Priority is hospitals and the nursing pavilion." (He meets your eyes, pleading for the logic to hold.)

You: "Priority is people who are already cut off. The promenade crews—construction workers—are trapped on the pier and there are calls from the Commons: water into the boardwalk entrances." (You push the map so close the edges bend.)

Amina: "We triangulated projections. If the seawall fails at sector three, the flood will take the promenade quickly and push back through the low blocks in twenty to thirty minutes. We can stage rescuers from the high ground but—" (Her voice goes thin. She points at a cluster of house icons marked red.)

Tommy lurches into the ring, breath rattling like fishing line. "My crew's boats are out. We can do shuttles from the harbor mouth, but the tide's coming in faster. We ain't got clearance for all the trips." He looks at you, at Elias. "You know us. We get people out. But we need a go."

Elias runs a hand through his wet hair. "Official directives—regional command—has asked to coordinate evacuations by sector. They don't want uncoordinated boats running into rescue lines."

You don't let him finish. The hub is a pressure cooker. People need decisions now, not rationales that will arrive later.

The center hums with competing tempos: machine-logic versus lived urgency. Your chest tightens with the gravity of every delayed second.

Elias: "If we do uncoordinated launches, we risk interfering with SAR corridors and risking professional teams." (He keeps his voice measured; you hear strain.)

You: "If we wait for corridors to be cleared, people drown behind chain-link." (You say it like a fact. The room quiets; even the generator seems to listen.)

Amina steps between you, palms flat on the wet map. "We need a triage line. Medical evacuation gets priority. After that—" She looks at you both as if you are the instruments she must tune. "We balance probability of survival, distance, and resource bandwidth. That's the only defensible calculus in the next hour."

Tommy: "Folks don't think in probability when their roofs are in water." (He spits the words. His voice carries the low salt of someone who has lost nets and men to the tide before.)

[Scene: Saltwren Commons → Boardwalk Entrance | Night — Wind Rising]
[Visual: Boardwalk slats groan; gardens slick with rain; driftwood hisses against pilings]
[Sound: Wind, closer now; a child's muffled cry carried like debris]

You run the Commons route with Rae and two volunteers, boots sucking in wet compost. The air is thick with seaweed and mashed kelp; every breath tastes like the edge of the ocean. Lanterns bob, their light shaking in the wind. You can hear the promenade beyond: metal grinding metal, something large groaning like an animal.

Rae: "They said the elderly cluster on Mariner's Row couldn't get out. Their generator's tripped. The boardwalk's flooded to the first benches."

You: "How many?" (You probe for the facts that will let you choose where to put your force.)

Rae: "Seven registered residents. Two kids who came down to help their grandparents. The rest are houses clustered under the mill's lee." (She swallows, the sentence small against the wind.)

You run a thumb across the copper bracelet at your wrist — your anchor, the old marsh coordinates carved into it — and feel the tremor of the moment. The karma of every past flood weighs small and heavy at the same time.

[Scene: Half-Finished Promenade | Night — Water Lapping the First Rebar]
[Visual: Concrete skeletons like exposed bones; survey flags whipped ragged; floodlights stutter]
[Sound: Aboard hulls groaning; the roar of water; someone yelling indistinctly]
[Music: Percussion peaks; a thin, urgent trumpet line cuts through]

A call comes across Elias' channel. "Pier crew—seven people—are clinging to the girders. They reported structural shift. The tide's taken the lower scaffold."

Elias looks at you and then at his tablet. He will cite protocols if asked. He will argue risk matrices. Instead, his voice is suddenly small. "They signed in as essential, but they're contractors. The official roster excludes independent subs without liability forms."

You feel the word "contractors" like a splinter.

You: "They're people," you say. "They have names."

Elias meets your gaze, and for a heartbeat you see the whole world's ledger reflected — stamps, permissions, the clean lines of institutional boundaries that have to hold in a bureaucracy. His jaw tightens.

Elias: "If I authorize deviation, I put my grant compliance at risk. That could—" (He stops. The technical language dissolves into something raw.) "—it could shutter aid that reaches the whole town later."

You: "Or we lose people now because the abstract future took precedence." (You don't realize you've raised your voice until Tommy looks up sharply.)

Tommy: "Mara—Elias—this ain't about forms. It's about hands on ropes and who can make the run without flipping."

Celeste arrives in a coat that refuses to look weathered. The storm cannot wash the precision from her posture. Her eyes scan the board like a strategist reading enemy lines.

Celeste: "We need triage that's transparent and defensible. Prioritize the institutional shelters—hospitals, nursing homes. The promenade crew is tragic, but exposure will be brief if we hold the north barrier." (Her words are deliberate, economical.)

You see the calculus in her face: quick wins, optics, the ability to sell stability back to investors. Her voice is the town's potential salvation hammered into a marketable plan, and you can tell, in how she tilts her head, that she measures human distress against the ledger in her head.

You: "If the north barrier doesn't hold, there is no 'brief' exposure. That 'exposure' becomes the Commons, the low blocks, the nursery at the mill." (You speak fast, breath shallow. The air stings.)

Celeste's expression tightens for a blink — unreadable, complex. She steps closer, close enough that the wind carries her perfume like a calm before a different kind of storm.

Celeste: "We cannot spend our capital on ad hoc rescues. We must think long-term protection for the greatest number."

Elias: "If we lose credibility with funders now, Celeste's plan won't exist to protect anyone. We need to balance the ledger." (He looks between you and her, and the tension warps the space like heat over asphalt.)

A minute stretches. The radios hiss again: more calls. The promenade's structure is shifting. The Commons' boardwalk is starting to float in places. Mariner's Row's lights blink out.

[Scene: Emergency Coordination Center | Night — The Hub Tightens]
[Visual: Faces in the light of screens; a damp map folded under clammy fingers]
[Sound: All channels feed into a white-noise chorus; pagers; someone sobbing quietly]
[Music: The tempo is relentless—no space to recover; cello and timpani hammering]

Elias slides a dossier across the table. "If I authorize the harbor launches, I reclassify them under municipal authority and I take liability. It buys them legal cover. But it means our regional partners might freeze funding until there's a report."

You feel the ledger again. You imagine losing future aid. You imagine losing present people.

You: "Authorize it." (The words rip out of you like a plead. "We take the liability. We run the boats. We get people up.") You expect Elias to protest technique. Instead, his hand hovers over the tablet like a diver at the edge.

Elias: "I can't just—" (He breaks, then steadies into a practical breath.) "Okay. Do it as a municipal directive. I will log chain-of-command, and we notify legal after."

You watch his fingers move, an act that binds him on two fronts: to the people and to the rules that will judge the people later.

Amina: "We need medevac channels. We need to mark no-go zones. I'll reroute the field medics and keep the triage nodes at the Commons high ground." (She is already speaking like someone knitting thresholds into place.)

Tommy: "We got crews on the dock. We can load two per run in these conditions. It's rotten math but it's the math we got." (His voice is terrified bravado.)

Celeste: "And who is coordinating air resources? Who is securing the north barrier?" (Her question is practical, snap-and-seal.)

You look at Elias when Celeste asks; his face falters. He is the hinge between two systems, and his center is cracking.

Elias: "Air assets are delayed. Regional command says they'll reallocate if we can show a defensible strategy." (His voice is small and bright like a fuse about to light.)

You: "Then we make a defensible strategy now." (You set your jaw. "We shore up the north barrier with volunteer sand arrays. We stage medevac at the Commons. We run boats on municipal authority.")

Elias nods, but it is not an easy motion. "I'll send the paperwork as I authorize launches. But Mara—" (He leans forward. The hub noise subsides around the two of you.) "If we do this, there will be audits after. People will ask who signed off on the risk. People will point fingers."

You know what he means. Fingers point in courts and in headlines. Relationships are not immune to that. You also know that if you let the finger-pointing be the reason you don't move, lives will write the postscript.

You glance at Celeste. Her expression remains complex. She is all policy and polish, but now there is salt on her collar. For a breath you see a version of her that is almost human: the jawline that clenches when she sees someone trembling, the small tightening at the corner of a mouth that might be worry.

Tommy slams a fist on the table. "We don't have any more minutes. We pick targets and we go."

The radio crackles with coordinates: two clusters—Mariner's Row (the elderly cluster), Promenade Pier (contractors), and a third ping you didn't have on your map: a fishing shack where a single family stayed behind to unhook gear, now boxed in by a sudden eddy.

Your throat constricts. The storm is a living logic now, outrunning models. Every allocation is a sentence.

You breathe. You can feel Elias beside you — his presence is warm and uneven. His hand brushes yours when he slides his tablet; it's a touch that wants to say something un-mechanical, but the words are drowned by the radios.

Elias: "Mara, I—" (He stops, any professional cadence undone.)

You: "I know." (You don't need him to finish. You know what the knot in his throat says: that he is about to strike a balance that will be judged by both the living and the ledger.)

Amina's voice breaks through: "Command needs a decisive mission order, now."

You look at the map. Coordinates glow like wounds.

Your throat tastes like iron. Time compresses into an acute point. The room around you seems to narrow to the width of the table, to the space between you and Elias, between orders and hearts.

You can still feel the warmth on your wrist, the copper that remembers the old marsh. You choose with more than logic. You choose with the gravity of loss in your bones.

You stand up. The table's paper slides under your palm. Everyone in the room lifts their eyes like they are waiting for a bell.

You know that whichever name you pronounce first will be etched into the night.
