[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Dawn — hours after the storm]

[Music: Fractured strings; a high, keening violin line beneath a low rumble]

You open your eyes to a world that has been rewritten in salt and splintered wood. The Commons smells of wet earth, algae, and something metallic—old pipes, new blood. Someone calls Tommy's name; someone else calls for water. Hands move—some sure, some trembling—through the wreckage.

You stand on a tilted stretch of boardwalk, boots half-covered in slurry, and trying to remember which thought came first: the radio, the maps, the choice you were about to make. Memory tastes like copper. The rain has stopped but the sky is a bruise. The town's edges are ragged.

Elias is there, his shirt muddied, a tablet clutched like a talisman. He looks at you, and for a beat you register something like guilt and calculation braided together—an expression that could be apology or resignation. He doesn't say anything. He just folds his hands into the wound between you.

Elias Navarro: "We set up triage on the high school gym. They're moving people—slowly. I—" (he stops, searching for language that might mend more than it will)

You: "Slowly?" (You let the word hang like a splintered plank.) "Tommy shouted that the lower lanes were cut off. The clinic—"

Elias Navarro: (interrupting) "I know. I was on the comms. We routed ambulances around the washouts. We—" (His voice cracks; he sucks it back.) "We did what we could."

You swallow. The plans you carried in your head—contingency, triage, the slow architecture of care—feel petty and brittle beside the immediacy of a neighbor's gashed forehead. Your hands make decisions of their own: handing out thermal wraps, unwinding a rope, pressing a damp cloth to a child's cheek.

[Scene: Solhaven Promenade | Late morning — charred and torn]

[Visual: Survey flags tangled in seaweed; a chunk of newly poured concrete lies as a mockery on the beach. Corporate banners hang in tatters from a shredded crane.]
[Music: Percussive, staccato; breaths like timpani]
[Sound: Heavy machinery groaning; people arguing; the clatter of loose metal]

You walk the promenade because the town keeps pulling you towards what was promised. The Promenade is a wound—iron ribs of rebar sticking from a concrete carcass, piles of linens where people have made impromptu beds. A woman rocks, nursing a baby, eyes white. Workers in reflective vests avoid looking at the crowd.

Celeste stands near a cluster of men in branded jackets, umbrella held like an ornament even though the sky is dry. Her coat is perfection in a place gone to ruin; it somehow makes her stand out like a brochure at a funeral.

Celeste Park: (soft, measured) "We are mobilizing emergency funds. The foundation will—" (she gestures to her team) "—coordinate shelter placement and infrastructure triage. We have specialists en route."

You: (you laugh, a short, bitter sound) "Specialists." (You step closer; your hands smell of compost and sweat.) "You have specialists now. Where were they when the shoreline flooded the east lane?"

Celeste Park: (cool) "I am here now, Mara. That is what matters. We can rebuild."

You: "Rebuild for whom?" (The question is not rhetorical. It is a blade.) "For investors who don't live here? For whoever signs the papers that let them come back next season? People are injured—people died."

Celeste's face hardens, then softens like someone practicing public grief on a mannequin.

Celeste Park: "Loss is terrible. It is also a reminder that the plans needed leadership. We will act."

Your words and hers collide and neither is satisfied. Around you, a man argues that the pumps failed because maintenance contracts were canceled in a cost-cutting sweep months ago. Someone else says the temporary barriers were never built. The air tastes of blame.

Elias steps between you and Celeste without meaning to, palms raised like an engineer offering a diagram as if that could bridge grief.

Elias Navarro: "We need a coordinated chain of command right now. Finger-pointing—" (he breathes) "—won't get generators to the south row."

You: "So you make the chain of command and we all march to it." (Sardonic.) "Why is it always 'we' fix the mess?"

Elias Navarro: "Because the mess is here. Because people are hurt. You're asking for answers—I'll help you find them."

You feel something in your chest crack. It's not just the town breaking, it's the fragile idea that he and you could meet on the same map.

Celeste Park: (to Elias, quietly) "If we coordinate, we can move resources faster. We need Mara on board."

Elias's jaw tightens. For a second the three of you are an equation with too many unknowns and only one constant: the town's ribs are exposed.

[Scene: Various Neighborhoods & Reconstruction Sites | Afternoon — rubble and hospital tents]

[Visual: A row of temporary shelters; a makeshift memorial of stones and driftwood; volunteers forming human chains to pass supplies.]
[Music: Low, insistent drones cut with ragged piano; the rhythm of a heart both breaking and continuing]
[Sound: Quiet sobs; hammers; the distant roar of a generator]

You move through neighborhoods that smell of smoke and character. Houses that once kept winter out now gape like missing teeth. People hand you lists—who needs insulin, who needs diapers, who needs a shovel. You fill the gaps with your small, human hands.

Rae is painting names on a battered plank. She paints slowly, as if each letter can be a prayer. Tommy kneels beside an older man, wrapping his hand in a bandage. Dr. Amina grafts triage signs to poles, voice steady as a drill.

You find a quiet moment with Elias behind a collapsed garden wall. He leans his forehead against cold mortar, wet with rain and salt.

Elias Navarro: (whisper) "You asked me once—why I stay. I thought I could prove there are ways to hold both sides of a town together. I thought—" (he exhales) "—I thought I could make structures so people wouldn't have to choose between livelihood and safety."

You: (you let your hands rest on the rubble) "And now?"

Elias Navarro: "Now the calculus isn't hypothetical." (He looks at you; his eyes are raw.) "I made calls. I signed things. I argued for a timeline under pressure. Maybe I leaned toward solutions that looked implementable."

You: "Solutions that prioritized structures over people."

Elias Navarro: (immediately) "I didn't mean—" (He reaches for you. You do not take the hand.) "I tried to keep the clinic on the list for evacuation."

You: "You tried." (The words are a small blade.) "You tried and somewhere the list was reprioritized."

He closes his mouth. The silence is enormous.

Elias Navarro: "I know it looks like I chose the wrong ledger."

You: "It looks like people are gone."

His whole body trembles for a second, and you see not the policy wonk but the person who was once patient enough to trace marsh grass with his finger and explain the arc of a tide. He is apologizing in a language that won't mend bones.

Elias Navarro: "I can't undo what's happened. I can't—" (He swallows.) "I can help rebuild."

You: "Rebuild what? The promenade? Our old assumptions?"

He steps forward; his voice becomes the one you leaned on in quieter days.

Elias Navarro: "Mara, I—stay with me. Help lead the recovery. We can demand accountability and build better at the same time."

You imagine his hand outstretched, steady, a bridge. And you imagine your own hands—callused from soil, from giving orders in a way no one ever obeyed quite right—accepting it. But the image fractures into shards: faces you carried, names painted on planks, the copper bracelet at your wrist warm against your skin.

You think of your sibling, of the way water took them in a different storm. You think of the coordinates on the bracelet and the stubbornness that made you plant seedlings where the town had been razed. Your throat tightens not with anger but with the rawness of a decision poured out of you and into other people's hands.

You: "I can't be the person who makes those choices with you in the way you want." (It is a small, brittle confession.) "I need to make sure the places we love—" (you point to the Commons) "—aren't the price of some polished promenade."

Elias Navarro: (voice breaking) "So we split on principle."

You: "Maybe."

He inhales sharply. Around you, the town hums a painful, practical lullaby—buckets, nails, names being read aloud.

Elias Navarro: (softly) "I don't want us to end like this."

You: "Neither do I." (Then you laugh, and it tastes like salt.) "But things end how they end."

His face folds as if taken by a small storm of its own. He reaches and this time your hands close around his for a second—no promise, only the warmth of two people who had tried to hold the same world.

Elias Navarro: "If we can't save us, save them," he says. "Please."

You: "I will save them the way I know how. Not always agreeing. Not always with your plans. But I'll try."

He nods like someone accepting a verdict. The admission settles between you: love is not a guarantee against different judgments; it is a place where grief sometimes makes its home.

[Scene: Rooftop Garden of the Old Mill | Dusk — the final light]

[Visual: The garden is battered; mason jars cracked; trellises bent. But the view holds the entire town—smudged lights, broken coastline, steam rising from tents.]
[Music: A single dissonant chord held and then allowed to decay; underneath, a low, regular pulse]
[Sound: The distant murmur of volunteers; a gull's lonely cry; the soft click of your bracelet against your wrist]

You climb by habit and need. The rooftop has always been a place where decisions feel proportionate to their consequence—a vantage point, a place that fits small griefs into larger sky. You stand at the edge and look down.

The Commons is a dark scallop against the sea; the Promenade is a bright wound. Homes are tents now, lights hung like temporary stars. There are people in the street who will not return to what they had. There are lists being made, names recorded, and fuel being bartered like prayer.

Rae comes up the rope ladder, her hair wild, paint smudged under her nails. She sits beside you without asking.

Rae: "We made a mural," she says. "Names, mostly. People come by at night and leave things—a shell, a bottle of tea. It's messy, but people need messy."

You nod. Messiness is a human grammar you understand.

Tommy stands at a lower ledge, holding a thermos. He looks up, meets your eyes, and gives a hard little smile that doesn't pretend everything is alright.

Tommy: "You did what you thought best, Mara. We all did. Doesn't make it not hurt."

You: "No." (It's a release rather than an answer.)

Elias appears at the top of the ladder then pauses, hands wrapped around the rope. He doesn't climb all the way up. He looks at you, and all the roads you might have taken with him are visible like broken maps.

Elias Navarro: "I have assignments outside town. They'll need someone on site to supervise. I—" (He falters.) "I can't promise I'll be here in the same way."

You: "I wouldn't ask you to."

He exhales—long, sharp—and then he steps back down the ladder as if unwilling to make the final step into a life that might mean staying. For a moment you think he will turn away and leave, and then he does.

You feel the world narrow to the copper against your skin, to the weight of being the one who remains.

You: (to Elias, low) "If you go, don't make yourself into the excuse for people not doing the work here."

Elias Navarro: "I won't." (He looks ashamed and fierce in the same breath.) "You were right about the marshes."

You: "Don't make that my victory." (The laughter that follows is bitter.)

He nods. Then he climbs down, and you watch him merge with the crowd—no dramatic exit, only the small, impossible quiet of someone leaving.

[Music: slows, then fractures into silence]

You are left on the rooftop with the weight of names and the taste of salt on your lips. The grief is a new geography. It has canyons of absence and plateaus of stubborn care. You trace the copper bracelet with a thumb until the engraving catches the last light.

This is not the neat ending you wanted. It is worse and truer: houses lost, promises frayed, livelihoods unmoored. People will rebuild; people will leave. There will be lawsuits and reparations and late-night council meetings and murals that weep color on plywood. There will be moments—small and sharp—where you are wanted and where you are not.

But beneath the despair is something like resolve. You plant seeds in your mouth like a promise to yourself: to keep the Commons, to keep naming the dead, to keep showing up in the messy work of repair. To be a person who refuses to let grief render the town into a diagram.

Rae stands and offers you a paintbrush, its handle wet. "Help me finish the mural?" she asks.

You accept. Your hands are steady for the first time in the day.
