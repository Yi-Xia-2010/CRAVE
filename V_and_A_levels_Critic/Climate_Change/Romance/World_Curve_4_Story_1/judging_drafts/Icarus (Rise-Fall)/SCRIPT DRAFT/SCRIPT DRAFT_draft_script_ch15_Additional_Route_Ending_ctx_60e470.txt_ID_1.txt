[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Dawn — Hours After the Storm]
[Visual: Boardwalk slats upended; raised beds sheared like ribs. A child’s rainboot floats in a puddle of brackish water.]
[Sound: Sirens wail distant and thin; a high, continuous keening of wind through broken signs]
[Music: Harrowing strings, relentless rhythm]

You wake to a landscape that is both familiar and betrayed. The Commons — your hands, your sweat, your careful rows of glass jars and seedlings — lies splintered. Mud maps your boots in concentric circles; peat and seaweed crust the hem of your jacket. The air tastes of iron and old rain. Everything is louder, or nothing is loud enough to name the hole in the world where things used to sit.

You move with a limp efficiency. You check the boardwalk — a missing plank, a deep gouge where a post once stood. You run your hand along a broken trellis and pick up a length of rope tangled with a child's hair ribbon. Your fingers smell of compost and salt and a bright, animal tang you can't place.

Tommy is already working a pry bar, his face a raw map of fatigue and purpose.

Tommy: "Mara — over here. We’ve got people trapped under the greenhouse skeleton on Bed 12. Rae flagged the alleyway; there's a collapsed wall by the promenade."

You nod. You do not trust the nod to hold the shape of what you mean, so you form the words instead.

You (Mara Lin): "Pull steadier on my mark. Keep talking to them. Tell them their names. Don't let any silence get a toe-hold."

Tommy: "Names are fine. Talking is fine. But we need more hands."

Tommy's voice is small against the wind, a thread of something like panic braided with workman's steadiness. You feel it press against your sternum and answer with action — because that is what you always do when the sea shows its teeth.

[Visual: Close on your hands, mud under your nails; the copper bracelet glints with a smear of silt]
[Sound: Distant collapse — the complaint of failing beams; someone sobs out of rhythm]

Rae appears out of the haze, camera slung loose, face streaked with salt and black. Her mouth moves before she catches her breath.

Rae: "They're livestreaming from the promenade. People are saying the wall held yesterday, then—" (she stops, the sentence breaking like a wave)

You cut in because silence grows teeth.

You (Mara Lin): "Focus. Who's hurt? Who needs blankets? Who's unaccounted for?"

Rae: "Mrs. Duvall from the north row — no sign. Amina is at the Tidewatch with an emergency monitor; she's calling for volunteers to ferry supplies. Elias—he's trying to coordinate triage at the mill."

At the name, something hot slides across the back of your eyes. Elias — you do not know what you expect from him in the raw hour after a break: explanation, apology, hands-on help, bureaucratic distance. You only know you want him to be human in front of you, because the town bleeding in real space is a test neither of you can take in theory.

[Scene: Promenade | Morning — The Breach]
[Visual: A jagged crescent of broken promenade where concrete has given way to foaming sea. Metal teeth of rebar point like accusation. A tilted sign with Celeste's development logo swings from a torn chain-link fence.]
[Sound: People shout in short, shocked bursts; someone curses; a child cries once, then silent]
[Music: Percussive timpani, rising]

You reach the edge where the promenade used to be. The ocean has taken a curving bite; part of the temporary cofferdam lies folded into the surf like a dead thing. Bodies are the wrong shape against the gray of the day — some wrapped, some moving with the slow, stunned motion of shock.

Elias stands near the makeshift triage line, his jacket plastered with rain. He is speaking into a radio, then to a volunteer, then to you. When his eyes find you, they are complex — not unreadable exactly, but layered: calculation, grief, a raw, private exhaustion that suggests sleepless nights stretched into a rope.

Elias Navarro: (breathless) "We need sandbags down the west lane. Hospital's running generators on half load. We can triage here, but if the tide comes up again—"

You take his words and feel their shape: what he offers is a plan dressed in bluntness. You want to ask whether the plan thinks of the kids who sleep in the Commons or the elder who lived in the house nearest the wall. Instead you put your hands on a volunteer's shoulders and start organizing stretcher teams.

You (Mara Lin): "Clear the access—two people for moving, one for stabilization. Somebody keep an eye on the east flank; if we see a surge we pull everyone inland immediately."

Elias Navarro: (after a beat) "I'll reroute the volunteer teams. Amina says the surge models show a second peak in three hours; if that holds, we need to be ready to evacuate north. I… I can set up a relay with the mill's generator."

You want to translate his engineering language into the human terms you live by: 'Amina says' means data, 'set up a relay' means light, warmth, power. You want him to say he sees what you see — the thin line between plan and people — but he offers systems that may or may not hold. His voice is steady; his hands do not tremble.

You look at him. You search his face for something you cannot name: the measure between his sense of responsibility and the cost of bending the town to plans he can sell.

Elias Navarro: "We do what we can. Together. One walkway at a time."

Those words could be a bridge. They also could be an instruction. They land against the chaos and do not solve how many things have broken.

[Scene: Various Neighborhoods | Midday — Search and Tending]
[Visual: Houses with salt-smeared windows; roofs peeled like postcards. Neighbors forming human chains to pass water, medication, lists of names.]
[Sound: Distant generator hum; sobbing underbreathed into sleeves; a dog barking hollowly]
[Music: Low drone punctuated by urgent staccato]

You walk the lines. You triage not just bodies but names. You hand out blankets; you fold debris into piles that look like tiny, failing altars. You watch as neighbors who argued at the town hall the night before shelter each other now, mouths working around practical things: "Give me that tarp." "Can you fetch Mrs. Duvall's cane?" "Hold this for an hour."

Dr. Amina Bhatt meets you by a flooded culvert, coat thick with mud.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Mara. We've got contamination in the lower drains — chemical runoff from the warehouse. We need to cordon the area and test the water before we let kids play in it again."

You: "How bad?"

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (grim) "Bad enough we have to treat it like an acute hazard. We've started decontamination points at the mill. We'll need gloves, neutralizing agent, lists of who's exposed."

You can feel the sentence collapse into its parts: work, lists, inventory. You think of the seedlings, the planters, the taste of clams gathered after a storm. Something dies under your tongue — the idea of simple things continuing unchanged.

[Scene: Temporary Reconstruction Site | Afternoon — A Conversation Frays]
[Visual: A line of volunteers resting on overturned crates. The corporate banner from Celeste's project flaps against a battered shipping container.]
[Sound: Heated voices in the near distance; the whisper of paper taped to wood — "MISSING: NAME — PLEASE CALL"]
[Music: High, metallic strings; undercurrent of a single, relentless drum]

You find Celeste near a stack of construction plans, hair still immaculate despite the weather. She has a clipboard, an efficient raincoat, hands that have not been marred by mud. Her face is the same exact mask it has been at meetings: polished, ready, with eyes that measure outcomes.

Celeste Park: (quiet) "We can mobilize a heavy crew. There are federal emergency funds that can be released if we prioritize certain zones. We could—" (she stops, the rest of the sentence precise) "—accelerate repairs to the promenade. It will reduce future risk and restore commercial access."

You want to say the plan's cost in living ways: which beds will go; who will be displaced by a quick rebuild; whether the town will trade a buffer it already loves for a paved certainty that promises a future of shops. Instead you hear yourself asking a question and it tastes like a threat you never intended.

You (Mara Lin): "And the Commons? The wetlands? Where do they fit in that accelerated model?"

Celeste Park: (tilt of head) "We preserve them as green pockets. But we cannot run a town on sentiment. People need work. They need a stable promenade that brings business. This is the most viable path forward."

You feel Rae’s presence before her words. She steps between you with the raw, incandescent anger that never had time to sharpen into policy — it's immediate, personal.

Rae Carter: "Viable for who? The promenade means cranes and concrete and a wall between us and the sea. People here fish, they farm the marsh, they teach children to read the tides. You want to trade that for shopfronts and investors who don't know the names of the houses you bulldoze."

Celeste Park: (cool) "What I'm offering is fewer people dying in the next big storm. We cannot afford poetic solutions when lives are on the line."

There is no resolution because you are not given time to make one. The town is a living mouth arguing in many tongues, and the rain has soaked the map of choices until edges bled into one another.

[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Late Afternoon — The Highest Point of Collapse]
[Visual: The greenhouse skeleton collapsed into a shallow pool. One of the raised beds — the one you planted with your sibling years ago — is gone, washed away to a gray smear.]
[Sound: Distant siren fades; a child's whispered question repeats like a mantra: "Where's our garden?"]
[Music: Full orchestral collapse; dissonant chords that never resolve]

You stand over the place where a little crop of native marsh grass used to mark a memory. The copper bracelet around your wrist feels like a stone. You press the band and remember the name you once slept under before everything changed. Memory is a sharp thing; it keeps you from falling entirely into panic by giving you one solid axis to spin around.

Elias comes up behind you. He has mud on his collar. He does not reach out at first. When he does, it is with hands that have been doing rescue work for hours; they are callused in ways that match yours but are coordinated toward different problems.

Elias Navarro: (soft) "We saved what we could. The models were wrong in places I thought they'd hold. I'm sorry."

You look at him. You listen for the apology and for the ownership in it. There is an admission in the smallness of his voice, but also an officer's reticence. Complex sits where reconciliation could be.

You (Mara Lin): "Sorry won't plant new beds."

Elias Navarro: (a breath, then) "I know. But I can help rebuild them. The relay feeds the pumps; we'll get power to the Commons by night. We'll stabilize the beds with coir logs and—"

You cut him off because this is where talk and action have to find a line that doesn't hurt people further.

You (Mara Lin): "Do you hear us when we say 'not at the cost of our place'?"

Elias Navarro: (his jaw tightens) "I hear you. I also hear people needing heat and hospitals. I can't make everyone whole with a plan. But I'll be there. I'll be at the pumps. I'll be at the meetings. I'll be at —" (he searches) "— what you ask of me."

His answer is both a promise and a plea. It feels like an attempt to straddle a fissure: he will be present in the practical ways he knows how. You do not know if presence is enough. You feel the familiar press of your sibling's absence like a tide pulling at the edges of your resolve.

[Scene: Various — Nightfall into Weeks After]
[Visual: Montage — volunteers erecting temporary flood berms; villagers planting salt-tolerant grasses with glued-together hands; a line of donated hot meals; a makeshift memorial of candles and driftwood with names pinned to it.]
[Sound: Muted hammering; whispered names around candles; rain that returns slowly like a metronome]
[Music: Slow, aching strings; a low, unresolved hum]

Time becomes a ledger of tasks. You count in lists: water tests, shelter rosters, supply runs. You keep waking at odd hours to check pumps; you stitch up tarps by lantern light. At the mill, Amina runs tests and tells you what the data is in the blunt language scientists use when people need to plan for the next assault.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Recovery will take years. There's contamination in three drainage basins; some homes are uninhabitable until we get mitigation funds. And the promenade's cofferdam failed — the structural engineers want to phase rebuilds with living shorelines instead of hard walls, but that takes time and money."

You: "Which means what for people with rent due now?"

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (softly) "It means we need more than plans. We need temporary housing, emergency relief, and a legally enforceable commitment to phased, community-led rebuilding. I'll draft a proposal. You can lead the community oversight."

You close your eyes. The possibility of work eases the raw edge of despair a fraction. Work is a way to carry grief forward instead of letting it ossify into something else.

Rae leads a vigil on a wet evening. She projects photos onto the side of the old mill: faces of those lost, faces of those who dug with bare hands, faces of neighborhoods now full of broken porches. People come: some weep openly, some stand with closed mouths, hands folded. You light a candle and lay it among the driftwood names, the light trembling.

Tommy folds his hands around a cup of tea and does not say the thing that everyone wants to say aloud: it could have been worse. He instead says, "We have each other, at least." The sentence is threadbare and true.

[Scene: Rooftop Garden of the Old Mill | Weeks Later — Quiet, Cold]
[Visual: The rooftop is a mess of replaced tiles and new planters. A single mason jar lantern hangs, sputtering. The horizon is flat with low cloud.]
[Sound: Wind hums through the ropes of a trellis; a gull calls once, thin]
[Music: Sparse piano with low cello — mournful, slowly steadying]

You climb the rope ladder up to the rooftop when you need a place to be a person who is allowed to miss without being useful for a moment. The rooftop holds the scent of peat and old coffee. You stand over a small bed and plant a cutting in trembling hands.

You remember the sibling again — not the outline of grief, but the small lived things: their laugh in the pantry, their hand on your shoulder when a seed took. Memory is not a relief so much as a constant companion that presses against the current.

Elias is there, already. He has carried a box of salvaged pots. When he sees you, he offers a half-smile that doesn't quite reach his eyes.

Elias Navarro: (quiet) "I was going to check the pump lines and thought I'd see if you wanted company. If that's a problem—"

You turn to him. The hand he offers is a practical one: a small, inexorable thing that might be consolation or may be a claim. Up close, you see new lines at his temples, the way his hands no longer move purely in gesture but in tasks.

You (Mara Lin): "I'm tired of things being measured in models. I'm tired of hearing 'we'll do better next time' while people sleep on gym mats. I'm tired of promises that sound like plans."

Elias Navarro: "I know. I am tired, too. I don't… I don't have answers that make the broken things whole. I can run pumps, I can set up relays, I can argue the models in rooms you don't want me to. But I can't bring back what we lost."

The words hang. They are the most human thing in the last weeks — no spin, no policy-speak, no accusation on either side.

You reach for him without thinking. You're not sure whether what you want is to be held or to be argued with, to be proven right or to have the rage shared. His shoulder is warm and solid under your palm.

He does not step away. He also does not close the distance like a reconciliation; instead he fits into the small hollow available right now: a lean, a shared breath. Complex, you remind yourself. Unreadable. And still a real, physical thing.

Celeste arrives later with an envelope of papers — a proposal, an offer, a shorthand of the world she knows how to move in. She leaves it on the crate between you and Elias as if that neutral bit of paper can be the third party in any future you might have. Her expression is not unkind; it is businesslike and heavy with the certainty she was raised to wear.

Celeste Park: (flat) "Funding for a phased rebuild, with oversight and an expedited timeline. It's not everything, but it's a start. The town will be prioritized in the regional allocations."

You look at the envelope and at the faces around you: the ragged volunteers, the kids who come here to climb the trellis when they need to talk, the sacks of seeds waiting to be planted. The decision the town makes with that envelope will mark what is saved and what is sold.

You think of your sibling and the way grief folded you inward so that only action kept you breathing. You think of all the hands that dug and carried and argued and soothed. Love in this town has never been a shout of flower petals; it's a hundred small practical things done in the rain.

You breathe.

You set the box of pots down and feel the weight of the rooftop underfoot. The future is a ledger of losses and commitments. You will make choices that cut away other things; there will be bodies to account for and promises to keep.

You do not know what the final shape of the town will be. You cannot know whether your relationship with Elias will harden into a partnership of shared grief and stubborn persistence, or whether it will fray under the pressure of different tools and time horizons. You simply know what you can do now: plant, tend, argue when you have to, rescue when you can, hold when someone offers their palm.

You press a cutting into soil and feel the small, immediate satisfaction of something that, for a breath, can still grow.

You look out to the horizon where the sea still lays claim to what it wants. You feel the salt on your lips and the bruise in your chest. The world has broken in a way that cannot be mended with plans alone. It will be mended — if it is mended — by the terrible, patient work of people with broken hands.

[Visual: The camera pulls back slowly from the rooftop; the town is a stitched map of light and shadow, of places lost and places stubbornly clinging to life.]
[Music: A single, sustained note — mournful, then finally easing into a minor chord]

You fold your hands around the small cutting and lift it like an offering to both what was and what must be kept. The grief is acute and raw; the work ahead is relentless. There is no triumphant fix. There is only tending. There is only the continuing sequence of small, resolute actions that will decide whether this place remembers how to be both sea and home.

You breathe in the salt and the soot and the faint promise of green. You let the sound of a distant generator steady you. You are tired in a way that is not new, but it has a different name now: responsibility.

You light the mason-jar lantern and set it at the edge of the rooftop garden, where the wind won't take it all at once. Its glow is small, stubborn. It does not erase what has happened. It insists on tending.
