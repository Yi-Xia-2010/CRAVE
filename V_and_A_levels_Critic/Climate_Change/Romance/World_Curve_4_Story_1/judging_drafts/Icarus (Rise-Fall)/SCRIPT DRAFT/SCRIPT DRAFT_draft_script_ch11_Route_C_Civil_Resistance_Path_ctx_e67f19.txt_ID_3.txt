[Scene: Solhaven Promenade | Dawn — Overcast, wind picking up]
[Visual: Chain-link fences, survey flags whipping; a line of protesters gathered near rusted rebar]

Dawn is a bruise of slate light behind the clouds. The world feels wound tight—so tight you can hear it ache.

Rae is at your shoulder, breath visible in the chill, voice low and urgent. Around you, the crowd is a ragged rope of resolve: fishermen in patched coats, teenagers with throat-deep shouts rehearsed on their lips, neighbors who once traded tomatoes now trade legal advice.

A line of county officers stands beyond the first barricade—rigid, professional, and waiting. Beyond them, tracked vehicles and the skeletal outline of a half-built anchoring point stare like a promise half-kept. That promise is what you're here to deny.

Rae: "We can lock chains to the pylons. Make a ring of bodies if we have to. Cameras—get them on the east side. If they remove one person, they have to remove fifty."

You can feel Tommy's eyes on you: steady, asking. He runs a hand over a coil of rope as if checking knot memory. Small muscles, old habits—these are the town's practicalities wrapped up as ritual.

Tommy: "If it comes to that, Mara—" (he looks at you, searching) "—we keep people in shifts. We keep medics ready. No mad dashes."

You (Mara): "No showboating. Discipline. Anchor points are dangerous when they're half-done. Someone goes under that steel and it'll be our faces in the papers."

Rae: (fingers tightening on a stencil) "We make it impossible to ignore. We make them stop."

Narration:
You taste the metallic tang of fear. It is sharp enough to cut. You remember the dead sibling—how water erased a life in a blink. The memory is bunched and raw and precise; it sits behind your ribs like a stone that will not be swallowed.

Elias arrives at the edge of the crowd with his tablet tucked under his arm, collar damp from the drizzle. He moves through the group with the careful patience of someone measuring wind vectors; his jaw is set, his gaze unreadable—complex—studying you as much as the site.

Elias: (quiet) "You called for a blockade."

You: "They were going to pour tomorrow. We couldn't wait."

Elias: "You know every action invites a response. The county will escalate. Arrests. Injunctions. They'll bring in heavy equipment—"

You: "They already have heavy equipment."

Elias: (a tight half-smile) "Right. I'm offering sensors, permits, a chance to force a pause with data. It's not spectacular, but it's safer."

You: "Sometimes 'not spectacular' is what buys lives a breath."

Elias: "And sometimes 'not spectacular' gets buried in red tape while people stand in front of jackhammers."

You: (low) "We don't know which will happen. But we know what will happen if nothing stops the pour."

Dialogue continues — multiple turns:
Elias: "I want to stand with you—truly. But I'm still connected with people who 'sign the check' if I don't read the ledger. I can help with witnesses and sensors. I can testify to anchoring risk. But if you chain yourselves to pylons, I won't be able to protect you in court."

You: "Protection in court won't stop the rebar from swinging in a storm. It won't stop a half-anchored foundation from tearing."

Elias: (voice softening) "I said I would be on your side. I didn't say I'd do everything your way. I won't let you die to prove a point."

You: (breath like a knife) "Don't tell me what my points are, Elias."

Narration:
His expression folds—complex, unreadable. Maybe he's hurt. Maybe he's afraid. Either way, the space between you hums with something that is not spoken: alliances under strain, a love tangled with logistics.

[Scene: Courtroom — Emergency Hearing | Two days later — Gavel strikes]
[Visual: Tight shots of faces; a city's weight coalescing in a lino-floored room]

Elias: (whispering) "We should have more sensors in place now. At the least, we can document any compromise of the site."

You: (angry) "You can talk about 'later'—I only have now."

Narration:
The hearing adjourns with the clause: further review, more documents, a deadline that will arrive like a low tide—uncertain and cooling. Outside, the sky is a bruise turning blacker at the edges.

[Scene: Promenade — Late Afternoon | Storm Threatening]
[Visual: The skeletal anchoring point with cables flapping; work lights blink on]
[Music: Chaotic strings and percussion pushed hard; breathless tempo]
[Sound: The scream of wind, the distant roll of thunder, shouts that are half instruction, half panic]

Narration:

You are at the edge, watching the anchoring point—half-completed, a jagged tooth of iron and hope. The engineers on the contractor's side are yelling into radios. Your group forms a human barrier. It feels like standing with your chest against a wave.

A cable—an incomplete securing line—snaps under the wind's merciless tug. It jerks a scaffolding arm sideways. A worker trips. There's a scream that rips through everything. Then, a metallic sound like a broken promise.

Rae is near the front. You see her shoulder ram against a swinging plate. The world narrows: the plate swings, Rae stumbles, and a scaffold collapses with a sound like a closing book.

Narration:
You move before thought. Adrenaline is a blunt instrument. You are running, hands reaching. The air is full of grit and salt and the sour tang of hydraulic fluid. You can hear a bone-deep cry—someone hit hard, the kind of cry that strips polish from words and leaves only rawness.

You: (voice breaking) "Stop apologizing. Breathe."

Rae's chest heaves under a rain-dark shirt; there's a bruise where metal met muscle. They load her onto an ambulance with the siren cutting a white line through the rain. The ambulance disappears like a small, frantic fish into the grey.
