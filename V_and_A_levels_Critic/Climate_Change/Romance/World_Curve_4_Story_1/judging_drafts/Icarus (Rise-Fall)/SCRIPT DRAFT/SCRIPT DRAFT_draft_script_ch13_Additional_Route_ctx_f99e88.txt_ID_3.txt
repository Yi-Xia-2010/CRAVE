[Scene: Town Hall & Council Chamber | Afternoon]
[Visual: Tall windows throwing flat, patterned light across a packed chamber. Microphones cluster on the dais like insect antennae. Cameras perch like hawks.]
[Music: Drums doubling, string clusters colliding; a sustained brass that suggests impending rupture]

The hearing starts ten minutes late and three degrees too loud. A line of lawyers stands by the council entrance; a clerk reads a litany of filings. National outlets stream it. The county prosecutor's suit looks like a blunt instrument: negligence, failure to disclose environmental assessments, accusations that some tests were falsified. A public records leak is played over the chamber's speakers: a recording—shaky, short—of an offhand conversation referencing "fast-tracking" and "economics over wetlands." The clip is not explicit; it is obscene in its implication.

Dr. Amina Bhatt rises to speak and carries the room with her because she speaks from evidence, from sediment cores and tidal charts rather than hashtags. Her voice is low but precise.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "The data we have is incomplete. There are anomalies in the sampling chain that demand immediate forensic review. If construction continues unchecked, we risk irrecoverable habitat loss and further harm to community infrastructure."

Lawyer: "Doctor, are you asserting—"

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "I am asserting that without a pause, we cannot rule out compounding harm."

The prosecutor leans in, a coagulation of will. Celeste's counsel counters with phrases scraped from press kits: 'economic stability,' 'job preservation,' 'accelerated mitigation plans.' The language is neat—an attempt to reroute panic into policy.

Celeste Park (quietly, to the chair): "We have contingency measures. We will open funding immediately for additional monitoring."

Mara (you): "Contingency measures mean trailers and tape, not living wetlands. They mean short-term fixes stitched to optics."

Celeste Park: (turning, unreadable) "And protests mean nobody wants to rebuild. How do you propose we keep people fed without investors' confidence?"

Your retort tastes like salt. Elias slides closer, lowering his voice so only you can hear.

Elias Navarro: "Publicly, we have to ask for the pause and the oversight without making this about personal vendettas. Let the data speak under subpoena. Let Amina lead the sampling protocol."

Mara (you): "Let the data be uncontested while machines move in to pour more concrete? Let them finish and then we appeal?"

Elias Navarro: "No. We petition for an emergency injunction. We push for independent monitors. But we don't make it personal, we—"

Your protest dies into the noise. Rae slams a fist into her own palm. She is not built for patience.

Rae Carter: "The court of public opinion is already deciding. We can't let them engineer our grief into a story about lost opportunity. People are hurt. People are scared. We have to stop the machines."

Counsel for Celeste objects; the murmur rises into shouting. Cameras swivel to catch every angle. A gavel bangs in the distance; a clerk calls for decorum that no one can possibly provide.

Suddenly a court investigator stands and hands a sealed envelope to the clerk. The room tilts.

Clerk: "An emergency petition has just been filed on county record. Federal investigators are invoking an immediate review. Counsel, you will receive notice."

A flash of camera light explodes against Celeste's composed face. Her jaw is a line; for a breath she's human.

Elias looks at you—his face is a palimpsest of calculation and something like fear. He reaches for your hand and the gesture is small and complicated. You allow it, then withdraw when the room snaps into a new order.

Celeste Park: (voice steadier, but with an edge) "This is politically motivated, opportunistic. We will comply and cooperate. But halting the project will cost workers their livelihoods. There is a cost to every delay."

Mara (you): "There is a cost to pretending safeguards are a box you tick and not a responsibility to people who sleep in these beds by the sea."

Celeste Park: "And there's a cost to scaring investors away, Mara. You know what I believe. We can't save everything by refusing to build anything."

The exchange is not just about plans. It is about trust—how it cracks, how it is wielded. Neither of you sound like you intend to soften. The chamber hums with the weight of that.
