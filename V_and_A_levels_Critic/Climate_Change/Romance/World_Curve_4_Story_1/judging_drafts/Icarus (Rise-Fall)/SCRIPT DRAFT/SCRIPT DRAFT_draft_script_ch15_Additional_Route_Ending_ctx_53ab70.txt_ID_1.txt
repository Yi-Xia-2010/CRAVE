In one impossible sequence you are in the water, on a roof, in the hospital corridor, and back at the pier — time folding on itself like wet paper.

Elias is there — you cannot say whether he's pulling someone from the water or shouting a coordinate. He is both hero and a man who has been made suddenly small by the scale of ruin.

Amina is cupping a baby, mouth working to teach a parent how to hold wet lungs. [Visual: The Commons is a field of broken ribs: boardwalks snapped, raised beds overturned, salt-crunching the leaves of plants that lived there yesterday. A thin, oily fog rises off puddles. A wind-chime's string is frayed, a small bell half-submerged.]

You walk the Commons and your feet sink into old plans. The smell is a hard, chemical salt layered over rot and compost. The soil you once coaxed has been rearranged by tides and by the weight of bodies. You kneel to a collapsed bed and find a small seed packet half-buried: bright, stupid hope, damp and sticky. Your fingers close on it, and your throat tightens.

Rae is painting a memorial on a salvaged sign — names in staccato strokes interrupted by sobs. She looks up at you, mascara washed into streaks, and for a moment you try to read what that look means. Is it accusation? Consolation? She opens her mouth to speak and then closes it because words seem obscene.

Rae: "They wanted to put a promenade where this was. We said no." (each word is a stone she cannot swallow)

You: "We did everything we could get people to agree to." (your voice is a worn hinge) "We tried to hold both — buffer wetlands and plans."

Rae: (a laugh that is almost a scream) "Both cost things, Mara. They always do." (She throws down the brush and pulls you into a hug that is all sharp bones and wet breath — a desperate clinging)

Tommy approaches with a tarpaulin and extra gloves. He hands you both and says nothing until you are both covered.

Tommy: "There'll be funerals. There'll be hearings. First, we pull people out. Then we put the town back together and the lawyers will have their toy." (He spits on the mud. "We'll do the work and they'll fight over the map. That's how it goes.")

You want to tell him what keeps you awake: the knowledge that some choices shift more than plans — they reassign culpability to bodies and names. But your words keep slipping into the same channels of "we must," "we will," which in the face of the world's loud collapse feel small and insufficient.

[Scene: Town Hall & Council Chamber | Two Days Later — The Hearing]
[Visual: The chamber is packed. Someone has tacked the salvaged sign with names above the speaker's dais. The fluorescent lights are too bright, making the crowd look pallid and thin. Water stains map the ceiling like bad weather charting its return.]
[Sound: Murmurs like a tide. A gavel slaps occasionally; a reporter coughs. Phones buzz not with calls but with messages that are condolence and accusation fused]
[Music: Low, insistent brass]

The hearing is a slow wrecking of pretense. Lawyers speak in measured syllables that have been commingled with the sound of ambulances. Celeste sits with a portfolio that feels oddly incongruous; she has lost something you can see in the clamp of her jaw. Amina reads the data into the record with the quiet ferocity of someone who keeps grief in graphs because graphs are legible.

You sit with your hands folded, fingers bruised from hauling debris, and you let the world interrogate you. Voices ask about timelines, about whether the clinic should have been evacuated, about whether the promenade would have changed anything. People ask: who ordered what? Who was in possession of the radio? Who authorized launch?

A reporter (insistent): "Mayor Lin —" (the title is not yours but the room wants a face) "—do you accept responsibility for the losses?"

You look up and the questions feel corporal. You are made of seasons and small community choices that never seemed like they would be judged by a courtroom. You think of the child whose name you have learned by heart; you think of the old woman who would not leave the cat; you think of Tommy's hands.

You: "We made the choices that we could with the information we had." (Your words are thin and honest.) "We tried to conserve life and place. That is what we did."

Celeste (interjecting, voice sharpened with policy): "And yet—" (she gestures to a slide show of steel and renderings) "—a properly funded infrastructure would have prevented part of this. We must think about scale."

Elias stands when it's his turn, not to push a plan now but to name the truth he has seen. He speaks not in models but in the small human architecture of rescue: which neighborhoods rose, which sank, which nets failed. His voice is raw.

Elias: "You can have walls strong enough to stop a tide, but there will always be someplace for water to go. We need a combination of protected cores and living margins; we need to listen to the people who read the tide by feel, and to the instruments that tell us the numbers."

A murmur runs through the chamber — compromise is an unwelcome word to some, and a lifeline for others.

[Scene: Reconstruction Sites / Various Neighborhoods | Weeks to Months]
[Visual: Scattered shots of rebuilding: a new floodwall half-built, a gash in the promenade braced with scaffolding, raised beds re-staked and new plantings at the Commons, a makeshift memorial by the harborside. Machinery and paint smell hang in conflicting layers.]

The long work starts. Volunteers, contractors, and people who cannot leave their damaged homes shovel the same mud with different vocabularies. There are days when the work feels like a vow — every nail a prayer — and days when the air itself presses with the memory of what was taken.

You move through it with a particular kind of grief: the slow grinding of a grief that insists on labor. You survey new plans and mark where mangroves will be planted, where the wall might end, where pathways will allow the tide to breath. You argue. You cajole. You do the humiliating, dogged work of persuading funders to allow for living shorelines, of convincing residents that moving a little inland will keep more people and fewer memories in the long term.

Sometimes you succeed. Sometimes you don't. Sometimes you wake and the town has chosen a different future than the one you dreamt in the Commons. A developer's sign goes up in the margins; a ribbon is cut at a rebuilt clinic; there are reconciliations and ruptures. People who once stood shoulder to shoulder now trade lawsuits in an antiseptic room. Hearts and policies don't align as neatly as you hoped.

Elias works beside you often in this season — not always in agreement, but close enough that the friction between you feels like a useful heat. He still brings technical drawings to the yard, still asks you to walk lines at low tide, still watches your face for the sign that an idea has landed. When you quarrel, it's with a raw honesty that leaves little room for pretense; when you are quiet together, it's like two hands shaping the same clay, neither wholly right but able to make something that holds.

One night, after a day of arguing over a new sea-gate placement, you stand on the Rooftop Garden of the Old Mill.

[Visual: The rooftop is a patchwork of salvaged tiles and half-rebuilt trellises. Lantern jars hang, empty but for the memory of light. The sea beyond is a long, bruised strip. Down below, construction lights blink.]

You and Elias stand side by side, not touching. The silence between you is full: with loss, with the shape of decisions, with the consequences that followed. The town below is both more and less than it was: safer in parts, scarred in others. The Commons is replanted but narrower; the promenade exists but interrupted by a living inlet where marsh was restored. It feels, in its halting way, like a compromise that carries the memory of the storm in every seam.

Elias: "Sometimes I worry that I tried to solve people with equations." (He looks at you and then away) "I wanted to do the humane thing in a technical language."

You: "And I wanted people to keep their places." (You think of your sibling's stubborn habits, of how place made a life legible.) "Neither of those translates cleanly into policy."

He smiles without humor. "We both had maps. They didn't have to be the same map, but we kept trying to overlay them."

You let out a breath that is half-laugh, half-sob. "Overlaying isn't the same as agreement," you say.

Elias: "No. But maybe it's the only way we keep each other from eroding. If we are together, even in different languages, maybe the town does not fall into the sea."

You: (You want to believe him. You want it enough to stitch it into your ribs. But the memory of people lost, of choices that cost, sits like a stone under your tongue.) "Maybe." (You do not promise marriage, or a future free of mud and hearings; you promise endurance by implication — the kind of daily mercy that looks like fidelity to a fragile place.)

He reaches out and, without thinking, brushes a smear of mud from your sleeve. It's a small, human contact. For a second time in months, you allow the touch to mean something more than a plan.

You know the endings are multiple — some streets rebuilt and thriving, others abandoned, a promenade that brings jobs and also takes away old fishing spots, lawsuits that never settle, and also gardens that bloom where there was only rubble. Mara (internal): The town taught you a grammar of loss: to name it, to tend it, to incorporate it into the next plan. You have learned that endings are not clean; they are built like levees — layer upon layer, always subject to the next storm.

You breathe in the salt and the cold and let yourself feel the whole ache of it — the very negative, very loud truth that some things cannot be saved, and those that remain will require constant tending.

Elias: (soft) "Stay. Help me keep the Commons alive. Help me argue for the living margins next council season."

You: (You think of all the names on Rae's sign, of the small seed packet you still carry in your satchel like a relic.) "I'll stay," you say. It is not an easy promise.
