[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Late Morning — after a light rain]
[Visual: Wide shot of the Commons; mist curling off tidal pools, sun breaking through low clouds]
[Music: Quiet ambient piano with soft wind textures]
[Sound: Distant gulls, water lapping, occasional muffled hammer from a nearby repair]

You step off the weathered boardwalk with the practiced surety of someone who knows where the rot hides. Mud clings to the soles of your boots in thin, patient ribbons. The air smells of wet earth, brine, and the faint sweetness of compost — the Commons doing what it's supposed to do: steadying itself, quietly, between tides.

The raised beds glint with rain and a few bright greens poke through the peat: sea purslane, saltbush, a row of hardy glasshouse lettuces. Board signs lean like friendly guardians, hand-painted slogans and plant names wobbling in the breeze. A chime you hung the week before tinkles with the current, a small, determined sound against the sea.

You run a thumb along the copper bracelet at your wrist, feeling the groove worn into the metal where the coordinates were engraved years ago. It's a small ritual: a way to measure fear against facts. The tide may be higher each season, but the marsh is where you choose to press your weight.

Tommy is already here — as much a fixture of the Commons as the driftwood bench — threading rope through a set of reclaimed stakes. His beanie is damp and his movements are patient. He looks up when you approach, and his face warms the way the sun will a few degrees later.

Tommy: "Morning, Mara. Rain didn't drown your seedlings?"

You: "Not yet." (You pull off your gloves and flick a line of wet soil back into a bed.) "How's the net mending? Enough rope to hang Rae's new signs?"

Tommy: (grins) "Enough to make them permanent. Not that anyone can tell Rae that, she'll paint a new slogan tomorrow."

You both laugh, the sound a small, local weather. For a moment the Commons is precisely what it's meant to be: a place where hands and habit reknit what storms fray.

Rae arrives next, cheeks flushed from a bike ride and sleeves already flecked with paint. She carries a roll of freshly stenciled flyers and a camera slung around her neck. Her energy is a little too big for the Commons, like a bright tide trying to find the right channel.

Rae: "They printed another batch. Look—'Hands, Not High-Rises.' Catchy, right?"

You: (smile tight) "Catchy. We'll pass them out at the market tonight."

Rae: (tilting her head) "You're alright with the forum? People want a face-off—Celeste's team scheduled the promenade pitch for next week. Lots of suits coming down."

You: (you measure your reply) "We'll be there. We need people who understand the land, not just technology."

Rae's eyes flick to you — bright and searching in the way younger activists are — and for a second you feel the old tug: the urgent, righteous pulse that made you stand in soaked boots and organize town hall evenings. But the tug is tempered now by a different rhythm. Long fights have taught you about fatigue; loss taught you about boundaries.

Rae: (softer) "And Elias? Heard the cooperative's sending someone to speak with community leads. Could be a bridge—"

The name lands like a small, unfamiliar stone. You don't know Elias yet; the cooperative is a faceless thing in most meetings: proposals, charts, numbers. A bridge can be a lifeline or a route for more concrete.

You: "If they want to talk, they can talk to the council, too. Celeste will pack the room."

Tommy: (shrugging, pragmatic) "Might be useful to hear what the cooperative's really proposing. Or it might be another pitch. Either way, it helps to know."

You fold the conversation into the day's tasks: a delivery of marsh seedlings, checking the boardwalk supports, an informal list you keep in your head in the margins between people and plants. Routine is a kind of armor.

A voice — measured, polite — comes from near the tide line. You turn.

Elias stands where the sand meets marsh, jacket still damp at the shoulders, a tablet tucked under his arm. He looks slightly out of place against the roughness of your reclaimed world: his jacket is clean, the lines modern. But his boots are field-worn and his gaze is straightforward, not the corporate blank you'd braced for.

Elias: "Hi. Sorry— I didn't mean to walk the grounds without permission. I'm Elias Navarro. The cooperative sent me to introduce myself. I wanted to meet the people doing the work here."

You can tell from the way he stands that he's practiced this: posture, tone, the readiness to note and nod. But there is a softness to the eyes, a patience that doesn't demand agreement. It's a different kind of coordination than the kind you do with spade and seed.

You: "I'm Mara. This place is a project of the community. We're careful about who we bring in. What's the cooperative proposing?"

Elias: (hesitates, then gestures to the distant flagged promenade site) "We're looking at a phased resilience plan — some engineered protections combined with nature-based buffers. We want to avoid displacement and preserve livelihoods. I thought talking to the people first would help shape the plan to local needs."

Rae: (arms crossed, quick to test) "Who said that? Because 'preserve livelihoods' doesn't always mean 'preserve our land.' Depends whose livelihoods you mean."

Elias: (earnest) "Everyone's. Small-scale fishing, local growers, shops — the idea is to match technical approaches to what keeps the town running."

Tommy: (eyes narrow with lived skepticism) "That's what they all say until the contracts come through."

The exchange rolls on: you, setting the boundaries; Rae, probing for sincerity; Tommy, keeping one eye on the practical risks. The conversation doesn't snap—voices don't rise. Each question and answer is measured, like notes in a slow, careful song. Elias answers with specifics, but you notice he often circles back to listening—trying to learn, not only to sell.

Elias: "I can show you the preliminary sketches, if you'd like. No hype — just data and options. If nothing else, feedback from people here will make anything we do less wrong."

You: (internal) You appreciate the phrasing. It refuses certainty, which is realistic. You also know how phrases like 'less wrong' can become 'not our problem' once the money starts moving. There's a tension between curiosity and caution under the polite words.

You weigh the options like you weigh soil — texture, smell, pattern. Meeting now could let you shape the project early. Waiting could keep the argument democratic, a public test of intentions. Neither choice will save or lose the Commons by itself; both are moves on a longer board.

Elias watches you choose with careful attention, his expression a soft map of patience. He doesn't push. The tide slides in, small and steady, and a heron lifts from the nearest pool with a slow, elegant folding of wings. Rain from earlier beads along the edges of the tablet, like tiny lenses reflecting the Commons in miniature.

Mara: (internal) You remember the last big meeting you organized — the one where too many promises were made and too few were kept. You remember a brother's laugh, a missing smile the season after. Those memories make you meticulous and weary in equal measure.

Elias: (quiet, almost tentative) "Whatever you decide, I want you to know we'll include community-led monitoring. Dr. Bhatt suggested joint sensors and open data. If you ever want me to walk through the beds with you... I mean, I'd like that."

You: (a quiet, honest response) "We have a schedule for watering and tide observations. If you want to help with that before any plans are finalized, you'll need to earn that trust."

He nods. No promise given; no promise asked — just a small, mutual acknowledgment of the work trust requires.

The morning unfolds in low-key increments: you mark off seedlings that need more shelter, give Rae a list of forum talking points, and accept an offer of coffee from Tommy, hot and salted with sea air. Elias lingers at the edge of tasks, offering small technical notes and asking about local names for plants you point out. His questions are specific, the kind that betray someone who intends to listen rather than to lecture.

As the sun climbs, the Commons hums and goes about its living. The conversation with Elias has shifted the day's texture, a thread of possibility woven into the usual routine. But it has not pulled tight. Choice waits like a horizon you can see but haven't yet stepped toward.

You take a breath, feeling the calm before you lean either way.
