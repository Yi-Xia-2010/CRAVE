You wake to the vibration under your palm: a dozen messages, a news alert, Rae’s name at the top of the thread. The Commons smells like wet earth and the last night's tea — compost and salt layered together, the way memory tastes here. Your fingers move before your mind fully catches up; the leak is already a thing outside yourself, running ahead of you.

You stand, boots thudding on reclaimed wood, and the Commons fills with small, corrosive sounds: the flap of a protest banner being unfurled, a camera shutter across the path, a journalist’s mumble to a producer. Someone has strung up a hastily printed copy of the contract, the paper trembling in the breeze, edges dark with damp. You don't force your eyes to read it—what matters is already broadcast: clauses, relocation funds, the line about "limitations on disruptive actions" that everyone is texting like a hot shard.

Your stomach pins like a tidepool caught between waves. Guilt flares, quick and bright; relief follows it, sour and immediate. You had meant to blow the lid open, to press for transparency. You had not meant for every headline to call it a scandal.

[Sound: A rising swell of distant voices; a siren punctures the air]

Rae intercepts you before you can sort a single coherent thought. She’s already ten steps past frantic — teal undercut plastered with a streak of mud, eyes bright as flint.

Rae: "They're live. Thousands. Mara, they're calling it a 'pay-to-play' scheme. Celeste lawyered up instantly. Council's in chaos. We have to—"
You cut her off with one hand because your mouth won't form anything else yet.

You: "Tell me what they're saying. Slow it down."
Rae: (exasperated, then softer) "They're parsing the clauses. People are calling for injunctions. National outlets are—look, I don't have time for steps, I'm telling you what the noise is."

You don't need the list. The Commons itself answers: neighbors pulling folding chairs to the boardwalk, children pressed to parents who whisper about jobs; someone sprays a stencil of the town's marsh with the words "OUR LAND" in bright blue. The leak doesn't translate easily into right or wrong; it folds into livelihoods, into the memory of your sibling lost to a storm. It becomes, immediately, a moral crucible.

You watch footage that yanks at something raw: a polished anchor holding a printout of the same contract, the camera pulling back to show an infographic that makes your town a case study in national policy debates. A legal analyst parses the "limitations" clause like it's a weapon. Comment feeds fill with people who have never been here offering verdicts and demands.

You feel the breath go out of you; air seems thinner in your chest. It’s not just the scale of attention — it's how quickly the focus has narrowed to a single sentence, the way public imagination prefers neat villains to messy truths.

[Scene: Town Hall | Afternoon]
[Visual: The municipal chamber, fluorescent lights harsh; chairs packed; banners hanging along the walls; a long table with microphones]
[Sound: Murmurs escalate into shouts; a gaggle of press in the back talking into devices; a gavel tapped in frustration]
[Music: High, urgent strings; brass stabs under the din]

You file through the crowd with Elias at your side. His wristband is a thin, steady presence; his jaw is set. He keeps looking sideways at you, as if to find a word that would anchor both you and the tide of commentary.

Elias: "You didn't—"
You finish the phrase for him because you can feel the question on his tongue: "You didn't mean for this to go national?" The air between you is taut; the weight of that "meant" contains something like accusation and something like fear.

You: "I meant transparency. You know that."
Elias: (a breath; then slow) "Transparency doesn't always come with vanishing consequences. If Celeste litigates, the town could be frozen for months."

You: "Or longer, if nothing changes and they break ground with no oversight."
Elias: "And if the injunction succeeds? Funders pull, people panic—relocation stalls. We both lose time. We both—" He stops, because he is as aware as you are that "both" includes something personal that you haven't untangled: his loyalty to solutions that scale, your loyalty to people's right to stay.

Before you can answer, Celeste steps to the dais like a tide drawn deliberately. Her suit is the color of a cleaned deck board; her face is an image scrubbed of any concession. She meets cameras with a practiced warmth that lands a little wrong in this room.

Celeste: (measured) "I appreciate the council calling this emergency session. Let me be clear: development will secure jobs, investment, and protection for Solhaven. The allegations—" she lifts a copy of the leaked contract with a tight smile, "—are being sensationalized. Our intent is to stabilize the town."

A murmur rises. Someone shouts, "Protect us from what?" The voice is swallowed by another voice, and then Rae pushes through the crowd, voice high with urgency.

Rae: "Stability isn't a brand name when you strip people from their land! We want oversight. We want assurances. You slipped a gag clause in here so you could—"
Celeste: (interrupting, cool) "There's no gag clause, Ms. Carter. There's language that prevents frivolous obstructionism. We cannot have a project derailed by individuals claiming bad faith for the sake of headlines."

Rae's response is a spray of words; you see the heat in her palms. The crowd reacts like a struck surface — sharp, immediate.

Celeste: (a thin smile) "Mr. Navarro, lovely in theory. The problem is time and capital. We can't be beholden to indefinite pilots that scare investors."

[Scene: Town Hall — Moments Later | Afternoon]

Then, the legal bomb drops. Celeste's counsel produces a sealed filing — an emergency request for declaratory judgment, an assertion that the published contract cannot be relied on because of "unauthorized dissemination," and, neatly, a motion for expedited injunctive relief to stop any organized obstruction that "threatens the project." The document is precise, litigious, and framed as protection against chaos.

A lawyer for the civic coalition (a woman with a tired, resolute face) fires back, producing an opposing motion: request for temporary restraining order pending investigation into possible undue influence and failure to disclose terms to the community. She cites the same clause you read earlier and folds it into a case for immediate judicial oversight. The words ricochet through the room: injunction, restraining order, undue influence, expedited hearing.

The chamber becomes a courtroom with no judge. Phones flare with footage. Somewhere outside, someone starts chanting; inside, the council's decorum frays into raw argument.

You feel something inside you trembling, then snapping — not like anger but like a splinter releasing. The leak has become a legal fuse and the whole system is now burning through its options.

Elias: (quiet, near you) "If they push a fast-track and we can't get an injunction, the work could start within weeks. And if the injunction goes through, Celeste's backers could sue the town for breach. There's no clean path."

You: "So we litigate, we wait, we risk jobs. Or we don't litigate, and we risk irreversible harm. There's no—" Your voice catches. The room is a pressure cooker; legal language becomes a battering ram and a lifeline at once.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "We can present the data to a court. Scientific testimony is admissible and often decisive in environmental injunctions. But legal timelines are slow where storms aren't. We need a bridge — legal, scientific, and social — that holds while we measure."

Rae: (from the doorway, voice high) "Or we make them stop with people in the way. That's the point."

Celeste: (to the room, voice like glass) "I will not have this town held hostage by theatrics. The development moves forward with or without unanimous blessing. My team will ensure legal channels are followed. For those worried about oversight, we've proposed an oversight board — industry experts, independent auditors." She pins a stare at you that is for once clearly directed: "We can do this with you at the table, Ms. Lin. Or we can do it without you."

You feel the iron weight of the offer — seat at the table, influence, and the smell of compromise — and the sting of how it reads in headlines. Your chest tightens with the echo of your sibling's laugh, of the smell of seawater after the storm that didn't let them go.

You find yourself thinking in fragments: lawsuits, headlines, relocation funds, living shorelines, the kids in the Commons, the veteran fisherman who said he'd lose everything if the promenade rerouted fish runs.

There is no elegant solution in this room. There is only noise and levered power. The council chair tries to regain control with a procedural motion — a vote to request an emergency judicial review and put construction on hold pending full disclosure. Celeste's lawyers object, citing the contractual assurances and the economic harm of delay. The legal battle compresses down into one single question posed by counsel to the council: can the municipality legally pause a private development under pending judicial scrutiny without exposure to liability?

Amina, steady and worn, whispers to Elias, who then looks at you with a clarity that hurts.

Elias: "You understand what this could mean for us — for how quickly we can act if a storm comes?"

As the chair calls for a motion to be drafted for emergency review, the room's frenetic energy crescendos. Camera crews jockey for position. Someone from a national outlet pushes a microphone into Celeste's face. Municipal staffers shuffle papers as if they can reorder reality by doing so.

You feel rain on the windows — first a few drops, then a wind that slaps a loose banner against the glass. Outside, the sky has gone the color of old tin, the sea forgetting the horizon. The Commons — your refuge, your bargaining ground — feels suddenly smaller than a headline.

You want to speak again. To steer, to clarify, to hold two truths at once without them tearing each other apart. But the room is already making decisions that will be hard to reverse. The microphones are a row of teeth. The legal filings are a new landscape.

Your pulse is a drum. You can taste metal on your tongue.
