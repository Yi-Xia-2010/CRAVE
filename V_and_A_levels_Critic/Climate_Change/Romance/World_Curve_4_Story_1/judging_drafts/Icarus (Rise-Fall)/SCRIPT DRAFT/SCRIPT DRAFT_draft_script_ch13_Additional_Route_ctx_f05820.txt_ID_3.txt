[Scene: Saltwren Commons | Morning]
[Visual: Mist lifting off tidal pools; dew on raised beds; a smear of sea-foam color across the horizon]
[Music: Low, anxious pulse; strings with a tight, restless tremor]
[Sound: Distant engine idling, a cacophony of phone notifications, gulls cutting the sky]

You wake to a dozen vibrating alarms on your phone and the taste of iron at the back of your throat. Headlines scroll faster than you can blink—national outlets, pundit threads, a highlight reel of yesterday's leak stitched into a trending segment. Someone in a city newsroom has already turned your town into a headline.

Your hands smell of compost and salt when you slip them into your jacket pockets. The Commons is quieter than usual—only Rae is here, perched on the low driftwood bench with a spray of posters, fingers stained the color of sea glass paint. Her breath clouds in the cool air; excitement and fatigue war beneath her eyes.

Rae Carter: "They ran the memo in full at two a.m. Celeste's PR team called it 'taken out of context.' The op-eds are arguing about intent instead of impact. It's a circus." (she taps the bench; a nervous rhythm)

You listen. The wind takes the words and scatters them back across the tidal grass. Everything feels exposed—like the wet soil here, vulnerable to each new footprint.

[Visual: Close on your copper bracelet catching the pale light; the engraved coordinates glittering like a tiny lighthouse]
[Music: Pulse quickens; a high violin whines briefly]

Your phone hums. Elias. You let it ring twice and pick up on the third vibration.

Elias Navarro: "Mara." (his voice is small, hurried) "Are you—are you seeing this?"

You exhale; the air fogs in front of you.

You: "I am. It’s everywhere. Who—how did it leak?"

Elias Navarro: "We traced the chain to a contractor's server but that's—" (he swallows) "—that's the technical side. The political side is already a wildfire. I don't know who feeds it, only that it's burning fast."

You: "Wildfires can be contained." Your words come out sharper than you intend. "They don't get to frame the town like we're the problem."

Elias Navarro: (a small, bitter laugh) "They don't have to frame the town to hurt it. Headlines bring donors and lawsuits. They make nuance impossible."

There is a pause long enough that the Commons seems to hold its breath—the little wind chimes at the boardwalk shiver and go silent.

Rae watches you with a look that could be admiration or worry; from here, the two feel the same.

You think of the places that matter—the low houses by the creek, the salt-stiff greenhouse where children learned how to coax life from brackish soil. You think of your sister's laugh, the weight of things lost. The leak twists everything into the public square: your grief, the town's survival, and the people placed on pedestals by cameras.

[Scene: Media Coverage Sites (Promenade & Main Street) | Midday]
[Visual: Satellite vans, logos, bright banners; a river of cables feeding into cameras]
[Sound: Microphones buzzing, camera shutters, distant chants]
[Music: Staccato percussion; a rising industrial rumble]

The road between the Commons and Town Hall is a gauntlet of lights and lenses. Reporters press questions into the air like nets; soundbites snap back like teeth. You move through them—one step, another—your jacket heavy with the weight of every overheard accusation. A volunteer holds a paper cup of coffee out to you like an offering. You take it but don't taste it.

A woman with a polished badge and a microphone corners you. Her smile is a practiced blade.

Reporter: "Ms. Lin—there are documents suggesting your group proposed delaying the promenade until your plans were implemented. Is that obstructionist behavior?"

You: "You're asking the wrong question." Your breath is loud in your own ears. "Ask the people living on the shoreline whether they'd prefer a floodwall built on top of their memories or a plan that keeps them in their homes."

Reporter: (tilting her head) "Isn't that what Celeste's plan promises—safety through infrastructure?"

You feel the word "safety" like a coin flipped against your teeth. On your other side, a crowd of people—some with handmade signs, others with corporate placards—threads the sidewalk into a living mosaic of claims.

[Visual: Celeste Park steps out of a black SUV nearby, her trench coat immaculate against the spray of mud and salt]
[Music: A cold, metallic chord; a breath held by everyone watching]
[Sound: Flashbulbs pop; a murmurous swell of interest]

Celeste Park: (to the nearest camera, smooth) "We are committed to resilience. We are committed to progress. There is a difference between emotion and evidence."

You watch her like someone watching a tide mark itself—a precise, engineered line. Her face is practiced, and if there is anything in it it's intangible—she seems unreadable in the most frustrating way.

You: "Then be the evidence. Come to the Commons and see what you're calling 'progress' through the hands that tend it."

Celeste Park: (tilts her chin infinitesimally) "I will meet science with science. But words must act. That's what policy is for."

There are cameras trained on the exchange. Elias appears at your shoulder like the shadow that finally decides to step into the light.

Elias Navarro: "We should keep that invitation open." (to Celeste, measured) "And I want to make sure any assessment is rigorous—Amina has protocols we can follow. Transparency."

Celeste Park: "Transparency is the right word." (her smile is thin) "We'll see whose transparency stands up in court."

Elias looks at you and the look that passes between you is full—fear, restraint, something like a plea.

Elias Navarro: "Whatever happens, we'll do this carefully. Sueing—" (he corrects with a small exhale) "—litigation could fracture what we can protect together."

You want to tell him that "carefully" can feel like "too late" when the tide line suddenly climbs a year early. Instead, you say, "We don't have time for 'carefully' to become an excuse."

[Scene: Town Hall & Council Chamber | Afternoon — Hearing]
[Visual: Floodlights through tall windows; a room crammed with plastic folding chairs and people standing in corridors; TV monitors broadcasting the session live]
[Music: Full, urgent orchestra; bass drum beats like a heartbeat turning into a hammer]
[Sound: Murmurs, coughs, the rattle of papers; the gavel clinks in a cold, echoing cadence]

The chamber is a pressure cooker. Overflow rooms are filled with people watching the stream; every phone is a second mouth. National commentators and local neighbors are layered together here, indistinguishable until something breaks.

Dr. Amina Bhatt stands near the panel, notes in her hand, eyes tired but precise. She moves like someone holding the skeleton key to a locked argument—a mediator between data and fear.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: (low, to you) "If they admit these documents as evidence, the framing changes. The judge will want chain-of-custody, but public opinion—" (she pauses) "—public opinion is already ahead of the law."

Rae shouts from the aisle, a single, fierce burst: "Show the maps! Show the marsh!" Her voice detonates into the room. Someone near the back answers with a chant—people split into waves of support and denunciation.

The prosecutor speaks: crisp, precise, and intent on narrative. They lay out the leak like a line of dominoes—emails, timelines, recommended fast-tracking. They argue motive. Celeste's counsel stands and counters with a methodical catalog of permits and deadlines, an array of lawyers as polished as armor.

Celeste Park: (when she speaks, measured and cold) "Our objective was never to obscure. We aimed to secure funding and protect the town's economy. Haste in words should not be conflated with malfeasance."

You feel a hand—Elias's—find yours. His fingers are cool and steady, though his knuckles strain against his palms.

Elias Navarro: "Mara, if this goes into litigation, they'll reduce the Commons to footnotes. We should—" (his voice catches) "—we should frame the story around scientific pause and joint oversight. That's defensible."

You: "Defensible to whom? To a courtroom full of people who have already decided what gullibility looks like?" Your voice is wrapped in static. "To those who will read 'pause' as 'delay' and translate delay into demolition?"

Elias Navarro: "There are lives at stake. I can't just—" (the words prick him) "—I can't gamble paperwork over safety."

You can hear the rasp in his chest like an animal caught in a net. The room presses in. A television near the ceiling flickers: an anchor proclaims "BREAKING: Construction halted in two counties; Solhaven leak fuels legal storm." The headline in the feed makes your stomach drop like a stone.

Then the defense releases another file. It is not a polite file. It is a video excerpt—meetings, off-the-record lines, a voice you do not recognize saying, "Manage narrative." The video lands in the chamber like a thrown stone; the water ripples everywhere.

[Sound: A camera shutter rapid-fire; phones snapping video; someone near you begins to cry quietly]
[Music: A shriek of violins; everything accelerates]

Celeste's jaw tightens. For a bare, human moment, her polish chips and something like fatigue surfaces.

Celeste Park: (to the room, voice clipped) "This clip is taken out of context. We operate within contracts and legal counsel."

You: "Context doesn't erase consequence." Your statement is not a question. It is a list: wetlands, children, the house on the point. Faces in the crowd turn toward you—hope and accusation braided together.

Elias leans close, words tumbling out in halting bursts.

Elias Navarro: "We can call Amina as an independent witness. We can demand chain-of-custody hearings. We can push for expert review panels. Please—let's make this about evidence, not theatrics."

You taste metal on your tongue. The room has become a place where every syllable toggles between accusation and salvation. Outside, a protest flares into a makeshift flash mob; someone unfurls a banner across the promenade, and a dozen camera feeds cut to it simultaneously.

The hearing becomes a whirlwind of testimony—legalese and lived memory spinning against each other, edited clips and lab reports. An attorney for a national environmental group files a motion that ripples into the public feed: class action potential; injunction possibilities. The legal gears grind louder and faster.

Elias returns to your side, eyes rimmed with exhaustion.

Elias Navarro: "They're talking about nationwide precedent now. If they rule to halt or to oversee, both outcomes will stretch beyond Solhaven. This could set a standard—good or bad."

You: "I know. I know what precedent looks like—it's a word people use to excuse decisions that don't feel urgent until it's a storm you can't ignore."

Amina steps forward, hands folded, and her voice slices through the noise with the weight of someone used to making complexity feel like a single, sharp instrument.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "Judge, I propose an independent triage—immediate scientific containment measures to protect critical habitats while chain-of-custody and legal claims are adjudicated. We can deploy rapid sediment testing and temporary cofferdams where essential, with community oversight."

The pushback is immediate—lawyers shout procedure, prosecutors question impartiality, Celeste's representatives argue for continuity of work to avoid economic collapse.

[Sound: A gavel thumps; a collective intake of breath like a storm lunging at the shore]
[Music: A single, sustained dissonant chord that doesn't resolve]

Rae—who has not stopped moving the whole day—leans into you, and the ferocity in her gaze is both comfort and accusation.

Rae Carter: "They want to make this scientific. They want to paper over human choices. Don't let them bury the story under test tubes."

You remember your sister's hands, the tilt of her head when she debated a tidal model with you—small domestic rituals that were never meant to be strategic assets but become the emotional currency of the courtroom.

Celeste stands, and for a second the chamber is quiet enough that you can hear the tide hitting the pilings outside.

Celeste Park: "We must balance livelihood with longevity. A pause with no plan will leave fishermen unpaid and shopkeepers shuttered. If the court halts construction without viable alternatives in place, you will see the town fracture."

You: "If the court allows unchecked construction, you'll see the town drown in half-measures."

The judge—and here the room tilts toward an axis you cannot see—silences the room with a look that holds part impatience and part exhaustion.

Judge: "I will accept the filings. I will take testimony into consideration. I will order an evidentiary hearing—"

Your heart lurches at the procedural phrase. Every clause is a hinge.

Judge: "—and I may issue a temporary stay pending an evidentiary review."

The words hang like a cliff's edge. There is a staggered sound—the rustle of papers, an intake of breath from the press gallery, a small, involuntary sob from somewhere in the back row.

Your palms are slick. The chamber shrinks to the span between your hand and Elias's.

Elias Navarro: (soft) "Whatever they decide, we have to be ready to act. I'm with you—on the record."

You: "Are you with me—unrecorded?" The question pierces the controlled chaos: part personal, part strategic. The difference matters like sunlight on a tide pool.

Elias's mouth opens, closes. For a beat his expression is unreadable; then a quick, human thing flits across his face—regret, resolve, something folded deep.

Elias Navarro: "I—" (he buries the rest into his fist)

[Music: All instruments collapse into a single, strained note; the room waits as if in the eye of the storm]
[Sound: A phone vibrates with a new alert—another outlet's push notification; outside, the rumble of gathering thunder]

You feel the arousal spike—every system in you tuned to the very hot edge of action. This is the moment where narrative tilts: the gavel's next cadence could order a halt, a review, or nothing at all. The leak has already changed the story; the court will codify the path forward.

You close your eyes for a breath and the Commons' map—mud paths, a child's boat half-buried—spreads behind your lids like a map of what you stand to lose.

[Visual: Close on the judge's gavel raised; dust motes in the shaft of light above it]
[Music: A held, dissonant chord that makes the chest ache]

You open your eyes and lean forward, fingers still entwined with Elias's, and the whole room seems to draw in toward the gavel as if it were a stormfront moving in.

A hand reaches for the microphone.
