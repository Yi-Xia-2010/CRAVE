Dr. Amina Bhatt is first to speak with the force of evidence: sediment cores, modeled sea-level projections, peer-reviewed indices. Her voice is calm, exact, and it lands in the room like mapped tide-lines — precise, indisputable.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "This site, when measured against our modeled storm surge intensities and soil porosity, shows a high probability of levee undermining within five to seven storm seasons without adaptive restoration."
(She slides a binder of charts forward. Her hands are steady; her eyes meet the clerk's like an anchor.)

You watch faces in the gallery shift: some frown, some soften. The defense lawyer smiles, thin. The corporate rep across the table folds his hands like a closed tide pool.

Defense Counsel: "These are models, Doctor. Models are useful, but they are not deterministic. There are economic stakes and wellbeing tied to continued activity."

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "No model is prophecy, but the empirical cores match the modeled risk. These are not projections divorced from site observations — they corroborate one another."

Her testimony is threaded with the kind of scientific rigor that sounds like law. It gives the judge a structure to say yes to a temporary halt and a stronger mandate for assessment.

Defense Counsel: "You testified that the alternatives were 'omitted.' Do you mean they were not considered, or that they were deliberately excluded?"

Lead Counsel: "He's solid. He held. That was the hinge."

Elias walks back to you after the bench recess. His face is unreadable in the exact way you've come to accept — a weave of relief and new worry.

Elias Navarro: "They tried to pin me on funding responsibility."
(He runs a hand over his hair.) "I chose the line that would be most useful to the judge."

You study him. You can see how giving testimony has yanked a thread in his professional life: someone from the institute caught his eye in the hall, fired a look that could mean anything. The Schrödinger of consequences hangs there; you won't know which until later.

Mara Lin: "You did what you had to. You said it plainly." (Your voice is steadier than you feel.)

The room exhales, then fills with a thousand small noises: someone sobs quietly; someone cheers under their breath; phones light up.

You move with the legal team back to Tidewatch Lab, where Amina sets up exhibits under stark fluorescent light. The air smells of kettle tea, wet sediment, and the microcircuit hum of monitors. Jars lined with stratified mud sit on the counter like geological testaments.

Dr. Amina Bhatt: "There is legal leverage in quantified thresholds, but legal leverage costs money. Each expert declaration, each monitoring station, each deposition — it stacks."
