You push open the ferry's hatch and the greenhouse breathes its own heat against the night: wet soil, solder fumes, the sweet, briny tang of leaf and tide. Jun is hunched over a scatter of printouts at the workbench, the lamplight turning his recorder into a tiny, red planet. The memo sits between you like a trapped gull — white paper, blacked-out headers, and a paragraph that folds policy into leverage.

You take it with hands that remember how cardboard soaked through the last flood. The first sentence is a cold, precise thing: thresholds, triggers, automatic relocation clauses calibrated to financial tolerances. The language is engineered to look like safety; the effect is displacement dressed as algorithmic inevitability.

Your notebook opens like a constable's ledger. You write, brutally and quickly, a single expletive that lands on the page with the same weight as a hammer blow. Then another, then a line of strategy—who to call, who to show, what to leak first.

Jun: "They embedded it in the trigger matrix so it looks like a risk-management tool. But the thresholds align with land-value curves—it's designed to make low-income zones statistically disposable."

Ava Maren: "They wrote the calculus to pick winners and losers. They hid the lever where only numbers can find it."

Jun rubs his temples. His voice is precise and bright with rage, the kind of outrage that sharpens rather than clouds. He slides a folder toward you: annotated timelines, email headers, a list of internal project codes that map to Saltway's sectors.

Jun: "If this goes public raw—we force transparency. But investors panic when certainty fractures. Panic triggers clauses of its own: funding freezes, emergency takeovers. We need pressure without giving them financial whiplash that boots people out before the ink dries."

You taste the arithmetic like iron. The worst outcomes are not storms but paperwork and profit margins that build walls where homes were.

You fold the memo to protect it in your jacket, the paper warm against your ribs like an argument that must be kept alive.

Ava Maren: "We need to control the narrative. If they call us hysterical, they win. If we make the memo the center but frame it with people's stories—tenure, the bakery that rebuilt three times, Old Mara's tale—then it's harder to gaslight."

Jun: "I can have a report prepped for a coordinated release—hard copies to select outlets, an embargo if you want to pace it. Or we go full on: streaming this, live testimony. Control the frames, control the sympathy."

The greenhouse hums like a held breath. You can feel the neighborhood leaning into you, a rope of expectation taut in your chest.

Ori Navarro: "When people see a thing that's been cared for, they don't want to see it replaced. They protect what looks like somebody's love."

People gather—neighbors with patched coats, a roving student crew with cheap cameras, a local breakfast seller with flour on her fingers. You stand in the center of it and feel small and enormous at once, as if the whole neighborhood's heart beat is in your chest.

A small live-stream bubble appears on a volunteer's phone—a VossCorp feed overlayed with corporate branding: "TOWARD RESILIENCE: A CIVIC DIALOGUE"—and for a breath you see Calder's face, composed and courteous, a man who speaks mercy with a scale-model smile.

VossCorp Moderator [on stream]: "We appreciate the community's engagement and propose a moderated debate with Saltway representatives. A live forum—us, you, civic leaders."

Your mouth tastes like the lamp oil. The offer is a blade wrapped in velvet. A debate gives you visibility, a stage you can stand on. But the word "moderated" carries the weight of what was already said back in the square—the power to narrow, to edit, to make your fury tidy.

Calder Voss [via live feed]: "Transparency is in everyone's interest. Let's put data and testimony side by side."

Ori Navarro's hand finds yours. He squeezes with the steady certainty of someone who believes craft can convert attention into affection.

Jun steps closer, voice clipped with energy.

Jun: "We can do the debate, or we can force a full public release. If we march with the docs tomorrow—hard copy in hand—we make it impossible to frame this as rumor. But it risks their legal counters."

Maya: "We can bring the neighbors, the bakery owners, Old Mara. Let them tell their history. People respond to faces. They'll see us, not policy language."

You feel the tide rising in your ribs—every plan a possibility, each with its own risk and ferocity. The crowd leans forward. Cameras swivel. The pier's lights sharpen. Your pulse matches the drum of the strings, building faster, relentless.

You imagine the memo streamed across the city's feeds: the public ignition of a kind that could topple a narrative. You imagine a courtroom that could bind VossCorp's hands if the evidence is iron-clad. You imagine Ori's art—if it catches hearts—seeding a human story that policy maps cannot anonymize.

All the options are dangerous precisely because they are powerful.
