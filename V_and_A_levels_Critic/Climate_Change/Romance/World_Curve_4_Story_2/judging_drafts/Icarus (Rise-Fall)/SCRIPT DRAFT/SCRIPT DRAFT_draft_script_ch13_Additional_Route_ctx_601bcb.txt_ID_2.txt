[Scene: Glass Pier | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Glass Pier's polished cranes angled like indifferent sentries; the pier's LED lines compete with the golden smear of a lowering sun. Your mural hangs between two pylons—a patchwork of reclaimed circuit fragments and seaglass charms threaded into living bio-netting. Small speakers are woven into the weave, rigged to play recorded neighbor-voices at soft intervals.]
[Sound: Low hum of generators, a microphone feed stuttering, children’s recorded laughter threaded through the speakers; distant press murmurs and camera shutters like rain on metal.]
[Music: Tense electronic ambience, building underneath voices]

You came here because you believed a story could hold weight—that telling the city who Saltway was might bend the tide of policy, even a little. You thought beauty could be proof: not just pretty, but demonstrable proof of life and tenure, a living thing that protests displacement by existing.

The pier smells of salt and hot metal. Your jacket is salted at the hem; your notebook is heavy in your inner pocket. Sunlight skates off a sea-glass charm and explodes into a brief, hard flare that makes you squint. Around you, volunteers move like birds—folding planters, adjusting speaker angles, smoothing the bio-netting so the circuits catch the light just enough.

Ori is at your side, hands caked with a soldering paste that turns to glitter in the light. He is laughing at some small thing—a joke about the speakers sounding like a haunted radio—then cuts it off when he sees you watching, as if measurement is now required.

Ori: "Ready?" (His voice is deft, quiet; the grin that follows it is half dare, half apology.)

You nod before your throat can betray you. You press a finger to the small control box at the mural’s base. For a moment everything is the mechanics of breath and counting: inhale, exhale, press.

The speakers bloom. A child’s voice—Mara's granddaughter, maybe—says, "My boat is made of cans," and the crowd laughs in that way that is both delighted and not quite comfortable. A woman you know from down Reddock Street says, "We keep each other warm," and the mural shivers with the weight of a hundred small testimonials that are not testimony in a court but in a neighborhood ledger of care.

Cameras tilt. Reporters' heads turn like sunflowers. For thirty bright seconds, the city pauses and looks at you.

The choice you make is small and human and it reverberates. In either case the footage starts circulating within minutes—faces pressed to phones, clips sent to feeds, a headline with a tender angle. "Saltway's Living Mural: Art of Survival." The framing is soft; the editors choose warmth.

"Look at them," someone says beside you—Maya, probably, though you don't turn. You can feel her at the perimeter, ledger collar visible like armor. People from the neighborhood are here, clustered near the mural: Old Mara with beads catching light, a kid with paint-smeared sleeves, Silas with a camera he keeps pointing at you and then lowering like he's scared to make the moment too permanent.

You let the moment have you for a second—the small swell of perfumed hope that comes when a crowd leans into a story on your terms. It is brief and exquisite.

Then Calder Voss arrives the way weather arrives: inevitable and wanting everyone to account for it. He walks the pier with those gray eyes that measure everything as possible leverage. A small contingent of executive-suited aides and a protective escort in crisp jackets flank him. The reporters, predators and parasites both, reorient.

Calder: "This is lovely," (his tone is warm, calibrated) "truly human—"

Calder's smile is not meant for you. It is meant for the cameras, the investors watching from laptops across the water, the boardroom that decides how 'heart' can be reconciled with 'efficiency.'

You step forward because you have to. Someone must name tenure in the same room where beauty is being applauded for being charming.

You: "It isn't just charm, Calder. This—" (you sweep your hand at the mural, at the speakers, at the faces) "—is people's lives. We need guarantees. We need tenure. We need the paperwork to mean what it says on paper."

Calder inclines his head. He is practiced at listening in a way that can translate questions into opportunities.

Calder: "What you’ve made is powerful, Ava. Truly. It shows what community looks like. But what you’re asking for—permanence in the face of rising seas—requires scale. It requires infrastructure that's been tested beyond sentiment."

You: "Scale doesn't have to mean displacement. Adaptive design can be community-managed. We have prototypes—"

Calder (interrupting, politely): "And prototypes are exactly how we proved concepts at VossArc. Imagine combining your local insight with our resources. Imagine protection that doesn't rely on goodwill alone."

Ori steps between you and the line of suits before you can say it—because he always prefers doing to debating, because the way he carries his hands makes people lower their voices.

Ori: "We aren't asking for pity, Calder. We're asking for rights. If you want to 'combine resources'—then the contract must guarantee that people stay. Written. Enforceable. No loopholes."

Calder smiles again, the thinness of it belying something like sorrow or calculation. His assistant presses a tablet forward and taps. A memo, displayed on a small sleek screen for the cameras, flashes with legalese.

Calder: "I will take that under advisement. We are committed to a fair process." (He lets 'fair' bloom into the microphones like a perfume.)

A reporter asks a pointed question—"Does that mean residents will be relocated for safety?"—and Calder’s answer is immediate and clinical: "Safety is paramount. We cannot gamble with lives in the face of increasing storm activity." He says 'we' as if everyone in the room has already decided.

You hear the subtle click in the background: assistive legal teams typing, memos being circulated, the kind of quiet machinery that translates a well-timed press photo into policy language. You feel your chest tighten to a pitch that won't be soothed by breath.

You choose, and your choice is a splinter against a tide that is already turning. If you call Jun, he works a recorder into the fray and runs a piece that focuses on your legal asks—but the headlines the next morning still favor Caldwell's 'safety first' rhetoric. If you step up and speak, the clip goes viral with tears and applause—but virality doesn't sign deeds.

The protective jackets shift. Somewhere on your periphery, a small team of security — not the friendly volunteers you worked with at Tide Garden, but professional staff in sealed waterproof shells — starts unrolling a strip of bright tape on poles. They tape off a section of the pier with the ceremonial slowness of policy made physical.

"Please move back," a security officer says into a bullhorn with apologies layered over the edges. "This area is restricted."

A neighbor, older than you remember and stubborn enough to have lived through worse, plants herself and folds her arms around a planter like it’s a child.

Neighbor: "We already live here. Where should we go?"

The question is an armature for everything unspoken. Calder's smile doesn't falter, but something in his jaw knits like a storm cloud.

Calder: "There will be relocation assistance where necessary. We can help transform lives away from hazard—"

Maya's ledger slams shut. She says nothing for a second, then says everything with a look that could cut metal. She steps forward and meets Calder's gaze. Words come out like a spat. They are not polite.

Maya: "When 'assistance' comes with eminent domain on the table, it isn't help. It's eviction dressed as charity."

Calder's hand lifts in a small conciliatory gesture. He tries to be both reasonable and final.

Calder: "Negotiations require give and take. I'm offering to bring partners to the table."

Ori is shaking now, that quick, brittle stir that happens when someone chooses hope as a weapon and then finds it insufficient. He moves to the mural, fingers trailing over the circuits and seaglass as if he can feel the narrative in his hands.

Ori: "You can't make people choose between preservation and punctuation. You can't make them invisible and then call it care."

The crowd murmurs. A runner in a reflective vest whispers into his sleeve. On the edge of hearing, you catch the phrase that lands like a punch: "Authorized advance of relocation protocols."

It is not shouted. It doesn't have to be. It is signed into some ledger in some office, then whispered down. Security teams begin quietly, almost almost apologetically, to fold planters into crates. Children who had been fishing at the mural’s base are told to step back. A uniformed pair places a small printed notice on an easel: "Temporary Protective Measures — By Order."

The mural's speakers keep playing neighbor-voices—artificially serene in the middle of administrative motion—and the contrast turns the whole scene into a found photograph: humanity as backdrop, policy as foreground.

You move faster than the breath in your chest allows. You reach for a planter and step in front of the line of crews as they lift it. Hands touch yours—neighbors trying to hold onto things that are not theirs to keep in law but are in heart.

You find yourself in front of Calder, and none of the arguments you rehearsed seem adequate.

You: "This is not temporary for us. These planters hold memory. You take them, you take stories."

Calder tilts his head, voice leveled like an engineer reading specifications.

Calder: "Stories are valuable. They belong in the record. But records also require institutional certainty, Ava. We cannot build a future on sentiment alone. We need replicable, scalable solutions."

The press writes his words as an answer. The cameras like his framing because it suggests a plan—a plan people can buy into.

Ori, quiet suddenly, presses something into your palm. When you look down, it's one of his sea-glass charms, warm from his hand and heavier with solder than it looks.

Ori: (no words) (He steps back, lips pressed thin.)

You close your fingers around the charm. It is a small, ridiculous thing—an island of green glass stitched to a little circuit scrap and a scrap of twine. It is not a contract. It is not tenure. It is only the weight of an intention that can be held.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand closing around the charm; camera pulls back to show rows of planters moving like a slow retreating line.]
[Sound: A child's voice repeats, "My boat is made of cans," then is drowned by the sound of crates sliding; a reporter's voice drones about 'practical solutions.']

The highest tide of noise hits: photographers shout for statements, a volunteer starts to weep, someone curses, a man in a suit tells a tech to encrypt a memo and then to brief investors. It is a waterfall of small violences, administrative and bureaucratic, that fold a neighborhood into papers and schedules.

You feel the breath leave you and come back as a faraway sound. The mural still plays. Someone records the scene, and that footage will matter—perhaps in court, perhaps never. But the runnable letters of law are already in motion.

You want to speak, to make one last public claim that tenure is a non-negotiable human right here. Your throat tightens. You open your mouth and find the words are thin, almost private.

You: "We tried to show them who we are."

Ori watches you. His face is a map of small, private regrets and stubborn affection. He steps closer, hesitates, and then rests his hand on your shoulder—brief, grounding.

Ori: "You gave them everything you had to give."

You: "Was it enough?"

He doesn't answer with words. Instead he tilts his head, a small, almost animal motion that says more than any speech could now. Then he presses his forehead to yours for the fiftieth time and perhaps the fiftieth way it has ever mattered.

The crowd thins as the sun drops. The pier is a stage cleared by policy. People fold planters into neat crates. The mural hangs there, still playing, its circuitry exposed to the dusk. Cameras click until the last of the press leaves. The tape remains. The notices remain.

You stand in the residue of a moment that has been framed and sold back to you as an anecdote.

Old Mara approaches without ceremony. Her hand finds yours, fingers wrapped with the memoried patience of someone who has seen tides sweep whole streets away and still planted mornings after.

Old Mara: "They will tell a story about this day," she says. "They will say you were charming or tragic or both. But memory—true memory—keeps doing the work, even when the cameras don't."

You look at the charm in your palm. It is warm from Ori's hand and feels improbably heavy for a thing made of glass and scrap. Rain begins, soft and obliging, like a punctuation mark.

You tuck the charm into your jacket, into the pocket where the notebook sits, and close the notebook as if to seal a wound.

You watch as a small convoy—official vehicles with discreet emblems—pulls up at the end of the pier. People you know climb into them with their children and their boxes, faces folded in ways you have not yet figured out how to read. Some cry openly; some are stubborn with the false bravado of people being made to fold their lives into suitcases. A few slip away down alleys, refusing to be catalogued.

The finality comes not as a storm but as a ledgered routine: memos, signed, delivered; notices pinned to doors; tents and relocation vans. It arrives under a softer light, with everyone repeating generous language: "We will ensure dignity."

You encounter Ori one last time on the shadowed boardwalk, where the lamps throw small halos on puddles. He is packing tools into his satchel as if the act of packing can convert grief into future action.

Ori: "I'll keep the prototypes moving," he says. "I'll take them where they'll be used—where people want them. This—" (he taps the satchel) "—this work goes on."

You: "And us? What do we become if the neighborhood is unstitched?"

He looks at you as if measuring whether the question is about logistics or the heart. He is younger than the job demands sometimes. He is always younger than the costs.

Ori: "We become whatever's left with the courage to keep showing up. Sometimes courage is staying. Sometimes it's carrying the work elsewhere."

You feel like you have been asked to choose between two versions of yourself that are both right and both cruel. The rain picks up. The charm ticks quietly in your pocket.

You stand on the Glass Pier and watch Saltway fold itself into the language of paperwork and policy. The mural continues to play a child's canned-boat line into the evening air—a recorded lullaby for a place being taken apart with polite hands.

You think of the tides, of the way water remembers channels even after you try to reroute it. You think of Maggie from the vegetable stall, of the boy who learned to fish at your shoulder, of Mara's beads clicking like a low, stubborn watch.

You slide your notebook into your jacket, fingers tracing the spine as if to locate a list of what was possible and what was not. The notebook doesn't answer. It never did.

You breathe. It is an effort both small and enormous.

You turn away from the pier. You do not run. You walk. Each step is a contract between the part of you that believes in publicness and the part that is breaking.

The night presses on you like a thing that remembers everything and forgives nothing. You fold your shoulders, the weight of the charm in your pocket a tiny counterbalance.

Somewhere in the press coverage the next day the mural will be called "a testament" and "a quaint reminder." Somewhere in the same batch of memos, the city will write relocation timelines with the language of care. The two narratives will sit beside each other, coherent to different audiences.

You think of what you would say if anyone asked you plainly: did you fail? Did you try? The truth is a complicated ledger: you aimed for tenure and found admiration. You gave your story and were given a photograph.

You do not have the answer; you have a decision made into consequences, and you carry them like the weight of wet glass.

You fold your coat tighter and begin the walk home through the half-lit boardwalks of Saltway, past people who are carrying boxes and people who are standing quiet as witnesses. The sound of the tide is steady and indifferent. You keep your steps slow enough to be deliberate and fast enough not to be left behind.

You keep walking because there is, stubbornly, still work left to do. It will be different now—more legal, more public, more brittle at the edges. It will be less naive and more armored. It will require different seams and new alliances. It will require you to be less certain that beauty could have saved everything.

But it will still require you.
