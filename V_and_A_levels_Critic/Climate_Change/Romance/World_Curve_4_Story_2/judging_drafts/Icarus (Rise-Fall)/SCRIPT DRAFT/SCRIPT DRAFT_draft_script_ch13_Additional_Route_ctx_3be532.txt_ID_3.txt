[Scene: Glass Pier | Dusk]
[Visual: The pier's glass panels catch the dying light; a mosaic mural of seaglass and reclaimed circuits unfurls along a corrugated bulkhead. Small speakers are tucked between shards. Floodlights wash the scene in warm amber.]

You stand at the edge of the installation as Ori counts down beneath his breath. Your hands are salt-rough and steady, your field notebook heavy at your hip, and every small hinge in your chest feels like it could snap if you laugh or cry too loudly. The pier beneath you has been a rehearsal space and a pressure valve for months; tonight it is a stage. You remember signaling him—quick, low, the way you taught the Tide Garden volunteers to pass a tool without stopping the work—and he answered with a grin that smelled of solder and seawash.

A cluster of children presses to the front, faces upturned. A reporter leans on the rail, camera like a ship's prow. The city's skyline slices the horizon: cranes like exclamation points. For a few seconds everything is suspended between two tides—you, Ori, and the whole neighborhood pressed into a single human breath.

Ori: "Three... two... one."

[Sound: A soft hum rises as speakers flicker to life. Seaglass catches the lights and the mural breathes.]
[Visual: Tiny LEDs pulse along recycled circuit veins; a collage of recorded voices folds into the air—neighbors, market sellers, Old Mara's threadbare laugh, a kid singing off-key about frogs. The sound is patchwork and whole.]

You feel the first wave before you understand it: the way people soften when they hear themselves. A child's voice—thin, bright—says, "This is my street," and the camera lens reflects that voice like a coin. Phones lift; broadcast mics find the rhythm of the piece. The mural's speakers are small, but so many of them stitched together make a voice that reaches beyond the pier.

Ori leans in, hand warm at the small of your back; you feel the familiar map of his fingers where wires and calluses meet. He speaks low, a sound for you only.

Ori: (quiet) "They're listening."

You try to make the triumphant sentence—tenure, rights, enforceable hearings—rise from your chest, but the moment has shifted. People are crying; the mural has turned them into witnesses to themselves. You had wanted headlines that could be turned into law. Instead, you have created an image that makes the city want to protect a memory.

Ori: "Don't let them edit the story into a postcard."

You: "They love postcards."

He looks at you—open, searching. There's that old compel-to-fix in his face, the thing you fell for in the Ferryworks when he welded solar petals at midnight. But beneath it is something taut: his avoidance of a fight he doesn't know how to win without breaking something or someone.

Ori: "We make them feel it. Then we make them notice the noticeable—Jun's piece, the ledger of clauses, the case—"

You interrupt because you need to. "We make them feel it and then we lock it into law. We make them write down that Saltway stays."

Ori's voice drops. "And if they don't write it down? What then—do we keep making beauty while they fill out the forms that take everything?"

You can hear the strain line by line. The mural plays on, the speakers threading words and laughter like a rope. People gather around an image of children tying lanterns to a stilts' railing. The press shoots slow, reverent angles; social feeds rattle with comment threads of admiration.

The applause comes—soft, then swelling—then a different sound: the quiet clearing of corporate throat. A video feed, projected behind the mural by a kinetic billboard truck, flickers to life with an impossibly glossy VossCorp insignia. Calder Voss's image fills the screen: composed, pale gray eyes steady. He looks like a promise in a suit.

Calder Voss: (on screen) "What Harborlight needs is scalable resilience. Art is beautiful; communities are beautiful. But beauty isn't infrastructure."

His smile is precise. The words have all the softness of glass. Reporters shift, pens poised to catch an angle.

You demand, raw: "This is our home. The living wall isn't an aesthetic—it's tenure, it's labor, it's—"

A press liaison's voice over the PA smooths everything into policy-speak. Calder's statement continues, and the camera cuts between children's hands and his composed, clinical certainty.

Calder Voss: (on screen) "We must think beyond sentimentality. When the next surge comes, we cannot rely on 'wood and story' to protect our city. Efficient, tested engineering—centralized—saves lives."

The crowd's warmth hardens into a pang of betrayal. A murmured argument breaks out in clusters: is he right? Can beauty resist physics? Someone curses, half under breath, then louder.

You move toward the truck, words colliding in your mouth—public, recorded, enforceable tenure—but someone slaps a news van microphone between you and the screen. Calder's people control the narrative flow like a dam; they have lawyers, lawyers have memos, memos have quiet lines that create permission.

A sharp, officious man in a VossCorp jacket steps through the throng. His tablet is small, but when he taps it the ripple feels like a contract crossing the pier.

VossCorp Rep: (polite, thin) "We'd love to meet with community representatives. For safety's sake, we recommend temporary relocations during construction. It's standard protocol."

You feel the words as a knife hidden in velvet. Temporary. Relocation. Protocol. The sentence lands like a jurisdictional tide.

Ori: "Temporary doesn't live in people's chests."

He moves forward, hands articulating as always—part maker, part orator.

Ori: "You don't get to wave a spreadsheet at a neighborhood and call the rest a 'protocol.' People here have lived through storms. We can build partnership prototypes. Co-management. Transparency."

The rep's smile doesn't budge. A camera tilts to capture the exchange. The public watching at home will see Ori's earnest hands and VossCorp's steady set of teeth. Both will be legible. Both will be simplified.

You don't notice the first sheet of paper until Silas—a teenager you know by the way his hoodie smells of algae—slides it into your palm. Typed, professional, stamped: Authorization for 'preparatory accommodations.' Underneath, small-print clauses: 'temporary displacement,' 'owner consent presumed,' 'municipal compliance encouraged.'

You read the words and the letters rearrange into eviction. It's not a public decree with a megaphone. It is the quiet machinery of policy that moves while the city watches the mural.

You look up. Neighbors fold planters, hands trembling; a woman you taught to assemble mangrove modules packs them into a cart with the practiced care of someone stripping her house of meaning. Children are being led away by parents who keep their faces blank so the TV doesn't see the panic. The cameras are still filming—their thrill has not dulled—but their gaze has already shifted from the content to its context: isn't it quaint, the city thinks. Isn't it sweet how humans make art in the face of storms.

Calder's statement is rebroadcast on loop across feeds, repackaged by pundits: a necessary sacrifice for safety; an efficient plan for a precarious future. Investors call the mural "touching" and "good PR," then ask their counsel how the rollout will affect property rights. Within hours, memos travel up and down the chain—quiet, legal, cold—authorizing more aggressive relocation tactics under the pretext of safety. Bulldozers are not yet visible, but the paperwork is heavier than anything you can carry.

You want to scream that this is not what you asked for. That the spectacle was supposed to make people see Saltway as citizens, not as an exhibit. You want to make the word 'tenure' sticky on every screen. But the mural's light stains the night in a dishonest gold, and your syllables feel like glass shards.

Ori finds you among the chaos and presses something small into your palm. His fingers are damp; his breath smells like ozone and solder.

Ori: (barely audible) "For when you need to remember the weight of what we tried."

You look down. A seaglass charm—smoothed by many hands, threaded with a reclaimed circuit fragment. It is warm with his palm's heat, heavy with an implied vow. He doesn't say keep fighting; he doesn't say leave. He doesn't say stay. He simply holds your gaze until you understand everything that's left unsaid: grief, apology, refusal to capitulate without making art a weapon too.

You: "Ori—"

He interrupts you with the gentlest of curses and a laugh that is more sermon than humor. "Don't make a speech. Not here. Not now."

You let the word 'not' sit between you like a pause for breath. Around you, the city's appetite for stories is being fed. The mural—your mural—becomes a photograph in editorial spreads that romanticize resilience while erasing the political teeth you need. Calder reframes the entire narrative by invoking a single, terrifying image: efficiency as lifesaver. It is a simple, marvellous lie.

[Sound: The tape line flutters; footsteps recede; distant engines idle like monsters waiting under water.]
[Visual: Security teams fold a banner with deliberate care. The mural's light dims under a flanking truck's shadow. Children are coaxed away, their small hands leaving phantom prints in the salt-sprayed boards.]

You walk the pier slowly, as though each step could undo a stitch. Planters are collapsed like folded skirts. Wires are snipped. The mural's voice—old laughs, a market seller's advice, a child's frog song—fades into the background like an old recording left on a high shelf.

Ori keeps pace beside you. He keeps his head low so the cameras catch only the outline of his cheekbone. You reach for his sleeve; your fingers find the indented place where he hooked a tool the night the Tide Garden almost failed. It's a small map of shared repairs.

Ori: (soft) "We made something true tonight."

You: "They took the truth and turned it into a postcard."

He doesn't answer with rhetoric. He answers with a look so complex it could be any emotion—love, grief, fury, fear. In uncertain pasts that might look like a kiss. Tonight it looks unreadable: a hollowed, beautiful thing that holds more than language will fit.

You fold the seaglass charm into your palm and feel its cold, then its warmth return from your skin. You press your thumb against the internal edge of the charm like a benediction. There is mourning here—sharp and immediate—and there is also something else: the recognition that the work of the living seawall, the ledger of rights, the written, enforceable protections you still need will not be won by beauty alone.

[Music: The strings snap; a single bowed note sustains and then thins into an almost inaudible thread.]

You look back once, at the mural dimming under corporate light, at the faces of neighbors folding in on themselves like origami. You slide the charm into your field notebook, behind a tide chart that now reads like a will. You do not know if the charm will be enough to comfort you tomorrow, or the next night when the municipality posts notices, or the day a work crew comes with trucks and polite apologies. You only know what you are carrying—a small, seaworn thing made by hands you trust—and the ache of a city that is learning how to let go under a softer light.

You stand on Glass Pier as the cameras leave and the trucks close their shutters. The mural is admired. It is co-opted. The press has its images; Calder has his reframing; the memos have been filed. The spectacle has been beautiful and it has not been enough.

[Visual: The pier lights dim to a cold blue. Rain begins—first a patter, then earnest—blurring the edges of everything into a watercolor of salt and regret.]
[Sound: The rain hits the seaglass; the recorded voices on the mural are swallowed one by one.]

You inhale the rain, the salt, the smoke of the lanterns being put out. You close your notebook and feel the little weight of the charm where your thumb rests. At the edge of the pier, Ori lets go of your hand and steps away—no distance dramatic, only necessary—his shadow folding into the crowd as the tow trucks begin to rove the strip.

There is nowhere else to go on this night that will make the moment undo itself. The mural will be a memory, a headline, a lesson in how beauty can be admired and then pasted into a sanitized narrative. The paperwork will move like a tide you can't climb against alone.

You let your shoulders drop. You do not know how much of Saltway you can save, or what the cost will be to the people who have already given everything. You know the face of each person who will lose a piece of their home and the sound of their small voices woven into a mural that ended up decorating the very plan that displaces them.

You stand in the rain, seaglass warm in your pocket, and decide—without fanfare, without a crowd—that tomorrow you will reopen the ledger. You'll find Jun; you'll find Old Mara; you'll find the legal file that binds words to power. You will speak to anyone who will listen, and you will make the beauty of tonight into evidence, not an exhibit.

But not tonight.

Tonight you let the grief come with the rain.
