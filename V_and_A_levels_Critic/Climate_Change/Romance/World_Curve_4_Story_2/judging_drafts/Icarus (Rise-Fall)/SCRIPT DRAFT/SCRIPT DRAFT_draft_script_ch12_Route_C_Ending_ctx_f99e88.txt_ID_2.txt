You arrive with the salt-smell still under your nails and the notebook closed against your ribs. The Ferryworks hums the way a choir hums before the first note — full of possibility and a little afraid. Your open-source release has done what a thousand petitions could not: people have come to touch the design, to try the math with their own hands.

Jun's footage plays on a loop across a reclaimed metal gable screen — his voice steady against overlaying legal footnotes. He runs the camera close to the living modules, then pulls back to show a cluster of volunteers teaching a family to plant halophytes into a braided platform.

Jun: "—and here, on record, you can see the community-built pilot sustained a 1.8-meter surge during the test last quarter. The documents we attached show how VossCorp's contracts tied municipal funds to non-disclosure clauses. Citizens deserve alternate paths."

You watch people's mouths move as they watch the footage. Someone claps when Jun mentions tenure rights; another wipes a tear. You feel the warm, dangerous lift of momentum like a tide pulling a boat past the dock.

Ori threads between visitors with his welding goggles on his head, stopping to hand a child a polished seaglass charm, showing an elderly woman how a tether is knotted. He moves with practiced lightness, but under the surface his hands are doing the heavy work of easing questions, deflecting thinly veiled skepticism, holding the room's temperature.

You meet his gaze. He grins — quick, showy, familiar — then lets it fall away when he remembers you are the one who will answer for the next step: what you will ask the city to do now the world is watching.

[Scene: Ferryworks — Afternoon]
[Visual: The Ferryworks is now a temporary classroom; folding tables of blueprints, flour-dusted bread, and soldering irons. Sunlight glares off puddles on the deck as a restless wind begins to test the welded seams of the solar sails.]
[Sound: Phones pinging, distant thunder like a thumb drumming across metal; laughter that tightens into quick conversations]

Workshops sprout across Harborlight the way barnacles do: clusters of hands and answers gathering more hands. You teach three different knot patterns in an hour and watch a student improvise an adaptation you could swear you should have thought of. Volunteers sign up to ferry materials. A municipal liaison appears in a gray raincoat and writes notes with a precise, skeptical face.

Then Calder Voss appears on a wide municipal feed — not at your door but on the glass pier's ceremonial stage, his image perfectly lit and designed to soothe. He offers language like balm: collaboration, due diligence, scalable resilience. He says the word "partnership" as if it were a bridge everyone could walk across together.

Calder Voss (on feed): "We are at a juncture. Innovation must be safe. We welcome grassroots models, but they must meet the rigorous standards our city relies on. VossArc will be open to oversight and co-development."

Jun, watching the feed, tilts his head. He has the look he gets before he asks the uncomfortable questions on camera.

Jun: "He says 'co-development' a lot. Watch his funding notes—there's a clause that ties technical oversight to VossCorp certification. The devil lives in the appendices."

You feel the old, familiar squeeze around your ribs: hope braided with suspicion. The good news is loud; the warning is a thin, steady whine under it. You and Ori trade a quiet look — you, counting tenure; Ori, already imagining how to thread beauty into compliance without letting beauty become shackled.

Ori: "Let him try to co-opt the language. We keep doing what works. We teach. We show. If they try to box us in, we'll open the box."

You: "We document everything. Jun's legal attachments go public simultaneously. No single gatekeeper."

Ori: "And if they close the gate?"

You: "Then we open more doors."

Your voice is small, but it catches. The room around you breathes in and out, like a hundred lungs filling. A buoyant optimism lifts everyone for a handful of days: local governments send delegations, DIY brigades travel from other districts, and the media treats the Ferryworks like the pulse of a new movement.

[Scene: Glass Pier / VossArc Construction | Early Evening — Storm intensifies]
[Visual: Cranes cut harsh teeth against a sky that has gone black. Work-lamps flare. A live broadcast splits the screen: Calder in a windproof podium and your sensor feeds, jittering and labeled with timestamps.]
[Music: Percussive, high-tempo strings; a low brass undercurrent like distant thunder]
[Sound: Waves hitting, the crack of a snapped cable, a public-address system straining to be heard]

Calder speaks into the wind with curated empathy. He gestures to the cameras with a steadiness designed to map onto public need.

Calder Voss (live): "This is a test of our systems. We saw failures in decentralized pilots earlier. Today we demonstrate why scale and unified engineering are essential. We will coordinate relief. We will protect citizens."

On your feed, one of the pilot's living modules holds—moss and root mats staying stubborn against a local surge. In another, at an unanticipated shear point where the tidal vector meets an engineered outflow, a seam fails. You see it as a sick, slow peel: braided cordage snaps, a living wall buckles, water finds a place to wedge and widen.

Time compresses. Jun's legal footnotes scroll beneath the live feed like a prophecy. You feel every breath in the Ferryworks as if it's a bellwether.

Jun: "Look — the shear's where the outflow meets the older landfill bulkhead. That's not a typical failure mode in the simulations—it's an upstream release effect. This isn't a design fault alone."

Calder's camera angles tuck away the worst images; his words are already building a narrative not of nuance but of scale.

Calder Voss (later, edited clip): "These isolated prototypes, while admirable, ultimately introduced variabilities that cannot be managed across a city. Centralization provides the rigorous, tested path forward."

The clip runs on loop in the feeds. In the crisis' immediate geometry, what should be a moment of communal testing becomes a political fact. You and Jun exchange shots of evidence, annotations, waterlogged blueprints. The city is watching the same images and drawing opposite conclusions.

Inside the Ferryworks, volunteers fight a spreading breach with sandbags and living braids. People yell names. A child cries. Ori appears at your side, soaked through, coat curtains of saltwater. His amber eyes, usually quick with mischief, are hollow, like light from a streetlamp behind fog.

Ori: "We held pockets. We saved porches, school gardens—places where people could keep their things. But a seam split in C-block; it opened like a mouth. They will use that."

You reach for him and your fingers find nothing but cold fabric. For a moment you both stand in the current-between-words, where plans and promises go to be tested.

You: "We catalog everything. We show the upstream release data. We make the legal record tie the failure to their flow management."

Ori: (bitter laugh) "And they'll say—'See? You made it dangerous. You introduced irregular elements!' They'll call it unregulated, a hazard. They'll take the leash in the name of 'safety.'"

You: "Not if we make the record louder than their talking points. Jun—get the timestamped flows to the registry now."

Jun works a recorder worn to the weather, fingers numb. The feeds are a blur of labeled timecodes and municipal e-mails that will someday be used in public inquiries. But legal processes are slow and the feed is loud. Lobbying is louder still.

[Scene: Ferryworks — Night — After the Peak]
[Visual: Rain smears everything into motion; light pools across puddles. The Ferryworks is battered but standing. The living modules look ragged, some intact, some like pulled teeth. A handful of neighboring blocks are abandoned under temporary levees.]
[Music: Dissonant chords, then hush — a high, thin violin that mourns]
[Sound: Distant bulldozers; official vehicles' amplified announcements; a quieter storm of human voices: anger, pleading, exhausted relief]

The immediate danger passes but the aftermath is bureaucratic and brutal. VossCorp's legal team circulates a terse statement: unregulated prototypes are a liability; for public safety, enforcement teams will secure unauthorized structures. The city issues emergency directives that cite "public hazard" and "unvetted installations." Your designs — the very thing you published to protect tenure — are suddenly framed as the cause of instability even when the systemic breach began upstream.

Jun: "They anchored the narrative overnight. The clips, the soundbites…they picked the failure point and looped it into their case. The legal text is already out—classifying private prototypes as 'unregulated hazards.'"

You watch families retrieve what they can from porches that were saved and watch others who are offered relocation behind newly erected levees. For some, the levee is safety and the only practical option; for others, it's the end of a way of life. Your policies have held here and there, in tendered blocks where neighbors organized to defend their tenure. But other neighborhoods—older, poorer, more contested—are being packed on trucks under storm-gray directives.

Ori moves through the crowd like a ghost, handing out wrapped bundles — a child's raincoat, a pot they'd loved. He does this because the world still needs small, beautiful things after the big machines have moved. He does this because it is what he knows to do when systems fail people.

You: "We made something that worked where people could tend it. We opened a path. It feels…so small next to the machines they roll out."

Ori: "Is small bad? You kept families in their houses. You kept gardens alive. But yes — you wanted scale, and the city wanted a single answer. They turned our evidence into a reason to centralize."

There is no single villain in this moment, just a creeping policy logic that prefers certainty over messy human arrangements. Calder does not look like a villain when he speaks on camera; he looks like someone offering a map through a storm. But the map costs land and memory.

[Scene: Saltway Pier | Night — The Rain Harder Now]
[Visual: The pier is lit by dipping streetlamps; water runs in rivulets down every surface. Ori stands at the edge, coat streaked with salt, hair plastered to his head. His silhouette is a splinter of warmth against the cold expanse.]
[Music: Low piano notes find a single, repeating motif; then silence filled by rain]
[Sound: The rain is loud, a constant thing; the harbor murmurs like an animal settling down to chew on its wounds]

You walk toward him, boots sinking into wet boards. He doesn't turn when you approach; he seems to be listening to the harbor the way someone listens for a heartbeat.

You slide your shoulder against his, an old habit of leaning into each other when the world is too loud. He makes a small sound that could be a laugh or not. You look at his face and see a house where there used to be a light.

You: "We did what we could. We made things people could live with."

Ori: (voice flat, then sharpening) "We did, but they took the footage they liked. They ignored the causes they couldn't control. They called what's dangerous anything that wasn't theirs to regulate. Our work—our hands—are now an exhibit and an accident simultaneously."

You: "What do you want to do next?"

He turns finally. Up close, his eyes are the amber of old streetlights. They're tired. They're honest.

Ori: "I don't know. I want to fix things. I want to build prototypes that are impossible and beautiful. But I—" He stops, as if the rest of the sentence would be too big to put into the rain. "I am tired in the way you are tired. I don't know if I can just keep wandering to the next place where they need a wall or a garden."

You: "You don't have to wander. Not if you don't want to."

Ori looks at you like he's trying on a future and it might fit — or it might pinch. His jaw works.

Ori: "Maybe I thought being everywhere was how I helped. But seeing a kid tuck their hand into the same pocket they always had? Seeing Old Mara sit where her shop stood? Maybe that matters more than being everywhere."

You hold his gaze until the rain sharpens it. You have both been remade by the same tide — bruised, practical, stubbornly hopeful.

You: "We'll keep teaching. We'll keep documenting. We'll keep pulling people onto the water to show them how to tend these places. We'll fight the legal fights with Jun. We'll even—" Your chest tightens, words catching on salt. "—we'll make places that are harder to call 'hazards' because their stewardship is visible, collective. Because the city can't just point a rule at a thing that people own with their hands."

Ori: "And if they do take them?"

You: "Then we bear witness. We build records. We make noise until the cost of swallowing communities is political and real."

He leans forward and presses his forehead to yours for a moment—no flourish, just the small, human measurement of proximity. The rain hammers above you as if to punctuate the promise.

Ori: (low) "I am with you. Even if it's a slow, broken sort of victory."

You: "Even if it leaves us hollow in places we didn't want to be hollow."

The words are true and they sting. You both feel the pull of exhausted triumph, the way relief tastes after a long defiant fight. The victory is partial; the losses are precise and raw. The city is rearranging itself around the images that played fastest on the feeds, and in those rearrangements whole neighborhoods are weighed against engineering claims.

[Scene: Ferryworks — Dawn After the Storm]
[Visual: Pale light through wet glass. The Ferryworks is quieter; people sleep on benches, damp blankets over knees. The living modules are bandaged. A hand-lettered sign reads: "REBUILD: WITH AND FOR US."]
[Music: A low, sustained chord that holds a note like a held breath; then another, softer chord that suggests a new resolve]
[Sound: Slow, deliberate footsteps; the squawk of a distant radio reporting policy changes]

You sit with Jun over a map dotted with names and notes. Some blocks bear green pins: saved, held by tenants. Others have red pins: moved, leveed, rehomed. The pattern of your best work is patchy — bright pockets in a wider, gray lattice of enforced relocation. Legal teams from VossCorp have already filed claims for "unregulated hazards," and the city council, frightened and tired, signs emergency ordinances to limit private structural experiments. But community workshops still sit full each night. People still call from other districts asking how to start. The movement is not dead. It is complicated and scattered.

Jun: "You changed the conversation. You forced people to see alternatives. But the institutions respond in noise. The press will call this 'a turning point.' For whom, and in what direction, depends on whether we keep pulling the thread."

You fold the map, feeling the edges of your hands sore from where you traced the lines for so long.

You: "We keep pulling it."

Ori returns the little seaglass charm to your palm and smiles a small, habitual smile that is full of private weather.

Ori: "Then let's be tired together."

You let yourself laugh — an almost guilty sound — and the three of you lean into the plan for what comes next: documentation drives, public assemblies, mutual aid brigades. These are the tools you have left that still bear weight. They are neither monumental nor perfect, but they have people in them.

You think of the families who kept their homes and of the ones who were moved. You think of Old Mara, of the children learning knotwork under the Ferryworks' lamps. The ledger of your work is uneven, a scrap-book of small miracles and stubborn, unavoidable defeats.

You tuck your notebook back into your jacket, feeling the salt-crisp spine under your fingers. Outside, the rain keeps falling, as if the city cannot stop rinsing the night's decisions into the harbor.

You look at Ori — at the salt-streaked coat, the tired jaw, the promise that is both fragile and tenacious — and you name what you feel not as certainty but as a commitment that can survive endurance.

You: "We'll keep tending this. Together."

Ori: (soft) "Together."

[Visual: Slow pull-back; the Ferryworks glows like a lantern against the gray harbor. The camera lingers on the living wall, bandaged but alive, and a small child on the boardwalk practicing a knot with solemn concentration.]
[Sound: Rain, steady; distant voices of neighbors trading tools and stories, a new day's work beginning]

You stand on the pier, rain soaking through where you had thought you were prepared. The city has been both companion and judge. The victory you pursued is smeared with aftermath; pieces of Saltway remain beautiful and stubborn, and pieces are gone. You have created a template and you have been used as evidence for consolidation. The world is not clean.

But you are not finished.

You lift your face to the rain and let it wash the salt and ache down from your cheeks. The lights across the harbor flicker, steady, like people staying up into the night to keep each other's lamps lit. You are tired. You are raw. You are accountable.

You breathe in the cold, briny air and for the first time in days you allow a quiet, fierce permission: to mourn what was lost, to count what was saved, and then to begin again.
