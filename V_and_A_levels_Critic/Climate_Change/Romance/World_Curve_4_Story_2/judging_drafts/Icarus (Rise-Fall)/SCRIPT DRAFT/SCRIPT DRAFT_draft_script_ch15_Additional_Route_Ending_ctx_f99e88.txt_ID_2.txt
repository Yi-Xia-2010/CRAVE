Your fingers smell of wet soil and machine oil. You breathe in brine and solder, and the memory of the Ferryworks' lamp-light hums under your ribs like a wound that keeps beating.

You: "We keep the ledger."

Old Mara: "Aye. But ledgers need people who will read them aloud."

Her eyes find you and hold. You feel the pressure—that slow, inevitable tide of everything you have chosen and everything you have refused. In the distance, the cranes at VossArc etch a new line into the skyline; levees rise like teeth in the mouth of the bay.

You move through the cove like someone who knows the language of risks—measured, steady. Meals are being shared under patched tarps. Someone plates steaming fish and seaweed stew on enamelware. A neighbor laughs too loud; it wobbles your chest like a small wave.

But you notice other things, too: a family packing a single crate; a faded "Available" sign tacked to a porch; a group of younger adults arguing about temporary relocation. The cove is a patchwork of defiance and exhaustion. The work you and Ori plotted—low-cost living seawalls, floating planters, shared sensors—has kept doors open and some families tethered. Yet beyond the green rows, the city is moving on a different tide.

You can see the VossCorp levees in the distance—an engineered line against the horizon—and you can see the cranes that string them into place. They don't look like salvation. Ori finds you by the old tide ladder—hands smeared with weld-slag and algae. His overalls are stained in a map only he could read. He moves with the restless energy you know too well: hands already turning ideas into miracles even as his mouth explains ten new cities that could use what you've built.

Ori: (fast, luminous) "They'll listen in Riveford. They've got a council willing to try hybrid walls. I can get a fab-crew together, maybe a modular prototype before next quarter. I could—"

You: (interrupting) "You're saying 'I could' like you don't have to ask anyone. Not me. Not Saltway."

Ori pauses. His eyes narrow almost imperceptibly; his amber gaze holds an island of want and apology.

Ori: "I know. And I'm tired of asking permission that takes years to arrive. You said—remember?—we wanted to show people how to keep what they love. We built a model that works here—"

You: "We built a model that works here because we stayed. Because we fought to keep people in place. You keep talking like that is a launchpad for you to go somewhere else."

Ori: (soft, then sharp) "Because it is. Because other places won't wait for us to write them a manual."

There is a crack in his smile you haven't seen before—small, like a fault line.

You: "There are things that can't be shipped with a welding kit, Ori."

Ori: "I know. I know. But I can't... I can't only tether myself here and pretend the rest won't drown."

You hear the urgency jag into his voice. It is not accusation; it's a confession. His hands circle the satchel of tools at his side like a patient fastening a life-belt.

Ori: (whisper) "I can make more places hold."

You: "We can make here hold."

He looks away, out toward the levees. The sound of the city is a distant tide pulling at both of you.

The cove changes around you as the day advances: children take shifts tending planters; volunteers secure tarps; Old Mara begins an inventory of names—who's stayed, who's gone, who is leaving tonight. The list is not neat. It is water-worn. That inventory is a ledger of compromises.

You are pulled in threads—on one side, the immediate, tangible successes: a dozen families who will not be forced out; a dozen more whose roofs leak because of this winter's tide but whose tenure is secure because of a living wall that flexed when it needed to. On the other, the knowledge that VossCorp's levees are being built for another future—and that many of the city's resources are following that build.

You want to make a civic future out of small things. Ori wants the prototype to scale. Both positions are honest. Both are urgent. Both carry loss.

[Scene: Saltway Cove | Dusk]
[Visual: The sky bruises and folds; lamp-light flickers on wet surfaces; the levees' silhouette slices the horizon]
[Sound: The cove is louder—voices, tacks of hammer, a distant motor-car. Rain begins, not yet heavy but insistent]
[Music: Crescendo—brass and staccato strings; heartbeat-like bass]

The parting happens fast because everything has been accelerating for weeks and because you both have been holding the same hurt like something necessary. People move around you with the calm of those who have rehearsed survival: bags latched, children snugged into blankets, promises traded like currency.

Ori's hands are warm and shaking. He takes your wrist and, without letting go, fingers the sea-glass bracelet you always wear. He tugs it free with a practiced gentleness and watches it catch the light.

Ori: (voice thinned) "Keep it. For me."

You: "I don't need it to remember you."

Ori: (laughs, then sobers) "No—it's for you. When the nights get slippery with waiting, wear it like an anchor."

You: "If you leave, who's going to weld the makeshift floodgate on the north spit next week?"

Ori: "You'll call me. I'll answer. I always answer."

You want to believe that ring of words can stitch distance. You want to believe in the improvisations that have kept Saltway alive: in solder and song and stubbornness. But distance is not a tool you can repair with wire. It corrodes like salt.

You: (low, urgent) "We built this so people could stay here—because leaving isn't the same as surviving. This is my life."

Ori: (soft, breaking) "This is my life too."

You both laugh, and the laugh fractures. You close your hands around each other like two people trying to hold a boat together in rough water.

Ori: "I wish there was another way to do both."

You: "So do I."

The rain begins to thud harder, a thin roar threading through the fabric of the cove. Someone calls a child's name. Old Mara crosses to you and sets a small, folded piece of wax-paper into your palm. Inside, a scrap of sea-worn fishing line and a coin—an old ledger token she'd kept. Her face is unreadable and terrible and kind all at once.

Old Mara: "Keep the anchor. Keep the list. Tell them the stories."

You: "I will."

Old Mara: "Then let him go before the tide cheats you both."

The words are small and blunt as a fossil. You feel your breath jag, and the world tightens to Ori's face—amber wet with rain, eyes luminous, then breaking.

Ori leans in and kisses you—not long, not desperate, but earnest enough to leave a taste of solder and salt. When he steps back the rain bites your skin like a challenge. He offers you the sea-glass bracelet in an open hand, and when your fingers close around it, they do so with a gentle violence.

Ori: (near-prayer) "Find me in the work."

You: "Find me in the staying."

The cove seems to hold its breath—an ocean paused between attrition and answer.

You walk him toward the launch where his battered skiff waits—patched with bottle-caps and optimism. Silas stands near the ramp, eyes wide, a small robot in his arms that chirps like a moth. Old Mara's shawl glows dimly under the tarpaulin. Neighbors edge close, giving those last small practical gifts: spare rope, a thermos, instructions scrawled on a bit of cardboard.

Ori kneels and ties off a line as if fastening a promise.

Ori: "Promise me one thing. When something works—when a council listens, when a prototype holds—send a message. Don't let it be anonymous. Tell them Saltway did it."

You: "I'll make sure they know."

Ori's hand brushes the inside of your wrist where the sea-glass bracelet sits. He hesitates, as if wanting to press the skin beneath into memory.

He rises. The skiff rocks, impatient to be gone. He looks back at you, and for a moment you see everything—his ache, his refusal to be caged, the way his humor was always a veneer to keep the truth from leaking out.

Ori: (voice raw) "I'll carry your ledger with me."

You: "Carry the people, not the ledger alone."

He nods, furious like someone who has been confronted with his own selfishness and who will nonetheless keep at it.

He steps into the skiff.

You want to collapse into him and beg him to stay. You want to thread your hands through his hair and anchor him with a thousand domestic promises—shared meals, steady nights, the mundane scaffolding of a life together.

You: (whisper) "Promise me you'll be careful."

Ori: "Promise me you'll keep shouting."

You both make the promises in quick succession, not trusting silence to hold either of you.

He cuts the line with a practiced flick. The engine coughs to life. The skiff pulls away, sliding over the rain-silvered water, leaving a thin wake that gleams like cracked glass.

You put out your hand towards him, not to stop him but to keep the distance honest. He turns, lifts his palm, and you mirror him. For one suspended second across bad weather and better intentions, your fingers are aligned; then the distance lengthens.

You stay on the boardwalk until the skiff is a small smudge, until the levee's built line intersects the horizon like an incision. Rain soaks you through to your bones. The sea-glass bracelet is in your hand; it is heavier than you expect. You press it to your sternum and the salt-water slides down into the fabric of your jacket.

Old Mara finds you and sets her hand on your shoulder—not to push, but to steady. Her touch is an old current.

Old Mara: "You did not fail. You kept a shore. Don't let the nights tell you otherwise."

You: "We kept a shore. Some left."

Old Mara: (short laugh) "A shore takes all kinds. Some come, some go. The sea doesn't pick favorites."

You look back toward the cove where lights blaze, volunteers keep moving, and children play at the water's edge with robocrafts Silas helped them build. There is work to do—repairs to tides, policy wrangles, the interminable choreography of keeping people where they belong. Your ledger is heavy with names.

You stand there for a long time as the rain eases to a fine mist and the sound of Saltway's small machines and human voices reasserts itself like a heartbeat. The ache inside you does not vanish. It deepens into something steadier—a hole you will carry like a lamp.

You: (internal) You chose the civic future. You chose the neighborhood over the certainty of a private life that might have been. It is not a victory that plays on billboards. It is a ledger entry and a pot of stew on a cold night and the sound of a child soldering her first sensor. You will live with what you saved and with what you let go. That is the shape of this work.

Old Mara lifts her voice—a small, rhythmic litany of names and small disasters survived. The community listens; stories tether memory to place. You take the bracelet and loop it back around your wrist. It fits the way a hand fits a glove: snug, a little rough.

Silas bounces up to you, triumphant and muddy, showing you the robot he'd soldered with kids—sturdy, quirky, its light blinking like a small beacon.

Silas: "We're giving these to the elders first. They like machines that don’t pretend to be perfect."

You: (soft smile) "Good. They deserve a little mischief."

Neighbors drift back to their tasks. Some will stay for years. Some will leave next week. The levees will rise; the city will continue to split its bets on adaptation. But pockets of resilience—your pockets—will keep beating.

You take a final look toward the distant lights of VossArc. The glass pier gleams like a false dawn. It didn't swallow Saltway, but it reshaped the horizon.

Ava—no, You—breathe, and the inhalation is an acceptance that life will be both work and ache in equal measure. Your choice keeps people here. It also creates absence. Both are truths you must carry.

You will write. You will speak at council rooms. You will argue for policy that recognizes community tenure. Old Mara: "Tomorrow, we start again."

You: "Tomorrow," you repeat, and the word is a small, fierce promise.
