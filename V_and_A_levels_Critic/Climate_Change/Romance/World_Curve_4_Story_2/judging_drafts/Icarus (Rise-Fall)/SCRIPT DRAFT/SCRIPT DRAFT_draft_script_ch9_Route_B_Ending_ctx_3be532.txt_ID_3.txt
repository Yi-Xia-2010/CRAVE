[Visual: A wide shot of the pier packed with bodies; corporate banners snap at the edges of bright floodlamps. Cranes loom like dark punctuation marks on the skyline.]

You step onto the pier with the crowd behind you and the city breathing close—metal, glass, and the thin mechanical scent of an industry that measures risk in margins and shareholder minutes. The air tastes of wet tar and something metallic, like a coin held too long at the surface of the sea. Cameras tilt toward you as if they could angle truth into a better frame.

People look to you. You can feel the pressure in that look: an expectation that your words will stitch a compass into the storm.

You lift the megaphone. Your hand shakes. Your voice through the cone is not the piano-note it is in smaller rooms; it is ragged and magnified, an exposed wire under the sky.

You relay names, clauses, a list of corners in contracts that read like teeth. You speak fast at first—numbers, dates, the technical stubbornness that has been your armor. Then the wind takes the words, then the first fat, cold beads of rain. The crowd's chant becomes a single, soggy line you can barely hear.

[Scene: Approach to the Perimeter | Cold Rain]
[Visual: Security lines—vested figures and a row of corporate-branded barriers—shadow the path toward the loading docks. Police units form a rigid frame beyond them.]
[Sound: Radios crackle; boots on plywood; the geopolitical hum of enforcement]

They meet you with practiced calm. Their faces are set like banks. An officer's voice booms over another speaker—orderly, clipped—telling the crowd to disperse. From the branded tent a legal man angles forward; his expression is neutral in a way that feels sharpened, like a blade dulled but still dangerous.

You answer through the megaphone. There is no argument for noise, only for dance: you speak compromise and demand, you offer terms and shine light on loopholes. Your voice cracks once; someone in the back shouts your name and the sound pulls at the seams of you.

Jun shoves a recorder into your palm. His eyes are gone-dark with anger; the red light under his jacket is a flare. "Keep talking. I have the leaks queued. Don’t—" his phrase breaks as he sees the first security shift, a motion into something more physical.

Ori is near the edge, sleeves rolled, desperately hauling at a crate of prototype planters. His topknot is soaked, grit streaking his forehead. He smiles at you the way wind throws alley-smoke—brief, unsustainable. "These'll show them what living walls can do," he yells, absurdly bright against the gray. "They—"

[Dialogue — You & Ori]
You: "Ori, get help—"
Ori: (cutting in, breathless) "Can't leave 'em out there. Not now. Not while—"
You: "Not while what? Our proof gets made into a reason to clear people out?"
Ori: (his voice outside the megaphone now, raw) "They're not tools, Ava. They're—" (he stops, fingers white on the crate)
You: (soft, desperate) "Then don't let them become that. Help me keep people here."

Ori's amber eyes flick to yours—full of apology and a sort of pleading that makes the rain taste like iron. He looks away, hauls. The crate slips; a planter tips. The crowd instinctively lunges to save it. A corporate videographer zooms in.

The storm arrives without the usual warning—no long breath before the plunge, only a sudden wall of wind and vertical rain that stings. Water slams the pier; waves spike and find new mouths. The lights flare; the megaphone blares with feedback. People shout and are drowned by the weather.

[Music: Percussion fractures into jagged, high-tempo beats; dissonant strings cut through the noise]
[Sound: The roar of a sudden gale; wood groans; a board snaps]

From the edges of the pier enforcement teams move like a tide in the opposite direction—swift, efficient, official. They carry clipboards and laminated notices. They tape them to doors, to shutters, to the frames of the community hall. The language is antiseptic: imminent hazard, mandated relocation, temporary protective measures. The words match the memos you had laid bare—same thresholds, same citations—only now they are the engine of removal rather than the text on a page.

You watch Old Mara on the boardwalk, her shawl a sodden flag around her stooped shoulders. Her hands—those hands that braided storms into stories—clutch at her beads. She looks at you and her face is hard to read: a reef of memory and hurt. A relocation notice flaps at her door like a terrible, absurd pennant.

[Dialogue — You & Old Mara]
Old Mara: (her voice thin but sharp) "They always had a thousand ways to call us temporary."
You: "Mara—"
Old Mara: (cutting, almost laughing) "Did you think the cameras would keep them honest, child? They take what good press needs and leave the rest to bad weather."

You want to answer. What words fix a tape and a tide? There are no words that will stop a notice or steady a child's shaking hands.

[Scene: The Collapse | VeryHigh Intensity]
[Visual: A montage of small tragedies—boxes trussed on porches, a stroller abandoned at the edge of a ramp, a banner ripped free and streaming like a wounded thing]
[Sound: Wails, instructions in calm voices, the sharp bark of bullhorns; the rain a constant percussion hammering the scene]

Images begin to arrive already processed: the network feeds cut to the parts of the night that read cleanly as spectacle. There are slo-mo shots of people pressing against cordons; a dramatic frame of plant crates tilting into the sea; a close-up on your wet face that isolates the strain and makes it a badge of disorder. Investors call it 'risk materializing.' Comment sections coagulate with tidy verdicts.

Jun presses the recorder in your hand until it stings. "They're going to edit this," he says. "They'll make us the story about how protests fall apart." His voice is not newscaster-cool; it is a raw edge of someone realizing his story will be read into a script not entirely of his making.

You try to summon the map of contingency plans—the dozen tiny mitigations you kept in your head. They fracture in the rain. The thresholds you flagged become thresholds that justify the very displacements you wanted to prevent.

A cluster of neighbors, faces thin and wet under cardboard boxes, stand watching a short-term housing van idling with its door open. The driver checks off names. Promises are delivered in the tones that make things go away: firm, bureaucratic, soft-selling permanence. An earnest young social worker offers next-step forms and warm, shouted reassurances. No one can tell you if the promise is a cushion or a coffin.

You call for volunteers to form chains to pass belongings. Hands—slick, raw—meet and pass. For a moment, community is a practiced muscle again: quick knots, efficient boats of salvage. Then the tide finds a new rhythm and some of the houses are lifted in the public eye as precincts of failure rather than victims of displacement. The frame narrows and public sympathy slides into a more useful narrative for those with power: disorder justifies control.

[Dialogue — You, Jun, Ori]
You: (barking) "Film the notices. Jun—get the wording! Document the numbers. If this becomes a lawsuit, we need ink."
Jun: (eyes wide, recorder capturing every bead of rain) "I—I'm getting it. But Ava, the edits—"
You: "We'll fight the edits. We'll name names. We won't let this be rewritten."
Ori: (cutting in, hands splayed, exhausted) "They're taking Mrs. Liao. They won't take her without—" (his voice breaks)
You: "Then we don't let them."
Ori: "We can't—" (he stops, swallowing the word "stop" like it might become a command to abandon the neighborhood)

Ori's hands are empty now; one of his prototype planters has cracked on the wet pier, soil leaking like a torn vein. He gathers it anyway, fingers raw, and looks at you as if to find a plan you have not yet offered. The look is more fragile than protest banners: a private plea without an obvious answer.

[Visual: A legal officer tapes a relocation notice to the door of a house where a family has clustered, boxes around their feet; a child's stuffed fish peeks from under a plastic tarp]
[Sound: The hush of a moment that is not respite but a pivot]

You feel, suddenly, the anatomy of failure: the point where strategy turns into spectacle and the spectacle becomes justification. The very honesty of your exposure—the documents you laid bare, the memos you held up as proof—are now the ordinance by which people are moved. The word "imminent" becomes a lever. Your own evidence becomes a tool used by others to enforce the very displacement you tried to prevent.

You want to scream until your throat empties. Instead you feel the old, familiar tightening in your chest—guilt like a bruise. People are moving not because of your failure alone but because of storms, because of infrastructure that favors capital flows over human tenure, because of a system that always had a plan for the city that didn't include all of its people. You are one small part of this machine, and your part has been turned into a hinge.

[Scene: Aftermath | Night — The Pier, Estranged and Quiet]
[Visual: The floodlamps throw long, watery cones. Plastic tarps flap against emptied stoops. A cluster of moved residents boards a van under corporate banners that read 'Protective Measures'.]
[Music: Sparse piano; a low, almost subterranean drone]
[Sound: The receding swell of the storm; far-off sirens; a single gull calling as if stunned]

Old Mara stands with her packed shawl. Her beads are dull with salt; she looks like a map of losses. She does not ask for consolation. She does not offer counsel. She simply folds her hands and begins to walk toward the van.

You move to follow. Ori is there—wet, hands in his pockets. He catches your sleeve and draws you back.

Ori: (voice small) "They took a lot, Ava."
You: (the words come like stones) "We warned them. We named the thresholds. We tried to stop this."
Ori: "But you—" (he swallows) "You didn't know they'd use your own proof to clear people."
You: "No. I didn't." (the sentence is not a defense; it is a thing breaking)

Jun stands a few paces off, his recorder dangling. He looks at you with something like accusation and grief braided together. "I have hours of this," he says. "People will see it. They will—" He trails off, because even the greatest exposé bears the imprint of the frame that finds it.

You walk the damp boards with the feeling of someone wearing shoes that are too big: you make a kind of forward motion out of a habit of momentum rather than conviction. The pier behind you is a smear of lights and interpretable images; the Saltway beyond it—where you were raised, where your plans lived—feels both nearer and cruelly more distant.

[Visual: Close-up: Your field notebook, soaked, its pages curling. You open it to a blank page and your pen leaves a single dark mark.]
[Sound: The pen scratches; the sound is absurdly intimate in the wide, ruined night]

You can catalogue the losses as facts: three households moved that night, six more given notices with temporary housing offers, score of possessions salvaged or lost. You can name the legal citations they used. You can piece together the timeline—because you are obsessive in the ways of people who try to protect neighborhoods. But cataloguing does not soothe the small betrayals: the neighbor who refused to stand in the rain, the line of cameras that rewrote the moral grammar of the event, the way your own proof became a key.

The cameras will keep rolling tomorrow. There will be op-eds arguing about tactics and tone. There will be people who call the night necessary theater for accountability and others who call it incompetence. Meanwhile, Mara's shawl will dry, and someone will count the exact number of boxes they left behind.

You think of the possible endings you used to imagine: victory celebrated in the Tide Garden, a hybrid pilot that allowed Saltway to stay. You think of the softer possibilities—the ones that kept you, Ori, and the neighborhood in one another's lives. They feel—right now—like fictions you told to keep fear from flattening you.

You stand on the sodden pier with Ori at your side and feel the very smallness of the human heart pressed against the vast apparatus of a city built on inequity. He reaches for your hand. You let him take it, because there is a gravity in human contact that the corporate frame cannot edit out.

Ori: (low) "We'll try again. Not tonight. But—"
You: (the reply is a small thing, honest and pained) "I don't know if I can do another 'try' that looks like this."

He does not meet your eyes. The admission is like a fissure widening in a dam.

[Visual: Wide shot: The pier recedes into fog. A single lamp flickers and goes out.]
[Sound: The tide speaks in long, indifferent breaths]

You gather your notebook and the broken pieces of whatever plans remain. You move back toward the Saltway, toward the houses left standing and those already gone. The neighborhood will count itself and begin the slow, procedural work of grief and salvage: meetings to re-form tactics, legal calls, lists of what was lost and what must be rebuilt.

It will not be tidy. It will not be complete. It will be the sort of messy, stubborn repair that old Mara once taught you—knots tied to hold what can be held, not illusions of total safety.

You have lost ground tonight. Some of the people you wanted to protect are in temporary vans with warm words and thin promises. The newspapers will show frames that make it look like your movement broke instead of bending. The cameras will give a new lexicon to the event—'disorder'—and that word will be used as if it carried the authority of weather.

You think of the tide and how it keeps coming; of how it carves and returns and takes different things each time. You think of the way heat rises and the city reorganizes itself around those who can pay for certainty. You think of the small combustions of human generosity you saw tonight—the chain of hands passing a box, the neighbor holding a child's soaked shoe—and you hold those gestures like a tiny illicit flame.

[Music: A single, sustained chord that does not resolve; distant thunder like a low benediction]
[Visual: Fade to a close-up on your hand—sea-glass bracelet damp and glossy; your fingers tighten around it]

You do not have the neat victory you once imagined. You have scars, a record, friends who are both shelter and accusation. The camera lenses have not erased the truth of what you saw and what you tried. They have only made it messy, available for interpretation.

You inhale the cold, wet air and let it fill the hollow the night made. There will be hearings, bargains, legal filings, and maybe—eventually—policy changes. There will be people who left and people who stayed. There will be a reckoning, perhaps, with VossCorp's tactics and Calder's machinery. But tonight the tide is the verdict, and it has spoken.

You turn toward the Saltway and the narrow path of work that remains—uncertain, heavy, necessary. You think of the community meetings that will form tomorrow, the urgent, angrier planning sessions, the quiet, tender moments of repair. You think of Ori's hand in yours and the way his presence both steadies and complicates what you must become: not only a leader but someone who must live with the cost of decisions.

You cannot call this victory. It is a collapse you will carry, a very negative ledger of loss, and the intensity of it is still thrumming in your blood. The storm has rearranged the map of your campaign and the map of your heart.

[Scene: Saltway Quarter | Night — Rain-slicked Boardwalks]
[Visual: Saltway's porch lights are small beacons in the dark. A few figures move under tarps. Someone has strung a lantern that throws a warm hand of light.]
[Sound: The low murmur of neighbors talking in small groups; the ferry horn in the distance]

You walk into the neighborhood and Old Mara falls into step beside you. She is silent for a long time, then:

Old Mara: (finally) "You fought, child. That is not nothing. But there is more to learn—about how storms are used to move people, and how truth must be matched by care."

You: (voice thin) "How do we keep truth from becoming the instrument by which we are spread thin?"
Old Mara: (a small, wry smile) "We make shelter first. We stop pretending that exposure alone will hold the walls."

You let the sentence land. It is a different kind of strategy: one that must be built on repair as much as revelation. It is a stratagem that will require patience you do not know you have.

You look at Ori. He looks at you. You know, with a cold clarity, that there will be consequences between you—blame, exhaustion, the slow erosion of trust—and maybe salvage, too. But there is no tidy ending in this night.

[Visual: Close-up on your soaked notebook being closed, the tide-map's margins smudged into an abstract of rain]
[Music: Quiet, minor-key piano; a single violin note that does not resolve]

You close the notebook. The cover settles with a small, wet sigh. The cranes on the horizon are dark and patient. The city will go on making plans, and you will go on making yours—different now for the truth of what unfolded.

You carry with you the weight of displaced boxes, the memory of Old Mara's shawl, Ori's broken planter, Jun's recorder still pulsing into the storm. You carry the knowledge that exposure can both protect and hurt, that the very evidence that sets light to corruption can be turned into the instrument of removal.

The last thing you do before you step into the low light of the boardwalk is something small: you fold your fingers around the sea-glass bracelet at your wrist and feel the salt in its grooves. There is no answer in that touch, only a tiny, stubborn insistence that some things are worth holding.
