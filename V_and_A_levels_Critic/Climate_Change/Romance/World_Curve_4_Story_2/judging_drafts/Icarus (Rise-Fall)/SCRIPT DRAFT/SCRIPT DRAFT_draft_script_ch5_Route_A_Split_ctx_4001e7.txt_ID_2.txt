[Scene: Ferryworks Greenhouse | Late Afternoon — Low, Sweeping Sun]

[Visual: Warm lamplight spills from portholes; rows of seedlings stacked on salvaged shelving. Solar sails flap gently above the deck, casting slow, moving shadows. Ori's welding sparks blink like small constellations.]

[Sound: Solder hiss mingles with the slap of water against the hull and the distant metallic cough of a crane. A chorus of voices—neighbors, youth—rise and fall in productive rhythm.]

You step into the Ferryworks and the air catches you—rich with wet soil, solder, and the faint sweetness of algae. It smells like work that wants to be loved into being. Your notebook is open at your hip; the draft you carried from the community hall still lives in the margin of your handwriting. For a heartbeat you let the warmth of the room settle you: hands moving in synchronized purpose, a plan that is no longer just ink but muscle.

Silas passes with a coil of algae-filtering tubing over his shoulder, leaving a salt-spray pattern on his hoodie. Old Mara sits threaded into a corner stool, a thin shawl across her knees, humming a tide-vigil that feels older than the hull itself. Maya flashes a grin at you from the staging table, ledger in hand, and then returns to marshaling volunteers.

Ori is at the far bench, welding reclaimed metal into winged planters. His curls are pulled back, paint smudges like a map along his cuffs. He looks up when you approach—amber eyes bright, a smear of solder on his cheekbone—and for a moment everything else rearranges itself to the margin. You have to fold that feeling small, practical, where it belongs: in the pocket of choices you make for the neighborhood.

Ori Navarro: "You cut the list in half and hand me the messy jobs, as usual." (He grins, sparks scattering.) "Silas, run me a t-junction. Maya, we need two more blocks at the Tide Garden."

You: "I'm not giving you the heavy lifting because I like you working—I'm giving you the heavy lifting because you make it sing." You tap the plan pinned to the bench. "Winged planters up at the seawall's outer lip—ornamental, yes, but crucial for root anchoring. Sensors go in behind the bio-concrete. Calder's team will film the install tomorrow morning."

[Sound: A shutter click from a distance as Calder's camera finds a frame.]

Maya Lin: "And the schedule—volunteers on two squads. One at first light; one after the tide slack. Jun will have the recorder, and I'll keep the legal packets on hand." (Her tone is brisk; she leaves no room for dithering.)

You feel the small, fierce bloom of it again: successful coordination. The pilot moves from paper to muscle. Your chest lifts in a way that almost feels like laughter.

[Music: Quick, bright strings underline the momentum—hope in motion.]

You set volunteers into motion the way you have learned to set tides: nudge here, steady there. You allocate a squad to hauling bio-concrete, another to sensor calibration. You sketch a revised drill on the back of your notebook as Silas threads an algae filter into place with fingers that never tremble. Old Mara hums, then narrows her eyes at you as if probing the weather of your soul; the question in her look is silent and exact.

Jun Park appears with a slim stack of papers and a recorder humming softly against his chest. He hands you a printout with a look that cools the room for a second—professional, careful, the color of someone carrying both spotlight and shadow.

Jun Park: "Everything's running clean on the feed. Sensors are reporting salinity stabilization in the test bays. The model's performing—real data, not just optics." (He meets your eyes.) "People are responding. This is the narrative the neighborhood deserves."

You: "We make it undeniable. We make it human." You watch as the garden modules flex against the tide, mangrove roots finding purchase in the bio-concrete like slow, stubborn hands.

[Visual: Close-up on the sensors' readout—lines flatten, the graph held steady. A child laughs somewhere beyond the deck; a volunteer calls out triumph.]

The prototype answers you. Roots take. Salinity finds a new steadiness. For a pulse-length you allow yourself to imagine the Tide Garden not as a pilot but as a pattern—replicable, stubborn, beautiful.

Then Jun slides another sheet across the bench.

[Sound: Paper whisper; the room's hum narrows.]

Jun Park: "Redacted addendum from VossCorp." His voice is level but there is a tightness at the corner of it. "It's on top—I've redacted what I can, but the relocation clause is still there. Undefined safety thresholds. Trigger language isn't technical—it's discretionary."

You take the page. The legal jargon rearranges the air. At first it's just print; you read the same sentence three times, then five. Words like 'safety thresholds' and 'relocation authority' sit in the whitespace like teeth.

Your breath shortens in small, abrupt sections. The room pulses around you—Ori's welding sparks, Silas's hand moving steady, Old Mara's hum—but a knot forms in your ribs. Hope hardens into a knot that you can feel all the way down to the soles of your boots.

Maya Lin: "Show me." She reaches, ledger closing with a soft thud. Her voice isn't just concern—it's calculation. "We can demand a redline. 'Tenure guaranteed'—explicit occupancy protections. Or we don't sign."

Ori's face shifts. He wipes his hands on his overalls instinctively, paint smears spreading like the map of some reckless sea. "We can't let this be a bait-and-switch," he says. "But also—if we bog this down in legal quibbles, Calder walks. The cameras go elsewhere. The pilot dies. Do you want our living wall to be a rumor?"

You feel both arguments like two tides tugging at you, each persuasive. The power of VossCorp moving fast could mean scaffolding, materials, media attention—the scale to make the pilot impossible to ignore. The other path—a fortress of ironclad community control—could mean slow legal months, bureaucratic delays, possible retreat by investors.

Your hands curl around your notebook until the paper dents your palm. You think of the families along Saltway, of Mara's stooped silhouette on storm mornings, of Silas's quick, honest hands. You think of Ori's laugh and how it steadies you in storms you never asked for. The pilot isn't just a project—it is tenure, memory, and the pressure of future tides.

The micro-choices land like stones in water; each sends ripples. Whatever you chose, the room takes it and rebalances, hands finding new tasks. But Jun's redacted clause still sits in your palm like a foreign tide-line, not yet washed clean.

Ori approaches you, one hand wiping his cheekbone where solder smudged. His voice is softer now, the kind that opens chambers in a closed room.

Ori Navarro: "If we go slow, the beauty of what we make might never be seen. If we go fast—" (he falters, searching) "—we risk handing control over in a package wrapped in polished glass. Ava—" (he stops, the familiarity of your name shaped differently by the need in his tone) "—what do you need from me? From us?"

You feel the gravity in his question. It is not just about the pilot. It is about trust measured in deadlines and clauses. You could ask him for restraint; you could ask for rebellion; you could ask for a middle. None of those would be a small thing.

You: "I need you to promise to build with the neighborhood on the same page. No surprises, Ori." Your voice is steady, but your fingers betray you—tap a pattern on the bench.

Ori Navarro: "No surprises." He says it with the lightness of a dare and the gravity of a vow. "But I also need you to let me try something that feels like hope, not only like defense. An annex—not to be hidden, but to be honest and impossible. Something that makes people want to stay."

Maya steps in, ledger pressed to her chest like armor.

Maya Lin: "Promise and poetry are fine. Paper and precedent keep people housed. We don't have to choose art over legal. We do have to choose words that lock tenure down."

You feel the pull: the creative, face-forward insistence of Ori; the protective, exacting caution of Maya. Both of them love Saltway, but their languages for love are different—Ori's is object and beauty; Maya's is ink and clause.

[Sound: The tide licks the hull; a volunteer laughs as a planter is lifted into place. The cameras from VossCorp pan, catching hands and faces—images that will be pressed into the city's memory.]

Calder's presence is visible on the periphery—a silent, corporate silhouette—his camera lens an unblinking eye. He steps into the glow briefly and offers a practiced, benign expression. He meets your gaze and for the first time since negotiations began, you see an openness there—a thin, careful hope that the pilot will anchor both policy and profit.

Calder Voss: "This looks…alive." His tone is precise. "We can amplify this. Timing will be key."

Jun: "Amplify, yes. But there's a clause here that needs clarity."

You inhale and the decision you have been holding against your ribs crystallizes into a point of heat. The room seems to inhale with you. The momentum you built—community hands, prototype success, corporate cameras—teeters at a hinge: sign and scale, insist and delay, or step outside the frame and keep building in private.

Your heartbeat accelerates, a drum roll that builds into something close to a roar in your ears. The positive charge of success—sensors stabilizing, roots gripping, cameras rolling—warps into a pressure that demands choice. This is the most alive you've felt in months: very good, very urgent, very loud; hope riding at full tilt against the teeth of risk.

You imagine the pilot in the press tomorrow—children fishing from stable terraces, the garden breathing against the tide; you imagine families staying because you held tenure in ink and in hand. You imagine Ori's annex, a bright defiant bloom. You imagine the clause twisted into a lever that could unmoor everything.

The decision feels seismic. Your breath comes fast. The room holds its breath with you.
