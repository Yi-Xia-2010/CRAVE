Calder (quiet, controlled): "Ms. Maren. Thank you for coming."

You feel the words in your throat like stones. Your testimony is not just testimony; it is the ledger of a neighborhood that cannot be replaced by a glossy render. You recite facts. You do not cry. The room pinches in on each syllable.

You: "On March 4th, the memo drafted by VossCorp's planning committee recommended reclassification of 'community tenure' as 'relocation efficiency' in order to reduce projected operational liabilities. That language was present in the investor briefing."

Counsel (VossCorp's attorney): "Objection, speculative—"

Jun's recorder is a small, humming thing in your blazer. You let it hum. You are meticulous: dates, emails, the exact wording that turns safety into profit. You lay out the practical consequences — which families received notices, which pilot maintenance contracts exclude resident governance.

Chair: "Ms. Maren, please keep your answers to the questions posed."

You: "My answers are the questions. Who decides that a person's home is a line item?"

The room reacts. Cameras lean. Calder's assistant passes him a slim tablet; he thumbs through investor memos as if reading weather reports.

Calder (leaning forward, smooth): "Ms. Maren, VossArc is intended to protect Harbortown at scale. We consulted experts. We projected failure modes and designed redundancy. Any corporate action taken was aimed at ensuring survivability."

You: "Survivability for whom? Survivability measured in displacement? Measured in reduced liabilities? You pitched tenure as a risk in your models. You called community governance an 'inefficiency' at investors' meetings."

Calder: "Language, Ms. Maren, can be—"

You: "—used to justify anything."

Silence cracks through the room like breaking ice. The chair fumbles with the agenda, the counsel prepares a predictable string of denials and half-admissions. Jun's presence is a steady weight at your shoulder; his eyes flick to yours, then to the cameras, then to the transcript he knows will be the hinge of policy.

You return to Saltway in the wash of media, deputies, and municipal promises. Temporary protections are announced: an emergency moratorium on demolitions and an interim maintenance fund. The words are relief's cousin — not salvation.

Maya: "Some families have already accepted relocation offers. There were legal pressures; buyouts slipped with settlements. They took the money."

You: "We should have—"

The city negotiates in spreadsheets and cheques; people's grief arrives like a weather system that policy cannot always intercept.

Jun: "This is messy. It's going to be a long legal slog. The board's under subpoena, but investors are already calling for triage. Municipal partners want an 'efficient' maintenance model. I'm worried about privatized upkeep in the fine print."

Your phone buzzes with a single message and you know before you open it who it's from.

[Scene: Ferryworks Deck | Midnight]
[Visual: The night is a dark water-sheen; Ori's silhouette bent over a crate of parts, welding goggles hanging like an amulet]
[Sound: The hiss of a torch, the ocean rubbing against the hull]
[Music: Low, insistent piano — notes like held breaths]

You step outside and Ori looks up. He smells of oil and metal and the faint tang of algae. There's a kind of exhaustion in him that is different from yours — less rigid, more restless.

Ori: "You did the thing, Ava."

You: "We did the thing. Jun did the reporting."

Ori (half-laugh): "Semantics. You still climbed the ladder."

You: "You lit the art-stunt and changed the feeds, too."

Ori: "It helped. People saw faces."

Silence settles between you, dense and complicated. He runs a hand through his sun-bleached curls, then tugs his coat tighter.

Ori: "I've been thinking... about scale. About seeding — not just here, but other quarters. There are places that would die without these prototypes."

You: "And—?"

Ori: "I can build faster if I'm not anchored to one civic office. I thought about staying, but I'm... I'm tired of being the person who makes things and then watches them get folded into someone else's ledger."

You can sense the direction his words are pointing, but you need him to say it plainly.

You: "Do you mean leaving Saltway?"

Ori: (a long breath) "I mean leaving Harborlight for periods. I'll come back. I'll—help steer. But I can't be the guy who patches a hundred roofs and then pretends that's a solution. I want to seed projects that will give people tools before the next tide hits."

His eyes search yours for less than a second, plea and apology braided together. You understand the impulse. You also understand the cost.

You: "We are being asked to run the policy office now. They'll want someone who can stand in the gray and negotiate—"

Ori: "Which is the opposite of what you do, and the world needs both."

You: "What I need is not simple. I need someone who will be here. Not sometimes."

Ori: "I know. I'm not asking you to choose now."

[Scene: Mayor's Interim Office | Midday — Weeks Later]
[Visual: A smaller room than you expected, maps pinned, legal pads stacked; a single window shows the cranes dormant]
[Sound: Paper shuffling, polite handshakes, a coffee machine sputtering]
[Music: Low brass, a slowening drumbeat — tension tempered by bureaucratic rhythms]

You are asked, in public and in private, to take a civic leadership role: to chair a new working group on community-managed adaptation, to sit at tables where funding and enforcement meet. The ask is framed as honor and burden. It is good work — vital work — but it is also the slow consumption of a private life.

Mayor's Aide: "We need someone with credibility in the community who knows the technical work. Your testimony moved the committee. If you accept, you'll have staff, a budget line, and a seat at the negotiation table."

You look at the ledger of losses and the list of people who accepted buyouts, the boarded windows, the children who fish from terraces that used to be streets. You think of the nights of organizing and the mornings when you patched leaks with other people's hands.

You: "What are the limits? Who holds oversight over the maintenance funds? Is tenure codified or deferred?"

Aide: "There will be oversight. It's a hybrid model—public funding with private partners for efficiency. We can write in resident representation."

You hear the word "efficiency" like a bell you have learned to distrust. It will be a negotiation where every clause can fracture someone else's home. But without someone at the table, you understand, the clauses will be written without the neighborhood's voice at all.

You: "I will do it. But I want binding tenure protections codified within the pilot. And a clause prohibiting privatized maintenance that strips residents of decision-making."

Aide (after a long pause): "We'll try."

You know 'try' is not a promise. You sign the preliminary acceptance because you cannot not be there. Because someone must answer the phone at three a.m. when a contractor tries to slip a clause past the clerk. Because a life built by a community is fragile and needs guardians.

[Visual: The boardwalk stretches into a bronze sunset. Some banners are new; some houses are gone. Children play on patched pontoons. A notice board lists community meetings and legal resources.]
[Sound: The softened clatter of wind against nylon, a radio playing a distant newscaster about policy reform]

You walk the quarter, fingers trailing the salt-stained banister. People nod; some look relieved, some hollow. You pass a shuttered shop with a note in the window: "Accepted buyout. Returning April." Your chest tightens.

Old Mara sits on a step, shawl wrapped, eyes like chipped glass full of history.

Old Mara: "You did what you could. The world will still take some. It always does."

You: "We stopped worse."

Old Mara: "You stopped a particular kind of worse. That is something, child."

You sit beside her and feel the fatigue that has lived inside your bones for months. It is a steady ache, a tide that never quite recedes. Ori appears at the far end of the walk, a pack slung over his shoulder. He pauses when he sees you, as if measuring whether to cross the space.

He walks over. Up close, you can see the decisions etched into him: the small gray lines at his eyes, the callus on his thumb from welding.

Ori: "I leave tomorrow morning. A caravan in the southern wetlands asked for help. They have nothing like the Tide Garden."

You: "Tomorrow."

Ori: "I don't want to pretend otherwise. I want you to know."

You fold your sea-glass bracelet between your fingers until the beads are smooth from being worried. You think of promises, of the public life you have signed onto, of the nights you will be at hearings and he will be sewing new prototypes into someone else's skyline.

You: "You could stay."

Ori: "I could. But I promised I'd seed, not anchor. This is my way of refusing to let these things become only monuments."

You: "Is this goodbye?"

Ori (a small laugh that holds both grief and a stubborn hope): "It's something like an agreement: we keep working. Different latitudes. We keep each other honest in memos. You do the policy. I do the prototypes. We meet at the intersections."

You: "Is that—enough?"

Ori: "Maybe not. Maybe it's what we've learned to manage."

You: "We are both making compromises."

Ori: "Yes. We're both making them."

He takes your hand — briefly, not the clasp of lovers bleeding into the night, but a steadying human contact that is both benediction and boundary. He steps back and lifts his pack, the movement final and practical. He turns and walks toward the ferry that will take him south.

You watch him go until he is a small figure against the water. The night swallows his silhouette, then the ferry's light blinks, and it is gone.

You are left with the boardwalk, the map pinned to your chest where years of meetings and unlikely alliances have been drawn in felt-tip and bruised hands. The city has yielded with teeth. VossCorp is under subpoena and VossArc's flagship pause is a victory with barbs. Temporary protections exist, but they are stitched with municipal compromises that may fray. Some families moved; some stayed. Accountability arrived as paperwork and political theater, not the clean justice you wished for.

You stand there, the sea-glass bracelet warm in your palm, the taste of salt in your mouth. The role you accepted will be a grind you never wanted in isolation: grant deadlines, oversight committees, press statements, the slow bleed of urgency into ordinary administration. You will be visible. You will be attacked. You will have to negotiate words like "efficiency" and "scalability" with people for whom those words mean livelihoods and with people for whom those words are shields.

You feel the weight of that work settle into your shoulders. You are tired. You are bruised and necessary. The city has reformed in law; people's lives have been rearranged.

You look out over the harbor — its surface is a restless black, cut through by the light of distant cranes humming in small, practical shifts. The tide is neither friend nor enemy. It is a force you must reckon with, the background rhythm to every policy and every heart.

You put the sea-glass bracelet back on your wrist, the beads clicking once, a small sound that anchors you to the quarter that raised and scarred you. You do not know if this is enough. You know only what you will do next: show up, argue clauses into being, defend tenure with the bluntness of someone who has seen what its absence does to a life.

The night is not triumphant. It is not a lie of ease. It is a quiet, heavy step forward — necessary, imperfect, and human.
