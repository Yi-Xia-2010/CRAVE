The notebook is already open in your lap when Jun slides the folder across the table. Your name—Ava—on the top sheet looks small under the lamplight, like a lifeline written in pencil.

You read fast. The memos smell faintly of printer smoke and cold coffee; legal headers march across page after page. At first it is a syntax of corporate caution, then a scar of intent: thresholds, "operational triggers," relocation contingencies tied to arbitrarily set sea levels and "scalability metrics." The language is precise and cruelly engineered to look like prudence.

Your pen moves before your brain finishes translating. Somebody scribbles a foul word in the margin and then another, angry shorthand that becomes a thread of strategy notes. Every line you underline dents your chest.

Jun: "They're not hiding it in code—it's in plain sight. 'Trigger thresholds' mapped to investor review dates. The clauses make relocation a default outcome instead of an exception."

Jun's voice is steady but bright—there's a hunger there, the kind that comes from having found a vein. He lays out the key memo on the table, sliding it so the lamp makes the watermark glow.

You: "So it's deliberate. They built a lever into the plan that pulls people out when the numbers look clean."

Jun: "Exactly. It's governance masked as engineering. If we roll this straight to the public, we force the narrative off VossCorp's timeline."

You feel the idea like a charge in your bones. Hope flares—sharp and immediate. Not naive, not frivolous. It is the kind of hope that tastes of salt and strategy.

Ori: (from the doorway, palms full of circuitous glass and solder) "We can make them see it. Not numbers, Ava—faces and voices and things that catch the light."

Ori's amber eyes catch yours; his grin is a bright, dangerous thing. He sets his satchel down and opens it like a promise. The reclaimed circuits glint, seaglass beads catching the lamplight with a stubborn, stubborn beauty.

Maya arrives moments later, breathless as if she has been running through rain and ledger entries simultaneously. She drops a stack of leaflets and a list of names on the bench, the handwriting a scattering of anchors.

Maya: "Press is sticky for the morning, but we can stage a showing tonight on the pier. Jun, can you timestamp your piece so we drop it when everyone sees the banners?"

Jun: "Yeah. Coordinated release, full dossier. I can timestamp and embargo it to go public with the march. If we time it with your people waving the evidence—nobody can ignore it."

You fold your hands over the memo, feeling the edges. The greenhouse smells of wet soil and solder—life and invention and the small machinery of resistance.

You flip to the back of your notebook and sketch a timeline: leak, march, legal release. Your handwriting runs rough with adrenaline. The lamp makes the ink run like tidewater. The arousal inside you—tense, urgent, exultant—coalesces into a decision curve you'd been preparing for without admitting it.

Jun taps his recorder, placing it on a tin tray like a small altar.

Jun: "If we go public tonight, I can run an anchor-ready piece. I'll include the memos, but I need more: testimony from residents who will speak to the clause's real impact, and footage of the clauses being read aloud. Make it visceral. Make it undeniable."

Maya: "I can get Old Mara and three other tenants to stand up. They'll talk tenure, not theory. They will make it personal."

You remember Mara's hands—the hooks of her necklace, the stories she told of living with water rather than against it. Her voice could unspin a whole corporate narrative if it came through on the feed.

Calder's people are already moving. The live-stream debate is a lure and an attempt to manage optics: control the frame, the questions, the tempo.

Jun: "They're offering a stage. Calder wants the narrative in a channel he controls—nice lighting, a moderator who softens hard questions."

You: "Do we take the stage and inflict our truth into their frame? Or do we fracture that frame—force our own light?"

Ori squeezes your hand again. His thumb circles, patient and small.

Ori: "If you go on there, let me make the visual that makes them look at faces, not numbers. Seaglass light to break the LEDs. Make the feed catch a texture. We'll make the screens ache with the neighborhood."

You: "And if we don't go? They set the rules for what counts as 'civilized dissent.'"

Maya: "And if we do? They mute voices, claim disorder, press the enforcement buttons."

The choices ricochet. Your breath is a meter tuning to a faster rhythm. Every option is dangerous in its own way. Every risk carries a possible victory that tastes of salt and sunlight.

You look at Jun. He is wired for this—the journalist's clarity, the reporter's hunger. You look at Maya, a human ledger—steady, exact. You look at Ori, who builds beauty as protest—a maker who belives in the persuasive power of things that reflect light back into people's eyes. Each of them is a channel for a different kind of truth.

You think of Saltway's boardwalks, the Tide Garden's living terraces, the children's laughter that pooled like gull calls after storms. You smell wet soil and solder and the faint cloud of cable smoke, and the hope that has been a long, difficult apprenticeship in you.

Ori: "Ava. I trust you. Whatever you decide—I'll make it impossible for them to look away."

You close your hand around his. The contact is simple, human. It feels like a promise amplified by the greenhouse's lamps.

Your chest tightens. The arousal that's been building—fear braided with hope—swells until your decisions become not only tactical but moral: the kind that name what you are willing to risk for the people you love and the place you grew up in.

You stand up, feeling the full length of your spine align with the force of the night. Outside the greenhouse, the Glass Pier will be lit; the city's feeds will hungry for anything that breaks the corporate script. Calder's debate offer hangs like a gauntlet.

You flip the page of your notebook. At the top, in bold letters, you write: UNMASK.
