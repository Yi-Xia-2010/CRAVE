[Scene: Saltway Boardwalks | Dawn]
[Visual: Soft watercolor light filters through morning mist; algae-green and sun-peeled turquoise palette]
[Music: Low, breathy ambient—distant gull calls folded into a slow, steady synth]
[Sound: Water lapping, boards creaking underfoot, a kettle somewhere on a neighbor's stove]

You step off the ferry and the boardwalk hugs your boots like a familiar spine. Damp wood gives a half-soft thunk when your rubber soles—treaded with recycled tire—meet it. Salt and wood smoke curl up into your throat; the smell is a map you can read with your eyes closed. Rain has left salt streaks in your hair, thin white lines that glitter when a stray sunbeam catches them. Your fingers keep fussing with the lapel pin: a small solar cell, warm from the night tide-watch, still feeding the handheld sensor tucked inside your jacket.

You carry the battered field notebook against your ribs because it makes you feel steadier than any argument, petition, or meeting. Tide charts stab across pages, margins filled with shorthand and coffee stains. You flip it open—no plan is safe from smudged ink here—and the first numbers you trace are the tide heights from your watch: a quiet ledger that feels like confession and promise.

[Visual: Close-up of notebook—inked tide lines, a pressed seaweed fragment, a child's doodle of a kite in the margin]
[Narration]
You inventory Saltway in a slow, trained sweep: repaired pontoons bobbed in neat rows, their seams sealed with strips of salvaged rubber; a child's solar kite—bright orange nylon stitched with reflective tape—hangs between two poles, a small flag of stubborn joy; Old Mara's layered shawl, heavy with damp and salt, hangs from a peg beside her doorway and is reflected, distorted, in a rain puddle like a ghost trying on her face.

Maya sees you first. Her streaked-gray braids are a shape you could identify in a blackout: her hands curl round a thermos, steam fogging the air between you. She slows the rhythm of her steps when she reaches you, measuring you with the same mix of affection and practical worry she offers every problem you hand her.

Maya Lin: "You were out on the tide again, weren't you? Didn't you get any sleep at all?"

You: "Not much. The salinity spiked an hour after midnight—had to recalibrate the root probes on the living wall."

Maya's mouth tightens but her voice is steady. She's a metronome for this neighborhood. Her eyes flick to the horizon where cranes perforate the sky; VossArc sits there like a promise you can't tell if it means safety or erasure.

Maya Lin: "We posted the meeting flyer up by the hall last night. Old Mara wants to talk about the planting rotation. Silas is fixing the planter anchors near the slip—he's been up since dawn."

You: "Good. Ask Silas to check the tether lengths again. If the swell hits from the south and the drift increases, those floats will sag."

Maya nods and reaches out—brass-lined fingers, quick—to take the notebook. You let her flip through while you stand two steps away, feeling both exposed and sheltered. Her thumb finds the tide graph and she hums, more to herself than to you.

You move on because there are things that have to be seen with your own eyes. Silas is crouched by a cluster of floating planters, hoodie patched with splashes of algae-green and paint. He startles when you approach, then grins—wide, nervous, a kid who still gets praised when a weld holds.

Silas: "Ava! (you) We got the bio-mesh on the basal roots like you drew. See? Ori's repurposed weights are holding even with that last tidal pull."

You bend to look. The mesh is a neat crude weave of fishing line and reef-safe polymer; tiny shoots of spartina thread through it like green teeth. Your hands find the damp edge of the planter and you can feel the tug of the water, patient and constant.

You: "Good job. Check the moorings again at noon. If the current shifts, we loosen the leeward tie first."

Silas: "Got it. Hey—Ori said to tell you he left a bit of his patchwork coat on the pier. Said you'd know which one."

You glance up and see, not far off, the cluster of floating planters anchored by Ori's coat—bright patches of fabric catching the morning light like a small, defiant tapestry. He is there too, mid-reach, hands dusted in salt and solder. He isn't speaking yet; he's watching the water as if he could stitch it into something quieter.

[Visual: Ori framed in sun-bleached topknot, the patchwork coat catching the light]
[Sound: Soft metallic clink—tools, not threatening; a child's laugh in the distance]

Ori Navarro: (smiling when you meet his eyes) "You coming by to steal my coat or to borrow my tools?"

You: "Neither. I'm stealing your stubbornness and borrowing your gauge. The root probes were reading shallow last night—thought maybe you had ideas on longer anchors."

Ori's smile warms in a way that makes the cold salt of the morning feel less like an accusation. He steps closer; his voice drops, easier.

Ori Navarro: "Longer anchors, huh? (laughs) I was thinking more like creative floats. Maybe a hybrid—your science, my scrap. Could make the planters ride the swell instead of resisting it."

You let the idea sit between you. It's not new, but hearing Ori say it in that particular cadence—half joke, half architectural dare—makes it feel possible and small enough to try.

Old Mara arrives then, with a gait that is slow but absolutely deliberate. Her shawl drips tiny tides onto the boardwalk; each weight in its hem a memory. She looks at you, and for a breath there is a history in that look: floods, bargains, the small economies of living where the water eats into everything.

Old Mara: (soft, a rasp like wind through driftwood) "You cut your hair different from the last time I saw you. You look like a woman who can argue with the sea."

You: "Old Mara—"

Old Mara: "Call me Mara, child. Not 'Old.' You came home because the sea is loud and because you always come when the board creaks the loudest."

You want to tell her that you stay because leaving felt like betraying the people who still needed you. You want to tell her about the nights you replayed the storefront collapsing, about how survivor's guilt is a muscle you flex until it hurts. Instead you tuck those words into the notebook margins, into diagrams and lists where guilt can be measured and planned away.

You: "I'm here to make sure the living wall holds. To keep folks in place where they want to be."

Old Mara's smile is a quick flash—approval that is less sentimental and more tactical, like a weather signal.

Old Mara: "Then you use your head, child. The sea is not an enemy; it's a neighbor who forgets sometimes. Remember to feed it roots and stories both."

Her hand rests on your shoulder, surprisingly heavy, salt-damp and warm. You feel older than your years and smaller all at once; the gesture is a benediction and a charge.

[Narration]
The horizon watches you back. The cranes that silhouette VossArc are at rest this morning, their arms folded like sleeping beasts. The tower is a geometry of glass and promise—distant, clinical—its reflection a thin blue line on the water. It is a presence that demands you translate your small, stubborn resistance into plans that can be read by funders and politicians who prefer numbers on slides to the sound of a child's kite.

You check the sensors on your handheld: salinity drift is within expected parameters for this season; root depth shows slow, healthy penetration into the bio-concrete. You make quick sketches—cross-sections, anchor lengths, a note that Maya will need volunteers at nine for replanting. Your pen moves with a pace that steadies your breath. The day lays itself out like a grid you can step through.

Maya nudges a coffee cup into your hand; its heat is immediate and securing. She watches as you sip, as if cataloguing which small comforts keep you upright.

Maya Lin: "The hall meeting is at two. I put out three extra flyers. Jun said he might swing by after his morning piece. Old Mara wants us to start with the planting rotation before we hear from anyone with suits."

You: "Good. Keep the order—planting first, then the update on moorings. If Jun comes, he can hear the rotation notes firsthand. No spin."

Maya's eyes soften. There's trust there—hard earned, easily broken. You feel the care of that trust like a weight you are glad to carry.

Ori lingers when the others start to move on, fingers tracing a nick in a length of braided rope.

Ori Navarro: (low) "You look tired, you know."

You feel a small heat rise—embarrassment, maybe. You want to deflect with data or a joke; you do neither.

You: "I am. But tired doesn't mean absent."

Ori studies you as if he is calibrating a tool. For a moment you consider telling him about the nights you stayed awake, tallying which roofs might be reinforced and which families might be coaxed into safer rooms. Instead, you give him the only honest lie that matters: that you'll manage.

Ori Navarro: "Alright. Then I won't pester you with grand plans. Not today."

You smile even though the smile feels like a compromise.

[Narration]
The morning wears its light like a patient thing. Activity hums at the scale of hands and small engines, not headlines. You catalog, you organize, you breathe in the constant brine and exhale lists. The stakes are present like weather—real but not yet storm—and your hope is a slow, deliberate thing. It sits at the base of your neck, a steady ember you feed with measurements and meetings.

You tuck the notebook closed, feeling the bulk of plans settle against your side. The boardwalk creaks under more footsteps as neighbors begin to gather—each sound a familiar chord in the orchestral hush of Saltway waking. You take one last look at the horizon: VossArc's silhouette, the cranes, the thin blue promise of engineered certainty. Then you turn your face back to the quarter, to the people whose faces you know by light and breath.

You make a mental checklist for the meeting: planting rotation, mooring checks, a volunteer roster, a note to bring the root-depth graphs. Beneath the list is the unnumbered thing you try not to make a habit of cataloguing—the reason you came back that isn't on any official form: the sense that staying is a kind of love.

You breathe in the salt and the smoke, and when you exhale the air tastes like promise and ledger lines.

You square your shoulders and begin walking toward the hall, the soft rhythm of the boardwalk underfoot like a pulse. There is work to do, people to steady, and conversations to have that will stitch plans to lives. The day ahead is not loud, but it is full; the current is slow, and you will meet it with the small, steady things you know how to do.
