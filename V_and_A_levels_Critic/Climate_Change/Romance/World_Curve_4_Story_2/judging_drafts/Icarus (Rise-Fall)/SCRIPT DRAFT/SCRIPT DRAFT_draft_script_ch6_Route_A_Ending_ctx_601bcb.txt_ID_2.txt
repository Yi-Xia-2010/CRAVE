[Scene: Tide Garden | Noon]
[Visual: Camera bulbs flare along the boardwalk; filaments strung from mast to mast cast a warm, brittle light. The living wall rises behind you—planted terraces glisten with salt and young green shoots. Children press their palms to algae-dark stone and laugh.]
[Sound: A cicada whine of small generators, the distant clack of a crane, the steady click of shutters; the tide whispers at the base of terraces.]
[Music: Bright, taut brass—triumphal but edged with tension]

You stand at the lip of the garden with the packet in your hand, the same packet you have memorized down to a margin note. The crowd folds around you like a tide folding over a reef: neighbors, volunteers, a smattering of suited faces for the cameras. Maya's pen is poised; Jun's recorder is half-hidden beneath his jacket, red light steady as a watch. Old Mara stands with her beads knuckled between callused fingers, breathing as if the breath itself were a prayer.

You watch children plant microgreens along the algae-streaked terraces and imagine those plants as promise made visible—tiny leaves like flags that say we will not be washed away. You want that image to last. You want the ink in the packet to keep it.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand—paper, a corner damp with salt from where you fidgeted. Ori stands close, paint on his knuckles, welding goggles pushed up like a crown; his amber eyes find yours and hold a little too long.]

Ori: "They'll take the photos. They'll say we made the future, Ava." (his voice is soft, as if comforting the thing he is afraid of losing)

You let him take your fingers for one breath. His skin smells of metal and lemon oil. It steadies you, until the sensor on the tripod beside the terrace hiccups—an electronic hiccup like a swallowed cough.

[Sound: A high, thin tone—then a sharper ping. The murmurs around you shift; someone laughs uncertainly at a joke that is not funny.]

Jun frowns and leans down to the recorder, tapping the device. You can feel the hairs on your forearms lift. On the small screen the tidal graph spikes: a vertical white spear cutting through the expected curve. Your stomach drops as if the ground itself has slipped.

You know what the spike means before Jun says it. Someone opened upstream flood gates. Not scheduled. Not warned.

[Sound: Emergency radios crackle from the Glass Pier; a supervisor's voice comes through a borrowed megaphone, controlled but thin. A crane halts mid-swing, polymer braces frozen and suspended like a question.]

At the pier, the VossCorp cameras keep rolling. Investors smile into their feeds. In the pictures they'll crop, the living wall holds back the water and children laugh. The PR lens flattens the panic into a tableau.

Maya's voice is a rope: taut, quick. "We need to check levels—get the pumps—Silas, on the terrace now!"

Silas is already a blur, socks slipping on wet boards, a small body of urgency. Hands move; volunteers hustle. The living wall flexes—roots quiver in their pockets of bio-concrete. For a moment it works. Water curls around terraces and slips away like a proof.

You taste salt and metal and the bitter aftertaste of something else—relief folding into fear.

Jun's voice is lower, harder. "There are emergency protocols, but this—this spike is anomalous. I want the upstream manifest."

You turn a page in your notebook as if the sensation of paper might be an anchor. Your pen skitters. Arguments are already beginning to bloom in the margins: contingency measures, who signs what, which clause covers what, how many lives are at legal risk if—if—

[Visual: The packet is handed back to you. You feel the weight of those sealed pages like a small country in your arms.]

A suited woman from VossCorp steps up beside Calder, polishing a smile into place. Calder's expression is the practiced gentleness of a man who measures storms in spreadsheets.

Calder Voss: "We're fortunate the prototype performed. This is exactly why a larger-scale approach is necessary." (formal, the kind of clarity that is a blade)

You hold his gaze. In his eyes you can see the photograph he would file away later: a living wall holding, a smiling community, a city inhaling relief.

You: "It performed because people were hands-on," you say. Your voice is not small. "Because this neighborhood watched and lived it. That—" (you lift the packet a fraction) "—is in there by design."

Calder's smile does not bruise. "Design is what scales, Ava. People scale design. We make it reliable."

You feel something harden behind your ribs—survivor's guilt, the old echo of a storefront turned to slush, a family photograph that drifted from a windowsill in a storm years ago. It is hot and sharp and it carries a single needle of question: Did you hand them a life preserver with a hole in it?

Jun clears his throat, voice brittle. "There's language in draft three that allows 'managed relocation' if certain thresholds are met." He lets the words hang; they are barbed and deliberate. "I—I've flagged it. It's...ambiguous."

Maya: "Flagged it? We negotiated those clauses."

Jun: "The public draft our office got—there's a private supplement. I found it buried in permit addenda—"

Your pen stabs the margin. You flip through, heart hammering against your palm. Your hands start to shake without permission.

[Sound: Rain begins as a smear on the horizon, thin at first, then a white hush. Camera shutters continue; the photographers don't think to stop.]

Maya's phone shrieks; she yanks it to her ear, face a map of anger. "They're issuing advisories," she says, voice breaking. "They're calling them advisories, but they're notices—evacuation language tied to the clause. They have templates."

A murmur moves through the crowd like wind. You feel the packet like a hot coal.

Calder steps forward, tone shifting to the velvet of a man trained to soothe. "Safety advisories are standard," he says. "We will ensure relocation assistance where necessary. We cannot risk lives."

You hear the word "assistance" as paper—thin and not quite covering what it needs to. Your mouth goes dry.

You: "Necessary how?" (the question is a blade)

Calder: "When risk calculations cross an operational threshold—" (he gestures to a tablet, the language clinical) "—we implement managed relocation to prevent harm at scale. We do it humanely."

You can taste the PR mouth-foam. You can feel the packet knife into your palm.

Ori's hand is on your arm, fingers steady. He looks at Calder for a beat too long. Then, quietly, he undoes the seaglass charm from his necklace and rolls it between his fingers, as if weighing something that cannot be measured.

Ori: "Humanely," he repeats, not a question. "For whom?"

His voice is small; the charm beads make a minor, almost private clack.

You: "For people who get notices," you say, harder than you meant. "For people whose names are on forms we haven't had a chance to review."

Calder: (smiles, practiced) "We had environmental reviews. Legal teams—"

You interrupt, the sound of your own breath a breaking thing. "Legal teams wrote clauses that allow removal when it's convenient for the corporation's portfolio. Not convenient for families."

There is a pause like the tide pulling back to reveal the sand. Cameras keep taking pictures; someone catches your expression and it becomes an image of fury and dignity, archived for headlines.

Jun moves faster than the pause allows. He holds up a document—printer paper, yellowed at the edge by handling. "This—this is an internal memo. 'Leverage pilot to accelerate relocation protocols'—I didn't want to believe it. But it's here."

[Visual: Close-up on the memo in Jun's hands; typed words look obscene in their polish.]

The crowd tightens—neighbors' faces folding into a hundred small betrayals. An elderly man drops to his knees by his doorway, the sound of something cracking sharp as glass. A mother grips a child and begins to cry with the practical, guttural sound of someone who knows how to make a bag with one hand in twenty seconds.

You feel the ground tilt and know the only true thing in your mouth is the salt that comes out when you scream.

You: "You hid this." (it is not a question)

Calder's jaw twitches. He straightens. "We did what we had to in order to secure funding and rollout a scalable solution. The pilot—"

Ori laughs, and it sounds like a splintering. "A pilot," he says, "that will be used to write people out of their homes when it suits your balance sheet."

Calder's assistant steps in, voice clipped, "The legal language provides necessary operational latitude—"

Maya: (cutting across, voice high with need) "We negotiated for tenure protections. We were promised community control clauses."

Calder: "Those were considerations. Not every contingency can be codified without jeopardizing the entire enterprise." (his gaze is steady) "We moved to protect the city."

You feel something in your chest you cannot name—fury flaring bright enough to drown thought. The packet is a paper blade; you want to rip at the paragraph, at the ink.

[Sound: A harsh series of beeps: VossCorp support teams are pushing advisories out. Phones buzz in pockets with templated language: SAFETY ADVISORY: Immediate recommended relocation. Assistance offered. Contact hotline.]

The first notice arrives at an old neighbor's door five minutes later on a volunteer's tablet. It's printed and stamped in corporate blue: SAFETY ADVISORY: Recommended Temporary Relocation. The word "recommended" covers a gulf. Someone in the crowd looks at it, eyes going wide, mouth working.

Boxes begin to appear as if conjured. Someone piles up pots. A neighbor you know from childhood now folds bedding with a speed that is the precise measure of resignation.

Old Mara does not speak at first. She sets her pack down and lifts one hand to the beads that are always wrapped between her fingers like a rosary. She finds each bead with a thumb like someone counting years. When she does speak, the words are small and inevitable.

Old Mara: "They made a place of paper where our lives used to be."

It is not an accusation at Calder alone. It is the ache of history speaking.

Ori drops his charm into his palm and stares at it until the glaze in his eyes dulls. He looks up at you and, without looking away, steps back a single pace.

Ori: "I didn't want this to be how it happened," he says. "I thought—" (he searches for a word and finds none) "—we could build something that showed them another way."

You want to shake him into clarity. Instead you feel the animal, old guilt: for every person you have kept, what cost have you signed them toward? You imagine your family storefront again, the way the water took the sign and left the frame empty, and the world narrows to a single chord of blame.

You: "Did we—did I—sign our safety away?" The words tear out like a confession you didn't mean to make public.

Ori: (quiet, circling) "We made a compromise. We thought it would buy time. I—" (he stops) "I didn't see the clause. None of us saw the private memo until Jun dug it up."

You look at Jun. He looks exhausted and raw. "I found it," he says, as if that explains more than it does. "I found it because something jangled my suspicion. I didn't plant it."

A family begins to shovel clothes into plastic bins. The boards creak underfoot like a heart under pressure. Rain is a steady percussion now, not the small brush it was. The filaments above you shiver with wet. Lanterns, strung for celebration, bob like warning buoys.

Anger breaks over you with the force of a storm front. It is not steady grief now. It is a hot, immediate thing. You taste your own salt and it is bitter.

You move toward Calder—not in a planned step but as if an ocean current has chosen your course. The crowd presses and cameras pivot to frame what will become a defining image.

You: (voice like a whip) "You made profit from our proof. You used our trust as leverage. You hid the part where 'scaling' means 'pull people out when it's profitable.'"

Calder: "That is an overstatement." (his tone is cool, lawyer-clean) "We operate within regulatory frameworks. We work with municipalities—"

You: "Municipalities that sign when frightened. Municipalities that sign when pressured by the optics of a 'successful pilot' you stage. You call it protection; I call it leverage."

The exchange spirals—words, defenses, polite refutations from Calder, sharp, public anger from you. Photographers take the frame of your confrontational silhouette; someone shouts that they'll be quoting you.

Ori moves to stand at your side and his shoulder grazes yours—a touch that should be steadying and instead is a question in itself. He opens his mouth and then closes it. The charm is warm in his palm; he lets it fall into his pocket as if deciding something he cannot say.

[Sound: A distant siren wails, then another; the city is reacting in pieces that do not fit together.]

The crescendo is immediate: voices overlapping, some neighbors yelling for fairness, others already leaving; a child crying; a VossCorp coordinator barking into a walkie; the rain turning heavy as if to wash the entire scene clean.

You want to speak to every person who has a box in their hand and tell them what you meant when you fought to keep them here. You want to explain legal nuance and the blind faith in engineering, you want to name the memo and tear it like tissue. None of that will put a pot back on a shelf or stop a landlord who sees relocation as a chance to sell.

[Visual: Lanterns bob along the boardwalk like small, resigned moons; their light is soft and ineffective against the gray. Boxes make islands.]

Night falls faster than sense. In the shell of the evening—lamps blinking, cameras on tripods, rain beading on the living wall's leaves—you feel your optimism crack. It is not a dramatic break; it is a hairline fracture that runs through everything you touch. The living wall still performs, still flexes against water that came too fast and left too sharp a taste. The plants hold; the terraces drain. The technical success is undeniable. So is the new leverage the success has produced.

You think of the people who will sign papers because "safety advisory" feels inevitable. You think of names on memos and line items in budgets that treat human lives as variables.

Ori finds your hand in the chaos and squeezes it once. You cling to that single human contact because you have to. You look at him and see, with painful clarity, that both of you are holding different pieces of the same map. He wants to show a different way by building more—beautiful, experimental, scalable at the edge; you wanted legal permanence and community control. You did not realize how much those aims could pull apart at the seams when the world was ready to bargain.

Ori: (soft, urgent) "We can still make more. We can show them—"

You: "Show them what?" (the question is raw) "That it looks pretty? That it holds in a picture? They already have that picture."

Silence folds in for the fraction of a heartbeat while rain drums the roof of the Ferryworks. Then Maya, voice ragged, calls out names of neighbors who have decided to stay and those who have already packed. She lists them like a ledger of loss.

Old Mara sits on the low step and watches lantern light smear down her shawl. "They will burn the edges of our maps," she says slowly. "They will keep the print and throw away the people who wrote the margins."

You kneel beside her and for once there is no speech that will repair it. Your notebook lies open on the ground; the tide chart is a jag of ink. The place where you once sketched a timeline that promised safety now looks like a torn map.

[Sound: The rain intensifies into something final, a curtain drawn.]

When the last of the cameras leaves and the field lights are dimmed by corporate vans pulling away, the Saltway glows in a different, lesser way—lanterns threaded through the quarter, small fires of domestic life gathering in doorways. People move like exiles in their own home: some carrying pots, some scraping pictures into plastic. The living wall keeps its slow, indifferent work of holding back a thing it was designed to resist—water does not know legal language.

You stand on the boardwalk as the crowd thins and feel the cool of the night press in. Ori's hand is gone from yours. He stands a step away, his coat dripping, the seaglass charm a memory in his pocket.

You: (to Ori, voice steady because you must be steady) "What do you want to do?"

Ori looks at you, amber eyes full but unreadable, and the answer is a thousand tiny things he cannot say aloud.

Ori: "I want to make sure people don't have to leave because someone decided it was cheaper than rebuilding. I want to be out there, building solutions. I—" (he stops, swallows) "—I don't know that I can keep both of us whole if I stay in a fight like this."

The words land like a small grenade. You feel both betrayal and a terrible understanding. He is not abandoning you out of malice. He simply cannot bear the legal calculus that has roped your neighborhood into a market.

You: "I didn't build my life to be a bargaining chip." (the statement is a keening clarifying itself)

He steps closer, hesitates, then presses his forehead to yours for a slow, painful second—an intimacy that is a benediction and a goodbye in the same motion. You cannot tell if it is a promise or an apology.

Ori: "I thought we were building a future," he says, voice cracking. "I still do. But I see now—maybe I was trying to build a future where you wouldn't have to make the sacrifices you already made. I can't—" (he rubs his face, the gesture of a man trying to gather his own pieces) "—I can't be the only one who keeps building."

You let the sentence hang. Between you is a geography of what you both want and what you will not let go of. Neither of you can name precisely how to fuse them.

[Visual: A slow pan out—the living wall a stripe of green against the dark sea; the Glass Pier's silhouette a diagnosis on the horizon; trucks idling like steel beetles.]

You walk the boardwalk alone after Ori walks away, his silhouette swallowed by salt and night. Boxes dot the planks like small islands; a child's shoe sits abandoned near a crate. The rain softens the world; the lantern light blurs into watercolor.

You pull your field notebook from your jacket. The tide chart you drew fills a page, margins full of notes you had once believed could bind words and people together. Your pen feels heavier than it should. You press the nib to paper and write in a hand that is steadier than your breath.

You: (to the empty night, to the water, to yourself) "We built something that can be used against us. I will not let them have the rest."

There is no fleeting triumph in that promise. There is only the slow, iron decision of a person who knows exactly the ledger of losses and chooses, with all its weight, to keep standing in the wreckage.

[Sound: The tide eats at the pilings; a gull calls once, long and solitary.]

You gather the packet and tuck it under your arm. You walk through Saltway as neighbors pack what they can. Some nod at you with the weary gratitude of people who have trusted and been betrayed; others look at you with something else—an accusation folded into grief.

Old Mara meets you at the narrow bridge and lays a single fishing hook in your palm. "Keep it close," she says. "Hooks catch what is slippery."

You nod. You do not promise anything you cannot keep.

[Visual: Final close-up—your hand tight around the hook, salt crusting the knuckles; the living wall bending in the background like a green, grieving lung.]

[Music: A low, somber chord; strings that do not resolve]

You do not know how many will leave. You do not know if the legal fights will hold. You know only the immediate things: the living wall's leaves glistening with rain, the papers in your pack that both protect and endanger, the warmth of a single hand whose fingers you still remember.

You look back once at the Tide Garden where the day began in hope and ends in this brittle, honest ruin. Somewhere in the glass of the pier a camera catches that look—the picture of a woman who tried.

You close the notebook. The cover settles with a familiar sigh.

[Sound: Rain, close and honest. The tide tick-tick-ticks. A lantern goes out down the line.]

[Visual: Fade slowly to black]

[Music: Fade to a low, unresolved drone]
