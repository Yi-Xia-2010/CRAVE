[Scene: Glass Pier | Late Afternoon → Dusk]
[Visual: The pier's polished surfaces catch a hesitant sun; reflections shiver as clouds mass offshore. A patchwork mural dominates the pier face—a living sculpture of reclaimed circuit fragments, seaglass charms, and braided fishing twine. Small speakers are woven into the weave; the mural breathes with low, human sounds.]
[Music: A buoyant, handcrafted chord—hand drums and bowed glass—rises under the crowd's murmurs]
[Sound: Children’s voices on the speakers; a fragment of a neighbor’s laugh; the distant rum of a helicopter and the softer hum of cameras]

You came here by choice and calculation. You and Ori chose spectacle over silence because silence felt like surrender; you chose to make the city look you in the eye. The delay—building proof before pleading—carved time out of your chest and filled it with prototypes, late soldering, and long nights on the Ferryworks. You convinced yourself that beauty could be evidence, that a city could be persuaded by the sound of its own people.

Now the mural sings.

Narration:
The first cluster of voices rises—a child describing how she learned to fish from a boulevard of boards; an old woman naming the plants on her stoop; a young man speaking, quietly, about why he cannot leave. The speakers stitch them together into a single, surprising chorus. Cameras angle, zoom; the mayoral feeds cut in and out. For a breath the glass towers look away.

Ori stands beside you, shoulders stained with paint and salt. He keeps laughing under his breath when the crowd finds the right moment to gasp. His hands, thick with calluses and grease, move through the mural like someone smoothing a wound closed.

Ori Navarro: "It looks like the pier is breathing. Like someone stitched a harbor into a seam." (he grins; fingers twitch toward a loose charm)

You: "It looks like all the reasons we didn't quit on this place." (your voice is steadier than you feel; the speakers thrum under your boots)

Ori: "They're listening." (he shrugs, then softer) "They're actually listening."

You watch a small cluster of children press their faces to the barrier to hear better; a reporter leans in, pen poised, training a camera like a hawk. Jun's by the railing somewhere—probably chewing his lip, recording the sound just in case the city forgets to be kind.

[Visual: Close-up on a seaglass charm, sunlight haloing it; a circuit shard reflects a child's grin]
[Sound: The mural's audio flips to a neighbor saying, "This is our work." A cheer breaks.]

Narration:
For an hour—maybe two—the city pauses. People who pass the pier slow their steps. A café on the main canal opens its doors and a barista holds a cup as if it were a relic. Threads of the web pick up a clip: Saltway sings. Commentators tweet wistful things; an investor's newsletter calls it "engagement theater" and posts a smiling gif. Both are true.

Narration:
The spectacle is generous; it astonishes you to your bones. You taste salt and solder and the metallic tang of a city's attention. But halfway through the broadcast, a new sound threads through the speakers—not part of your mix. A recorded, lacquered PR voice: calm, measured.

[Sound: A corporate feed—polite, precise—interrupts. The crowd hums like a line of beaded nets clinking together.]

Calder Voss (Voice, over the feed): "What you see today is a testament to community spirit. VossArc has always supported resilient design. What the Saltway shows is beauty—it's inspiring. However, we must be honest: posterity requires scale. Without broad, engineered certainty, neighborhoods like this risk more than charm can protect. We are prepared to act in the public interest."

Narration:
Calder's voice is a knife honed into a smile. It reframes the angle with surgical ease: your mural is now anecdote; Saltway is a portrait that validates his premise—people are charming, but they are endangered, and only centralized certainty can save them. You can feel the shift in the crowd: admiration folding inward, softening into concession.

A hush ripples through the pier. The cameras that had been filming faces start to pan to screens where Calder's presentation plays in a cutaway: models, wind tunnels, a proposed "protected zone" with clinical renderings of levees and clean, depopulated promenades.

Narration:
You don't see the memos at first—the quiet bureaucratic things that arrive like weather reports. You see them later, posted thin and legally precise: authorization for enhanced "relocation assistance," contracts for temporary housing, a line item for security services to manage "site transitions." They are stamped like rain on the prosecuting day.

[Sound: The sound of paper sliding; a security van's engine growls. Somewhere, someone clears a throat.]

Narration:
Security teams—uniforms taut and instruction-book white—begin to tape off sections of the pier. At first it's a perimeter for crowd control. Then it's a line that says: this area is under management. Neighbors with planters and portable beds fold them up with hands that have lived long inside the work of holding. The planters, your living experiments, are lifted and placed in neat rows like evidence.

Ori presses a hand to the mural, one palm on the braided twine, and you can see the shape of something giving under his weight.

Ori Navarro: "They'll take the poetry and put it in a lobby." (he laughs, too sharp) "They'll make a postcard and sell 'resilience' as an amenity."

You: "We tried to speak with their language—proof, prototypes. We tried to make what we do legible to them." (your mouth tastes like the script you never wanted to read aloud)

Ori: "And it worked in a way that doesn't help." (he looks at you like a man who has been making things and realized too late they are being repurposed.)

Narration:
The media—ever eager for human interest—circuits the pier, and a clip of a child singing over the mural becomes the definitive image in every feed. The legal papers appear like a tide undercutting the beach: memos signed, clauses rubber-stamped. Calder's team spins an op-ed with a warm header: "Compassion Requires Tough Choices."

Narration:
No one shouts. There is no cinematic confrontation. This is quieter, more corrosive: a thousand small permissions signed in quiet rooms while a mural plays on the pier. Investors in glass towers call the display "cute," and Calder reframes morality as efficiency. The city consumes the image while the bureaucratic machinery chews the ground beneath your feet.

Ori moves close and, without speaking, reaches into his coat. He produces a small seaglass charm—smoothed by hands and tide, threaded with a scrap of circuit that once blinked. He places it in your palm. There are no words. The charm is cool; it fits into the hollow of your hand like a found fossil.

Narration:
The weight of the charm is disproportionate. It is an artifact of what you tried: beauty, proof, a plea that had laughter and solder and first drafts of policy. In the press frames you'll recognize later, the charm will be a quiet detail—two people, a tender exchange amid the fanfare. But the cameras do not capture what the charm actually holds: the residue of hope that refuses to be owned.

Ori Navarro: (quietly) "Keep it." (his voice has the tilt of someone who measures everything with tools, suddenly careless) "So you have something that won't be repurposed into a 'feature.'"

You: "I don't know what a charm can do against a clause." (words sharp as salt) "But I can keep it close."

Ori: "Then keep it." (his thumb brushes yours; he steps back, like someone who needs to be somewhere else and chooses not to tell you that)

Narration:
As the sun slides and the clouds press lower, the cameras linger. They frame your mural as an emblem—beautiful, then archived. The security lines become more orderly. Notices are handed out with the same neatness as pamphlets advertising a museum exhibition. People sign for boxes of belongings, for temporary lodging, for relocation cheques that look like consolation.

You walk the length of the pier as neighbors file past, eyes low or bright depending on the flavor of their anger. The living sculpture watches you go. Someone in the crowd calls your name; you answer with a wave so small it is almost invisible. Ori watches you from the mural's shadow, hands in pockets, the top of his profile paper-thin against the sky.

Narration:
You catalog what is taken in a new ledger the way you used to catalog tide-lines: names, addresses, the color of a child's sweater. But a ledger is not a levee. A beautiful sculpture is not tenure. The city's appetite for image, for a softened history to hang on its gleaming walls, is hollowing the community out from the inside.

A gust of wind lifts the edges of a tarp, and for a moment you imagine everything—the mural, the planters, the laughter—unglued. You imagine Ori’s face in that wind, a silhouette you might try to chase; you imagine the pier as a map with tears.

You stop at the mural's base. The speakers are on low now; children's voices echo thinly like seashells held to an ear.

Ori Navarro: "I'm not saying leave." (he presses his hands into his pockets; there's a tremor in the set of his jaw) "But I can't watch you sink into fighting them on their terms forever. If they take this—" (he gestures at the pier, the cameras, the neat lines) "—then someone should be out there making other versions. Making things they can't commodify so easily."

You: "So you want to go?" (the question doesn't feel like betrayal; it feels like a hinge opening)

Ori: "I don't know." (he laughs, a short, embarrassed thing) "Maybe. Maybe I'm tired of being careful. Maybe I need to make a place that can't be put on a postcard."

Narration:
Silence collects like rain in the troughs of the crowd. Ori says things he couches as possibilities, and you think of all the nights soldering fabricated life into prototypes. You think of the possibility that some missions aren't meant to be finished in one place.

You reach up and tie the seaglass charm into your bracelet, the knot rough but secure.

Narration:
There is grief here—raw, encyclopedic. There is culpability, too: for believing spectacle could substitute for law, for thinking beauty would protect tenure. You count the ways you tried and the ways they repurposed your offers. The mural will be photographed, footnoted, then installed in a corporate lobby. The living terrace prototypes will be written into a VossArc case study. The neighborhood will be reframed as charming, needing protection, and then as a logistical obstacle to be moved.

[Visual: The pier’s lights come on; the mural's tiny bulbs stitch a false dusk of warmth. Beyond the pier, cranes continue to parse the skyline with indifferent rhythm.]
[Music: A single, low bowed-glass note—sustained; the crowd's murmur becomes a distant tide]

Narration:
You take a breath as if you could breathe the whole thing in and hold it. The charm in your wrist is cold, a private weight. Ori folds his coat tighter and starts away—toward the Ferryworks, toward another shoreline, toward the pragmatic stubbornness of someone who makes. He doesn't make a scene. He doesn't promise to return.

You: (to Ori) "If you go—" (the words thrum) "—go knowing this: Saltway isn't just a scene. It's people who will keep the seam together long after the cameras leave."

Ori: (he stops, looks back, and for a moment the laugh is gone) "I know." (then, half-smile) "And you—" (he taps the charm at your wrist) "—keep making things they can't put behind glass."

Narration:
He steps off the pier and into the press of jackets and cameras. You stand, the mural at your back, the line of security threading forward like a new tide. The crowd disperses into pockets of resignation and quiet resistance. Later, the news will call the day a 'touching display' and 'a compelling humanity angle.' The memos will be quoted in footnotes under press releases. A committee will convene and delay. A relocation order will be executed.

You close your notebook. Your handwriting looks small on the page. You begin to write not a plan for the seawall—this time you write a list of names, of porches and plants, of the people you saw folding their lives into boxes. You write so the ledger will be more than a memorial; you write so you can remember the exact color of a child's laugh before it is edited for air.

[Visual: The camera angles away; the mural glows softly as headlamps sweep across the pier. A foghorn in the distance marks the hour.]
[Sound: A tide, inevitable and mechanical; the sound of boxes being taped]

Narration:
This is defeat organized like a meeting. It is not violent in the way storms are violent; it is patient, bureaucratic, relentless. Beauty was a tool—manifest, tender—but tools are harvested by those who can patent them.

You look down at the charm on your wrist. It is a cold, small thing against your pulse. You slide a finger across its edge and feel, for a second, a flash of all the faces the mural tried to hold together. You scrape the flash into a memory and lock it in the small chest of your ribcage.

Ori is already a silhouette moving toward the Ferryworks. You step after him—not to stop him, not to call him back, but because movement is cleaner than staying still in a place where someone else has decided the terms.

Narration:
There is a lastness to how the pier closes for the night that isn't final in the way a book might be final. There are still plans, still petitions and small uprisings in basements and kitchens. But the day has given you the taste of co-optation: that your most sincere acts can become ornaments in a narrative you did not choose.

You fold your hands around your notebook as if it were an anchor. You feel the weight of responsibility, the ache of lessons learned in public. You feel, too, the ember of a stubbornness that will not be catalogued or polished. That ember is not a promise of victory; it is a promise of fidelity to the neighborhood you came from.

Narration:
You turn away from the mural once more. The pier hums; the charm warms slightly against your skin. The city's lights reflect on the water as if to say: we will remember your image, but not the contract you tried to bind. You let the thought be true.

[Music: A low, aching cello line—then silence]
