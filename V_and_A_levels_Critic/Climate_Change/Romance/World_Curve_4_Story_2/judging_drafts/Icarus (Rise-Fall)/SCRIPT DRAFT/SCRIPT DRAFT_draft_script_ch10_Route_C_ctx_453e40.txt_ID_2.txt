You step through the hatch and the Ferryworks folds around you like a secret. Heat and salt and the odor of wet soil hit first — a green, earthy note threaded with the metallic sting of solder. Worklight pools on benches cluttered with circuit fragments, algae in jars, and a stack of blueprints you smoothed earlier until the creases lay like tide-lines.

Ori looks up from the bench. His hands are a map of the night: paint in the creases, a thin scar on the knuckle that catches the lamp and looks for a second like a crescent moon. He smiles the way he smiles at new machines—part mischief, part devotion.

Ori Navarro: "There you are. Thought you'd vanished into your notebook forever."

You set your notebook on the table, wrists brushing the battered cover. For a beat you only listen—the timbre of the Ferryworks is small and knitted: someone scraping a plank, the soft clink of a spanner, Silas's footsteps on the lower deck. This is the kind of silence that isn't empty; it's expectant.

You: "I almost did. Wanted to see how the sensor mounts were coming along."

Ori holds up a tiny sensor the size of a thumbnail and fits a reclaimed circuit fragment into it with a tenderness that surprises you. He hums, fits the piece, hums again like he's tuning not only the device but the room.

Ori Navarro: "They're singing. Just need to cradle the algae filters and the weave for the mangroves. Then the sails—I've got that hinge idea that should make them feather under swell."

You watch his fingers, the practiced choreography. There's a steadiness to him that makes you want to map the trajectory of your own breath against his rhythm. Your phone is off in your pocket; this is deliberate. You don't want corporate trackers, don't want well-meaning cameras. Tonight, the Ferryworks belongs to the people who built it.

You sketch where the living modules will lock into the bedrock of the cove and where the community-tenure markers—brass tags stamped with family names—will be recessed into the concrete ribs. Every line you draw is a measure of what you refuse to lose.

You (internal): If this holds—if one cove stands—then we have a mirror. A way to say: we can stay. We can build our safety without selling our names to someone else.

Silas drops through the hatch with a bag of salt-stained rope and a grin that keeps trying to be audacious and failing into earnestness. Two neighbors you trust—Ali and Mateo—follow, coats clinging to them like halos of fog. They set down crates of seedlings and a battered drill.

Silas: "You called. I'm here. Ori, where do you want the algae vats?"

Ori Navarro: "Right there. And Silas—pass me the braided copper. The micro-sensors need a clean run."

Silas moves like a boy in a tool shed, quick hands, quick smiles. He glances at you, and in his face is that urge of youth—wanting to make something that matters.

You (to Ori): "We're still sure about keeping this quiet?"

Ori's expression shifts, small clouds crossing. There's a flirtatious lift he uses to cover the nerves, but you see the refusal beneath it: he wants it this way, to control the edges of risk.

Ori Navarro: "We are. For now. If we go public before we have data, VossCorp swoops in with open palms and a contract you didn't know you needed. We show them something concrete—handmade, stubborn—then we decide what's next."

You: "And if it fails?"

He pauses, the weight of the question like weather on his shoulders.

Ori Navarro: (soft) "Then we learn and try again. We don't hand our neighborhoods over because fear makes us scramble."

The two of you trade a look with the kind of shorthand people build under pressure. There's trust here that isn't easy to name—layered, tested, not sentimental, but real. It heats the room more potently than the lanterns.

The sensors come alive in stages. First a tiny LED breathes, then another. You watch numbers crawl across a borrowed tablet—conductivity, flow rate, micro-voltage from the algae cells. The Ferryworks hum changes; the machines that were mere promise become a chorus.

Your chest drums. This is where the risk and the hope meet: a lit sensor, a green line that says the filter is drawing properly, that the mangrove roots are anchoring the planters' buoyancy. It's small—one cove's worth of data—but it is the universe in a seedpod.

Silas pumps his fist and everyone laughs because the sound feels like moving forward.

Silas: "Look at that. We did it. It's alive."

Ori steps close, the light catching his amber eyes; he leans so near you can feel the warmth at the base of his neck. He sets a careful hand on the back of your head to steady the soldering iron you hold and in the gesture there's both affection and an unspoken request not to look away from what you're building together.

Ori Navarro: (quiet) "You always knew how to make plans pretty."

You: "You make me believe we can build them."

He grins, softer now, and when you reach up—because every tide has a moment like this—you press your lips to the salt at his temple, the tiny grains catching, the scent of sea on his skin familiar and strange at once.

The prototype breathes under the moon. The algae filter pulses in gentle cycles; the solar sails fold once and settle; tiny pumps click in tide with the readings. You press your forehead to his and for a flicker you are both children again and adults who bear entire neighborhoods on sketch-lined pages.

You (internal): Everything we are—our stubbornness, our grief, our laughter—condenses into this low, electric moment. If it holds, we'll have given ourselves a place at the table. If it doesn't, we will have learned the cost of secrecy and carried it together.

Ori whispers, a sound you store in the place in your chest reserved for soft things.

Ori Navarro: "Promise me you won't carry it all alone."

You: "I won't. Not anymore."

His hand tightens, not possessive—but anchoring. The room's energy tilts upward; everyone is aware of the meaning of what they're doing. It's no longer only an act of craft but a small political rebellion: beautiful, functional, and insistently human.

You stand at the center of it and your pulse is a drumline. This is high-stakes joy—urgent, electric, dangerous in the best way. The Ferryworks feels like a living thing that has just learned to speak.

You gather the crew, hands still smelling of soil and solder, and look at the humming prototype, at the ledger of names in your notebook tucked beneath a paperweight. Tomorrow you'll lower the modules into Saltway cove and watch to see whether the water takes them as ally or enemy.

You savor the warmth at the base of your throat—the kind that comes from work done beside someone who won't let you carry everything alone. Tonight is proof that hope can be handcrafted.

You linger in the doorway, watching the sensors blink like constellations wired for purpose. For a moment everything is perfect and precarious in the same breath.

You (internal): Morning will tell us more than any plan. Tomorrow, at low tide, we will test the proof we have made. If the cove receives it—if the measurements hold—we will have the beginning of an argument that can't be dismissed as idealism.

You close your notebook and tuck it under your arm. Your fingers find Ori's and you give them a squeeze that says both: stay, and: be ready.
