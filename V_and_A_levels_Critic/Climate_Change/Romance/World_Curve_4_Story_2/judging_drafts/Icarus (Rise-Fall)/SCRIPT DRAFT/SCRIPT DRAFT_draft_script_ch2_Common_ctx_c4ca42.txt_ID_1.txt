[Scene: Saltway Community Hall | Morning]
[Visual: Warm amber light pools over a long plywood table; maps and tide stamps scattered across it]
[Sound: Rain-muted traffic from the far pier; a kettle hissing in the corner]
[Music: Low, hopeful strings — steady, unhurried]

You set the notebook down where the light catches the salt crystals on the cover. Your handwriting is still damp at the edges from the walk; the ink blooms the way the shoreline blooms after a tide. The hall smells like wet banners and strong coffee, the kind that holds the weight of a meeting past midnight and a dozen hands around a single plan.

Maya is already there, sleeves rolled, a patchwork of pins clinking when she moves. Her coat brushes your arm with the ease of a tide finding its boardwalk — familiar enough that it quiets the small, sharp worry always pressed behind your ribs. She taps a battered tide-chart stamp into a corner of the plywood and hands you another marker like an invitation.

Maya Lin: "Alright, Ava — show me where the water wants to go and where we want it to stop."

You breathe and let the room settle into you. Mapping is a ritual: tide-lines traced in crude arcs, annotations in the margins, the cramped shorthand you've learned to trust. The stamps smear with salt where your fingers slipped; it feels honest that nothing here can be perfectly clean.

You: "If the high tide reaches this notch, the back alleys flood in two hours. We reinforce here, raise these pontoons, and redirect runoff into the tide garden's basins."

Maya leans in, eyes bright. Her voice is all motion, a conspiracy of plans.

Maya Lin: (folding your lists into a plan) "Volunteers for morning shifts, materials from the Ferryworks, Jun can vet the permits quietly. We'll put up a sign-up and get Old Mara to host a walk-through. People show up when they feel this is for them."

You watch her rearrange your messy columns into neat blocks of action. It feels like being held steady by a hand that refuses to let worry win.

Maya Lin: "We need a narrative. Not just 'stop the water' — 'hold our homes.' Jun can make it human. He'll find the clause that matters and we make sure everyone knows what it means."

A mutter of excitement passes through the few early arrivals. The kettle clicks off, and Jun steps in damp and earnest, rain staining the lapels of his blazer smaller every week.

Jun: "You called, and I came—discreetly. I can look through VossCorp's filings without making waves. If there's a backdoor or a clause that lets them press for relocation, I'll find it. But—" (he pauses, fingers worrying a strap on his recorder) "—no public splash until we know what we're swimming against."

You let the pause between you lengthen into something careful and deliberate. Jun's promise is the kind of steady thing you crave: not grandstanding, just one person committing to the work that moves things forward.

You: "Thank you. Quietly is good. We don't need headlines yet — we need leverage."

Jun looks at the tide-map, then back up at you.

Jun: "Leverage and stories. Names. If we can show that Old Mara's place and three others are at immediate risk, it's harder for them to spin 'collaboration'."

Maya: "I'll knock on doors after lunch. Get the volunteers lined up. Ava, you'll take materials procurement?"

You feel the familiar pragmatic rush when lists arrange themselves into tasks. There's a part of you that wants to say yes to everything — to hold every line — and another part that already counts the hours you'll lose to it. Survivor's guilt thrums beneath your decisions like a low tide: it makes you over-commit to prevent loss.

You: "I'll handle materials and grants language. If anyone can make grant-speak readable, it's me. And Ori can help with prototypes—his living wall could be our demonstrator."

Maya quirks an eyebrow. Jun nods, the recorder silent for once.

Maya Lin: (softly) "Don't burn yourself out, av. People need you whole."

You almost smile because you know she's right. You close your notebook with a light, decisive motion.

[Scene: The Tide Garden | Noon]
[Visual: Solar sails cast soft feathered shadows over terraces of salt-tolerant greens; sunlight filters through a thin, silvered sky]
[Sound: Gentle slosh of water against algae-streaked terraces; children's laughter somewhere beyond a mangrove clump]
[Music: A soft, lilting guitar — airy and measured]

At noon the Tide Garden is a breath you can take all the way in. Ori waits where a lattice of mangrove-rooted platforms meets a new patch of bio-concrete. He's in paint-splattered denim overalls; the welding goggles perched on his head tuck a shadow over his forehead like a private smile.

Ori Navarro: (grinning) "I brought you something to steal your heart and practical mind—one plank at a time."

He gestures to the prototype living wall: a textured lattice of roots and porous stone, tiny microgreens catching light like a constellation. The solar sails above it sway with a soft click, and the whole thing seems to breathe in time with the bay.

You run your fingers along a piece of bio-concrete — cool and rough, flecked with shell fragments. It smells of sea and wet clay and something sharp heaped onto it: the optimism of invention.

You: "It's… beautiful, Ori."

Ori's amber eyes light with mischief and something else you don't quite name. He steps close to steady a plank, and your hands find the same space for a moment. Skin to skin, the contact is small and ordinary and electric in a way that doesn't demand drama.

Ori Navarro: (softly) "You steady the line; I'll make it sing."

He tucks a sun-bleached topknot behind his ear, a habitual gesture that makes you thrifty with words. The garden's damp air presses warm against your face, and for a single measured second the shore feels like a private thing only you two are allowed to defend.

Ori: "I can scale this. Not by myself, but with volunteers and a few tweaks — lighter lattice here, water channels there. We can make it modular. Demonstrator in a month, public walkthrough after that."

You want to say yes to everything — to the way his plans feel like scaffolding for hope — and part of you is already calculating the hours, the materials, the way your name will be on the sign-up sheet.

You: "If we demonstrate it, Jun can back us with what he finds. Maya can mobilize. This could be the story we run, Ori — not VossCorp's sleek certainty, but a living thing that keeps people where they are."

Ori laughs, a small, half-lidded sound, then becomes deliberate.

Ori Navarro: "Promise me you won't carry all of this alone."

The plea isn't theatrical; it's a quiet thing that lands with the soft urgency of suturing. You feel your survivor's guilt gathering at the edges, a familiar itch that says you're the one who must hold every break. You look at him, at the way his hands are ink-stained and sure, and you see someone who builds to soothe a fear you both share.

You: "I won't promise to stop caring. But—I'll try not to let it hollow me out."

Ori's hand brushes yours again as he hands you a small test sample of porous stone. The contact is a deliberate, grounding act.

Ori Navarro: "Then let us be inefficient together. Build messy and beautiful—like the Tide Garden."

He keeps talking — about lighter anchors, about a public walkthrough designed like a neighborhood festival, about modular panels that residents can repair themselves. You answer; the conversation loops and returns to practicalities, which is how you know it's real.

Ori Navarro: (careful) "And if Calder shows up with his glass pier and investor charm, we already have people who can tell their stories. People don't talk contracts; they talk about the night the water lifted their roof. That's our leverage."

The sun slides past a thin cloud, and the light softens. There's a small, shared laugh at the edge of your mouth—an agreement born of tiredness and stubborn hope.

You feel a steadying momentum settle into the day. Tasks line up in your head: materials lists to finalize, a tentative date for the demonstrator, Jun's discreet investigation, Maya's door-knocking rounds. The tide garden hums around you — volunteers bent over planting, a child coaxing a crab into a net — small ecosystem of care and work.

Your survivor's guilt tugs, and you fold it into action: you sketch grant language on a wet napkin and tuck it into your notebook. You mark places where Old Mara's oral histories might be recorded to make the case personal. Everything you do feels like stringing bulbs along a pier at night: one small light at a time.

Ori watches you for a breath, eyes patient.

Ori Navarro: (quiet) "You don't have to carry our momentum alone, Ava."

You meet his gaze and, for a moment, the steady weight in your chest shifts. There's work to be done, yes — but also a hand beside yours, a presence that promises to take a board when you need to rest.

You feel, without melodrama, the day's arc bending toward possibility. The plan needs allies, resources, and a public narrative that makes the city's heart beat with your kind of solutions.

[Music: Strings linger, then fall to a gentle hum]

You fold your napkin, tighten the strap on your notebook, and feel the quiet resolve of someone who is not finished yet.

[Visual: A thin line of clouds gathers on the horizon — weather pending, but the air is still]
[Sound: Distant voices carrying plans; the soft clink of tools]

You stand at the edge of planning and action, the next steps forming like tide-lines traced in salt and ink. The day has given you hands to hold and people who will show up. That, for now, is enough to keep moving.
