[Scene: Ferryworks Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: Warm lamplight through portholes; rows of seedlings sway on recycled pallets. Metal workbenches cluttered with blueprints and soldering irons. Ori hunched over a half-welded planter shaped like a wing.]
[Music: Rousing, urgent BGM — strings and low percussion building]
[Sound: Solder hiss, the gull-call tinny through the hull, boots on wet metal]

You step in with the same small load of certainty you carry when plans are real things: paper edges softened by thumbprints, a list of volunteer names in the inside pocket. The Ferryworks smells of wet soil and hot metal—the raw, honest smell of things being made to live. Light slants across Ori's paint-smudged cuffs; the topknot at his crown catches a stray sun-bleached strand like a signal.

Silas slides past, quick and sure, algae filters looped into brackets with the sort of delighted concentration you remember having at twelve. Old Mara sits in a corner under a basket of halophytes, humming a low tide-vigil in a minor key. The sound holds the room steady while everything around it rushes.

You tighten the straps of your jacket and set your notebook beside a tray of sensor modules. The page beneath your palm is circled with tasks: volunteer schedules, tide windows, cadences for public demonstrations. The pilot is not theory anymore. It's a scaffold you can touch.

Ori Navarro: "Hey — you reading my weld marks or just praising my aesthetic choices?" (He grins, sparks flaring like small, obedient lightning.)

You: "Both. The wing actually looks like it could catch wind and seedlings, not just applause." (You reach out to tap a cooling seam; the metal is warm and honest under your fingertips.)

Ori wipes his hands on a rag and stands, the grin softening. "Good. If it can be stubborn and pretty, that's our manifesto."

Maya Lin arrives with a stack of folders and the ledger-face you know too well—exact, sharp corners. Her eyes flick to the schedule in your hand and then to the prototype with a warmth that has no time for theatrics.

Maya Lin: "We have three volunteer shifts, a school group for the noon walkthrough, and Jun's asking for a press window after the tide sensors update. Calder's people are already on-site with the cameras."

You: "Then we choreograph. Volunteers on the western terrace for planting; Silas leads the kids; Ori, you keep the annex demo for the press slot?" (Your voice speeds; there's no room for dithering.) "And Jun—"

Jun Park (leaning in, waterproof recorder hanging from his neck): "—has something for you. Redacted. In person."

[Sound: A metal clink; the hum of a generator deepens. The BGM lifts, heartbeat-quick.]

You move through the Ferryworks with a dozen tiny commands lodged between ribs: check the sensor calibration, double the algae filter tests, have Mara hold the tide vigil at quarter tide, and on it. People move because the pilot is a living thing and everyone knows how to feed it.

You and the team move the prototype to the Tide Garden on a trailer patched with composite panels. The living wall—their living wall—unfurls in sunlight: mangrove root clusters cradled in bio-concrete, winged planters anchored like small promises, solar sails folding shadow into motion. Children run along algae-streaked terraces, pockets of laughter like wind through reeds.

Old Mara: (softly, to no one and everyone) "Roots remember where the water goes. We give them room and they give us time."

The sensors sing. Salinity curves flatten; micro-readings show nutrients cycling cleanly. You taste victory in the air: chlorophyll, salt, the metallic tang of tools, the somewhere-hum of a future you can almost name.

Calder's cameras pivot; the lenses find faces—your neighbors planting, Jun snapping candid frames, Maya catching a child's hand steady on a plank. It's public relations gold: human stories braided to technical success.

Jun Park: (lowering his camera) "This will put pressure on the council. People see this, they will ask why the city isn't funding more of it."

Maya radiates satisfaction, but there is a crease you can't miss—it's the part of her ledger that remembers risk. She passes you a slim envelope; Jun's movement makes a soft sound that is almost apologetic.

Maya Lin: "Jun found a clause. Redacted lines, but there's a relocation clause. VossCorp's addendum—something about 'safety thresholds' that could trigger tenure reviews. It's vague." (She hands the paper across the board — the paper between your hands feels like it weighs more than the sensor modules.)

Your fingers hover. You read the addendum twice, then a third time. The legal language is polite and slippery: contingencies, thresholds, discretionary enforcement. The words don't scream—they whisper possibilities where a community might be uprooted because a number was crossed, because someone judged a threshold differently.

Hope tightens into a knot in your chest. But the knot is not fear alone: it's hot, quick, and alive. It wants to be turned, examined, unpinned. The pilot has shown what you can do. The living wall took. The sensors stabilized the salinity. The city watched. The stakes are simply higher.

Ori approaches, cheeks still ash-smudged, a small steel hinge clasped in his hand like an argument.

Ori Navarro: "We can bolt an annex to the wall. Autonomous, off-grid—art and function. If they try to control the narrative with clauses, they can't claim the same ground. Make something that refuses to be erased." (He says it as if it's obvious; the conviction in his voice is raw and infectiously bright.)

You: "An autonomous annex could show independence, but it could also be a provocation that gets Calder to pull support." (You fold the redacted page back onto itself.) "We need their money and their engineers for scale. Maya—" (You look at her; she meets you with that ledger-face again.)

Maya Lin: "Money that comes with a hidden hand isn't money. We need ironclad community control in the contract—tenure guarantees, defined thresholds with clear metrics and community oversight. No wiggle words."

Jun Park: (quietly, sharp) "I've seen clauses hide everything. Public pressure helps, but law holds. If we roll this out and someone points at a 'safety threshold,' families could be moved before the cameras stop smiling."

Old Mara: (humming softer) "Storms don't wait for ink to dry. But ink keeps promises remembered. Both are necessary."

Silas, who has been calibrating a filter, looks up with a patience older than his years. "We can finish the prototype tonight. If it's clean and shows independent function, it's harder to spin it as liability."

The conversation spirals — no, it accelerates — each argument layered with urgency. The room is small and full of people who are both tired and unstoppable. Voices overlap. You cut in, because someone must thread the needle.

You: "Ori, an annex is brave. It proves we can be autonomous. Maya, ironclad clauses are non-negotiable for tenure. Jun, we need your pressure timed with evidence. Silas, can you get a full performance cycle before dusk? Old Mara, can you hold the vigil tonight so we have community presence if anyone comes by?"

The list lands, and everyone moves to action. Very quickly—too quickly, in a way that feels like sprinting toward the tide line—the team divides into what needs to be done now versus what can wait for careful lawyering. The Ferryworks becomes a choreography of small, fierce tasks.

You stand between two very large truths: the pilot needs visibility, scale, and the tools a corporation can deliver; the pilot needs protections and definitions that will keep Saltway in place. Your chest lifts with a fierce, almost obscene joy—the prototype worked. It took. Your neighbors smiled while planting mangrove roots. That is evidence and it is proof of possibility. It is the thing that will make the city sit up.

But Jun slides another sheet across the bench. This one is smaller, marked in Jun's careful hand: "Redline suggestions." He looks at you as if he's offering both a gift and a gauntlet.

Jun Park: "You can take the quick win. Cameras, public credit, money to scale. But the addendum as written gives Calder discretionary power. We can push for redlines that make any 'safety threshold' community-defined and legally enforceable—but that will slow things, and they might walk."

You: (you can feel your pulse in your throat) "If they walk, we lose momentum. If we sign now and protect later, we might lose homes."

Ori steps closer, earnest and a little reckless. "If we can make the annex show the world we don't need them to own our fate—if the annex can be both art and an operational node—then we don't have to trust their language. We take the stage and the thing taking the stage is ours."

Maya's fingers drum the ledger. "You are imagining a public argument we may not win. Contracts bind reality for neighborhoods. We need ink that says: tenants of Saltway remain tenants, unless the community votes otherwise. Metrics for 'safe' must be mutually determined."

The BGM tightens to a single ascending line. You can hear the tide outside the Ferryworks like a metronome. The pilot's successes push like a tide up against a seawall of clauses. The room smells of solder and wet soil and the bright, stubborn heat of people deciding to protect what they love.

You close your eyes for a breath and see the faces in the Tide Garden when the sensors read green: a child leaning over a planter, Old Mara's fingers wrapped around a root, Silas's grin when a filter cycles clean. The camera catches those faces now; it will be harder to argue against them later. That thought is a small, quick, incendiary hope.

You open your eyes and know—acutely—that the next sentence you speak will change the pilot's shape, and perhaps the shape of what you and Ori are building together.

You step forward, voice pitched to hold the room.

You: "We can't let this pilot be a showpiece only. We need scale, and we need protections. We need to decide where we bargain and where we refuse. Tell me—how do we move forward?"
