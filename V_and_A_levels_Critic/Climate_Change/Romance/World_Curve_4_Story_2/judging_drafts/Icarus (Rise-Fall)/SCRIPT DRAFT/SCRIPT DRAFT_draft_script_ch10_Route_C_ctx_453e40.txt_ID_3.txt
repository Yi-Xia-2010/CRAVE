You chosen the quiet route. You closed the door on the public meeting with a small, decisive motion—no press, no Calder's polished handshake, no paper promises that smell like the city beyond. That choice is a small rebellion; it shivers in your chest like a battery charging.

The Ferryworks smells the way it always does after rain: wet soil and solder, old wood and the bright metallic tang of fresh-cut circuits. Lantern light pools along the workbenches and turns the spray of salt on the benches to flecks of glitter. You feel the thrum of the place under your boots, the ferry's old hull answering the tide like a patient lung.

You set your notebook on a paint-splattered table and run your thumb along the tide-chart stamp on the cover. Ori is under the lamp across from you, sleeves pushed to his elbows, hands moving with a speed that looks almost private—quick fingers grafting circuit fragments into a cluster of compact sensors. He hums something like a half-song, half-instruction set. His welding goggles are shoved up into his hairband; a smear of copper dust marks his cheek like war paint.

Ori Navarro: "You look like you swallowed a council agenda whole. Bad for digestion." (grins, then his hands don't stop moving)

You: "Bad for sleep, worse for my back. But better than swallowing the sea." (you tap the notebook) "These sketches—cove A, the lee of the old crabbing pier. Mangrove plugs where the current eddies. If we can get the algae filters balanced, the bay will take less of the storm's first teeth."

Ori: "Algae will laugh at the teeth. That's the joy. Plus—" (he lifts a bent, polished metal shard—it glints like a tiny wing) "—solar wings. Pretty and useful. You're allowed to feel smug about designing something that looks like a bird."

You watch him fit the sensor into the housing; his hands are so steady you almost forget the politics that pushed you into secret-night builds. There's tenderness in his speed—an intimacy to the way he treats the fragile things that will stand between Saltway and displacement.

Silas lopes in, hoodie wet at the edges, cheeks flushed from the cold. He carries a crate of knobby planters—mangrove-root forms half-grown in reclaimed pots—and drops it with a proud clatter.

Silas: "We scored tide-grade mesh. Old Mara said to tell you she approves. Said if this works, she's beating you both at the harvest." (smirks)

You: "Old Mara and her elbows of steel. Good news." (you lift one planter, feeling the weight of mud and root) "We need volunteers tonight to stitch the mounting frames. Quiet hands only—no one who needs their name in a press release."

Silas: "Quiet hands, loud work. Got it." (he gets to it, dirt under his nails, a grin that is all fearless and young)

A couple of neighbors slip in—Marta from the fish stalls with a battered trowel, and Deon, who can solder faster than the ferry's own clockwork. You hand out roles like a captain passing star charts. Everyone's faces are lit from below by the lanterns, shadows cutting their cheekbones into maps of decisions.

You: (softly, to the group) "This is one cove. One proof. If we show the city what a living seawall looks like—if it holds—then we have leverage. If not, we own the failure. We don't hide the consequences, we share them, but not the corporate strings."

Marta: (hesitates, voice small) "And if VossCorp walks by the pier and sees our sails? They own parts of the channel now, right? They'll—"

Ori: (cuts in, gentle but firm) "Then we pack. We move. We keep what we can. The point is not permanence from day one—it's a model. Proof. Beauty with function. They can't argue with a cove that stops swallowing houses."

You feel the room tilt with that compact logic. It is blunt and exact: concealment buys you time and autonomy but limits the scale of your shield. Your chest fills with that peculiar mixture of elation and pressure—hope braided with responsibility. The arousal of the plan accelerates; there is no slow simmer tonight. It's all quick-coal heat.

You trace integration lines in the margins of the notebook: where the planters thread into living walls, how the mangrove plugs will anchor into modified pilings, the geometry of a solar sail that folds when the tide spikes. You scribble shorthand notes about community tenure markers—small plates that can be bolted and unbolted, easily visible, hard to argue against once the plants have rooted and the cove stops eroding.

Ori leans over your shoulder, breath warm against the nape of your neck. He nudges a loose strand of your hair back with the tip of a greasy finger and smudges a streak of copper across your forehead. You blink, and for a second the strain of leadership evaporates into a simpler, immediate thing: two people at a bench, making something that will stand.

Ori Navarro: "You make the best tidal diagrams. You should charge the city a royalty." (he wiggles his eyebrows; then softer) "Or you should charge me. I accept barter: coffee, paint, kisses."

You: (a small laugh) "Kisses are a non-refundable currency." (you press the heel of your hand to his shoulder, meaning steady. You're not sure if you're buying time or buying trust.)

Ori: "Good. Consider us solvent." (he lifts a sensor, tests it. The LED brightens; the cluster emits a clean, steady chirp)

Silas: "You two are gross, but I'm in." (he grins; another laugh breaks the tension, the room loosening like a knot)

The prototype takes shape across two tables and the ferry's aft deck: winged solar sails—thin, translucent films patterned like bird feathers—folded into compact booms; planters that will act as breakwaters and nurseries for salt-tolerant grasses; algae-filter conduits that pulse water through mucilaginous beds to strip turbidity; sensor arrays that will measure salinity, current, and stress on the structure. The aesthetic is deliberate: you're building something the city will want to look at, because people protect what they love.

Orders come from you and bounce back faster than you expect. The group is small but practiced; every hand knows a knot or a solder joint. You are riding a rush—each micro-decision folds into the next, each solved problem releasing the pressure to new velocity. The arousal curve climbs: the room is breathless, concentrated.

Outside, the sea is a black sheet with moonflecks. You carry the finished module like a small, promising animal and set it onto the skiff waiting in the cove's lee. The water rubs against the hull, swallowing the sound of the city a little. The ferry hum is lower here; the night is a patient witness.

Ori stands close as you lace the bolts. He doesn't offer to take over; he just stays near, like an anchor. You feel the warmth of his coat against your forearm. Moonlight pools on the metal.

Ori Navarro: (whispers) "This is beautiful. You know that? It's almost obscene how... beautiful this is."

You: "It's obedient to the tide." (you tighten the last bolt) "If beauty can stop erosion, I'm ready to charge people for peanut butter sandwiches to fund it."

Ori: "I'll take sandwich profits. You focus on the speeches." (he grins, then soberly) "If this works—Ava, if this works—we can go further. Not just one cove. We can show them a new vernacular."

You hesitate—there's that caveat folded into every midnight build. Concealment keeps VossCorp's hands off your nails, but it also means you have limited copies, limited reach. If the model fails, the visible damage is contained—but so is the gain.

You lean into him, into the gravity of the moment. He smells like solder and sea-salt and the faint citrus of his lunch. You brush a thumb across the salt crust at his temple and kiss it off—an oddly domestic, intimate thing in the face of mechanical possibility.

The prototype's core hums under the moonlight—low, even, a small engine of hope. The lights on the sensor cluster pulse in a slow syncopation, as if breathing. Around you, the crew laughs, tucks hems, checks wire runs. The night tightens into a crystalline focus; everything that was diffuse in the day sharpens into a single line of action.

You feel your heartbeat in your throat. The high arousal becomes physical—your hands quicken, your speech shortens, your steps become deliberate. There is sweet panic and an exultant kind of courage: the ocean is vast, but tonight your small number stands between its teeth and your neighbors' doors.

You step back to watch the module float in the small bay, mangrove planters bobbing, solar sails folded like a sleeping greatwing. The cove breathes differently already—less hungry at the edges. The algae filters murmur as they begin to move water, and a spume of microbubbles rises and scatters in lantern-light.

Silas: (voice caught) "It's... holding."

You: "Hold on to that word." (your voice is thick, small with relief and gathering excitement) "Hold on to it."

Ori reaches for your hand, and your fingers lock without a thought. You don't separate the personal and the civic tonight—they braid. It feels both reckless and necessary.

The moment stretches into something incandescent: the module's gentle rocking, the collective quiet of people who made something that moves, the salt on your lips from the brief kiss, the way Ori's thumb rubs across your knuckle as if erasing doubt. You want to shout. You want to call Old Mara and Calder both and shove this tiny, living thing under their noses. You want to sleep and then run the tests all over again at once.

Instead, you stand in the dark with lanterns and laughter, the Ferryworks' hull behind you, the bay cradling your experiment. You tuck one hand into your jacket where the notebook lives and trace the faint tide-stamp with a fingertip.

A thought slices through the warmth like a cold current—if the prototype fails, the consequences are layed on you and your people alone. No Calder to fix it with spreadsheets. No press conference to absorb the recoil. The secret buys autonomy and risks isolation.

Yet even that fear tastes like something you chose: responsibility. You chose to be the person who does the quiet, dangerous work. That choice thrumms under your skin, and for the first time in a long string of public debates and compromises, the panic that rises is tempered by a luminous, fierce hope.

You look at Ori. He meets you with that amber, open gaze; it's bright and steady as a flare. In it you find a thousand little reasons to keep going.
