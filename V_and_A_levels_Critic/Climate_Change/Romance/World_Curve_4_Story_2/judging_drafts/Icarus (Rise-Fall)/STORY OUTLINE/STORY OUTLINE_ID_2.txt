## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
Harborlight is a near-future coastal metropolis remade by water: neighborhoods glide between land and sea, cranes and glass towers puncture a braided skyline, and boardwalks stitch communities together where streets used to be. Power is a contested resource—corporate capital builds engineered certainty while residents jury-rig living infrastructure from salvage and biology. Politics oscillate between technocratic promises of large-scale redevelopment and grassroots networks arguing for residency rights and distributed resilience. Religion and ritual persist as neighborhood lore rather than centralized dogma—Old Mara’s stories of past tides are as sacred as municipal ordinances. Class lines map onto elevation: high investors occupy glass piers with hydrophobic suits and private levees; the Saltway Quarter is the braided, salt-streaked commons of artisans, elders, and makers. Conflicts center on tenure, safety, and the moral cost of displacement. Geography folds mangrove terraces, glass piers, and ferry-labs into a single city where weather acts like a character—fog, sudden squalls, and golden lulls that linger in memory. Atmosphere is melancholic but resilient: film-grain evenings, watercolor light over rusted metal, and the smell of brine and smoke.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I live and breathe the Saltway Quarter: I am Ava Maren, a pragmatic idealist who translated coastal ecology into community defense. A corporate titan, Calder Voss, proposes VossArc, a sleek, centralized redevelopment promising engineered certainty. Ori Navarro—the warm, irreverent maker I’ve grown close to—urges riskier, more beautiful community-built experiments that could scale. I organize residents, the Tide Garden becomes a symbol, and initially we see rapid success: pilot living seawalls, salvage synthesis, and local victories that charm the city and draw investor attention. That success culminates in a public showcase that seems to swing political favor our way (the Icarus ascent). Then, crisis: exposed clauses, accelerated timelines for VossArc, and a storm-triggered failure reveal systemic fragility. Choices about compromise, civil disobedience, and love determine outcomes: some paths end in community triumph but at personal cost; others in corporate victory and displacement; some end with a journalistic exposé that forces systemic change. Emotionally, the arc is Icarus—rising hope and visible success followed by a steep fall that tests relationships, ethics, and the future of Harborlight.
