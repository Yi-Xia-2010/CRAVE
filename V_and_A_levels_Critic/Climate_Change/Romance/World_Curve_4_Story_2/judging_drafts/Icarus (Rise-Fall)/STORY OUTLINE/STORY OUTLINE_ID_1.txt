## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
Harborlight in the 2040s is a city rewritten by water: neighborhoods have become tidal districts where boardwalks thread between stilts, pontoons, and reclaimed gardens. The political structure is fractured—municipal authorities broker emergency contracts with private firms (most notably VossCorp) while community councils and informal collectives hold civic life together in low-lying quarters. Wealth and influence concentrate at the Glass Pier and corporate waterfront; Saltway Quarter sits under the shadow of cranes and investor banners but radiates local governance, mutual aid, and artisanal adaptation. Culture values salvage, craft, oral memory, and public rituals (tide-day repairs, seed-swaps). Religion is personal and syncretic—practical rites to the sea and storms sit beside civic petitions. Class hierarchy cleaves on tenure: those with legal title and capital relocate to hardened arcologies; those without improvise resilience from driftwood, algae, and circuits. Conflict centers on scale: high-capital engineered certainty versus distributed, ecologically integrated adaptation. Geography blends dense glass towers with mangrove-root terraces; the weather is an active character—fog, sudden squalls, relentless salt, and washed gold after storms. Atmosphere is melancholic resilience: salt-streaked murals, film-grain light through solar sails, and the constant smell of brine and fermenting hope.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Ava Maren, returning organizer and coastal ecologist whose life is braided with Saltway Quarter’s survival. The story thrusts me between three pragmatic paths when Calder Voss offers VossArc: negotiate with Calder for a public-private pilot; double down on a community-led living seawall and escalate grassroots action; or pursue legal and media exposure to force corporate accountability. Each path rises quickly—momentary triumphs, public praise, prototype reveals, or political leverage—fueling hope and intimate proximity with Ori Navarro, the maker whose clever hands and laugh become the center of my days. But the Icarus arc governs us: the rise is bright and intoxicating—pilots unveiled, funders convinced, prototypes birthed—then the crash follows: political compromise that displaces neighbors, an unprecedented storm that tests the promises, or a public backlash that fractures trust. Choices shape the scale of loss and the fate of our romance: sometimes we hold each other through retreat; sometimes we split, hearts rent by duty; sometimes exposure forces systemic change but at personal cost. Through boardwalk meetings, ferrywork labs, and a storm that makes the city reckon, the narrative tests whether love can be a refuge or an accelerant when the sea demands sacrifice.
