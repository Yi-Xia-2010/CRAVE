### DRAFT
# Route: Additional Route


[Scene: Glass Pier | Dusk — Press Night]
[Visual: The living mural ignites—seaglass and reclaimed circuits woven into a sprawling tapestry of light across the pier. Small speakers are embedded at intervals; panels shift like scales.]
[Music: A swelling, urgent synth; a high metronomic beat like a heartbeat under it]
[Sound: Camera shutters, murmured gasps, the metallic rasp of a megawatt crane in the distance; a child's recorded voice threading through the speakers]
[Visual: Flood lamps throw hard edges; lanterns from the Tide Garden bleed warm halos into the cold corporate light beyond.]

You remember the exact instant you said yes—the little nod you gave Ori beneath the sailcloth, the way your fingers tightened on the notebook—and now the city is watching what you let loose.

The mural breathes. Tiny recorded mouths—neighbors, children, Old Mara telling a reef story—rise and fall on the speakers in a cadence that feels like a pulse: "My pa taught me to mend nets…", "When the water came, we moved the stove…", "We don't want a fence. We want a home." Voices thread over one another and stop a thousand deadlines in the city for a single, shuddering beat.

You can smell salt and solder, coffee and the acrid tang of stage smoke. Up close, the metal feels warm from the work-lights; the seaglass trembles in your palm like something alive. Cameras pivot; a drone hovers at your shoulder like a second gull. You are adrenaline and lullaby at once—hands raw from wiring, throat tight with the speech you almost gave.

Ori leans in, grin gone soft. He is elbow-deep in the tapestry, fingers moving with the practiced patience that once welded ship rails. His voice is close, a warm thrum against the noise.

Ori Navarro: (low, urgent) "You ready for them to see us? For them to hear them?"

You: "They'll listen. For a moment." (Your voice is small and precise, like a well-cut rope.) "But listening isn't the same as listening to keep us."

Ori: (a breath, half-sad) "Then we'll make them want to keep us. Make it impossible to look away."

You laugh because you have to, a sound that cracks. The laugh is a small release; it sparks a round of applause from one side of the pier and a click of skepticism from the other—the investors’ tent—where LED logos flicker like distant stars.

[Visual: A bank of screens lights up at the edge of the pier—live feeds, a curved LED displaying a polished face. Calder Voss appears on the screen, framed in cool light; beneath him, a ticker scrolls calm assurances.]
[Sound: A corporate PA, then Calder's voice—smooth, measured—over the public feed.]

Calder Voss: (on screen, composed) "Beautiful. Heartfelt. Harborlight has always been a place of stories. But stories do not stop storms. We are proposing safeguards that will ensure lives are secured, not sentimentalized."

The crowd exhales, some in indignation, some in agreement—emotion rearranged into policy language. You feel the air tilt, the tide of opinion changing with the ease of a practiced hand.

A reporter shouts a question you cannot hear. Calder smiles with a measured kindness and lets the implication slide into the broadcast: sentimental communities make humane headlines; humane headlines make efficient solutions seem necessary.

From somewhere behind the cranes, a muted phone buzzes with investor messaging—"cute," "socially resonant," "leverage for relocation." The word lands like a stone in a quiet pond and ripples outward.

It starts as theater—then it becomes leverage. The mural is republished across feeds: snippets of children, the slow hands of Old Mara, a shot of your notebook clutched to your hip. The city pauses; for several minutes the press cadence stutters and a different kind of attention blooms.

A man in a suit claps politely near the investor tent. Across from him, a younger executive scrolls through responses, lips pursed, and taps a short memo with a thumb. That memo moves faster than the tide.

You feel it—the change in wind—before you read the words. Security teams begin to fan out. Not the polite municipal stewards you’re used to, but firm, efficient people in neutral jackets, radios warm against their shoulders. They tape off a perimeter with yellow, their movements precise; they do not speak loudly. The sound is the sound of containment.

Ori sees it too. He tugs a strand of hair from his forehead and his fingers are suddenly small. He crosses the slats of the pier to you, voice low and taut.

Ori Navarro: (half-joking, edge showing) "They like the art. They love the image. They'll say it's why we need the model."

You: "They're framing us as an object, Ori. A reason, not an owner." (You taste the words—bitter, necessary.) "We needed tenure, not an exhibit."

Ori: (forcing a laugh, then softer) "We made it feel like home. That's not nothing."

You open your mouth and close it. The speakers play a child's laugh; someone in the crowd sobs quietly. Everything is amplified into a thousand rooms where it will become something else.

People shout—some your name, some orders. A clipboard is held toward you; a badge is shown; the officer's voice is a sanded kind. You feel the weight of every family who might be forced later to lift their plants and leave. For a heartbeat it seems you could stop the rope. The memo reaches the hands that matter. It is not a broadcast piece; it is a quiet authorization—"increase relocation efforts"—the kind of document that never screams but executes. You don't see the signature on the tablet; you feel the consequence of the sentence threading through the pier like a current.

Calder's PR pivots immediately, economically: aesthetic admiration reframed as rationale. He speaks of "efficiency," of "protective corridors," of "the responsibility to save lives." On-screen, his jaw tightens for a fraction of a second—just enough to tell you that his kindness is an instrument.

Calder Voss: (on screen, measured) "We must do more than honor memory. We must guarantee survival. That will require bold choices."

The word "bold" is a blade. Cameras cut to your mural; then they cut to a corporate blueprint overlay, a proposed levee line annotated with rectangles labeled 'Relocation Zones'. The juxtaposition is clean. The narrative edits itself: from "look at these people" to "these people need protecting from themselves."

You move through the pier as if underwater: light refracted, voices muffled. Neighbors fold up their planters, kiss their hands to fragile leaves, tuck seeds into pockets. The communal life you built in shared work tangles into shopping lists and legal forms in the hands of clerks you will never meet.

Ori catches your sleeve and — for the first time tonight — he cannot find a joke that fits. He presses something into your palm: a small seaglass charm threaded with a salvaged circuit fragment, the two elements soldered together with an impatient tenderness.

Ori Navarro: (no words, only his hands)  
[Visual: He holds your gaze until you take it. His thumb presses a small warm dent into the glass.]

You close your fingers over it. The charm is heavier than it looks. You feel the texture of the wire, a faint heat from where they soldered it minutes ago. It is both a thing of beauty and a talisman against forgetting. You do not know whether it's for luck, guilt, or promise; it does not matter which.

You: (soft, shredded) "We made something real."

Ori: (voice tight) "We did. They just... turned it into a reason."

You try to put the grievance elsewhere—into Calder's smile, into the investors' memos—but it lands in your chest like a stone. The murmur of the crowd swells and, for a terrifying second, you imagine all the mural's small recordings as driftwood: pretty to see, useless to stop a tide.

Calder's feed cuts to a live interview. He speaks the language of safety and inevitability, his sentences clean and unassailable. You feel the reporters' questions sharpen toward policy; the mural becomes a symbol in a headline that will justify moving people for "their own good."

A young security supervisor steps toward the crowd and reads an official-sounding line into a portable mic: "For public safety, this area will be regulated per municipal directive." His voice is calm; the tape is being rolled tighter.

Someone cries—soft, broken—behind you. Old Mara's shawl is a small sea on her lap as she tucks a string of beads into a pouch. Jun is there somewhere in the press crush, camera steady, mouth a thin line. There is an ache in your throat that tastes like both salt and old blood.

You move along the mural's edge. The lights are still bright, the speakers still playing neighbors' voices, but the rhythm is fractured now—burst into segments by legalese and news bites. The visual that was meant to humanize has been folded into a case study for why the very people it portrays should be managed.

You think, with a cold clarity, of tenure forms, of councilrooms, of lawyers with polite faces. You think of the years of careful work, the meetings with boards, the promises you coaxed from weary officials—how fragile those agreements feel under the weight of an economy that prefers a single engineered solution to the messy, costly justice of community rights.

Ori watches you watch the pier being sectioned off. He reaches to take your hand and for once does not pat the small of your back with a joke. Instead, he speaks softly, the way one might speak to a child stepping into a puddle too deep.

Ori Navarro: (quiet) "Whatever happens—this mattered. To them. To us."

You look at him, and something in you unravels and resettles. You want to argue—about strategy, about the next move, about whether to fight in a courtroom or in the streets—but your voice is a thread pulled taut.

You: (a hard, cold laugh) "It matters and they'll still use it to move us."

Ori: (silence, then) "Then we keep making things that matter. We keep putting our work where it can be seen. We don't give them the language to erase us."

You feel the truth of both words like two sides of a horizon. Neither will stop the memo already signed. Neither will undo the yellow tape being clipped into place.

[Visual: Security tapes a final loop. A small child reaches through and runs fingers along the underside of the mural; a guard gently pulls her back. The cameras still hover, hungry for the last image.]
[Sound: The speakers play Old Mara's voice—"We are water people."—and someone in the crowd begins to hum.]

You step away from the tape. Your boots make a soft slap on the wet boards. The charm heavy in your palm is a small, private proof that something true was made tonight. The lights glitter on the glass like a field of caught stars, and for a second the beauty burns with a clean pain.

You want to carry every person on your back. You want to haul the memo into the light and show its seams. You want to fold the cranes into paper boats and set them adrift. Instead you walk.

The pier narrows, and beyond it the glass of the corporate tents reflects a sky thick with the promise of another storm. The mural plays on behind you, voices spilling into a world that will choose what to keep and what to catalog.

Ori falls into step beside you without asking. He does not speak for a long time. When he finally says something, his voice is small and steady, as if testing an old shore.

Ori Navarro: "Come with me tomorrow. We'll salvage what we can, catalog the stories, put them where they can't be turned into props."

You: (you nod) "Yes." (The word is both a promise and a surrender.) "We'll make records. We'll keep the names."

He squeezes your hand—the pressure neither here nor there. For a moment you allow yourself to imagine a different ending: tide turned by community law, Calder stepping back, tenure security assured. The image is bright, impossible, like a lighthouse in fog.

Then the memo presses its shadow across your memory. Tonight's applause becomes tomorrow's justification. The city's appetite is not for restitution; it is for an image that can be managed.

You stop near the end of the pier and turn to look back. The mural gleams; someone has draped a length of plain white cloth across one corner—an attempt, perhaps, at reclaiming the image. The lights catch on the cloth and for an unbearable second it looks as if the community has hung a flag.

Ori presses the seaglass charm into your palm once more. His face is close; you see the outline of his mouth setup for a joke that does not come.

He murmurs, almost to himself, almost to you:

Ori Navarro: "I wish... I wish it had been enough."

You swallow. The word 'enough' lands like a verdict.

You fold the charm into your pocket and feel its shape—proof, memory, weight. You let your fingers close around it until the metal cools.

You walk away from the mural. Behind you the speakers keep speaking, and Calder's face fades back to the screens where it will be edited, quoted, and republished. The security tape flutters faintly; the cranes keep casting long patient shadows.

You walk into the rain that comes without drama—just a steady, thin curtain that makes everything look like water-color blurring at the edges. Your jacket soaks and the salt prickles on your skin. You think of all the small lives folded up tonight, the plants bagged like apologies, the plans that will need rewrites.

You will not say the word 'defeat' aloud. It will not help. But you will hold the memory of the mural like a ledger—evidence of what people can make when they are seen. You will keep it in the pocket of your jacket, pressed against the charm and the notebook and the notes that smell like tide.

[Visual: Camera pulls back slowly; the pier recedes, the mural's lights dim in the growing rain, Calder's screens blink away into the corporate night. A lone gull cuts the frame once, then is gone.]
[Music: A single, low chord that does not resolve—sustain and fade into the sound of rain and distant thunder.]

You step off the pier and onto the boardwalk. Behind you, under the glow that still holds for others, the mural plays on—beautiful, persuasive, and ultimately reshaped into a reason to move the things it had tried to protect.

[Visual: Fade to black]
[Music: Fade Out]
