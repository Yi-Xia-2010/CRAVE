# Route: Additional Route
# Context: 601bcb

[Scene: Glass Pier | Late Afternoon → Dusk]
[Visual: The pier stretches like a blade into a clinical horizon. Work-lights throw white pools over polished scaffolding; corporate banners snap in a wind that smells faintly of diesel and ozone. Your mural—an arcing patchwork of reclaimed circuit fragments and seaglass—hangs between pylons, backlit by battery-powered lanterns and the blue wash of a news van's floodlight.]
[Sound: A low, insistent hum of generators; reporters' murmurs; the speakers woven into the mural catch and replay a child's voice, then another, then a chorus like tide-rippled memory.]
[Music: Tense, urgent strings building beneath the noise]

You tell yourself this was the point of going quiet with Ori: to make something undeniable where the numbers and memos could not. You remember the long nights on the Ferryworks, solder smoke and salt on your sleeves, Ori's hands stained with algae and solder. You remember the way he insisted the story had to be human—small speakers, clipped tape of neighbors telling where they had learned to swim, where they had learned to fish, where their aunt kept a tin of spices through three floods. You remember the weight of that decision: to show, to place vulnerability where it could be filmed.

Now the show is live.

Ori Navarro is there—close, a shadowed figure against the mural, welding goggles pushed up like a crown. He catches your eye and grins, and for a instant the world narrows to the familiar cadence of his smile. Then the camera shifts and the city widens again.

Ori Navarro: "They can take our permits, take our plans—can't patent a grandmother's laugh."

Ava Maren [Thinking]: "He says it like a joke, but your throat tightens. Laughter and grief live in the same place on you lately."

Ori Navarro [Softening]: "Hey. It's not nothing. Look—"

Ava Maren: "I know it's not nothing." 

You step closer to the mural. The speakers crackle; a little boy's voice says, "My boat is named Lila." The sound slices clean through the press's chatter.

> Choice B: "Let Ori take the first words"
[Outcome A: You walk a few paces into the light, paper script folded in your hand. The camera finds you; the crowd hushes. Your voice shakes, but your words land—short, true. Faces soften. For three heartbeats you think we've done it.]
[Outcome B: You stay behind him and let Ori step into the glare. He jokes, he slips into charm, and the room leans in—he makes them feel it. You feel proud and strangely hollow, as if the feeling belongs to him and to the moment, not to the tenure you were trying to secure.]

Reporters move like tide-lines, searching for an angle. Someone asks for a soundbite about tenure, about legal safeguards. A camera's lens reflects you back—a fractured self: determined, salt-streaked, oddly small under the floodlight.

A young reporter with a damp notepad: "Will this—this living sculpture—be a legal argument against displacement? Will you sign any public partnership?"

Ava Maren [Measured]: "This is a people's archive—not a pause on policy. We want tenure. We want enforceable rights tied to these designs. We are not asking for charity."

Reporters' heads tilt; a man with a pressed coat—the kind of man who sells reassurance in tidy percentages—makes a quick note. The tone is good. The footage is beautiful: sunlight caught in seaglass, children giggling on the fringe of the crowd. For all its fragile eloquence, the piece flattens into images easy to bracket in a longer corporate narrative.

The press cycle loves a human face; it also loves a simple solution.

Someone taps your shoulder and points. Calder Voss appears on the far pier—staged three stories up on a temporary glass rig, polished and practiced. The cameras swing. You feel the blood drain to the precise edge of your wrists.

Calder Voss [On the live mic]: "What Saltway shows us today is the very reason we must accelerate robust, scalable protection. Small, beautiful gestures tug at the heart—but our city needs certainty: engineered flood mitigation, predictably maintained, accessible economically to all. We will partner to ensure communities survive with dignity."

Ava Maren [Internal]: "He says 'partner' like a promise, like a contract. His voice folds the mural into a pretext for his project: efficiency reframed as compassion."

Ori Navarro's jaw tightens. He leans close enough that the smell of solder and lemon oil from his satchel hits you.

Ori Navarro: "He calls it 'partnering' again. They give them a parasol and then tell them it's a roof."

Ava Maren: "He makes theater of our work to sell his certainty." 

You keep your voice low, but it is hard as a plank. "We built this to be our voice, not his backdrop."

Ori Navarro [Hands flexing]: "You want to speak up—say it. Or you want me to burn the feed down with charm and song? I can—" He forces a laugh like a lever. "No. That was a joke. Bad timing."

Ava Maren: "Not a joke." 

You swallow. "We have to do something. That phrase—'accessible economically'—what does that translate to? Displacement dressed as protection."

He looks away, and for a fraction of a second his face is unreadable—close to the old irritation he hides behind mischief and projects. He wants to fix things with objects: a mock floodgate here, a buoyant planter there. He hates the slow bureaucratic erosion where clauses and footnotes make hearts and houses disappear.

Ori Navarro [Quiet]: "I know. I see it. But right now they're watching the mural. The footage—if we can get Jun on this, if Jun publishes the memos—"

Ava Maren: "Jun doesn't have the memos. He has leads. We don't control Jun's arc either." 

The crowd shifts; someone nearby folds a planter. The sound is small and granular—like someone folding a letter into silence.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Interrupt Calder with a public call"
> Choice B: "Record Calder quietly and let the footage speak later"
[Outcome A: You step forward and raise your hand. The reporter nearest you flashes a light. 'Mr. Voss,' you shout, voice cracking, 'will you sign a legally binding community tenure clause—today—rather than speaking in abstractions?' The rig goes still; Calder smiles, answers a PR-polished rebuke. The exchange is viral for a day, then edited, then softened.]
[Outcome B: You hold your recorder low and stay out of the shot. You watch his smile and file it away, a slow, cold thing in your pocket—evidence to be used like a scalpel, not a blowtorch.]

The mural does its work. Neighbors weep into each other's sleeves. Children dance under strings of recycled LEDs. For a breath, the world tilts toward tenderness. A mayoral aide steps forward to speak, eyes wet in the camera glare; a television host calls it a 'charming resistance.' The word "charming" stings like salt.

But while the cameras find faces, the city bureaucracy moves in a different, quieter register.

A security team in tactical vests tethers a perimeter. Young men in crisp uniforms tape off side-alleys and fold up planters with efficient, practiced hands. A foreman—too middle-aged to be idealistic, too young to remember Old Mara's first flood—passes a slim folder to the man with the pressed coat. You catch the edge of the folder: a red-stamped memo, the kind that authorizes "accelerated relocation protocols" under the banner of 'public safety.'

Ava Maren [Breathless]: "They've moved—already."

Ori Navarro [Rasp]: "Quiet memos. They do those things in the paperwork, then film the kindness."

Ava Maren: "We made a thing of beauty and the city used it to say 'see how placid they are.' They won't push back when the notice comes because they already imagined us as quaint."

Ori Navarro clamps his mouth closed. His fingers find yours—callused, warm—and squeeze. For a second the world is only the small, immediate tether between your palms.

Ori Navarro [Soft]: "I'm sorry."

Ava Maren [Hard]: "Sorry doesn't stop a notice in a mailbox."

He flinches, then pulls a small object from his palm-pocket and presses it into yours: a seaglass charm, smoothed by tide and time, threaded on fraying twine. No words—only the weight of it: cool, reliable, bright. The charm is an old habit between you, an oath without forms.

Ava Maren: The glass is heavier than it looks; it catches the floodlight and throws a tiny, stubborn green back at you.

Ori Navarro [Laugh that feels like a sob]: "We tried—God, we tried."

Ava Maren: "It feels like not enough."

Ori Navarro: "Sometimes enough is a pile of stones the sea can't take in one night." He looks at the mural, at the taped planters being folded, at the faces stacked with fatigue. "Other times—other times you need law, Ava. You need paper with teeth."

Ava Maren: "And when the law is written behind closed doors and smiles, teeth don't mean a thing."

He opens his mouth, wants to argue, to turn this into a strategy session. But around you, the mood fractures into action: people packing, a councilman giving an interview about 'managed retreat,' a construction drone making a slow surveillance arc above the pier. The press will run yesterday's human interest—our mural, our laughter—and the memos will be footnotes in last night's evening scroll.

Calder Voss's smile blooms across every screen again. He speaks of 'efficiency' and 'scale' as if they are virtues not weapons.

Calder Voss [Over the pier speakers, calm]: "We owe it to our citizens to move decisively. Scale prevents catastrophe. We will fund relocation packages with dignity."

Ava Maren [Cold]: "Dignity in a relocation packet is not tenure."

Ori Navarro: "He knows what he's doing. He's rebranding displacement as care. It slides down easier that way."

Ava Maren: "And by the time they realize, it's law."

The very-high pressure of everything converges into that sound: the rustle of tarps, the clip of a camera shutter, the distant rumble of cranes being ordered to wake. Your chest feels hollow and full at once—like a bell before it rings, about to propel every note into air.

You move through the pier in a blur—speakers playing neighbor stories as a soundtrack for people folding their lives into parcels. Old Mara's shawl, folded with care; a child's favorite boat slotted into a foam case. Someone presses a paper to your palm—a notice, stamped, official. You read the details with a clarity so sharp it hurts: timelines, compensation terms, a clause that allows expedited access to property for "safety retrofitting" under the city's emergency powers.

Ava Maren [To Ori]: "We built an archive. They built a narrative."

Ori Navarro: "We held up a mirror. They bought the reflection."

Ava Maren: "It's not over—" 

You find yourself lying to steady him and yourself. "We can still press this. Jun—if he gets the memos—"

Ori Navarro [Cutting in]: "We didn't take the public path. We wanted proof first. Now the proof is a photo-op."

He looks at you, and for a second the usual Ori—inventor, irreverent and bright—peels away to show a man who knows he is partly to blame for the timing of all this. He is capable and culpable in the same breath.

Ori Navarro: "I thought if we showed them enough beauty, it would make them stop."

Ava Maren: "Beauty doesn't stop bylaws."

The voices around you thin into a chorus of small betrayals and necessities: a neighbor thanking you with trembling hands, a volunteer handing you a thermos, a child asking where their boat will sleep. Your script, your protests, your speakers—everything that felt like a firewall against displacement now feels like honey poured over steel; it glitters and it doesn't change the metal's course.

You walk away from the mural. The speakers keep playing—your neighbors' voices looping, every phrase a pocket of longing you can't stitch into policy. The light softens; the pier loses the harsh blue that flattered Calder's image. Security teams finish taping their lines and walk away without looking back. The city will call this a transition carried out with care. You will call it a removal dressed in ceremony.

Ori Navarro catches up to you and, without ceremony, simply presses his forehead to yours for a few seconds. No grand promises. No plans. A small, human punctuation.

Ori Navarro [Whisper]: "Whatever comes, I'm with you in the work."

Ava Maren [Internal]: "The sentence should tether and steady you, but it also admits a limit—'in the work' is not 'in the house' or 'in the lease.' It is not a guarantee of shared nights or fixed addresses. It is the promise of shared action, not shared permanence."

You close your hand around the seaglass charm until your knuckles blanch. The pier smells like brine and hot metal and the acrid tang of paperwork. The mural glows behind you like a benediction the city intends to archive and forget.

You look back once more at the mural and the crowd—at the way the cameras have turned neighbors into images and images into a marketable problem. Your throat catches on what was beautiful and what the city has made of it.

Ava Maren [Final thought, steady]: "Beauty will be remembered, and memory will be used. You will grieve that fact. You will also, in the days to come, compute risk differently and carry new stubbornness into each meeting room and into every late-night fabrication session. The mural will not stop notices. It will not unwrite memos. But it taught a truth too: that people will speak when given a shape to speak in. That truth is a fragile weapon—and you will sharpen it."

[Visual: The lights over the pier dim to a softer amber. The mural's speakers play a last looping story as the press vans pack up. A gull cries once, a long, tired sound. Ori walks with you toward the Ferryworks; he presses your hand once more as if to make sure the charm hasn't slipped free.]
[Music: A single, aching cello line dissolves into the sound of lapping water]

You keep the charm. You keep the memory of every face under the lantern light. You keep, too, the small burning knowledge that beauty shown plainly can be stolen as easily as a banner taken in wind. Tonight you will write letters and call allies and rest your jaw and count every small win like coins in a jar that is never quite full.

You will not let this be the end of your work, though you know the route has changed. The city has rewritten the terms; your fight will take new forms, and you will show up. You do not know whether that is enough to keep every household in place. You do know, with a kind of stubborn clarity, that you will keep trying.

[Visual: Fade out slowly as the mural's lights go dim; the final image is the seaglass charm catching one last stray beam and winking like a small, defiant star.]
[Sound: The murmur of the departing crowd, a distant crane, the single gull's cry repeating once and fading]
[Music: Fade to silence]

[Visual: Fade to Black]
