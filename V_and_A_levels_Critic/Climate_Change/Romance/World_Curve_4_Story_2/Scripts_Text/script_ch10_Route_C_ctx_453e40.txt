# Route: Additional Route — Ending
# Context: 453e40

[Scene: Ferryworks | Dusk]
[Visual: Lantern light pools at the ferry's gangway; portholes glow amber against a bruised sky]
[Sound: Distant hammering from the Glass Pier; the slow creak of hull against tide; a faint, private hum of electronics]
[Music: Warm, taut strings under a rising tempo]

You slide along the gangway with Ori at your shoulder, and the Ferryworks smells like the things you make hope out of—wet soil, solder smoke, and the clean tang of salt on iron. You can feel the decision from the meeting still sedimented in your ribs: the room had leaned on you and waited. You chose the quiet path. The choice sits between your teeth like a promise you haven't spoken aloud beyond this hull.

Ori's hand finds the satchel at his back without looking—habit, muscle memory. He flashes you a crooked grin that does more work than the words will tonight.

Ori Navarro: "Rogue greenhouse, co-captain. We're officially very sneaky."

Ava Maren: "We are very sneaky, yes. Also slightly illegal, technically."

You tap the notebook against your hip.

Ori laughs—short, relieved. His laugh is a small weather clearing. He shoulders past a stack of reclaimed pallets and the warm lamplight paints his paint-streaked overalls like armor. Up close, he smells of oil, jasmine from the soap he never remembers to rinse out, and the same sea-breeze that prickles your face. His hands are already moving: quick, precise, and oddly tender with the tiny circuit fragments he folds into a sensor board.

Ori Navarro [murmuring as he works]: "These sensors will sing if the algae concentration spikes. It'll hum, like a warning bell you can actually enjoy."

You watch his thumbs solder with a practiced patience that used to be reserved for corporate prototypes. Now he grins at the tiny, functioning LED as if it were a small miracle.

Ava Maren: "If it hums, we'll call it the Saltway Choir."

You trace a plan on the nearest scrap of plywood—mangrove anchors, modular planters, the winged solar sails sketched quick and confident. "We put the tenure markers on the inside faces—visible to residents, not to investors. Names, dates, a map."

Ori lifts his head, amber eyes soft in the lamplight.

Ori Navarro: "Names. Good. I like names. They stop things from being just projects."

[Visual: Close-up on your field notebook; inked tide lines and a stamped place for tenure markers]
[Sound: The scissors' snip, a tool tumbles, hands passing through one another in the dim]

The secrecy feels like a thin blanket. It's warm, intentional. It keeps corporates out of meetings, saves your language from being rebranded, but it narrows the field. You know, with painful clarity, that in this cove your proof will be irrevocable proof—beautiful and limited.

A low voice from the shadow—Silas—answers before you can paint the whole worry into words.

Silas: "Limited is good. Less to hide. Less to lose if something goes sideways."

You hold his gaze. He’s still young, hands quicker than most adults, the dirt under his nails new as courage.

Ava Maren: "Less to lose, but also less to protect. Which means we have to get it right."

Silas nods, shoulders hunched in a way that makes him older for a second.

Silas: "We'll get it right."

The Ferryworks itself vibrates with hands: a handful of neighbors you trust materialize at narrow intervals—someone with a battered soldering iron, another with mangrove seedlings wrapped in wet burlap, a pair carrying the curved ribs for the solar sails. Each arrival tightens the coil of urgency. You don't ask where they came from; their faces say they chose the same small rebellion tonight.

Neighbor [low, breathless]: "Jun sent a tip that Voss scouts are in the area. Keep it small, keep it dark."

Ava Maren: "Exactly. Small, bright pockets. Proof, not proclamation."

[Visual: Time-lapse of hands building—metal ribs hammered into curves, sensors pinned into plinths, seedlings tucked into peat, the winged sails unfurling like small birds]
[Sound: The work is a chorus—hammer, breath, soft expletive when a measurement slips, quick laughter]

You keep sketching integration plans in the field notebook—ink smudge, thumbprint, tide notes. Your pen hovers over the tenure-marker notation: "Resident name, home coordinate, year." The act of writing each name feels like an incantation of belonging.

Each mark is a refusal. Each name is a tiny levee against erasure. You know that; the writing steadies your hands when the building accelerates into a late-hour frenzy.

Ori leans close, breath warm on your ear as he fastens a sail to its mast.

Ori Navarro [soft, clipped with humor]: "If Calder ever sees this, he'll write a poem about humble beginnings and then patent the rhythm."

Ava Maren [smiling despite the pressure]: "Then we'll patent the taste of tavern stew instead. For the people."

He snorts and brushes paint across your cheek with the back of a hand—an affectionate mark that leaves a salt-smeared smear and a laugh between the two of you.

You hesitate, then reach up.

Ava Maren: "Come here."

You kiss the salt from his temple. The motion is small, private, and very real. The contact is quick but lit—something like sealing a pact in the humid, soldered air. For a moment, the rest of the build softens into the edges of that warmth.

Ori Navarro [breathless]: "You ruin my paint. And my dramatic aura."

Ava Maren: "Worth it."

[Sound: A low, mechanical purr—someone triggers a prototype pump; the algae filter cycles for the first time]
[Music: Strings swell; a brighter, insistent rhythm rises]

The first modules go into the water with the careful brutality of something half-mythic and totally engineered. The winged sails catch the last of dusk and flex like living skins. Sensors spike with data—salinity, flow rate, microalgae density—and Ori's face lights at each readout. He reads them aloud like a scoreboard of very small, very precise victories.

Ori Navarro: "Flow is within expected variance. Algae filter engaging—good. Solar sails are stabilizing at fifteen degrees. The hull heaters are cycling just right."

Ava Maren [rapid, precise]: "Clamp the northern splice—no slop. Silas, take the sensor cluster and mount it under the lee; we need a clean baseline. Neighbors: hold the lines steady. We don't have time for a drift."

Silas moves like a response wave. The tempo is high. Your breath shortens, not from fear but from the heat of shared work. The Ferryworks vibrates under the thrust of many hands acting in precise concert.

Neighbor: "Light on the pier—two minutes."

Your chest contracts. Two minutes is enough to finish or to freeze. You feel the weight of the secret decision again: this is not city-level validation; it's a cove-level miracle. If it sings, people will come to the cove. If it fails, the story will belong only to you and these nights, and the cost will be nearer, sharper.

There's a thrilling terror in being the first to make hope visible. It is a kind of bravery that smells like hot metal and warm algae and Ori's laugh.

[Outcome A: "You clap your hands, and the crew doubles down—wrenches turn, valves open, the pump whines to life. Data floods the readout, and your chest does that taut thing, hopeful and terrified."]
[Outcome B: "You catch a loose splice with a gloved hand and tighten it; the delay is a sharp slice of tension, but the module sits truer when it kisses the water."]
[MERGE: The prototype engages and the night settles into a humming watchfulness as sensors stabilize.]

[Visual: Night folds in; moonlight slants across the cove, picking out the edges of the solar sails like silver.]
[Sound: The prototype hums—low, coherent, a little like a choir tuning. Water slips under flotation, plants stir in their planters.]

The hum under the moonlight is ridiculous and perfect. It feels simultaneously like a machine and like a lullaby for the neighborhood. You can almost hear threads of futures weaving: wet feet on reclaimed boardwalks, children fishing from terraces not fearing flood notices, names etched into tenure markers that refuse erasure.

Ori stands close enough that you can feel the heat through his jacket. He brushes algae from his fingers as if from a painter's palette.

Ori Navarro [searching for the right word]: "Do you know how small this feels and how enormous? It's like—planting a flag, but the flag is full of moss."

Ava Maren: "It's more than a flag. It's a living map."

Silas [quiet, urgent]: "What if it punches through expectations? What if VossCorp notices before we're ready?"

Ava Maren [steady]: "Then we adapt. We always have. But right now, we buy time with light and roots."

[Music: Instrumental crescendos—high strings, a single brass note—then a sustained, bright chord]
[Sound: The main sensor registers a clean baseline; a small green LED pulses steady]

The arousal in your chest climbs toward a peak that smells of ozone and hot metal. Your hands, the ones that have built and patched and held and been burned, hover above the main junction. In the notebook you slide your thumb over the inked tenure mark you added—Mara's name, Silas's home coordinate, a tiny careful impossibility.

Ori's hand covers yours—steady, warm—and for a second you both exist only in the pressure of that contact and the humming under the moon.

Ori Navarro [soft]: "Ready when you are, Ava."

You inhale, tasting salt and solder, and you understand sharply that the moment you let the prototype sing to the cove, there is no anonymous safety net. The success will be narrow and luminous. The failure will be narrow and yours.

This is the risk you chose. The secret path has weight. It is handmade and honest. And it will, for better or worse, bind you and Ori together in ways that public negotiation never could.

[Visual: Your hand inches toward the main breaker; the camera lingers on the illuminated tenure mark in your notebook]
[Sound: The hum grows a fraction louder; the wind leans in through the sails]

Ava Maren [whisper]: "For Saltway."

Ori Navarro [breathes]: "For Saltway."

[Music: One bright chord, then the music drops into a sustained, anticipatory pulse]

You reach out.

The breaker is a small thing—metal, familiar, humming with the future. Your fingers close around it and, at the exact line between do and not-do, the world narrows to a single bright point: the hum, Ori's hand, the inked names, the possibility of staying.

[Visual: Fade to Black]
[Music: Fade Out]
