You step into the room carrying the memory of the contract — the edge of the paper still pressed against your palm like an accusation. The air tastes of salt and coffee and cut grass from the nursery next door. People turn; there are faces you know and faces you don’t, and all of them measure you the way a tide measures a shoreline: for what will hold, and for what will wash away.

You set down your own pile of prints — a composite of community plans, annotated by hand — beside Dr. Sima’s model outputs. The physicality of the charts makes the stakes tactile: lines that could be erased only at the cost of someone’s roof garden, someone’s ancestral path.

Dr. Sima: "The models show improved flood attenuation if we integrate living corridors no later than year two. The tolerance bands aren't forgiving, Maya."

You feel the name land like a buoy. You answer before you think, the urgency rising in your chest like surf.

You: "We aren't asking for delay to be sentimental. We're asking for time so the biology can work with the engineering. Concrete buys minutes, wetlands buy decades."

Kai Navarro steps forward then — precise, coiled, the corporate suit against the warehouse’s rough-hewn edges. Her voice is clean and sharp; when she speaks, the room turns. The drones' prints at her elbow starkly delineate a sweeping, fast timeline: scaffolding zones, drop points, sensor nodes.

Kai Navarro: "We can move in weeks, not years. Temporary barriers, staged pourings. Our performance clause protects your people — if the structure underperforms, we remediate at corporate expense."

Elias watches you both, clipboard clutched like a bridge between two currents. He looks tired in a way that speaks of long meetings and longer compromises.

Elias Kahn: (soft) "We need both speed and accountability. Maya, Kai — there is a legal framework we can use to bind oversight. It’s messy, but it’s workable."

Rafi clears his throat; he’s the muscle and the memory of the neighborhood, soil under his nails and a voice roughened by long afternoons yelling instructions to volunteers.

Rafi Odeh: "My people don't want a promise on paper if it evaporates with a budget cut. We need people in chairs at the table — real oversight, not lip service."

An elder from the Low Row — a stooped woman whose hands look like knotted rope and who remembers a festival before reclamation — rises slowly. When she speaks, the warehouse hushes in a way that feels like prayer and indictment all at once.

Elder: "When I was a child we danced on that promenade and the moon watched us. They took that moon away with the first reclaiming. We cannot let this happen again."

Her words crack everything open; the warmth of the room shifts and splits into a dozen heated currents. Someone inhales sharply. Lio presses a paint-splattered palm against a mural board, as if steadying himself.

The sound in your chest is a drum now. Very high tension — nothing tentative remains. You sense the building pressure want to transform itself into a practical scaffold: words into clauses, clauses into enforcement.

You press your palms on the table and let what you want be unsentimental and fierce.

You: "Fast protection is not a gift if it strips our right to steward what’s left. If you want access to our shoreline, it must come with binding governance. Community seats — legally guaranteed — on oversight. A jointly managed monitoring node with open data. Contractual thresholds that trigger third-party audits."

Kai’s jaw tightens; there’s a flicker of something like respect and annoyance in her eyes.

Kai Navarro: "Binding seats sound appealing. But you have to understand corporate investors need performance guarantees. If we can't guarantee timelines, funding is pulled. The clause I proposed is there to ensure project continuity."

You: "Guarantee the community won’t be priced out when the project becomes profitable. Guarantee the wetlands are not declared expendable budget line items next cycle. Guarantee, in black and white, that oversight cannot be sidelined."

Elias exhales — a long, weary breath that seems to shove the room a fraction of an inch closer to consensus.

Elias Kahn: "We can draft performance metrics tied to maintenance funds and legally bound steering committee seats. That means municipal countersignature and a public registry. But it will take time to iron out legal language — which is why Kai’s schedule and the community’s timelines clash."

Dr. Sima steps in, eyes bright with both data and a scientist's impatience.

Dr. Sima: "The biology needs at least one growth season before full load bearing. If the engineered elements go in too fast, we risk compaction and wetland failure. There is a middle path: phased pours matched to the establishment of living systems, with engineering buffers in meantime."

Rafi: (voice rising) "Phased, phased — and in practice that could be used to delay protections beyond any reasonable horizon. I don't want phased to become a polite word for 'kick the can'."

Voices overlap. The warehouse becomes a coral of argument, each voice a living limb flexing. The duel between speed and sovereignty is no longer theoretical; it is hot and full of hands and papers and trembling hope.

Lio, who’s been painting a panel of the Old Promenade with an impossible palette of mauves and neon, speaks up, sudden and sharp.

Lio Corvin: "Paint my mural with your timelines, but remember — murals don’t stop the water. People do. If you make promises to us that you won’t keep, I’ll paint that, too."

A ripple of laughter cuts briefly through the tension, the lowest kind of relief. You seize it, leaning into the momentum.

You: "Kai, if you insert a performance clause, make it conditional on a binding oversight board that includes the neighbors, municipal representatives, and an independent scientist. Make the clause trigger external audits and—" you look to Dr. Sima "—automatic remediation funding if metrics are missed."

Kai: (tight) "External audits are acceptable if they are strictly defined: who conducts them, on what schedule, what thresholds trigger remediation. Vague language invites exploitation."

You tap a finger on the table — the motion small but decisive.

You: "Define it with us. Not a signature from the top that we see months later. Open logs, open data, community seats that cannot be dissolved without public vote."

Elias watches your exchange, dialing his calm into action.

Elias Kahn: "Municipal co-signature is necessary for legal permanence. The city can enact ordinances that lock these governance structures in — but the city needs lead time to draft and pass them. Kai, could your timeline accommodate a municipal ratification window tied to the initial temporary protections?"

Kai inhales. For a breath the warehouse feels like a held lung, waiting.

Kai Navarro: "We could stage deployment: temporary barriers within three weeks, with municipal ratification within sixty days. If ratification fails, remediation funds are escrowed for community relocation and habitat compensation."

The words hang, heavy and complicated. You taste salt and adrenaline. Very high arousal — everything pulses.

An argument blooms from the elder’s earlier reflection: someone mentions lost festivals; another speaks of displacement; someone else names names of families who cannot afford relocation. The room grows hot, ethical questions like sparks landing on dry kindling.

Tension peaks and the tempo of voices accelerates — sentences cut each other off, hands punctuate points, someone slams a palm on a table. You can smell hot metal and damp paint; Lio’s mural fumes mingle with exhaust. This is the moment where policy becomes skin-deep: promises become wakes in the water, and you can already feel the ripple.

You gather the room with your eyes and push every syllable into service.

You: "We are not bargaining over abstract efficiency. We are bargaining over whether my neighbor's clinic floods and whether a child's first memory is a wall or a tide. If you want the project, you take the governance. You take the open data. You take seats. You take escalation funds. Anything less is a corporate cosmetic."

Kai’s mouth tightens; her hand finds the pendant hidden beneath her collar for a second — a private thing against public armor. For a moment you see the person behind the posture: a child who kept something small to remember home. Then she smooths the expression into corporate resolve.

Kai Navarro: "We can make oversight legally binding, if the wording ties installments to demonstrated performance on agreed metrics. If the municipality can guarantee a ratification pathway within a defined window, we can compress mobilization and open the monitoring to public feeds. That is our concession."

Elias exhales and lets the municipal fatigue fall away, replaced by something like resolve.

Elias Kahn: "I can push for an emergency ordinance. It’s unorthodox but possible. The mayor will need to be convinced. But if we all agree to the framework here — performance metrics, escrowed remediation, community seats, and an independent audit clause — I can draft the ordinance language and convene a fast-track hearing."

A murmur runs like electricity through the room. Hope spikes — it’s bright and dangerous and very, very high.

Your heartbeat is a drumline. You can imagine the nursery beds taking root as sensors blink live feeds; you can imagine Lio painting a mural that marks a new kind of coastline. The moment tightens into a blade of decision.

You know what you want — the hybrid of speed and sovereignty — but you also know how fragile legal words can be. The choice compresses into three sharp edges: accept Kai’s fast-track with enforced oversight clauses, push for an expanded municipal compromise that buys more autonomy but moves slower, or withdraw and throw everything into a community pilot that is yours alone.

You stand in the middle of those edges, and the room leans with you.
