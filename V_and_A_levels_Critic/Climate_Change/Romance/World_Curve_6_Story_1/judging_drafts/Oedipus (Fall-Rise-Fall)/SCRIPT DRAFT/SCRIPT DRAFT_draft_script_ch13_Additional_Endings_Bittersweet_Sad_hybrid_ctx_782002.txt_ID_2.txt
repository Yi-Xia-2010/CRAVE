[Scene: New Promenade | Dawn — Surge Day Aftermath]
[Visual: The new seawall rises like a hard spine along the horizon. Morning light, thin and cold, skims the concrete and slick metal. Foam beads where breakers meet engineered rock.]
[Sound: Residual roar of the sea, distant sirens down in the Low Row, and a municipal praise-recording faint on a plaza speaker — clipped, official.]
[Music: Low, patient cello; a thread of wind chimes underneath]

You stand on the promenade's edge with the trefoil at your wrist pressed to your palm. Salt tattoos the air; your jacket breathes out a small cloud. The wall holds. That single fact moves through you with two pulses at once: gratitude and a size of grief you can taste.

People mill along the raised walkway — engineers with reflective vests checking sensors, municipal aides clipboarding, a handful of reporters trying to find a human face for the celebratory headline. Mayor Ana Velez's recorded voice offers precise phrases about "leadership" and "collaboration." The cameras frame the wall like an answer.

[Visual: A municipal drone swivels; temporary fencing and relief tents poke like small islands below]
[Sound: Paper flapping from a displaced-business sign; a child crying and then being shushed by soft hands]

You remember the surge — how the air bent, how the sea tried to eat the line between city and salt. The seawall took the brunt; canal sluices closed in time; municipal pumps ran like patient beasts. Lives were saved. At the same time, the Low Row is different now: gaps where storefronts once threw shade, a couple of houses leveled to foundations and taped over like an open bruise. You feel the geometry of loss in the way the rooflines break.

[Scene: Low Row Relief Center | Midday]
[Visual: Makeshift tents, tarps, a long table stacked with forms and donated food. Hand-lettered signs list hotlines and shifted addresses. Lio's spray paint cans lie on a drop cloth nearby.]
[Sound: Rafi calling names into a megaphone; distant laughter from a child who has found a puddle.]

Rafi sees you as soon as you enter, the grin and the exhaustion colliding on his face.

Rafi: "You're late this morning, organizer. Or are you saving your energy for the press?"
You (internal): You tell yourself you're here for the neighborhood, not for the cameras — but the cameras make the damage legible.

Rafi: "We have ten families in temporary housing and three small businesses that won't open again. Lio's already started a mural on the bakery's shutter."
You: "Show me."

Rafi's hands are everywhere — a bandage, then a tally, then a map. You and he move in a practiced orbit, the logistics of consolation: food distribution schedules, legal aid contacts, volunteer rosters. He presses a clipboard into your hands like a baton.

Rafi: "You should sign this — it's a list of people who need immediate relocation assistance. If the municipality approves, they'll get vouchers and space in the northern shelters."
You: "I'll start liaising. Who's covering the community archive boxes?"
Rafi: "Lio said he'll take the boxes to the garden shed tonight. He wants to paint a memorial wall near the promenade. We can close the street for him."

You choose — but whichever you pick, the same ache presses at your ribs: the neighborhood stitched and yet frayed. Children run past carrying damp blankets; an older woman hands you a thermos and does not look you in the eye. Small economies have rearranged themselves around what remains standing.

[Scene: Temporary Fences and Relief Center — Later]
[Visual: A line of makeshift plaques propped against boards: "In memory," "Closed," "Reopen?"; Lio's mural shows layered scenes: a tide line, a child's hand, a row of houses]
[Sound: The scrape of a brush; low mutterings from volunteers; the distant, clipped applause of a municipal press event over a tiny radio]
[Music: A slow violin line and a muted percussion — steady, like breath]

Lio steps down from his crate and stares at you, paint smudged across his hoodie.

Lio: "You did good. You saved people."
You (out loud): "The wall saved people."
Lio: "But it took other people — it took our shop, Maya. It looks like protection but it keeps a piece of us out."
You: "I know."

There is a hardness in his voice that is not childish. You try to fold your guilt into something operative: more rallies, more legal petitions, archival salvage. He paints anyway.

[Scene: New Promenade — Mid-Afternoon]
[Visual: You walk the length, fingers brushing the new railing. A plaque dedicates the wall to "public safety"; someone has taped a child's drawing over the plaque with a note: "Don't cover our marsh."]
[Sound: A polite cheer from a televised ceremony elsewhere; behind the barrier, the Submerged Promenade's rusted lamps glint like teeth; gulls argue over a scrap]
[Music: Cello deepens; an undertow of sympathetic piano]

Elias appears before you does — not the sudden presence of a lover in a movie, but the slow arrival of someone who's used ingenuity to make space in a bureaucratic world. He looks tired in a way that makes his warm amber eyes a little dim.

Elias: "They've asked me to lead the municipal oversight program for coastal resilience. It's... a big ask."
You (internal): You already know what that sounds like: stability for months, maybe years, but a distance you smell like ozone after rain.

You: "When does it start?"
Elias: (shrugs) "Next week, officially. They'll want me attending council meetings, signing off on blueprints, coordinating corporate liaisons. It's—it's what I said I'd try to do. I thought I could pull things toward people."
You: "So you'll be the city voice inside the machine."

Elias: "If I can make the machine listen from the inside, isn't that worth something?"
You: "It is worth something. But what about being here — with us?"
Elias: (a slow exhale) "I can't be everywhere. You know that. And this is the fastest route to change at scale."

The conversation stretches. You press your palm to the trefoil on your wrist, like a talisman. His hands are steady when he reaches for yours; hesitant when he withdraws.

Elias: "You look at me like you're deciding whether to let me in or push me away. I stay in my lane to keep you safe. Is that... enough?"
You: "It's not a question of safety, Elias. It's a question of whose hands hold the levers. I can't ride the back of your bus and still be at the front with everyone else."

Elias: "So you want me to choose?"
You: "No. I want you to remember the people you'd promised to carry with you."

He looks at you, and for a breath there is all the old soft spaces between you and the present tightness. Your relationship seems to compress into a single fragile axis: devotion that draws him into compromise, and your refusal to accept that compromise without naming its cost.

Elias: "I don't know how not to do both. I keep both of us in my head and then—" (he shakes his head) "—I end up a little of both and none of either."

The words land like small stone and you both bend to pick them up. He leaves before you can answer, folding the space between you into a quiet that feels like a small, accumulating disaster.

[Scene: A Closed Construction Office Near the Promenade | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Kai stands beneath a metal awning, sleeves rolled despite a neat corporate field suit. Her hair is wind-pricked, the undercut shadowed.]
[Sound: A shutter clanking; somewhere, a tool snapping loose in a distant lot]
[Music: A thin, anxious piano thread]

Kai waits for you with an envelope that looks like evidence and eyes that do not pretend to have softened.

Kai: "Publicly, they're praising the team. Privately, some of the engineers walked off the site last night. They said the timeline was unsafe, that corners were cut." (She slides a battered tablet across the metal table; on it, a flurry of messages and a short, grainy video.)
You: "Is this true?"
Kai: "Yes. There were safety complaints. We have backups, contingency protocols, but I'm not blind to the cost. The strike forced us to slow a sequence and that delay probably made the difference for some of the weaker anchors. It was messy. It was necessary. And people still complain that we moved too fast."

You: "Do you regret it?"
Kai: (for a moment, she is not the corporate script) "I regret that the system asks us to run faster than we can ensure everyone is heard. I regret that the men who left felt they had no recourse. But I don't regret that the wall is standing where it needed to. If there was a way to keep all the shops and keep the line of the marsh we would have done that. We couldn't find that in the time we had."

Kai's hands are steady on the tablet but her jaw tightens. There is a private fatigue behind her efficiency, a small vulnerability that flashes and is gone.

Kai: "Look — I don't expect you to forgive me. I expect you to watch. Watch the concessions and I will try to hold to my side of the agreement where I can."
You: "Your side of the agreement has corporate teeth, Kai. I will watch."

Conversation with Kai is not closure — it's a ledger of compromises and a reminder that even people who act decisively carry the burden of what they couldn't save. She offers you a small, almost clumsy apology that feels more like a truce.

Kai: "If it helps — some of the engineers who walked have been in touch with me. They wanted to say they didn't want to be the headline. They wanted to be safe. They asked me to pass this on — that safety should be our first ledger, even when money is in the room."
You: "Then make it yours."

She nods. The exchange leaves a metallic taste on your tongue — not reconciliation, not pure antagonism, but something brittle resembling an accord.

You choose a path, but again, the day seems to demand more than single acts of good will. The strike evidence complicates the picture: the wall held because of speed, because of cuts and compromises; and because some people pushed back in time to alter outcomes. Praise and quiet protest sit side by side, like two boats tied to the same dock.

[Scene: New Promenade — Dusk]
[Visual: The light slants thin and gray. The seawall throws a long, blank shadow over the boarded shops below. Lio's mural glows under a single bulb: a line of small houses, a salt marsh, a child looking out.]
[Sound: Folding chairs being stacked at the relief center; the low hum of a generator; a dog barking far away]
[Music: A single piano note, held and fluctuating]

You walk the promenade again. The tide is a slow, greedy thing that rubs its face along the engineered edge and leaves the smell of iron. The city will write op-eds tomorrow; the municipality will hold a ribbon-cutting soon. They will call it a victory.

Your phone vibrates with a string of messages from volunteers, from Dr. Sima with a cautious data read, from an old neighbor asking if their porch can still be used for the memorial service. Elias's last message is short, procedural: "Meeting at 8. I can't be as present this week. Call me when you can." It reads like a preface to absence.

You press your palm to the trefoil again. The tattoo feels like a compass gone blind; it points internal now, to endurance rather than direction.

You breathe in the briny air, and grief arrives as a practical thing: lists to make, people to contact, memorials to build, legal language to draft. The moment is not cinematic — it's a small, sustained labor that will continue in the margins of official timelines. Vigilance as daily work.

Your relationship with Elias unspools in soft increments: missed community meetings, emails that arrive too late to matter, dinners turned into planning sessions where the only talk is policy. With Kai, the closeness was transactional and uneven — sometimes a late-night confession, sometimes a corporate memo slipped between two human sentences. Neither easy nor utterly barred.

You accept — with a kind of brittle clarity — that the neighborhood survives the immediate storm. The cost is measured and unmeasured. The lives saved are real; the homes and businesses lost are real. The cultural spaces, the cadence of afternoons, the small economies — they have been remade.

[Visual: Close-up on Lio's mural; on the painted child, a streak of salt runs down the cheek]
[Sound: The scrape of a brush being put away; distant applause from an official event receding]
[Music: The cello falls away to a single, low held note]

You stand and make a list in your head: petitions, nightly watches, a legal team to make "temporary protections" less temporary, community archives to digitize before boxes mildew, talks with municipal lawyers, and — not least — the slow, attentive work of repairing the thin lattice of trust between you and Elias.

You keep that list because it is a map you can carry forward.
