[Scene: New Promenade | Early morning, after the surge]

[Visual: The new seawall rises like a pale spine along the coast. Floodlights still puddle on wet concrete. Reporters cluster by the ribboned barricade; municipal banners flutter, glossy and new.]
[Sound: The low murmur of voices, camera shutters, the distant, measured thrum of pumps running.]
[Music: Sparse piano notes, slow and resigned]

You stand on the promenade with the trefoil on your wrist pressed into the chill of the air. Salt rides every inhalation. Across the way the corporation's spokespeople pose beneath neat logos, smiling into lenses as if grief could be polished away. A municipal official gives a prepared speech about "engineering triumph" and "saved lives." The words are accurate and thin.

Elias is near the edge of the crowd, trench coat collar turned up against the spray. He watches the speakers with the particular stillness of someone cataloguing everything: what was said, how it landed, who clapped. When he sees you he tries for a soft smile that doesn't reach his eyes.

Elias: "You made it up here."

You: "I had to see what was being celebrated." (your voice is even; you keep it small so the cameras can't take it and make it a soundbite)

Elias: (hesitates) "They stopped a lot of flooding. They kept—" (he searches for the right word) "—major infrastructure intact."

You don't let him finish. The words sit between you like concrete and salt.

You: "At what cost, Elias? Half our storefronts are gone. Mrs. Ortega's sewing shop is a ruin down the block. The cultural hall—"

Elias: (cuts in softly) "We protected the hospital, Maya. We protected the water plant. That saves more lives than one hall can."

You: "You always do that—you count lives the way they fit into budgets." (anger seeps in; it's not loud, but it's sharp) "Is that the calculus now?"

Elias: "It's the calculus the city has to work with. I—" (he thumbs his field notebook, the leather worn at the spine) "—I took the oversight role because I thought I could steer things, Maya. I thought I could keep what matters from being erased."

You: "And what about steering away from the people who will lose everything? You promised oversight. But oversight hasn't stopped bulldozers from knocking down a family bakery this morning. You weren't there when the owner stood under the tarp and cried."

Elias: (his jaw tightens) "I know. I know. I'm sorry. I can't be everywhere."

You: "You can be here, Elias. You could have been here." (a clean, cutting thing; you say it because it is true and because you want him to feel what the ledger shows)

Elias: (looks away, then back) "If I'm in this role—" (he speaks slowly) "—I can file requirements for community replacement funds, enforce the monitoring clauses, make sure the wetland corridors are actually implemented."

You: "So you chose implementation over presence." (the accusation lands, then you try to soften) "That's not the same as standing with us when the tide came."

Elias: (his amber eyes shine with something like regret) "I thought—God, I thought I'd be doing both. I thought the system could be bent."

You: "And if it can't? What then, Elias? When bending just looks like breaking?"

Elias: (searches for words) "I don't have an answer I like. All I have is the work I said I'd try to do. Please—"

You: (let his plea hang; you can't promise what you don't control) "I need someone who stays. I need someone who doesn't measure absence in memos."

Elias: (quiet, almost resigned) "I know."

[Scene: Low Row | Mid-morning, after the tide]

[Visual: The Low Row's terraces are a street of damp grief. Tarps sag over charred doors; the smell of soaked paper and fried wiring hangs thickly. A stretch of iron fencing marks a cordoned-off block. Paint flakes from a shutter plastered with stenciled slogans.]
[Sound: Creak of a swinging tarp, distant calls of volunteers, a radio playing a half-muffled bulletin.]
[Music: Low, sustained strings; a steady, aching undertow]

You come down the stepped alleys and are hit by the particulars: Lio's spray-painted canvases stacked against a wall, a pot of burnt herbs, the odd child's shoe half-buried in silt. Lio moves like someone learning to be an anchor at speed—fast hands, precise brushstrokes, cheeks raw.

Lio: "They took the front half of the café," he says without preface. "Painted the sign, took the awnings. But I got the back wall. I'm doing the mural there."

You walk to the wall Lio has cleared: a blank of exposed brick like a waiting wound.

You: "What are you painting?"

Lio: (his voice is small) "Names. Faces. The things that make us us." (he taps the white space) "I want it to look like the sea picked up everything we hold and put it right there—in colors."

You crouch and run a hand across the mortar. It's cold; your palm comes away with a smear of gray.

You: "Will people come for it?"

Lio: (nods) "They already do. We paint it because the city calls this 'managed loss.' But art remembers details the reports skip." (he meets your eyes; there's a steadiness there that steadies you a little)

Rafi appears from between tarps, vest marked with community patches, a clipboard under his arm like a weapon against chaos. He organizes volunteers like a conductor—efficient, human.

Rafi: "We set up a temporary processing center at the school. Blankets, food, legal aid. Dr. Sima's got the layout for triage. We're staggering shifts so people don't burn out."

You: "Do we have enough?"

Rafi: (flat) "No. But we're making enough. People are showing up with what they can. Elias got us expedited transit approvals for the relief buses, and he forced the contractor to leave temporary housing modules at the lot."

You: "He did—" (you nearly add "something right" but the phrase tastes sour)

Rafi: "We say thanks where it's due and we keep building. The city's headlines will say 'engineered success'; our posters will say 'remember who lives here.'"

You: "What about the families who won't come back? The small businesses?"

Rafi: (swallows) "Some will. Some can't. We have to help them get compensation, keep them in the decision loops—if anyone's keeping them in. You'll need to push for real guarantees."

You: "I will." (you mean it, and the word is a little brittle)

[Scene: Temporary Fences and Relief Centers | Afternoon]

[Visual: Rows of tents and plywood desks. Hand-lettered signs offer "LEGAL AID," "MEDICAL TRIAGE," "CHILDCARE." Volunteers move like a small, battered army.]
[Sound: The clack of typing, a child's soft crying, the murmur of volunteers exchanging information.]
[Music: Soft percussive pulse, steady but strained]

You move through the relief center where people check in, where the smell of instant coffee tries to cover the damp.

Kai finds you between a stack of donated lifejackets and a line of people waiting for counsel. She is immaculate despite the chaos: field suit brushed clean, the corporate band on her wrist catching the light. Something about the contrast is a fresh kind of insult.

Kai: "You look like you need to breathe."

You: "You look like you brought the PR team."

Kai: (a small, humorless half-laugh) "They're upstairs. I'm here because the on-site engineers asked to meet with me. They're striking—quietly—a few of them. They think the schedule was pushed too fast. They worry about compaction and untested joints."

You: (surprise, then a cold tightening) "They think it was rushed?"

Kai: (nods) "They do. I thought the numbers were sound, but the crew felt pressured. I didn't dismiss them. I opened channels. Some of this is my fault; we pushed to meet the municipal deadline."

You: "You pushed to meet the deadline and lives were remade to meet it." (that very negative, bitter undercurrent again)

Kai: (the steel gray of her gaze softens, almost unguarded) "There were mistakes. There are always mistakes when you move at that speed. I can't undo what happened. But the wall held. It did what it was meant to do."

You: "It held. And it destroyed the patterns of life that made the neighborhood. That doesn't balance cleanly."

Kai: "Balance isn't how the world works, Maya. We triage. We triage until someone says we shouldn't triage at all. Then everyone drowns." (she says it like a fact, not a boast)

You: "Is that what you tell yourself at night?"

Kai: (a very brief vulnerability) "I tell myself I saved a city's core. I also get the messages from field engineers that say we could have delayed, that the crew felt unsafe, that mistakes were likely. They talk about being forced to accelerate. I—" (she looks at you as if deciding how much to give) "—I have transcripts. I can show you the dissent."

You: "Why show me?"

Kai: "Because you won't be satisfied by the applause. You want proof before you call for hearings. You know how to wield evidence, Maya. I thought you should see it before it disappears into legal filtering."

You: (for a second, the closeness between you and Kai is an unsteady thing—part strategic, part something else) "What do you want from me?"

Kai: (shrugs) "I want someone to use it well. And I want someone to stop pretending a single victory is a finished story."

You: "You're asking me to be your judge."

Kai: "I'm asking you to be a witness."

[Dialogue continues; the interaction stretches on, textured with friction and unexpected tenderness. Kai's tone is controlled, but you sense something like guilt or the residue of the porcelain pendant she keeps hidden. She offers evidence; you think about what calling it out would mean.]

Kai: "This won't make the headlines unless you make it so."

You: "And what will it cost the people already living with the cost?"

Kai: "Pain. Hearings. Maybe arrests. Maybe reforms. Maybe nothing."

You: (you close your eyes for a second, breathe) "I don't want to make people suffer more because someone else accelerated a schedule."

Kai: "Nor do I. This is why I'm telling you."

[Scene: New Promenade | Dusk]

[Visual: The sun slides down and the seawall casts long, somber shadows. The city's lights pick out the new divisions; a glow along the engineered line of defense. The air is colder; your breath fogs slightly.]
[Sound: The distant rush of pumps; a child's laughter muffled from behind a tarp; a radio reading the day's casualty and saved lists.]
[Music: A low, single cello line; a mournful, patient cadence]

You climb the promenade steps again as the day's formalities fold into evening. The ribboned barricades are untied; the polished speeches recede into municipal memos. You stand where the engineered line meets the altered shoreline and press your palm flat against the cold of the railing. The trefoil on your wrist is a small, stubborn weight.

Inside you a slow, gathering grief arranges itself. The wall did what it was built to do. The city will print figures about lives saved and market them as success. But in the Low Row the arithmetic reads differently: the sum of what was lost is not so easily offset. The neighborhood survives, yes, but not as it was. You have to keep naming the parts erased so that they don't become footnotes.

Elias approaches from the shadows, distant and small under the flood lamps. He doesn't speak at first; he watches the tide line like a man understanding a ledger after the fact.

Elias: (softly) "You look tired."

You: "I am."

Elias: "I thought the role would let me protect more. I thought—" (he stops, and there is the worn-out look of someone whose compass keeps pointing to compromise) "—I didn't want to be the person who leaves you."

You: (the line between you tightens) "You didn't stay. You left, Elias. Even when you stayed in title, you left in the ways that mattered."

Elias: "Maybe I'm not the kind of partner who can do both. Maybe I'm the kind who makes decisions that make sense on a scale I can only see from the inside."

You: "That's not who I need."

Elias: (there is a long pause) "Then don't wait for me to be who I am not."

You: "I won't." (it is a small, final vow; more of an acknowledgement than a declaration)

Elias: (searches your face) "I—" (the rest is unspoken; the municipal role, the nights of paperwork, the slow drift of two lives on diverging currents)

You: "Go," you say at last, not cruelly. "Do what you think you must. I'll keep the neighborhood from becoming a statistic."

Elias: (nods, eyes wet at the edges) "I'll be around. In reports. In meetings. In whatever way I can be."

You: "That's not enough."

He knows it. You both know it. He leaves across the promenade toward the municipal shuttle, each step measured. When he is gone the space between his absence and you is an ocean.

The closeness with Kai is transactional and fraught; Elias's distance is a slow unspooling. You realize that the people you might have leaned on are now occupying roles that necessarily reshape proximity. Love and commitment look like schedules and memos; intimacy has been subsumed into duty. The grief for that loss is layered on top of everything else.

You sit on the railing and let the city noise become a distant tide. Lio's mural will go up. Rafi's relief grid will become routine. Kai's transcripts will either open a path to hearings or be bureaucratically eaten. Elias will carry on with his oversight. The wall will hold at the measurable moments, and the Low Row will bear the cost in ways that don't fit neatly into the headlines.

Your heart is hollowed and steady. Vigilance is no longer a campaign or a song; it's a daily labor, a list of small, stubborn acts.

You think of Kai's worn eyes when she confessed the strike and of Elias's amber gaze capped in compromise. The arithmetic of safety is a ledger you did not choose, but it is the ledger the city will use. You realize the work ahead is not to undo the wall but to make sure the walls you defend are alive with memory, rights, and real guarantees. You will have to convert grief into processes, loss into policy, mourning into meetings. It is not heroic. It is necessary.

You stand, the cold settling into your bones, the arousal of the day uncoiling into a steady resolve: to keep the living parts of your neighborhood inside every future draft, to make sure each engineering 'success' carries an account for what it broke.
