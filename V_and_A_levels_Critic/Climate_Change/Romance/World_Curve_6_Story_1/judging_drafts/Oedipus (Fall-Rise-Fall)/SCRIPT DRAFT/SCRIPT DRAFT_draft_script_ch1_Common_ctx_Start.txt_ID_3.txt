You step off the last warped tile and onto the terraced walkway, boots thudding against reclaimed wood that smells faintly of algae and varnish. The air tastes like salt and old engines; the high tide last night still embroidered a wet ring along thresholds, a thin white line of salt where water kissed wood and stone.

Your braid bobs against your shoulder — auburn caught in a single loose plait threaded with tiny shell and seed beads that catch the light when you turn. The brass compass on its cord hangs heavy against your chest, worn smooth at the edges. You know it doesn't point anymore; it's a keepsake that remembers directions you don't.

You run your fingers over patched elbows on your olive jacket, the fabric pilled and stitched in places where neighbors have mended it with thrifted thread. The jacket smells like soil and ocean and someone else's smoke. You catalog damage because cataloging is how you keep panic at bay: a sagging sandbag outside Mrs. Delacruz's door, a hairline crack under a raised board by the communal water barrel, algae filigree at the base of the seawall steps — small things, but where salt and patience meet, things widen.

Maya: "Morning, Mrs. D. You sleep all right?"

Mrs. Delacruz (soft, guarded): "Sleep's different now. Tide comes like a rumor and stays like a fact." (She taps at the threshold, eyes on you.) "We heard the council bulletin."

You crouch, palms on damp wood, and feel the faint give where water has swollen the grain. You want to say every plan you have, every model, every backup. Instead you fold your hands over the sandbag and keep the list internal, reciting figures the way you used to in labs before grants were folded into gloss and corporate logos.

You breathe out. Your sea-glass green eyes sting with salt — not just from yesterday's spray but from the weight of what the neighborhood is up against.

[Scene: The Low Row — Walkway toward the Rooftop Nursery | Morning]

Rafi is already at the rooftop nursery, sleeves rolled, palms muddy. Raised beds crowd the space in a cheerful confusion; a banner stitched from old sails reads: OUR WETLANDS, OUR LIVES. Lio's stencils curl near a wall — paint still tacky — little marine patterns dotted among slogans.

Rafi (grinning, then sobering): "You look like you slept with a tide chart."

You laugh with too little sound. "I checked the sensors at two a.m."

Rafi: "Figures. You always do the midnight checks." (He pauses, glances at a tray of seedling shoots.) "We lost two troughs on the south side. The water ran straight through where the old dune used to be."

You move among the beds, fingers brushing seedlings. Soil is cool and smells of earthworm and compost; the green is stubborn, alive. You list contingencies aloud — not to prove anything, but because saying plans quiets them into steps.

Maya (soft, precise): "We reinforce the berms tonight. Lio and the kids can help with sandbags. Rafi, can you set up the diversion channels by the east gutter? If we reroute runoff into the barrels, we give the roots a chance."

Rafi: "We can. But the municipal notice—" (He wipes his hands on his vest.) "—it says hearings are being expedited. Corporate teams showing up."

The words feel like a stone dropping in shallow water. The nursery hums; bees do not care for political language.

You take a stool and set your bruised notebook on your knees, thumb tracing margin notes. Your training is a quiet scaffolding here: coastal systems, sediment flows, community-scale interventions. You came home after grant cycles ended and funding shrank like low tide. You sold equations for conversations; dense models turned into plain sentences for neighbors, for children, for people who measure time in tide tables and rent due dates.

[Scene: Community Center — Meeting Room | Late Morning]
[Visual: Folding chairs, a whiteboard scrawled with last week's notes, sunlight through high windows]
[Sound: Murmured voices, the soft whir of a projector cooling]
[Music: Low, sustained cello]

Neighbors drift in: shopkeepers, older couples with sleeves rolled, a teacher with a stack of waterproofed charts. The room smells like coffee, lemon cleaner, and damp coats. You set a laptop on the table, the screen displaying maps and gentle, pale-gradient model outputs from Dr. Sima. The figures are neat; the consequences are blunt.

Dr. Sima (measured, tired): "Probability curves show increased inundation along the Low Row at five- to seven-year intervals if mean sea level continues. The engineered seawall provides a high short-term reduction in flood frequency for the central corridor but at the cost of sediment starvation on the adjacent shallow wetlands."

Neighbor 1 (sharp): "In plain words?"

Dr. Sima (softening): "It means the seawall will protect the boulevard and municipal assets. The mudflats and some homes closer to the marsh would see increased erosion. Some of the old cottages may be displaced."

A hush settles. You feel the room tilt — faces drawn like shorelines.

Maya (translating, steady): "They build a big wall and the water's directed away from the city center. That sounds good if your house is on a hill or your business is on the promenade — but that 'direction' pushes the problem onto the wetlands and into homes that sit where the marsh used to slow storms."

Neighbor 2 (quiet, hurt): "They're talking about 'collateral' like it's a spreadsheet line."

Maya: "Collateral is someone's living room, someone's grandmother's garden." (You step closer to the whiteboard, tracing a hand along contours.) "We have alternatives: hybrid barriers, living shorelines, managed retreat corridors where feasible. The models Dr. Sima ran can show where small-scale interventions reduce flux without starving sediment downstream."

Dr. Sima (nods, slightly impatient): "We can overlay vegetative berms and tidal channels; but funding and rapid deployment are constraints. Large firms offer speed. Community models take longer and need labor and time the city may not give."

Rafi (supportive, blunt): "We can get labor. People will work sunup to sundown. But we need buy-in, and we need the city to not sign a blank check to a corporate firm that promises miracles."

Neighbor 3 (angry): "Promises—like the 'miracle' that razed the west docks last year."

You listen to the back-and-forth and feel a tiredening behind your eyes. The conversation loops between what must be done and what can be sold to those with power. You translate technical nuance into concrete choices as a matter of habit and love.

The multiple voices make a ragged chorus. You feel impatient — an internal pressure like the hush before a storm — but you keep your tone practical. There is grief beneath the practicality; every plan you offer is a negotiation with loss.

Maya: "We have until the hearings. We need documentation, occupancy maps, names, an on-the-ground record of erosion and cultural spots. Lio's art, Mrs. D's garden, the old promenade benches — everything that's not in the municipal ledger. If we can show the human cost, we change the calculus."

Neighbor 1 (hope and exhaustion tangled): "How do we go up against their glossy brochures? Their renderings look like paradise, and none of it shows what they'll erase."

Maya (quiet): "Because their paradise is built on erasure. We make visible what they edit out."

Dr. Sima (soft): "I'll refine the projections in a format the council uses. If you can gather the neighborhood records, I'll map the most critical sites. But you should know — the models are limited. There are tipping points we can't predict perfectly. My job is honesty, not comfort."

The room holds that honesty. It is a small kindness and a severe one.

[Scene: Community Center — After the Meeting | Midday]
[Sound: A printer whirs; a notification tone from a municipal channel]
[Direction: The projector dims; the room leans toward the single small screen displaying the municipal bulletin]

You are packing sheets into a folder — lists of volunteers, photos of the nursery erosion, timestamps from your tide sensors. Every sheet is a small hope: evidence to make abstract harm concrete.

A municipal bulletin slides onto the projector: a clean template, municipal seal, the kind of voice that smooths over friction with syntax. The bulletin promises expedited hearings. Below that, an automated ticker announces a corporate delegation arriving within the week to present a "comprehensive coastal protection initiative."

You feel the room constrict, like the sea pulling back before a swell. Expedite. Delegation. Words meant to end discussion rather than begin it.

Rafi (low): "So it's happening fast."

Maya (quiet, controlled): "They'll time the hearings to limit public input. We need to prepare for that."

Lio (small, defiant): "We can protest. We can make noise."

Neighbor 2 (weary): "Noise doesn't always change what the money wants."

Your hands close around the folder so tightly the paper creases. You translate the bulletin in your head: speed favors the well-funded; hearings under time pressure mean fewer opportunities for in-depth alternatives. The corporate plan's glossy renderings will be displayed on municipal screens, accompanied by charts and PR lines rehearsed to perfection.

You taste salt again. This time it's bitter with the knowledge that the city might choose permanence over place.

You have an inventory of what you can do and the list is long. You have neighbors who will show up, seedlings that will not stop trying to grow, a younger sibling whose art refuses to be erased. You have models that speak gently and honestly. And you have a city that has just made the hearing move faster.

The pressure is not yet a storm; it is the tightening of a line. You are impatient, practical. You know arguments, mapping, testimony. You know the language of compromise and the taste of salt when compromise feels like surrender.

You stand, the weight of everyone's eyes a tide pulling on your shoulders. You fold the folder closed and slide it into your pack.

You step out into the heat-tinged light of midday, and the noise of the Low Row rises around you — children on the terrace, a radio playing an old ballad, the distant thunk of construction — all ordinary things that might, in time, be mapped and renamed. For a moment you imagine the seawall's shadow sweeping the shoreline and the hollow sound that would follow: the hush over the marsh, the silence where laughter used to echo.

That imagined silence is not a defeat yet. It is a pressure. And pressure forces choices.
