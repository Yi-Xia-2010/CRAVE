[Scene: Outside Nueva Mar Municipal Hall | Morning]
[Visual: The municipal façade — glass and concrete — catches a wet, pale light. The sea's rumor plays across the panes in wavering reflections.]
[Sound: Distant gulls; a soft mechanical hum from the plaza screens.]
[Music: Sparse ambient tones, low and steady]

You stand where you left off: braid at your palm, the brass compass warm against your palm before you slide it back beneath your jacket. The city smells the same as yesterday and all the yesterdays the maps remember — diesel and salt and the brief, sweet rot of seaweed. The breath in your lungs is measured; the tightening in your chest is familiar as tide marks.

You push through the revolving doors. Cool air meets the heat like a small lie; it soothes while reminding you of the gap between rooms and realities.

[Scene: Nueva Mar Municipal Hall Atrium | Morning]
[Visual: Wide atrium, polished floors, a vertical garden that looks planted for optics. Digital billboards scroll project timelines in clinical blue type.]
[Sound: Footsteps, low conversation, the distant clatter of an espresso machine. Your own shoes scuff the floor with a dullness that feels obscene.]
[Music: A single bass note, patient and unhurried]

Mayor Ana Velez moves through the atrium, scarf moving like a small banner against the institutional gray. Her staff orbit her with practiced angles; their clipboards make a faint cartography of intent.

Ana Velez: "Maya—" (she nods, warm but practiced) "Thank you for coming early."

You: "I couldn't not be here." (your voice fits into the atrium like a stone dropped into still water)

She smiles, the kind of smile that carries momentum and then stops. "We appreciate your participation. Today's preliminary will set the frame for the hearings."

Her words land flatly: they will "frame" things. Frame, you think, as if the neighborhood is an exhibit to be lit and observed.

Elias arrives between her and you, a slate-blue trench coat dampened at the collar. He tucks a weathered field notebook more tightly under his arm as if anchoring himself.

Elias Kahn: "I'm glad you came." (he avoids looking at the billboards) "I—" (a small, private clearing of throat) "I want you to know I've pushed for more time, but the timeline's been tightened."

You: "By whom?" You taste the question like metal.

Elias Kahn: "Investors. Insurers. There's pressure from the coastal authority and the firm handling indemnities. They want a solution that minimizes liability and appeals to funders." (his amber eyes search yours, careful with the angle of truth)

You press, because the motion to press feels like the only honest thing you can do here. "A solution that minimizes liability is almost never a solution for the people who live on the line."

Elias Kahn: "I know. I hear you. I hear Rafi, Sima, everyone. But the municipality can't promise what it can't guarantee. Consensus takes time — and time is something the investors say they don't have."

He lets that sit between you. The sound of the atrium becomes a background of small mechanical respirations.

You: "So the default is the big engineered barrier. Fast. Insured. Irrevocable." (the word "irrevocable" tastes like rust)

Elias Kahn: "It's often the path laid out by indemnities. If something fails, the contracts say who's responsible. They're trying to avoid a legal cascade." (he rubs his thumb along the notebook's spine, a nervous mimic of a seamstress smoothing cloth)

You: "That's not governance. That's risk transfer."

Elias looks at you — for a heartbeat you see the man inside the municipal suit: someone who believes in policy as scaffolding, someone who wants to hold lives in the shape of statutes.

Elias Kahn: "I want solutions that don't displace people. Truly. But my hands are tied in ways I can't always show you. Trading time for guarantees is a negotiation; it can be clumsy and it can be brutal."

The humidity thins a hairline across your skin. You feel every concession like a small loss already accounted for.

Elias studies the maps that bloom at the edges of your talk like tentative plants.

Elias Kahn: "If you can give me the technical specs in writing, with staged milestones and risk assessments, I can try to make the case in the right rooms." (he is asking for structure rather than idealism)

You: "The community has numbers already. Sima has models. The problem is getting them into the narrative that matters here before the contractors break ground."

Elias: "Then we do that. But we have to be careful on how we present it — the investors want tangible near-term metrics. Sima's models are persuasive, but they need translating."

You: "We know how to translate. The problem is the clock."

Elias's gaze lingers. There's warmth in it that feels dangerous because it hints at something steadier than politics: a willingness to bridge. But he immediately folds it away, the municipal brace returning.

Elias Kahn: "I'll do what I can, Maya. I can't promise a veto, but I can try for a postponement, a formal slot in the hearings. That's the most I can move right now without breaking the chain."

You: (silent, because 'most' is a word that can mean anything and nothing) — you fold the word into your chest.

Footsteps go crisp on the atrium tile. A presence approaches like a blade pushed through water.

[Visual: Kai Navarro enters; her neoprene jacket is immaculate; the reflective panels catch the atrium light and scatter it.]
[Sound: The squeak of polished soles; a faint, electronic chirp from her wristband.]
[Music: A colder, thin harmonic line threads in]

Kai Navarro: "Ms. Corvin." (her voice is smooth; the salinity of the room seems to tighten under her words) "Elias."

You take in her — platinum bob precise as if cut to a contour map, eyes like storm glass scanning. You taste the gulf again: your patched linen against their modular suits.

You: "Kai." (the syllable is short)

Kai Navarro: "Our team has the schedule; we have crews ready. We don't want conflict; we want to secure the coast before the next season." (there is a directness that reads as certainty)

You: "At what cost?" Your question is softer than you feel. You're practiced at this: push, then watch the answers mould truth.

Kai Navarro: "We model loss. A hardened barrier reduces immediate exposure. Wetlands can be integrated, but they slow construction and increase cost profiles. Investment timelines are tight." (she does not meet your eyes when she says "cost" — she measures it numerically)

You: "You keep saying 'we' like you include the people who sleep with the sea at their feet."

Kai Navarro: (a flash of something — irritation, perhaps surprise) "Community is important. But sometimes, incremental measures expose more people to risk while we tinker." (she chooses 'risk' like a scalpel)

You sense the undercurrent: Kai's conviction that scale and speed save more lives, even if the ledger erases nuance. There's a steel in her argument that could pass for morality if you look hard at certain spreadsheets at three in the morning.

Kai Navarro: "We can discuss corridors and managed retreat at the margins, but a top-down structural baseline is what secures critical infrastructure and business continuity."

You: "And critical infrastructure is not who I pass on Low Row morning."

Kai stares at you, the reading quiet and fierce. "Ms. Corvin, I'm not indifferent. But the city needs to remain — not just sentimentally, but as a functioning node." Her mouth tightens. "The firm can incorporate cultural sites where feasible. We are not monsters."

The word "not monsters" lands in the atrium like an accusation of its own: you barely know whether to accept it or to believe it.

Mayor Ana Velez steps between small escalations with the kind of practiced neutrality that keeps public rooms intact.

Mayor Ana Velez: "We all want the same end: a living city. Today's briefing will parse the options. I ask everyone — please — to present evidence and listen."

The word "listen" is soft as gauze. You know the municipal ritual: evidence is often ceded its authority to contracts within days. The atrium hums with that thin, bureaucratic lullaby.

Your comm vibrates against your hip. Rafi's name flashes, frantic.

[Sound: A phone vibrates; the atrium noise seems to swallow it]
[Music: A single, subtle string note tightens]

You step to the edge of the atrium and answer.

Rafi (over phone): "Maya — the Low Row seawall's holding but there's seepage under the western buttress. The emergency pump's been patched twice. Mrs. Ortega's back room has water coming in under the floorboards. We need a tech and a tarpaulin and—"

His breath is ragged; there is the precise cadence of someone who has been patching for years and is tired of patching as a long-term plan.

You: "I'm in a briefing. Tell me exactly where, and I'll arrange volunteers and a pump." (you find your voice thirty percent steadier than you feel)

Rafi: "We don't have a pump with the right head pressure. The city's stock is tied up at the northern depots. We can jury-rig but—"

You: "Do it anyway. Buy whatever they'll accept. Patch it tonight. Photographs. Note tide times. I'll push for a hearing slot."

Rafi: "Right. And Maya—" (he hesitates, the community voice softening) "We need more than talk. The kids are asking if we'll still be here next year."

The question lands like an old salt's boot: final, inevitable.

You: (your mouth closes around the image of Lio on a terrace, the paint on his hoodie dry-cracked from sunlight) — the ache you carry is a hard, low flame.

Elias's eyes find you across the atrium again. He doesn't say anything; he only looks, and the look contains both apology and a promise that's thinner than paper.

Kai watches too, expression unreadable — something like calculation, perhaps pity, perhaps dismissal.

You leave the room with the briefing in your wake: formalities, recorded statements, a schedule that speaks of inevitability.

[Scene Transition: Descending the municipal steps into the glare | Late Morning]
[Visual: The plaza light is harsher outside. The digital billboards scroll, talking about progress. Your jacket feels heavier.]
[Sound: The city is louder here — engines, a distant alarm; a child's laughter filtered through the steam]
[Music: Ambient tones recede; a patient, low drone underlines your steps]

The Low Row feels closer now in your bones than the municipal hall does. Rafi's words trail along with you like floodwater that hasn't receded.

You think of the seawalls you've helped maintain, the tarps you've sewed, the elders who remember when the promenade was whole. The indignity of being told the future is not yours to shape is a dull, persistent ache.

Your decision feels less like an epiphany and more like a settling: you will mobilize the neighbors. You will demand time to present the community plan before anyone breaks ground. It won't be enough to ask politely; you understand now the depth of the contracts, the speed of the machines, the way indemnities make empathy bureaucratically expensive.

You pause on the steps and let the city's noise wash over you. Anger is a slow, cold burn; hope is a sliver, thin and sharp. The arousal in your chest rises only a little — a tightening that gathers like pressure before rain.
