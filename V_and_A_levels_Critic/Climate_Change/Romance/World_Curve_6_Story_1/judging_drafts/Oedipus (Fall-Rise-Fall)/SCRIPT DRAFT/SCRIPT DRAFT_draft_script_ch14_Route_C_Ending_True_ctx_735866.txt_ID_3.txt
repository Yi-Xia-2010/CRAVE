[Scene: Coastal Construction Site | Midday]
[Visual: Crane arms cut the overcast skyline; scaffolding bristles against wind-blown spray]

You stand at the edge of the construction perimeter where the seawall takes form—slower now, segmented with gaps left for marsh corridors and tidal flow. Engineers pace in stamped boots; survey markers poke like small flags across a changed shoreline.

Kai Navarro is there, crisp in field neoprene that refuses to pick up the weather. Her face is everything efficient—tight, ironed by necessity. Where she meets your gaze, something like recognition flickers: the grudging admission that the project is not only hers anymore.

Kai Navarro: "You're making me build around constraints I didn't write. It's messy. It costs time and money."

You: "It's not just constraints. It's life. The corridors are how the system breathes. If we strangle them for a neat drop in water level today, we lose options tomorrow."

Kai: (tilts her head) "We both want fewer flooded hospitals, Maya. I just think speed buys lives."

Dr. Sima steps forward, tablet open to a live model. Her voice cuts through the machinery clack.

Dr. Sima: "If we implement Kai's original continuous barrier, we reduce immediate surge zones by 40 percent. If we keep corridors as mandated, we reduce peak surge less but retain habitat function that decreases long-term erosion and subsidence. It's trade-offs—hard trade-offs."

You: "So we make the trade-offs visible and binding."

Kai: (dry) "Binding is legal language for 'someone will hold my hand while the city sues me.' Fine. Put oversight in. Make it watch us build."

Kai's eyes are not hard; they're just unsparing. You feel the pressure ratcheting—the very-high arousal—because this is the seam where ideals meet engine-room reality. Auditor: "We have a discrepancy in contractor estimates between phase one and phase two. We require an immediate clarification on cost overruns."

The words are small and bureaucratic, but they unfurl like a gale. A cost overrun could mean scaled-back corridors, faster deadlines, or a political scandal that would undo weeks of trust.

Rafi: (low, urgent) "We can't let 'discrepancy' become an excuse. Publish every ledger. Open the feeds."

Elias: (to you) "I can hold the procurement meetings. I can demand transparency clauses be codified—immediate audits, public dashboards, community seats with veto power on modification."

You feel the room contract. Eyes on Elias. Eyes on Kai. Eyes on you. The air tastes metallic.

Kai: (a flash of something softer) "If you bind me, make sure your oversight can actually adjudicate technical variance. Not because I don't want to comply—because sometimes things break in nature in ways paperwork didn't predict."

You: "Then we build adjudication into the governance. Fast channels for adaptation that don't let companies rewrite covenants."

Kai's mouth twitches. She offers a handshake that is almost graceless in the noise—practical, not ceremonial.

Kai Navarro: "So we watch each other. Painfully public."

You accept. The handshake is neither victory nor surrender—just another folded agreement in a long stack.
