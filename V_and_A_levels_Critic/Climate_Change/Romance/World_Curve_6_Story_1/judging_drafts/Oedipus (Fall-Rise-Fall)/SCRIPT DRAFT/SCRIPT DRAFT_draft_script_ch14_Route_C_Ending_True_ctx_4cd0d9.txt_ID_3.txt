Elias steps out of the corridor before you can reach the committee doors, his face steady but with a tremor at the edges. He holds a sheaf of amendments and a small tablet glowing with annotated clauses; the glow paints brief, careful bands across his jaw.

Elias Kahn: "They tightened one of the oversight clauses at two in the morning. I kept the community seat language, but they narrowed the audit windows. We'll have to push for rolling audits rather than one-off reports."

You: "Narrowed how?"

Elias Kahn: "They wanted quarterly windows that can be appealed away if a 'force majeure' - which the legal counsel reads as 'operational leeway' — is declared. I argued we need continuous access. I convinced them on rolling audits, but the language is—" (he lifts a hand, the motion a small apology for bureaucracy's bluntness) "—technical and fragile."

You feel the air compress. The words 'technical and fragile' land like cracked glass. This is the municipal middle way — small victories wrapped in legal ambiguity. Your throat tightens with a mixture of relief and dread.

You: "We make oversight routine, not exceptional. That's non-negotiable."

Elias Kahn: (a slow nod) "I know. I'm taking point in the oversight board. Mayor Velez asked me to shepherd implementation. It's… it's a lot. For me, for us."

The way he says 'for us' is soft, a fragile bridge offered between two different languages: yours, forged in the field and in dirt; his, in committees and late-night policy memos. You recognize the offer. You also feel the weight: his new role will keep him in rooms you can't always enter.

[Scene: Construction Site Office — Midday]

You and Elias walk the perimeter with Dr. Sima at your elbow, her taped glasses reflecting the scaffolding like a grid of possibilities. Kai Navarro stands by a stack of plans, her suit immaculate against the chaos; rain beads off the neoprene like hard argument.

Kai Navarro: (hands clasped, voice even) "Sequencing will slow the build. That's a fact. The longer the exposure window, the greater the cost overruns. My contract doesn't like open-ended oversight."

You feel anger rise like heat. Not a sharp flame but a slow, dangerous simmer.

You: "The cost of erasing corridors is measured in the futures you can't buy back. You can quantify concrete; you cannot quantify a living lap of marsh in people's memories."

Kai Navarro: (her eyes, steel-grey, flick) "Emotion won't keep the city from flooding when the models shift. I build systems that move the needle quickly — not slowly. Speed saves lives."

Elias Kahn: "We can lock the sequencing with contractual milestones tied to ecological measures. Penalties for schedule change should be enforced, but we must also allow for legitimate adaptations if new scientific data comes in."

Kai Navarro: "Penalties create adversarial relationships. If you make my contractors liable to constant audits, they'll cut corners elsewhere."

Dr. Sima: "Then make audits transparent and technically grounded. Use independent sensors, open data streams. Leave no space for 'we couldn't know' to be an excuse."

You: (internal) The debate spirals into clauses, into 'if's and 'should's. The urgency is constant — storms don't wait for legal language to be tidy. Your chest feels like a drum being played too fast.

You stand on the nursery roof watching the city tilt into evening. Lio climbs up with a paint-stained ladder and the mural's newest panel rolled under his arm. He breathes in, the air full of salt and exhaust.

Lio: (half-smiling) "They gave us 'adaptive public space' instead of bulldozers. I painted a doorway opening into marshes. It's still a doorway."

You: "A doorway that other people might repurpose later. A doorway that needs vigilance."

Lio: "You're going to make sure it stays a doorway and not a façade, right?"

His look is simple and enormous; it holds the future in a small, expectant way. You feel the ache — the negative valence — sharpen: proud that lanes remain, furious that something had to change hands. Elias leans against the railing nearby, hands empty, watching you watch the city.

Elias Kahn: "I accepted the municipal post. It's to make sure the board functions, that the oversight is more than a bulletin. I won't be perfect. I won't be where you work in the mud. But I can keep showing up in the meetings that count."

Elias Kahn: "Exactly. We guard the same places differently. That's… that's not the same as standing side by side in a trench, but it's something."

There is a tenderness — not flashy, not heroic. It is bureaucratic tenderness: shared schedules, midnight emails, a hand sent across a table in a council meeting. Your relationship reframes into a partnership worn with policy and petitions.

You feel the pull of the city like a tide tugging at the hem of your life. The chapter's arc is not a single victory nor a single defeat. It is slow and loud, a machine you have to keep turning.
