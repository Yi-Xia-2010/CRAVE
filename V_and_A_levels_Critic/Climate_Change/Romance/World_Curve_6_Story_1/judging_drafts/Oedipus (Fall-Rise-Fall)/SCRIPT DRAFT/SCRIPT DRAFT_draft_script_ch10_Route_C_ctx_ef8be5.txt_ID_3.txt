A whiteboard stands propped on crates, covered in diagrams: segmented barriers, green corridors like veins, sensors in tidy arrays. A low bank of monitors flickers with tidal models and budget spreadsheets.

Kai Navarro is already there—exact as the rumor: platinum bob precise against her jaw, slate neoprene jacket immaculate despite the spray outside, steel-gray eyes fixed in the way of someone who measures every angle before they smile. She rises with a quick efficiency and inclines her head. There is a crispness to her movements that keeps edges from fraying.

Kai Navarro: "Maya. Thank you for coming. I didn't expect you to accept so fast."

You: "It was the most direct invitation I've had in weeks. Show me what you have."

Kai's mouth softens for half a beat—just enough to make the line of her face less like an instrument and more like a person. The monitors shift to a digital cross-section of the proposed hybrid: a heavily reinforced wall punctuated by living corridors—marsh swales, planted terraces, permeable inlets. Her finger traces the corridors as if they were seams she can stitch.

Kai Navarro: "We can build a rapid protective line now—modular plates, articulated seals. That buys neighborhoods immediate reduction in overtopping risk. Then, integrated into that shell, we design corridors that sustain tidal exchange and biodiversity. Hybrid, not replacement."

You lean in, the petition folder pressing into your palm. The diagrams are persuasive: curves that promise both safety and breathing space. Your engineer's brain catalogues the possibilities. Your organizer's heart checks the map: which parcels of land will the corridors touch? Who will control the maintenance nodes?

You: "And the clause about removal? I read the summary. A performance-mandated escape hatch if maintenance budgets dry up."

Kai's jaw tightens—so does the air. There's no theatricality to her answer, only mechanics.

Kai Navarro: "Yes. If designated performance metrics aren't met—sensor data, maintenance logs, funding disbursements—there's a trigger that allows the firm to replace living works with hardened components. It's a fail-safe from our end; investors insisted on a deterministic exit."

You have a flash of Lio's paint-streaked grin and Rafi's callused palms. The word 'fail-safe' tastes like a double-edged shovel.

You: "That clause makes a living corridor a conditional thing. It puts ecosystems on a ledger. How do you propose we stop 'maintenance failure' from becoming a path to erasure?"

Kai Navarro: (quiet) "Guardrails. Transparent live monitoring. Independent audits. Municipal co-ownership of the monitoring nodes would help. We can make access protocols public, immutable—blockchain not as slogan, but as assurance."

You pause—blockchain is a city policymaker's word that warms every bureaucrat's hands. It could be changeable rhetoric, or it could be a real lever.

Elias's voice, when it arrives, is a different temperature: warm, careful, and anchored. He stands in the doorway, a trench coat slightly damp from the spray, holding his field notebook like an offering. He didn't need to be here; he chose to come. The sight steadies you.

You: "We need a path where living corridors are not temporary tokens. We need hard contract language ensuring maintenance funds have priority, not an afterthought. Otherwise the corridors are placeholders for loss."

Kai Navarro: (leaning back, exhaling) "We can include a maintenance ring-fencing clause. Dedicated escrow. Performance bonds tied to municipal budget milestones. Audited by a third party acceptable to both sides."

Elias Kahn: "That starts to sound like municipal liability protections. Who underwrites the bond? The firm? The city? A public-private vehicle?"

Kai's eyes flick to you. For the first time, the corporate armor thins into something unbuttoned at the collar—a seam you can see beyond the rhetoric. She reaches beneath her jacket, fingers brushing something small and hidden. You see her thumb touch porcelain for a second.

Kai Navarro: (softening) "I don't like seeing things lost either. My family—" She stops, the sentence hanging like salt in the air. "I grew up in a coastal town. We had a pendant—porcelain, chipped by the sea. I keep one like it. Keeps me from forgetting what I'm trying to protect."

She pulls the pendant out: a small, pale disk, glazed with a child's glaze, worn at the edges. It's not a corporate talisman; it's fragile and personal.

You feel an unexpected swell of warmth. The sight narrows the negotiation into human terms. For a breath, Kai is not the firm; she is a child of coastline memory.

You: (quietly) "You carry that with you?"

Kai Navarro: "When decisions are abstract, I need something that isn't. It keeps me honest about trade-offs."

Elias Kahn: (watching her) "It helps to know the person behind the proposal. But it doesn't replace legal teeth."

Kai Navarro: (measured) "No. Legal teeth still matter. Which is why I'm offering a technical compromise: we deploy temporary rapid-protective modules now—deployable barriers to stop immediate overtopping—if you agree to let our engineering teams integrate the corridor interfaces over the next six months. Integration rights mean our teams can design the seams so the living corridors are structurally continuous with the hardened portions."

You can feel the room tilt: an immediate shield for neighborhoods in exchange for giving the firm time and technical coordination to make the hybrid possible. It's a trade that could buy lives months or years of safety while keeping wetlands connected—if the contracts are good.
