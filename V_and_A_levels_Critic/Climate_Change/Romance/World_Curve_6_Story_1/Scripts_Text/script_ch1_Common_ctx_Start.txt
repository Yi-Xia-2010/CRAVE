[Scene: The Low Row | Dawn]
[Visual: Salt-streaked terraces, laundry whipping in humid breeze]
[Sound: Distant mechanical thump, gulls, the soft slap of water against warped boards]
[Music: Sparse, low-anchored piano; slow tempo]

You step down the narrow terrace, boots finding the patched concrete by habit. Salt and diesel cling to the air beneath the morning heat, an acrid tang that presses behind your teeth. Your hand drifts to the salvaged brass compass at your chest—not because it points any truer now, but because its weight steadies the quick ache behind your ribs.

You can feel yesterday’s high tide in the grain of the steps: sand mixed with scoured shell in the creases where doors meet thresholds. Someone has shoved a flattened tarp under a door and tied a cord. Across the row, a bird's wing catches a laundry line and the cloth snaps like a small flag.

You run a mental checklist as you walk. Sandbags—repaired. Diversion channels—cleared. Rooftop rain barrels—topped. The motions are automatic because they have to be. Your auburn braid, threaded with shell and seed beads, brushes the collar of your worn olive jacket; the elbow patch on the left sleeve is a pale crescent of mended hope. You taste salt. You blink it away.

You were an engineer once who wore crisp lab coats and presented models in blue-lit auditoriums. You came home when the grants ran thin and your family’s roofs started to sag. You came back because the plans the city signed off on two administrations ago never matched the shoreline you remembered—because your childhood friends raise seedlings on tarps and your brother sprays stencils of tiny hands across concrete. You keep telling yourself the work is the same; only the audience is different.

Rafi is already at the nursery rooftop when you climb the ladder. The greenhouse domes glow with held heat; the scent of wet soil and cut basil folds into the diesel. He’s crouched over a tray of saplings, gloved hands patient and deliberate.

Rafi: "Morning's heavy. Tide came in farther than last week's, but the barrels held. Lio patched one of the eastern levee pockets. He's got your paint all over his hoodie again."

You kneel beside him, palms caked with compost. Your fingers like the feel of the soil remind you of why this place still matters.

Maya Corvin: "Did you get electricity from the panels for the pump last night?"

Rafi (shrugs): "Barely. Solar's decent when it's calm, but the clouds came in. I ran the hand pump at midnight. Mrs. Ortega says she'd rather help than leave."

You twist the rim of a seedling pot between your fingers, slow. The rooftop hums with small life—bees, a rooftop cat that watches with baleful interest, the soft ticking of a makeshift water gauge.

Rafi glances up at you, the lines at the corner of his eyes deepening.

Rafi: "Mayor's feed said 'expedited process' again. Corporate ads all over the plaza screens. Glossy renders. It's like they're selling a resort instead of a wall."

You feel that thin low-burning—impatience masked as a steady ache. You hate the word 'expedited' when it applies to other people's homes.

Maya Corvin: "Expedited for them means less time for us to respond. Less time for alternatives. It means bulldozers before conversations, probably."

Rafi: "We can still call the neighbors. Get a meeting going. Dr. Sima said she'd look at the models again. She thinks—" (he hesitates) "—there are maybe modifications we can argue for."

Maya Corvin: "Maybe," you say, and the word tastes like a stone. You think of the maps that show wetland tapestry, the thin green veins that feed the city. You think of the houses that sit in those veins—old, stubborn, threaded to families who refuse relocation.

You walk between the raised beds and brush your sleeve against a stencil on the rooftop wall—Lio's handiwork: small hands reaching for a tide line. Paint flecked across the concrete. The sight gives you a tight, half-smile.

Lio appears at the ladder mouth, dripping a thin rope of spray paint from his fingers like a badge.

Lio: "You see? I moved the tide line up a bit. Make it scarier."

You laugh, a dry sound.

Maya Corvin: "Don't give the kids nightmares."

Lio: "They're already living it. Besides, the news needs emotion—graphs don't make people cry."

You meet his eyes—sun-faded, earnest, proud. He leans his shoulder against the railing and watches the city waking up below the promenade.

Lio: "You gonna tell them to calm down about the wall here? Or will you make a big speech like Sima?"

Maya Corvin: "Neither. I'll tell them the facts. Then I'll tell them what those facts mean for us."

He snorts.

Lio: "Promises, Maya. Promises with an engineer's footnote."

You feel the humor but also the weight. You want to be blunt and right, not showy. The rooftop smells of basil and salt and a city adjusting to a new normal.

[Scene: Community Center Meeting Room | Late Morning]
[Visual: Folding chairs in a semicircle, hand-painted banners flanked by a stack of printed maps]
[Sound: Low muttering, pages turning, a slow fan pushing humid air]
[Music: A single sustained cello note under the hum]

Neighbors file in: Mrs. Ortega with flour-dusted hands, a pair of fishermen with sun-creased faces, teenagers with paint on their sleeves, a young family clutching a stroller. The room settles with the patient noise of people who have made too many emergency plans.

Maya Corvin: "Thanks for coming. I know mornings are busy. I know 'expedited' isn't a word any of us wanted to hear. We'll keep this short and useful."

A murmur of agreement. Someone coughs. The fan clicks.

You open the first map. It shows the proposed seawall in glossy charcoal, a line that eats the wetlands in a clean, economical arc.

You feel the practical thing settle in—catalog, translate, prioritize. It's what you do to cope: reduce the spinning worry into plottable items.

Maya Corvin: "The seawall as proposed cuts through the marsh corridor here." (You tap the map. Fingers steady.) "Where the city needs green space most. It would protect the transit hub, yes, but it would require dismantling these homes and filling the wetlands."

Mrs. Ortega: "But they'll tell us it's for the greater good. More safety for the whole city."

Maya Corvin: "Maybe. But 'greater good' often gets measured in dollar-per-meter. The models Dr. Sima ran show us the hydrology changing—faster wave reflection, higher erosion downcoast. It protects a section, and it damages another."

Murmurs rise and fall like a small tide.

Rafi: "We can present a hybrid plan. Small reinforced nodes here, coupled with marsh restoration. It spreads the load."

Fisherman: "Who'll pay for that?"

Maya Corvin (this time you let your voice show the impatience you've held thin all morning): "That's the hard part. We need to show it works—show the metrics. Make the cost arguments. Make the human ones."

A teenager: "Won't the city listen if it's cheaper?"

Maya Corvin: "Sometimes. Sometimes not. Sometimes the city listens only when the public pressure is sharp enough."

The real work is always translating urgency into something bureaucrats can process: numbers, deadlines, cost-benefit language. That's the engineering brain in you—old habits of model and projection.

> Choice B: "Begin with a personal story—Mrs. Ortega's house, Lio's stencils—to make it human first"
[Outcome A: You lay out the graphs and projections. Eyes track the lines; a couple of heads lean forward. Technical clarity settles like a balm over the room.]
[Outcome B: You begin with Mrs. Ortega's memory of the old tide clock. Faces soften; someone reaches for your hand. The technical details still wait, but the room's attention is slower, warmer.]
[MERGE: The meeting proceeds with both technical and personal points being addressed.]

The door opens and Dr. Sima steps in, carrying a tablet and the tired look of someone who has been in too many late-night model runs. She sets her glasses back into place with the tape on the bridge and offers a small, tired smile.

Dr. Sima: "Morning. I ran the models overnight with the new boundary conditions. Some of the worst-case projections are—" (she exhales, searching for words) "—not good. But there are multiple feasible scenarios that could reduce displacement if the construction is rerouted."

You move to her side and lower the tablet so the group can see a simplified overlay you prepared: blue gradients that mark likely inundation, green streaks of surviving marsh, red patches where homes would go.

Maya Corvin: "Translate, Sima."

Dr. Sima (a faint, wry smile): "Okay. The short version: if the wall goes in as designed, expect more concentrated wave energy to the south. That means erosion and the likely loss of the eastern terrace over decades. If we include corridors—spaces that allow water to funnel and wetlands to breathe—the system dissipates energy better. Immediate construction costs rise, but long-term maintenance drops."

Neighbor: "So we lose some homes now or we lose more later."

Dr. Sima: "There's no free pass." (she meets your eyes) "Engineering is trade-offs. I can give you the numbers. I can show confidence intervals. I can't tell you which homes are worth less to history."

You translate again, slower, because people need both the numbers and the meaning.

Maya Corvin: "We don't have an answer yet. We have options. We have trade-offs. We have a clock that's been moved forward by the Mayor's bulletin. The municipal timeline is going to force decisions quicker than we like."

You fold the maps carefully. The room is quiet in that particular, raw way—people holding futures in their palms.

A neighbor raises a hand, voice small: "Who comes to the hearings? Do we need to speak? Will that matter?"

Maya Corvin: "Yes. We need to speak. Not just at one hearing—at every public forum. We make our case in language they understand and in numbers they respect. We show the neighborhoods that will be impacted. We make alliances with people outside Low Row who see the long-term costs."

Lio's fingers drum on his knee.

Lio: "What about the corporate renderings? They're slick. People get dazzled."

Maya Corvin: "We'll counter the dazzle with the lived-in detail. We'll put up our own visuals—photos, testimonies. We'll show how the marsh works, not just how a wall looks. People respond to what they can imagine living in."

The ticking fan sounds louder for a beat. You press your palm to the table to steady yourself. You are impatient—always impatient—because the work needs to move faster than the machinery of politics allows. But the intensity is low; there's a slow, careful sorrow under everything, like a tide pool holding a reflected sky.

Dr. Sima: "I'll prepare the model runs in public-friendly formats. Give me the specs you want emphasized. And—" (she hesitates) "—I'll testify about the uncertainty. Not to frighten, but to be honest."

Maya Corvin: "Good. We'll make a timeline for outreach. Rafi, you coordinate volunteers. Lio, keep the street art coming, but make it clear where the facts are posted. Mrs. Ortega, can you organize the food for evening meetings?"

Mrs. Ortega: "Of course. You say when."

You ball your hands into fists inside your jacket and let them unclench slowly. The room organizes itself like a small organism acknowledging a threat.

Outside, beyond the tall windows, a municipal bulletin scrolls across the plaza screen in cool corporate type: EXPEDITED HEARINGS SCHEDULED. CORPORATE DELEGATION ARRIVING. It moves with bureaucratic indifference while a boy across the street chases a stray dog through ankle-high water.

You feel the pressure increase, subtle as a rising tide—still a slow swell, but inexorable.

Rafi: "They'll send their team. They'll have renderings and lawyers and a media plan. They always do."

Maya Corvin: "Then we prepare on our terms. We shape the narrative. We bring science and stories together. We make sure the hearings are more than a checkbox."

Dr. Sima (soft): "Also—be careful with promises. Politicians and companies sometimes weaponize good intentions. Keep the community's agency central."

You nod. You think of all the compromises waiting like thin ice. You think of lives threaded through this place: Lio's future, Mrs. Ortega's kitchen, children who have only ever known these terraces. Your sea-glass eyes sting—not from wind, but from the chronic low ache of knowing how little time neighborhoods often get.

You gather the maps and prints, fold them with methodical care, and hand them out. The meeting disperses into a hundred small conversations: who will speak where, who will take notes, who will photograph the marsh at low tide. The collective low hum of organized worry fills the room.

You catch Lio's eye on your way out. He gives you a brief, wry nod—half challenge, half plea.

Lio: "Don't let them make this a done deal. Not before we speak."

Maya Corvin: "I won't let them."

You step outside into the heat and salty air, the city opening before you like a problem with too many possible fixes and not enough hands. The diesel and the distant machinery hum again. The municipal bulletin on the plaza screen flickers. For a long second you simply stand and breathe, counting the small, deliberate steps you will have to take.

You feel a low tightening, like the moment before rain when the air holds its breath. It's not panic. It's not frantic energy. It's the steady, grim recognition that the next moves are limited and consequential—and that you are already committed to making them.

You glance at your compass one last time, fingers curling around the tarnished brass. The needle does not move. You tuck it back beneath your jacket, braid brushing your palm.
