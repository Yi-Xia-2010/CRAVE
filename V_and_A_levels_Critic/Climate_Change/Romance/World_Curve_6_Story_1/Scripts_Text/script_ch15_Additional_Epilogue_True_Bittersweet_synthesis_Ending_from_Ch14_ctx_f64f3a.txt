The board meets quarterly now, a ritual that binds municipal law to neighborhood presence. You've learned to read the room: who will speak for the city, who will ask for records, who will hold up photos of what was lost. Your voice is steadier than it used to be; your grief has found a cadence that becomes argument and policy and evening debriefs.

Camila 'Kai' Navarro stands when it's her turn. She does not make the old, crushing promises. Instead she presents the maintenance logs, the audit results, the conditional clauses where the firm is held publicly accountable. Her tone is clipped, efficient. You can see the old Kai — the one who would have pushed concrete and deadlines — reshaped by being constrained in public.

Camila 'Kai' Navarro [to you, quietly as she passes]: "You kept at it. I didn't expect that."

Maya Corvin [quiet]: "Neither did I. Neither did any of us."

Camila 'Kai' Navarro [a flicker of something like apology]: "We could have moved faster. We moved in the ways the city would fund. I'm here because the system needs to be watched too."

Maya Corvin: "Watching is a skill we learned the hard way."

The exchange is short but held. There's an uneasy respect — not warmth, but recognition that both of you were necessary in different registers. The room votes on a maintenance budget and a new river-watch protocol. The result is procedural, a turnout of signatures and recorded minutes that will translate into work orders and nights lit with fluorescent lamps.

You listen to the public comments: an old woman recounts a night of flooding; a young volunteer reads a poem she wrote about sedges; a vendor asks for a waiver for storm-shelter fees. Each testimony is a small archive of loss and insistence. The vote passes by a narrow margin. It is not victory. It is time bought and obligations renewed.

You step outside after the hearing under a sky that looks older than the day. The promenade glitters with the aftermath of maintenance lights and the faint phosphorescence of seaweed in the shallows. The ocean beyond is patient. It has a shape indifferent to policy and petition.

You find a low wall and sit. Your fingers go without asking to the salvaged brass compass at your throat — the compass that never worked but always pointed to the memory of direction. You close your eyes and feel the accumulated weight: the amended laws, the lost roofs, the seedlings that survived, the ones that didn't, the board's ink-stamped promises.

Elias Kahn joins you after a while. He doesn't say anything at first. He puts a thermos between you and tilts it toward you in the small, domestic ways you've kept — an offering of warmth against the salt wind.

Elias Kahn: "We kept the corridors. We lost the corner houses. We got a board, but the board will take its toll in meetings. People will keep getting tired."

Maya Corvin: "They will. We will too. That is the part that never gets written into budgets."

Maya Corvin: "—I know the work will never end. I also know the work made people keep living here."

Elias Kahn: "And us?"

Maya Corvin [turning to him, the light catching his face like a reminder]: "Us is not a single scene. It's a string of mornings where we choose to show up, even when showing up hurts."

There is no dramatic reunion, no resolved arc. There is the slow, reliable folding of daily obligations around something human and fragile. The romance that once felt like a single bright hinge has softened into ritual: coffee shared between schedules, a hand held during hearings, messages left on the kitchen counter when one of you travels. It is smaller and truer and marked by the same municipal calendars that govern the rest of your life.

You look back at the waterline. The reworked promenade is a palimpsest of interventions: modules here, preserved corridors there, plaques for the houses that will never stand again. Wind tugs at the strings of a banner that reads "STEWARDSHIP" in a child's uneven script.

You press the compass into your palm. Its edge bites a little into your skin. You breathe in the salt and the oil smoke from the market and let the accumulation of years settle into your bones like sediment.

This is the moment the arousal reaches its steady peak — not a fevered climax but a gathering: lists of maintenance tasks, the faces of people who stayed, the ones who left, the work that never leaves you. The feeling is very negative in its clarity: grief sharpened into a watchfulness that does not soften. But it is not collapse. It is a durable, exhausted insistence.

You place your hand on the compass and then on Elias's palm. You don't promise the future. You promise presence. It is not grand; it is necessary.
