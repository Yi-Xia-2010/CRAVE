## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Repair

---

## World View
A near-future coastal metropolis reshaped by chronic flooding and repeated storm surges. The city is a patchwork: retrofitted blocks, raised boardwalks, and pockets of informal, DIY resilience coexist with sleek proposals from private developers. Political power centers on City Hall, which juggles municipal budgets, emergency response, and pressure from contractors promising quick, hard infrastructure. Grassroots networks—kayak couriers, urban gardeners, volunteer engineers—hold informal power through community trust and salvage economies. Religion and formal ideology are backgrounded; civic rituals center on tidal calendars and seasonal preparations. Class is geographically visible: Harborfront terraces and reclaimed lots belong to long-term residents defending homes; corporate offices and redevelopment proposals concentrate behind floodproof glass. Geography is a ring of neighborhoods edged by an eroded shoreline and an inland ridge where officials make policy. Atmosphere: salty air, wet reflections, and low-saturation lighting that emphasizes weathered textures—reclaimed wood, mossy walls, rusted metal. Conflicts are systemic: short-term redevelopment vs. long-term adaptation; personal: the risk of commitment versus the need to share burdens. Technology is practical and patched—solar pendants, tide-monitoring smartwatches, salvaged sensors—mixed into intimate, human-scale practices of repair and care.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maya Rios) race to secure an adaptive, community-led protection plan for my Harborfront neighborhood before the next storm season. The city, led by Irene Voss with support from developer Luca Marin, favors rapid, centralized redevelopment and concrete barriers. Elliot Chen, a warm-eyed designer-builder from the Green Spire, pushes for a living seawall and neighborhood-led retrofits; we have technical synergy and an emotional friction of trust. Sami and Dr. Noor flank me with grassroots energy and scientific legitimacy. Across branching choices—negotiation, protest, technical compromise—the stakes shift: we can coax City Hall toward a hybrid, community-anchored solution, be forced into painful trade-offs, or lose ground to developer-led displacement. Romance grows through late-night prototypes, shared risk on rain-slicked walkways, and the emotional strain of choosing public good over private safety. The narrative arc is Rise: from constrained, grief-tinged responsibility toward progress, agency, and mutual commitment—yet each route tests what kind of progress we achieve and at what human cost. Endings range from a negotiated community triumph and a morally complex federation to bitter loss when technocratic fixes displace the vulnerable.
