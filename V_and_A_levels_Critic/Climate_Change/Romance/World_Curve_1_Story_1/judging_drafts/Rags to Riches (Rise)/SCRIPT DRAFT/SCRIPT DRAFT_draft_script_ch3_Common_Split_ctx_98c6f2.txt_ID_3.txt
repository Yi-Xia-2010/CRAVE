You taste cold coffee and the metallic tang of the building's air vents. The room smells faintly of damp wool and printer toner; the small thermal hum of the council's mic rig is a background you could map to your pulse. You stand where the Harborfront delegation has been given ten minutes. Ten minutes is both tiny and enormous.

You feel your hands — callused at the edges, solder scars warm against the skin of your palms — they shake almost imperceptibly. The memory of your brother's laugh rises like a tide: the way he ran when water first came through the alleys, the bright, reckless speed in his shoulders. It steadies you and sharpens your teeth. You bring that image forward not as grief alone, but as a geometry of why the details matter.

From the dais Irene Voss watches the room with the measured attention of someone who reads people like policy. Her steel bob is a dark edge; her gray eyes are a strip of flint. To her right, Luca stands beside a towering rendering of slick concrete barriers—slick light slides off the digital surface like a promise. The image fills part of the wall and casts a cool shadow across the council table.

Irene rises now, smooth as a tide coming in slow. Her voice is practiced.

Irene Voss: "We've all seen last season's damage. The city needs a solution at scale. The proposal before you guarantees structural protection, jobs, and the fiscal stability our neighborhoods need."

Luca (leaning in, smile calibrated): "We can fast-track procurement. Public-private partnership means the work gets done before next season. We fund, we build, we ensure no one is left behind."

A few chairs shift. Someone from another district nods. A developer-funded plan is attractive in a room where budgets are constant hunger.

You breathe, slow and deliberate, letting the council clock mark relative time. You have the Harborfront in your chest — neighbors who sandbag at midnight, who tape plastic over windows, who sleep with the radio on during storm watches. You picture the raised walkways of your block, the hand-painted signs, Sami's neon lifevest slung over a mailbox post. You fold those sights into the first line of your pitch.

Maya Rios: (standing, voice level) "Members of council, thank you. We're not asking for an abstract gesture of protection. We're proposing a living seawall prototype. It protects — but it also breathes. It reconnects the shoreline to the people who live here, offers labor credits to residents, and includes a retrofit pilot for the most vulnerable terraces."

You lay out the plan in practical strokes: modular planted frames, root-stabilized substrate, sensor arrays to measure erosion and adaptively tune barrier height, a stipend system that converts community labor into materials credit so that residents become owners and stewards. Your tablet lights graphs; Elliot's sensor feed runs as a visual cue beside your words.

[Sound: A soft beep as Elliot's sensor graph increments; a neighbor in the gallery inhales sharply]
[Visual: Cut to Dr. Noor in the audience, flipping pages of a laminated tide-scenario sheet; her face is steady]

Dr. Noor: (from the gallery, voice carrying) "Our models project that with adaptive vegetation and staged deployment, peak surge energy can be reduced in critical corridors — buying time and lowering force on fixed structures. Paired with retrofits, we've got a measurable path to risk reduction."

You hand the floor briefly to Elliot to show a short demo of the prototype's response curve. He steps up beside you, palms warm against the tablet, eyes bright.

Elliot Chen: (soft, earnest) "The sensors register flex and drag in live testing. If the frames are installed incrementally, we can target the worst-hit stretches first. It scales through community labor and local material sourcing — less transport, less embodied carbon."

Maya Rios: "And—" you cut in, needing specifics for the council — "what's the timeline if we get a pilot approved?"

Elliot Chen: (quick hands, precise) "We can deploy Phase One in six weeks with volunteer crews and salvage runs. Phase Two follows after a season of monitoring. The key is permission and a small seed fund for materials."

The exchange is prosaic and technical, but each sentence lands with soft weight in the chamber. That's the language you trade in: timelines, numbers, human hours.

The gallery tightens. Irene rises again before you finish. Her smile is the kind that smooths over complication.

Irene Voss: "Ambitious, Ms. Rios. But ambition can't pay for every margin. The city cannot wait while we test theories. We need broad protection now. Our plan guarantees coverage, protects industry, and stabilizes the tax base."

Luca: (leaning forward) "We expedite. We compensate. We displace as few as possible and relocate where necessary. There are trade-offs, yes, but they're structured and funded."

A councilmember at the end of the table raises a hand, voice mild with managerial exhaustion.

Councilmember: "Ms. Voss, do you anticipate any eminent-domain actions with your plan?"

Irene's hand is a stillness.

Irene Voss: (measured) "Where necessary, yes. It's a legal mechanism to ensure continuity of construction in critical zones. But compensation is fair and statutory."

The words — "eminent sale" or "eminent domain" — land like a cold stone. You feel it in the room: a quiet panic for people who know that compensation rarely equals what people lose.

Sami leans forward, whisper-sharp.

Sami Alvarez: (low, urgent) "You can't let them talk about buyouts like it's paperwork. People are homes, not line items."

Maya Rios: (quiet reply, under breath) "I know."

You step back into the microphone because this is your ten minutes and the clock is persistent.

Maya Rios: "We are not against protection. We're for resilience that centers residents. A living seawall buys time and preserves community control. It creates local jobs through labor credits — jobs that keep money in our neighborhood rather than funneling it out to distant contractors."

Irene: (tilting her head) "Ms. Rios, with respect — 'buys time' is not an assurance councils can vote on. How many households are you willing to prioritize if resources are limited? Who pays for the monitoring? How do you guarantee upkeep beyond volunteer cycles?"

Questions like knives. You answer with the specifics you rehearsed, but every answer is also a risk of sounding naive.

Maya Rios: "We propose a fund matched by a small initial municipal grant and private seed contributions tied legally to maintenance obligations. Labor credits transform volunteer hours into legal equity in the infrastructure — a recorded claim on housing protections. Repair costs drop when residents are trained and materials are locally salvaged."

Dr. Noor: (interjecting, steady) "And the modeling shows reduced maintenance burden when adaptive planting is implemented. The ecological services reduce wave energy and the sedimentation patterns slow erosion — measurable savings over fixed concrete in the medium term."

Irene's jaw tightens faintly; she looks like someone who has weighed numbers and found them thin. Luca smiles again, setter of deals.

Luca: "You're asking council to gamble with public money on a pilot when we can guarantee coverage. Residents need certainty. Jobs matter now."

Neighbor (from the gallery): "Jobs that come with clauses to sell my house?"

The question is small and precise. It hangs in the air with an awful intimacy.

The chamber is splitting — visible in the small body language of the room. Some councilors lean toward tangible, familiar fixes; others tilt toward innovation and the moral argument you are making. The mood is electric: high, urgent, and threaded with a hopeful edge. You can feel community presence like a current: Sami's knees tapping, Dr. Noor's composed breathing, Elliot at your shoulder ready to hand up another chart.

Irene steps forward now, the subtleties replaced by a cleaner argument.

Irene Voss: "The city must choose between delay and action. A pilot sounds responsible until you calculate the time for scale. We have an offer on the table that delivers a contiguous barrier system this council can approve tonight."

Maya Rios: (leaning in, voice steady) "And that offer takes what from us, Ms. Voss? The Harborfront is not a blank slate. Putting in a contiguous concrete barrier displaces local economies and severs the shoreline from the people who fish, dock, and build there. It creates a different city. We are asking for a solution that reduces risk and keeps residents rooted."

A murmur, then a few sharp questions. Council members press on liability, on insurance, on the fiscal schedule. You answer, turn to Dr. Noor for a harder scenario comparison, and she supplies it — tide projections overlaying both approaches; the living seawall slows energy and reduces peak loads in key corridors, while a fixed barrier elevates floodwaters elsewhere unless massive drainage is also built.

Dr. Noor: "If you build in one place and ignore the hydrodynamics, you create new stress points. Our proposal seeks to distribute intervention where it aligns with natural sediment flow."

Elliot: "We can phase and test. Even a small install will tell us how the structure behaves under real surge. We can scale quickly if data supports it — faster than you might expect."

Irene: (cool) "Fast isn't open-ended. The city cannot be in constant testing mode."

The tenor is sharp but not cruel; it's a contest of visions with livelihoods hanging between them. Around you, loved ones and neighbors lean forward as if they could tip the scale.

Your ten minutes are draining down; you can feel the clock like a tide-line on your skin. Energy is high, the stakes vivid, your pulse a drum. All the options press hotly at you: insist on piloting with Elliot; join Sami and Dr. Noor to raise public pressure and force more hearings; or take Irene and Luca's resources and try to buy protections quickly even if it costs autonomy.

You can feel the room's attention fold on you like a wave. Elliot's hand finds your arm for a second — an unspoken buoy. Sami's jaw is a livewire. Dr. Noor meets your eye with a look both clinical and fierce: there are models, yes — but models don't hold people's kitchens warm.

You inhale once, a deliberate, ocean-deep breath. The chamber waits as if the tide itself had paused to watch your decision.
