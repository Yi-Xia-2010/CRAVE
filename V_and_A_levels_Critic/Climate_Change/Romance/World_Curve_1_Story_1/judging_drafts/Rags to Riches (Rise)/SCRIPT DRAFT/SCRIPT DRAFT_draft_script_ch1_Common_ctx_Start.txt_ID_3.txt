[Scene: Harborfront Terrace | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Wet, weathered planks stretching along low pilings; half-submerged market stalls and children paddling between them]
[Music: Sparse, low piano undercurrent — slow, contemplative]
[Sound: A distant gull, soft creak of wood, steady hiss of tidal wind]

You stand on the raised walkway, the boards damp beneath your boots. Salt clings to the cuff of your jacket; a stray curl has come loose from your utilitarian bun and stings your temple with each gust. The harbor smells of kelp and diesel and something older — the mineral, patient smell of rot and repair.

Your fingers find the faded orange field notebook at your hip. Sand grains grit between the pages where you've scrawled equations in haste, a list of names folded into the margin like a vow. Your hand brushes the solder-scarred multi-tool on its cord, and for a second the metal is the only quiet thing you can trust.

You: "Okay. Pilings map, three weak points by the old rope vendor, spare pilings at Sami's locker... and—" (you exhale, tasting the salt) "—a rehearsal that doesn't sound like I'm begging."

Sami appears from the end of the walkway with one of his courier kayaks slung over his shoulder, neon lifevest catching the dying light. He moves like someone who's spent his life negotiating water and wills — quick, easy, reliably loud.

Sami: "You're up early for a speech, huh? Or is it that sea breeze finally got to you and you're writing poetry about pilings?"

You let a small, dry smile happen because it softens the muscles around your jaw even if it doesn't reach the rest of your face.

You: "If I start rhyming about pilings, you have my permission to mock me. Otherwise, help me check the pilings map. Sami, do you have time to run courier routes after this? I need to know who can haul materials."

Sami sets the kayak down and wipes his hands on his shorts, grin settling into something steadier.

Sami: "I can move three teams this week, maybe four if we stagger departures. Council's chatter scares some people, though—Luca's ad trucks are everywhere. Some folks think redevelopment means paychecks, not pilings."

You: "That's the talking point Luca wants. We need bodies and timelines, Sami. Not just outrage."

Sami hesitates, then reaches into his bag and hands you a damp stack of laminated route sheets. The edges are frayed; someone has scrawled 'NEEDS SAND' in a child's handwriting across one.

Sami: "I also talked to Rosa. She's worried about the kids—about the market stalls collapsing if the next surge hits at high tide. She asked if we can prioritize the area by the sea stairs."

You flip through the sheets, fingers sticky with salt. Each name is a person. Each route is someone trusting you to say what to do and when. That trust sits warm and heavy in your chest; you know the weight of promises now better than you ever wanted to.

The sound of children's laughter ricochets across the water and calms some of the sharper edges in you. Two kids in a patched kayak shout as they dodge a half-submerged stall; a vendor calls after them, amused and exasperated. You feel the memory of another child's laugh — smaller, sharper — press against your ribs and refuse to leave.

Elliot's light on your wrist pulses: he has arrived. You catch the soft glow from his patched smartwatch as he steps down from the Green Spire's docking ladder, vest streaked with soil and light. He moves like an idea made visible — hands that have planted things and coaxed mechanics into being.

Elliot: "Hey. Tide's behaving predictably for once. Patch predictions show low-water window for two hours. I thought you might want the head's up."

You: "Thanks." The word is short. You don't want to make it into a conversation about whether you want help. You don't yet want to answer the other, larger question: whether you will let someone stand beside you when the storm comes.

Elliot watches you with that soft, quick expression he has when he's parsing a problem you haven't stated. He leans past you to look at the notebook.

Elliot: "Those equations look... familiar. You been refining the pilings model?"

You feel the reflex to close the notebook like it's an instrument and not a map of all your failures and possible saves.

You: "Always refining. I like numbers that won't wash away. They don't talk back."

Elliot lets out a small laugh that is more warmth than mockery. He doesn't press; he simply sets his smartwatch on the planks and taps it. A tiny, stubborn graph glows: tide height, predicted surge, windows.

Elliot: "If we stage the materials now and run the younger crews through the basics, we can shore the rope vendor area within the low-water window. I'll bring the living-root anchors from the Spire. Sami's routes will cover the rest if we coordinate."

Your mind lists off the obstacles like a metronome: timing, materials, volunteers, Luca's PR vans running interference, the council hearing that will be a theater of compromises. Each is a muscle you have to test. Each time you imagine asking for help, a hundred small losses flicker by — a trust betrayed, a team misdirected, your brother's face again in the suddenness of water.

Elliot: "You don't have to do all of this alone, you know. I mean—" (he searches for the words) "—we can make the call together. We'll map, rotate teams, and I can rope in a few more people from the Spire."

Your throat tightens. You taste metal — maybe nerves, maybe the salt in the air — and you think of the Green Spire's glow in the distance, a promise you have been circling like a cautious bird.

You: "I know what you're offering." Your voice is even, but the steadiness doesn't hide the quaver under it. "It's not that I don't appreciate it, Elliot. It's that when I ask, and people answer, I feel obligated in a way that scares me. Obligated like I'm counting on people to keep me from failing. I can't—" (you pause, because saying the next part aloud feels like making a map to a wound) "—I can't have more people depending on me unless I'm certain I can protect them."

Elliot's face shifts; his optimism doesn't evaporate, but the edges pull inward, careful now.

Elliot: "That's fair. And it's also... human. But it's okay to be scared. You don't have to carry the whole harbor in your hands. We can take the lifting wheel by wheel."

You: "Wheel by wheel," you repeat, testing the metaphor. It runs up against something inside you that doesn't want to be believed because belief makes you vulnerable.

Sami, who has been watching the exchange with that easy loyalty he reserves for you, cuts in with a grin that isn't quite light.

Sami: "If Maya gets poetic about pilings, Elliot will start doing interpretive construction dances. It's a slippery slope."

Elliot swats at Sami, smiling despite the tension. The sound is small — a brief, human chord that steadies the moment.

Sami: "Seriously though, Maya. You mapping vulnerabilities is good. But a plan's only as good as the people who follow it. We can lean into that. We can teach. People here want to keep their homes. They'll come if we make it clear what to do."

You want to believe him. You want to believe that the neighbor with the crooked smile will show up with two friends; that the teenager who sold you a sack of nails last month will trade labor for lessons; that Rosa will stand on the market steps and tell everyone to help.

But the image of standing in a council chamber and watching a polished rendering of Irene's project slide across the screen — all glossy, all promises — is a cold, hard thing. Your mouth closes around the rehearsed speech you have been practicing: measured facts, data points, the human cost framed not as a plea but as evidence. The words feel like tools, careful and blunt. Trust, though, is not a tool you can sharpen.

The children's laughter has faded into the background now, replaced by the soft scuff of boots and the muffled conversation of neighbors curving into the market. A gull circles above, unmoved by human calculations. You map the pilings with your eyes: rotted wood, patched brackets, areas where algae holds like a bandage over old rot. You can hear the unseen current, the city breathing in the way of a living thing that has learned to hold its breath.

You think of your brother — not in a single flash but in small, clipped memories: a skateboard left by the stairs, a cough of panic, the suddenness that taught you how quickly stakes could change. That grief has been an artillery of sorts; it taught you how to plan, how to measure, how to catalog what can be protected. It also taught you to close your mouth when offers of help approached, because each offer felt like a new lever someone else could turn that you might not be able to control.

Elliot picks up his smartwatch, thumb rubbing a worn patch as if it has the answer. He looks at you with an unspoken question that isn't about technical specs but about whether you'll let someone cross that invisible shoreline.

Elliot: "Maya — you don't have to answer now. But tomorrow, before the hearing, could you let me run through the council pitch with you? Just once. I can hold the technical points while you frame the human cost."

You hold the notebook tighter, the leather creaking. For a moment the thought of letting someone else take the technical speaking role flares a guilty relief and something like fear. The city's decisions don't forgive mistakes easily.

You: "I'll think about it." The phrase is small and non-committal, and that's exactly what you intend. Thinking is a defense; it buys you time to reconcile the ledger in your chest.

The light thins. The Green Spire gleams like a promise across the water — a place where plans are sketched in soil and solder and where people meet to make things survive. Behind you, the market's neon starts to blink awake against the evening. A far-off thunderhead rims the horizon, not yet storm but a suggestion, like a held breath.

You fold the route sheets into your notebook, the list of names pressing into the paper like an inventory of possible futures. Every plan depends on people showing up. Every calculation you make is an invitation to trust. Your chest tightens at the thought: the meeting, the speech, the teams you might ask for help.

You stand there, the harbor breathing softly around you, tallying vulnerabilities and the small pieces of courage it would take to turn a plan into a collective action. The thought sits at the surface of your mind like a tide — inevitable, slow, and coming.

A child splashes nearby, and for an instant the sound pierces you with both joy and fear. You realize what the real risk is: not the technical failure of a piling or the political success of a redevelopment, but your own refusal to ask when asking might save people.

You close your eyes and let the sea-salt wind work its way into your lungs. The rehearsed speech, the lists, the tools — they are all ready. What you're not certain of is whether your voice will be the one to bind the neighborhood into action or whether you'll let the chance drift by, another casualty of cautiousness.

The low light begins to fold into evening. Somewhere beyond the market, a radio starts up with a distant, inconsequential song that none of you will remember tomorrow. Your notebook is warm against your thigh. The Green Spire's light blinks once, twice, steadying.

You take another breath.
