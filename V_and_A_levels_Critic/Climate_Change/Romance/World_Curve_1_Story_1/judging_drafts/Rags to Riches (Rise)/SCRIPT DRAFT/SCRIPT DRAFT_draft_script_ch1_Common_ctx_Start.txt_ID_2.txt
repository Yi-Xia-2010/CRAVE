[Scene: Harborfront Terrace | Low Tide, Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Low, salt-streaked terraces; puddled reflections on uneven planks. The Green Spire’s mossy silhouette glows in the distance.]
[Music: Sparse, low-register piano, slow tempo]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the steady hiss of tidal wind, patched pilings creaking underfoot]

You stand on the raised walkway and let the wind find the stray curl at your temple. It tastes like iron and sea—salt and something older, the dust of streets that have been rebuilt too many times. Your field notebook is warm at your hip from the sun and the day’s handling; the orange cover is scuffed, its elastic stretched thin. When you slide your thumb along the solder-scarred multi-tool, it answers with a dull, familiar weight. Tools are easier than words.

The board beneath your boots creaks in a slow rhythm that your bones remember. Out beyond the stalls half-submerged in the lower tidal flats, children paddle between sunken tables, laughing as they treat this town like a messy, beloved toy. A kayak glides past with a boy who’s learned the channel like breath. The sight should be harmless—the neighborhood’s resilience in miniature—but your chest tightens anyway, as if the memory of a different tide is trying to push forward.

You check the notebook: sand-dusted pages, scrawled equations that smell faintly of coffee and salt, a list of names—neighbors, volunteers, brackets for pilings that need reinforcement. At the top of the list, a margin note in your handwriting reads: rehearse speech. Say the facts. Say the stakes. Don’t let it get personal.

But the memory sits behind your ribs like a small stone: the day the water arrived too fast; the way your brother’s boots floated away; the way you learned to measure everything by what you could hold. Practical steps feel like the only honest answer. Mapping pilings, inventorying salvaged planks and steel, checking tide predictions—these gestures are control in a place that feels defined by losing it.

You raise your face to the Green Spire. Its windows are warm with lamp light, knots of plants spilling down its exterior like slow green waterfalls. The tower promises hands and ideas; it promises the kind of collaboration that makes your spine unclench for a fraction of a breath. And yet trust is a shoreline you’ve been too afraid to cross. You know how quickly promises can erode.

A voice calls from the water—a practiced shout that folds around the wind. Sami swings his kayak up to the ramp, hopping the last foot onto wet wood with a grin that doesn’t reach his eyes. He claps you on the shoulder in a way that’s both affectionate and tactical.

Sami: "Maya. You always pick the coldest bench."

You: "I pick the bench with the best sightlines." (You let your mouth pull into a small, dry smile.)

Sami drops a waterproof satchel at your feet with a thud. The bag smells like engine oil and citrus—supplies and the route manifests he’s been nursing. He waits, hands on his hips like a man ready to organize the weather itself.

Sami: "Routes look good. I can push two runs at dawn and three in the evening if we stick to the narrow channels. I even rerouted Kiko to avoid the bad pilings by Stall Twelve." (He leans in, voice low.) "But you gotta tell folks to actually show up."

You inhale. The sound of the bay seems to fill your lungs and then leave them hollow.

You: "I’ll call it. I’ll... outline the vulnerabilities. Show the pilings that need priority." (You tap the notebook.) "I’ll do the speech."

Sami studies you—friendship and history folded into the way his brow creases. He knows which words will make you bristle and which will make you look away.

Sami: "You never ask. You always—" (He searches.) "—do it yourself, and then you burn out. That was your plan at City Hall, remember? You walk into a room and carry it for everyone."

You: "I do what needs doing." (Your voice is smaller than you intend.)

Sami: "Yeah, but 'needs doing' doesn't mean 'across your shoulders alone.' People will help if you let them."

You swallow. There's a softness to his insistence, a push beneath it—protective, impatient.

A low cloud crawls in from the west; the light dims and makes the puddles deeper, the reflections sharper. You check your watch reflexively. The patched smartwatch on Elliot’s wrist would blink its tidal predictions more accurately than municipal alerts—if only he were here. You look toward the Green Spire. A figure detaches itself from the studio’s warm silhouette and walks toward you, hands tucked into an architect’s smock. His hair is a mess; there are flecks of dried mud along his cuffs.

Elliot: "Hey. I promised a tide window and I—" (He holds up his wrist; the watch LED blinks a patient blue and projects a small curve of numbers toward your face.) "—we’ve got a six-hour trough, worst surge predicted at ninety minutes after high. If we stage volunteers now, we can hit low repairs before things get weird."

You meet his soft green eyes. He always has a way of saying technical things like comfort. He looks like he wants to stay and talk, and maybe stay even longer, but you see the caution around his smile, the habit of softening when stakes are too personal.

You: "Show me."

Elliot taps his watch and the curve shifts, the numbers clear. You listen to the small series of beeps and the rustle of the band. Your mind quickly maps the data to the notebook margins. Tides, yes. But also the pilings by Stall Twelve that Sami mentioned, the two rotten pilings by the market, the hollow one under your neighbor Rosa’s porch. You begin to speak in inventories—materials needed, numbers of hands, weighted priorities.

Elliot: "I can run a prototype buoy at Stall Twelve, rig it with tether points for laddered walkways. We can reuse the bolted steel from the old pier. I’ve got some reclaimed lattice at the Spire that’ll do—if you sign off."

You stop mid-inventory. Agreeing is a small thing, technically; but 'sign off' is a different animal when it means trusting someone with decisions that will affect people’s roofs and thresholds. Your throat tightens.

You: "I don't sign off on prototypes without testing. People sleep on those roofs."

Elliot looks like he expected that. He shrugs, a little sheepish, then folds his hands to keep them from reaching for yours.

Elliot: "Okay. Then whatever you need to feel safe—tests, doubles, measurements. I’ll do them. I'll re-run the calculations with Noor if you want. But, Maya—" (He pauses.) "—we can’t do everything alone either."

There is subtext in the pause: he’s trying to wedge trust into a sentence without asking outright. You can feel the gentle pull. Your default is to brace.

Sami, who has been listening, leans in with a grin that’s more serious than it looks.

Sami: "Also, if you guys are doing prototypes, I want in. I have rope that survived three storms and a cousin who can weld anything from a sandwich."

You look between them—Sami’s practical bluntness, Elliot’s patient optimism. Both want to help. Both, in their own ways, are offering you the very thing you've been trained to handle alone: other people.

You rehearse the speech silently once, then let a small, bitter thing slip: the sentence you always avoid.

You (internal): If people don't show up, all these lists are just paper soaked in the next storm.

The children’s shouts pull the sound away from your chest for a moment. A kayak bumps against a sunken stall and a boy’s laugh skitters like a loose coin. The neighborhood—this messy, stubborn community—lives in the small defiance of continuing to play where the water tries to erase them. Your plans are a promise to keep that defiance possible. But asking for help has always felt like handing someone else a rope you’re still tied to.

You: "I have to rehearse the words for the meeting." (The statement is technical; you make it sound like a schedule item.) "If people see the maps and the priorities—and the numbers—maybe they’ll understand why some pilings must wait and some can't."

Elliot: "Numbers translate to human stories when you say them right." (He bites his lip.) "Say the story too."

Sami scoffs lightly. "You always make hearts and data compete. Maya, low tide's an invitation—it's when we move. We scare fewer people when the water's out of the way."

You look down at the notebook again; your handwriting loops in pressure where you'd pushed harder. There's a torn corner with Rosa’s name written in the margin. You can't be certain you can carry them all. The stone behind your ribs shifts.

The wind picks up a degree. It pushes the smell of kelp more sharply into the air, and somewhere, a tin can chimes against a piling. Your heart rate is steady; the day hums with low, practical energy. Your arousal is not a spike but a prolonged incline—small adjustments, gradual shifts, the tightening of a hinge.

You rehearse sentences until your tongue tastes of salt and the words lose their edges. You map pilings with a patient, mechanical clarity: which to prioritize, which to shore, where the temporary scaffolds can take weight. There is dignity in the work—numbers and planks and hands—but grief makes your mouth close whenever offers of help feel less like collaboration and more like risk. Giving others the rope might mean losing the control that kept your brother’s memory contained. Not giving it might mean carrying too much.

Elliot watches you when you hesitate. He doesn't push. Sami’s shrug is half-joke, half-accusation: 'You can’t keep doing this alone.'

The children’s laughter rises again, higher this time, and a little girl splashes past on an improvised raft. For a breath, you imagine time folding into that laugh—small, defiant continuity. For a breath, being practical and feeling feels like two sides of the same plank.

You realize, in a quiet way that makes your throat burn, that the whole plan depends on a single, fragile variable: people showing up.

As the idea settles, the wind steadies into the hollow of the walkway. You can feel every syllable of the sentence you haven't said yet, like a pebble catching in the tidal run.

You: "Everything depends on people showing up." (You say it like a fact. But it asks a question.)

They look at you—Sami with a half-smile, Elliot with a careful openness. The Green Spire’s lights seem to pulse once, as if in answer. The board beneath your feet creaks again, slow and waiting.

You know you can either ask for their hands or tighten the knot yourself. The sound of the harbor wraps around you. Behind your ribs, the old stone shifts.

You inhale, and the thought arrives as a small, bright point of pressure:

Can you ask them?

[Visual: Close on your hand over the notebook; the orange cover is frayed. The tide reflects the last strip of late sun.]
[Music: Piano fades to a held, unresolved chord]
[Sound: The creak of planks, muted laughter, a distant buoy bell]

You pause at the edge of the sentence, the sentence that is also a decision. The air tastes like the before of a storm—heavy, anticipatory, and full of the ordinary things that will have to be asked for if anything is to change.

You do not answer out loud yet. The meeting is hours away, the speech still rough, the lists incomplete. The Green Spire waits, Sami waits, Elliot waits. The neighborhood keeps doing what neighborhoods do: living in the interstice between tides.

You look at your hands—callused, ink-smudged—and the path forward feels both impossibly small and enormous.

A gray gull slices low across the harbor. A child’s laugh bounces off the wet wood. The wind lifts the page of your notebook and then drops it as if testing whether you’ll catch it.

You reach for the corner.
