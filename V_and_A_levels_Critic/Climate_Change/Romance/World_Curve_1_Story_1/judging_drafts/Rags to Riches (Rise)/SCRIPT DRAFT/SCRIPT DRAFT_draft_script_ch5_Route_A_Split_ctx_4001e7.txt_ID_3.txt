Your hands are a map of salt and grease; the solar pendant at your throat flickers with the reflected light of a thousand fairy bulbs.

A woman two stalls down calls out the name of a child. Someone dumps out a bucket of reclaimed gravel for ballast. Sami bows at the waist and snaps a volunteer team into formation like a quick command from an excited captain. You bark — half instruction, half comfort — and your voice catches in the damp air less than you expect. This is what you chose. This is what you promised.

Elliot: "Think of it as breathing. Short breaths now, long breaths later. If we synchronize the irrigation with the predicted surge, the mats will expand their root networks before the worst comes in."

The panel smells faintly of old rope, compost, and the metallic tang of electronics. A child paddles by on a leaky raft, waving a flattened paper pirate flag; neighbors call out encouragement like a strange benediction. Above all this, your phone buzzes in your hip — Dr. Noor.

Sami: "Already told them. Moving now. Everyone, two-meter steps toward the water — careful with vendor ropes."

Hands move in choreography: knots tighten, ballast is shifted, and the mats are eased into a position that feels like a held breath. The tide is a living thing at your ankles now, tugging and whispering of consequence.

You replay your brother's running silhouette — the older echo now and then, the sound of the storm that took him — and it clacks against your ribs in a way that sharpens rather than freezes you. That loss taught you how to weigh risk; it also taught you how to make fast maps in danger. The memory is not a door you avoid: it's a metric.

Elliot looks at you between these motions — at your fingers, at your jawline, at the yard of sea between pilings. There is a softness in his eyes that almost contradicts the pace; when he smiles it is quick and pragmatic.

Elliot: "If we time the irrigation pulses to Noor's correction and the anchors hold, the mats will intercept the worst of this surge at the market’s lip. It buys us an hour of saturation that lets roots knit."

You: "An hour is what we need to prove this works. But if anchors slip, if the mats tear, vendors lose inventory and faith in us. We can't hide the mess with a smile."

Elliot: (quiet) "We won't hide it. We'll own it, fix it, and show the improvements. That's how something durable gets built."

[Sound: A shout — Luca's liaison appearing at the fringe, voice smooth over the cacophony]

Luca's Liaison: "Maya Rios? Elliot Chen? A word, if you have a minute. Luca sent me with an offer — engineered anchors, funded installation, expedited permits. Our engineers can secure a line now and scale this across the harbor by next week. We ask only for occasional access points for maintenance and data collection."

The crowd hushes a fraction. Conversations slow; fairy lights bob like they’re listening.

You feel the old reflex — a hard, immediate no — rise like tide foam. It tastes like everything you've pushed against: private hands scooping public shoreline toward profit. Your fingers tighten on a coil of rope until the skin around the knuckles goes taut.

But pragmatism leans into your ear with a different voice. You imagine stabilized pilings across the market; you imagine the tent shelters that could be bolstered, children not paddling in frantic currents next season. You think of community confidence when things look like they work; you think of being able to point to bolstered homes and say, "We did this."

Luca's liaison is smiling in a way that practiced politicians wear. He knows the pause you are taking is valuable.

Luca's Liaison: (low, conciliatory) "We can take some of the load off you. No strings you don't want — just functional access. And we can cut through some of the permit friction you’ve hit with council."

You: (steady) "And 'functional access' reads as… ?"

Luca's Liaison: "Quarterly maintenance windows. Data-sharing for model improvements. We can craft language that leaves community oversight in place."

Elliot leans in, voice tense but level.

Elliot: "We can't trade the neighborhood's agency for speed. If this is a band-aid over design, it defeats the purpose."

Luca's Liaison: "It's not trade—it's partnership. Think of scale. Think of lives saved sooner."

Sami cuts in, fists on hips, eyebrows up.

Sami: "And who pays when the anchors fail? Who pays for repairs after a miscalculated tech push?"

Luca's Liaison: "We do. That's part of the offer. A guarantee."

You look across the halting faces of the vendors, at Juno’s hands white-knuckled on a post, at the kids making small waves. Your chestnut bun is damp; the solar pendant warms against your collarbone. You measure consequences like a surveyor — distance, slope, material — but now the measure sticks to people.

Inside, something unreckonable zips with possibility: a hybrid that lets community hold design and private capital catalyze scale. It could mean faster protection for some neighbors. But your brother's absence is a ledger you read in small print. Compromise can be a first step — or a seam that unravels later.

Elliot: (soft) "If we let them anchor, we need strict, written boundaries. Full transparency over data use. Community veto on access points. No unilateral hardware changes."

Luca's Liaison: (smile barely frayed) "Negotiable clauses. We have lawyers."

You feel the moment tilt. The tide has reached the underside of the bench where an old man sleeps; the water is no longer ankle-deep but creeping, patient. Dr. Noor’s earlier text plays in your head — peak sooner — and your inner clock runs faster.

Everything narrows to a single, sharp choice. You imagine three paths and how they smell:

- Slow, messy, democratic — neighborhood hands, a strong sense of ownership, slower rollout but resilient buy-in.
- Quick, engineered scale — anchors and funding that speed deployment but lodge strings in your work.
- An experimental push — Elliot's open-source tweak that could accelerate the mats' establishment but that carries unknown ecological and legal risk.

The neighbors look at you for the call. Your mouth tastes like salt and iron and the small, persistent ember of hope. This is the point where policy, community, and personal history braid together.
