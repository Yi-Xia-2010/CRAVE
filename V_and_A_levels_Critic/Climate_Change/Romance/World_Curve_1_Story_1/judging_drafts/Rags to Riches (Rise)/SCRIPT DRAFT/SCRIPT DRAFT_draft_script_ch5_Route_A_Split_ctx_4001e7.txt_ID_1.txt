The Spire's decision follows you down the planked walkway like a second shadow. Your hair is pinned up in its usual low bun; damp curls have escaped at the temples. The solar pendant at your throat hums, flickers—small, steady, like a heartbeat trying to be braver than it feels.

You take stock as you move: where to lash the prototype so its living panels will drink salt and not drown; how to route micro-irrigation lines through vendor stalls without cutting off anyone's access; which neighbors will keep watch through the long, crowded night. Elliot is kneeling by the prototype, palms dark with damp soil, his drafting glasses perched on his head. He’s tuning valves with a tiny screwdriver, humming under his breath — the sound is both technical and oddly domestic. When he looks up, his green eyes are bright in the fairy-light glare.

Elliot Chen: "Flow rates look good but we need three anchor points that won't corrode. If Luca's anchors meet specs, they'd solve the mechanical problem. But they're—" (he swallows) "—not community-built."

You answer without meaning to, voice flat with the day sharpening it: "Community-built takes longer, and we don't have long."

Elliot pauses, then meets your eyes. He leans back on his heels, fingers rubbing a salt line across his knuckle, and there is that half-smile you recognize—the one that starts in his hands, in the way he still talks to salvaged wood like it's a person. He speaks practical, because that's how he loves.

Elliot Chen: "There are ways to keep accountability and still move fast. Dual-signing on maintenance schedules, custody of access points, a public audit log—"

You: "Those are paperwork solutions. Paper rots faster than pilings."

He winces. "Noted. Then—boots on the dock, more bodies. Sami's already organizing five rings of volunteers. Dr. Noor just texted a tide correction. If we shift deployment window by thirty minutes, the panel will sit in the surge but not slam the pilot studs."

Your phone vibrates in your pocket. You don't look; you already know: Dr. Noor's last-minute intelligence is a thread you can pull. You can almost hear the scientist's careful, exhausted voice in the brief line of text in your head: read the charts, respect the physics, temper the rhetoric. Practical and moral, like a ledger made of tide tables.

Sami appears at your shoulder with a neon lifevest slung over one arm, energy like a snapped tidal current. He tosses you a coil of synthetic rope and grins too-wide, all teeth and wind. The crowd gathers tighter along the walkways, neighbors with patched jackets, kids wrapped in salvaged raincoats, a woman balancing a frying pan on a folding stove whose flame refuses the damp.

Sami Alvarez: "We got anchor-hands, watch-rotations, the food stall's lending us heat lamps. You're the call, Maya."

Your throat tightens in a way that is almost ritual. Every time you are called the call-holder, the memory of your brother at the edge of high water flashes—running, the sudden incapacity of the boards, then the quick, horrible silence after the gasp. The image is brief and terrible; it is also the metric you measure risk by.

You press your thumb into the pendant at your collar. The metal is cool and steady between your skin and grief and it steadies your voice.

You: "If we go with external anchors, we get scale and speed. We also hand someone physical keys to the structure. If we don't—"

You don't finish. The crowd fills in the rest with nods and a hush that tastes like the sea.

Before the choice spells itself, a man in a tan waterproof suit steps from the shadow of Luca's delivery van. He carries a tablet that glints with corporate renderings—clean lines, concrete promises. He moves with the quiet practiced politeness of someone trained to wear charm like armor.

Luca's Liaison: "Evening. Luca sends his support. Engineered anchors, installed by our crew, guaranteed specs, and expedited permits if you allow limited access points for inspections. We can fast-track this and minimize risk."

You watch his mouth as he speaks. He frames the offer like a net—the city's ledger balanced, lives protected with professional tools. The lights behind him halo the tablet and make his suit look like a uniform of inevitability.

A neighbor, older and knuckled from years of fishing, spits a small curse into the water. "You mean they'll own the damn thing after?" she asks.

The liaison smiles, practiced. "Not own. Access, for inspection and maintenance. Public oversight can be written into contract."

You think of the contracts your mother used to sign to keep a roof over the apartment after the last storm—promises framed as rescue that turned out to be forfeiture. You can feel the old reflex tighten in your ribs: close the gate, refuse any private hand. Your fingers find a wrench on the floorboard. The tools are heavy and honest.

Elliot stands, wipes his palms on his trousers, and comes to your side. He doesn't block the liaison. He also doesn't yield.

Elliot Chen: "We appreciate the offer. Could Luca's team work under community oversight? We need physical custody and the right to sever access should terms be violated."

The liaison's smile thins, just for a second, and in that sliver you see the political machine's gears. "Terms are negotiable," he says. "But we need operability and some assurances."

You feel the gathering like a physical pressure—neighbors leaning forward, waiting for your call, Sami's fingers nudging the rope at your hip. The tide laps cold around your ankles and the fairy lights blur through the salt in the air like constellations dragged low.

Elliot's hand finds yours, fingertip at the base of your thumb, as if testing that you're still there. His touch is quick, practical—no drama. But the contact unspools something inside you: not weakness, not surrender, but the distinct, fierce certainty of teamwork.

Elliot Chen: "Whatever we choose, make it reversible. Make it visible. Make the village signs as much a part of the anchor as the bolts."

You: "And keep the maintenance public—logs, cameras, audits. No unilateral lockouts."

He smiles, relief quick across his face. "Good. You're impossible to compromise with—which is precisely why I wanted you on this."

There's laughter—a short, surprised sound that rides the wind. The neighborhood's mood shifts minutely: from waiting to doing.

The prototype settles. Pipes kiss, seals click. For a suspended second the world holds its breath with you. Then the surge arrives in a rising, urgent sheet: cold water pressing under the panel, pushing, testing every bond. Your chest thuds. The pendant at your collar flashes like a lighthouse.

You watch gauges—Elliot murmurs numbers, translating the machine’s tiny anxieties into steady hands. Sami calls, "All clear on rotation!" A child splashes and shrieks with joy. Dr. Noor's updates tap through like a heartbeat, small corrections that keep the plan from snapping.

And then Luca's liaison steps closer, tablet in hand, his offer reframed in softer legalese, a carrot suspended over timelines.

This is the moment the neighborhood gave you: to call the play that will define who holds power over these pilings and, by extension, the neighborhood's future. Your brother's face flickers again—this time not as a fault-line but as a reminder: decisions have bodies at their edges.

Your fists unclench unconsciously. The tide climbs toward your knees. The fairy lights blur into bright, hopeful orbs. Around you, hands are steady; voices are taut with that dangerous, electric thing—belief.

You could refuse the corporate hand and keep the slow, democratic fight. You could accept engineered anchors and scale faster. Or you could follow Elliot's risky modification—the open-source tweak he proposed at midnight that might buy you necessary seconds but could carry ecological surprises.

Every option tastes like sea-salt and promise.
