The chair is too formal for what you know this is: not a negotiation between strangers but a fracture mended with numbers and signatures. You feel the room's air as if it's been filtered through ledger pages—cool, deliberate, and expectant. You came because the city is tired and resources are real; you came because people you've promised to protect are watching how you will weigh immediacy against long-term care.

Irene opens without theatrics. Her voice is the kind that has been trained to carry in chambers and leave no loose threads.

Irene Voss: "We do not have the luxury of ideal timelines, Ms. Rios. Concrete barriers can be mobilized at scale within months. Lives are at stake in the next season. We stabilize the fiscal situation, contractors deliver, and we buy time."

Luca slides the glossy model forward like a prop in a pitch. It reflects the lamp as though each glint were a promise made real.

Luca Marin: "We've got partners ready. The materials are certified. We can move faster than piecemeal pilots. Speed equals fewer displacements in the short run. That's the arithmetic, right? Immediate reduction in risk."

You taste the brass of the lamp in the air—clean lemon polish and the ozone of the storm outside. Elliot sits a few chairs away, hands folded, watching as if studying the light on the model, not the sheen on Luca's smile. Dr. Noor's face is in your mind—lining of caution you can't shake. You remember her voice: top-down projects look fast until the ecology untethers them.

You press up from your seat, feel the weight of a thousand small houses behind your collar bones, and speak because you were raised on the kind of stubborn that answers with detail, not slogans.

You: "I won't pretend there's time to build every perfect thing. But there are non-negotiables. Community oversight on contractor selection. Binding housing protections for residents in zones affected by construction. And a clause—the city funds pilots for living infrastructure in targeted neighborhoods so we don't lock ourselves into a brittle fix."

Irene's face is an economy of expression. Her fingers find the legal tablet at her side almost casually.

Irene Voss: (soft, deliberate) "You ask for safeguards and pilots. Reasonable on paper. But pilots cost time. Time is what we do not have. Contractor oversight must be centralized—one chain of command reduces liabilities. Deadlines cannot be porous."

Her words tighten the room like a wire pulled taut. Luca leans in, voice smooth as varnish.

Luca Marin: "We can set strict milestones and performance bonds. The city won't be left shouldering overruns. We can include a pilot, limited in scope, to test living elements—if it shows promise, we scale. But we can't allow open-ended timelines that push the entire project past the season."

Something in you responds to that pragmatism. It's the same practical muscle that makes you patch leaks at midnight. You want to see people safe first. You also know what happens when 'limited in scope' becomes 'never again'—you've seen projects swallow community processes in the name of efficiency.

Irene doesn't flinch at your demand; she catalogues it and pulls numbers from the air.

Irene Voss: "We can include oversight language—but not open veto powers. We can promise housing protections—conditional, tied to eligibility criteria to prevent speculative claims. We will fund a pilot—select neighborhoods, not the whole line. Deadlines will be firm. This is how we execute at scale."

Your throat tightens. The clauses she offers are precise and dangerous: they give you the things you asked for, filtered through frameworks that might talk policed protection into exclusion. You imagine signatures that look like salvation but feel like shackles to those who cannot jump through each bureaucratic hoop.

Elliot's voice cuts across the room—not loud, but steady enough to nudge the current.

Elliot Chen: "Conditional protections sound like a start, Irene, but you and I both know conditions often mean moving goalposts. Can the pilot have a community steering committee that has real say in performance measures? Not advisory—binding metrics."

He meets your eyes for a second; there's warmth there, and a risk. He is throwing his weight against the tide with you. Luca smiles, the kind of smile that calculates margins.

Luca Marin: "Binding metrics are possible—if they're objectively defined. We don't want ambiguous measures that allow projects to be held hostage. If you want stewardship, define clear parameters: biodiversity targets, erosion benchmarks, cadence for review."

Dr. Noor's voice—the memory of her, calm but sharp—reminds you that metrics can be both savior and trap. She taught you how to read them, how to spot the ways a single number can unravel a whole wetland.

You: "We can work with objective measures. We need community-seasoned benchmarks: lived experience as data. We need review cadences that include independent ecological verification. And there must be a clause: if living pilots perform within agreed tolerances, the city commits to halting further permanent concrete expansion in those zones."

Irene's fingers hover above the tablet. For the first time, a flicker—unexpected—crosses her expression. She is practiced at presenting inevitabilities but not at confessing doubt.

Irene Voss: (quiet) "If we put a moratorium contingent on pilot performance, we risk political paralysis. The council will demand certainty. Can you guarantee that pilots will meet ecological criteria on an accelerated timeline?"

The room leans in. Outside, a gust drums the window; the rain's tempo increases. Your pulse matches it—a staccato that makes your hands tremble at the edges of reason.

You search for language that both holds your community and is legible to the city's apparatus. You speak with the low, steady cadence that has calmed neighbors after storms.

You: "I cannot guarantee nature on a timetable. But I can guarantee rigorous methods, transparency, and community enforcement. Give us a committed pilot program with measurable milestones and independent verification, and we will co-design the metrics. That is not a hollow promise—it's labor and accountability. And it's faster, in many ways, than retrofitting damage later."

Luca taps the model as if it's a metronome.

Luca Marin: "You ask for co-design and independent verification. We can structure that into the contract—an ombudsman, perhaps, or an independent panel with defined authority. But the overall timeframe tightens. We will need an executed letter of intent within seventy-two hours to keep our procurement timelines intact. After that, delay risks our partners withdrawing."

Elliot's hand rests briefly against your knee—no question, only contact. It grounds you. His eyes are bright, but there's a shadow at the corners; he wants this to be both a technical victory and a proof of trust.

Irene studies you. The tilt of the lamp catches her brooch again, mother-of-pearl throwing a pale, precise light.

Irene Voss: (measured) "Seventy-two hours. Binding oversight language, conditional protections with defined eligibility, a living pilot in specified neighborhoods—three-year performance evaluation with independent auditors. The city commits funding contingent on milestone delivery. The moratorium you propose can be written as a conditional pledge, triggered by pilot performance metrics."

This is the point where policy and human stakes meet in a narrow room. The offer is substantive and immediate; it protects some things and leaves others precarious. There is a glimmer of hope—resources allocated, a plan with teeth. There is also the ache of compromise: deadlines tightened, oversight centralized, and the awkward truth that safeguards written by power often require constant defending.

Your pen—inked from a life of sketches, lists, and emergency fixes—rests near the edge of the table. The ledger Irene slides over is crisp, the clauses already compiled into neat, legal language. You can see lines that would create protections and lines that could, over time, gatekeep the very people you swore to protect.

Dr. Noor's face forms in your memory again, and this time her voice is less a cautionary bell and more a simple prompt: measure the risks you can live with, and make the rest auditable.

You inhale. The strings in the room swell—urgent but not panicked. There's a current here that could carry neighborhoods to safety or shift them under new foundations of control. You are not blind to either possibility. Your hands hover above the contract. The city is offering materials, money, a timetable. The community is offering lived knowledge, sweat, and trust.

You can imagine, in small bright detail, the first living pilot bed thriving at the edge of a reconstructed shore, children planting native dunes with their parents. You can also see, in colder light, fliers tacked to pilings announcing relocations because eligibility forms were misread or funds redirected.

You are the hinge.

The lamp throws a last white against Irene's brooch. Luca's smile is steady. Elliot watches as if measuring the air between you and the paper.

You inhale again, and the moment stretches like a tide pulled back before a swell.
