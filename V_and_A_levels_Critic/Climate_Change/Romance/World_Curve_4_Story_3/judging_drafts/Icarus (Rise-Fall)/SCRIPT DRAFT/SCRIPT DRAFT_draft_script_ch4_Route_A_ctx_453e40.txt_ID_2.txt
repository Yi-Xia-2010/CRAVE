You stand beside Elias, wind tugging at the hem of your field jacket. Elias grins at the jitter of activity. "Looks like the harbor finally decided to show up," he says, voice warm with mischief and something softer underneath.

Elias reaches across and slides the pins into the board with his bare fingers, tiny grooves of salt in his palms. He mends a ripped flyer with a strip of colorful fabric while he works, humming. The small repair feels like a promise in miniature.

Iris: (nodding at the charts) "This finally looks like something folks can hold onto. Not just numbers, but the why."

You feel the familiar tightening in your chest — the old guilt that taught you to measure everything twice. It is quieter today, folded into a steadier thing: determination. Around you, people begin to read, to sign, to argue softly over details; a child traces the contour lines on your map with a grubby finger.

[Scene: Boardwalk / Old Pier | Early Evening]
[Visual: Folding chairs in a semi-circle, a whiteboard balanced between crates; the crowd flanking, sea like a living wall beyond them]
[Music: Rhythmic percussion — hearts synced to a march]

Professor Julian Kim arrives, thermos in hand, hair wind-tousled, spectacles slipping down his nose. He sets up a laptop with a clumsy reverence and starts translating your models into plain sentences. When he says "sea-level equivalent to a year's worth of rain in a week," a low murmur ripples through the crowd — shock braided with a new clarity.

Professor Kim: (clearing his throat) "If we build modular platforms and invest in marsh restoration, we can reduce storm surge impact locally by measurable margins in five to ten years. The models show—"

You cut in, because you've seen the eyes that glaze when numbers fly: "—and the marsh is not just protection. It's livelihoods. Spawning grounds, nursery habitat. We can't sell this as only a defense."

Professor Kim smiles at you, small and tired. "Right. Numbers with context. I'll start there."

Samir angles his camera, catching Professor Kim's half-smile and the way people lean in when scientific language turns human. Signatures pile on sheets — fishers who know the tideline, shop owners who remember the last flood, families who want their children's toys to survive.

Elias: (to you, voice nearly a shout over the noise) "We did it. They heard us."

You want to let your shoulders drop, to admit relief. Instead you feel the old pebble sharpen into something else: protectiveness. You remember the nights of broken promises. You remember the cost of being second to expediency.

The mood on the screen shifts; a new feed cuts in — Maren Voss at a press conference, her face composed, a sharp silhouette against studio lights.

Maren Voss: (on-screen) "I will oppose any funding allocation that does not ensure Harborwell's immediate economic survival. If necessary, I will take this to a referendum. The town must not gamble its jobs on an untested co-op experiment."

A tremor of unease runs through the gathering. The victory tastes suddenly thin. Maren's words are not simply opposing policy — they are a public threat, a line drawn for everyone to see.

Mayor Ana's eyes flick to you over the dais, an unspoken calculus in them. After the feed, she steps away from the chamber, approaching the crowd with the practiced softness of someone who must broker storms as well as weather them.

Mayor Ana: (lowered voice) "Maya. This is a win, but it's fragile. Maren will mobilize hard — donors, PR. We need a plan to lock this in without blowing the community's unity."

You reply before thinking, urgency quickening the words. You: "We need to make it so the community's stake is unassailable. Visible work, tangible beginnings. Show them salt marshes breathing."

Mayor Ana studies you. "There are political levers — a quiet path to secure partial funding fast. But it asks for compromises, and compromises are what break movements when trust is thin."

Elias: "We built this from the ground up. We won't—"

Iris squeezes your other hand, voice low and steady. "Protect it, yes. But how we do that will be the part that either seeds or sours everything."

Samir snaps photos, fast and deliberate — cataloguing not just faces but the twitch of resolve in your jaw.
