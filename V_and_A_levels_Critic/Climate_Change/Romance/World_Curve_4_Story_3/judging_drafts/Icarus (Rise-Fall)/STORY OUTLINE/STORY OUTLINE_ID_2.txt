## Visual Novel Title
Harborwell Tides

---

## World View
Harborwell is a mid-sized coastal town in 2035 where tides have crept into everyday life and policy debates are literal shorelines. Economically, it hangs between small fisheries, family shops, and an emergent redevelopment lobby promising jobs through a large-scale commercial retrofit and sea wall. Politically, a pragmatic municipal center (Mayor Ana Delgado) negotiates between grassroots organizers and municipal developers; Maren Voss represents a technocratic, top-down approach backed by external capital. Culture is localist and tactile: dockside storytelling, fish stalls, rooftop gardens, and community festivals that now become logistical nightmares when storms arrive. Religion and ritual are modest—memorial shrines to lost homes and communal tide-watch vigils—while class lines harden between those who can afford raised foundations and those who cannot. Geography is tight: Tidewatch Harbor and the Boardwalk meet glassy dunes where Glasshouse Research Lab studies wetlands; the abandoned sea wall is a ledger of past politics and promises. Atmosphere is populated by salt, fog-diffused sunsets, wet wood gleams, and an undercurrent of urgency about resources and relationships. Romance and climate are intertwined: personal choices ripple like tides in a community that must choose who it shelters and who it abandons.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Maya Reyes, a coastal resilience planner returned to Harborwell to repair a town I once failed. As municipal debates heat, an initial momentum of community hope — led by Elias Hart and local co-ops — aligns with my technical proposals, producing a momentary victory: grant funding, media attention, and mass volunteerism. That success is intoxicating: my chestnut braid is pulled tight, my field journal fills with plans, and Elias’s sea-green eyes mirror our momentum. Then the pressure of economics, Maren Voss’s redevelopment push, and an urgent storm timetable force decisions that trade safety for speed. Each route through the story is a different moral calculus: band together for modular, community-led adaptation; accept a partial compromise to protect some while sacrificing others; or ally with institutional power for immediate protection and jobs. Across branches, the narrative follows an Icarus arc: a sharp rise into a hopeful apex and then a catastrophic descent where choices translate into loss, fracture, or moral clarity. Romance—between me and Elias—complicates every choice, and every ending shades the town’s fate with personal consequence: local triumphs, bitter compromises, deep losses, or quiet principled resistance.

From Chapter 4 onward valence declines across all branches (Positive → Neutral/Negative → VeryNegative in some variants), ensuring each branch traces the rise-to-crash arc.

- Route depth constraint respected: Any path from Chapter 1 to an ending contains at most six chapters (e.g., 1→2→3→4→5→6 or 1→2→3→7→8→9, etc.).
- Characters, visual traits, and locations strictly adhere to provided designs: Maya (chestnut-brown hair, hazel eyes, olive jacket, field journal, compass locket), Elias (sandy-blond hair, sea-green eyes, denim jacket, wooden whistle), Maren (sleek black bob, steel-gray eyes, charcoal trench), Iris, Professor Julian, Mayor Delgado, and Samir appear per world setting.
- POV is consistently first-person (Maya), focusing on internal guilt, ethical calculations, and relationship tensions that bind climate choices to intimacy.
