# Route: Route B — Ending: Bittersweet


[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Late Morning]
[Visual: The glass conference room overlooking wet pavement and the neon blur of storefront signs. Blueprints and holographic models glow on the table.]
[Sound: The muffled hum of HVAC, the occasional ring of a notification; distant gulls are swallowed by city noise.]
[Music: Tense, insistent strings building beneath dialogue]

You enter with Mateo’s blueprints tucked in their waterproof tube, the tube warm from his hand. The memory of the Council vote — your recommendation for the municipal hybrid — still hangs on your tongue like salt.

Councilor Nguyen sits at the head of the table, palms steepled. He is the picture of procedural calm, but his eyes flick to his tablet every few seconds. Pressure has a face here: a bald timeline, an election calendar with days circled in red.

Councilor Nguyen: "Ms. Navarro, thank you. We need to agree on metrics today. Recovery percentage, planting survival rates, displacement figures. We measure, we report, we show results."

You feel the room tighten around those metrics. Data as a lever; the lever has weight. You notice how the word 'community' is already condensed into a footnote in one of the projected slides.

Aiko Navarro: "We should include qualitative indicators — fishing access, seasonal knowledge transfer, elder-led patrols. Those can't be reduced to a single survival percentage."

Mateo Ríos: "We can translate those into proxy metrics. Participation counts, scheduled transect checks, recorded oral histories digitized and timestamped. It's clumsy, but—"

Tala's name arises in your mind like a flare. You can see her, arms folded, at the Aquarium, bristling at anything that sounds like formalization of their lived memory. You picture Old Man Rohan's slow, careful way of explaining tides with a story, not a chart.

Councilor Nguyen: (patient, then firmer) "Proxy metrics are viable, Ms. Navarro. The council needs comparables across districts. If we can't show progress in ninety days, funding becomes politically untenable."

The sentence lands. Ninety days. The timeline compresses the tide into a deadline.

You answer, because silence would be assent: "We can pilot the proxy approach, but we must build in review points that let us adjust methods if they fail to capture what matters."

A murmur. A councilor taps their pen. Across the table, Mateo's jaw loosens into an almost-smile. Relief smells like coffee and urgency.

Councilor Nguyen: "Make them explicit. Submit a monitoring plan by Friday. Include costs, responsibilities, and community liaison roles."

You nod. You write it down in your waterproof notebook, thumb pressing into the page until the paper creases. The notes already look insufficient.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand as you underline the phrase "community liaison".]
[Sound: Your pen on paper is loud as thunder in the room's thin air.]

You leave the meeting with the tube under your arm and a hollow tightening in your chest. Outside, the city presses at the glass; a municipal pickup idles, its hazard lights blinking like a heartbeat.

[Scene: Tidepark Municipal Lab | Afternoon]
[Visual: Reclaimed warehouse split into clinical bays and indoor marsh beds. Stainless steel meets peat; LED grow strips cast everything in a clinical cyan.]
[Sound: The low whirr of centrifuges, soft beeps of monitoring rigs, and the slap of wet boots on concrete.]
[Music: Percussive, rapid — a heartbeat accelerating]

Mateo walks beside you between beds where seedlings are arranged in neat grids, labels clipped like little flags. There's comfort in the order here. Machines take variables and make them legible.

Mateo Ríos: (softly) "They're running the first set of survival simulations. The models predict a seventy-two percent take rate under current tidal forecasts."

You study the seedlings. They look small and misplaced in their tidy rows.

You: "Our elders talk about micro-topography — subtle hollows where seedlings survive storm surges. The grids flatten that variability."

Mateo: (hesitating) "We're trying to balance reproducibility with nuance. For the pilot, officials want clearly attributable results."

You can hear the pull in his voice: the knowledge that measurable success will unlock the next tranche of funding, the resources that could give the community time. He looks at you like someone asking permission for a compromise.

Old Man Rohan stands nearby, fingers stained dark from peat. He points to a marsh bed without looking at the instruments.

Old Man Rohan: "You put them in straight lines, they drown same as they dry. Tides don't like tidy. They eat your plans."

A lab technician straightens, uncomfortably. A spreadsheet flashes on a screen: "SURVIVAL RATE: 72% — FORECAST ACCURATE."

You: (quiet, but sharp) "The models are useful — but they have blind spots. If we don't let place talk, data will tell a different story."

Rohan's words hang heavier than any output.

Mateo: (touching your arm) "We can do both. We can map the hollows, then run the grid where it fits. I promise I'll push for the mixed protocol in my report."

You want to believe him fully. You do, with the part of you that counts tides and probabilities. But another part — the part that keeps a pressed bit of marsh grass inside her notebook — is already bracing for erosion.

Rohan watches the exchange between you and Mateo with a slow, unreadable expression. He says nothing more, but when he leaves he pats the lab's metallic rail as if testing whether it's warm enough to hold memory.

A notification blinks on Mateo's tablet: Council wants a preliminary report tonight. The urgentness sharpens to a blade.

Mateo: (voice low, urgent) "If we present mixed-methods as the primary protocol, Nguyen will ask for stronger assurances. If we prioritize the municipal metrics, we get scale. We can't have both without time."

You feel the air pull thin. Choices feel less like options and more like a narrowing channel.

[Scene: Community Hub - 'The Aquarium' | Early Evening — Town Meeting]
[Visual: The repurposed aquarium is crowded: string lights, clusters of people, damp coats hanging on hooks. The tile murals of sea life are patched with protest flyers.]
[Sound: The room is a wash of voices — whispers, murmurs, a rising chorus of frustration. The ocean beyond the walls drums a steady, impatient tempo.]
[Music: Tense crescendo; a tremor of discordant piano]

Tala stands at the makeshift podium with a bullhorn cradled like a talisman. Her purple hair is a flame under the lights. She looks at you before she speaks and then at the crowd, and the silence she holds is alive with accusation.

Tala: (sharp) "They promised community-led decisions. Now City Hall's saying 'pilot' and 'metrics' and 'comparables.' That is not how we remember our marsh."

Ivy stands beside Tala, hands clenched, anxiety and practical fear visible on her face.

Ivy: "We need places to fish tomorrow. We need roofs tonight. If the pilot takes sides with the machines, who will keep our families housed when the tide comes higher?"

A chorus of agreement rises. Old Man Rohan sits forward on a folding chair, eyes small and furious.

Dr. Selene Park arrives in a sheen of calm, accompanied by polished assistants and a live-streaming crew. Her AR spectacles reflect the room like ice. She is all lit edges and polished promises.

Dr. Selene Park: (measured, public-facing) "Municipal partnerships are the pragmatic path forward. Technology scaled appropriately, with oversight, protects more people, faster. We are offering resources, and City Hall's pilot is the accountable framework to deploy them."

Her words fall into the room like neat stones. Some people reach for them; others flinch.

Tala: (interrupting, voice cracking) "So the company sweeps in, puts a sticker on our marsh, and we clap? Where's the part where we decide what to keep? Where's Rohan's map? Where's the story of how this place lived?"

Dr. Selene Park: (cool) "Community wisdom is valuable — and we can incorporate it through formal liaison roles."

The phrase is a smooth parry. But you can see how 'liaison' already smells of translation by other people's tongues.

Mateo: (stepping between you and Dr. Park) "We aren't asking anyone to be erased. The pilot is a way to secure funding for everyone's needs."

Tala: "Funding that erases who we are isn't security."

The room fractures into small quarrels. Voices compete like gulls fighting over a fish. You feel the pressure like a physical thing against your sternum.

You: (to Tala, low) "I pushed for the municipal hybrid because I believed it would give us leverage. You know I mean it."

Tala: (bitter laugh) "Do I? Or did you believe the council would protect us and forgot to tell us how much of us they'd be willing to cut out?"

Her words sting — clear salt in your mouth. There is a crowd watching. Live streams hum. Dr. Park's PR team smiles under the light as if everything is working according to design.

Old Man Rohan: (soft, to the room) "We have to ask not just if it works, but what it costs. Money and science can give you numbers; they can't put your grandmother back into her house."

For a heartbeat, the room holds on the image of loss. The crowd exhales together, a wave pulling away from the shore.

You fold your hands on the table, feeling splinters under your palms. The pilot's timeline is a drumbeat now, the election a clock with teeth. You have to decide how to respond to the institutional pressure that grows like a storm on the horizon.

The room is fractious, the air metallic with expectation. Tala stares at you like a wound and Dr. Selene's cameras find every furrow in your brow. Mateo's hand finds yours briefly and then pulls away toward his tablet, fingers flexing over a draft.

You taste fear, and beneath it a fierce, animal instinct to protect. You think of the locket against your chest, the pressed marsh grass inside your notebook, Rohan's hands in wet peat. You think of Ivy's small, practical anxieties, of Councilor Nguyen's circled dates.

This is the point where one decision will push the pilot down one axis or another. You feel time like a tightening line.
