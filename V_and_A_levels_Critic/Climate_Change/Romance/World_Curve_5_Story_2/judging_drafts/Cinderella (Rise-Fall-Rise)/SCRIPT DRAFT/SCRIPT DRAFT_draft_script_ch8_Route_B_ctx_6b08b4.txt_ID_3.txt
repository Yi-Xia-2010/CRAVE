[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Damp soil beds under diffused skylight; technicians moving between racks of prototype scaffolds]
[Sound: Low whir of dehumidifiers; soft clink of metal tools; distant gulls]
[Music: Tense, urgent strings — insistent pulse]

You are here because you said yes. You remember the room — the aquarium's stretching hush, voices like currents — and how the word "scale" left your mouth with a taste of salt and something like promise. The municipal rollout was not a betrayal in black and white; it was a calculation, and you made it believing numbers could buy time. You made it believing you could steer a machine that wanted to make everything tidy.

The greenhouse smells of wet peat and ozone. Your boots leave soggy prints along the concrete path. Technicians kneel beside rows of replicated scaffold frames — woven bamboo and composite mesh, the municipal technicians' version of your careful prototypes. They work in synchronized, practical gestures: measure, tighten, mark, log.

Mateo is already here, sleeves rolled, a tablet humming with graphs in his hands. He hasn't smiled yet; the crease between his brows has deepened into a line you know means negotiations are still happening in his head.

Mateo Ríos: "Readings came in from Plot C. We have a 1.9% increase in trapped sediment overnight. Salinity dipped where we added the scaffold buffer."

You want to be pleased — 1.9% is something. It is not salvation, but it is a number that breathes. Instead a smaller sound tightens in your chest: the new technicians' hands are faster but rougher; they knot the mesh differently. You catch a technician reinforcing posts with a faster grade of epoxy, not the biodegradable resin you specified. Your throat goes dry.

You step closer until the technician looks up, startled.

You: "Hold up. The resin — that's not the blend we tested. It'll change the degradation curve."

Technician (young, apologetic): "We needed something that sets faster for the city schedule. They gave us a proc order."

Your mind runs a checklist: degradation curve, nutrient leaching, fish larvae entrapment. The greenhouse is supposed to be where your slow, iterative knowledge meets municipal capacity. In practice it's where you translate nuance into protocols someone else will read once and then apply over a thousand shorelines.

Mateo comes to your side, lowers his voice but keeps the urgency threaded through it.

Mateo Ríos: "We can flag it in the logs and swap the shipment next run. For now — we keep building. The pilot needs continuity."

You want to say: continuity at the cost of the marsh's breathing is not continuity. You want to say: we cannot let shortcuts make the river lie to us. Instead, you press your thumb along the edge of your locket, feeling the tiny map beneath the metal. The map is all coastline and absent houses. It reminds you of what this is for.

You: "Flag it. Record it. And run a parallel piece with the original resin on Plot D. If the city insists on speed, we need a clean comparator."

Mateo nods, reluctance shaded with the lawyer's pragmatism he sometimes adopts.

Mateo Ríos: "I'll re-prioritize technical resources. But you should know — if the Council hears any further delays, they'll push the rollout schedule. They want metrics and progress on the books."

Your internal voice argues with what you are seeing: progress when measured by ledger lines; loss when measured by memory and mouths that won't get fed. The greenhouse's humid air feels like a skin that will not let you breathe freely.

[Scene: The Aquarium (Community Hub) | Evening — Doors Open, Protest Rhythm Building]
[Visual: String lights, cracked tile murals; clusters of people, placards leaning against columns]
[Sound: Murmurs rising into chants; a megaphone crackle; the distant pounding of a heart-shaped drum]
[Music: Percussive, rising — heartbeat under words]

You leave the greenhouse with sand on your boots and the taste of copper under your tongue. The Aquarium has become the town's shared throat: warm breath, flickering candles, arguments that smell of coffee and the sea. The protest is not spontaneous; it's organized — Tala's flyers pasted like small flags, lists of grievances, procedural demands. Your phone buzzes with messages — angry, pleading, impatient.

Tala meets you near the mural of a faded whale. Her purple hair is streaked with damp salt; her eyes are bright and blown wide like a warning.

Tala Kumari: "You promised ownership, Aiko. You told everyone we'd be the authors. Instead, you brought their machines and their contracts."

The word "you" lands heavy. It's not just a finger-point; it is a rope tugged. People gather, listening. Old Man Rohan stands by the columns, stooped but steady; Ivy is there too, arms folded and jaw set.

You feel every pair of eyes as an accusation and an inventory of trust. You want to dismantle Tala's words into data and rebuild them into reassurance. You want to tell her: I am trying to save as many as possible. You want to explain that municipal resources can scale what we invented. Instead, you hear your voice first because not speaking would be a larger violence:

You: "Tala — I brought the models to City Hall because I thought it would let us protect more ground. We needed funding to make the prototype replicable. I wanted the community at the table with the power to say no to things that harm the marsh."

Tala steps closer, her voice low but sharp.

Tala Kumari: "Table with the power to say no? Aiko, the contract gives you a seat that can watch them draw the plan. It doesn't bind them. They can pick and choose. What we need is guaranteed governance, not honorary attendance."

Old Man Rohan interjects, his voice like driftwood rubbing against rope.

Old Man Rohan: "When my father left this bay, he marked stakes and sang names into the mud. Things the city can't measure have kept us alive longer than any algorithm. Don't let them take song out of the shore."

The room fragments into a hundred small arguments. A young technician tries to explain the latest positive draft numbers, but the spreadsheet is a poor salve for mouths and memories.

Ivy corners you with a fury that's brief and blazing.

Ivy Navarro: "I cook for the crews. I patch roofs. If you don't make sure our people stay, what use is your 'scale'? We don't want a model that shows success on paper while my aunt moves inland in a truck."

Your chest tightens. Everything you were trying to hold — data, compassion, procedural leverage — feels like a brittle plate at the edge of a great table. Someone begins to chant; someone else begins to cry.

You do both in the space of a breath: you catch Tala's eye and then climb; you reach Mateo and slip him the list while the crowd listens. Words spill, and so do small violences — interruptions, accusations. The Aquarium's murals watch like old witnesses as the town frays at the seams.

A technician shows you a live feed on his phone: Plot A's sensor cluster reports a sudden drop in a micro-invertebrate population. The technician swears it's coincidental, a deployment hiccup. Your throat closes. The pilot that proves you right is also recording the marks of harm.

[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Late Night]
[Visual: Glass and steel lit by pale office lamps; blueprints stacked like briefs; a round table of tired faces]
[Sound: Rain beginning outside, the fat, fast lash against the window; the digital tick of notification pings]
[Music: Low, looming strings — pressure building to a cusp]

You arrive exhausted. The courthouse smell — sharp paper, stale coffee, disinfectant — is another layer of the night's skin. Mateo is already speaking with Councilor Nguyen and two aides. A projection on the wall shows the greenhouse models stitched into municipal maps. Numbers march in neat columns: sediment accretion, reduced overtopping probability, cost per meter.

Councilor Nguyen (measured, practiced): "These indicators are encouraging. We can present this as progress to our funding partners. We approve moving to the next phase."

The words are precise as scalpels. Approval. Progress. Your mind runs a thousand possibilities: funding comes with strings, strings become clauses, clauses become the scaffolding that decides whose memory the shoreline will keep.

You press for the one thing that matters to you right now — community authorship that is binding — because without it all the work will be a thin veneer over displacement.

You: "Councilor Nguyen, will the oversight seat have veto power on materials and site selection?"

Councilor Nguyen folds his hands once, the practiced politician's pause.

Councilor Nguyen: "The seat will be advisory, Ms. Navarro. It will ensure community concerns are heard and logged. Binding decisions rest with the municipal authority for the purposes of liability and compliance."

The word "advisory" hits like a small stone thrown into a pond. Ripples, not walls. You feel a cold place in your chest where trust collected; it leaks.

Mateo reaches for your hand and squeezes it, a private, human pressure in a room of sterile diplomacy.

Mateo Ríos: "We get procurement oversight written into the monitoring plan, maybe conditional clauses on material use. It won't be perfect, but it's a lever."

You want to press harder — to insist on enforceable guarantees — but you have seen the thinness of leverage when pressed against timelines and electoral calendars. The Council wants a deliverable it can show the funders. The funders want quick wins to justify scale. The scale flattens the jagged edges of lived experience.

From the adjacent room you hear a muffled shout; someone at the Aquarium is being pushed by a cop. The sound travels like a shockwave, even through the glass. You think of Ivy's face, of Rohan's slow hands, of Tala's worn fists.

You were promised the capacity to translate human memory into policy. Instead the policy hands you a seat that reads the minutes back to you like an echo.

Technicians report a small statistical anomaly: Plot B shows a net gain in sediment but an unexpected redistribution pattern that could stress a neighboring eelgrass patch. The model didn't predict this reallocation. Mateo sees the readout and his face registers the new variable — the one that tilts a win into a question.

Mateo Ríos: "We run a targeted mitigation tomorrow. Redirect flows, increase monitoring window, check biodiversity nets."

You nod, cataloguing the mitigation as another thing that must be done. Mitigation is also work the community will absorb if the municipal machine retrenches. Your chest is tight. You have a seat at the table, but the table keeps shifting.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand, fingers curled around a clause printed in Councilor Nguyen's draft — "advisory oversight" underlined in neat blue pen]
[Sound: Rain intensifies; a distant megaphone wails]

You realize how tired you are of translating grief into charts that can be read by strangers. You realize the pilot gives you numbers that someone else can use to say they 'saved' a place while quietly erasing the people who lived there. The greenhouse produced evidence; the City Hall produced authority; the Aquarium produced anger. None of these pieces fit together the way you thought they would.

You stand in the planning office, the rain a percussion on the window. There is a low, urgent humming in your ears — not the greenhouse machines now, but something inside you quickening, a fear that unfurls into something colder: the town might be protected in spreadsheets but not in life.

For a moment, you imagine the locket warm against your sternum, the tiny map pressed into silver. The map does not know legislation. It knows tides and names.

[Music: Climactic rise — percussion like a dozen heartbeats]
[Visual: Fade to the faces in the room — Mateo's; Councilor Nguyen's unreadable placidity; your own reflected eyes in the glass]

You taste salt and iron. The room holds its breath.
