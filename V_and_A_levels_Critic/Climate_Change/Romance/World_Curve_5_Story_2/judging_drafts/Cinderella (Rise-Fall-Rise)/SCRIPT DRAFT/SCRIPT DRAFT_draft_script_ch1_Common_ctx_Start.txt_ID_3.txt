[Scene: Low Street and Docks | Morning]
[Music: Soft ambient tide; distant gulls]
[Sound: Low foghorn, muted footsteps on wooden planks]
[Visual: Salt-streaked rooftops as morning fog lifts; puddles mirror a pale sky]

You open the waterproof notebook and find the pressed blade of marsh grass folded between two pages. Your thumb traces its brittle edge—the same slope and bend you memorized as a child while poking at tidal pools. The paper smells faintly of brine and algae; your fingertip remembers the dampness it kept.

Your hands are callused where wading boots have rubbed and where you used to haul nets with Old Man Rohan. You flex the fingers—small, efficient motions that catalog the world: measure, note, file away. The silver locket at your throat clicks softly when you tilt your chin. Inside is a sliver of shoreline drawn on a scrap of paper, a map that always looks smaller than the loss it represents.

You stand on Low Street. The town exhales: diesel and kelp braided together, a warm metallic tang undercut by sweet rot. Corrugated sheets glint where the fog peels back, and gulls argue over a spool of frayed rope. You pull up the hood of your faded navy raincoat—patched elbows like quiet badges—and step toward the docks.

[Visual: Close-up of tide markers—faded paint, handwritten notches; a bent piling leaning into the water]
[Narration]
You walk the planks slowly, notebook tucked under your arm, fingers finding the pencil behind your ear. You speak to the crates and ropes more out of habit than need, a running commentary that makes the work feel less solitary.

You: "Erosion here—two centimeters at last high tide. Pilings leaning starboard along the second berth. Old wall scar at meter mark three."

No one answers, but the harbor returns a slate-smooth silence. You make a small, neat sketch: a map of bent pilings, lines of bare earth where marsh used to press up against the sea wall. You press the pencil harder where the scar is deepest.

From across the quay, a laugh slides over the water—a warm, easy sound you know by contour. Mateo leans against the rail, blueprints tucked under his arm, sea-spray flecking his cuffs. The linen blazer looks unexpectedly soft in this light; he has sand on his trainers like he tracked the shore with him.

Mateo: "You always talk to the sea like it's an old friend that owes you rent."

You pause, then grin despite the ache in your shoulders. You close the notebook a fraction, then set it atop a crate as if placing a small claim on the morning.

You: (soft) "She owes all of us something. Preferably not more storms."

Mateo crosses the planks with easy steps. Up close, he folds his blueprints and offers them without ceremony.

Mateo: "I've been going over the preliminary zoning. Thought you might want to see where the council thinks they'll draw the line."

You take the tube. It's warm from his hands. The paper inside smells faintly of coffee and printer toner: municipal hygiene.

You: "Council lines change with whichever envelope arrives first. What's the hard number?"

Mateo hesitates, a small furrow between his brows—your cue to know he's balancing politics against people.

Mateo: "They're running simulations. If the storm model shifts another tenth of a degree, the low-income strip gets prioritized for relocation. I tried to push the buffer outward, but—" (he lets the sentence hang) "—they want defensible assets."

You look at him, and the harbor seems to inhale. There are things you can measure and things you have to feel. This is one of the latter.

You: "Defensible for whom?"

Mateo: (measured) "For the infrastructure that pulls in revenue. It's practical, Aiko. It's what keeps the wider funding doors open."

You fold your hands around the tube as if to steady both it and the world it represents.

You: "Practical isn't always just. Jobs, safety, dignity—people want all three, Mateo. If we edge every pocket of community out for a 'defensible asset,' there's nothing left to defend but a map."

Mateo glances at the docks—the crammed little homes, the market stall where Ivy sells morning catches, the patched awnings that hold up under wind like stubborn stitches.

Mateo: "I know. That's why I wanted to talk to you. You have the community trust; the data needs the stories. If anyone can make them listen to both, it's you."

You feel the familiar tug: the weight of responsibility you carry toward people who stayed while the sea took houses and neighbors. Your first instinct is to shoulder whatever comes next quietly—find a solution, present it, fix what can be fixed.

You: (soft, honest) "I still don't know how to translate a tide chart into dignity."

Mateo smiles, small and sympathetic.

Mateo: "Start with what people say they need. Numbers are persuasive, but stories seed the same space. Bring them together."

His voice is low; the morning keeps its peace. You write "jobs. safety. dignity." under the map sketch, each word a careful tally.

[Scene: Low Street Market | Mid-Morning]
[Sound: Murmured bargaining, bicycle bell, a kettle boiling at a stall]
[Visual: Ivy at her stall—paint-splattered apron, halogen light over mackerel and scallops]

Ivy lifts a tin lid and hands you a wrapped piece of something warm. Her grin is quick, habitual—like she has a spare laugh for every trouble.

Ivy: "Don't tell me you're saving your appetite for council meetings. Eat. Then scold your elders for making you worry."

You accept the wrap, warm and salty. Your fingers remember childhood suppers, plates scraped under gaslight. The market smells of smoke and citrus; someone nearby strings fishbones on a washing line to dry.

You: (half-smile) "I've got my notebook. I'm scolding in ink today."

Ivy: (frowning) "Ink won't stop water. Promise me one thing—don't let them take our dock stalls. How will I pay the rent if they decide the sea is more valuable than my oven?"

You swallow. You think of the mothers who patch nets at night, of kids who learn the tide clock before school bells. You want to promise everything and know you can't.

You: "Ivy. I won't let them make 'move' the only option on the table. Not without us having tried every alternative."

Ivy: (softening) "Good. Somebody's got to be stubborn."

She pats your shoulder like a benediction, then turns to a customer. The gesture should comfort you; it does—just enough.

[Scene: Old Man Rohan's Wharf | Late Morning]
[Visual: Rohan mending a net under a striped tarp; the salt etched in his beard]
[Sound: Scissors cutting twine; the creak of an old boat]

Old Man Rohan looks up when you approach. His eyes gloss over with the kind of memory that maps tides against birthdays.

Old Man Rohan: (gravelly) "You still write it down, eh? Can't trust the surgeons of the city to remember where the good mud is."

You settle on a crate. Your knees remember the way you learned to balance on these boards.

You: "You taught me to watch the marsh lines, Rohan. I write them so others don't forget."

Old Man Rohan stabs a finger at the water, not unkindly.

Old Man Rohan: "The marsh remembers. We forget. Keep your ear to both."

He tells you a small story—about a winter the marsh swallowed a pier and then gave up a headlamp a month later. It's not data; it's a cadence of belonging. You note it anyway, because stories anchor numbers to people.

Old Man Rohan: "Don't let them paper over the stories with shiny algorithms."

You: "I won't."

There's a pause. He looks at you and then, almost shyly, places a small strip of tar on your palm—a remnant of rope mending.

Old Man Rohan: "For keeping things tied."

You accept it like a talisman.

[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Translucent panels, humid air; rows of wet soil beds and cool LED strips]
[Sound: Water drip, low hum of dehumidifiers]
[Music: Quiet, reverent synth pads]

You duck into the greenhouse to check on a sample bed—part habit, part need to see living things that respond to care.

Tala is there, sleeves rolled, arranging seedlings with a meticulous tenderness. Her purple hair is damp; she looks up and brightens.

Tala: "You're here. I grabbed some of the community's survey notes—people want work programs that don't feel like charity."

You kneel at a bench, fingertips brushing the peat. The air smells warm and loamy, a different kind of salt. You think about the ethics of planting engineered roots where elders remember wild grasses.

You: "We can design labor programs around restoration—training locals, paying fair wages. I want something that builds skills, not dependency."

Tala: (eager) "And public events. Teach kids to map the marsh. Make it ours, not theirs."

You imagine children with little clipboards tracing tide patterns, voices making the shoreline into a memory that resists erasure. It's the kind of thing that roots planning in people.

You laugh softly. It's a promise and a strategy.

[Scene: Community Hub - 'The Aquarium' | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Cracked tile murals, warm string lights; folding chairs arranged in a loose circle]
[Sound: Low conversation, a kettle on the stove, distant sea]

You sit with the notebook open on your lap. The pressed marsh grass peeks from between pages like a bookmark for what matters. People drift in—neighbors, activists, a council intern who knows more metrics than manners. Conversation is gentle, the kind that folds work into tea-steeped rhythms.

You write: "Draft pitch: community restoration plan. Objectives—jobs, habitat, dignity. Combine seasonal work programs, local governance board, small-scale engineered supports."

You flip pages, fill margins with notes and concerns. The line between 'data' and 'story' crosses your writing like a seam. You feel the familiar pull to make everything tidy—graphs, quotes, budgets—so the council cannot dismiss people's lives as anecdote.

Ivy appears in the doorway, a tray of pastries balanced against her hip.

Ivy: "You look like someone who needs sugar before saving the town."

You smile and accept a pastry, then close the notebook for a moment and let the warm lamplight settle over the words you just wrote.

[Narration]
You feel the weight of the locket at your throat again—tiny map folded into metal—the thing that reminds you that home is a series of shapes and losses. You think of Mateo's patient, consensus-seeking calm; of Old Man Rohan's stubborn, weathered lore; of Tala's insistence that work be work and dignity be a paycheck. The impulses seem to pull you in three directions at once: scientific rigour, community memory, and a practical plan that people can live with.

You jot one last line: "Pitch a plan—community-led marsh restoration." The handwriting leans a little with resolve.

You close the notebook. The press of the cover is decisive, soft as a breath but final enough to make the words stop moving.

You stand. The light outside the Aquarium has shifted toward a cooler shade; the day's fog has thinned but not gone, as if the town itself were holding its breath.

You: (to yourself) "I'll pitch it."

There is no trumpet, no sudden applause—only the sanded sound of a town that has always had to plan in quieter ways. Your resolve is not a flare but a slow, steady ember that will need tending.

[Visual: Close-up of the closed notebook; marsh grass edge still visible]
[Music: Single soft chord, then a gentle fade]

You take one more look at the harbor, at Mateo leaning against the rail, at Ivy arranging chairs, at Rohan's hand steady on a net. In the hush between tides, you feel the first clear outline of what you'll say—how you'll hold data and story in the same hand.

You tuck the pen into the notebook's spine, loop the strap, and let the locket rest against the cover as if lending it some small courage.
