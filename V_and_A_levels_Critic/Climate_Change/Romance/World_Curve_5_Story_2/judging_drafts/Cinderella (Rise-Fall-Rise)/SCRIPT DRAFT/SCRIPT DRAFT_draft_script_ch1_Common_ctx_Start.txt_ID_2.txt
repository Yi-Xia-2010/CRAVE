[Scene: Low Street & Docks | Early Morning]
[Visual: Fog lifting off salt-streaked roofs; puddles catch soft gold]
[Sound: Distant gulls, a slow thud of a mooring line]
[Music: Sparse, ambient piano — slow tempo]

You open the waterproof notebook and find the pressed blade of marsh grass where you tucked it yesterday. It is fragile between the pages, still holding the smell of damp earth and cut salt. Your thumb smooths along its spine without thinking; the motion is a small ritual. The notebook's cover is scuffed, the elastic loop frayed where you hook a pen. A pale ribbon of algae is caught in the binding.

Your hands are honest about what you do—callused at the webbing between fingers from wading, a faint line of old blister under the pad of your left thumb. When you lift the locket to check the tiny map inside, the silver feels cool against your collarbone. The sliver of shoreline inside is more outline than place now, but you keep it because there are mornings you need to remember what used to be beneath your feet.

You stand on Low Street where patched roofs slope like crooked teeth toward the harbor. The town smells of kelp and diesel, the two scents braided into an everyday tide. Rope and crates sit like tired furniture along the quay. You talk to them as you pass—not aloud, not really—the cadence of habit more than speech. "Morning," to a coil of rope. "Steady," to a leaning crate. Saying the words anchors you in small, practical ways.

You run your pen along the margin of a page and sketch tide-mark lines: erosion depth at the east gate, a bent piling near slip seven, the old sea-wall scar that looks like a healed wound. Numbers and shorthand crowd the page—angles, soil samples, a quick note: "Old breakwater buried at +0.8m — check sediment strata." Your head catalogs what your hands cannot stop touching: the dampness of the timbers, the algae sheen along the pilings, the sting of salt in the air that makes your eyes water if you look at the horizon too long.

Old Man Rohan sits on a crate by the net menders, hands folded over a thermos. He looks smaller in the morning light, but his voice is steady when he greets you.

Old Man Rohan: "Aiko. Up with the gulls again, eh?"
You: "You know me. The gulls are punctual, the tide less so."

He chuckles, the sound like rope against wood.

Old Man Rohan: "Still measuring the sky with that notebook?"
You: "Measuring the coastline. Trying to keep the rest of it honest." (You pause, then softer.) "And listening. The harbor has things to tell if you don't rush it."

Old Man Rohan: "It tells the same stories, more or less. Different storms, same sea. But you've got a way of looking at it that makes me think there might be new stories to tell."

He taps the thermos, then the page of your notebook when you set it open. You don't tell him about the locket; you don't need to. He squints across the water and points with a knobby finger.

Old Man Rohan: "Mateo's over there, with the rolled plans. He looks sharper than the morning."
You: "He always does. Nice blazer for someone who spends his days in tide charts."

Across the way, Mateo stands under the low awning, blueprints clutched in a waterproof tube. The linen blazer looks softened by sea air; the field vest underneath has smudges of mud at the elbows. His smile lifts like a small current—quiet, steady.

Mateo (calling over): "Aiko! You got your morning rounds done already?"
You: "Indexes and gossip. The usual. How's the city thinking this morning?"
Mateo: (walks closer, lowering his voice) "Council slow, as ever. But the new survey gives us something to work with. Satellite imagery shows subsidence along the eastern marsh—nothing we can't model. How's the community reading—any new urgencies?"
You: (your pen pauses) "Jobs, safety, dignity. Not always in that order. People want their work and a place to live that doesn't feel like borrowed time."

Mateo: "Right. Numbers tell us where the ground is moving. You tell me what people need when the ground stops listening."
You: (a small, wry lift) "Someone has to translate from tide to teeth."

Mateo laughs soft, and you both look out over the harbor—the sun cutting through fog in thin knives.

Mateo: "I've got a meeting later. I can walk a bit. Tell me what you find, and we can map priorities."
You: "Fine. But you're buying me coffee when this mapping session turns into a rescue operation."

Mateo: "I'll bring the good kind. Not from the vending machine."

Your conversation threads back and forth: the practical exchange of data and hope, a light friction that is really comfort. You write "Mateo—ally, cautious" in a margin, because notes are both memory and plan.

You move along the docks, making a slow, clinical tally: pilings bent at 12 degrees, sediment receding faster by the public mooring, an old warning, half-buried, that reads: "Do not shore beyond this line." You press your fingertips to the wood; the grain is softened by salt. There is a rhythm here—measure, note, breathe—and it steadies you.

[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: Rusted beams draped in polymer panels; soft cyan LED glow]
[Sound: A slow drip of condensation, distant fan hum]
[Music: Low, warm synth pads — patient]

You kick the hem of your raincoat free of salt and step into the humid hush of the greenhouse. The air is thick with loam and the faint electric tang of lab LEDs. Beds of wet soil shine under diffused skylight; small vials hold bioluminescent cultures like bottled stars.

Tala is bent over a table, hands stained with mud, sorting seed bundles. Her purple hair is tucked back but a few defiant strands still fall loose. She looks up and grins—energy in her smile, but not the frenzied kind. It's the steady kind that keeps meetings moving.

Tala: "You found anything poetic out there? Or just numbers and threats?"
You: "Both. Poetry with a side of paperwork. Which is to say—I've got erosion lines and folks who want work they're proud of."
Tala: "Good. Proud work is how they stay through the next tide. The bioluminescent beds are holding; we saw germination in the last round. Small victories."

You lean over the workbench and touch a damp leaflet. The cultures smell faintly of the sea and rain—an edible, mineral scent. Tala watches you with an observant tilt.

Tala: "If you plan to pitch a community restoration, I can rally people. Folks trust the hands-on stuff. It gives them room to be part of the solution, not just the data point."
You: "I want the plan to listen—listen to numbers, and to stories. I don't want a fix dropped from a contract that forgets faces."

Tala: "We can make sure it doesn't. I've got names already. We'll take the greenhouse as a staging ground."

Tala's conviction is quiet but infectious. You write "Tala—organizer, unshakeable" beside a quick diagram for seed beds. The greenhouse hums like a contained tidepool—alive, patient.

[Scene: Community Hub — The Aquarium | Late Morning into Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Cracked tile murals, string lights, garden beds spilling salt-tolerant greens]
[Sound: Murmured conversation, a kettle boiling somewhere]
[Music: Soft guitar arpeggios, minimal]

The Aquarium is warm, a reclaimed place that smells of lemon oil and wet concrete. Ivy is at a folding table near the mural, sanding a reclaimed sign; paint flecks freckle her forearms. She looks up when you come in and laughs at how much salt is still crusted on your cuffs.

Ivy: "You're still pretending the sea doesn't kiss your sleeves."
You: "It kisses everything. I'm just slower to wipe it off."
Ivy: (drops the sanding block) "You and Mateo going to save the town with blueprints and puns?"

You sit across from her. Her hands are quick; her voice is blunt in a sisterly way that cuts through your tendency to keep things to yourself.

You: "I'm going to try. I want a plan that keeps people working here, keeps dignity. Not just a bulwark that pushes people out to make way for a glossy promenade."
Ivy: "Sounds expensive. And complicated. Also, you're going to ask people to do the work—when some of them need rent money now."
You: "I know. That's the part I'm trying to figure out. Short-term cash, long-term security. It's a balance."

Ivy: "Balance," she says, smirking. "You say it like it's a scale, but it's a tightrope between people's paychecks and a sea that doesn't care how pretty your sign is."

You talk longer—about the market, about whether temporary work subsidies could be paired with training, about who would run the restoration crews. Ivy is practical, listing local names, shops, and who owes who favors. The conversation has a rhythm of real people's lives—interrupted by logistics, softened by gossip.

Ivy: "If you're going to pitch this, make it clear: folks want control. Not charity. They want to be paid for what's their work, and not shipped out. Promise them that and they'll show up."
You: (you write "jobs = agency" on a fresh page) "I'll make it part of the plan. Agency, not charity."

You close the notebook to tuck the pages against the pressed marsh grass. The weight of the silver locket at your throat feels like a hinge—anchoring as you decide what to carry forward. You thumb through the day's notes: erosion lines, people named, a tentative schedule for greenhouse volunteers, Rohan's tide stories, Ivy's list of potential crew leads, Mateo's promise to bring maps. The notebook feels full but not heavy. It feels like a ledger of what matters.

Tala arrives at the Aquarium a few minutes later with seed bundles slung over her shoulder. Old Man Rohan ambles in too, moving with his usual careful pace, and Mateo slips in with the blueprint tube under his arm. The small group creates a loose circle of warmth in the cooler room. No loud announcements—just presence, like the sea moving against a hull.

Mateo: (folding open a corner of the blueprints) "If we stitch these edge designs with local labor—use modular, repairable structures—it buys time, and jobs."
You: "And if the community runs the crews, they keep skills and paychecks. Not to mention agency—"
Ivy: "Agency, yes. Also, somebody needs to write the grant that actually covers the paychecks."
Tala: "We can run a campaign. People will show up if there is dignity attached to the work."

Old Man Rohan: "And tell the council they'll get better outcomes if the people who know the tides help build it. You listen to the people, they'll listen to the plan."

The conversation is soft, a slow weaving of concern and practicality. Nobody raises their voice. You make a note: "Pitch = community restoration plan. Goals: jobs, safety, dignity. Use greenhouse as hub."

You close the notebook with a firm but gentle press, the blade of marsh grass making a thin relief where the pages meet. Resolve settles not like a new storm but like a dawn that has decided to stay: persistent and steady.

Your inner voice, the one that catalogs impulses and doubts, speaks in the careful way it always does.

You (thinking): "You are the person who puts other people's problems into a form that can be worked on. You do it because you care, and because you want the landscape—both human and marsh—to continue. You know your flaw; you carry others until it pinches. But today, that carrying feels deliberate."

There is a small, near-silent swell in your chest that is not panic, not joy—only the clarity of a path chosen. You close the notebook and imagine the meeting you'll call, the words you'll say, the maps you'll lay on the table. The plan is not solved; it is only born as a promise you intend to keep.

[Visual: The group disperses in pairs; the Aquarium's string lights sway as a slight breeze finds the doorway]
[Sound: A single gull calls, the town exhales]
[Music: Piano returns, even slower]

You button your faded navy raincoat at the throat, feeling the weight of the silver locket again. The harbor waits, patient and indifferent and necessary. You tuck the notebook under your arm, the marsh grass a quiet presence against the cover.

Your resolve is small and precise: you will pitch a community restoration plan.

The day is soft, and the tide is a slow thing. You could do this.
