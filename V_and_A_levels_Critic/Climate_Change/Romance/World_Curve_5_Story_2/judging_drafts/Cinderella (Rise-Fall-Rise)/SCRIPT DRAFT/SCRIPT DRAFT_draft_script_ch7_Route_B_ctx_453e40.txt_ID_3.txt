Aiko (you): "If we omit the southern shoal from initial metrics, its current condition might be used to argue it's not worth protecting."

Mateo Ríos: (hesitates) "I know. But if we don't deliver something measurable, the pilot loses leverage. Council will move to the option that shows immediate returns."

Aiko (you): "Qualitative evidence isn't always heard the same way. Data gets budgets."

His insistence is tender; he is offering himself as a translator between two languages: the language of community memory and the language of municipal metrics. You want to believe it's enough.

People gather in clusters; some faces are familiar — Ivy's freckled determination, Rohan's stooped silhouette — others are new, neighbors drawn by the rumor that the pilot might not include an enforceable community seat on oversight.

Tala: (sharp) "They'll measure our marsh like it's a lab rat and decide what lives and what gets paved. We won't let that happen."

Her fists are clenched, a drumbeat you can feel in your ribs. She's not wrong. The municipal machinery favors what can be counted, and counting has a way of turning living things into line items.

Aiko (you): "Tala, I'm trying to keep community governance in the pilot's formal scope. Council listened to Mateo's model because it's a practical path to funding. I'm pushing for binding representation."

Tala's laugh is brittle for a moment, then raw.

You see Ivy step forward, fingers twisting a ragged apron hem.

You study his face — the lines are a map of storms and survival. His eyes find yours. You can't be sure what his look means — fondness, distrust, something unreadable — but it presses on you the uncertain truth that models can miss the lived memory of place.
