[Scene: Reclaimed Lots | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of reclaimed lots—patchwork beds, muddy paths, string lights still drooping from last night's rain]
[Sound: Boots squelching through puddled earth; distant gulls; a generator's low hum]
[Music: Tense BGM — a taut string motif under a quickening percussive pulse]

You wake with the salt still in your lungs. The sky is the color of bruised linen; the tide is high and impatient. Volunteers move like small shoals around the beds: Tala barking a schedule into her comms, Ivy hauling canvas bags of compost, a couple of teenagers shoveling with stubborn, beautiful grit. Your gloves are already flecked with dark silt; the notebook you keep habitually closed at your hip is open now, pages damp at the corners from your hands and the morning fog.

The plan you pitched yesterday is in motion, raw and thin as paper but holding. That fact should be comfort enough. Instead, a low pressure builds under your skin—an animal readiness that has nothing to do with pride and everything to do with fear. Fear that the logistics will fail, that the budget won't stretch, that the safety protocols you keep repeating to yourself will break in the first storm.

Old Man Rohan sidles up, boots thumping, nose reddened by wind.

Old Man Rohan: "You doing okay, girl? The tide's been funny these last three nights. Felt different on my toes."

You: "I'm—" (You hesitate, feeling the words catch.) "Busy. We're testing nutrient circulation in Bed C. If the drainage's off the sediment could strand the seedlings."

Old Man Rohan: "Ah." (He squints at the sluice you and Tala set up.) "You and all your models. We used to eyeball the marsh and know with our bones. Not saying your way's bad. Just saying—watch the east run."

You sense in his voice a mixture of trust and worry. You want to reassure him—want to translate the numbers into the cadence of the creek so he and everyone else can breathe—but the volunteer roster is a ragged edge already. Ivy calls out, tugging at a tarpaulin.

Ivy: "Aiko! Can we get another wheelbarrow here? The seedlings are getting sun-baked if we don't move 'em under the tarp during the hour."

You: "On it." (You lift, feel the strain in your shoulders. The town expects steadiness from you; they expect you to carry an order that was never yours to invent alone.)

Tala appears with a printed sheet, rain-soft at the edges.

Tala: "Council needs the budget by noon. They want line items: labor, materials, contingency, insurance. Also: safety protocol for volunteers in tidal conditions. Can you—"

You: "I'll compile it. But we need accurate nutrient flux numbers for the marsh model first. If I fudge projections just to meet a deadline—"

Tala: (interrupting) "—we don't fudge. We rally. People will show if they know what they're helping with."

The air tightens; the generator kicks, a metallic staccato. You flip pages, cross-referencing field notes with the spreadsheet on your tablet. The model scrolls with delayed inputs: sediment loads, nitrogen uptake, runoff rates. Each variable is a small, bright alarm when it doesn't sit where it must.

[Scene: The Aquarium — Back Room | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: A cramped room lined with monitors and soil samples; string lights cast small warm ovals]
[Sound: Clicks of keys; soft murmur of volunteers outside; the kettle whistles faintly]
[Music: Tense BGM intensifies — low synth undercut with pulsing heartbeat]

You carry in wet boots and the smell of iron. The community hub is a small world that forgets the city for a moment: someone's knit scarf draped over a dug-up chair, a child's drawing of gulls pinned to corkboard. Here is where numbers become a story you can pitch to a council whose language prefers budgets to anecdotes.

Mateo waits by the table, blueprints rolled and damp at one end. He looks like someone who sleeps with deadlines—eyes bright but edged with the late-night fatigue of a planner who has tried to make bureaucracy move. He lifts his chin when you enter.

Mateo Ríos: "You're soaked." (He smiles, but it's small; his fingers tap a tablet.) "We have a window this afternoon at City Hall. Councilor Nguyen wants a preliminary budget and a safety plan. I—" (He stops, choosing.) "I can match municipal funds up to thirty percent of your projected materials if we sign an oversight addendum."

Your heartbeat flickers at the word "match." Thirty percent could mean more seedlings, proper scaffolding, paid stipends so volunteers aren't taking unpaid risk. But "oversight addendum" tastes like cold metal. You imagine committees, extra reporting, slowed decisions—maybe a leash that will strangle the consortium's community-led spirit.

You: "Oversight addendum? What kind of oversight?"

Mateo Ríos: (leans forward) "Quarterly audits, an appointed municipal liaison, and infrastructure standards that may require us to change some of the low-tech deployments you prefer. I know it's—" (he searches your face) "—a compromise."

You: "A compromise that might bend our work into something else. A compromise that could cost trust." (Your voice is softer than you feel. The room presses in with the weight of expectation.)

Mateo Ríos: "Or it's the difference between funding and stagnation." (He meets your eyes.) "I'd offer it without strings if I could, but Council needs accountability. They're worried about liability and long-term maintenance."

You run a hand over your face; the line between practical and principled is a razor.

Tala slides into the doorway, hands on her hips.

Tala: "We can't wait for red tape. People need shelter fast when the next swell comes. If matching funds get us ahead, we take them. But not at the cost of being steamrolled."

Ivy: (faintly, from a chair) "Can we even trust them to put money into local labor? Or does it vanish into contractors?"

Mateo Ríos: "Labor's in the line items. We can specify local hiring. I can put clauses for community oversight committees."

Tala: (to Mateo) "And who sits on those committees? When you say 'community' do you mean the council-appointed representative or actual folks who fish at dawn?"

Mateo Ríos: (defensive, then honest) "I mean people from here. I can push for seats for you, for Aiko—" (he makes a small, uncomfortable smile in your direction) "—for local stakeholders. But I need you to accept that some standards will be non-negotiable."

You can hear the tightening of Tala's jaw. Rohan's voice enters, low and steady, carrying the cadence of coastline memory.

Old Man Rohan: "Standards don't tell you how the tide will lie in a year. People do."

The room thrums with a dozen small anxieties folding over each other. You open your mouth, then close it. There is a ledger in your head—moral credit versus practical gain—calculating in the same dull motion that the tide calculates the shore.

You thumb a pen against the edge of your notebook and watch the ink form a small, dark tide mark. The phone in your pocket vibrates—Councilor Nguyen. The buzz is a small hammer on the thin shell of your calm.

[Scene: City Hall — Planning Office | Noon]
[Visual: Glass conference room with a long table; holographic models hover above a central projector]
[Sound: Low hum of HVAC; a murmured chorus of Council aides; the clip of a laser pointer]
[Music: Tense BGM reaches a higher register—staccato strings and a discordant piano note]

Councilor Nguyen's face is practiced neutrality. Behind him, a municipal clerk slides a dossier across the table toward you—hard copy of requirements, bolded sections labeled "Liability," "Insurance," "Performance Metrics." The room smells faintly of citrus cleaner and old coffee.

Councilor Nguyen: "Ms. Navarro, your community consortium is an admirable initiative. The town wants to support local resilience. That said, Council requires a detailed budget, role-specific safety protocols, and a maintenance plan. We're especially concerned about volunteer safety during tidal work."

You feel each word as a bead of cold sweat along your spine. The documents are not mean, but they are precise and slow: the bureaucracy's way of insisting time into policy. Your volunteers are not paid professionals; they are neighbors and family. The math for insurance premiums alone looks like an impossible climb.

You: "We can provide a line-item budget and standardized training modules for volunteer safety. We'll specify the tidal windows, buddy systems, and emergency evacuation plans."

Councilor Nguyen: (tilting his head) "And how will you fund these—insurance, stipends, proper protective gear?"

You open your mouth. The thirty percent match gleams in your mind like a promise. So does the possibility of doing all of it with grassroots labor and donated materials. Both options are brittle. Both will break under different pressures.

Mateo: (leaning forward, calm) "The municipal match will cover insurance premiums for the pilot and a small stipend pool for volunteers if Ms. Navarro agrees to the oversight addendum. It brings accountability and scope."

Tala: (sharp) "Accountability doesn't mean erasing community voice."

Mateo: "It means auditing for safety—"

Tala: "And who's doing the auditing? Contractors from the city office who never smell the marsh?"

Their voices ricochet; your hands curl into the edges of the paper until the margin threatens to fold. Council members exchange looks. Some are sympathetic; others wear the polite distance of people who must juggle budgets and votes.

Old Man Rohan: "We don't want to be managed out of our own land."

Ivy: (quiet, raw) "We don't have time to set up a thousand committees."

You feel the room tilt inward, the pressure like a weather front. The consortium you recommended now depends on you to name what kind of compromises are acceptable. You see faces: the teenager shoveling in the cold, Tala's clenched jaw, Mateo's steady professional patience. You imagine Rohan's hands on a line, guiding a net, and the sting of being told his wisdom is an optional dropdown in a municipal form.

Your chest tightens. For years you've mapped tides; today you're measuring the tides of authority. The numbers on the page seem small compared to the human shapes they hide.

You: (internal) "If I take the funds, we run faster, safer—maybe. If I refuse, we keep autonomy but risk fatigue and delays that could cost seedlings and people. If I open to Dr. Selene—" (the thought finishes itself like a warning) "—the money comes quick; the terms could hollow us out."

Mateo watches you, compassion folded under urgency.

Mateo Ríos: "We can't stall. Council will not fund open-ended pilots without assurances. I can push for community seats on oversight, but we need a yes or a no today so procurement can start."

Tala: "And if your 'yes' means we're boxed into designs we didn't vote on?"

Mateo Ríos: "Then we negotiate those terms now."

The meeting feels like a narrowing channel. You can hear a storm echo in the low rumble of the city outside—an actual weather system on the forecast—and it makes each moment sharper. Your throat is dry; your palms are cool. This is not the time for philosophy. This is not the time for slow tenderness. This is the time for a decision that will tilt the town's future and some tender things you cannot rebuild if broken.

Your notebook lies open before you, a map of compromises both made and possible. You breathe. The air tastes of citrus cleaner and something metallic—fear, maybe, or the smell of policy.

[Visual: Close-up on the open notebook; a pressed blade of marsh grass flutters against the margin]
[Music: Tense BGM swells, then sustains—a single unresolved chord]

You look at the faces around the table, then down at the budget line items where "insurance" lives like a crossroads. Your responsibility is a mantle that sits heavy; you are the one who will speak the word that will send people to work with new helmets and pay or send them back to fish and gardens with nothing but hope.

The crescendo of urgency tightens. The moment to choose lands like a wave.
