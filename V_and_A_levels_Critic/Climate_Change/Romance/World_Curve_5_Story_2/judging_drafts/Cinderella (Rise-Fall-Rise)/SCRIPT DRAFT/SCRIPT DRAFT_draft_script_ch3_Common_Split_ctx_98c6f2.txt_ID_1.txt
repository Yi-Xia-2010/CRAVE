You step into the planning chamber still carrying the residue of the Aquarium: the faint steam of kettle tea in your lungs, the smell of wet paper, the small dried blade of marsh grass folded in the corner of your notebook. The momentum you've cultivated — the slides, Rohan's stories stitched to the numbers — hums like an engine under your ribs.

Mateo sits in the front row, sleeves rolled, knees angled toward you as if the bench itself leans in agreement. He gives you a small, private nod when your foot finds the podium's mat. There's no grand gesture, only that steady, steady look that reads like a promise: I'll hold this with you.

You place your notebook on the podium. The touch of its cover is a compass you know by feel; you tuck a finger under the locket at your collar, feel the tiny map press against your sternum. You breathe once, and let the air calibrate.

Aiko Navarro: "Thank you, Councilors. Thank you, everyone, for being here. My name is Aiko Navarro. I'm presenting a community-led marsh restoration pilot designed to protect our shoreline habitat and the livelihoods that depend on it."

You speak simply. You lead with Rohan's voice first — a lived memory of tides that remembers when the water receded and when it took houses — then you let the figures take up the work: projected erosion lines, material costs, volunteer training time. Your slides shift from portrait photos to a map that highlights nodes where local scaffolds could slow erosion and where community nurseries could grow native transplants.

[Visual: Slide changes. A faded photo of Old Man Rohan sitting in the rising morning light; the projection flares softly.]
[Sound: A quiet rustle of paper as Council members consult dossiers.]

Aiko Navarro: "This is built to be low-cost, replicable, and to center those who live here. We can deploy the first scaffold in six weeks with donated materials, a short training program, and a maintenance rota led by neighborhood teams."

Councilor Nguyen: "Your timeline is optimistic. The council needs measurable mitigation with guaranteed coverage."

You anticipate the question. You have prepared metrics and fallback scenarios — but here, you choose tone as much as data.

Aiko Navarro: "We will monitor sediment accretion, plant survival rates, and social metrics like job-hours created. We will publish weekly progress and adjust — learning in place rather than imposing a one-size solution from above."

Mateo's hand slides, almost imperceptibly, to the rolled blueprints at his side. He clears his throat, the motion an offer rather than interruption.

Mateo Ríos: "If I may — integrating municipal technical support can scale community efforts while ensuring public accountability. I can work to secure matching funds and expedite permits."

There's an ease in his voice that makes the phrase "we can" feel contagious. You hush a flicker of doubt and answer the soft gravity of his proposal.

Aiko Navarro: "That'd be the ideal of shared ownership. But the pilot's governance would stay local — council oversight, community-led operations. The municipality as partner, not manager."

Before you can move on, the holographic grain in the corner of the room resolves into Dr. Selene Park's avatar. The corporate feed is crisp, professional light sharpening her features like a scalpel. She smiles, narrow but practiced.

Dr. Selene Park (Hologram): "Aiko, thank you for the local approach. For the record, our firm can deploy engineered mangrove scaffolds within three weeks and guarantee a measurable reduction in breach risk by fifty percent in the first six months."

A murmur. The corporate representative in the back — a neat, neutral suit carrying a waterproof portfolio — steps forward as if to stand in for a promise.

Corporate Rep: "Immediate funding, trained crews, and a warranty on outcomes. We can protect critical infrastructure swiftly."

You feel the room tilt: speed and measurable outcomes on one side, community stewardship on the other. The air tastes of warm metal and coffee. This is not an argument about competence; it is an argument about who gets to tend the place that is also a home.

Tala, who has been standing near the doorway with a folded petition in hand, plants herself and raises her voice.

Tala Kumari: "With respect, we need to be part of the hand that plants and maintains this. Corporate warranties don't answer who gets to keep their fishing boats or market stalls. They don't keep Aunties' memories."

Her words ripple through the chamber. Old Man Rohan lets out a small sound that might be a laugh or a sob — it's both. Ivy, who has been sitting by Mateo, leans forward, hands clenched.

Old Man Rohan: "I watched those walls rebuilt before. They worked on paper. But the sea has memory, too — we don't know it all from sensors."

Councilor Nguyen frowns, doing the mental arithmetic between ballots, contracts, and timelines. He looks at you, expectant.

Councilor Nguyen: "We need a pilot partner. Who would you advise the council to invite? Time is a real factor, Ms. Navarro."

Your stomach tightens, but not from fear — from the sense that this is precisely the hinge you'd been preparing for. You can feel the arousal curve rising, a steady cadence of stakes: community trust, institutional muscle, corporate speed. Each option offers a shape for the town's immediate life.

Dr. Selene Park (Hologram): "If I might add — we can structure the pilot as a public-private partnership, but we move on our timeline once contracted."

Mateo interjects quietly, careful not to be a shadow tugging at the podium.

Mateo Ríos: "The municipality as lead with community co-management could bind funding and local knowledge. It'll take concessions, but it keeps the town in the driver's seat."

You listen, and in the listening you inventory values: speed, scale, local ownership, equity. Your internal monologue organizes them like field notes, practical and impartial — then folds them back into the feeling that this is about people you know.

Aiko Navarro: "Councilors, this is more than a technical choice. It's about who holds the stewardship of our marsh. I recommend—"

Your voice slows, and time tightens to the beat of your pulse. Before you finish, Tala cuts in, urgent but controlled.

Tala Kumari: "Invite us. Invite the people who live here daily. We will show we can do this — with some help."

Councilor Nguyen raises a hand to restore order, gaze moving from face to face.

Councilor Nguyen: "This room is split. We need a clear recommendation. Ms. Navarro — your opinion carries weight here. Who should we invite into a pilot program?"

The chamber leans in. Cameras tilt. Dr. Selene's hologram is immaculate; Mateo's confidence is as quiet as a tide. Ivy's fingers flex at her knees; Rohan's eyes hold something like anticipation.

You feel the swell peak: the moment you speak will push alliances into place, unlock funding lines, define governance. It will shape not only infrastructure, but who gets up early to tend seedlings, who answers late-night calls when the scaffold shifts, whose knowledge is counted.
