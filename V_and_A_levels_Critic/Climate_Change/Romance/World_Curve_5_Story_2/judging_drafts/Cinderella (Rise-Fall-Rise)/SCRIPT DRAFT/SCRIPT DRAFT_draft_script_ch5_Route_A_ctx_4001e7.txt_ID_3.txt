[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Morning]
[Visual: Glass facade wet with a morning drizzle; inside, conference lights scatter on wet suits and open tablets. Blueprints roll like tide lines on the table.]
[Sound: A muted rain against the windows; the soft electronic chirp of an incoming notice; distant muffled voices from the council floor.]
[Music: Tense, staccato strings; a heartbeat bass underlining the conversation]

You walk into City Hall with Mateo at your elbow, the municipal contract folded in your bag like a small, hard animal. The matching funds are real. The stipend accounts are open. The ink that made this possible smells faintly of printer toner and wet paper — bureaucratic rain.

Councilor Nguyen waits in the planning office with the procurement officer and a slim, efficient tablet. Their faces are polite in the way of people who have learned to smooth tension with policy. The tablet screen reflects in their eyes like a clean ocean.

Councilor Nguyen: "Aiko, Mateo — thank you for getting this moving. The council wants the pilot to be compliant from day one. Procurement’s attached a list of mandatory protocols for materials and labor. We've included a labor-stipend schedule to keep local workers on payroll."

You feel the words as a ledger entry against the thing you carry: community knowledge and the ragged little inventions that kept your neighborhood alive through storms. Mandatory protocols, you read them later, forbid 'unverified organic matrices' and require 'certified supply chains' for all planting substrates. It is dry language that smells of risk aversion and legal forms.

Procurement Officer (folded-back tablet): "We have liability constraints. Any unsterilized organic inputs — handmade rooting bundles, fish-skin compost, reclaimed fishing line used as scaffolding — are non-compliant. If any method jeopardizes the grant's audit, funding will be withdrawn."

You hold the paper at your throat. Your hands are steady; your stomach is not.

Mateo Ríos: (squeezing your shoulder) "We can work within this. We'll document Rohan's methods, test them in parallel under supervised conditions, validate them. We don't have to abandon local practice to accept support."

You want to nod because Mateo's belief in process is one of the reasons you let him in — professionally and, in small ways, otherwise. But you also hear Rohan's voice in your head, the rough edge of his certainty, the smell of salt-cured wood and elderly hands that braided stakes by moonlight. You did not say yes to municipal matching funds because you wanted to replace that.

A small, taut sound — part irritation, part fear — leaves your mouth.

Aiko Navarro: "The project is about protecting livelihoods and the living marsh itself. If we sterilize the work out of it, what are we protecting?"

Councilor Nguyen: "You're protecting people from being sued. You're protecting the town's ability to continue doing this at scale. We can set aside a pilot variance, but that carries political cost."

The words hang like a buoy line. You can see, in the procurement officer's eyes, the spreadsheet of 'political cost' catalogued in black and red.

[Scene: Reclaimed Beds - The Community Plot | Midday]
[Visual: Rectangular beds, wrapped in damp burlap. People bend over soil that smells of iron and seaweed. Plastic tarps flap; the sound of work is close and repetitive — shovels, soft curses, laughter trying to hold its shape.]
[Sound: Rain thinning to a brisk wind; voices layered in the small, busy chorus of labor. The slap of rubber boots on wet planks.]
[Music: Faster, cyclical percussion — urgency in rhythm]

You lead the morning's planting with the municipal stipends loaded into a digital register. Workers who'd been unpaid last season now have names in a ledger that will loop money back into their hands. That is not an abstract good. It is rent paid, medicine purchased. You repeat the stipend schedule aloud so everyone can hear: hours, rates, break times. The sound of your voice cracks the clouds of tension for a moment.

Ivy: (under her breath, wrenching a stake) "At least Ma can buy oil for the stall this month."

Tala moves through the beds like someone scanning a map for weak points and strengths. She nods to you once — fierce, expectant.

Old Man Rohan arrives with a small cart. The cart smells of dried kelp and sun-baked rope. His hands are wrapped in cloth. He sets down a lump of what the grant calls 'non-compliant substrate' — old oyster shells wired into a matrix, braided fishing line that has been rinsed with tea and blessed in his way. He looks older than the morning, but his shoulders still hold a particular steadiness.

Old Man Rohan: (soft) "Small things first. We root the bed in the old way. The storm didn't take those who remembered how to bind the soil."

Mateo approaches with a roll of certified coir plugs and a weatherproof tube of approved biopolymer root starters. Practical, clean. He speaks to Rohan with the care of someone bridging two languages.

Mateo Ríos: "Rohan, we'd like to test both methods. If the municipal auditors see unapproved inputs, they can halt disbursements. We need a controlled approach."

Rohan's eyes narrow into the tiny slits they become when he smells something wrong.

Old Man Rohan: "They stop the work, Aiko. They stop people's mouths from being fed. What then? Paper pays for nothing when the marsh is gone."

You feel Rohan's sentence like a wet weight thrown into the shallow pool of decision between your ribs. People look at you. Ivy watches your face like a test to be read.

Aiko Navarro: "We will document both. We'll run sterile trials and —"

Before you finish, a municipal inspector in a yellow vest steps up, breathed-out condensation from his mask. He holds a small, glossy tablet that will become the instrument of this day's reckoning.

Municipal Inspector: "Project Manager Navarro? We have a complaint on scope. A variance wasn't approved. We need immediate compliance. Any further non-compliant application will flag the audit."

The word 'audit' is a hammer.

Tala: (sharp) "So we let them tell us how to plant our marsh? We didn't give up our chores to field certifiers."

Ivy: (picking up a stake) "They don't know these tides like Rohan does."

The crowd's murmur climbs like a tide. The work slows. Gloves stop mid-dig. Shovels sink and stay.

Mateo reaches for you — fingers finding the small of your back, steadying. His face is softer in public crisis, the planner's calm face. But you hear an undercurrent — the thing he will not say out loud.

Mateo Ríos: (low) "If they cut the funds, everyone's work stops. We've got to keep payroll active. We can do tests privately, validate Rohan's method, then press for inclusion."

Aiko Navarro: "Private testing is not the same as honoring the people's knowledge. They deserve to be seen using the methods that kept them afloat."

Mateo Ríos: "I know. That's not what I'm trying to buy. I just — I can't afford to lose you to a fight that costs the project everything."

You feel the phrase like a small, honest wound. He isn't asking you to choose between him and Rohan; he is confessing the cost that his path might take from you. The wind lifts a paper schedule and you chase it, a small comic chase undercut by the gravity of the moment.

[Visual: Close on Rohan's face; he looks like the sea has carved him into a cliff. For a heartbeat, you hear nothing but the wind.]

Rohan steps forward, voice steady as tide line.

Old Man Rohan: "You sold your first answer to a ledger, Aiko. I ain't asking for a fight. I'm asking for root."

Something in the crowd shifts — a collective intake of breath. That sentence lands in you with a clarity that makes your mouth go dry. Sold your first answer. The echo of Councilor Nguyen's 'political cost' and the procurement officer's 'liability' join Rohan's words into a thrumming chorus in your skull.

Mateo: (reaching, urgently) "Aiko—"

Aiko Navarro: "I didn't sell anything. I did math and listened to people who are still breathing these tides. There are ways to protect both knowledge and livelihoods."

Mateo: (quiet) "Sometimes protecting both means swallowing a part of one. I don't like that either."

He tries to smile; it doesn't reach his eyes. The municipal inspector clears his throat and steps closer, the tablet held like a blade.

Municipal Inspector: "We have a procedural directive. Any deviation requires written variance approval from City Hall. If local methods continue for public work without variance, the disbursement will pause pending review."

The pause is not theoretical. You see the work-halting gears: stipends freeze; workers may be left without pay. The sound of a phone ping rips through the air like a gull, and you know — before you read it — that the council has been alerted.

[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Afternoon]
[Visual: Polymer panels blur rain into light. Rows of small vials catch a blue LED glow, sensors blinking like reluctant stars. A steel cart holds the certified plugs the municipality supplied.]
[Sound: The greenhouse hums with climate control; distant dripping; the soft click of a tablet as data uploads.]
[Music: A restrained, fretful motif; strings layered with a distant, high piano]

You bring a handful of the approved materials here to run tests you promised. The greenhouse smells of damp compost and antiseptic cleaning fluid. You line things up: Rohan's braided matrices in a corner, municipal coir plugs under a soft lamp, both labeled and logged. Scientific practice is a language you learned to speak; you can translate community knowledge into controlled terms. You can, in theory, prove its worth.

Tala watches the instruments with a hostile fascination. Ivy folds her arms and watches the greenhouse's humidity gauge like a slow clock. Mateo stands near the entrance, the rolled blueprints under his arm, his expression split between relief and something like dread.

Mateo Ríos: "If these parallel tests show equivalence or superior performance for Rohan's method, we can argue to Council for inclusion. That's leverage."

You want to believe him, with a ferocious intensity that tastes like seawater and old coffee. But belief is not evidence; it is the kindling. You flick a switch and sensors begin to read.

[Sound: A distant phone vibration. Your phone lights up with a message from Councilor Nguyen: 'We need public assurance this afternoon. The audit team wants a compliance report by close of business.']

Your heart claws at your throat. The timeline compresses. Overnight proofs become demands for immediate compliance. The municipal machine favors immediate, measurable certainty. Rohan's slow, weathered expertise does not fit well on a spreadsheet due by five.

[Scene: The Aquarium - Community Hub | Evening]
[Visual: Warm bulbs and a low ceiling. Mismatched chairs cluster; an unfinished mural of a whale arches the far wall. The reclaimed space smells faintly of frying fish and waxed wood.]
[Sound: A murmur of voices; the kettle whistles softly in the kitchenette; someone flips open a tin of breath mints.]
[Music: A single melancholy violin line rising into something rawer]

You convene a debrief at the Aquarium as dusk stains the water outside. People come with wet coats and wet questions. The stipend payouts are tabled, but so is the specter of funding withdrawal. The room is small, and the feeling of being seen is intense. You sit at the head of the table, the municipal notice folded in front of you like a thin blade.

Ivy: (quiet) "If the money stops, Ma closes. We can't buy back the house with pride."

Tala: (leaning forward) "Paper doesn't root a marsh, Aiko. But people with empty pockets won't stand for any plan."

Rohan sits near the back, his knuckles white from gripping the cane. He does not look defeated; he looks like someone who has been wronged for a long time and is tired of the grammar of being right.

Rohan: "We ain't asking for lawyer-scented mercy. We are asking for a place to put our hands down."

Mateo moves closer to you across the table and lowers his voice.

Mateo Ríos: (soft) "We can file for a variance tonight. There are precedents — small towns that allowed traditional inputs under oversight. I can draft the petition, call in favors. But it will take time."

You can feel the night pressing at the windows like a tide. The room seems too small for the weight of the choices stacked on the table.

Aiko Navarro: "How long will that take?"

Mateo Ríos: "A week, maybe two. If the audit doesn't pause or the inspector flags the first deployment, funding can be paused in twenty-four hours."

The timeline is a guillotine. You imagine the beds left unplanted, the paid workers turning away with empty hands. You imagine Rohan's method never gaining official recognition because the ledger closed while the testing loop was incomplete.

A silence spreads, thick and clinical. Your locket feels like a stone against your sternum. The city has offered you a lifeline with one fist, a constraint with the other.

You think about the look on Councilor Nguyen's face earlier — conciliatory, but protective of the town's dossier. You think about Mateo's hands, callused and steady, the way he steadied you at the tent when the rain first came. You think about Rohan's breath, the sound of a history that refuses to be catalogued in sterile terms.

Your chest tightens. The room feels like an inlet that can tip into a storm without warning.

Mateo: (reaching, intimate) "Whatever you choose, I stand with you. But I can't promise what the town will do if the audit stops the money."

Aiko Navarro: "I don't want to choose between people and knowledge."

Mateo: (quiet, broken honesty) "Sometimes choices carve out pieces of us we don't get back."

You feel the words like a confirmation and a curse at once. The weight of responsibility collapses into a single, sharp point: the municipal inspector's tablet, the audit, Rohan's braided line, Ivy's rent — all aligned like points on an incoming storm.

You hear the room breathing around you, expectant, tired, waiting for the single decision that will reroute work, loyalties, and perhaps love.
