[Scene: The Aquarium | Night]
[Visual: Damp string lights; rain streaking the aquarium windows in thin, watercolor lines]
[Sound: Rain against corrugated panels; muffled conversation; the tide whispering beyond the building]
[Music: Urgent, staccato strings under a low, insistent pulse]

You step back inside with the taste of council chambers on your tongue — metal, rain, paper. The choice is a flat, bright thing now; it sits in your ribs the way a pebble might, pressing until you name it. You refused Dr. Selene Park. You told the council no.

Tala is already there at a long folding table, her purple hair flattened by the wet, fingers moving through a litter of flyers and maps as if scrubbing them clean. Around her, volunteers talk too loudly and too softly at once: lists of needs, names of streets, a ledger of the town's debts.

Tala: "You did it. You said no."

You: "We have to keep our autonomy. We can't let a contract replace the people who live here."

Tala's mouth tightens, not quite a smile. There's a fierceness behind it that has nothing to do with cheer.

Tala: "Good. The firm's tech would've worked fast, but—do you know how many families they'd have fenced out? How many memories they'll bulldoze into spreadsheets? We can't trade that for short-term flood relief."

You close your eyes and feel the weight of the room press in: the wet coats, the rusted kettle on the counter, the smell of algae and fried dough from the community kitchen. The moral clarity of the refusal is sharp; it cuts. But the cut is already bleeding.

Ivy appears in the doorway, hair in a loose braid, overalls spotted with paint. Her hands are empty but stained with market fruit.

Ivy: "Do you know how many people called me this morning asking if rent's still due? Shelters are filling. The landlord's sending notices." (She laughs once, a broken sound.) "As if notices mean anything when the dock's a bathtub."

You: "We knew this would be hard."

Ivy: "Hard doesn't pay the electricity bill, Aiko."

The laughter dies. The room leaks that small, precise anger — the kind that smells like damp bread.

[Visual: Close-up on your waterproof notebook, pages splayed with pressed marsh grass]
[Sound: The kettle whistles a brief, sad note]
[Music: A single high violin line, an anxious thread]

Tala spreads dry ink sketches across the table — alleys, community gardens, the line where seawater is already staining the concrete. She points.

Tala: "Blockades at the southern slip. Mutual aid at the Aquarium and The Aquarium's annex — cooking, sorting donations, rotating sleepers through the spare rooms. We can't stop the tide, but we can slow some of what the contracts would have taken."

You: "And the berms?"

Tala: "Jackets, sandbags, welded sheets of scrap metal, driftwood. Old Mr. Rohan says we can use the king-tide patterns he remembers. We don't have excavators. We have our backs."

You breathe, and the air tastes of iron and possibility. The plan is a thin thing, stitched from what the town has left. It will fray. You know it. The knowledge presses like cold water at your ankles.

[Scene: The Docks | Late Night / Storm-pressing]
[Visual: Flood-laced planks; rain by now is horizontal; tarps flapping like exhausted flags]
[Sound: Crates thudding, ropes screaming over pulleys, the sea smacking wood]
[Music: Percussive low drums; a rising wind motif]

You walk the wet boards with Tala, hands numb in gloves. The southern slip reeks of kelp and old diesel. A handful of neighbors heft a makeshift barrier — pallets lashed together, sacks of gravel borrowed from a fishmonger. The work is clumsy and righteous.

Mateo arrives, water seeping from the collar of his linen blazer. He carries, incongruously, a waterproof tube of blueprints under one arm. His arrival is quiet — not a surrender, but an attempt to thread something steady into the chaos.

Mateo: "You turned them down."

You: "We couldn't let them own the solution."

Mateo studies the improvised pallets like they're a sketched plan, runs his fingers over a roped knot. His face is a careful ledger of empathy and worry.

Mateo: "I don't trust their motives either. But —" (he breathes) "— we need to be realistic. There are families with nowhere to go if the winter tide comes worse. The council had numbers. You heard them."

You: "I heard an offer that would have boxed out our people. We build this together, or we watch outsiders sweep through with machines and contracts."

Mateo: (low) "And if it fails? If people lose homes because we rejected help, will it still have been worth it?"

You say nothing. The question is a stone cast in the dark. It lands with a hollow sound.

Tala steps between you, palms spattered with sea-slick mud.

Tala: "We can't wait for permission. Mateo — we need engineers to help with load distribution. Not to sign away our rights, but to make sure these piles don't just wash away."

Mateo's jaw works. He glances at you; his sea-green eyes are small harbors of doubt.

Mateo: "I can draft reinforcements. I can talk to sympathetic folks on the council who'll give us emergency tools — not contracts, tools. But we have to be clear. No corporate overseers. No strings."

You: "You'll help, without strings."

Mateo: (after a breath) "Yes. Without strings."

There's relief, fragile and immediate. But the sound of relief is short-lived; a wave slaps the dock like a hand seeking purchase.

[Scene: Neighborhoods — The Improvised Berms | Pre-dawn]
[Visual: Low houses pressed together; piles of pallets and sandbags forming ragged silhouettes against a bruise-sky]
[Sound: The far-off roll of waves; footsteps through mud; whispered prayers]
[Music: Tension swells; layered chords building toward a peak]

The first king tide comes faster than the maps predicted. The sky is a bruise-pale sheet, and the ocean smells like a buried thing unearthing itself. Volunteers run between piles, carrying sacks, stacking, jamming driftwood into the seams. Old Man Rohan is there, smaller than his stories suggest, but his hands remember the way the coast used to be.

Old Man Rohan: (raspy) "When I was a boy, the wall was higher. We had more sand. The sea takes and spits. You keep dancing, it keeps changing the song."

You: "Are the patterns the same?"

Rohan: "Same shapes. Different teeth. You can't talk to the sea like it's a ledger. You listen. You move with it."

Ivy tends a seam where the water is slicker, fingers raw, face pinched by cold. She presses a rag into a hole and sobs once into her sleeve — a sound that tries to be small but isn't.

Ivy: "My friend called. Her mother— the landlord gave her one week to leave. One week. Where are they supposed to go? The shelters are full; people sleep in the annex."

You: "We rotate. We cook. We have to do better."

Ivy: "It isn't enough."

You taste salt and iron and exhaustion. The moral clarity that once cut like a surgical blade now feels like salt poured into an open wound: necessary truth, but agonizing to bear.

The tide hammers at the berms. Each crash is a fist. The pallets shake, splinter, sigh. Volunteers shout, hands slapping against wet wood, voices sharp with command and pleading. Someone cries out — a rope slips, sacks tumble.

You push at a rotting pallet until your shoulder aches. The wood is splintered, the nails slick. Rain stings your face.

Tala: "Hold the line! Fresh sacks! Rotate! Mateo, the reinforcements — now!"

Mateo runs like he's trying to outrun something he knows he'll never catch. His hands are steady, but his eyes are the same weather: anxious, counting failures like small stones.

The berm sways under the sea's insistence. For a moment, everything is muscle and breath and a single, terrible rhythm.

[Visual: Close-up on your soaked hands gripping a sack; knuckles white under a veneer of mud]
[Sound: A long, bone-deep groan — the sound of failing structure]
[Music: All instruments cut briefly to a raw, high string; then a heavy percussion slam]

You feel the earth give in a way that goes through your bones. The pallet beneath your feet shifts, then collapses with a sound that is not quite wood and not quite sorrow. Water finds the seam. It is not subtle; it is imminent and blunt.

Tala's voice splits the air: "Back! Pull back!"

You can see faces — Ivy's mouth open, Rohan's jaw set, Mateo's hand extended — and you know, in a way that leaves no alternative, that this is the moment that will tell whether everything you refused is noble or merely stubborn.

You have refused the corporate quick fix. You have chosen the harder, lonelier road. The sea is answering with teeth.

[Visual: A wall of black water, lit sickly by streetlamps, rises and bears down on the ragged barrier]
[Sound: A roar like a freight train; the world narrowed to wind and water]
[Music: Crescendo — all instruments pushing to the edge]

You brace, lungs burning. Every muscle fires. The night folds into a single decision: to hold, to run, to save who you can.

Your hands slide on the wet sack. The grip fails. You lurch.

[Visual: Slow-motion of a wave creaming over the berm; spray freezes in the light like glass]
[Music: The chord hangs — a suspended, shattered note]

You push.

If it holds, the strain will continue. You can't tell which will be the truer result; only that you are here, making the same choice again, drenched in salt and consequence.
