[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Late Morning — Rain Threading the Windows]
[Visual: Wide shot of the conference table littered with printed clauses and a single, damp coffee cup. Mateo stands by a holographic model that flickers with tide projections. Dr. Selene Park sits immaculate, AR spectacles dimmed to reveal a face like polished steel.]
[Sound: Rain hitting the glass in a steady, urgent patter; a distant speaker murmurs municipal updates.]
[Music: Tense BGM — tight strings under a slow, insistent percussion]

You taste metal at the back of your throat — not from blood, but from the constant churn of decisions. The negotiation didn't end in a clean victory; it ended with language. Words that can be enforced, audited, summoned into court. You hold the final draft in both hands like a map whose coastline has been redrawn.

Aiko Navarro: "We will not sign unless 'community oversight' means seats with real vote — not just listening panels. Audit reports must be publicly posted quarterly, with third-party review. Local hiring can't be an aspiration; it has to be a procurement clause."

Dr. Selene Park: "I understand. (pauses, deliberate) Those are reasonable conditions. My firm has no interest in gestures. Contracts mean deliverables."

Her voice is smooth enough to polish a rust spot clean, and you hear the undertow beneath it — efficiency measured in milestones. The conference room feels smaller with every clause you speak aloud.

Mateo Ríos: "We'll draft the governance charter with those seats specified — two community representatives, one fisheries expert, a rotating audit chair. Contractually-binding language is messy, but it's possible."

You look at Mateo. His fingers drum the side of the hologram like he's counting seconds. He wants order as much as you do. His calm is a small lighthouse in the room, but the light is thin and the storm is wide.

Old Man Rohan leans on his cane at the far end of the table, his beard still carrying damp salt from the harbor. He doesn't speak for a moment. When he does, his voice carries the weight of the tides he's watched his entire life.

Old Man Rohan: "Words don't keep the nets. They'll sign words and send men with good boots. They won't teach my boy how to read the reedbeds."

You can't tell whether his look is anger, fear, or something older — a slow, fossilized sorrow. It's a complex thing that lands in the room like a dropped stone.

Dr. Selene Park: "We will include a procurement schedule, prioritized vendors from Marisma, training quotas for local hires, and a clause for independent audits." (her hands move, precise) "However, procurement cycles are bound to supply chains and liability. We will require certain certified suppliers to meet warranty standards."

The sentence is reasonable. It contains the words you wanted, but it also reveals the lever they will pull when corners get tight: "certified suppliers." You know what that means — pressure to centralize purchasing, to substitute local knowledge for certified expediency.

Aiko Navarro: "And if our audits show noncompliance?"

Dr. Selene Park: (eyes cool) "Remedial steps. Financial penalties. Contractual termination as a last resort."

Mateo's jaw tenses. "Termination could mean stopped wages. People here can't afford interrupted paychecks."

The room seems, briefly, to tilt. You feel the jerk of the town's economy in your chest: a thousand small incomes tied to a single contract. The clauses you fight for can protect people; but their failure could unmake what they were meant to save.

You press your palm flat on the table. The paper trembles beneath your fingers.

Aiko Navarro: "Then the contract must include a contingency fund — an escrow to keep local wages moving if remediation is required."

Dr. Selene Park: (a thin smile) "Escrow is acceptable, provided the triggers are clearly defined and the threshold for release is administratively sound."

Her smile fits like a seal around an engine: functional. You feel a coldness settling, like fog rolling down the cliffs.

Her promise hangs in the air, warm and dangerous. Faster. The word tastes like relief and like a knife.

You sign. Not because the contract is perfect, but because you have watched your mother's street disappear into a gray of seawater and bureaucracy, and you have learned that waiting for a perfect world can be a form of betrayal.

You watch Dr. Park sign with the same seamless calm. Her handwriting is decisive. She places a corporate seal like a small, metallic wave over the clause on audits.

Aiko Navarro: "We will hold you to every line. Public audits. Local hires. Oversight seats."

Dr. Selene Park: "And we will expect the community to follow agreed protocols. Cooperation is part of the contract."

Mateo: "We make a team for the oversight, publish the charter, and get the escrow in place. The council will rubber-stamp this once the legal language is finalized."

Old Man Rohan watches the seal like a man watching the tideline swallow a rope.

Mateo leans close, voice low. "Procurement looks good on paper, but watch for subcontracting. That’s how a lot of the work can slip out of local hands."

[Scene: Tidepark Research Greenhouse | Next Morning — Humid, Filtered Light]
[Visual: The greenhouse smells of wet soil and antiseptic. Engineers and local trainees circle a low, folded mass of scaffolding material laid on a tarp. Soft LED strips make the scaffold glimmer faintly, veins like artificial kelp.]

[Music: High, insistent strings — the tension is near its peak]

You stand at the edge and watch them unfold the engineered scaffold for the first time. It is both gorgeous and obscene — a latticework designed to anchor root growth and dampen surge energy, its surfaces seeded with living algal matrices. It hums faintly when a technician brings a handheld power unit to life. The hum vibrates under your feet.

Mateo: "It looks… effective." (his breath catches) "If it holds, it'll buy the marsh time."

Dr. Selene Park kneels beside the scaffold like a sculptor admiring a completed work. Up close, the engineered material smells faintly of seawater and plastic, the chemical memory of a lab.

Dr. Selene Park: "This is what modern restoration looks like. Scalable designs that respond to tidal forces. We can adapt these modules to most shorelines."

You recognize truth in her words — the design is elegant, clever. But the sight of it, being fed into the living marsh, feels like handing a town a prosthetic for something that might have been cured by gentler means.

A technician flips a control. The scaffold expands with a precise, hydraulic sigh and clicks into place. It anchors, a mechanical promise set into the mud.

You feel a squeeze in your chest so sharp it’s almost physical. The sound of the scaffold locking is the sound of futures being constrained.

Old Man Rohan: "My father would have called that a machine that came to take the place of the reeds." (his voice is low, threaded with a lifetime) "Machines can help. They can also — slowly and quietly — make us forget how the water used to be."

Mateo reaches for your hand without prompting; his fingers are warm, steady. For a second you consider leaning into that warmth and letting the worry fall away. But the oversight seats, the escrow, the audits — they are words on paper until they are teeth.

A technician unclips a panel and shows you the seed cartridges embedded in the lattice. Tiny vials of engineered mangrove propagules, altered to root in damp, brackish soil. The novelty is beautiful and quietly domineering.

Aiko Navarro: "The lab wants sensor access for remote diagnostics," Dr. Park says. "It helps us monitor embedding success rates."

You remember the clause you fought for about independent audits. You remember Rohan's hands in the nets and Mateo's careful diagrams. You remember your mother's laugh. The scaffold breathes under the technician's hand, alive and foreign at once.

Whatever you choose, the scaffold goes into the mud. The engineered lattice takes depth and then tugs at it, changing the shape of the ground. Workers cheer — small, bright noises against the hum. Wages are being paid; hands are learning new tasks; a dozen families will not have to choose homelessness this week.

But your relief is a thin paper boat on a churning sea. You can see the places where the plan could fray: the procurement chain that might reroute work overseas; the audit committee that could be delayed by administrative wrangling; the escrow that could be frozen in protracted arbitration. Each is a crack that will let water in.

The hybrid board meets under a tarp beside the greenhouse tables: a corporate representative, two community seats you helped place, Mateo moderating. Cameras press in subtly, capturing the handshake you already performed on paper.

Dr. Selene Park: "This board will ensure transparency and quick adaptation. We have engineers on call and a plan for scaling."

You listen to the words as if they are another tide: inevitable, rolling, insistent. Your stomach tells you the truth that the sentences will not hold against every bureaucratic impulse. The words feel thin compared to the living marsh.

The hum of the scaffold under the wet light becomes a pulse — a mechanical heartbeat that matches the rising pitch in your chest.

You picture the coastline as it was on the map in your locket, then the line we've drawn together, then the line that will be drawn by the scaffold and the contracts. Each line is a different kind of promise. Each is a different kind of loss.

Your throat tightens until speaking is an effort.

Aiko Navarro: "We start monitoring at dawn. The community oversight will meet weekly for the first quarter. Any deviations will trigger the escrow release automatically."

Dr. Selene Park: (tilts her head) "And the audit firm?"

Mateo: "Independent. Preselected. We'll all have access to the raw datasets."

Dr. Selene Park gives you a look that is almost a compliment: "You negotiated well, Ms. Navarro. You held to the language."

Her compliment lands like a stone dropped into still water. Ripples spread through you — validation and a sinking at once.

You think of all the people who will breathe easier tomorrow and of the things that will not be fixed by scaffolds and escrow accounts: the stubborn, human things that need time and tending. The urgency that forced you to sign keeps humming; so does a deep, very negative knowledge: that this is both a rescue and a surrender.
