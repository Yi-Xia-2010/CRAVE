[Scene: Cliffside Promenade | Dawn]

You name the morning by the pressure in your chest. It's not just anxiety — it's a tide you can feel in your bones: election-day pressure, funding timelines, and the countless small lives folded into the language of contracts. The locket is heavy against your sternum. You thumb its cool edge until the sea-salt on your fingers is a print of the place you won't let vanish.

Old Man Rohan stands with his hands in his pockets, eyes fixed on the horizon as if he is reading the water like a page. Tala paces between people, voice rising and falling as she rehearses a point and then swallows it. Ivy carries a crate of flyers, her jaw set against the wind. Mateo watches you the way someone keeps a lighthouse in sight — steady, calculating, and quietly, insistently present.

Mateo Ríos: "They're setting up a satellite feed at City Hall," (he says, low) "Councilor Nguyen wants the cameras. Makes the vote show-ready."

You: "Election pressure means the optics matter — but optics also got us public oversight seats. We use both. We keep the pilot accountable and visible."

Mateo's eyes shift, searching for any hesitation he can help take away. He doesn't find one.

Mateo Ríos: "If Dr. Park agrees to the revised clause about community advisory vetoes on deployment in traditional fishing zones, the contract can be signed by noon."

The wind catches a loose flyer and it flutters like a gull before crumpling against Rohan's boot. Rohan looks down, then up at you with a face that carries weather and history in equal measure.

Old Man Rohan: "Your plan's got weight, girl. And it carries what needs keeping. But weight can break the bridge too."

You nod. His meaning is the ache in your chest that isn't just your own: the small boatwrights whose hands make nets the way other people make bread; the children who learn tides the same way elders learn prayers. You're not naive to tradeoffs. You are incandescent with the urgency to make the trade-offs just.

[Scene: City Hall - Planning Office | Late Morning]
[Visual: Glass-and-steel conference room, wet pavement reflected outside. Holographic marsh models spin above the table, scales and overlays projecting sediment flows.]
[Sound: Muffled radio chatter; reporters whispering in a gaggle outside. The hum of projectors, the click of a pen like a metronome]
[Music: Percussion and brass spike, then tighten into a quick, relentless tempo]

Councilor Nguyen sits at the head of the table, the contract unfurled like some austere map. Dr. Selene Park is there, immaculate, her AR spectacles flickering with data. Tala, Rohan, Ivy, and a handful of fishers occupy the benches, faces pinched in a mix of hope and exhaustion. Mateo stands by your side like a steady reef. You stand in the place you've practiced — half presenter, half guardian.

Councilor Nguyen: "We appreciate the work from both municipal staff and the community group. The vote will be on the amended contract — yes or no. We need a simple majority."

Dr. Selene Park: (cool, precise) "Our modifications increase survivability of critical zones by twenty-seven percent within five years. They are scalable, measurable, and fundable."

Tala Kumari: (interrupting) "Measured in what? Dollars and cubic meters? How do you measure a tradition, Selene? How do you measure the morning when nets are braided together and stories cross from elder to child?"

Dr. Selene Park: (a small, almost imperceptible shift) "We can create metrics that track cultural continuity. They will not be... qualitative, but they can be proxies."

The room stalls. The word 'proxies' lands like a stone.

You: "Metrics don't replace presence." (your voice is tight; you let the conviction in) "They should support it. The advisory board must hold deployment decisions for heritage zones until alternative livelihoods and relocation support are guaranteed. And there must be a process for immediate, short-term aid when displacements happen."

Dr. Selene Park: (studying you; an unreadable look) "Those are operational constraints. They add complexity and time."

You: "They also save social capital. They save lives."

Mateo Ríos: "We'll accept slightly longer pilot phases in prioritized heritage areas in exchange for guaranteed municipal funds for relocation support and small-business continuity grants."

Councilor Nguyen looks at the room. Cameras tilt, capturing faces for live feeds. Somewhere outside, someone shouts a slogan. The decision compresses into a vector: political survival, public pressure, scientific promise, human cost.

Old Man Rohan: (soft but fierce) "If you take my jetty, you take my history. Take care of it."

Dr. Selene Park's face tightens. For the first time, an edge of something like recognition crosses her features.

Dr. Selene Park: "Fine. We will embed community advisory vetoes for specified heritage zones as operational pauses, contingent upon verified relocation support and continuity funds. We'll also incorporate traditional breakwater techniques into engineered designs at scale."

There's a collective inhalation in the room, like a tide pulling back and revealing a strip of beach.

Tala Kumari: (eyes shining) "You mean you'll actually listen. Not just consult."

Dr. Selene Park: (a quick half-smile) "I'll listen, and we'll be accountable to what the board signs off."

Mateo squeezes your shoulder. It is small. It is enough.

Mateo Ríos: (softly) "We keep the board empowered, and we keep the process transparent. That's the compromise."

[Scene: Marsh | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Machines and volunteers work in measured chaos — mangrove scaffolds being driven into place, small teams transplanting salt-tolerant beds, community members hand-seeding terraces with green, patient hands.]
[Sound: Rhythmic thumps of drivers; laughter and shouted instructions; the indefatigable cry of a gull]
[Music: Triumphant brass and strings, hammering rhythm that does not let up]

You wade into the work. Mud squishes between your boots. The smell of peat and wet metal is familiar, grounding. Volunteers hand you tools. Someone presses a thermos into your fingers. A young fisher jokes about learning to read the scaffolds like a new kind of tide-line. The new ribs take sand; the shoreline for now leans toward staying.

Ivy finds you between two transplant rows, face flushed.

Ivy: "They're assigning market stalls closer to the docks that won't flood. They'll give us rent subsidies during transitions. Rohan's dock gets a fund to rebuild at a safe elevation."

You: (relief floods you — real dollars, real help; it isn't everything, but it's something) "Tell me everything. We'll make sure the grant deadlines line up with the construction schedule."

Rohan approaches, hands smelling of peat. He sets a small carved hook — something he made years ago — on a crate by your boots.

Old Man Rohan: "You did more than argue today. You carried our stories into a place that measures things. That matters."

You kneel and touch the hook, feeling the worn grooves of rope against the wood. For a moment, grief and pride braid together until you cannot tell where one ends and the other begins.

[Scene: The Aquarium - Community Hub | Dusk]
[Visual: String lights hung between columns, the tile mural of a whale lit in warm amber. People cluster in pockets of light, eating donated stew, swapping stories. A makeshift stage holds the contract, now framed.]
[Sound: The murmur of conversation; a kid's shoe tapping on tile; distant sea like a heartbeat]
[Music: Warm strings with a subdued, persistent rhythm — celebration threaded with melancholy]

A small crowd gathers to mark the day's work — not a victory party, but a vigil that tastes faintly of triumph. Someone brings a battered speaker; Mateo and you stand before the framed contract. Faces crowd close: volunteers, fishers, council staff who have become allies, Tala with tears drying on her cheeks, Ivy with a half-smile that says more than a cheer.

Mateo Ríos: (into the microphone) "Today isn't the end of anything. It's the start of a hard, honest work. We will be accountable. We'll build, we’ll learn, and we'll keep listening."

You: (you add, voice steady) "We won a place at the table, but we didn't win without cost. There will be relocations. Traditional crafts will shift. Some of you will have to relearn where and how to make a living. We will fight to make that as fair as it can be."

A hush. The concession lands like a wave — real and unglamorous.

Tala Kumari: (standing, voice raw) "We kept our voice. We kept power in the hands of those who live with the tides. That matters."

Dr. Selene Park stands at the edge of the crowd, hands folded, notched with paperwork. She catches your eye and this time her expression is not unreadable. There is, briefly, an almost human flash: the realization that the engineered future must be stitched with place.

Dr. Selene Park: "I underestimated the value of rooted knowledge. We'll be integrating traditional breakwater patterns and craft-based monitoring into our models. It will slow deployments but make them more resilient."

You: "Then teach us the tech. We'll teach you the tide."

Dr. Selene Park permits something close to a smile.

Dr. Selene Park: "Deal."

Mateo steps closer, and the space between you is full of everything you have both been holding: fear, hope, exhaustion, a fragile, fierce tenderness that is more action than ornament.

Mateo Ríos: "What's next?"

You: (you look out over the crowd of faces, at Rohan with his hook, at Ivy's bright, tired grin, at Tala's clenched hands slowly relaxing) "We keep working. We watch the builds. We guard the board. We don't let anyone privatize the story of this place."

You feel pride like heat under your skin. It is not the clean triumph of total victory. You feel grief for crafts that will change, for houses that cannot be saved, for mornings that will be learned in new harbors. But grief sits alongside a stronger rod of hope — here are real funds, a community at the table, and a corporate actor redirected toward partnership.

Mateo finds your hand and holds it without words. The contract that will shape the next phase of Marisma Bay is framed on the table behind you: signatures looping, inked with fatigue and determination. People drift into smaller groups, trading plans and recipes and lists of who needs what.

You: (you let yourself accept it — a small, foreign grace) "We did it together."
