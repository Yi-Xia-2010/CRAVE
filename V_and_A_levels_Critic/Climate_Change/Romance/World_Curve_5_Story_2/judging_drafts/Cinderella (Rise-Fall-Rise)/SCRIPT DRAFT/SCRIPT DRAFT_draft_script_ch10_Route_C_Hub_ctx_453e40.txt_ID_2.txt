[Scene: Tide-sprayed Conference Room | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Framed windows streaked with salt; rain-bright patches on the concrete floor. A slim holographic logo of Selene Restoration shimmers beside a projected map of the marsh.]
[Sound: Low drone of a rental generator; muffled gull calls and the distant slap of tide against pilings]
[Music: Tense BGM — pulsing strings under a restless piano]
You are standing where your voice opened the door. The room still smells faintly of the Aquarium's kettle and something sharper now — ozone and the tang of antiseptic from the biotech crates, like the coast itself has been translated into lab-scented shorthand.

Dr. Selene Park: "Ms. Navarro, thank you for... taking the pragmatic step."

You hear the word pragmatic press against the rafters. It is both a compliment and a quiet dismissal. Her hologram folds back to reveal neat columns of data: deployment timelines, projected erosion reduction, jobs created per quarter. The numbers are clean; the promise is quick.

You feel the notebook under your palm, a familiar anchor. The pressed marsh grass in its pages trembles with your pulse. You remember the flooded threshold of your childhood home, the taste of salt on your mother's lips. Remembering makes the room smaller and louder.

Dr. Selene Park: "We can seed forty linear meters by this weekend. The engineered rhizome scaffolds will take root within weeks. We can roll crews in—machines, technicians—no long procurement, no months of deadlocked meetings."

Tala, arms folded, does not hide the way her jaw tightens when she speaks.

Tala: "And who runs the crews? Who decides who stays and who leaves when scaffolding changes the harvest patterns? You bring in tech and take home the contracts."

Dr. Park's smile is precise; she does not flinch.

Dr. Selene Park: "We contract locally where possible. We provide training programs, employment opportunities. This isn't extraction, Ms. Kumari. It's restoration through innovation."

Old Man Rohan, voice like a rope dragged across wood, leans forward so his words collect like barnacles.

Old Man Rohan: "Your scaffolds might plant faster than the marsh remembers. That kind of speed makes the tide forget the old ways. What if the sea—what if the fish respond differently? You engineers ever ask the coast to speak?"

Dr. Park's eyes flick to him; an unreadable smallness passes over her face. You have learned that look: curiosity refracted through algorithmic certainty.

Mateo steps closer to you, the dampness in his hair darkening the curls at his neck. He holds his waterproof tube of blueprints like a broker of calm.

Mateo Ríos: "If we can secure the deployment window, we can write binding clauses—monitoring, community oversight, job guarantees, revenue sharing. We can insist on local hires and clause-based sunset provisions."

He looks at you for the calculus — for the measure between speed and safeguard.

Mateo Ríos: (softly) "I can negotiate. I'll push the council. We can keep custody of the monitoring data."

You study his face: steady, hopeful, and undercut by the tension that comes from promising what may not be deliverable. You want to believe him; believing feels like a lifeline and a risk in the same breath.

Dr. Park inclines her head. Her tone softens with practiced empathy.

Dr. Selene Park: "We are not enemies of place. We scale what works. Contracts need speed; the sea doesn't wait for consensus."

A murmur runs through the room like wind under a tarp. Council members glance at each other; some fingers drum steady rhythms on laminated agendas. The divide in the room feels physical now, an eddy that would pull anyone standing alone.

Ivy crosses her arms and plants her feet. She presses her lips into a thin line, the kind you've seen when someone puts a hand on something they won't give up.

Ivy: "If they do it, what happens to the fishers who know the tides? What happens to the market stalls if the marsh changes and the catches move off-shore?"

Mateo: "We include transition funds. We can shape where work goes. We can set retraining that begins on day one."

You want to ask what 'transition' will look like when your neighbors can barely afford rent on a good month. You want to ask whether a 'training program' will be enough when a lifetime of tacit knowledge is devalued by a lab-grown root.

[Scene Transition: The docks — twilight. Damp wood, pallet stacks. A cargo skiff hums as it ties up.]
[Visual: Metallic pallets marked with Selene Restoration insignia being offloaded; workers in neutral uniforms unsealing biodegradable crates labeled 'Rhizome Scaffolds - Sterile.' Flickering LED work lamps cast hard shadows.]
[Sound: Clack of crates, the hiss of pneumatic seals, low conversation in clipped tones]
[Music: Percussion-driven tension, heartbeat tempo]
You follow them to the docks because seeing the materials unload makes the future literal. The air tastes of diesel and salt; flecks of algae cling to boots. The scaffolds look like ribs of a living thing, pale and unnatural against the harbor's worn colors.

A tech lifts a transparent packet of what looks like compressed seed-matter. It smells faintly of iron and something sweet — like canned ocean breeze. A label prints on the crate: growth metrics, genetic lineage codes, a QR array that, when scanned, unfurls a cascade of approval stamps and deployment logs.

Tala touches one of the crates and withdraws her hand as if burned.

Tala: "They bring packets that decide what grows here. It is not just restoration; it's choice by ledger."

You remember Rohan's voice about the coast speaking. Now the coast has an author, a corporate one, writing in sterile packets. The word 'author' tastes like an accusation.

A dockworker with tattooed knuckles looks at you, eyes searching.

Dockworker: "So—this is the fast fix, right? Jobs soon?"

You can see the calculation in his face. Getting the crate off the pier is survival in a town where torn roofs and unpaid bills are daily things. He is not thinking about sunsets. He is thinking about next week's rent.

Mateo notices the tension in the line of faces and pulls you aside, breath visible in the cooling air.

Mateo Ríos: "If I can get even three legal safeguards—community data custody, mandatory local hiring quotas, and a sunset clause for corporate control—it's something. It'll slow them maybe a week, but it's leverage."

His fingers, damp and steady, find the edge of your notebook.

Mateo Ríos: (urgent) "We have to be precise in bargaining. If you let them deploy now, they set the frame. If we negotiate, we risk losing the window but keep a chance to bind them."

You feel the pitch of the night raise your chest. The crates are already being moved onto trucks bound for the marsh. Every pallet shifted is a feather on a scale. The pace is fast; the sea behind you is faster.

[Scene: City Hall — Planning Office | Night]
[Visual: Long conference table under fluorescent lights. Holographic marsh projections flicker; city councilors sit with folded hands. Wet coats hang like flags on spare chairs.]
[Sound: Hum of the building's HVAC; the occasional distant siren; the soft plastic rustle of council papers]
[Music: Tension swells — strings and low brass emphasizing an oncoming fracture]
You return to City Hall with the taste of crate dust behind your teeth. Councilor Nguyen's lamplight creates halos on the table. The projection of the marsh spins slowly, its colors edited by Dr. Park's overlays: smoother shorelines, engineered root lines, proposed access paths.

Councilor Nguyen: "The timelines are tempting. Our constituents want immediate protection. Which is a reasonable demand."

His hands fold in the quiet economy of someone used to balancing votes and contracts. He is not unkind; he is practical in the way politicians must be when storms threaten their margins.

Mateo leans forward, the blueprint tube beside him like a staff. He speaks with the careful cadence of someone who has practiced making complex things sound simple.

Mateo Ríos: "We can insert legal clauses that guarantee community governance over monitoring, require third-party ecological audits every quarter, and create a fund that directly supports displaced fishers."

Dr. Selene Park: (smiling, cool) "We can accept oversight. We can agree to independent audits, provided they meet scientific rigor standards. Contracts can be crafted."

Tala's laugh is short and brittle.

Tala: "You can sign anything. Corporations sign with one hand and lobby with the other. Audits are delayed; 'independence' is defined by who pays."

Dr. Park's jaw tightens, just for a bare instant.

Dr. Selene Park: "Our models are public. We will commit to transparency."

Old Man Rohan: "Transparency on paper doesn't mean the sea won't change. The sea doesn't sign papers."

You feel your chest tighten with a pressure like a hand closing around a throat. Negotiation is not a clean arithmetic; it's a tug-of-war where people's livelihoods are the rope.

Councilor Nguyen turns to you. The room narrows to the space between your ribs and your words.

Councilor Nguyen: "Aiko Navarro — you asked us to open the next phase with Dr. Park. What's your recommendation now that the materials are on our piers and residents are split?"

Your choices fork like marsh channels. Every route smells of salt and consequence. You can ask Mateo to wrestle clauses into the contract; you can accept deployment for the sake of immediate jobs and cover your neighbors for a week; you can refuse Dr. Park and side with Tala's grassroots resistance, risking the immediate protection.

Mateo: (soft, urgent) "If we don't act fast, they deploy and define the terms by default. We can ask for legally binding community governance now. I'll get council to table a clause package."

Tala: "Or we refuse and build when we have control. But we risk floods while we wait. People will lose roofs and fishers could lose seasons."

Dr. Selene Park: "You have the option of speed or of control. But speed buys lives."

Ivy's knuckles white on the edge of the table, voice small but fierce.

Ivy: "People are hungry for work. People don't want another meeting. They want help."

Your internal monologue is a whipcord of memory and calculation. You feel the weight of your mother's locket burn against your sternum. You recall nights bailing small boats, the ache of ropes under blistered palms, the way community decisions once felt like shared weather — inevitable, not violent. Now decisions feel like storms. The town is split down a seam that will not reseal easily.

The conversation accelerates; people talk over each other. The air is sharp with fear and argument. Your heart is a drum, each beat louder than the last. This is the crescendo you have been steering toward since you opened your notebook.

You think of the engineered packets stacked on trucks, of Rohan's lined face lit by dock lamps, of Tala shouting into a small megaphone outside the Aquarium, of Mateo's calm hands on a pen that might write protective clauses or might be nothing more than ink on wet paper.

Everything feels urgent and fragile at once.

You open your mouth, and the options lay out like tide-lines before the sea pulls them under.
