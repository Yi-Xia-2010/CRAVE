## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Heart

---

## World View
Marisma Bay is a near-future coastal town remade by water and will. Rising tides and brittle policy have stitched the town into a patchwork of reclaimed lots, salt-streaked buildings, and communal gardens—an ordered improvisation against climate change. Municipal authority sits between a city hall banking on engineered fixes and a fractious community whose livelihoods are tied to the marsh. Private restoration firms promise fast, measurable security with biotech scaffolds and algae barriers; community organizers press for low-tech stewardship, local knowledge, and equitable retreat. Religion and ideology are diffuse here; ritual takes the shape of shared meals, sea-bound memorials, and the oral history kept by elders. Class lines follow the floodplain: long-time fishers and market vendors cling to Low Street while investors and planners convene in the glass of City Hall. Geography matters: the harbor, the cliffside promenade, and the reclaimed Tidepark greenhouse are not backdrops but actors—each storm, sluice, and salt-bloom reshapes power. Atmosphere: weathered, bittersweet, and tactile—wet fabric, algae sheen, and late-golden light that catches on sea spray like a question. The engine of conflict is both ecological and intimate: how to protect a marsh while preserving the human ties rooted in that place, and at what personal cost.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Aiko Navarro) return to Marisma Bay after losing my childhood home and bring ecological engineering skills to a town at a tipping point. The story is a Cinderella arc: a hopeful rise toward a community-led pilot plan, a sudden collapse when bureaucratic pressure and seductive corporate fixes split allegiances, then a final, determined rise that reconciles innovation and place-based stewardship. Romantic threads bind choices to consequence: Mateo, the conciliatory planner, offers institutional bridges; Dr. Selene Park, the charismatic corporate director, offers rapid techno-solutions; Tala and Ivy press the human cost. Each major decision forces me to weigh love and responsibility, and each route compresses the drama into a tight six-chapter arc that escalates in intensity. Some endings lean toward communal triumph, others toward wrenching compromise, and one forces a personal sacrifice for wider systemic change. Throughout, the marsh—its tides, sounds, and the textures of the town—functions as a moral presence, shaping how I and the people I love live, grieve, and rebuild.
