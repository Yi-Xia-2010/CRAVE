### DRAFT
# Route: Route C


[Scene: Harborfront Boardwalk | Late Morning]
[Visual: Wet wooden planks stretch along the harbor, slick with recent spray. Tarps flap at the edges of stalls; a string of damp flyers clings to a lamppost.]
[Sound: Low murmurs, the soft slap of waves, distant gull calls. Occasional creak of wood underfoot.]
[Music: Gentle, hopeful piano undercurrent — steady, patient]

You descend from the rooftop into a city that smells of salt and old rope. The printed notice you slipped into your notebook presses against the basil leaves; its edge is a small, stubborn weight against your ribs. You keep your seed vial at your throat — a glass pulse that crosses your breath with the memory of dirt and stubborn life.

The boardwalk is braced against a thin, bright rain. Neighbors huddle in scarves and patched coats; their faces are lined with worry and the quiet strength that comes from shared labor. Kira stands at the margin in her blue raincoat, a coil of voice then silence, a protest placard half-rolled under one arm. Mateo leans on a walking stick near the front, his fisherman's cap pulled low. The sea beyond the pilings looks both placid and intent, like a thing thinking about change.

[Visual: Jonah Hale steps forward. His blazer sheds a faint trickle of rain; his hair is precise even in the wind. He carries a tablet. Behind him, a temporary screen shows glossy renderings: a sweeping seawall and a gleaming Model Plaza bathed in warm lights.]
[Sound: A polite ripple of applause, the rustle of paper as people shift]

Jonah Hale: "Good morning. The plan we present will protect key commercial districts and create a civic plaza that attracts investment — jobs, security, and long-term resilience." (He smiles, measured.) "This design is modular; it can be implemented with minimal interruption and scalable maintenance."

You watch his mouth move the words like someone polishing a tool. The imagery on the screen promises safety in tidy lines. It promises certainty. Jonah's amber-brown eyes sweep the crowd as if appraising a project for auction and a neighborhood for valuation at once.

Mayor Serena Okoye: "Mr. Hale, the council appreciates your clarity. We'll open the floor to questions and community input."

[Sound: Microphone rustle. A few hands rise. A soft cough from the crowd.]

Elias is near the front, sleeves rolled, a slim wristpad clasped at his side. When his steel-blue eyes find you there is a small, steadying recognition — the way someone meets a familiar latitude on a map. He nods, not a solution but an encouragement to speak by the shore.

Elias Rook: "We're advocating for a hybrid approach," (he begins, clear and calm) "—a system where engineered elements work with green corridors. Living shorelines, vegetated berms, structural supports only where strictly necessary. It reduces both cost and ecological disruption."

You feel the logic in his voice, the careful scaffolding of a plan that can be measured and modeled. It tugs at the practical part of you that loves systems almost as much as you love soil. But there is an underside to the calculations — stories that cannot be entered into a model, the memory of a fence post lost to a storm.

Kira (from the edge): "We don't want your polished plazas instead of our roofs!" (She calls, voice bright as flint.) "We want gardens that feed us, not promenades that price us out!"

[Sound: A ripple of assent. A few claps. A murmur of tension, but not heat — contained, like breath held under water.]

Mateo steps forward when the microphone finds him. His voice is roughened by years of wind.

Mateo Alvarez: "When I was a boy the pier had a sign that creaked in storms. My father tied nets there. You can't put a price on that creak." (His hand trembles as he gestures toward the harbor.) "If you cover the water with concrete, you take away the place that remembers us."

The crowd leans in. You taste copper and salt. The seed vial at your throat is warm against your skin, a small repository of what you protect.

Jonah returns to the microphone before the murmur settles.

Jonah Hale: "We are not advocating erasure. The plaza includes green spaces and public amenities. But the reality is —" (he inclines his head toward the models) "— these measures are what will guarantee protection without endless, ad hoc fixes."

Mayor Serena Okoye: "Mr. Rook, your hybrid proposal — those models indicate potential savings and broader protections. How would you ensure displacement doesn't follow construction?"

Elias Rook: "We can build strict displacement safeguards into the contract." (He speaks carefully, fingers brushing the wristpad.) "Phased construction, community co-management clauses, funding earmarked for relocation assistance, and prioritizing existing plots for restoration post-construction."

A practical machinery of promises. You can hear the gears of governance clicking into place in his phrasing. It is reassuring in the same precise way that a blueprint can be — but the imprint of lived memory is not always neatly phaseable.

You step forward to speak, but the river inside you is soft and patient. You want to hold both hope and hesitation between your palms.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Frame your argument around community stories") -> Reaction: "You imagine the faces of people who tend the green roofs, the scraped hands and the shared coffee. When you speak, your words carry those small, irreplaceable textures." || Choice B ("Lean on Elias' hybrid language to suggest a compromise") -> Reaction: "You borrow Elias' measured cadence, offering integration as a bridge. Your voice becomes a translator between models and memories."]

You choose both in the way you breathe: memory threaded through measure.

You: "Thank you. I want to begin where Mateo left us — at the places that remember. Green infrastructure isn't just a soft add-on; it's the fabric that holds our food, our shade, our stories." (You feel your voice land steady, unshaken by the cold.) "We can integrate living shorelines and create corridors that buffer storm surge, yes — but only if the people who plant and maintain them keep control and are not moved aside for glossy surfaces or 'model' plazas whose promises don't include the cost of losing what we already have."

[Sound: A hush, then low murmurs. A few fingers drum on tarps.]

Jonah Hale: (leaning forward, not unkind) "Ms. Moreno, community stewardship is valuable — but can it scale? How do you fund maintenance without predictable revenue streams?"

You: "We scale by making stewardship part of the plan, not an afterthought. Create paid community stewardship roles, seed a maintenance fund from public bonds, and require contractors to partner with local co-ops. If investment is the carrot, let the carrot feed those doing the work." (You tap your notebook where the pressed basil is, the gesture small but deliberate.) "We need displacement safeguards written into any contract. No project gets a shovel in the ground unless we know where people go and how they're supported."

Elias Rook: (softly) "We can draft those clauses. There are models — legal frameworks that bind developers to community commitments. It will slow timelines, but it'll make outcomes durable."

Kira (sharp, earnest): "Durable only if you keep them honest. Contracts get rewritten. We've seen 'community benefits' evaporate when the lights shine on the plaza and the cameras leave."

Mayor Serena Okoye: (weary but pragmatic) "This is the balance the council must find. Mr. Hale has resources; Mr. Rook offers integration. Ms. Moreno, Ms. Tseng, Mr. Alvarez — your demands are essential. The question for the council is whether the compromise will be sufficient."

[Visual: The screen behind Jonah cycles images — a plaza at dusk, people laughing, trees in planters. The images feel like promises packaged in warm light.]
[Sound: The tide tapping softly at the pilings; the crowd’s breathing synchronized under the cloudy sky.]

You feel the tide of attention shift, gentle and inevitable. Loyalties are not being ripped; they are being negotiated in the quiet arithmetic of policy and memory. It is a soft sort of pressure — not the flare of anger but the patient pressure of time and tide.

Elias meets your eyes again for a fraction of a second. There's something like apology there, folded into practical hope. His expression is complex — the kind of look that holds numbers and neighborhood names together without resolving which should bend to the other. You keep your reply to him small, because words here must serve the many.

[Sound: A bell from the council table — a call to close public commentary soon. People shift, a chair scrapes.]

Mayor Serena Okoye: "We will take these proposals into committee. There will be time to refine language on displacement safeguards and co-management. For now, we need to move toward a decision framework."

You sense the room collectively inhaling, measuring how much to give and how much to hold.

Your heart is steady. The positive note — the belief that solutions can be stitched together — hums quietly through the crowd. The arousal is low but purposeful, like the steady turning of a key.

You look once more at the renderings, then at the rows of faces: Kira with her bright coat and fierce trust, Mateo with his weathered hands, Elias with blue eyes that keep finding the maps. The tide is shifting; loyalties will follow the seam of how well promises are kept.

[Visual: Close on your hand resting lightly on the seed vial at your throat; the glass reflects the harbor light.]
[Music: The piano motif lingers and softens, a page-turning cadence]

You step back into the press of neighbors as the council adjourns for committee. It is not a resolution. It is not yet a victory. But the air tastes faintly of possibility — rain-soaked, stubborn, insistently green.
