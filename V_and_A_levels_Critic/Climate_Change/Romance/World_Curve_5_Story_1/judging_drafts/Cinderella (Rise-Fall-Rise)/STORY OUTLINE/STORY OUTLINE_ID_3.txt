## Visual Novel Title
Tides of Seed and Stone

---

## World View
Near-future Harbor City (2042) clings to life around a battered inlet. Once-bustling docks and industrial chimneys now sit beside patches of reclaimed rooftop farms and community greenhouses; salt and soot mingle on brick facades. Politics is a negotiated arithmetic between corporate consortiums and grassroots coalitions: private developers promise security and profit through engineered defenses, while neighborhood networks argue for nature-based adaptation, food sovereignty, and social continuity. Religion and formal creed are secondary to a civic ethic of possession and memory—who owns the shoreline’s past and future. Class fractures run along waterfront lines: long-term residents like fishermen and gardeners who remember tides by name, and newer investors who read risk as an asset. Geography folds into the story: raised terraces and sagging boardwalks, seawall construction sites, and warm greenhouses that feel like sanctuaries. Atmosphere is conflicted: soft realist cinematics in pastel storm palettes; rain-washed concrete and warm biophilic interiors juxtapose industrial grit. Climate trauma is omnipresent—the city negotiates survival through trade-offs that test intimacy, memory, and the idea of home.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Ayla Moreno: practical idealist and gardener-activist who built a rooftop network to protect my harbor neighborhood. The city faces a high-stakes choice: Jonah Hale’s decisive seawall and levee plan promises quick security and commercial revival; Elias Rook, a climate engineer I am slowly falling for, invites hybrid collaboration between rigorous engineering and my community’s green solutions. I must steer the rooftop network and greenhouse to protect people and ecosystems. The story follows a Cinderella emotional arc: an initial rise of hope and connection (I bond with Elias and crest with community momentum), a sudden fall as a compromise, disaster, or betrayal imperils the neighborhood and my relationship, and finally a rise where some version of durable adaptation emerges—sometimes through hard negotiation, sometimes through sacrifice, sometimes in bittersweet compromise. Across branching routes, I face multiple pivotal choices: whom to trust, where to compromise, whether to accept funds, to protest, or to gamble on a risky direct action. Romance with Elias is tested by differing priorities—my insistence on community-led methods versus his modeling-led pragmatism. Every branch keeps the same characters and places (green roofs, greenhouse, lab, seawall site) and explores how love and civic duty shape one another when the sea keeps rising.
