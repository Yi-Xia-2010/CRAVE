[Scene: Converted Market Hall / Late Afternoon — Stormlight]
[Visual: A wide, vaulted interior—timber ribs and hanging lanterns—slick with drizzle. Wide glass panes streaked with salt and rain throw gray shards of light across rows of folding chairs.]
[Music: Low, steady string underscoring; a measured heartbeat of ambient percussion]
[Sound: Rain drumming, boots on wet stone, occasional coughs and the scrape of a microphone stand]

The hall tastes of coffee and salt. You feel both on your tongue as you weave through the press: the bitter heat of a commuter mug, the cool tang of the harbor air pulled in through the wide doors. Wet wisps of hair cling to your forehead; your gloves are tucked into a belt loop where the leather rubs warm against denim. Your seed vial hangs steady at your throat like a small, quiet promise.

Mayor Serena Okoye sits raised behind the dais, the civic sash a band of calm responsibility across her chest. Her eyes are ringed with tiredness; every line on her face has been earned by negotiations and sleepless nights. When she leans forward, the light from the panes catches the decision in her shadowed eyes.

Serena Okoye: "We need clear recommendations for the committee. We need to weigh safety, equity, and the social cost of each option."

You nod even though you've already spoken to the staffer who handed you the agenda. Nodding is something everyone here does—an economy of assent and intent. Around you, people shift into the small roles the room has always given them: the skeptic, the hopeful, the tired elder who remembers a pier that once hummed with nets and laughter.

[Visual: Elias at the center glass pane, tapping; pale blue floodlines bloom and retreat in layered waves.]
[Sound: Soft electronic chirrups from the projection; murmurs as residents lean to see]

Elias Rook: (calmly) "These are ensemble runs—probabilistic flood envelopes layered with livelihood maps. When we overlay infrastructure footprints, we can see where engineered measures must be highest and where vegetated zones reduce peak loads."

Elias' voice is precise but warm. He speaks with the steady cadence of someone who builds safety with models; his fingers trace trade-offs, and each gesture is an argument. The projection makes the invisible visible: where food gardens will face regular inundation, where the market will slip into tidal reach, where people will commute through deeper water.

You listen for the places your rooftop network appears on those maps. Seeing them as colored polygons is both necessary and strange—you are used to the soil giving you an answer, not contour bands and probabilities.

Jonah Hale: (interjecting, measured) "We can accelerate construction timelines. With private financing and phased zoning we buy time—secure critical commerce and then layer community spaces into the second phase. The economic data supports rapid deployment."

Jonah's amber-brown eyes cut through the room like a blade wrapped in velvet. He lays a financing timeline on the lectern like a promise, fingers tapping the page as if to keep time. For everyone who has watched his renderings—plazas lit with curated trees and clean promenades—he is the voice of deliverable futures.

Kira Tseng: (sharp, immediate) "And that's exactly why we need a moratorium on displacement. Promises to 'layer in' community spaces become fine print. People don't get 'layered in.' They get moved."

Kira stands with her raincoat bright as an exclamation point. Her words are not quiet; they are urgent and honest. The room hums with sympathetic clicks—phones lifted, heads turning. Activist energy pulls at the seams of the meeting like wind at a tent.

Mateo Alvarez shuffles to the front carrying a folded paper map, edges soft with use. He wrestles it open with slow reverence.

Mateo Alvarez: (soft, remembering) "There was a pier here—old wood and netting, my father's lines used to snag on the rotted planks. They buried it under landfill for a road that lasted ten years. We built on top of memory and called it progress. Don't let them bury what keeps us fed."

His voice lands with the authority of someone who has lived the harbor's cycles. A hush follows. The map he lays out is old, smudged; salt has smudged ink until names become suggestion. It smells faintly of oil and seaweed. You run a thumb over a line of folded paper and feel the grief of loss and the sturdiness of memory pressed into pulp.

Mayor Serena Okoye: (carefully) "We have engineering imperatives—people's safety is not theoretical. We also have legal and moral obligations to prevent displacement. These are not mutually exclusive, but they are not simple to reconcile."

The conversation becomes a triadic dance: Elias' models, Jonah's timelines, Kira's insistence. Each argument loops back to the town's core worry—how to keep people safe without erasing the places that feed them.

You take the platform. Talking in this room is a practiced economy; you parcel out memory, fact, and hope in equal measures.

You (Ayla): (clearly) "Before we sign timelines or finance packages, let's map what won't rebuild if it's gone. Mateo's map—historical points—paired with Elias' models and the rooftop network—if we produce a combined overlay, the committee can see the human footprint not as anecdote but as a measurable risk."

Elias: (responding) "Yes. That overlay can show avoided costs as well—gardens as buffers, subsidies saved when storm peaks are attenuated."

Jonah: (cautious) "I won't dismiss the idea of incorporated overlays—but we need milestones. I can work with a mapping pause if it doesn't blow the schedule."

Kira: (earnest) "A pause that delays construction is a pause that gives people time to mobilize. We want safeguards in law—not footnotes."

Mayor Serena Okoye: (decisive) "A legally mandated community overlay study, with clear binding protections for marked sites, is a legitimate condition. If the committee accepts a phased schedule that includes binding protections for identified community assets, I will consider it. However, I need clarity on timelines and enforcement mechanisms."

Her words are a hinge; the room senses it. She is not promising everything, but she is opening a procedural crack through which protections might pass. The political machinery whirs with possibility.

Mateo: (softly) "If you can mark the old pier, you mark what doesn't come back once it's filled. That's all I'm asking."

Elias: (to you, quietly, conspiratorial) "I can model enforcement triggers—legal clauses tied to funding disbursement. If a community garden is in a protected zone, construction there can't proceed without judicial review."

Jonah: (raising an eyebrow) "Enforcement clauses complicate financing and raise holding costs. Investors are sensitive to added legal friction."

Kira: (heated, but hopeful) "Then we make it politically risky to ignore. We build public oversight. We make the cost of displacing people higher than the profit of easy land."

You feel the room tilt toward procedural solutions—legal language, mapping, enforcement triggers. The tone remains collaborative more than combative. The arousal has risen from a steady cadence to a purposeful urgency: the kind that gathers people into a shared project.

Inside your chest, an image surfaces—dew on greenhouse glass, the warm smell of damp soil, seedlings ringed by string lights. You imagine those trays weighed against timelines and concrete pylons. The stakes are personal, but the air here tastes of possibility: legal language that protects, models that respect lived knowledge, and a mayor willing to demand enforceable conditions.

Mayor Serena Okoye: (looking around, summoning closure) "People of the harbor—what is your stance? Do we pursue an integrated hybrid plan with binding protections? Do we halt construction and demand a moratorium until we map and legislate? Or do we open direct negotiations with the developer now to secure concessions?"

You breathe in coffee and salt and let the decision hang like stormlight through glass.

The conversation has brought you to one hinge where the policy and the personal align. It is a hinge that will shape not only the plan but who stands with you at dawn: engineers at a drafting board, activists on the boardwalk, or negotiators in a backroom with the developer's lapel pin catching the light.
