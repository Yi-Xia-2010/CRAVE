[Scene: Harborfront Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — Stormclouds Gathering]
[Visual: Wide shot of the boardwalk—slick planks, sandbags piled like low fortifications, banners of reclaimed sails stitched with paint, market stalls converted into podiums]
[Music: Driving percussion under a rising choral swell — urgent, hopeful]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the slap of tide on pilings, the murmur of a crowd turning into focused noise]

You chose the street. The idea that became a plan now breathes in wet fabric and shouted names. Kira stands near the center, megaphone clutched like a baton; her raincoat gleams with protest badges, short hair plastered to her skull by damp wind. Around her, neighbors rearrange tarpaulins into banners, drag old crates into stepped platforms, and stack sandbags until the line looks less like a blockade and more like a deliberate spine.

You feel the weight of the vial at your throat—seed packets clicking softly against glass. Your jacket smells of soil and salt; the inner lining has dried fingerprints where you've taken the notebook in and out. This is not pageantry. This is a map of what you love turned outward, exposed to wind and accusation.

Kira: "All right, people—eyes here! When Mateo speaks, give him room. When the vendors call, let them be heard. We hold the line, we keep it peaceful, and we make our history visible." (She taps the megaphone; the sound is rough, immediate.)

You step closer to one of the makeshift platforms, feeling the vibration of voices through the wood. The air tastes of coffee someone is passing around and the metallic edge of tidewater. A vendor with flour-dusted hands offers you a damp bread roll; you take it with one hand, thumb brushing the salt-crusted seam of a sandbag with the other. Small trade; small solidarity.

A man with weathered hands—Mateo—steps up. His cap is the same one you remember from childhood runs to the docks; the fabric is thinner, the brim softer, but he holds himself like ballast. He inhales once, and his voice comes out a ledger of tides and names.

Mateo: "You lot remember the winter of '31? We stacked sand, we prayed. Jonah's plans—big concrete, fine renderings—sound like salvation until you count the porches lost, the green patches gone." (His fingers tremble against the mic; the crowd hushes to hear the tremor in his throat.)

You move forward before you think. Your voice needs to be the clear thread through the sound—a story, not a statistic.

You: "We are not against safety. We are for a city that keeps the people who made it. Green roofs, shared waterways—these aren't sentimental. They're insurance written in roots and hands." (You can't see the council chambers from here, but you feel them watching.)

A vendor interrupts, voice urgent: "My stand feeds fourteen kids after school. Where will they go if they take the pier?" The cadence of the question ricochets off the crowd and finds a hundred other mouths to answer.

Kira (into megaphone): "Tell us your name. Tell them what this block is. Make it human." (She gestures at you, then waves toward the line of cameras someone has set up.)

The crowd begins to form a ledger of stories—quick, breathless testimonies that carve the seawall's renderings into real consequences. Each voice is a knock on glass. The atmosphere tightens like a drum skin ready to snap, and with each account the energy pulses higher.

Voices swell into a single, ragged chorus—then a hush snaps as footsteps pound the planks. Elias appears at the edge of the assembly, mud darkening the hem of his boots, a tablet still tucked into a holster at his side. He looks out at the crowd, at the banners, at the faces that have been his models and data points for months, and for a second you see the human calculus in his eyes.

Elias: (softly) "Ayla—" (His voice holds the equation of apology and urgency.) "The models show the wall would reduce catastrophic inundation—thousands saved. I'm—" (He glances at the crowd, falters.) "I didn't expect them to be here like this."

You want to reach across the distance that has lately felt like spreadsheets and negotiations. The sun—if the thin light can be called sun—catches the wet planes of his jacket, turning the technical shell into something almost vulnerable.

You: "We didn't expect to be erased for a render either, Elias. Models are maps, not the people who live on the lines." (You keep your voice steady, even as it tightens. You're careful: this is about bridging, not burning.)

Elias: "I—" (He presses his thumb along the edge of his tablet like a talisman.) "I've seen worst-case projections. I keep thinking of the people who lose everything in a storm. I'm trying to save them, Ayla—"

Kira: (cutting in, bracing) "Then help us save our places, not just property values. Help us keep the rooftop gardens, the fish stalls, the places kids learn to knot a net." (Her tone is clipped with wear but luminous with insistence.)

The crowd murmurs, a tide shifting underfoot. You and Elias speak around each other for a moment—every sentence loaded with the work you've both done and the miles between professional models and lived memory.

Elias: "There are hybrid approaches. Sea-facing structures combined with managed wetlands—" (He motions with his chin toward schematic lines only he can see.) "But I can't sell a plan to the board without a clear 'safe' story."

You: "Our story is already here, Elias. It's in Mateo's hands, in that vendor's bread, in Kira's badges. We can make 'safe' mean everyone—"

Before you can finish, a different sort of sound cuts through: a polished step, the air around it clean with a different certainty. Jonah arrives, comm device on his lapel flashing, his lapel pin catching a shard of light like a spear. Where you smell wind and coffee, he smells of engineered solutions and media framing.

Jonah: (smiling, arms open) "Good people. I appreciate civic engagement. But this blockade—" (He gestures toward the sandbags as if they were child's play)— "is obstruction in the face of objective risk." (His voice is smooth; he doesn't shout. He doesn't need to. The words land with the weight of a press release.)

Kira: (hot) "It's obstruction to take community out of the equation? Or obstruction to your bottom line?" (She jabs her thumb toward Jonah as if puncturing a slide.)

Jonah: (tilting his head) "No—it's obstruction to decisive action. We can build protection, stabilize commerce, attract investment. We can save lives, but not if we stall behind symbolic gestures." (He lets his gaze sweep the crowd like someone appraising a market he intends to buy.)

There is a crackle in the air as cameras pivot, someone curses at the sight of a corporate smile rubbing against a rusted lamppost. The boardwalk has become an amphitheater: every gesture a political signal, every shouted sentence a pressure point.

Mateo steps forward again, voice wavering but steadying with the force of memory.

Mateo: "Investments don't feed kids. Concrete doesn't knit nets back together. You promise a plaza—what about the fishermen who never set foot on a plaza? Who remembers us then?" (He looks at Jonah as if the man might reply with more than percentages.)

Jonah: (soft, practiced) "We do remember. That's why there are compensation plans, relocation packages—"

Kira: (spitting) "Compensation is not the same as continuity. You don't get the smell of a fish market back with a check."

The crowd shifts: some nod, some jeer, some hold up placards. Mayor Serena Okoye stands at the periphery—her sash jacket a political sash of both authority and bone-deep exhaustion—eyes sweeping from the podiums to the council cameras. She does not speak yet, but the way she holds herself—measured, aware—says clearly that the optics of this moment will be a ledger she cannot ignore.

Mayor Serena: (quietly, to a council aide) "Record everything. We will hear this in committee, but the public must see that we listened." (Her voice carries across a few yards like a gavel without the bang.)

The tension ratchets higher. Chanting starts and stops; someone bangs a pot; a child cries and a dozen hands reach to hush or cradle. Elias's steel-blue eyes meet yours across the press of people. He swallows.

Elias: (lower, sincere) "Ayla—I'm torn. I brought data because I thought it would protect people, and now I see what those protections might cost. I... I don't know the right compromise." (He runs a hand through his damp hair, mud on his boots like proof that he walks the same streets.)

Your chest feels tight with so many possible futures converging on this plank of wood and salt. Around you, each face is a counterargument and a hope. Your shoulders braced; you feel like an anchor and a sail at once.

The crowd leans forward as if the next moment could change the tide. Jonah folds his hands in that exact way CEOs fold hands—measured, served, definitive—and the boardwalk seems to inhale. Mateo's voice trembles as he pleads for measured action, and for a heartbeat the whole scene holds.

You know, without thinking of the vote's mechanics, that this is the key public moment—the one that will be replayed on screens and in memory. You are not alone here. Your friends are loud, your neighbors are present, and even Elias stands with mud on his boots. The storm offshore grumbles like a drumroll.

You breathe in sea-salt air, the hum of megaphones and the bright, fierce human chorus. The decision about how to proceed is imminent; the city's eyes are on you and on the boardwalk that has become a living argument for what kind of future will be allowed to stay.
