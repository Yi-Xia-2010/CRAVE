You press the napkin into your palm, feeling the fibers under your skin. The ink has bled where your thumb brushed it in Chapter Ten; it is small and human and has the weight of an entire shoreline. Your pen trembles in your grip the way soil shudders before a frost: a tiny thing that could kill what it touches or feed it, depending on how you move.

Ayla Moreno: "I'll sign—only if the first draft is on my desk, and community counsel is invited to every step. No surprises."

Jonah Hale: (smiling thinly) "No surprises. Only work. Only delivery." (He taps the edge of the contract with his pen, casual as a metronome.)

You taste lemon polish and tin of seawater; the fluorescent light gives everything a washed-out certainty. Around the table, faces bend with intent. Elias's jaw is tight; his tablet glows with timelines and redline notes. Mayor Serena leans forward, hands folded like she can fold the city into manageable parts. Elias Rook: "Ayla, the covenant secures land trusts and restoration grants. It sets oversight. If we pass this today, those protections are enforceable. Delay risks losing leverage because Jonah's timeline accounts for political momentum."

A pause. The word "enforceable" lands like a pledge; it smells of courtrooms and fluorescent air.

Ayla Moreno: (quiet, steady) "Enforceable on paper doesn't keep hands off the soil when the bulldozers come at sunrise. I know how the city writes its history."

Elias Rook: (soft, urgent) "I know. But we don't have to surrender the entire playbook. We can litigate later, call audits, demand escrow for disputed parcels—"

Jonah Hale: (interrupting, businesslike) "We need to be realistic. Construction windows must include emergency triggers. We cannot be held back when immediate fortification is required—"

Jonah's voice is smooth, practiced. He taps a clause: 'expedited construction in defined "emergency" windows.' The clause sits between you like a reef reefing the flow of the sea—designed to keep some safe, while letting others drown.

Kira Tseng: (fingers twisted around a rolled flyer, suddenly sharp) "That's the catch. Emergency today, privatize tomorrow. Don't hand them the language to seize what our community grew."

Kira's voice cuts—bright, raw. She leans forward, eyes flaring beneath her cropped hair. The binoculars are gone from around her neck, the megaphone slung under her arm like an accusation.

Mayor Serena Okoye: (tired, diplomatic) "We can define the triggers. Judicial oversight—"

Kira Tseng: (bitter laugh) "Define? You think 'define' is a wall? It's a loophole with a red ribbon."

Mateo Alvarez: (rough, slow) "My boy, listen. Paper can mean something if we make it mean it. But paper ain't hands in the dirt."

The room tightens. You feel the pressure—paper, ink, the hum of policy. Your throat is a dry garden. Every clause smells like a possibility and a threat. The covenant promises community land trusts, restoration grants for the greenhouse, legal standing for oversight. It promises ability to sue, to reclaim, to hold Jonah accountable—if the law moves like a tide we can read.

Jonah Hale: (leaning in, smile gone polite) "And for clarity, we will include an escalation clause for construction in instances defined as 'imminent failure' by certified engineers. It's not a grab; it's safety."

Elias Rook: (soft snap) "That's dangerously vague. 'Certified engineers'—who certifies them? Who names them?"

Jonah Hale: (smooth) "A panel—public and private. A hybrid model. Consensus."

Your eyes flick to Elias. He meets your look and for a heartbeat his steel-blue softness is all calculation and care.

Elias Rook: "Ayla, I'm saying sign. We keep the protections. We plan litigation. We pressure the panel appointments. It's the least-worst."

A murmur passes—a spoon on a glass, a breath. Kira's jaw works; her hands curl at her sides like someone holding a rope.

Kira Tseng: (voice broken) "So we sign and then sue? That's politics. That's letting them win while we bleed over time."

Alya Moreno: (internal) You can see the two halves of a coin. On one face: protections, legal guarantees, a roof for the greenhouse, a promise to keep plots from sudden seizure. On the other: a clause with teeth that could bite later, legal fights that will take years and money and faith. You think of Mateo's hands—brown and scarred, patient as tide stones. You think of the smell of tomato leaves in the greenhouse, the humidity fogging panes at dawn. You can almost feel the texture of the seed vial pressed against your sternum.

Jonah Hale: (flatly) "This is a distraction. The covenant stands. We proceed. We must keep the cadence of approval—political momentum is perishable."

Kira Tseng: (scoffs) "Momentum for you. Momentum to sell our shore."

Alya Moreno: (voice small, then firm) "We can't be reactionary, but we also can't be naive. Our people deserve guarantees, not promises we litigate into oblivion."

Jonah Hale: (leaning forward, voice hardening) "Then sign. Secure what's there. Further disputes can be remedied in court. Delay risks project derailment and, yes, immediate exposure of neighborhoods to unchecked flooding."

Kira Tseng: (standing, breath hot) "So we sell our future for an IOU."

Elias Rook: (reaching, pleading) "You know I'll stand with you in court. But the protections aren't hypothetical—Ayla, they're real."
