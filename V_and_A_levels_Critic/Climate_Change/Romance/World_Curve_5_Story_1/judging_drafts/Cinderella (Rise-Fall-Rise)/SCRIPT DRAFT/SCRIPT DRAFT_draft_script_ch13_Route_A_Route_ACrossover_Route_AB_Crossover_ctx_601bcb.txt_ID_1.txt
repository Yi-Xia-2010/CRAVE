You stand at the edge of where the garden was, and the world narrows to a handful of details: the glass vial at your throat, its seed packet sliver catching the gray light; the smell of wet soil, now raw and exposed; the thin, stubborn warmth of the greenhouse you still keep tucked in memory. Kira is pressed into the front line of voices—bright coat plastered with mud, fists clenched as if to squeeze the tide back into its bed. Her words are a serrated thing, public and private all at once.

Kira: "They promised no bulldozing without community consent. Where's the consent, Ayla? Where's the law they said they'd pass?"

You can hear the accusation landing against the promises that once sounded like anchors. You want to tell her every bargaining table, every signature, every letter to the Mayor that tried to hold the line. You: "We pushed for binding covenants. We got guarantees in writing—leases, relocation funds, co-management clauses. It's not everything. It's not the same soil, Kira, but it's more people saved."

Kira: "Saved? You call this price-saving? You handed them a page of earth and a handshake."

You: "I didn't hand—it was a trade. We traded one plot to protect dozens. There are legal protections now; cooperative leases, restored beds, food access clauses. The Mayor signed them."

Kira's face goes hard, and then softens to something like worn velvet, threaded with betrayal.

Kira: "So you choose the ledger over the lives that lived here. Beautiful."

The rain increases, a fine cold sieve that makes coats cling. Elias is near the machines, sleeves rolled, tablet abandoned at his feet. His eyes are the color of winter harbor water—clear but shallow with the residue of sleepless math. He moves through the crowd like someone moving through a tide chart—knowing where pressure points will break.

Elias: (quiet) "Ayla—" (he reaches, then retracts; the language of gestures is careful when words rust under grief) "We argued for the strictest restrictions we could get. I pushed, and they—"

You cut in because you need to hear the truth from him, not the rehearsed consolation promised to the press.

You: "Did you—did you get them to put it in irrevocable text? Not just a clause the consortium can weasel?"

Elias: "Mayor Serena attached emergency orders. Not perfect, but legally binding for ten years with mandatory review panels that include community stewards. Jonah agreed to a fund for soil remediation and to co-finance relocation beds. It was the only way to lock protections quickly."

You hold that: an emergency order is real, but it's also the kind of thing that can be revised in calmer times. Your fingers close around the vial at your chest until the glass hums against your sternum.

Mateo: "For the things we plant again."

Jonah stands a short distance away, his coat dry as if he'd been spared the rain. He speaks into a recorder for a visiting journalist with a practiced calm, the picture of a man who builds futures with his hands already wrapped in contracts.

Jonah: "It was a difficult decision. We moved quickly, but the protections enacted will prevent wholesale loss elsewhere. Sometimes you sacrifice a part to save the whole."

Kira explodes toward him, voice sharp enough to cut through the rain.

Kira: "You call it a sacrifice; we call it theft."

Jonah: (without flinch) "Concessions were part of the negotiation. Legal protections, relocation funding, community oversight. The investment protects the harbor's economic life and the people who depend on it."

Kira: "Protected by whom? Protected for whom? Your plazas for investors?"

The love between you and Elias endures, but it is edged—like glass that refracts light differently after a chip. You share work and cold dinners under shared shelter, and sometimes you fall asleep with hands entwined without saying much. There are conversations that go in circles; there are apologies that feel both necessary and insufficient. Trust grows like a graft: slow, precise, requiring both sides to trim and bind and wait.

Elias: (one evening, as you both scrub tools under a streetlamp) "I didn't want you to lose your history. I wanted to prevent a disaster. I thought I could do both."

You: "We did save a lot. People have roofs; gardens will exist. But part of me keeps listing what we traded—names of plants, old jokes, the place where I learned to bend a blade. That loss stays."

Elias: "I know. I keep finding that blank space in your corner of the rooftop when I see you looking at it. I want to help fill it, but I can't pretend I didn't make a call that cost us something."

You: "We survived. And we'll keep surviving. I won't let the city forget where it came from."
