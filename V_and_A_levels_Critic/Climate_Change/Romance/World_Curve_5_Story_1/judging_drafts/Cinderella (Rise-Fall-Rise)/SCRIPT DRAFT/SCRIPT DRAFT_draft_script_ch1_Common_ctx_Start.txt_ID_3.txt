[Scene: Green Roof Network | Dawn]
[Visual: Wide shot of layered rooftop terraces; misty harbor horizon]
[Music: Sparse ambient piano, slow tempo]
[Sound: Distant gulls, soft lapping of tide, the hollow clank of a crane far away]

You wake with the smell of wet soil in your nose and the ocean’s distance threading the air. The dawn is thin and gray, but the terraces already hold a small, smudged warmth—soil still damp from last night's mist, leaves weighed low with beads of water. Your chestnut hair is knotted into its usual loose twist; a stray tendril brushes your cheek and catches the salt on your skin. Your fingers find the glass vial at your throat without looking: a tangled string of seed packets rattles faintly, a small, private clatter against the cold glass.

[Visual: Close-up on vial and seed packets]
You let the vial rest there as you breathe in the rooftop's quiet—moss and reclaimed wood and the metallic undertone of the city. The fairy lights hung along the beds are still cool; no one switched them off. Raised planters stitched from salvaged boards lean into each other like old friends. The tomato plants lean toward you, leaves dark and heavy with water. You press your thumb to the topmost leaf; it gives a little, and soil grits at your palm.

Kira is crouched below you on an overturned crate, her ink-blue raincoat a bright punctuation against the muted morning. She spreads a small stack of flyers across the crate—block letters, hand-cut edges—then straightens and looks up, eyes bright as the bin of markers at her side.

Kira: "Morning. You awake enough to read my terrible handwriting?"

You: (half-smile) "If your handwriting's terrible, your rally's charming. What time did you get here?"

Kira: (tucking a flyer into a loop of twine) "Before the sun. Mateo's already argued politics with a thermos. He says he can smell city hall tightening its jaw from here."

You hum, tasting salt and a slow apprehension you have been carrying like a folded map. Your hands move without thinking—turning the pages of your repurposed notebook, tucking a small pressed leaf into a margin you reserved for notes on the best companion plants. The notebook's paper is rough under your fingers; the leaf presses flat with a faint click.

[Visual: Pan to Mateo, leaning on a tangle of netting, hands stained]
Mateo's laugh reaches you—a low, creaky sound threaded with sea-weathered patience. His hands are grubby with last night's fish oil stain and the edges of a patchy net. He looks up at you and squints, the early light painting his face with a salt-scraped map of lines.

Mateo: "Ayla—" (he pauses, searching for your name as if it is an old address) "—you keep the sun in these pots better than the city keeps it in its plans."

You: (soft) "Sun's easier to coax than politicians, Mateo."

Mateo: (shrugging) "Maybe. But don't forget, sometimes the sea changes the rules. It don't ask permission."

You feel the memory lift up briefly—mud between your toes, a porch you used to sweep clean, the small stubborn garden you lost to a flood when you were ten. The memory sits like a small stone beneath your ribs: heavy but not crushing. You smooth your palm along the stem of a tomato, letting the tiny hairs at its base prick your skin like a quiet reminder.

Narration: You came back here after study and left again and came back—always to this edge where city and sea keep renegotiating. These roofs are your answer in practice: a stitched-together network of green that feeds people, shades buildings, slows down rain where it would otherwise outrun the drains. It is one of a hundred small experiments that, together, might mean the difference between neighborhoods that adapt and neighborhoods that get paved over.

Kira: (leaning in, low) "You saw Jonah's dump of renderings on the feed last night, right? Model plaza, promenade on top of a wall the size of a building. Slick enough to make people forget the truth."

You: (glancing toward the horizon) "I did. They look impressive from far away—the lights, the plaza. From out here, they look wrong. Polished instead of patched."

[Visual: Shot of harbor horizon—cranes, a half-built seawall skeleton, a tablet screen reflecting renderings]
You pick up your tablet, the screen reflecting the long skeletal rib of a half-built seawall that sits like an interrupted sentence across the harbor. Jonah's rendered plaza gleams there—warm LEDs, clean pathways—presented as a promise. The image feels flat against the textured, living green at your feet.

Mateo: (tapping his thermos) "Promises don't grow food. They stack concrete and call it safety. I've seen promises before. They don't mend the nets."

You: (quietly) "They also keep money moving. That's why Mayor Serena called the town-hall. The vote's today."

Kira's fingers still the stacks of flyers. For a breath, the rooftop hushes—the fairy lights, the soft scrape of a gardening spade, the distant low throb of construction equipment; everything seems to listen.

You let the small ritual of tending make a pool of calm in your chest. There is comfort in the measured work: loosening a compacted root, checking for pests, pinching off a spent blossom. You think about the childhood plot you lost—how the soil once remembered the footfalls of your family, and what it meant to have that continuity end overnight when the water found a new path.

Kira: (softly, conspiratorial) "If we can get a few more rooftop blocks certified as food-security zones, the council can't just... sweep them away without argument. We have a chance to present a real alternative."

You: (measured) "We do. And we need a technical brief—crop yields, water retention stats, case studies. It has to read like policy to get past Serena's assistants. It can't just be pretty words."

Kira: (grins, then gets serious) "And we need people. Eyes and voices. You will—" (she looks at you, searching) "—you'll talk. Your way of talking makes things seem... doable."

You: (a beat) "I will. But I won't promise what I can't deliver. We shape things that can be maintained by neighbors, not by a grant that evaporates after a fiscal quarter."

Mateo: (folding his fingers together) "Listen to her. She says what keeps the lights on and the soup in the pot. You always were practical, Ayla. That's why folks listen."

Your chest tightens around a friendly ache—gratitude tangled with responsibility. You fold a flyer over the edge of your knee and press it flat, the fibers catching on the callouses of your thumb. The rooftop's small community moves in its slow orbit: someone waters a tray of seedlings, another string-lights a trellis, the city beyond rearranges itself with the indifferent rhythm of cranes and tide.

Narration: The day ahead is a thin hinge. A town-hall vote doesn't feel like a thunderclap—especially not here where storms have taught you to measure the strength of things in inches—but it matters. It matters for seed packets and for the porches and for whether Mateo can keep his nets where they've always been. It matters for the shape of a neighborhood and the texture of daily life that cannot be quantified easily in Jonah's polished charts.

Kira folds another flyer with a practiced hand and looks up with an animation that isn't quite fear but isn't far from it either.

Kira: "If you want to make them see—if you want the mayor to hear—do you want me to put you on the first speaking slot? Warm them up with the heart angle."

You: (considering) "The heart angle helps, but I want facts. Let me open with the numbers, then Kira follows with the people. I can keep it steady."

Kira: (nods slowly) "Steady. You'll be the tangent that keeps them listening."

Mateo: (chuckles) "And when they look slow-thinking, you can throw in a tomato anecdote. Works every time."

You laugh, a soft little sound that feels like a release. The laughter sits easy, but under it there is the steady weight of what you woke for: to tend, to argue, to keep what little green you can carve into the city's plans.

[Visual: The skyline again; sunlight spooling through cloud for a moment on the seawall renderings]
You find yourself staring, quietly, at the glint on your tablet one more time. Jonah's renderings look like a different world—clean, ordered, expensive. They are a promise made with tools you understand in theory but distrust in practice. They are also, undeniably, persuasive.

Narration: You taste salt and a thin, careful apprehension. The day opens like a seed—small, certain, and fragile. Mayor Serena's announcement feels close in a way that makes the hair at the back of your neck lift: not panic, not yet, only the precise alertness of someone waiting for a door to open.

You tuck the vial back against your collar and stand, boots scuffing the gravel path between beds. Kira hands you a flyer, her fingers warm and smudged with ink.

Kira: "Ready?"

You: (soft, resolved) "Ready enough."

[Visual: Close on your face, the harbor behind you; the cranes keep their slow rhythm]
Narration: The rooftop hums with small movements, and you move through them with the practiced calm of someone who builds life in narrow places. The town-hall stands ahead like a clause in the morning—an event that will force plans from ideas and drawings into votes. Whether your quiet work will hold against polished promises hangs like a thin line between this rooftop and that half-built seawall.

You step down from the crates, the cool metal of the fire escape under your hand. The city smells of seawater and diesel and rain that hasn't fallen yet. For a moment you let the salt in the air fill your lungs and steady you for the day.

[Music: Ambient piano holds, a single sustained note]
Narration: You check the clip of your tablet—Mayor Serena's public notice pinned to the top of the city feed. The words are neutral, procedural: "Town-hall: public consideration and vote on coastal protection measures." Nothing tells the story. Everything else will.

[Page-Turn Moment]
You pause on the edge of the roof, looking at the harbor, at the half-built wall, at the green you helped coax into being. Your fingers close once around the seed vial at your throat, and for a sliver of time the past and future feel layered—your childhood garden, the gardens you hope to save, and an auditorium where a roomful of people will choose what the city becomes. The decision is not yours alone, yet it will shape how you spend the next parts of your life. You breathe in, tasting salt and a careful, very small hope.
