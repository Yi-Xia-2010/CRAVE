You chose the street. The choice settles into your bones like packed earth—solid, stubborn, alive. The boardwalk smells of brine and hot tar and the sweet green rot of uprooted weeds. Your jacket is damp where the salt spray found seams; the tiny glass vial at your throat clicks against your sternum like a heartbeat. Around you, the city’s people make a line of resistance that looks at once vulnerable and impossible: folding chairs propped against sandbags, children perched on crates, a fisherman’s cap cast onto the makeshift stage.

Kira is already centerstage, megaphone rough against her palm. Her raincoat gleams with badges in defiant constellations; each one is a memory, a promise. She speaks and the sound ricochets across the water and through the crowd—an urgent, ragged percussion.

Kira Tseng: "Neighbors! They say the seawall will save us. We say: not at the price of our gardens, our stalls, our stories!"

The crowd answers with a shout that vibrates under your boots. A woman near the fish stalls lifts an ancient wooden crate like a banner; a boy drums on its side, calling rhythm to the chant. You move between people—checking sandbags, tying a banner to a lamppost, smoothing out a map that lists green-roof locations and market plots slated as 'relocation possibilities.' Each name is a finger pressed to the city's pulse.

Mateo Alvarez steps forward. His hands are the same hands that once hauled nets and once rebuilt a collapsed pier; now they tremble only where memory jars him. The microphone trembles in his grip.

Mateo Alvarez: (voice thick) "You think a wall’s a promise? The tide took my brother's slip in one night. But it also took our bench, our stories—there's no levee for that. We can build—better—if they don't bulldoze us first."

His words land like stones. Conversations bloom and fork: a vendor tells a short story about the tomato seedlings that fed an entire block through a summer blackout; another elderly woman lists plants that used to grow where Jonah's renderings show polished plazas. Each testimony is small and precise, a ledger of what the seawall could erase. Your chest lifts with each voice—this chorus is what you came here to make: human measurement against a spreadsheet.

Then Elias arrives—mud on his boots, a smear of clay on the cuff of his company jacket where someone else might have scrubbed it away. He crosses the plank slowly, like someone carrying more than one set of charts. When he reaches the barricade he doesn't make a show of it; he comes close enough to be seen but not so close as to step onto the stage. He leans on a crate beside you, breath fogging in the cooling air.

Elias Rook: (low, earnest) "Ayla—I'm glad you called this. I ran the models again last night. With the full wall—storm surge scenarios drop dramatically. Lives are saved."

You can see the truth of his maps in his hands; they make sense in a way that tugs at your practical side. You: "Lives matter, Elias. So do the living parts of our lives—gardens, stalls, the ways we find food and company. There's a cost your models don't price."

Elias Rook: (quiet) "I know. The models don't tell story. They tell probabilities. But probabilities can save people, Ayla."

You press your thumb against the seed vial at your neck, a small cool weight. The crowd around you does not quiet; they swell forward with testimonies, hands raised like open palms.

You: "Then tell them the probabilities that preserve what we are. Tell them how a hybrid plan adapts with us, not over us."

Elias hesitates. You can see him calculating—his mouth opens, closes—then he reaches into his jacket and pulls out a small sheaf of printed charts. One edge is smeared with mud where he's kept them tucked against his chest.

Elias Rook: "I—I've been trying. But the lab reports are never enough without people pushing. If you want me here, really here, I will stand here and say what I believe can be done differently."

He looks like a person stepping into a new role; you hear the rally shift its tone—curiosity layered over suspicion. Kira gives a sharp, pleased whistle and pushes her megaphone back toward Elias.

Kira Tseng: "Talk then. Tell them what we can fix—what we can keep."

Elias takes the megaphone with clumsy hands, as if he'd never held one for a chant before. He raises it, breath steadying, and speaks into the wave of sound.

Elias Rook: "There are ways to integrate living systems—retention basins, adaptive floodplains, elevated market lanes—that reduce peak surge demands. We can design a phased plan that protects life and space."

A ripple of relief and suspicion. You trade a look with Elias—his conviction is earnest, not performative. He prattles through technical terms, and you translate them in your head into soil and beds and water channels.

You: (soft, urgent) "Say we'll bind those promises to legal protection for community plots. Say we require co-management and funding for green infrastructure."

Elias Rook: (after a breath) "I can—I'll say that. But I need help making it stick. We can't do this only on models."

Your exchange stretches; the mayoral van slows at the end of the promenade. Cameras tilt like hawks. The galvanizing hush before a storm hits; you feel it in the slight catch of the crowd’s breath. The air tastes of electricity and resolve.

Jonah Hale: (smooth) "I understand the fears of change. We all want safety. But obstruction now risks lives. We need decisive action—swift, comprehensive infrastructure. The city cannot wait for a thousand-step plan while the next storm is stamped on our doorstep."

His words are well-tailored rhetoric—sharp angles smoothed. He gestures to a rendering on a portable screen: a glossy plaza above a clean, monolithic seawall. The image feels like a different city, one that shines because it has been stripped of the rough edges that make a neighborhood live.

You: "You call it 'decisive.' People call it displacement."

Jonah's smile doesn't falter; he steps closer, lowering his tone so the cameras can't take his subtext.

Jonah Hale: (confidential) "Ayla—this isn't about taking from anyone. It's about saving the most possible. Compromise is sometimes surrender in the name of safety."

You can feel a thousand small arguments forming behind his sentence; he's trying to frame the blockade as a risk to population statistics. The crowd reacts—some faces harden, others falter under the weight of his framing. Mayor Serena stands at the edge of the scene, hands clasped in front of her civic sash, eyes like a judge balancing a ledger. Her expression is unreadable—concern braided with calculation.

Mayor Serena Okoye: (measured) "This is a city that must protect its people and its character. I cannot let public safety be put at risk deliberately. But neither can I ignore what I hear here."

Her voice is both balm and blade. The crowd surges at the reminder of the imminent vote; shouts bloom into chants again, faster, higher, the rhythm building like surf climbing a break.

Mateo steps up to the mic once more, voice close to cracking but held by the force of what he means.

Mateo Alvarez: "If you build a wall around a few high-value blocks and call it safety, you'll drown the rest in the name of profit. You must include the people in the plan. We will not be moved to make room for someone else's dream."

The crowd answers with a thunder of feet and clapping that feels like a heartbeat the city can hear. You taste copper on your tongue with the adrenaline—your limbs are lit with a fierce, clear energy. The boardwalk is no longer an argument; it is a living lobby, a cathedral of human testimony where every cracked plank holds a story.
