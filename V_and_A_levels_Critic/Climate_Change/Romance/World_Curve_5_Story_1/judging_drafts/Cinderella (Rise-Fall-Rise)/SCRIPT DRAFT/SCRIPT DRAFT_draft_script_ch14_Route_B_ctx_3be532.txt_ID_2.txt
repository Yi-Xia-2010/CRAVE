Jonah Hale: (leaning forward) "No one here wants displacement. We can write covenants—legal safeguards. But the footprint needs to be predictable."

Elias Rook: (thumbs through a tablet, voice quiet but authoritative) "Predictable in model terms means: defined zones for hard infrastructure, defined limits for commercial conversion, and a clear review timeline tied to measurable thresholds. Those thresholds must be verifiable and actionable."

Mayor Serena Okoye: (intervening, weary but steady) "What I hear from both sides is the desire to protect people and the city. The problem is enforceability. Ayla, Elias, Jonah—let's focus clause-by-clause."

Your pulse is high. The hum of fluorescents becomes a drumline. Each clause is a battleground; each phrase, an artery. You mark a margin in your field notebook with a pen that smudges under your thumb.

Jonah's counsel: (folding long fingers) "We propose a chapter of temporary easements, with compensation for displaced commerce. The moratorium, 18 months to finalize construction plans—"

Jonah Hale: (interrupting, not unkindly) "And include professional oversight. We can't have every decision stalled for lack of expertise."

Elias Rook: "That's where balanced composition matters." (He looks at you, earnest.) "Technical counsel shouldn't override lived knowledge. It should translate the models into clauses: trigger events, metrics, timelines."
