Jonah Hale: "Mayor, we need decisive action. An emergency vote could authorize the work immediately—fewer lives at risk."

Mayor Serena Okoye: "I will not— I will not have people arrested if there is another way."

Jonah smiles too easily at the thought, as if the compromise itself were already folded into his ledger. You smell his cologne, a faint citrus masking the night's wetness.

You feel every breath become someone else's responsibility—your neighbors', Kira's fury, Mateo's memory, Elias's calculations. Your pulse explodes behind your ribs; it is sharp, irregular, and very loud in your ears.

The crowd mutates as quickly as weather. Some faces go hot with the possibility of tactical victory; others go cold with the fear of betrayal. Mayor Serena: "This moratorium would halt new work here for thirty days while a mediated committee—representatives from community groups, engineers, and neutral legal observers—examines binding protections. We can write seats into the agreement now."

His sentence breaks into the roar of protest. You look at him and you feel the world compress into two stark maps: one of street-level resistance that risks immediate harm and one of institutional negotiation that risks slow erasure.
