Across his other shoulder, a stack of glossy renderings lies like a promise.

Elias stands near the window, sleeves rolled, tablet glowing in his hands. He is an island of calm—an engineering rhythm you have learned to read for steadiness. Mayor Serena sits to one side, civic sash folded, face lined with weight. Kira’s voice is a clipped thread coming through speakerphone from outside the room; you can see her in the little video square, rain on her raincoat, binoculars at her belt. Mateo is there too, a small, weathered presence at the far end of the table—callused hands folded, eyes full of harbor memory.

Jonah pushes a single folder toward you. The edge of the paper presses against the varnished wood.

Jonah Hale: "A flagship seawall. Phased construction, prioritize the commercial corridor to protect critical infrastructure. In exchange—legal guarantees for selected community lot corridors, funding earmarked for your greenhouse restorations, and a binding schedule with public audits."

You take the folder. The renderings inside are slick promises: a clean line between water and city, plazas above. The offer tastes like metal where it touches your mouth—practical, cold, and oddly bright with possibility.

You want to say no on principle. You also want the roofs and the greenhouses to survive the next storm.

Your pulse quickens. This is the moment you chose by stepping through Jonah’s door. The arousal in your chest is a living thing—sharp, hopeful, impatient. You unfold one of the sheets and let your eyes trace the inked timeline. The numbers are precise. The caveats are buried in legalese.

Alya (you): "Legal guarantees and funding are a start. I need enforceable clauses—clear triggers, penalties, and a community oversight board with veto power over relocations."

Jonah smiles—careful, practiced charm that opens doors and closes objections.

Jonah Hale: "Veto power is a hard sell. Investors need certainty. What we can offer—binding covenants that specify protected corridors, an independent audit committee, and accelerated restoration funding. We can codify land trusts for compensatory plots."

Elias leans forward, fingers drumming the tablet bezel.

Elias Rook: "We can build monitoring into the contract. Sensors, public dashboards—metrics that automatically release contingency funds based on agreed thresholds. That keeps investors at ease while giving the community transparency."

Kira’s small video square flares. Her voice is hot and immediate.

Kira Tseng (on speaker): "Sellout. Ayla, don’t let him palm off your roofs for his plazas."

Your jaw tightens. Kira’s accusation lands like hail—familiar, furious, righteous. You hear the overtone of the streets in her words: chants, banners, the smell of wet cardboard and resolve.

Jonah watches you with a patience that is almost affectionate.

Jonah Hale: "We can do covenants. We can structure land trusts and fund greenhouse restoration in year one. We can cut the first phase to show investors their money is moving. But—" (he taps the table to emphasize the but) "—we need a timeline and assurances that the parcels slated for commercial protection aren't constantly challenged. Investors need certainty—time-sensitive milestones."

Mayor Serena’s voice, low, careful, enters the fray.

Mayor Serena Okoye: "Ayla, Jonah—both of you know the city can’t afford a stalled project that leaves key infrastructure exposed. If this is to pass council review, it must be defensible in court and in the press. Binding language is necessary, but so is political cover."

Mateo speaks last, gravel-soft, and it lands in the center of the circle.

Mateo Alvarez: "When the tide took my brother’s net, we thought it was a storm and then another. You can paper over promises with kindness, but the city remembers who showed up to fish in the morning and who only came for the light. Legally binding is good. Make it hard to break."

The room tightens; your chest hums like a struck wire. The High arousal you carry is not panic—it's a forward-driven energy, a kind of committed excitement that this can, finally, be shaped.

You press your thumb against the seed vial until the glass fogs. You are not naïve. Compromise always stings. But an enforceable covenant that secures corridors, funded restorations in the first year, and community oversight is beyond what you had dared to expect when you first walked off the rooftop and into town halls.

Alya (you): "We need three things: a legally binding oversight board with structural independence, immediate restoration escrow, and a public audit mechanism tied to automatically releasable contingency funds."

Jonah leans back, his fingers steepled.

Jonah Hale: "Independent oversight—I can work with that. Structure it with a mix of municipal, community, and an independent third-party arbiter. Escrow—yes, first-year funding prioritized and audited. Audit mechanism—metrics and triggers are technically feasible. But—" (he looks pointedly at Elias) "—timelines. If you add too many slow points, the investors pull out."

Elias meets Jonah’s gaze; their exchange is a meeting of two different vocabularies—models and markets—but there is a common tongue: risk mitigation.

Elias Rook: "We can phase deliverables. Phase one secures the most at-risk commercial nodes; phase two expands protections to the corridors identified in the covenants with a verified, monitored schedule. If we tie investor returns to progress milestones, they stay invested."

Your internal monologue becomes a fast, focused tally: oversight board equals power; escrow equals immediate action; auditable metrics equal trust. Each clause is a living thing that can save a plot of soil or let it be paved. The negotiation accelerates—words stack, counters, clarifications, legal phrasing pulled into plain speech like thread through a needle.

Kira’s voice cracks across the line again, softer now but urgent.

Kira Tseng (on speaker): "Listen, if you give them a path to walk, make sure it's a path the city can follow without being led off a cliff. No hidden waivers, no discretionary carve-outs."

Alya (you): "No waivers. No carve-outs. Any exceptions need supermajority approval from the oversight board and a time-limited sunset clause."

Jonah’s brow momentarily tightens; his fingers work at a napkin as if the motion grounds him.

Jonah Hale: "Supermajority is steep. But—" (he exhales, then leans toward the napkin) "—binding covenants can include supermajority language for relocations, with emergency powers narrowly defined and reviewable."

He begins to sketch—quick, decisive strokes. Tradeoffs, compensatory land trusts, language for covenants, and a table of milestones. The napkin fills with the architecture of a compromise: lines, checkboxes, a one-line heading: COMMUNITY CORRIDORS PROTECTION.

Jonah Hale: "Those are firm asks. I want to be clear: I can make most of them happen. Investors need to see phasing and a path to return. Elias Rook: "If we lock in a first-year escrow, audited and publicly accessible, and bind investor payouts to verifiable milestones, we can square investor timidity with community protection."

Kira watches through the speaker with a mix of suspicion and hunger for concrete proof.

Mateo Alvarez: "Make the paper hard to tear."

Your hand moves toward the napkin. Your pen hovers above ink that could be translated into binding lines. For a beat the world narrows to the warmth of the table, the smell of lemon, the cool glass of the vial at your throat, and the small tremor in your fingers.

Mayor Serena Okoye: "Council will want to see proof, Ayla. But this—" (she gestures at the napkin) "—this is the most balanced starting point I have seen."

Alya (you): (internal) "If this holds, we keep soil, seeds, and the people who care for them. If it fails, we have avoided nothing. This is the knife-edge."
