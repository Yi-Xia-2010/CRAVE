## Visual Novel Title
Tidebreak Hearts

---

## World View
Brinehaven is a near-future coastal town stitched between saltmarsh and sea, a place where the smell of kelp and diesel mingle and the boardwalk creaks underfoot like an old memory. Politically, the town is a microcosm of climate-era governance: municipal authorities (led by Mayor Lynette Cole) must balance emergency funding, media optics, and long-term permits; a corporate-backed engineering firm offers robust defenses with tight deadlines and economic strings; community networks — fishing crews, repair gardens, elders like Old Man Rafi — preserve local practice and mutual aid. Culture is maritime and practical: rituals of boat blessing and harvest mingle with late-night town-hall debates. Class lines blur in a place where livelihoods are shared between small proprietors, fishermen, civil servants, and scientists who returned home. Religion is diffuse — a practical reverence for the sea and seasonal cycles rather than doctrine. Geography compresses choices: the town sits at a finger of land hemmed by fen and open water; every design decision has visible consequence on beaches, marshes, and rooftops. Atmosphere is tactile — salt on skin, painterly skies that switch from honeyed gold to bruised storm blue, and a persistent sense that every intimate decision will be measured against the tide.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Mira Kestrel: a local climate scientist pulled back to Brinehaven to help steer adaptation planning. The story follows an Oedipus curve (Fall → Rise → Fall). Act I (Fall) opens with rising stakes: storms, funding fights, and Mira’s survivor guilt pulling her into public conflict. At a municipality hearing you must pick an approach: ally with Jonah to pursue community-led marsh and livelihood-focused resilience; work with Cass to secure engineered defenses and funding; or take a third route of exposing opaque funding channels to force transparency. Each choice sends you down a different path of collaboration, intimacy, and trade-offs: community mobilization brings warm bonds and tender nights on the boardwalk but risks scalability; corporate engineering promises infrastructure and broader safety but cools trust and reshapes town character; whistleblowing forces governance reforms at great interpersonal cost. Mid-story (Rise) brings temporary victories — restored marshes, successful pilot defenses, or public accountability — and the closeness of new or renewed relationships with Jonah and/or Cass. The final act (Fall) delivers the inevitable catastrophe: a storm or political backlash that reveals the limits of any single choice. Every route ends with moral and emotional collapse — loss, fracture, or ambiguous moral victory — forcing Mira to reconcile her guilt, love, and the ethics of choosing when everything is on the line.
