## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Hearts

---

## World View
Brinehaven is a near-future coastal town where salt, storms, and memory shape every conversation. Once a small fishing village framed by wooden piers and reed-lined marshes, it now balances on engineered proposals, contested grants, and the stubborn muscle of a community that still repairs its own roofs. Power is shared uneasily among Mayor Lynette Cole (politics and optics), community elders like Old Man Rafi (oral memory and local practices), grassroots organizers led by Jonah Reyes (livelihood, boats, rope), and visiting professionals such as Cassian "Cass" Romano (infrastructure, contracts). Climate funding, corporate contractors, and municipal deadlines press inward from the city; the Saltmarsh Fen, boardwalk, and Research Lighthouse are the town’s emotional geography. Religion and class are subtle — rituals around the sea, reverence for elders and lineage, and an economic hierarchy between those who own boats and those who repair nets. The Research Lighthouse hums with instruments; the Municipal Hall projects waterlines; community gardens and boardwalks are where human-scale trust is built. The atmosphere is painterly coastal realism: golden-hour warmth that can flip to bruised, storm-blue clouds in an hour, with everyone aware that each decision — a sandbag, a grant acceptance, a public forum — will show up in weathered wood and the next tide.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mira Kestrel) return to Brinehaven, a scientist haunted by past loss and determined to center community-led resilience. A public battle between grassroots marsh restoration (Jonah’s insistence) and large-scale engineered defenses (Cass’s models) becomes both a policy fight and a love triangle. I must negotiate data, emotion, and moral consequence: siding with Jonah preserves tradition and daily livelihoods but risks failing under a major storm; siding with Cass buys time with engineered barriers but threatens social cohesion and long-term cultural loss; and exposing political corruption risks immediate backlash while promising structural honesty. The arc takes me through a descent into crisis as tides and funding collapse the town’s naive assumptions (Fall), a brief co-creation and hope as hybrid strategies emerge and intimacy shifts (Rise), and finally a catastrophic storm or political breakdown that undoes some gains, forcing a painful moral reckoning (Fall again). Across divergent choices, endings vary: hybrid triumphs, compromises with cost, catastrophic loss, or moral victory with ambiguous private closure. Every route keeps the same faces, same weather-streaked visuals, and my first-person internal balancing of guilt, duty, and desire.
