[Scene: Municipal Hall | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: The municipal conference room — glass walls, chrome table, rows of folding chairs; the harbor looms in the windows as a distant, restless line of silver.]
[Sound: Low murmur of the crowd, the occasional clack of a heel, soft rustle of paper.]
[Music: Sparse piano undernote, deliberate and thin.]

You step up to the lectern like someone carrying a small, private obligation. Your field notebook is clipped under your arm; the waterproof wristband hums faintly against your pulse. The room smells of reheated coffee and antiseptic wipes, and the glass walls throw the town back at you — faces stacked into blocks of expectation, each reflecting the other until individual features blur into a single patient silhouette.

Mayor Lynette Cole stands to your left, all sharp angles and practiced warmth. She has a packet of glossy renderings in her hand that catch the light. Cassian Romano is beside her, tablet open, diagrams folded into a rhythm he speaks in. Jonah sits farther back with the fishermen, salt crusting the cuffs of his patched trousers; his hands are around the carved wooden compass in his pocket like a talisman. Tessa perches on the edge of a chair, jaw set, eyes too young for the exhaustion on them. Old Man Rafi watches the room like someone watching tides — not much moves him that he doesn't read.

[Visual: Projected waterlines shimmer across the chrome table, animated layers of blue and gray moving like a slow, obedient tide.]
[Sound: A quiet click as you toggle a layer; a breath held in the room.]

You place your notebook on the lectern and push a slide forward. Modeled scenarios fold into the air — projected lines that trace future storms, possible marsh gains, and engineered profiles. Your voice finds a low place first, measured.

You: "These are ensemble projections — multiple runs, different assumptions about storm frequency and sea level. None of them are certainty. They are possibilities we must weigh against lived realities: where people fish, where homes sit, where children learn to read the water."

Mayor Lynette: "Mira has prepared a thorough report. The town owes her — and itself — an honest look at the trade-offs."

Cassian: (stepping forward, palms open) "If I may — the seawall proposal I'm presenting is scalable. We can phase construction, prioritize the harbor and the school, and adapt as we learn. It's a replicable model with predictable maintenance costs."

Jonah: (from the back, voice flat) "Predictable for who, Cass? Predictable for people who can afford to lose their slips? For the ones whose nets will snag on concrete where there used to be shoals?"

Cassian: (measured, a touch of impatience) "With community oversight. With seats at the table. We don't want to erase livelihoods; we want to protect them."

Your chest tightens the way it does before you speak for people who cannot keep their own words safe. You remember the house you couldn't save — beams like broken ribs in your memory — and the small, private vow that still tastes of salt.

Old Man Rafi clears his throat; the room hushes in the habitual way towns do for the ones who have been carrying stories longer than anyone else.

Old Man Rafi: "When I was a boy, the sea taught us a lesson. We learned to watch the birds, to read the black lines on the water. Once, the tides turned overnight and took more than they were meant to. But we rebuilt without forgetting. That memory is not a rejection of change — it's a warning against gifts that forget where we learned to stand."

Tessa: (interrupting, urgent) "And while we talk about warnings, how many more storms do we have to survive before someone signs something that actually helps? People are scared, Rafi. I'm scared. We need action now."

A low ripple of agreement moves through the crowd, threaded with a few sharp exhalations. The air is taut, but not with shouting — a slow, anxious tension that makes the light look fragile.

You watch the reactions like a scientist watching a controlled test. Numbers pull some people toward calculation; story pulls others toward tenderness or frustration. Neither is a solution on its own, and you know that in the way your wristband knows the contours of a tide.

Mayor Lynette: (leaning forward) "We must be tactical. The town needs something demonstrable before winter funding dries up. I am responsible for the municipal payroll, for the grant applications. The seawall gets results people can see."

Jonah: (barks a short laugh, then quiets) "Results that change how we fish. Results that put a concrete lip between the harbor and the marsh. Does that count as survival if we can't work the water the way we always have?"

Cassian: (softening) "No one wants to erase the fishing culture. The designs can include living edges, ferry points, fish passes. We can—and should—integrate marsh restoration where structurally possible."

You: (quietly) "Integration requires time, site work, and—" (you flip to a map layer that overlaps proposed seawall segments with eelgrass beds) "—this is where 'possible' becomes 'expensive' or 'complicated.' But 'complicated' doesn't mean 'impossible.' It means we negotiate."

Tessa: "Complicate faster. People can't wait for negotiated complication. We need promises people can believe in. Talk is good; commitment is better."

Old Man Rafi: "Promises are thin. I've seen many. But commitment is something you can see in people's hands: how they turn a net, how they repair a boat. Commitment is built in places we live in, not in renderings."

The room answers with a chorus: someone boos — a sharp, single sound like a gull's cry; someone claps — tentative, searching for solidarity. The volume of opinion swells and then sinks, more like tides moving than a sudden storm.

Every option feels like moving the shoreline — gradual, inevitable, and not entirely under your control. Your hands curl into the lectern's edge; the wood is cool and grainy under your palms. You can taste iron at the back of your throat — not quite fear, but the metallic tang of stakes.

Cassian steps forward, voice leveled low as he addresses the room, and you notice a flicker in him that is not all corporate polish. He is trying, in his way, to make room for what he knows he would otherwise sweep away.

Cassian: "I don't want to be the bulldozer. I want the firm to learn from you. I am asking to be judged by how we incorporate local priorities. If we fail, I'll resign my contract before any concrete goes down."

Jonah: (after a pause that holds more than resentment) "Words that would mean something if we could trust the players, Cass. How do we build trust when the first offers come with profit margins and press kits?"

Cassian: (a beat) "By showing the process. By letting Old Man Rafi and Tessa and the fishermen come into the planning briefings. By publishing budgets line-by-line."

Mayor Lynette: (smiles, but it's a smile that calculates) "Transparency is good politics. It keeps voters happy and funders honest. But transparency takes time, and the grant window—"

You feel the room tilt toward schedules and windows and the machinery of governance. You also feel, under that, the human calculus: what must be sacrificed to secure what can be saved.

You: (slowly) "We need a plan that recognizes the town as a socio-environmental system, not a checklist of priorities. Marsh restoration offers ecosystem services that reduce wave energy and support fisheries. Living shorelines create jobs and co-benefit habitats. Structural defenses protect immediate infrastructure but risk altering access and livelihoods. We must decide what mix of these outcomes we are willing to accept."

Tessa: (voice tight) "And if we wait to decide, what if the next storm takes a school or a harbor? How many 'what-ifs' make us complicit?"

Old Man Rafi: (soft) "Complicity is a heavy word. But so is cowardice. Deciding neither is choosing a path, too."

The mayor clears her throat, the sound a gavel without wood.

Mayor Lynette: "I will call for a consensus. I want us to leave this room with a direction that allows me to pursue funding and—" (she looks at you, briefly, weight passing across her face) "—and that gives people something to believe in."

The hall turns toward you as if you are the compass everyone forgot they were holding.

You inhale. The air fills your lungs with the taste of coffee, salt, and the faint metallic tang of worry. The waterlines hover on the table like quiet accusations. Your notebook is a tide under your ribs, carrying a hundred small, fragile facts.

You can feel the weight of every choice in your scuffed boots: livelihoods mapped against engineered promises; marshes that buffer waves against the allure of a single, clean fix. Your mouth forms words that could bind numbers to people's lives or let them remain abstract.

You must choose how to lead.

[Sound: The room exhales, a set of held breaths.]

You feel the moment stretch thin — a low, aching tension that does not snap but waits.

A thought moves through you, unformed and heavy: the town is asking for a direction, and every direction asks something of people you love.

[Music: The sparse piano undernote narrows to a single, sustained tone.]

You inhale again, and the decision rests in your throat like a stone.
