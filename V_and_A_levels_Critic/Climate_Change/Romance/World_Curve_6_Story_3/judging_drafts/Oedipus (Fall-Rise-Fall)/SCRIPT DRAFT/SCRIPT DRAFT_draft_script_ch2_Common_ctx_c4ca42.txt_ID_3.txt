You enter on the tail of what happened at the harbor: the notebook under your arm feels heavier than it should, as if the pages had absorbed the salt and the night's quiet verdict. The Municipal Hall smells of coffee and antiseptic; the air tastes faintly metallic where the projector's fan exhales. Your waterproof wristband presses warm against the pulse under your sleeve, an internal metronome that reminds you this is not a debate about ideas on a page — it's about people who will wake under the tides.

[Sound: Chair legs scrape; a cough somewhere near the back]

Mayor Lynette Cole stands at the front, her bob as immaculate as her talking points. Behind her, a chrome table hosts a holographic model — Cass's firm's rendered seawall arcs in neat, authoritative slices of light. The renderings shimmer with promises: straight lines, measured heights, a pronunciation of certainty.

Mayor Lynette Cole: "Thank you all for coming. We've invited expert testimony and community representatives so we can move toward a decision that protects Brinehaven and its future."

Her smile is professional, not unkind. It doesn't have to be. There are cameras on the side table and a live feed for the neighboring districts; optics matter. You feel the way the room's reflection fractures on the glass wall — faces duplicated in faint overlays.

Cassian "Cass" Romano steps forward, tablet in hand. He speaks with the steady cadence of someone used to funding cycles and deadline slides. He lays out the engineered seawall: modular, scalable, marketable.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (points to the hologram) "This system reduces overtopping by sixty percent in modeled storms of the next thirty years. It can be phased to match funding availability and designed to minimize visual impact. It's replicable — Brinehaven becomes a model for other towns."

Someone near the back murmurs approval. The model gleams. You watch Cass's hands — precise, confident — and the way his jaw tightens when he waits for the applause that follows his figures.

Jonah Reyes sits in the third row of folding chairs, the fishing crews clustered around him like a small, salt-weathered island. Salt beads at the cuffs of his patched trousers; his carved wooden compass is visible when his fists unconsciously curl around it. He doesn't clap.

Jonah Reyes: (low, to the person beside him) "It sounds tidy. For someone who's not losing nets."

He catches your eye for a beat; there is warmth there, but also a tautness you both carry. He is not a man of powerpoint assurances. Old Man Rafi plants himself at the podium next, voice small but precise, and tells a story — not of numbers, but of a storm that taught the town to read the tides differently.

Old Man Rafi: "We learned to listen. The bay has memory. We built with the bay, not against it. You don't slap a plaster on a living wound and call it healed."

Tessa, your sister, can't hold it. She stands before the room like a banner.

Tessa Kestrel: (sharp) "Stories don't stop the water. We need action now. Kids are asking if their houses will still be there next year. Waiting for perfect science is another way of watching things get swept away."

A ripple of agreement runs through the room; someone shouts, "Listen to her!" Another voice counters with a practical question about timelines. The tension is low but persistent — a pressure that sits in the chest and pushes out the back of the throat.

You step up to the lectern because the mayor gestures in your direction. The microphone is cool under your palm. Your field notebook is a tide-mark of sketches and tide logs; your notes are a map of what happens when sea and town meet. You could unfold every dataset you have, drown them in graphs and projections. Or you could lean into the stories that make those graphs mean something.

Your voice comes out measured, the way you've trained it to be when numbers must hold human cost.

Mira Kestrel: (you) "Engineered defenses have a place. So do restorations and living shorelines. The question isn't 'which' — it's 'how do we temper one with the other so the people who fish, who raise families here, don't lose their way of life in the process?'"

A hand rises. A fisherman with knuckles like driftwood asks about the impact on mooring and harvest seasons. His worry is not abstract; it's the ledger of paychecks and pantry shelves.

You answer with specific examples: marsh restorations that buffer surge while creating nursery habitat, distributed defenses that allow boats access, phased engineering that includes community labor and oversight. You do not overpromise. You're careful about the word "guarantee." The room listens—some faces soften, others close like shells.

Cass interjects, not to contradict but to refine.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (earnest) "Adaptive designs can incorporate living features. If we set community management clauses into contracts, include local labor provisions, and stage construction to avoid the fishing season, we can marry reliability with stewardship."

Jonah shifts in his chair; the sound is a soft leather creak. He speaks up, voice carrying because he wants to be heard without being used.

Jonah Reyes: "But who's signing those clauses? What stops the next election cycle or some corporate pivot from changing the tune? We've seen promises and then the ribbon-cutting and then the forgetting."

Cass meets him directly with a look that is complex — not all hostility, not all alliance; a professional trying to make human things fit into institutional boxes.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (softly) "Transparency. Community seats. Binding oversight. I don't want to be the kind of outsider you distrust."

Old Man Rafi lets out a chuckle that smells of salt and patience.

Old Man Rafi: "Words are cheap in bad storms. We'll need teeth in the words."

The room laughs under its breath, a sound that is half humor and half an exhale of accumulated nerves. The pace here does not spike; it compresses. Anxiety is a low, steady heat.

The push-and-pull is not dramatic; it is like a tide that hesitates on the shore — coming and retreating in gentle, consequential breaths.

Mayor Lynette: (decisive) "We need consensus to unlock the next tranche of funding. I propose a vote on a staged plan that includes both engineered elements and natural defenses. We'll appoint a committee — with seats for the community and technical advisors."

Your throat tightens. The word "committee" is a hinge both for possibility and for delay. You think of all the ways committees can become waiting rooms for action. You also think of the times they've been the only thing standing between a good plan and a bad politician's whim.

Tessa leans forward, voice pitched with urgency.

Tessa Kestrel: "A committee that meets quarterly doesn't stop a roof from leaking this winter."

The mayor's gaze flicks to you. The room quiets as if someone had drawn a curtain. Your heartbeat is a small thing against the actual machinery of the hall. The waterproof band against your wrist feels like a bright coin of responsibility.

You could answer now with a procedural suggestion — a timeline, a first-phase action with immediate relief funds. Or you could say something about trust, about binding agreements and community oversight, and risk the room thinking you are political. Or you could step back and nominate Jonah or Old Man Rafi to co-chair, an olive branch that might be seen as abdication.

A reporter murmurs into a recorder; a child's sob is muffled outside a door. The soundscape of the hall is layered and domestic, not cinematic. The mood is low in intensity but layered with long shadows. This is not the crescendo of a single storm — it's the slow press of seasons and decisions that will accrete into something irreversible.

You return to the lectern and speak, not to win applause but to name the ledger of losses.

Mira Kestrel: (you) "If we only secure the shoreline and lose the marshes, we buy a few years at the cost of every small fisher's livelihood. If we only restore the marshes without structural support for key docks, we imperil those same livelihoods this winter. We need both — phased, community-governed, and immediate where people are vulnerable."

Jonah stands when you mention "this winter," not to make a scene but because he cannot be still. He pins you with eyes that are an ocean of things he won't say aloud.

Jonah Reyes: "Mira, what will you do if the vote divides us? If people demand immediate walls and the funding only covers a start? Will you be the one who signs something that splits the town?"

The question is not rhetorical. It lands like a small stone in your palm, cold and decisive. You remember houses you couldn't save — the memory is a low hum under your ribs.

Mira Kestrel: (you) "I will not sign away people's futures for the sake of a clean headline. I will work to ensure funding is tied to accountability, and I'll push for immediate relief where it's needed. But I will not pretend those are the same thing."

Cass watches you, expression folding into something unreadable again. There is an earnestness there, but also the professional machinery of his firm ticking behind his steady voice.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (quiet) "Then let's build those mechanisms into the contracts now. Hold me and my firm to it. Put community oversight in the legal framework. I'll agree to the phased approach you want, if we draft it with teeth."

Mayor Lynette: (folding her hands) "If we can get legal language in place and a clear, phased schedule, I will bring this to the council. But I need a motion. I need a name that brings everyone together."

Silence settles — a low, expectant blanket. The town is looking at you.

Your throat feels like a room after rain. The arousal in the room has not spiked into frantic crescendos; it has narrowed into a focused, quiet pressure. Negative valence hums through every line — the implicit threat of loss, the memory of what failed in the past. Your internal monologue tightens into a single, necessary calculation: people need reassurance now, and structures need to be bound to accountability. There is no romanticized certainty here, only the sober arithmetic of risk and repair.

You inhale.

Everything waits on the exhale.

You must choose how to lead.
