[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk | Early Evening — After the Hearing]
[Music: Low, hopeful strings; the rhythm is steady, not urgent]
[Visual: Golden-hour light fighting through bruised storm clouds; wet planks gleam underfoot]

You step out of the municipal hall into the humid breath of the town. The crowd disperses like water through fingers: clusters of argument, the tight hugs of people who have survived a season's losses, a few faces set in exhausted resolve. Salt hangs in the air, sticky on your lips; the boardwalk smells of wet timber and coal-smoke from a street vendor's stall.

The coral pendant at your throat is cool against your skin. Your notebook—its spine soft with rain—tucks against your ribs. Both are small, steady weights. You press the pad of your thumb against the paper as if to anchor yourself to decisions already written and those still only half-sketched.

Mayor Lynette moves through the crowd with practiced openness, her polished coat collecting droplets. She leans close enough that you can feel her breath: official, precise.

Mayor Lynette: "Council will vote next week. We need a plan that shows immediate action — concrete, visible. The public wants certainty."

You hear the edge in her voice; certainty is currency and she intends to spend it.

Cass stands a few steps away, his coat pulled tight against the chill. He flicks his multifunction watch and a topographic overlay hums up between you like a fingertip-carved tide line. The projection ripples: gradients, predicted waterlines, neat blocks of color that promise safety in tidy increments. He looks at you with those steel-gray eyes—clear, practiced calm.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "We can start shoreline reinforcement in sixty days. I can move funding forward, underwrite the first phase. We don't have to wait on committees to stall progress."

You look at the map and then at the people milling behind him — their faces reflected in the glossy black of the projection. Cass's tone is measured, earnest; he talks about efficiency the way some people pray. There's comfort in that math. There's also a shape of control: deadlines, deliverables, sign-offs. It could be the hand that holds the town steady, or the hand that decides what part of the town counts as worth holding.

Jonah is already half-open-mouthed before you can make room. His coat smells of tar and brine; his fingers still bear salt-scrapes. His amber eyes find yours and the laugh lines at their edges make your chest ache with something tender and old. When he talks, the town is in his voice.

Jonah Reyes: (low) "No. Not if it means taking our crews out of the loop. Not if it means building walls that cut us off from the sea we depend on. Blueprints don't know how to mend a net, Mira."

Jonah's words carry the low percussion of the docks: tools against wood, the cadence of a community that rebuilds by hand. He steps closer, close enough for the warmth of him to touch you. Your instincts list the way they always do—toward steady work, toward people. You can feel the pull.

You swallow. The strings under the surface of the day tighten, an accessible, rising current. You owe the town more than talk; you've already felt what it means to fail at that owe. Guilt presses—not sharp so much as shaped—into a cautious resolve. But under it sits a small, unexpected lift: a ridge of light pushing through the clouds, thin but real.

Old Man Rafi's voice carries from the back of the crowd like a memory stepping forward. He props himself on a cane and leans into the conversation with the slow authority of the sea.

Old Man Rafi: "Will we honor what was here? Or swap our stories for neat plans that keep our land but not our language? That is the question."

His voice is weathered bronze, the kind of voice that has cataloged every rising and receding tide. Where others hear practicality, he hears loss — the intangible things plans too often leave behind.

Tessa cuts in, impatient and open. She meets your eyes and there is no pretense between you; she needs work, she needs guarantees, she needs you to promise that her future won't be a string of unpaid days.

Tessa Kestrel: "Mira, listen. People need jobs. I don't care if it's planting marsh grass or laying concrete — just tell us there's something to do."

Her hand finds yours for a second. It's a literal, human plea. You feel the weight of it like a current underfoot.

You fold the notebook closed and let the sound of people's choices wash over you. Midway through, your phone buzzes: Dr. Saira N'Goma calling. Her voice is immediate in your ear, a clean analysis wrapped in warmth.

[Visual: The Research Lighthouse's beacon winks in the distance, instruments humming through the dusk]

Dr. Saira N'Goma: (over the phone) "Mira, I heard. Mayor's leaning fast. Cass is offering money. I can mobilize community grants—there are foundations that would back a living-shoreline pilot. But windows close. If you want true leverage, we have to act before the council's vote."

You feel the steady competence in Saira's words; it's the quick, bright thing your training built. She lists options like a surgeon listing incisions: specific, surgical, clean. You feel some of the old solace — data, plans, a path that doesn't require sacrificing values.

Mira Kestrel: "How long does that take? And what would be public-facing? People need to see something tangible."

Dr. Saira N'Goma: "A month for grant proposals if we push. Two to three for pilot implementation. It gives us a public project and leverage at the council."

You close your eyes for a beat against the drizzle. Saira offers you time and legitimacy. Cass offers speed and capital. Jonah offers cultural continuity and labor that stitches the town back together. Each is a kind of rescue; each is a commitment in a different language.

Cass steps closer, softer now, as if your decision is intimate. He taps his watch; the projection zooms to overlay economic estimates. When he speaks, there's a willingness to meet you halfway and a pressure that asks you to trust the numbers.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "If we sign the partnership—I mean, if Brinehaven partners with us—we start the most vulnerable segments within two months. We can subcontract local crews; we can train them. We don't have to erase your knowledge, Mira."

Jonah frowns; you see the calculation of betrayal and negotiation flicker across his face. He lets out a breath that sounds almost like laughter, half-hopeful, half-skeptical.

Jonah Reyes: (firm) "Subcontracting isn't the same as leading, Cass. We don't want to be a side job in someone's portfolio. We want ownership."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (quick) "Ownership can be structured. Community boards, revenue shares, roles—I'll put them in the contract."

Your head swims with clauses. The legal language would hold a lot, if it is written as promised. You remember past meetings where promises were smooth and empty. You also remember what it felt like to watch the sea take the roof off your childhood home, thinking, if only there had been money to do this earlier.

You shake off the memory and rest your palm on the balustrade. The wood is salt-slick and honest. Your voice comes out steadier than you feel.

Mira Kestrel: "We need both accountability and speed. We need to protect the things that aren't measured by models—the names, the routes, the ways people use the water."

Cass leans back, an almost imperceptible tightening across his mouth. Jonah watches you like someone reading tide-marks.

Old Man Rafi: "And there is another way that keeps light on both sides—transparency. If the money's coming from under the table or from strings that snap, we ought to know."

You nod. The idea sits between you like a buoy. Exposing who funds what would drag the decisions into public view, slow them down, potentially stop them from being steam-rolled. It could give the town leverage. It could also open a raw, ugly political fight.

Tessa squeezes your hand again. Jonah's fingers curl inward, clenching the same worry your chest holds. The town hums: voices, clattering cups from a nearby café, the percussion of rain beginning to pick up. The emotional current rises — not with panic, but with a building insistence. Choices sharpen like the edge of an incoming tide.

You close your eyes and let the three faces before you gather: Jonah — dirt-under-the-fingernails loyalty and a promise to preserve way of life; Cass — ordered plans, capital, the possibility of quick protection; the third way — public exposure of funding and leverage through information — a path that speaks to accountability and slow, sometimes messy justice.

Dr. Saira's footsteps are audible in the hush of the call; she isn't just offering technical support, she's offering time. Time that could be used to draft grants, to make pilots, to show the town a visible, community-rooted project before any ribbon is cut.

Mira Kestrel: (soft) "We can do more than pick a side. We can bind any plan to the town we want to be. But I need everyone at the table now—the crews, the elders, the young hands, and the contracts with teeth."

Jonah's jaw loosens, not entirely convinced but listening. Cass's projection dims slightly as he considers what "teeth" would mean for deliverables and deadlines. Old Man Rafi watches you like someone waiting for the tide to tell him where to tie his boat.

For a moment, the rain eases as if reluctant to disturb the decision. The ridge of light you felt earlier grows; it paints the far-off lighthouse rim in a thin, golden line. The air tastes of salt and ink, of possibility borne on careful planning.

Your chest unclenches a fraction. The clarity that had been a distant ridge is now a path forward—three forks, equally consequential.

You know the choice will shape Brinehaven's grammar and your personal tether to it.

You breathe, steadying yourself. The crowd's hum becomes a chorus of expectation. This is not a cliff to leap from in a panic; it's a harbor with three mouths and one tide running through them.

You open your mouth to answer the town that has already begun to speak.
