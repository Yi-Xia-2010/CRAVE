[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk & Docks | Dawn — hours after the surge]
[Music: A percussion-driven, frayed string motif — relentless, pressing]
[Visual: Wide shot of the boardwalk; rain-slick planks strewn with broken pilings, overturned crates, a sign reading "LEGAL HOLD" flapping in a puddle]
[Sound: The sea grinding like a throat; distant sirens; people arguing; a tinny emergency broadcast sputtering]

You come up the boardwalk on boots that slip on salt and oil. The air tastes like iron and wet cedar; a thin smoke curls from somewhere inland where someone has lit a fire to keep from freezing. Where pumps should be thrumming, there is only stubborn silence and the rattle of abandoned scaffolding. Yellow tape flutters from a half-built seawall—an official paper notice pinned to a piling: LEGAL INJUNCTION — DO NOT PROCEED. The ink looks obscene against the spray and the ruin.

Your notebook, damp at the edges, presses against your ribs. The coral pendant at your throat swings like a small, frantic metronome. Every step makes the town rearrange itself in your head: the months of hearings, of freeze-frames in conference rooms, the hours you spent parsing clauses. You can still feel the cold, bureaucratic weight of those clauses — the stop signs lawyers put where hands needed to be.

You hear someone shout your name before you see him: Jonah, lungs burning, hands full of seawater–slick rope. He turns when you step into the light, and his face is a map of betrayal and grief.

Jonah Reyes: "Mira. You—" (he cuts off, the word that could be 'promised' or 'took' lodged like a fishbone) "What did you think would happen if we sat on a penalty clause until winter? What did you think?"

You open your mouth. The air is abrasive. You want to say: I tried to keep us honest; I tried to make them accountable. But the words come out small and wet.

You: "Jonah—(your voice rough)—I didn't want to freeze the work. I wanted safeguards. I wanted—"

Jonah slams a palm on a crate until it tips, crates of rope fanning across the boardwalk. He takes a step toward you; anger and sorrow make his amber eyes raw.

Jonah Reyes: "Safeguards? People are in the water. Tessa's neighbor lost the roof off his trailer. Samir from crew three—his whole shack's gone. Safeguards don't bail out a boat when the pumps die."

You: "The injunction—" (you gesture at the paper on the piling) "—it was meant to stop the firm from moving without community oversight. If we didn't stop them from cutting corners, we'd have lost this place to prettier concrete. That would have been on us, too."

Jonah's laugh is sudden, bone-dry.

Jonah Reyes: "And now? How does calling for transparency pay for a child's baptismal dress? For Old Man Rafi's shed? Tell me that, Mira."

Before you can answer, Cass's voice arrives—low, controlled, professional even with the wreckage behind him. He stands near a toppled holographic marker, tablet in hand as if the map might still be reanimated into defense.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "We were blocked. We had the crews staged, equipment on-site. Legal delays give the sea time. That's the hard reality here. We told you that project continuity mattered."

You look at Cass. He looks exhausted; there is a sheen of salt in his hair, and a smear of mortar on the sleeve of his trench. His jaw is tight. There is blame in his tone, but there is also a tremor—an admission that this isn't only about contracts.

You: "You said you'd write oversight clauses in. You said you'd guarantee local hires. You said—"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I wrote them. I drafted them this morning. But draft words don't bolt pumps into place overnight, Mira. They don't call back crews when injunctions bite."

Mayor Lynette bursts from a doorway to the planning office, her raincoat plastered to her shoulders, eyes flaring with a thousand small political fires.

Mayor Lynette Cole: "Everyone, please—this is not the time to finger-point in front of the cameras—"

A child's wail cuts her off. Somewhere a roof snaps like a branch.

Mayor Lynette Cole: "(forced, then softer) We need a coordinated tally. We're opening the municipal relief center at the old gym. I want lists. I want—"

Jonah: (bitter) "Lists? Lists don't fix Samir's home."

Mayor Lynette Cole: "And what's your plan, Jonah? To bulldoze the legal system because you rage? We had an injunction because of real concerns about the contracts being opaque."

Jonah Reyes: "Real concerns? They're called livelihoods, Lynette. They call it home."

The words hit you like a wave. The town is folding in on itself, not because the water chose sides, but because people are forced to take them.

[Scene: Lower Docks | Mid-morning — the pumps and partial seawall]
[Music: Tense, rising strings; percussion accelerates]
[Visual: Close-up of the breached seawall; exposed rebar like ribs; the unfinished concrete slumped into the sea]
[Sound: A hydraulic pump trying to catch, stuttering then failing; shouted coordination over two-way radios; a child's muffled crying through a wall]

You move down to the lower docks where the project was half-built—rows of imperfect concrete buttresses that should have been a line of defense now lie in fractured confusion. A pump array sits silent, its control box a vinyl-and-legal-caution sticker, wires dangling. Someone has taped a court document to the casing; the edges ripple in the wind.

Tessa meets you there, sleeves rolled, face smudged with ash. Her eyes are too big for her face. She walks up without knocking and the moment her shoulder hits yours, something inside you knees over.

Tessa Kestrel: "You signed off on that clause, Mira. I saw the stamp. You could have—" (her voice breaks) "You could have pushed for a temporary release. We could have had backup crews."

You: "I—(you swallow)—I thought a pause would give us leverage. I thought if we forced transparency, the firm would be cornered into the guarantees we need. We thought—"

Tessa: "You thought." (she spits the words) "People didn't need 'thought,' they needed pumps."

You: "I know. I know that now."

Tessa's fists curl. Her nails scrape into the damp wood of the railing.

Tessa Kestrel: "Old Man Rafi lost a boat. He told me to tell you he isn't mad at you. He's 'sad,' he said. Like an animal that looks at something fallen and calls it by a different name."

You: (your throat tight) "And he—?"

Tessa Kestrel: "He's lighting a fire down by the pilings. People are gathering. He wants to talk to whoever's left standing."

You follow her. Down on the rocks, a small knot of neighbors has collected. A barrel fire hisses and sends up a petrichor of smoke that tastes of peat and salt. Old Man Rafi crouches by it, his beard streaked with ash. He blows on a smoldering twig like someone coaxing life back into a fragile idea.

Old Man Rafi: (voice low, dry) "Sea don't aim for spite. She takes what she takes. We can pick what we hand her, or she will pick for us."

He looks at you—his gaze is not accusation, precisely, but it is not absolution either. It is an ancient inventory.

Old Man Rafi: "You all argued about lines on paper while the tide kept its own ledger. That's the truth that sits on the shore now."

Jonah, who has been pacing like a caged animal, stops and folds his arms. He speaks then not as the organizer but as a man whose private ledger is a list of faces.

Jonah Reyes: "She couldn't have known everything. She couldn't have fixed everything. But—" (he points at you) "—she could've seen this coming sooner. She could've told us there was no safe middle ground."

You feel the accusation slant into your chest like a breaking rib. You can hear Cass behind you, hands palming his tablet as if it might harden into something useful.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Mira—" (he tries for calm) "We pushed for reviews. We suggested phased deployments while securing guarantees. The writs came—"

Jonah Reyes: "The writs came because you wanted to document every penny, Cass. You wanted paper before people."

Cass bristles; his reply is sharp and brittle.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "We wanted to avoid making permanent mistakes. We weren't trying to paper the town into paralysis."

Jonah Reyes: "And while you painted 'mistake' on mortar, people were drowning their stories."

A woman near the fire screams—it's the pitch of grief that shreds reason. The group surges, a wave of hands and mouths. Someone calls for water, someone else for blankets. The emergency broadcast repeats instructions about evacuation zones; the irony is ferocious.

You do what you can—the hands that help are many but not always the right ones. You hand out tarps, tie off a sodden boat to a piling, pass a thermos to a shaking man whose grammar has dissolved into thanks.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Noon — the town fractures publicly]
[Music: Dissonant horns; drums like a warning]
[Visual: Crowds cluster; a fold-out noticeboard shows two columns of petitions and countersuits; a humming drone hovers, filming]
[Sound: Shouts overlap; a child's sob; a clerical voice on a megaphone trying to restore order]

Mayor Lynette organizes volunteers and logistics with a machine-like efficiency that now reads as brittle. Her staff moves with a weary competence; two aides argue in hushed tones about whether to release contingency funds without legal clearance. One clerk holds up a stack of paperwork that looks impossibly thin for the devastation it must answer.

Mayor Lynette Cole: "We are opening the emergency fund. We'll fast-track claims as best we can, but my hands are tied without explicit authorization—"

A staffer (whispering, urgent) interrupts: "The council majority refuses to release until legal says it's clear. They're afraid of fiscal liability."

Mayor Lynette: (voice hardening) "Then tell them the liability is a collapse of lives. Tell them to sign."

A shout—someone recognizes an old photo of the harbor in the paper; fingers point at you and at Cass and at Lynette. Each pair of pointing fingers is a verdict in itself.

You stand there and feel the town split into lines on either side of an invisible fault: those who want quick, blunt action at any cost, and those who insist on procedure and permanence. You are somehow, always, between these lines. You are the hinge on which this moment depends, and the hinge is groaning.

Cass approaches, the tablet tucked under his arm like a flag. When he speaks, his voice is a mixture of apology and practical arithmetic.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Mira—(he searches you for some sign)—I couldn't foresee the injunction timeline. None of us could. But if there's a way to salvage something—materials, crews—I'll do it. I will fight whatever paperwork I need to."

You study his face. He's offering labor and perhaps penance. His eyes flick to Jonah and then away, and for a breath you see the man who wanted to be seen as an ally, not a conqueror.

You: "I don't—" (your words fracture) "I don't know if that's enough."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Then tell me what is."

You have no tidy map for that answer. Nothing that doesn't leave someone bereft.

Jonah stands a few paces away, hands jammed into his pockets. He doesn't look at Cass. He looks at you, and his look is not only accusation; there's grief there so large it almost has a voice.

Jonah Reyes: "If you stay, Mira, if you keep doing this—pushing for checks, for time—people will keep dying because time is a luxury we don't have anymore. If you leave, you'll be accused, but at least they'll have a scapegoat. Stay, and you'll be a martyr to process. Either way, we're broken."

You feel the air inside your chest become dense, a concrete you can't lift.

Tessa catches your hand. Her fingers are callused, and her grip is sharp.

Tessa Kestrel: "Don't let him make you the only scapegoat. We all had hands in this."

You: "We all did."

Her eyes search yours for some absolution you don't have.

[Scene: The Docks Edge | Afternoon — the slow, terrible tally]
[Music: A single cello line, low and unrelenting]
[Visual: Long shot of the ruined pier; the horizon is bruise-colored; the town works in small, stunned motions]
[Sound: Occasional sobs, the slap of cold water against wood, the distant drone of an approaching helicopter]

You wander the ruined pier alone after the crowd thins. Ladders of tidewater mark the past hours across pilings. A child's stuffed fish, caked in mud, lies half-buried in a crevice. You pick it up; the fabric is soft and salt-stiff. You press it to your cheek because the gesture is small and human and maybe a kind of penance.

Old Man Rafi finds you there. He comes without ceremony and stands for a long time looking at the sea. Then he reaches into his coat and takes out a small, charred matchbox and offers it to you without speaking.

Old Man Rafi: "We think we can hold forever because we have hands and plans. But the sea keeps a different calendar. You asked what to give. Maybe the question is, 'Who will we still be after the sea takes what it takes?'"

You: "We can rebuild. We will rebuild."

Old Man Rafi: (bitter) "Rebuild what? The same stories? Or do we build something that can't be auctioned in a year because it looks tidy?"

You feel the old man's words like a weatherbeam across your ribs.

You: "I wanted to stop the erasure. I thought if we forced transparency, we'd get a version of safety that kept us whole."

Old Man Rafi: "Transparency doesn't keep roofs from going. Neither do seals and signatures. People keep each other."

You close your notebook. The pages are swollen, but you can still make out the sketches—maps of marsh restoration, staggered breakwaters, lists of names. Each plan is a compromise written in your hand: sacrifice a wetland here, preserve a dock there. They are a ledger of decisions whose sums now read like arithmetic performed too late.

You smell smoke and brine and something else—an old rug burned for warmth. Your fingers feel like a stranger's.

You: (soft, to yourself) "Every path asked for trade-offs. Inertia asked for patience. The sea asked for nothing; she took."

You put the notebook back in your jacket. The weight is familiar and literal, like a slab of wet concrete pressing at your sternum. For a long time you stand at the edge of the water and let the town's noises wash through you. There is no clean answer, no triumphant soldering of people back into a single arc. Instead there is a ruined net we all must mend together, each stitch slow and sharp.

Cass appears at the tail of the pier, his coat damp, a hard line across his face. He doesn't reach for reconciliation, but he doesn't leave either. He slides a rolled plan across a crate between you.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I can mobilize a small volunteer crew. The firm will donate materials without charge—temporary pilings, pumps we can run off generators. It's not the full deployment, but it's a start."

You look at the paper—with diagrams that look almost like hope if you squint—and then at Cass. His expression is complex, unreadable: professional resolve threaded through something like regret.

You: "Why? Why put your name on it now?"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Because someone has to do something. I'd rather it be me than a closed file in a courtroom."

Jonah lingers at the far edge of the pier and calls out once, low.

Jonah Reyes: "If you do this, Mira, make sure we hire local crews. No more outsourcing. No more 'efficiency' that means we lose the people who know the water."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I will sign that clause with you."

Jonah stares at you. His shoulders are knotted.

Jonah Reyes: (quiet, raw) "Make sure this patchwork doesn't become an excuse to forget."

You: "I will try."

He does not embrace you. He does not leave. He walks toward the town, shoulders set, hands in his pockets. The walking looks like a choice and a punishment both.

Old Man Rafi's hand finds yours for a moment, pressing a wordless benediction into your palm. You close your fingers around the charred matchbox and the idea of light it represents. The matchbox is useless for rebuilding, but it's not nothing.

You stand, notebook clutched, and watch as people begin the small impossible work of picking up. There will be lawsuits, and there will be hearings, and there will be a long inventory of who said what and who did not act in time. There will be blame. There will be grief. There will be a ledger of choices you can read in skeletons of walls and in the ash of a kitchen.

Your chest is full of things you cannot unload. You can catalog each decision like a clinician, but the clinical catalog does not bring back the wet porch or the smell of Sunday fish stew. What you have brought is the knowledge that every path demanded a calculus, and some sums included people.

[Visual: Close-up on your coral pendant; the bead is dull with salt; rain begins in a hush along the lip of the harbor]
[Sound: The sea's slow, indifferent exhale]

You close your notebook and feel the weight of choices like concrete on your chest.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Twilight — the town dims into a hush]
[Music: Low, mournful brass with a faint, contemplative piano theme]
[Visual: Lanterns swung from poles; people tend fires; the town's silhouette is jagged, defiantly alive]
[Sound: Murmured conversations; an old radio playing a halting ballad; footsteps on wet wood]

You walk the length of the boardwalk one last time before the light goes out, greeting those you pass with a nod that means more than words can hold. There is no tidy reconciliation: Jonah's distance is a line not yet crossed; Cass's hand remains extended but ungrasped; Mayor Lynette will convene another emergency meeting at dawn that will read like competence and grief mashed in equal measure.

Old Man Rafi's fire smolders, and by it a small cluster of people share a thin pot of stew. You sit, not to claim absolution but to participate in the slow, ordinary economy of staying.

Old Man Rafi: (soft, to you) "We will rebuild, some things different. Some things lost. That's the sea's trade, and it's not the worst teacher."

You: "Will it ever stop hurting?"

Old Man Rafi: "No. Pain is the price of memory. It keeps us honest."

You let the honesty in. It is bitter and exact and necessary.

You lift your head and look out at the water—at the impossible silver seam where sky meets sea—and make a promise, not to a plan but to the act of continuing. You'll be part of the mending. You'll stand for transparency, but you'll also learn where time matters more than process. You will carry the ledger not as a shield but as a warning.

You will stay.
