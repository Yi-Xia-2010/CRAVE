[Scene: Municipal Hall | Dawn]
[Visual: The conference room's glass catches the first thin light; a holographic coastline model spins silently on the table]
[Music: Rapid, insistent strings build under a steady percussive heartbeat]

You wake to the phone's pale glow like a lighthouse blink you can't ignore. The thread header—Municipal Operations—stares back with Mayor Lynette's clipped subject line: "Final blueprints attached. Enactment begins Monday."

You sit up so fast your head tells you the room is still. The tablet's schematic hangs in your mind like a diagram you can feel in your bones: lines of concrete etched across curves of marsh you once learned to walk along as a child. Your wristband buzzes; tide alerts chirp with the polite ruthlessness of an approaching schedule. Hope—strange, bitter-sweet—surges through you like hot coffee. It tastes of rain, yes, but also of machine oil and undone work.

You pull the tablet closer, thumb tracing the rendered crest of the seawall. The model is beautiful in the way a promise can be: clean edges, reassurance quantified into percent reductions, staged timelines that make risk look manageable. You read the contract clauses; legalese folds into your mind and refuses to stay neutral. Maintenance responsibilities. Escalation clauses. Funding windows that close with teeth.

You: "If they're counting on 'adaptive funding,' what happens in year five when budgets tighten?"

Cassian: "We'll design contingencies. Maintenance funds are part of the model." (He taps the tablet; the model shows phased reinforcement.) "There are federal backstops. There are insurance structures. The engineering's solid."

Cassian's voice is a steady metronome. He stands near the projection, sleeves rolled, the trench coat at the chair back like a flag. He brings diagrams and liaison teams and a public face that can translate technical certainty into reassurance. The camera crews love that translation. You're aware of how his fingers hover near the tablet as if the work were as tender as any prayer.

Jonah enters with wind in his hair and salt still in the seams of his sweater. He is a human weather, incandescent and immediate. He drops a wet rope on the table and the room smells for a second of fish and diesel and honest labor.

Jonah: (rasped, low) "We can't let them turn our river into a graph and call it a home."

You: "Graphs don't bury your boat, Jonah."

Jonah's jaw tightens. He leans forward until the rope is a line between you—literal and figurative. The room's humidity seems to loosen like a held breath. Around you, town councilors trade glances; cameras shift lenses from one face to another.

Mayor Lynette: "We need a decision that delivers results before the next two high-tide windows. Public confidence will follow visible action."

Dr. Saira: (soft, urgent) "Visible action isn't neutral. Hard walls change habitats. Marsh migration, nursery grounds—there are trade-offs we can predict and ones we can't. You know that, Mira."

You read Dr. Saira's worry like an equation that also needs a heart. Her eyes, behind tortoiseshell frames, are maps of places you've studied and scars you still carry. She does not dramatize. She hands you a stack of peer-reviewed projections and then straightens as if offering a lifejacket.

Dr. Saira: "If we go full-engineering, there will be measurable reductions in surge—immediate protection. But there will be long-term ecological shifts that could cost fishermen seasons of yield and push the marsh inland—or trap it seaward. Maintenance funding is political; political winds change."

You flip to the annotated pages. Each number feels like a promise you could make and then watch unravel under a storm. Your survivor's guilt—always a low murmuring current—hisses louder: Which loss will you carry?

[Scene: Seawall Project Site | Mid-Morning]
[Visual: Cameras, orange safety vests, steel stakes like teeth along the shoreline]
[Music: Percussive brass, rapid drum hits; the tempo accelerates]

Cassian stands before a ribboned stake and a swarm of reporters. Renderings ripple on the projection behind him, the wall cutting a clean horizon into the sea. He speaks with the precise cadence of someone used to making promises that are measured in risk assessments.

Cassian: "This wall reduces surge by sixty-eight percent in worst-case scenarios. It protects homes, utilities, and critical infrastructure. We can start mobilization next week."

Reporter: "What about the fish runs? The marshlands?"

Cassian: (measured) "Our environmental mitigation plans include engineered channels and scheduled habitat translocations. We'll monitor and adapt."

Your stomach flips because 'mitigate' is not the same as 'preserve.' Mitigations are a language of attempted repair. They may work. They may not. You think of the saltmarsh fen, of crab traps and narrow boardwalks that have memories older than funding cycles.

Jonah organizes people with the raw charisma that doesn't require a podium. He has hand-drawn signs and faces that the camera catches in wide, empathetic frames: a crew—people who welded your boats back together after the last storm, teenagers whose futures are stitched to tides.

Jonah: "We'll put rafts in the water. We'll show early crews and the old practices. It's not about stopping progress; it's about questioning whose progress."

You watch as jelly-voiced anchors narrate the flotilla with the softening lens of human interest. The town looks alive; people look like they belong in the story. Your chest aches with the proud, private ache of belonging.

[Scene: Boardwalk | Afternoon]

Jonah meets you between two poles, his hands rough and salt-raw where they find yours. There is a long, complicated history in that touch—memory braided with grief and comfort. He doesn't smile hollowly; he offers a geography of small reassurances.

Jonah: "They'll bring cranes. They'll block off the old boatyard. They'll put fences where we used to set crab pots. This isn't the same town afterward."

You: "We can't pretend the storm never happened. We owe people safety."

Jonah: "You say that as if it's money in the bank. What good is 'safety' if our kids don't know how to mend nets? If our crews have nowhere to launch at high tide because concrete sits where the water should be?"

You look at him and see every summer you ever had—sunburned shoulders, Old Man Rafi's stories, Tessa trying to hold a wriggling fish. The townspeople clustered around the flotilla nod; their faces are stories too.

Jonah: (softer) "Do you remember when the old pier held the lantern festival? Do you want to be the one who sells that memory for a model?"

Your throat tightens. The question lands like a hook.

[Scene: Municipal Hall | Evening]
[Visual: Conference room lights glow against the dim, storming sky; rain makes runnels on the glass]
[Music: High-tempo strings; timpani rolls punctuate each political volley]
[Sound: Press phones pinging; distant thunder like the town's collective pulse]

Mayor Lynette keeps a schedule and a smile in equal measure. She circles back to the calendar as if schedules could outrun weather.

Mayor Lynette: "We need you to authorize the deployment, Mira. Delays cost grant windows and contractors have hold costs. The optics of inaction are dangerous."

Tessa: (quiet, fierce) "There's more at stake than optics. Mom used to say the town is made up of how we remember it. Don't erode that."

Tessa's voice is the hint of a future in the present—insistent, sharp. You pull the coral pendant up from under your shirt and twist it against your sternum. It is small and warm and makes you remember the house that was and the hands that rebuilt it.

Dr. Saira sits with a laptop open to tidal migration models. She does what she always does: give you the hardest plausible truth and leave the moral part for you to carry.

Dr. Saira: "Hard infrastructure gives you predictability. It also gives you dependency. The marsh will migrate if given a corridor. If we harden here, we trap ecological transition. That could reduce long-term resilience."

Cassian: (leaning in) "We can build adaptive faces into the wall. We can create corridors—engineer the migration."

Jonah: "Engineer migration? Sounds like putting a bandage on a moving river."

The voices overlap like surf and squall. You listen to their urgency and feel the energy of the town compress into you—hopeful, frantic, committed. The arousal in the room is nearly violent now; every second hums with decisions and consequences.

You: "So the question is… do we prioritize speed and scale or stewardship and oversight? Or do we walk away and force the whole system to change?"

Mayor Lynette: (pressing) "We don't have the luxury of walking away."

Cassian: (gentle, pleading) "Give me your signature and we lock in transparent oversight seats. We can structure the contract to include community liaisons."

Jonah: (bare, immediate) "A liaison won't stop a crane from taking the old boatyard."

You look at both men and feel the pull like a tide ripping between two rocks. With Cass there is the smell of coffee and calibrated plans; with Jonah there is salt on his knuckles and the map of every alley and dock you grew up in. Love, in this town, is a complicated infrastructure too—some parts engineered, some native, all fragile.

Your survivor's guilt no longer whispers; it argues. It lists losses you've already carried and asks which additional weight you will shoulder to save others. Your heartbeat is a drumline under your ribs. You think of the kids on the boardwalk, of Tessa's blunt, brave eyes, of Old Man Rafi's stories that know the sea as both giver and taker.

Tessa: "Make the choice that leaves us with stories, Mira. Don't make the easy one."

Cassian: "We can protect those stories by protecting the town. Sometimes preservation means construction."

Jonah: "And sometimes 'preserving the town' means preserving the ways the town breathes."

You stand at the exact point where engineering meets memory, and the world narrows to a point of decision. The storm-bruised sky outside thrums like a held breath. Cameras wait, council members watch, your notebook sits on the table full of your margin notes—concern lines, possible clauses, sketches of hybrid solutions you half-wanted to test.

The arousal in you—electric and crackling—has climbed in a curve that runs from weary hope to urgent resolve. This is the climax of months of talk and late nights and compromises. You know the stakes; they are not hypothetical.

You: (voice steadier than you feel) "I have to choose what to authorize—how far to lean on speed and scale, how much to insist on oversight, or whether to withdraw and force a different kind of reckoning."
