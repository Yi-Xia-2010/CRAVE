In the months and years that follow, the town rebuilds in a way that is different from what anyone promised in a press release. The cooperative processes bite and drag and iron out corruption. The collective decision-making you forced into existence bleeds into the everyday: council evenings, shared procurement, open contracts. It is bureaucratically brutal and morally cleaner.

You live in the leak between public victory and private cost: a moral clarity that keeps you awake and a body that keeps learning to sleep again in small slices. You are neither untroubled nor triumphant. You are awake and full of mourning and work and a thin, stubborn thread of hope.

The moral victory is modest. It is messy and has teeth. It costs you friends or possible intimacies, it costs a season's catch, it costs a shed of seeds. But it births a different kind of power — one rooted in public accounting, in community seats, in the slow, stubborn labor of people agreeing to be responsible to one another.
