Old Man Rafi moves through the crowd like a slow reef, steady and inevitable. He has wrapped a coat around his thin shoulders and carries with him, improbably, a small tin. Old Man Rafi: (soft) "The sea takes. That is as old as the rock under our feet. It takes. It will not be flattered by our plans."

Tessa: (brittle) "We told them. We told them the wall would change the tides in places we didn't expect. We warned—"

Jonah: (almost a whisper) "You didn't think, Mira. You thought procedure would save us."

You walk alone when the crowd thins, notebook in hand, pages damp at the edges. You close it with a practiced motion. Each item is a notch on a plank you've helped mark.

You think — brutally, with the flat arithmetic of someone cataloging what remains — that every path had trade-offs. The hybrid you imagined, the hard-engineered plan Cass wanted, the slow, community-first repairs you fought for: none guaranteed salvation. Bureaucracy was a blade we sharpened on both sides.

Tessa appears at your elbow, the heat of her breath a warm punctuation.

Tessa: (soft, less certain than angry) "What do we do now?"

You: (the answer feels like stone in your mouth) "We heal what we can. We count. We make lists. We put fires in little tins. We keep the children fed."

Old Man Rafi's small fire burns nearby, the scent of tea and tobacco waltzing together. He sits more steadily than the night should allow and meets your eyes with a look that contains no accusation, only the kind of old clarity that hurts precisely because it's simple.

Old Man Rafi: (quiet) "The sea will always take some things. It is how we choose what else to give that marks us. We choose badly sometimes."

Narration:
His words are not comfort. They are true. They land with the weight of the years and settle in your chest like concrete poured into a mould shaped by your past choices. You close your notebook fully now. The pages are wet, the ink blurred at the corners where sketches of marsh restoration and purchase orders once lived. The notebook is suddenly too heavy to carry and yet impossible to leave.

You stand at the dock's ragged edge and feel every decision like a stone in your boots. You thought you had time to align values and contracts, to negotiate oversight into something that would hold when the storm came. You misjudged the speed and ferocity of an ocean warmed in ways your models had only begun to whisper about.
