Mayor Lynette's smile is the kind the cameras love: unshakable, practiced, and lit for photographs. Her voice is all smooth angles as she speaks about funding, timelines, and "responsible stewardship." The firm’s legal counsel recites clauses that smell of lawyers and certainty. Around you, the town's representatives — fishermen with callused knuckles, teachers with tired eyes, Old Man Rafi with his braided beard — listen like weather vanes turning toward the promise of shelter.

Cassian stands beside you, trench coat buttoned against the coast's damp. He hands you the tablet as if offering a sacrament. On its screen, models unfold in cool steel-blue; revetments like folded armor, modular seawalls that clamp the coast in repeating geometry, pump arrays that glitter with a proprietary sheen.

You touch the screen. The models hum. You can see, in elegant rendering, places where the sea will be asked to stop its erosion and where marshland would be compromised for structural integrity. Data ticks in neat corners — risk profiles, return-on-investment, projected lives saved. The numbers are clean. The proposals are beautiful in the way machines are beautiful: precise, implacable, and almost aesthetic.

Cassian: "We can stage this to minimize construction impact on the fishing season. Phased implementation, with local crews trained first." (He leans in, voice low but fierce.) "We want you — the town — at every pause, Mira. We can't do this without your knowledge."

You hear the urgency beneath the polish. It is there in the slight tremor at the edge of his words, in the way his fingers brush the tablet's case like someone steadying a ship. You remember the clasp — the brief, human pressure of his hand on your shoulder after the signatures were passed. It was an impulse under the waterproof trench: furtive and grounding.

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Night]
[Visual: Interior lamps glow warm against rain-drum beats on the stone tower]
[Music: Pulsing, propulsive strings; hopeful major chord progressions]
[Sound: Instruments hum, the soft click of the tablet, the whisper of rain along the panes]

You climb the ladder to the lighthouse with the tablet and your notebook. The tower smells of oil, paper, and the sea — the comforting, complicated scents of work that keeps people awake. At the small lab table, Cass unrolls high-resolution schematics while Dr. Saira N'Goma rigs a model sensor to a mock tide.

The three of you crowd close, shoulders almost touching, poring over light that makes the edges of maps glow like promises. Cass speaks in the clipped, efficient language of engineers while Saira translates trade-offs into human terms. You stitch them together: policy, model, memory.

Dr. Saira N'Goma: (with warm insistence) "If we angle the revetment footings here, we preserve the eelgrass beds on this lee side. It costs more up front, but it keeps fishing grounds viable."

Cassian: (nods, rubbing the bridge of his nose) "We can run that in the adaptive matrix. It increases initial capital, but it reduces maintenance cycles."

You: (leaning over the tablet) "Run both scenarios. Show me the community impacts — not just the hydrology. Show me what a fisher's season looks like under each."

Cassian taps, fingers moving fast; the models re-render. Data cascades, numbers resolving like a tide line. He pauses, then looks up at you with an unguardedness the trench and lapel pin rarely allow.

Cassian: (quiet) "I don't want to be the one who signs something that doesn't work. If it fails — if it doesn't do what we promise — I will..." (he stops, breath caught on something private) "I will have failed people."

The steel-gray of his eyes loses a degree of armor. For a moment he is not the polished emissary of a firm but someone who fears the same thing you fear: the small, gnawing shame of not saving enough.

You feel the room tilt on its hinge toward something fragile and true. The lighthouse keeps its steady light, and the rain makes a percussion that tucks the world into focus.

You (Mira Kestrel): (soft) "We won't pretend there's a guarantee. There are only calculated risks, transparency, and humility. We'll build with the town's knowledge. We'll measure and adjust."

Cassian: (a half-smile) "Then we'll build like an apprentice learns — hands on, humbling every day."

[Scene: Brinehaven Docks | Early Morning]
[Visual: Crews at work; formwork and pylons rise like a new, geometric reef]
[Music: Fast, rhythmic percussion — the sound of construction and momentum]
[Sound: Hydraulic hammers, shouted commands, gulls squawking; the sea smells of iron and kelp]

The shore has become a choreography of machines and hands. Crews driven by contracts and families lift pylons into place; modular sea-walls click together like solving a giant mechanical puzzle. The municipal scaffolding glints. Your embroidered tide-line hem bobs against the wind — a small, stubborn flag of the town's presence among neat lines of concrete.

Jonah stands apart near the piles, his rope-and-shell bracelet twisted around his fist until the shells creak. He watches the new geometry with a look you know well: a harbor-sound grief that could harden into anger or harden into an organizing fire. He doesn't approach; he watches as if trying to memorize how the town will look after its edges are altered.

You want to go to him. You want to say, This is still our town; help us steer this; don't let it take you away. The urge is a physical thing, a tide pulling at your ribs.

Jonah: (low, when he finally speaks, not to you but into the wind) "They build for the worst and call it safety. They call it love for the town, but it smells like someone else's wallet."

You (Mira Kestrel): (moving closer) "We're trying to keep people safe, Jonah. We're trying to keep livelihoods."

Jonah: (turns, amber eyes hard and wet at once) "At what cost, Mira? Boats out of the water? Nets made useless by new currents? Will they put up a wall and call it our shore?"

You: "No. Not if I can help it. That's why you're here — your crews, your knowledge. We need to blend their engineering with your seasons."

Jonah: (a bitter laugh) "Blend. They bring machines; you bring us. And who decides when they've got the louder microphone?"

The words land between you like a thrown rope. You catch them with both hands.

You (Mira Kestrel): "We decide together. We set the milestones. We push back if something isn't right."

Jonah: (after a beat) "Then make it mean something, Mira. Don't let it become a thing the town wears because someone told us we had to."

There is a tremor in his voice that makes your chest tilt toward him and away at once. He turns and walks along the docks, not to storm off but to keep watch. The amber of his gaze is a quiet storm that teaches you the cost of this work.

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Deep Night — Days Later]
[Visual: The lab is cluttered now — weather reports, checklists, a mason jar half full of dried tea bags]
[Music: High-tempo strings layered with heartbeat-like bass; hope braided with pressure]
[Sound: The incessant tick of a clock, distant breakers, a phone ping you ignore]

The nights are long and shorter than they feel. You fall into a rhythm of data and argument: model adjustments at midnight, community consultations at dawn, and the slow calculus of livelihood preservation in between. Cass becomes a constant presence in the tower, not always in coat and tie but often in rolled sleeves, hair falling out of its knot. His laugh sometimes comes out too loud at a joke; other times he is all business.

Dr. Saira sits across from you, hand on a mug, eyes sharp as an instrument.

Dr. Saira N'Goma: (firm) "You can be conciliatory, Mira, but you must also be a fulcrum. People will look to you to stop what doesn't fit."

You: "I know. I'm trying to make sure we don't lose who we are while we protect ourselves."

Cassian: (soft, leaning in) "And I'm trying to make sure we stay honest about what engineering can and can't do. We'll monitor. We'll adapt. We'll hire from the town, train apprentices, rotate crews to avoid seasonal collapse."

His plans are specific now — apprenticeships with Jonah's crews, a community-controlled maintenance fund written into the contract, contingency clauses. Every bureaucratic line he carves becomes a promise you can hold on to.

Cassian: (after a pause, quieter) "There are nights I wake up and run the models in my head, thinking of how a miscalculation could cascade. That's not just corporate responsibility — it's personal fear."

You: (surprised) "Personal?"

Cassian: "Yes. I came here to help, but I also came because I don't want another town to be a cautionary tale under my name. I want to be part of something that works."

You study the way his jaw flexes when he says 'works' — a man who layers intent beneath reputation. There is tenderness there, small and brittle, that you find yourself protecting as fiercely as you protect the town's salt-marred wood.

Your shoulders touch in the crowded lab; it is the kind of contact that begins as professional proximity and becomes something else under the hum of instruments. Conversations slip from models into confessions: why you returned home, the price of staying, the sleeplessness of responsibility. The late hours yield a brittle intimacy — not yet promised romance but something that arranges hearts in the same room.

[Sound: The lighthouse foghorn somewhere far off, a low, patient pulse]

Dr. Saira N'Goma: (gently but sharply) "Don't let private worry dictate public policy. Use it to make the models better, not to make deals behind curtains."

Cassian: (nods, chastened) "Agreed."

You: (to both of them) "We will build this together. We will hold each other accountable."

A tired, small chorus of laughter follows — a relief so physical it nearly knocks the air from your lungs. For the first time in months, the knot in your chest loosens enough that you sleep in solid, patchwork stretches.
