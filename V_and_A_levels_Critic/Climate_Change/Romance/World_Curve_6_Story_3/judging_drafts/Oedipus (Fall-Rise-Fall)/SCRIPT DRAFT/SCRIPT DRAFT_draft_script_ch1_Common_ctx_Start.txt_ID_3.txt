[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk & Docks | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Weathered wooden planks glisten with salt; gulls wheel lazily against a bruised-sky horizon.]
[Sound: A low, steady wash of waves; distant gull calls; a soft scrape of rope against timber.]
[Music: Sparse piano, slow tempo]

You step off the bus and the town folds around you like a familiar bruise — tender, impossible to ignore. Salt is in your hair, the coral pendant at your throat knocking once against your collarbone with the soft insistence of memory. The boardwalk smells of old rope, rain held in the grain of wood, and the metallic, mossy tang of seaweed. Every scar on the pilings reads like a sentence you already know; you catalogue them with the trained, automatic slide of your eyes, measuring where the water's teeth have come closer this year.

Tessa is waiting where the boardwalk widens near Rafi’s bench. She greets you like sunshine someone put on too bright — too-bright grin, freckled arms thrown into an awkward hug that wants to fix everything. The hug is clumsy and earnest and exactly what she offers when she doesn't know what else to do.

Tessa: "Mira. You're—you're here. You made it back." (She squeezes tighter as if the hinge might hold.)

You return the hug because refusing would be harder. Her hoodie smells faintly of lavender and fryer oil from the café. There’s a question in your chest — how long you can keep being the one who returns with answers — and you tuck it beneath her shoulder like a small, private stone.

You: "Hey, Tess." (Your voice comes out softer than you'd planned.) "Sorry I'm late. Flight delays." You feel the excuse before it is even an explanation, and you know she knows that 'late' is a polite thing to say for 'I left because I needed to know what I could do.'

Tessa: "No, it's okay. We—" (She pulls back, searching your face.) "We put some of your old tide logs on the community board. Rafi keeps saying 'about time' with a smile and everyone else is… doing things." Her smile wavers. "You okay?"

You want to tell her everything and nothing at once: the nights in the lab, the models that kept you up, the house you couldn't save. Instead you keep to the practical contour that steadies you.

You: "I need to see the lighthouse instruments first. Then—I'll meet the council." You fold your fingers around your notebook without meaning to; the leather is ridged with tide marks you made as a child.

[Visual: Old Man Rafi on a bench; his braid bead glints as he turns his head slowly to look at you.]
[Sound: The bench creaks under his weight; a pocket radio faintly plays the looped news in the background.]

Old Man Rafi watches with that patient, seeing sort of look that predates the council. He clears his throat.

Old Man Rafi: "Back at last. The sea's been talking like a fool with a secret." (His voice is gravel-soft.) "You look tired, Mira Kestrel. Did she treat you well?"

You hold your tongue and let him keep the metaphor. You're used to his way of saying things in two layers: the literal and the story that holds it. You sit beside him for a beat, feeling the bench's salt-stiff grain under your palms.

You: "She treats us all unkindly right now, Rafi." (You meet his eyes.) "I'm back to help."

Rafi: "Help's a wide word. You know that." (He taps his cane on the boardwalk.) "This town needs steady hands, but it needs voices that listen more than they shout."

The words are soft but not small. They settle into you like the tide sifting into channels: familiar, unavoidable. Listening is the thing you preach; listening is also the thing that keeps you from stepping too fast. Guilt waits just beneath that restraint — an old, impossible ledger of what you couldn't save.

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Dusk]
[Visual: A battered stone tower at the town’s edge; warm lamplight leaks through the windows. Instruments hum under the ceiling.]
[Sound: Low mechanical hums, the steady tapping of a rain gauge, the whisper of pages.]
[Music: Soft ambient tones, grainy strings]

The lighthouse smells of machine oil and wet wool. Inside, instruments hum like a distant chorus and a bank of monitors casts pale maps across your face. You run your fingers over tide-etched marks in your weathered leather notebook; the grooves are a map of small defeats and adjustments you’ve made to live with the sea. Each line is a private, stubborn record.

On a corner screen a news reel loops: footage of a storm tearing through the storage district — sheets of corrugated metal folding like paper, crates tumbling into dark water. The footage is too familiar. Mayor Lynette Cole appears at the end of the clip, impeccable even in a floodlight: polished raincoat, voice modulated for cameras.

[Sound: MQ-style news anchor voice morphing into Mayor Lynette's recorded address.]
Mayor Lynette (on screen): "We will present a comprehensive defense package. We will secure funding, deploy professional engineering teams, and protect Brinehaven from future storms."

Her words are crisp as cut glass. They are also policy-shaped decisions spoken with the public clarity of someone used to ballots and press releases. The words add a weight to the room — administrative inevitability pressing at your ribs.

Dr. Saira isn't here, but you can hear her in your head — the reasoning behind restraint, the math that folds big decisions into cautious steps. You run through models mentally. The lighthouse instruments are patient but unsparing: they record, they do not console.

You sit on a stool and let the hum fill the space between thought and speech. Responsibility tilts again — not just to make the right technical choice, but to figure out how to translate that into human terms people will accept. That's harder.

[Scene: Docks | Early Evening]
[Visual: Docks under a sky inked with approaching storm; Jonah is leaned against a piling, salt-stiff sweater folded around him like armor.]
[Sound: The ropes creak; a distant boat horn; low voices of fishermen.]
[Music: A single muted cello note underlines the open air conversation]

Down at the docks Jonah waits like someone who has made waiting a craft. He sees you before you approach; his amber-brown eyes are soft but there is a guarded line at the edges. He jokes about your field jacket's embroidered tide lines in the way people joke when they want to bridge a distance they don't know how else to cross.

Jonah: "Look at you. Tide lines and all. Figured they'd let you in without the trench coat, at least." (He grins, but it doesn't reach the guardedness in his eyes.) "You been measuring my patience, scientist?"

You want to laugh and let the sound break some of the tension. You also want to refuse the role of the town's conscience — it's too heavy to carry and your shoulders are already tired.

You: "I brought logs. And coffee. Old tricks, same data." (You tuck your notebook under your arm like armor.) "How's the fleet?"

Jonah: "The fleet's okay. Boats are patched. People are… trying." (He looks at the horizon, hands shoved into worn wool.) "Mira, we heard the mayor's speech. Heard you're back and that Cassian's here talking big models. People are nervous. People are scared we don't get asked what's best for us."

The words land quiet and earnest. There is an argument in every syllable: protect the way of life, don't hand it to outsiders. There is also something personal in the way he watches you — an old recognition, a question about where you stand now that you're home.

You: "I don't—" (You stop because saying "I stand with you" would be easy and possibly false; saying "I don't know yet" would be honest and dangerous.) "I came back to help the town make choices that don't, you know, drown us or sell us out."

Jonah: "That's what we need." (He lets a small, mirthless laugh out.) "Not speeches. Not glossy fixes. People who actually know what the tides do to a dock at three different moon phases."

There's a pause, the kind that tightens inside your chest. You remember the house you couldn't save: how the water came in one dark night and how you had to stand on the other side of the street and watch. Guilt coils, familiar and heavy.

Cassian arrives minutes later, slate-blue trench clicking in the wetness, tablet in hand, eyes already scanning the angles of the harbor like a man reading a blueprint in a language only he and his models understand. He has a way of stepping into a space and making it conference-room small.

Cassian: "Mira." (His greeting is polite, measured.) "Good to see you back. The models you ran last year—some of them align with what our simulations suggest. We could—" (He taps his tablet.) "—present phased concrete barriers that would blunt projected storm surges."

You feel an old professional reflex: to ask for the constraints, the cost projections, the impacts. But there is also the way he says 'phased concrete barriers' that feels like a solution built for a map and not a place — efficient words, heavy with consequence.

You: "Concrete changes the shoreline. It changes livelihoods. It changes where the sea goes, and sometimes it goes where we live." (Your voice is careful; you think of marsh grass that dies when hard edges crowd it.) "What about softer measures? Marsh restoration? Community-led breakwaters?"

Cassian: "We can combine strategies. Harden where it's critical, restore where possible. Funding will need a clear deliverable." (He watches your face, earnest.) "Mira, I don't want to be the outsider who bulldozes a town. I want to prove engineering can preserve what matters."

Jonah watches you and Cassian like someone listening for a crack. There is a line in his jaw you remember from storms; it's the same line when he's deciding whether to forgive or to fight. Between them — between practical, rooted community care and the promise of engineered security — you feel the town tilt.

Mayor Lynette's recorded address hangs over everything like a second tide. The public rhetoric offers certainty: funds, teams, timelines. People want certainty. You want to be certain too, but certainty that robs people of their agency isn't a gift.

The conversation stretches on, longer than the minutes. Jonah presses small, local truths into the gaps of Cassian's proposals. Cassian replies with modelled curves and funding windows. You sit between them like a hinge that might hold or might split.

Jonah: "We don't want our kids teaching in a town no one recognizes. We want nets and boats and the smell of fish, not boardwalks that look like a museum exhibit."

Cassian: "I understand that. So do you see this as incompatible?" (He leans forward, earnest.) "Because I truly believe we can keep what you love and secure it."

Jonah: "Belief doesn't pay for nets, Cass." (His voice is low; the word 'believe' tastes different when someone has rebuilt a boat with a bruised hand.)

Cassian: "Then let me show you exact numbers." (He opens his tablet and the glow is precise.)

You watch their faces — Jonah's lined with practical worry, Cassian's taut with controlled hope — and feel something gapless inside: the knowledge that every word you will speak in the coming days could be a blade or a balm. The memory of the house you couldn't save tightens like a knot in your throat. You want to promise everything and you know you can't.

The wind off the water picks up, carrying the damp smell of storm. It presses your hair against your neck and the coral pendant makes a small, accusing knock. Mayor Lynette's voice loops again from a portable radio near the cafe: "—comprehensive defense package—secure our future—"

Everything feels patient and heavy. The town is waiting for the shape of your answer, but you have to assemble it from people and grief and shifting data.

You breathe in the salt and the sound of the harbor. The guilt sits with you, familiar and blunt. Your mouth is dry as driftwood.

You know you must speak soon.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand tightening around the leather of your notebook; the tide-etched grooves press into your palm.]
[Music: A single held piano chord]

You: (thought) "If I say the wrong thing, someone loses more than pride. If I stay silent, someone loses more than hope."

[Scene fades slightly; the sky bruises darker at the edges.]

You stand between them, the tide of obligations pressing in from all sides, and the lighthouse hums behind you like a careful, sad heart.

[Page-turn thought] The next hearing will ask for answers. Your voice will need to carry more than data; it will need to carry the town. You feel the knot in your chest pull tighter, not from panic but from the slow, heavy responsibility of knowing how much of people's lives hinge on the words you choose.
