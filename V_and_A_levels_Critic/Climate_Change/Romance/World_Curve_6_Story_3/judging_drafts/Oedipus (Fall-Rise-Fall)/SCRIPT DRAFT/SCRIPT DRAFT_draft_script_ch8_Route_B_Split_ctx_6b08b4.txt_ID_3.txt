Cassian: (nods, measured) "We can write that. Help me shape the clause. Your voice matters in the wording."

Dr. Saira leans over the model, fingers tapping a projected map where marshlands meet proposed wall anchors. Her face is lit from below by the glow of topography. She is the scientific conscience you invited into this complexity.

Dr. Saira: "Hard infrastructure will alter marsh migration corridors, Mira. Species that rely on slow shoreline retreat will lose breeding grounds. We can mitigate with ecological offsets, but those aren't full replacements."

You: "Can we phase it? Pull concrete back, leave soft buffers?"

Dr. Saira: "We can change the design to be more adaptive, but there's cost. There's political risk. And the firm—"

Dr. Saira's voice tightens like a wire. She taps a data point: erosion curves that spike at the edges of engineered defenses. The numbers are clean. The losses are not tidy.

Dr. Saira: "You know what I would advise if this were only biology. But you must balance that with what the town can handle emotionally and economically. There's no neutral ground here."

You: (internal) Her honesty is a cold lantern. You flip through contract pages with stiff fingers: indemnity clauses, maintenance guarantees, a section titled "Limitations of Liability." Your wristband buzzes; an alert notes tidal prediction shifting halfway through the week.

Tessa appears at the doorway, hair pulled into a messy knot, eyes wide and impossible to read.

Tessa: "Sis — if you take away the boardwalk — if they put a wall there — what happens to the summer markets? The kids' races? The stories?"

You: "We'll fight to keep those things. I promise you that."

Tessa: (bravely) "Promise doesn't stop storms, Mira. It doesn't stop boats from listing. But it makes people feel like you, like me, like we're trying."

Her look is small, a shard that bounces light back at you. You want to wrap her in certainty but your hands are full of clauses and deadlines.

You feel every eye in the room sharpen into a small pinpoint of weather. Your mouth tastes of metal and salt and coffee; your notebook is a heavy thing in your lap. The town has always been a negotiation of urgent, immediate needs and slow, structural truths. Today is the collision of both.
