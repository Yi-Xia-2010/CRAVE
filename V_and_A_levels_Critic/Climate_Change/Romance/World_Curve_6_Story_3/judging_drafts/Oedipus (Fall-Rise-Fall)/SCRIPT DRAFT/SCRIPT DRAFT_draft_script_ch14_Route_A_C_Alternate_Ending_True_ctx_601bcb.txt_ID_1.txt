Woman: "And now what? They'll tie our hands with regulations while the contractors rebuild their portfolios."

You taste bile. She is right; the stakes you chose carry their own violences. The town has not all rallied behind you. Some see the exposure as a wound that invites federal footsteps. Others see it as a scalpel that might cut out the rot.

Work becomes paperwork and the paperwork becomes a kind of warfare. The town rebuilds slowly. Funds arrive in fits. Grants are transparent and slogged through public votes. The cooperative governance is bureaucratically brutal — meetings that strip the romanticism from heroism and replace it with minutes, deadlines, and signatures. Some people leave; some stay. The traditional boats are patched, the marsh restoration proceeds in handfuls rather than a flood of cash. That restraint is a small mercy and a slow crucible.
