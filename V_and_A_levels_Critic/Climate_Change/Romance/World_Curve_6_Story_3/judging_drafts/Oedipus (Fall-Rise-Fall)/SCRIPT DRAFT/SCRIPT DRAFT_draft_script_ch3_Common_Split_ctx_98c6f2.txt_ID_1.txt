[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — After the Hearing]
[Music: Low, hopeful strings; undercurrent of steady percussion like distant waves]

You step out onto the boardwalk and the town comes with you — a slow tidal surge of voices and feet. Salt rides the humid air like a fine dust; wood underfoot gives a wet, familiar creak. Your notebook is closed against your palm; the coral pendant at your throat is cold enough to draw your breath.

Mayor Lynette stands at the head of the crowd like a lighthouse gone municipal: polished, deliberate, forcing the room to look at the shape she gestures toward. Beside her Cass's watch throws a pale topographic ribbon into the air — floating lines and shaded bathymetric swells that insist on being understood as problems with solutions.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Fast action gets results. We stage the seawalls, raise critical infrastructure, and we keep insurance rates from spiking. The firm will underwrite the early risk, provide labor guarantees, and—" (he taps the projection, smoothing a ridge into a straight, reassuring face of engineered certainty) "—we make sure Brinehaven survives the next three storms."

Jonah is leaning on a railing, boots planted where salt has darkened the wood. His jaw sets in the way you know means he's cataloguing every cost that isn't written in concrete: livelihoods, traditions, the smell of the marsh. He steps forward before you can.

Jonah Reyes: "Survives, sure. But at what cost? You bring in walls and half the harbor's charm is boxed out. Kids won't learn to read tides; they'll learn to read permits. Who's building those things? Outsiders or our crews?"

Cass answers with a practiced calm that smells faintly of urban air and new gear.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "We hire locally. Training programs. Long-term maintenance contracts. Jobs. Jonah, I'm not offering aesthetics over people. I'm offering structure so people can keep their lives."

Jonah: (a laugh that isn't light) "You say jobs. You say maintenance. You say 'structure.' You sound like you're talking about something you can control from a desk. Brinehaven's not a model to be shipped and installed. It's people and memory and the way the marsh gave us work. Do you think a blueprint will teach the kids to mend nets?"

Cass's eyes flick to you — steel-gray, even, trying not to be brittle. The crowd exhales; the argument ripples like wind over water.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (to you) "Mira, you know the data. You know timelines. We can start before the next season. We can keep lives afloat."

You feel the accusation in the quiet after his sentence: not his intention, but the practical edge of it. Your fingers tighten on the notebook. You picture a past storm — the one that still lives in your marrow — and how small decisions then cost more than money.

[Visual: Close-up on your coral pendant, the bead warmed by the heat of your palm]
[Sound: A child laughing in the distance, then hushed voices]

Old Man Rafi pushes forward on his cane. His voice is a sand-rough ledger of memory.

Old Man Rafi: (slow, certain) "We honor the places that gave us tools, not just the tools themselves. If you erase where we listen, what do we pass on? Not everything that looks like safety is a blessing. I've seen promises that wore fine suits and left after the party."

Mayor Lynette folds her arms; the decision already in her calendar. She looks at you, then at Cass, then at the town like a judge who has to weigh more than law.

Mayor Lynette: "We don't have indefinite time. A political window closes fast. Either we present a plan that looks decisive, or we risk losing funding entirely. Mira, if you hold us up, what then?"

You inhale. The town leans on you in all the ways that don't make sound. Tessa shows up beneath your arm — cheeks flushed, eyes bright and worried.

Tessa Kestrel: (quick) "We need jobs, Mira. People are freaking out. If there's a way to get work now—"

You catch the tremor in her voice; it's not only personal but generational. Her hands twist, practical and impatient. You want to steady her. You want to promise the promise she needs.

Cass steps closer, lowering his voice as if to make the offer intimate rather than campaign rhetoric.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (softening) "We can be partners. Funding now, rebuilding faster. I'll sit on oversight committees, take community input seriously. Let me prove it — let us start with the wharf, the storage sheds. We can protect the harbor."

Jonah answers before you can — he isn't waiting for proof, only for the possibility that your town will be turned into a case study.

Jonah Reyes: "And if they cut corners? If the firm changes priorities? What then? Who answers for the livelihoods those decisions break? I won't sign us over to a promise wrapped in press releases."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Then build the checks in. We make them legally binding. We put your people on the boards. We write it so it can't be walked away from."

Mayor Lynette: (decisive) "Those clauses are negotiable in a contract, but the public wants action. Standing in the way risks everything."

The afternoon light shifts; a hard edge of silver cuts the clouds. You feel the tide of the conversation rising — not loud, but inexorable. You'd planned on mediation; now it feels like the town's grammar is being re-punctuated in the space between breaths.

[Sound: Your phone buzzes; Dr. Saira N'Goma calling]
[Visual: You step under a tilted awning; rain picks up to a steady drizzle]

You take the call. Her voice is both warm and brisk across the line.

Dr. Saira N'Goma: "Mira — I heard the tail end. There's a grant window opening, but it's small and it expects a community-led design. I can pull strings to get us in, but we need a coherent plan and a set of deliverables they trust."

You: "How fast?"

Dr. Saira N'Goma: "Weeks, not months. Mayor Cole will love the optics of a fast solution, but this grants route buys local control. You'll need project leads, measurable milestones, and proof the work is community-run. Also—" (she pauses, a clinical softness in her voice) "—political windows are fickle. If Lynette thinks Cass's plan looks better publicly, the grant could go to someone who already has a ribbon-cutting photo."

You imagine grant language: deliverables, community seats, measurable marsh restoration metrics. Saira always cuts to what can be done, not what should be wished.

You: "I'll organize a pilot. A marsh restoration near Old Pier. Labor by locals. Measure sediment deposition and fish nursery recovery. Can you back it in the grant paperwork?"

Dr. Saira N'Goma: (encouraging) "Yes. But you have to move quickly and publicly. And Mira — be clear about what you need from the town. Don't hide the costs; people respect honesty."

Her words are a small scaffold beneath the usual weight. It feels possible, practical, and slow enough to honor the place. Your chest loosens a fraction.

Jonah overhears the name of the restoration plan in your reply and a soft smile ghosts his face — not victory, but recognition.

Jonah Reyes: "That's the kind of thing I'd get behind. Let us show what our hands can do. If we can prove it works, we've got bargaining power."

Cass watches, fingers pressed together like he is smoothing a wrinkle in the world. There's something almost wistful in how he studies your face when you speak — as if he's measuring how much of you he can afford.

Tessa looks at the notebook, then at you.

Tessa Kestrel: (steady) "If we do the marsh, can we promise work? Even temporary? People need roofs and money."

You: (honest) "I can't promise every roof, but we can build phased labor plans. Local crews first, stipends for apprentices, a public accountability board. We make jobs part of the deliverable."

Old Man Rafi raps his cane softly against a piling and the sound is an old metronome.

Old Man Rafi: "Names matter. If you call it restoration, call it by the right names. If you call it a bailout, we'll remember. Will you honor the memory of what was here — teach the children what the marsh did for us — or will you let those stories be paved over in concrete?"

You feel the old scar of the storm press into your ribs. Your guilt is a low, steady pressure: the one time you didn't act quickly enough, the house that slipped from your aunt's clasp. That memory has taught you caution and also given you the ache that quick action can numb.

You close your eyes for a moment and see the ridge of light breaking through a cloud — not a blinding beam, but a thin, decisive line. It warms the pendant against your sternum.

Inside your head, decisions stack like weather maps. Each has a code: speed, trust, integrity. Each will rearrange the town's sentences. Each will rearrange your heart.

You open your eyes. Around you, faces are waiting — Jonah's steady, Cass's controlled, Tessa's expectant, Rafi's world-weary. Dr. Saira's voice is still in your ear: urgent, possible. Mayor Lynette's calendar is ready. The town's grammar is about to change.

You feel something settle — not certainty, but clarity. Enough light to choose by.

You consider your options, the pulls each will exert on the town and the people you love. The decision will be public and it will be personal. For a second, everything is quiet enough for you to hear the tide — patient, inevitable, asking only that someone decide which shore to build toward.
