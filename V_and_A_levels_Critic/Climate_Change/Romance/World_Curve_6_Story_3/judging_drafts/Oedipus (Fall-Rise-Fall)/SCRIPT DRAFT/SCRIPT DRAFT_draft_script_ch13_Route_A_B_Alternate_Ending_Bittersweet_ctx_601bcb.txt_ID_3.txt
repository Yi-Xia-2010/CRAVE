[Scene: Docks — Night; Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Sky shredded with quick, low clouds; rain starts in thin sheets; the harbor becomes a dark, moving mirror]
[Music: High, frantic strings; percussion like pounding rain]
[Sound: Loud wind, the wild slap of waves, frantic shouts]

The quartering gale arrives abrupt as a snapped tether. You barely have time to register the cold before the town is a score of urgent noises: alarms, radio chatter, the clanging of shutters. Someone cries out about the sluice feed — the mixed system, where old channels meet new gates, is a seam under stress.

Mayor Lynette (through a megaphone, strained): "Evacuate the slips! Move inland! Secure the garden—"

Annotation panels on Cass's tablet flash warnings. You are moving before thought finishes forming; the adrenaline in your veins sings like a struck wire. This is the crescendo you feared: complexity vs. chaos.

Cass (shouting over the wind): "Override the automated feed! Manual override now — the sensors are spiking false positives!"

Jonah (yelling back): "The manual wheel on Gate Seven's seized! The crew's trying to free it!"

You run toward the eastern sluice where the new gates meet a crooked, older channel. The rain drives into your face; salt and metal and the sharp electric tang of ozone prick your lungs. Your hands find the wheel; it turns halfway and refuses. Somewhere down the line, a sluice that had been scheduled for a full inspection last month — and wasn't — fails to seat.

You find yourself at the edge of the ruined slip less than an hour later. The water is a latticed mess of oars, tarpaulins, and floating crates. Your dreams of the hybrid — the careful blend of community oversight and engineering — fracture into a chorus of failures: deferred maintenance, misread clauses, outside actors gaming contract language to shift costs, a funding delay that should have been a short line of red but became an empty shelf.

Cass arrives beside you, rain plastering his coat. For a moment he looks younger than the circuits of a thousand models would allow; his eyes are raw, accusing, and utterly human.

Cass (voice close, brittle): "We designed redundancies. We tested for this pattern. I—"

Cass: "We did what we could. We... I thought—"

You: "It wasn't just models." (Your voice is a ragged edge.) "We signed a promise that we'd keep community seats. We said we'd make this town's knowledge part of the system."

You find your mouth forming a dozen answers and none of them solid enough to move a tide. The arousal through your body hums with an almost unbearable speed — shock, grief, fury, the need to make amends that cannot fix what has been lost. You close your eyes. The coral pendant against your sternum is slick with rain; it feels heavier than brass, heavier than memory. Gratitude coexists with an invitation to grief — two tides colliding.

Jonah (a crack in his voice): "Your best wasn't theirs."

You open your mouth to answer and find every possible response folding into one hard, almost physical decision point: how to repair what's broken — not only the docks, but the trust, the contracts, the sequences that allowed this to happen.

Your role had been to translate lived knowledge into enforceable obligation. You succeeded in writing the sentence. You failed in making the sentence the same thing as a living repair culture. The truth of that sits between the two men and the people around you like a low, humming wire.

You inhale the salt and iron and rain and make yourself listen to the noise — the music of loss that will take a long time to unwrite.
