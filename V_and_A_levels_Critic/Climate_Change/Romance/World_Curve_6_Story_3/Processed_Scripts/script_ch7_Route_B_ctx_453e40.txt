You sit at the metal table and let the models spin. Cassian's renderings are clinical and sure: cross-sections that take account of wave refraction, load-bearing pylons spaced on scientifically determined intervals, panels that lock together like vertebrae. The color palette is businesslike — slate, steel, careful cyan highlights. Yet when you zoom into the nodes where human use interacts with engineered barrier, your stomach tightens. Cultural access points. Boat launches. The old rope-and-shell moorings.

Your phone buzzes with a string of congratulatory texts. Tessa sends an excited emoji. Old Man Rafi leaves a voicemail that sounds like prayer and a promise. Jonah's message is a single word: Watch.

Cass lingers in the doorway for a long beat before he steps inside. He leaves the heavy door open so the rain sings across the turret and his shoulders look narrower in the shelter of the lab light.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [hesitant]: "You look tired."

Mira Kestrel: "We all do. It's been a long—"

Cassian "Cass" Romano [interrupting]: "—year. I know. I wasn't sure you'd take the partnership."

Your throat tightens. Partnership had sounded like a clean meeting of minds; it has turned into a knot of concessions, promises, and the private arithmetic of what you could risk.

Mira Kestrel: "I took it because I want people to be safe, Cass. Because I think engineering can help if it listens."

Cass moves to the table and, unprompted, sets his hand flat on the tablet screen. The gentle press distorts the holograph for a second. His fingers are cool, precise.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [low]: "I didn't come here to bulldoze your community. I came because —" (he laughs, a brittle sound) "— because I was tired of seeing places like this disappear. And because I kept thinking, if we don't build a model town that doesn't lose what makes it human, who will?"

His eyes flick to yours, steel-gray made soft by the lamplight. His lapel pin glints; for the first time, the polish seems like armor rather than identity.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [voice small]: "I'm afraid, Mira. Of failing. Of being the reason another family loses what they can't replace."

The confession lands like a struck bell. It's raw and immediate and unknown to the polished man the town sees at meetings.

You didn't expect to hear fear from someone who sells certainty. It shifts the whole geometry between you. Fear means stakes; fear means he can be wrong. Fear means he understands loss.

Mira Kestrel: "What are you afraid of, really? Losing your career? Being blamed? Or losing people?"

Cass looks away, fingers tracing a line on the laminated map as if mapping an unknown fault.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "All of it. But mostly —" (he meets your gaze) "— being the person who promised a fix and watched it fail."

The rhythm of the instruments fills the silence. Dr. Saira's voice hums in your memory: adapt with humility. Cass's admission is the humility of a man who has practiced control and found it brittle.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [softer]: "I want to do this with you, Mira. Not over you."

The difference between with and over is a small word with big teeth. You let the word settle between you like a shared map.

Jonah Reyes stands on the docks, a single silhouette against the milling activity. The rope-and-shell bracelet circles his wrist; his knuckles whiten around the rope. He doesn't call out. He watches, a quiet storm contained in amber eyes.

Mira Kestrel [soft]: "We can make choices that don't write off people's work."

Cass leans back, exhausted and defiantly vulnerable.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I get up in the morning thinking about which bolt needs a different grade. I go to sleep thinking about whether a wrong calculation could make me the man who took a town's pier. I don't know how to be anything but useful."

Mira Kestrel: "You're not just useful, Cass. You're learning to share the work."

He laughs, a short, wet sound, and then something loosens — a confession not tied to engineering details.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [faltering]: "Sometimes I think the thing I want most is to stop being so sure about everything. To be allowed to be wrong in private and still wake up to try again."

You share the lighthouse's small meals. You talk about a future where pylons don't have to be knives across the water but a seam that people can work around.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [near dawn, voice hushed]: "Will you sleep, Mira? Properly?"

Mira Kestrel: "Tonight? Maybe. Maybe because I feel like we're moving somewhere."

He meets your gaze with an unguarded look and, for a breath, the world reduces to the hum of instruments, the harbor beyond the glass, and the feeling that you might be building more than walls together.

A sense of fragile victory settles across your shoulders like a well-fitting coat. It's not total. It won't be quiet. But for the first time in months you sleep.
