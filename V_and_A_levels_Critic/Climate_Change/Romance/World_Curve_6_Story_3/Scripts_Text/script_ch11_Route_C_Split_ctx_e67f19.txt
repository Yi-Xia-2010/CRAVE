[Scene: Municipal Hall | Midday]
[Visual: Conference room packed; holographic waterline models float above the table; windows show the flattened saltmarsh beyond]
[Sound: Murmured arguments, the cough of a stylist's pen; a projector whirs]
[Music: Percussive rhythm—very high, heartbeat-fast]

Mayor Lynette stands with the practiced poise of someone used to bad weather and press conferences. Her coat is immaculate; her face is a map of calculation. Behind her, a legal team—tablet screens bleached white with spreadsheets—paces.

Mayor Lynette: "These allegations freeze the grant transfers. That's correct. Until audits clear this, our supplier contracts are on hold."

You hear the room intake as if the air itself were a tide going out; for some, this is relief, for others, a knife to livelihoods. The freeze is both a victory and a risk—accountability, yes; delay, also true.

Dr. Saira steps forward, carrying a stack of printouts like offerings. She looks at you, then at the crowd, her face a study in focused warmth.

Dr. Saira [warm]: "We need to make the models speak in plain terms. People here don't need the math—they need the implications. If a wall goes up here, this marsh loses exchange, and these families lose seasonal beds and breeding grounds. I can show that with a map anyone can read."

Mira Kestrel: "Translate it with me. Use the same language we used at the lighthouse—tide-lines, not tech—so a fisherman or a schoolkid grasps the trade-offs."

Dr. Saira [quiet]: "We will not let numbers be the only argument. We will show what they mean."

Jonah Reyes moves through the room like an undertow—solid, rough-handed, earnest. His rope-and-shell bracelet catches the light when he gestures, a band of small, stubborn history.

Jonah Reyes: "I'm organizing assemblies at the docks, at the washer's shed, at the bar. People need the contract language in plain speech. If the state-funded teams won't talk in straight terms, we will. We can't let lawyers speak for fishermen."

You and Jonah Reyes exchange a glance—long enough to carry the weight of shared pasts and the ache of mutual burdens. There's a riot of feelings you don't name. You are allies; you are possible more; you are complicated, threaded through a public fight.

Jonah Reyes [low]: "We'll make flyers. I'll read the clauses out loud if I have to. You're not doing this alone."

Mira Kestrel: "You never do. But I'm asking you to hold people to the facts without turning them into fear. We need accountability that builds, not collapses, the town."

Jonah Reyes [half-smile]: "Isn't that what you always do? Make hard things sound like home repairs?"

A dry laugh escapes you both, the kind that is more relief than humor.

The door opens and in walks a man in a slate trench coat: Cassian "Cass" Romano. The subpoena press badge hangs at his belt like a brand. He stands at the defendant's table, small in the fluorescence, a professional in a town of weathered hands. His presence pulls half the room's attention like a magnet.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I came because they summoned me. I will answer what I can."

Mayor Lynette [tight]: "We want transparency. We'll start with the procurement timelines. Mr. Romano, your firm provided the tender documents?"

Cassian "Cass" Romano's jaw is taut. When he speaks, his voice is precise, but you see in the small, involuntary tremor of his fingers that this is not just corporate theater—this is fear of failing coastlines and the professionals who depend on him.

Cassian "Cass" Romano [measured]: "Yes. We did. The timelines were aggressive; the funding windows narrower than the safeguards warranted. We pushed for pilot steps but were met with political pressure to proceed quickly."

A low murmur ripples—some skeptical, some relieved. Then a voice from the gallery, sharp and raw.

Audience Member: "So you knew it was rushed and still kept quiet? You jeopardized our nets!"

Cassian "Cass" Romano [hands lifted]: "No. I argued for more tests. I—" His voice cracks, a brief human fissure. "I was told a delay would cost the town the grant entirely. We were told to deliver results, to keep the momentum."

You watch him closely. The corporate sheen can't mask a small, human thing: he looks exhausted and afraid, trying to juggle deadlines and people's lives. Seeing him this way does not absolve him, but it reshapes him from villain into someone culpable and fraught. Your empathy does not betray your demand for answers; it sharpens it.

Mayor Lynette [snapping]: "We will subpoena the internal memos. All communications will be produced."

Legal assistants begin to pass folders like rescue lines. The pace accelerates—auditors, subpoenas, mouths forming new strategies. Grants sit, frozen, the town's ledger suspended between exposure and need.

Tessa bursts in, cheeks flushed with the vigor of someone who sleeps little and runs on conviction.

Tessa: "This is all fine, but we can't wait for slow tribunals while the tide comes in. We need sandbags, we need marsh bundles, we need labor. If we don't mobilize fast, audits won't matter."

You turn to her. She is a live wire—impatience braided with moral clarity. Her eyes track yours like a challenge and a plea.

Mira Kestrel: "We won't wait blind. We will organize response teams. But mobilizing funds without oversight could hand the same power to the wrong hands."

Tessa: "Then redirect funds. Put money directly into community coffers, into the people's hands. They know how to stitch a dike. They know the marsh. We don't need middlemen who hide clauses."

Her urgency is a flare. Around her, a few heads nod; other faces tighten at the risk.

Mayor Lynette [coolly]: "Redirecting public funds without due process opens us to legal reprisal and logistical chaos. There are procurement rules for a reason."

Dr. Saira [interjecting]: "What we can do is design transparent oversight—community stewards as signatories. We can rework the clauses so that money disburses with milestones that the town votes on."

The room erupts into a dozen overlapping voices—fear, hope, accusation, strategy. The air tastes like iron and coffee. The models hum on the table like small weather systems. You feel adrenaline knotting into a blade inside. This is your win—proof the system needed cleaning—but the stakes are higher than ever.

The garden has always been a place for repair and small miracles. Tonight it is a tribunal of neighbors and a church of possibility. Pots of coffee steam on a folding table. An old banner reads "OUR COAST, OUR FUTURE" in letters made from scavenged paint. Faces glow in candlelight—Old Man Rafi, a crew of fishers, council members, volunteers and young people clutching clipboards.

You stand before lantern-light and the crowd hushes. Their eyes on you feel like a thousand small tides converging. Somewhere in that breath you remember why you did this. Accountability was never an abstract principle; it was the name of people whose boats have been rebuilt too many times, whose ancestral nets are now fragile instruments on an altered shore.

Jonah Reyes is at your side, voice low but firm, coordinating a small group to explain clause-by-clause in plain language. He speaks as if each sentence were a rope to be passed around the circle.

Jonah Reyes: "Clause one—release conditions. If oversight is not met, funding stops. Clause two—procurement steps. We vote on signatories from the community. I'm forming reading groups at dawn."

You hear the crowd ask practical questions—Who signs? Who audits the auditors? Who breaks ties with firms that hide memos? The debate grows precise; people are hungry for real mechanisms.

Cassian "Cass" Romano arrives quietly, not at the center but along the hedge, his multifunction watch dimly reflecting lantern light. He looks smaller here than at the hall, like a man stripped of formal defenses. He waits for a pause before stepping forward.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "I—" He clears his throat. "I don't hold all the answers, but I want transparency as much as anyone here. My firm made mistakes. I'm willing to reopen contracts if the town sets clear oversight that we must meet."

A murmur—skeptical, and then, by degrees, curious. He looks at you when he says this. You cannot read his future in his face, only the urgency in his tone. There is regret there, but also a real offer.

Tessa paces in short, purposeful arcs, her hands rake-scraped from volunteer work, her voice hard but not unkind.

Tessa: "Talk is not action. We need immediate control of funds to start the marsh teams. We have the labor, the knowledge. We can show at scale what community management looks like."

Old Man Rafi leans on his cane, his voice dry as driftwood.

Old Man Rafi: "Young ones, listen. We fix and we sing while we do. We do not give our shore away to promise alone. But smart hands and smart plans together—that is the only thing that keeps a town."

The night hums with competing urgencies—mobilize now, but with safeguards; bind corporate accountability, but do not paralyze response; pursue legal cancellations and risk service gaps, or rework contracts and accept slow, thorned compromise.

In your pocket, your notebook is heavy. Your coral pendant warms with the press of your hand. Jonah Reyes's bracelet brushes your hip when he steps closer; Cassian "Cass" Romano's watch blinks a notification and then goes dark. The artifacts feel like a living triage—history, loyalty, engineering—each offering a different future for the town and for your heart.

Your chest thrums. The crowd leans forward as if the decision itself will do the tidal work.
