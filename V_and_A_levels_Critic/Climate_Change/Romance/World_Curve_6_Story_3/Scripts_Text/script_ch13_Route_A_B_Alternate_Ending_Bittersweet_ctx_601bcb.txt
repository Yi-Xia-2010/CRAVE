The language you fought for is being read like a suggestion by people who never smelled salt in their lungs.

You feel the old pattern: systems built to save can fail where they meet the human and the weather. Energy ramps up; townsfolk are called into emergency shifts, faxes and keys and trucks moving like veins in a body roused for battle.

You try the manual release. It sticks. You push until the muscles in your forearms scream. Nearby, a subcontractor technician fumbles with the override, eyes darting to a remote terminal whose connection blinks. Your coral pendant thuds against your chest as you lunge — the metal buckles a half-inch and the sluice gives.

For a breath the water moves where it should. You make choices in seconds that will shadow your name for years. You flip switches, reroute flows, shout into wind that seems to swallow words. A dozen families—names you learned by heart during the Repair Garden project—are down to nets and memories tonight.

Tessa moves like a shadow among people, offering wrapped tea and blankets, hands that never stop. Old Man Rafi sits on a splintered crate and hums some old, low sound that is less song than a letting go. Jonah Reyes: "You did what you could. You have to live with the rest."

He turns and walks away along the wet planks, his shoulders hunched against wind and memory. You watch him go until the bend of the harbor hides him from view.

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "If you'll have me. Professionally. We can revise maintenance language, create redundancy audits that are not so easily gamed, fund local stewardship positions directly tied to payroll. I can fight for that in my firm. I will."

His voice is not devoid of emotion; you hear a tiredness that might be hope. You nod because there is work to be done. Because something has to answer for what happened. Because the town needs people to keep building.

Mira Kestrel: "Do it. But listen to them. Listen to Old Man Rafi. To Tessa. To the crews."

Cassian "Cass" Romano [measured]: "I will. I promise to make the oversight enforceable."

You clasp your pendant until the coral warms between your fingers. The future is a ledger you will spend the rest of your life balancing. There will be new policies, new contracts, new maps. In the days that follow there are hearings, emergency funds, the making of a municipal labor trust to staff maintenance positions, and tenacity that looks like bureaucratic paperwork. The town rebuilds in the slow, indecent way of people who have no other option. You sign amendments and you stand at memorials for small boats, and your hands learn the texture of both paper and rope.

Cassian "Cass" Romano keeps his steady, professional presence, and sometimes you catch the way his eyes find yours across a table and hold, not with the old promise, but with the new: of partnership that is competent, necessary, and not the same as love.

You will not say that everything is well. You will not write in flowery terms about redemption. The ending here is not an ending of full mending. It is a ledger closed for now, accounts balanced on a painful arithmetic: lives saved, livelihoods lost, friendships altered. You stand and breathe the sea and the smoke and the diesel and let the weight of all you tried and all that failed wash over you.
