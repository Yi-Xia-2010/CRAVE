[Scene: Brinehaven Boardwalk | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Salt-streaked planks stretching toward the harbor; gulls wheel lazily above]
[Sound: Low gull calls, a distant gullocking of waves, a far-off tinny newsreel playing on a portable radio]
[Music: Sparse, slow piano with a single sustained note beneath]

You step onto the boardwalk and the town greets you with the same smell it always has—old rope, wet wood, and rain tucked into the seams. Salt flakes in your hair catch at the light; the coral pendant at your throat knocks once, twice against the bone of your collar. You feel its weight like a question.

Your eyes trace the pilings. Where they were smooth when you left, time and tide have written lines across them—scar tissue from storms, laddered marks where people hauled boats free. You catalog them the way you always have: tilt, wear, recent gouges. It is the engineer's habit even when your hands belong to memory.

Tessa is waiting with that grin that tries to be everything. She comes at you like a tide—sudden and earnest—wrapping freckled arms around you. The hug is awkward in the way of people who want to fix what they cannot rename.

Tessa: "You finally made it home, Mira. You look—" (searching for the right word) "—like the ocean's been at you."

You let her hold you for a heartbeat longer than you expect. Her hair smells faintly of detergent and seaweed and the kind of optimism that has kept this town from falling apart before.

Mira Kestrel: "Long drive. The tide charts were unhelpfully poetic." (your voice is quieter than you planned)

Tessa pulls back and examines your jacket with exaggerated seriousness, fingers on the embroidered tide lines. "They're real. You really embroidered tide lines." She laughs, then sobers. "We—there's a thing in the square. Old Man Rafi's on the bench. He says your notebook smells like the past."

Old Man Rafi watches from his usual bench, the blue bead braided into his beard catching the light like a small, stubborn star. He nods once—a slow, fossilized motion that reads like both welcome and weighing. His presence is a fulcrum: approval and history wrapped in a coat that's seen worse winters than most of Brinehaven remember.

Old Man Rafi: "You come back with salt in your hair and stories in your pockets, Mira. Sit. Tell us what you see." (his voice cracks the way old wood does; steady, inevitable)

You shift the leather notebook under your arm. The cover is pitted with tide marks—the same ones you traced as a child and the same ones that make your fingers cling in memory. You are careful with the pages; their edges hold the weight of a dozen storms.

Mayor Lynette (on-screen): "—a comprehensive defense package, immediate funding for hardened docks, and a public-private partnership to secure Brinehaven's future—"

The words feel antiseptic, shaped to camera angles and press releases. They fill the air with a different kind of weight: promises that sound like answers even before anyone has asked the right questions. You watch the mayor's mouth and count the syllables of a solution that might skip over the people it will rearrange.

Mira Kestrel: (quietly) "Comprehensive. That's the word they love."

Old Man Rafi's jaw tightens. He spits out a small, resigned sound that might be laughter or might be a sigh.

Old Man Rafi: "They make plans like they're drawing maps, but the maps forget the people living on the edges."

There is a slow agreement in the boardwalk's murmurs. You can feel the town modulating its breathing to the mayor's cadence—hope, suspicion, a tired readiness to fight.

[Scene: Research Lighthouse | Early Evening]
[Visual: Weather-stained tower, instruments glowing through fogged glass]
[Sound: Low mechanical hum, distant rain percussion; the sea is a steady, patient metronome]
[Music: Sparse strings, held notes]

You walk the lighthouse path with the notebook heavier now, as if filled with answers you have yet to earn. Inside, instruments sheave light across the walls and make the world visible in thin lines and grids. The hum of the lab is a small, constant prayer: sensors, buoys, a chorus of data that refuses to lie.

You set fingers to tide-etched marks on the leather cover—tracing, measuring, memorizing. Each nick is a story; you know the house that sank behind one, the boat that came back with a new scar because of another. You think of the house you couldn't save and the way memory keeps replaying the moment at low volume, as if trying to teach you resilience by repetition.

Mira Kestrel [to the humming instruments]: "Tell me something I don't already know."

The instruments do not answer in words. They pulse in numbers and tilt, patient and indifferent. Dr. Saira isn't here; you have to be both scientist and interpreter for a town that needs more than translation.

Mira Kestrel: "If we build too heavy, we lose the shoreline we love. If we do nothing, we lose the people who keep it alive." (your voice is a soft argument to no one and everyone)

[Scene: Docks | Twilight]
[Visual: Docks lit by a single lamplight, water like a black sheet reflecting smudged sky]
[Sound: Boards creaking underfoot, rope slackening, a fisherman's laughter caught and gone]
[Music: A single, sustained cello note]

Down at the docks, Jonah Reyes waits in a sweater stiff with salt. He leans against a piling the way someone leans on memory. When he sees you, his amber-brown eyes are soft but carefully guarded, like a harbor after the storm has passed but before salvage begins.

Jonah Reyes: "You look like you walked out of a lab and into my nightmares." (he tries for humor; it winds up smaller)

Mira Kestrel: (half-smile) "I brought charts and nightmares. Which do you want first?"

Jonah Reyes: (he shifts, then laughs, but it doesn't reach his eyes) "Charts, definitely. Nightmares take too long to explain." (he taps the planks between you) "We fixed a roof last week. Mostly. Rafi brought extra tarps."

Mira Kestrel: "You always make do." (there's something like pride in your voice, and shame layered under it)

Jonah Reyes: "We do what we can. But there's talk—people heard Mayor Lynette on the radio. Some of them are glad; some of them are scared." (he studies you) "You gonna tell us what you think or make us guess?"

His question hangs like a buoy: necessary, buoyant, and tethered to deeper currents. You want to answer honestly. You also know that honesty can reconfigure loyalties like storm-driven tides.

Jonah Reyes: (after a pause) "You coming back for good, Mira? Or is this—" (he gestures at the town) "—a mission with an expiration date?"

Mira Kestrel: (your throat tightens) "I came back to help. I don't know how long that takes."

Jonah Reyes: "That's not an answer." (he isn't angry; he's looking for a promise he doesn't expect to get)

Before you can shape the reply, a different sound moves through the air: the measured click of a tablet, the soft footfall of someone used to carrying their work like a cloak. Cassian "Cass" Romano appears under the lamplight, trench coat still catching flecks of sea spray, his tablet held at rest where maps and models live.

[Visual: Cassian "Cass" Romano steps into the lamplight, tablet glowing with topographic lines]
[Sound: Tablet chime, soft electronic rustle]

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (gentle, professional) "Mira. Jonah. It's good to find you both here." (he smiles—precise, practiced)

Jonah Reyes: (bristles, but keeps it low) "You're up late, Mister Engineer."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Late is a relative term when the tide keeps you honest." (he thumbs his tablet, bringing a faint projection of waterlines that dissolve like smoke) "I've been running scenarios. There are options that preserve most livelihoods if we act quickly."

Jonah Reyes: "And if we let you act quickly—what are we losing?" (his voice is blunt because time has taught him bluntness)

Cassian "Cass" Romano: "Nothing out of faith. We model landform changes; we design with the cultural footprint minimized." (his eyes flick to you, steady) "But it needs coordination and investment."

Mira Kestrel: (listening to the two ways the town could be saved) "Coordination and investment are words. They stand for checks, strings, and people who don't show the scars. How do we keep what matters intact without locking out the people who built it?"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (a beat) "You can ask that and still accept support. It's not a contradiction."

Jonah Reyes: "Sometimes it is." (the single syllable tastes like accusation and memory)

The three of you circle the same worry in different dialects: practical care, communal memory, and engineered promise. The harbor holds your breath with you.

Mira Kestrel: (the old guilt presses—a tightened rope around your sternum—the house you couldn't save flickers in the back of your eyes) "I can't promise anything I haven't measured. And I won't sign away the things that make this place ours."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (softly) "Nor would I. I want a town that survives for the right reasons."

Jonah Reyes: (there's a question in his look—more complicated than welcome or fealty) "And what about you, Mira? Which 'right reason' do you choose?"

Mira Kestrel: (your answer is a small, private thing that won't fit into policy papers or talk-show segments) "I choose the people. I choose not to let them be the collateral for a neat solution."

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (his face flickers—respect, irritation, something unreadable) "Then let's talk about how to do that. Together, if you'll let me."

Jonah Reyes: (his jaw sets) "Together how, Cass? With companies that own half the materials and politicians who want a ribbon-cutting before winter?"

Cassian "Cass" Romano: (measured) "With transparency. With community seats at planning tables. With adaptive design."

The town around you murmurs; the moon edges into the sky and the water keeps its slow, indifferent score. Your notebook rests against your ribs like a tiny, tide-scented weight. Everything you have done so far has been preparation for this peculiar, slow kind of war.

You know, as though someone has hushed the world and left only you listening, that what you say next will be measured against memory and fear and hope. The knot in your chest tightens, not in panic, but in the quiet, heavy way grief collects.

You inhale. You taste salt and pressed tea and the lingering tang of recycled promises.

You must speak.
