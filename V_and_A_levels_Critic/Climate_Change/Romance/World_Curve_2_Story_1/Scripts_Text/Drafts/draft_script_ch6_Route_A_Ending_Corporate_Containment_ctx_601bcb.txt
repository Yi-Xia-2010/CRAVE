### DRAFT
# Route: Route A (Ending: Corporate Containment)


[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse | Morning]
[Visual: Warm amber light through steamed panes; condensation threads like tiny rivers]
[Music: Low, restless strings under a single insistent piano]
[Sound: Rain tapping the glass; distant gull calls]

You press your palms into the damp soil of a tray of salicornia seedlings and let the familiar grit anchor you. You chose this. You chose the slow, stubborn work—coir logs, transplanted reeds, hands-on community days—after the plaza. The choice still sits in your throat like a pebble.

Etta moves between the benches with the ease of someone who knows every inch of humidity by heart. Her hands are an atlas; she taps a seedling, then cups her chin and looks up at you with those warm hazel eyes that have outlived worse weather than this.

Etta: "You held the line today, Ari. You made people look at the marsh again."
You: "I tried. We made noise. It wasn't enough for some of them."

You keep your voice even because if you let it crack it will sound like pleading.

Etta: (frowning) "Nothing about saving something worth keeping is easy. You know that. But it shouldn't have to be... legal wrangling and mud-slicked days alone."
You: "I know. I just—" You stop, because there is nothing to finish the sentence with that doesn't either sound like an excuse or a confession. "We planted three more sills last week. The sensors logged improved deposition at the north bend. The math is there."

Etta: "Maps and numbers are fat with possibility until somebody pulls a contract from a folder and signs it in a meeting room."

Her words land like a cold gust. You feel the greenhouse's warmth press against your damp jacket like a fragile cloak.

You: "Then we make them remember the people. We go door to door. We show the footage. We—"
Etta: (soft but firm) "—You rest when your hands tremble. You are not stronger than everyone."

You: "I don't know how to stop. If I stop—"

Etta steps closer. She sets a gentle hand over your knuckles, soil smudging both your palms into the same brown print.

Etta: "Then find the people who will hold it with you. Not all of it has to be on your back. Promise me you'll let someone else carry a corner."

You want to promise, because she deserves that softness. But promise feels like surrender tonight.

Etta watches you make the small decision. She does not scold. She only nudges the tray so that two fragile stems sit straight.

Etta: "When it all collapses, it does it quickly. If you're going to keep building, you have to be ready for the crash."

The greenhouse feels like the last safe room. You close your eyes to the faint perfume of citrus and peat and, for a heartbeat, imagine the estuary as it was before walls and contracts: wide, breathing marsh water, crickets in the eelgrass.

[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Fractured piers, orange construction lights, damp banners flapping; a thin line of press cameras]
[Music: Percussive staccato, rising in tempo; low brass undercutting like a storm drum]
[Sound: A sudden, loud CRASH—wood and water, the howl of a blowhole; shouts; a horn repeatedly blasting]

You run down the harbor path, boots throwing spray, the camera heavy in your tote. News vans line the cracked quay—Mara's recorder is probably on someone's lap—and contractors in high-visibility vests cordon off a new scar: a chunk of the old jetty ripped away where the sea found a weak seam. The water is hungry. It took the jetty like a fist through paper.

Crowds cluster—neighbors, investors, a lone crew from Elio's firm with matte lanyards and the calm faces of people who have prepared talking points. A cameraman steps back to get a wide shot; the red tally light blinks hungry.

Jun is there, coat soaked through, jaw set. He spots you and falls into step before you can reach him.

Jun: "They put the heavy plates out last week and it held. Now this. They say it's 'anomalous surge' on the feed. But we saw the water creep higher by the hour."
You: "You okay?"
Jun: (bitter) "My boat list's swollen. The family house took a corner of the mudroom last night. The council said 'monitoring'—'monitoring' when my keel lists."

The contractor with the matte lanyard is arguing in low, precise tones with a council aide. Every word he uses is measured to sound inevitable: 'public safety', 'asset protection', 'escalation'. The press leans forward for the phrases the public wants to hear.

From across the breakwater, Elio stands with his hands folded, coat buttoned, the slick calm of someone who reads maps rather than feelings. When he looks at you, his gaze misses warmth by a narrow margin—it calculates.

Elio: (to camera) "This breach is the exact kind of failure we modeled. With a focused containment program and the right infrastructure, we prevent loss of life and secure economic continuity."
You: (to yourself) "Modeling doesn't feel a child's schoolhouse under water."

You lift your camera instinctively. Documenting feels like a defiance: each frame a ledger against erasure. A contractor tries to block the angle and you step around, pulse skittering. The crowd is small but the volume is large—voices forming a clatter that presses on your chest.

The contractor glances away, and someone cheers. For a moment you have the floor." || Choice B ("Stay back and record the breach, let the footage speak") -> Reaction: "You keep steady fingers and let the camera do the yelling. The footage will speak when mouths are louder than hearts. You capture the water swallowing the jetty; the image is a small, terrible proof."]

Mateo appears at your shoulder like a half-sure promise. He's soaked, hair curling at his temple, field notebook clutched like a talisman. His satchel is open—sensors, a half-assembled buoy bobbing with a camera. He looks exhausted and furious in equal measure.

Mateo: "We could have installed the modular anchors sooner. We had the design. The supply chain—"
You: "You could have—" The accusation burns. You taste salt and iron.
Mateo: (quiet) "I could have pushed for more funding. I could have gone to Elio then, taken the money, done it fast. But you know why I didn't."

You want to say he chose pride. Instead you say: "We needed scale, and you walked away from a lot of promises."

Mateo: "I tried to keep it community-first. I still do. But the breach—look at it." He jerks his chin toward the broken jetty. "Councils respond to spectacle. Investors respond to footage. Elio's legal team is already drafting a brief. They're not slow."

Your camera clicks, heart clicking with it. The harbor tastes like iron. You can feel the moment compressing—each second a compressed spring.

[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Evening]
[Visual: The plaza slick and reflective; council chamber windows glow; protest banners droop, some abandoned; workers erect a temporary stage]
[Music: Full, urgent orchestra—strings in fevered tremolo, brass closing like a fist]
[Sound: Construction hammers begin in the distance; a low cheering and crying mix—disparate, impossible to separate]

The council meeting stretches into the early evening. The chamber smells like varnish and wet coats. People sit jammed together; phones glow like trapped stars. On the stage, Councilor Rosa Menendez reads the motion aloud with practiced gravity. Elio watches from the front row, the firm's documents in a neat stack beside him. His presence is the quiet of someone who has rehearsed grief and market share until the two blend.

Councilor Rosa: "Because of the breach and the rising risk to life, this council will vote to approve the emergency containment contract with Satori Infrastructure Consortium, to commence immediate construction of seawalls and managed barriers. The contract will also authorize a program of voluntary buyouts for at-risk properties."

A rustle runs through the room. 'Voluntary' sounds like a hedge. For some, it will feel like a cord with a noose on the other end.

Etta squeezes your hand. Her face is a map of grief; elders have ways of expecting losses but not of accepting them with anything near peace.

You: (low) "They're going to call it safety."
Etta: "They will call it safety and make it mandatory for some. They will offer checks and counselling and relocation assistance. There will be a glossy brochure."

Jun stands, and his voice is a rope thrown into the gale.

Jun: "My family's seafood quota was the only thing that kept them afloat. If they buy us out, who buys the quay? Who keeps the boats? Who pays rent when the harbor is privatized?"
Elio: (careful) "The program includes provisions for local contractors and transition funds. Our models project..."

You want to scream. Instead you listen to the measured words that will bury people in legalese.

Mateo steps forward then, and the room softens around him because he has that strange gravity that is not showy but is real. He smells like wet rubber and salt and disappointment. He carries a thin envelope—one of the grant letters the firm is offering to innovators who partner on scaled deployments.

Mateo: "I… I was offered a grant. It would let me build at scale, reach farther than our little arrays. I—" His words snag. He looks past the council, at you. "I partnered with them briefly in the lab to test materials. I left when it became clear they wanted to own the systems. They asked me to come back, but only under terms that tie deployment to their contract."

You: (voice rising) "So you take the leash so the dogs will fetch? You stitch our tools to their walls and call it saving us all?"
Mateo: (flinches) "It's not like that. I can make the buoys work with their infrastructure. I can keep them open-source inside the deployment. I can argue for community oversight from the inside."

Your mouth tastes like all the times you swallowed your fear. You have built arguments for the marsh. You've flipped charts, shown sensor arrays, walked people through the slow recovery. Now the room's temperature drops to a thin point: the price of leverage.

You: "Inside their terms is inside their control, Mateo. Do you think they'll let us keep the access? The rights? The view?"
Mateo: (silent for a long beat) "I don't know," he admits—small, terrible honesty. "But I can't watch the town dissolve while we argue about perfection."

Etta: (voice breaking) "Perfection was never the point, Mateo. Stewardship was. You can't wage stewardship from a shareholder report."

Someone bangs on the chamber's wood. The vote is called.

Clerks tally paper slips. Councilor Rosa's face is lined; she has to make policy with the echoes of mothers and mortgages pressing in.

Councilor Rosa: "All in favor?"

A single, heavy clap—the sound of the plank falling. The motion passes with a narrow majority.

Outside, the plaza erupts into a chaotic bloom of sound: a mix of cheering, curses, the metallic hush of contract printers, and the thunder of equipment being mobilized. Heavy trucks begin to roll toward the waterfront like an invading tide. Workers in corporate jackets hand out pamphlets with neat diagrams of 'controlled safety zones' and town maps with shaded buyout regions.

Mateo stands in the doorway for a moment, the thin envelope limp in his hand. He turns to you.

Mateo: (soft) "I can put pressure from the inside. I can fight for clauses—local labor, nonexclusive access, open data. If I get pulled into the project, I can make sure it's not total privatization."
You: "And if they use your clauses as a fig leaf? If they sign the last clause and hand the waterfront to people in suits?"
Mateo: "Then I try to make the leaf stronger. I—" He chokes on the rest. "I want to help as many people as I can, Ari."

You: "Help them how? By giving them less to complain about? By taking their view away with a wall they can't see beyond?"

His eyes burn with a fatigue you both share. He touches the frayed strap of the satchel he carries—your satchel—once, as if remembering how many field days you've had together.

Mateo: "I will argue, I will fight in meetings, I will publish the sensor data and demand oversight. I can't promise the world. I can promise to try."

The promise tastes thin. You know what 'try' has meant between you both: late nights, whispered plans, projects that almost made it. You also know what 'try' means when the tide is taking the living room floor.

You: (quiet) "You can try from here or from there. I'm choosing here, Mateo. I'm choosing to stay with the people who have already lost everything but each other."

Mateo: "And you're choosing to risk everything staying put."

You stare at him until the words stop being about policy and become about windows, about who gets to see the morning light. Behind them, the first modular sheets of a temporary barrier are craned into place at the far end of the harbor—metal glinting under a gray sky, a line that will forever redraw where the town stops and the sea begins.

Jun collapses to his knees when the buyout announcement hits the loudspeakers. Etta wraps her arms around him. The crowd fractures: some begin to pack boxes, others shout into phones, and some stand stone-still with the look of people who have been given a map to nowhere.

You walk back to the greenhouse because it is where things still grow, and you need to feel something living under your fingers.

[Visual: The greenhouse glass fogs with your breath; outside, a far-off clang of steel on steel]
[Music: Single bowed violin—high, thin, then cut; silence for a heartbeat; followed by a long, low organ tone]

You set a seedling in the tray and press the soil around it with the gentleness of someone burying a small, stubborn hope. Your hands are covered in the same mud Jun's family has found in their cellar, and in it you read out small histories: the summers your father taught you the tide lines, the first seedlings you planted with Etta, the nights Mateo rewired a sensor until dawn.

You think of all the faces that will be folded into relocation packets, of the dinners that will no longer smell of clam and sea-salt because the docks will be fenced, because access will cost a fee. You think of the children who will never learn the rhythms of the estuary by getting their socks wet.

You: (to yourself) "We chose this. Or we were chosen. Either way, they built the wall."

A long, steady rain follows like punctuation. The seawall will hold storms. It will keep salt out of basements and secure pipelines and ensure that businesses with deep pockets remain. But a different tide rises: one of lost porches, closed shutters, the invisible making of neighborhoods that no longer meet the water.

You lift the camera to your eye one last time and take a photo—of Etta's greenhouse, backlit and tiny against the metallic line of the new barrier—an image that will not change the vote but will mark the place it was interrupted.

Mateo appears at the greenhouse door again, the envelope gone. He is quieter now, as if something heavier has been placed inside him.

Mateo: "They offered me the lead on the buoy field. Under contract, yes. But it comes with funding to scale, and a clause to keep certain bits open. I argued. I got concessions. It isn't everything."

You: "You took it."
Mateo: "I took what I thought would let me reach more people faster. I thought—" He swallows. "I thought if I could bring the tech, even under theirs, we could keep community access, keep an open data standard, let our sensors feed everyone's knowledge."

You: "You thought like an engineer and not like a neighbor."

He flinches. This time he doesn't defend himself with models. He looks like a man who has chosen a line and then watched it cut something out.

Mateo: "I can't look at those children on Jun's block and not do what I can with the means offered."

You search his face for the version of him you fell in love with—the inventor who rebuilt a motor from a fisherman's scrap. It's there, but it's shadowed.

You: "You might make things better for the many by making them worse for the few who are left."

Mateo: "I know."

There's a tightness in your chest that is less anger and more the raw exposure of loss. The arousal that has run like an electric current since the breach culminates now in a sharp, aching clarity: you are not in the same trench anymore.

Etta: (eyes wet, voice steady) "Neither of you chose this alone. The town split before today. Today it just formalized the seam."

Jun: "They put a price on the harbor. They took the view and called it 'protection.' They'll mark us as 'at-risk properties' and hand us a check like a wound dressing."

You: "Then we document. We make the stories loud enough to be inconvenient. If they drew a line, we make sure the people who were erased are not forgotten."

Mateo: "I will publish the sensor logs. I will demand oversight committees. I will fight the most predatory clauses—at every board meeting, in every press release."

You watch the first concrete slabs lower into place on the far pier. People clap—some out of relief, some out of gratitude, some because their mortgage is safe now. Others curse and walk away.

You: (internally) "I stayed. I stayed and we failed in the way that matters—the way that doesn't get into models. I stayed and watched our town be drafted into someone else's plan."

A cold, terrible clarity settles: the estuary is held, but the social map of Tidebridge is not. Whole families will leave. Porches will be boarded. The harbor will be functional and empty in a way that haunts.

You drop to a bench, palms on your knees, and a single, incoherent laugh cracks out of you—half sob, half release. The greenhouse hums around you like a wound dressing.

Etta sits on the bench opposite and folds her hands in her lap.

Etta: "You'll have choices to make now, Ari. Nothing about this is over. Some of it you can still fight—contracts, clauses, oversight. Some of it you can't. You can help the people who go, or you can be the person who stays with those who don't."

You: "I don't know which is harder."

Etta: (gently) "They're both hard. You'll learn to carry the weight. It won't make it lighter."

You pick up your camera and thumb through the images—a harbor torn, Jun on his knees, Elio's polite grimace, a line of concrete across the horizon, Mateo's hands unsteady as he folds the envelope into his pocket. Each frame is an accusation and a record.

Outside, the first sections of the seawall kiss the water. A mechanized sound grows, metal against metal, and a line of men in corporate jackets unroll a banner: 'Protection. Stability. Progress.' The words are neat and terrible.

You stand. Your legs feel like someone else's. You have stayed. You have watched your town change into something safer and lonelier.

You: (final, to no one) "We kept the tide out. We lost the shore."

[Music: A single, long chord fades into a hollow wind; then silence]
[Visual: The greenhouse lamps glow, small and defiant against the engineered line on the horizon]

[ENDING]
