[Scene: Municipal Hall — Council Chamber | Late Afternoon, overcast]
[Visual: Long wooden table under high windows streaked with rain; a circle of folding chairs, laptops open, paper stacks. A digital map of Tidebridge glows on the projector, tide lines pulsing red.]
[Music: Tense, relentless percussion — a heartbeat under strings]
[Sound: Muffled rain on the roof; the distant rumble of an incoming storm, like a giant breath]

You step back into the chamber with the flyer folded in your palm. Your shoulders still remember the weight of negotiation—its methodical pressure, the small, surgical cruelty of clauses. The projector throws Elio's firm's timeline across the wall: fast-build dates and milestone bars that slide like teeth toward the next season. Each bar reads like an ultimatum.

You set your tote down. The room smells of cold coffee and wet raincoats and the iron edge of municipal paper. Your palms are damp; the coral strap of your camera brushes your hip like a talisman. You are supposed to shepherd the opposition voices into language the committee can actually use—but this room is a pressure cooker now. Decisions here will become deeds; deeds will become levees, bonds, buyouts.

Etta is already at the table, hands folded over a dog-eared binder of maps and covenants. Her face is the same precise map of stubbornness you have always trusted. Jun leans against the radiator, fishing-stiff, jaw clenched. Councilor Rosa sits at the chair’s head, forehead pinched, an official pad open. Elio stands by the projector with a remote, his shadow long on the map; Mateo sits a little apart, a neat line of distance between you, notebook closed like a barrier.

Etta: "We have to put protective covenants into any contract that touches the shoreline. Public access must stay—we've seen what privatization does. It smooths over people's names with line items."

You hear the heat in her voice that always comes when a memory of loss meets legal language. She is not asking for what feels ideal—she is carving a shield with words.

Elio: (cool) "Covenants add months, Ari. They add legal review, indemnities—time we don't have. Our models show a window to prevent catastrophic erosion if construction starts before the big swells. Delay equals risk."

He clicks the remote. The map shivers: projected surge-intervals, probable breach points. There is a bluntness to his data—useful, clinical—and in the same breath it reads like a threat wrapped in a rescue package.

Councilor Rosa: (measured) "We need to weigh both. The town can't float between proposals while the weather calendar keeps moving forward. My vote will find whatever balance keeps people safe."

Your throat tightens. "Balance" sounds like a place to hide clauses that hurt.

You clear your voice. You are assigned to fold the opposition's concerns into the committee language. That means turning Etta's conviction into something the legal team will accept, translating Jun's fear into mitigation language, and—most treacherous—bringing Mateo's prototypes and credibility without letting them be used to steamroll the community voice.

You inhale brine-thick air you can almost taste through closed windows. The storm feels like a hand on the town's shoulder, nudging hard.

Mateo: (soft, guarded) "I don't want us to be the people who refuse help and then spend the next three storms rebuilding the same losses. Engineering can be quick and measured—if we accept the baseline contract now, we can get buoy lines out, get sensors live. That buys time."

He looks at you, and for a second—no easy read. His face holds something compressed: worry, the calculation of someone who has tried to compromise with institutions before and found the cost. He does not attack Etta; he speaks as if between two technical points. But his distance from you is palpable, a deliberate professional cool that stings because it wasn't always like that.

You: (quiet) "Buying time isn't the same as protecting people. What's in the baseline saves infrastructure but doesn't stop displacement. You know the clauses—"

Elio: (interrupting) "We can add community programs as riders. Resilience bonds, relocation assistance—financial instruments to reduce harm. The town's stability is a function of capital flow. Our proposal is pragmatic."

Etta's hand slams flat on the binder. "Riders are lipstick on a levy. Once access and land titles are signed away, you can't un-sign them because a ledger told you it was cheaper."

Jun: (rough, urgent) "My father's shed was on the buyout list of one of those 'assistance' plans. They told him he could sign and get three months' rent to move; they didn't tell him he wouldn't be allowed back past the fence. We don't get to measure souls in bonds."

The room tightens. Councilor Rosa rubs her temple. You can hear the rain move from patter to a low staccato; the storm's rhythm enters the chamber like a new, implacable council member.

You feel the committee's eyes drift to you—Etta's pleading, Jun's flare of anger, Rosa's searching for compromise, Elio's calm insistence, Mateo's careful distance. You are the translator, the battering-ram, the seam that has to hold.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Early Evening — wind picking up]
[Visual: Boardwalks roll like ribbon into glassy tidal pools; newly planted coir logs sag against surging waterlines. The sky bruises purple-gray; the light goes thin.]
[Sound: Wind whips, a gull cries sharp then is cut off; the slosh of water against pilings grows louder]
[Music: Strings spike with dissonant chords, tempo quickens]

The committee follows to the flats because some truths need to be walked to. The mud smells of peat and salt and a memory that is not yet dead. Etta walks the edge with a cane and a certainty you feel in your bones—the land speaks to her, and it is private in the way only old grief can be.

Etta: "These sills keep our seedbeds from being shredded. If they pull the access strips when they build, we lose the families who lay claim here. A covenant keeps that in law."

Elio stoops, touching a coir log with gloved fingers. "This is important work. We can integrate living shorelines with engineered breaks. But the timeline is non-negotiable if we want to prevent immediate failure."

Mateo walks beside you, his hands buried deep in his jacket pockets. Up close, his jaw is set but not closed—there's friction, a man trying to reconcile two necessities. He says, quietly, "Ari—if we insist on too many legal entanglements, their lawyers will bottle us. They'll write around the language with loopholes. We need enforceability, not poetry."

You want to tell him: enforceability is what Etta wants. You want to tell him: poetry is what keeps us human. Instead you ask, steadying your voice against the wind, "Can we demand escrows or third-party oversight?"

Mateo: "We can. But oversight needs resources—sustained funding, an audit clause, and local enforcement capacity. Which the town doesn't currently have spelled out."

Etta: (to Mateo) "You were in their world. You know how they count."

Mateo: (wincing) "I tried to make them count differently once. They hired consultants to run the show, not to surrender control. I left because I couldn't sign my grandmother's name to empty promises."

He gives you a look that is complicated and not easy to read—enough history to sting, enough restraint to hold it. You want to ask whether he left because he believed in tech or because he couldn't stomach the trade-offs. You don't. The wind takes words and spits them out.

Jun: (low) "If they move fast, my nets might sit behind a fence. My kids won't learn the flats. Is that worth a roof that might keep them warm?"

The gull returns, shrieking in the gust. Somewhere farther out, a buoy blinks — a small, lonely promise.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Night — sheltered, humid]
[Visual: Amber light, steaming panes; seedlings pressed to glass. The smell of damp soil is close and warm; hanging bulbs glow like bioluminescent promises.]
[Sound: Rain hammers the glass in steady sheets; inside, the drip of a small leak and low, urgent conversation]
[Music: Low, aching cello — sorrow knitted with urgency]

You and Etta end here, because people need counsel rooted in soil. Inside the greenhouse is a different kind of pressure—warm and fragrant instead of electric. Etta sets a steaming mug in your palms like an offering you didn't know you'd asked for.

Etta: (soft) "Covenants are not romantic. They're stitches in the law. They'll require text that an attorney will not love. But they're the only thing that keeps a person from being a line item."

You: "What if we ask for an escrow fund and a third-party oversight board? Force money and enforcement into the contract."

Etta: "Then make it iron. Make it citizen-ratified. Get signatures. Make the oversight local, not a name in their ledger. And—" she taps your wrist like she can tap the future—"you have to be ready for them to threaten to leave. People will be scared."

You think of the flyer folded into your pocket, the projectors, the ledger-bars on the map. You think of Mateo's notebook, of his silence that is not exactly neutral. You think of Jun's nets and Etta's hands and Councilor Rosa's balancing act. You think of the town as not only people but histories stacked into porches and picnics and funeral pyres.

Jun arrives soaked, and when he speaks his voice is raw with an urgency that tightens every muscle in you.

Jun: "There are rumors on the docks. They're saying the firm will pull out if we force covenants. They're telling folks they'll lose access to insurance if they 'delay'—they're whispering moving money and legal threats. People are scared enough to sign anything."

Your stomach drops. Fear is contagious; you feel the town's outline wobble. The greenhouse hums like a held note. You want to be brave; instead you are a sieve for other people's terror.

Etta: (decisive) "Fear is the stock they sell. We can burn it with public pressure. Or we can write clauses that make it impossible to fence people out and force oversight that can't be ignored."

You realize the options compress themselves: bind and slow; accept and move fast; or expose and delay. Each path has a cost—some immediate, some deferred and sharp.

Mateo's measured voice enters the threshold. He looks drained in a way that is not purely professional anymore. He says, quietly, eyes not fully meeting yours, "If you push them too hard, they will go to someone else. Then the town's only defense is gone. I'm telling you because I don't want you to be surprised when they step away."

His words land like stones into water—circular ripples of consequence. You are between empathy for Mateo's long-bore strategy and the fire of people who have already lost small things again and again.

You want to make everyone safe and keep everyone whole. Those are mutually incompatible in a room with deadlines and lawyers.

Your chest feels like a tide pool that is being emptied by a storm drain. Panic presses behind your ribs. This is the moment you were warned about—the one where your sense of responsibility meets the impossible.

You stand, hand cold on the mug, and hear the rain as a drumroll.
