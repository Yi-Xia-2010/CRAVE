[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Morning]
[Visual: Wide shot of soggy estuary, flagging tape and neat rows of coir logs; overcast sky hangs low]
[Sound: Squelch of boots in mud, muffled voices, an occasional cry of a gull]
[Music: Tense, propulsive string ostinato]

You stand at the edge of the flats where you announced your choice yesterday—the words still settling into the bones of the town. People have come back with shovels, bright vests, and a kind of brittle hope. The collective tent is a ragged sunburst against gray; Etta moves beneath it like a steady flame, handing out gloves.

The peat smells sharp and alive: brine, old seaweed, the iron tang of wet earth. Every breath fills you with it. Your camera is in your tote but you are worse than ready to record now; you are already halfway through the work, pulled by the rhythm of muscles and instruction. It feels urgent—because it is.

Etta: "Ari—start with the coir line. Anchor to the old piling there. We need that sill to hold until the reeds take."

You: "Got it." Your voice is taut but steady. You shoulder a coir log; it weighs more than you expected. The fibers rasp against your palms—cutting, honest. Etta's hands find the other end and the world narrows to two pairs of hands in mud.

Etta's hands are a map of seasons: knuckled, stained, small scars that run like rivers. She is not theatrical—she moves and instructs and remembers how long things take. Her voice is low and matter-of-fact, the kind of authority that is tired but refuses to be cynical.

Etta: (kneeling) "We plant the eelgrass in plugs. Not too deep. Let the tide do the sorting. Little hands steady the shoots; don't rush them, or they'll tear. The marsh is patient but doesn't forgive bluffing."

You: "We don't bluff," you say, and the words land harder than you'd planned. Somewhere behind your ribs the old guilt flares—the promise you made to keep everyone here—and you translate it into a list of tasks: plant, secure, monitor.

Jun: (from a few meters over, breathless) "Ari—these stakes. Lay them tighter. If the last storm taught us anything, it's that half-measures don't keep people dry."

You walk toward Jun. He squints through the drizzle, sleeves up, hands like carved wood from years of hauling nets. He is the town's weathered ledger—what remains of old livelihoods. His distrust of anything that smells corporate is a taut cord; you feel the slack pull if you so much as mention complicated machinery.

Jun: "They're offering cash, by the way. Came by at dawn. Clean shirts, polite smiles. Say they can pay debts. Heard any of you take it?"

You: (you hesitate, sorting the rage from the worry) "No. Not us. Not the collective."

Jun: "Not everyone thinks like us."

You: "I know." Your tone is softer than you want. The space between knowing and convincing feels like a cliff.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: Amber light through steamed glass; rows of trays, the soft drip of condensation]
[Sound: Low hum of an electric heater, tinny radio under a clatter of plant pots]
[Music: A quieter, anxious piano motif]

You duck into the greenhouse to grab more trays. The warmth presses on your damp jacket like a promise. Seedlings press against fogged panes; the scent here is sweeter—soil and citrus and something green that tastes like possibility. Mateo is bent over a workbench, a tangle of sensors, braided wires, and a battered buoy hull.

Mateo: (glancing up) "You're early." His smile is thin, threadbare around the edges. He wipes his hands on his shirt. "I knew you would be."

You: "You said you'd help with sensor integration. I didn't expect you to bring half the lab."

Mateo: "Scale isn't the first word I would use for this," he says, lifting a sensor that blinks reluctantly. "But if we can get tide and turbidity readings—coupled with your planting maps—Etta can argue with numbers she can take to the council."

You: (hope and suspicion braided tight) "Numbers help. But they're not the ground under our feet."

Mateo: (softly) "I know. I know where you stand. I'm here because—" He pauses, fingers fiddling with an ocean-salted bolt. "—because the buoys can tell us if the sill is holding or if we need a repair before the next storm. Not to replace the marsh, but to buy time."

Mateo's hands are deft; the sensors fit together with a craftsman's patience. He wants to be useful and to avoid the spectacle of confrontation. You read that restraint in the way he avoids Elio's name.

You: "We need them deployed at dusk. Can you have a baseline reading by tomorrow?"

Mateo: "I'll run a net and a sample tonight." He hesitates, then adds: "But, Ari—this won't scale fast. You know that. If this becomes the town's plan alone, we could be busy for years while the rest of the coast goes corporate."

You: "Then we'll be busy for years." Your words are an edge. "Better than leaving."

Mateo: (looking down) "I don't disagree." He looks up, more direct now. "Just... promise me you won't make our only argument 'we tried because we felt like it.' We'll need allies who believe the data, too."

You: "I promise to use both," you say. "Hands and numbers. Stories and charts."

Mateo: "Good. Then pass me another tray. These connectors are stubborn."

The two of you bend over wiring, a small island of concentration in the humid air. Your shoulders brush; for a beat the world narrows to solder and soft breathing. He doesn't tell you about the funding line he considered taking last month, the one that would have put him back into the city's orbit. You don't ask. The omission is a quiet bargain.

[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats / Tidebridge Harbor Edge | Afternoon]
[Visual: Half the volunteers slogging back and forth; the harbor beyond thrums with small boats and a sleek corporate launch tied at the endpier]
[Sound: Waves slapping pilings, muffled conversation, the precise click of a briefcase closing]
[Music: Rhythmic percussion ratchets up—heartbeat tempo]

Back on the flats the tide climbs; the mud darkens, swallowing boots in slow, sticky gulps. Your muscles ache with the neat, relentless exhaustion of honest labor. Someone laughs—a short, nervous sound. You catch Jun's hand as he steadies a buoy.

Jun: (through grit) "They're at the pier. Fancy jackets, polite lies. They brought those folding chairs like they expect a picnic."

You feel the line in your chest tighten. The harbor smells different—diesel, a hint of bleach, a sweetness of new paper. Corporate color flags snap in the wind.

A small group of tidy people in understated suits disembark. The lead woman wears a badge and a smile perfected for cameras. Behind her someone opens a thin, glossy folder and the pages glisten like bait.

Corporate Liaison: (approaching, hands slightly out) "Morning. We're from Seastone Partners. We have an offer we'd like to present to Tidebridge homeowners."

You move forward because silence would be doom. There are cameras recording—Mara's, perhaps, at the back of the crowd. Mateo stops, sensors silent in his pack. Etta wipes her palms on her apron and plants her feet in the peat.

You: "We have our work scheduled. We're not for sale."

Corporate Liaison: (eyes flick to the crowd) "We understand community sentiment. We're offering buyouts to those who prefer a managed option. We can provide relocation assistance, debt relief, guaranteed compensation."

Etta: (voice like gravel and river) "You will not buy what a town is. Money can't replace a childhood in a house that leans to the bay. It builds condos elsewhere."

Corporate Liaison: "Mrs. Hargrove, we only want to offer choices. We aren't forcing anyone. We're proposing a solution that protects infrastructure and livelihoods."

Etta: "We have a solution in progress. It's not magical. It is hard, and communal, and slow. It's not yours to commodify."

A dozen voices come in at once—some pleading, some shouting. You feel the air vibrate with a dozen versions of desperation. The liaison's smile thins. She steps forward, the folder like a shield.

Corporate Liaison: (slightly colder) "We also have legal teams ready to streamline projects of necessary public interest. Waiting for grassroots processes risks funding windows. We can expedite protections."

A small, efficient man in a dark coat—your stomach names him before your mind does—hands a slim envelope to a town official who looks like she hasn't slept this week. The name on the return label is familiar: a legal firm used by Elio's partners. Your throat goes dry.

Jun: (speaking for half the mud) "You can't just come in and decide we've been slow. We were here before your reports."

Corporate Liaison: "No one is deciding for you. We're offering a way to keep essential services running."

You: (your voice tight) "By buying waterfront access and fencing off the bay? By moving our schools inland where our kids won't know how to swim?"

Corporate Liaison: (softly, almost rehearsed) "We will preserve access where possible. But safety must be prioritized."

Mateo steps beside you, his presence a thin bolster. He looks at the liaison as if trying to measure the truth in the smile.

Mateo: "There's data showing that living shorelines reduce erosion and maintain habitat. You can sponsor that at scale. Help the community do it."

Corporate Liaison: (brows knitting) "Scaling local methods is expensive and uncertain. Our sponsors want predictable outcomes."

Etta: (leaning in, voice like a riverbank) "And what of the people who can't risk uncertainty? They'll have the money to leave. The rest will be left to improvise. Is that how you measure responsibility?"

The liaison's hand tightens around her folder. There's a pause—too long. The air tastes metallic. Someone from the crowd starts to weep quietly. You feel your chest constrict with a hundred small accusations you've been carrying since you were younger than most of these neighbors.

Then a lawyer steps forward and in a tone that is both polite and cold announces that Elio's firm has filed notice of intent to begin public-private protective works unless the town consents to negotiations within seven days. It's legalese but it lands like a blow: a deadline, a lever, an ultimatum. The volunteers around you slump as if someone closed a lid over the work.

You: (you hear your voice in a small, hollow place) "You can't rush consent. You can't—"

Corporate Liaison: (cutting) "We're presenting options. The clock isn't our invention."

Jun: (yelling then, raw) "You give deadlines to people whose roofs keep leaking and expect them to listen? Who will decide whose house is 'essential'?"

Etta: "We decide together."

The argument spirals. Threats of injunctions, murmured promises of funding, the rustle of contracts. People shout. A teenager launches a pebble that skitters and disappears into the mud. Someone drops a coir log; it sinks with a dull, terrible finality. Your hands go numb—cold, salt, adrenaline.

You feel every choice you made yesterday thrum through you like a live wire. The town is split along the seams you swore you'd hold together. Mateo's sensors blink faintly in the background—little blue lights that feel suddenly absurd.

Your phone buzzes in your pocket—an incoming message that you don't allow yourself to read until you can step aside. The crowd swells against you; a woman near you wipes snot on the back of her hand and tells you about her mortgage default. A man you know from the docks stares at the liaison like a man watching a knife.

Your internal voice presses forward, a thin, relentless whisper: you did this to give people a chance, not to put them at risk. But the whisper is cramped by how fast legal levers move and how easy it is to make a safe-sounding offer to someone with a roof that leaks.
