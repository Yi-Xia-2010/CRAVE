[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor — South Low Block | Evening, Storm Approaching]

You stand on a platform, soaked through, camera tucked inside your jacket though you barely remember taking it with you. The town is a collection of breaths and prayers. Etta is beside you—older, smaller, a jaw set like flint. Jun is at the front, hands clenched, the Torres family under a tarp with a packed duffel. A cluster of fishermen argue with a uniformed maintenance crew whose badges read the firm's name.

Someone near the water shouts that a surge is arriving early. The buoys—your hope—begin transmitting jittering data: a spike in incoming energy, a pressure front in the channel. Mateo’s voice is in your ear, clipped, technical, human: "We can adjust damping remotely—reduce the stiffness curve now—I'm sending the command."

You feel the moment like a hinge. For a breath it seems to tilt toward salvation: the buoys drink the swell, flex just so, the water’s energy diffused into the system. The south low block shudders but holds.

Then a new alarm. One of the maintenance nodes reports unauthorized access attempts—the company’s remote ops, perhaps pushed by an executive in a far-off control room, is trying to overwrite the damping algorithm. A hard ethics wedge arrives in an instant.

Mateo: (shouting) "No—don't override! That will lock out local control. We lose the fallback!" (His fingers fly across the lab's console through a live feed. He’s fighting with people who have the resources to win on a corporate clock.)

On the pier, Jun steps forward, voice raised so everyone can hear: "If I can't bring my nets through the corridor without a fee and a form, then what good is saving this block? You give us protection and you charge us for the right to exist in it?"

An engineer in a branded jacket replies, voice carefully rehearsed: "Maintenance costs are allocated to ensure system integrity. There are operational burdens—"

Jun: "You call our lives 'operational burdens'?"

The rain is louder than anything. The town is a small animal on the edge of something larger and indifferent.

Mateo’s face contorts—pride, grief, fury. He turns to the live feed and punches a command into the console that forces a manual reassertion of local control. For an instant the feed flickers; the remote attempt fails. The south line flexes and sings in a way that suggests survival.

The crowd's reaction is a hairline split between relief and further fury. Some cheer, some curse. The company rep steps back and calls headquarters. You can imagine the legal calls already starting: breach of contract, unauthorized intervention. Your chest becomes a block of ice and lightning.

You: (internal monologue) You wanted to stop more than the water; you wanted to stop the feeling that survival here required selling pieces of yourselves. Jun: (quietly) "I'm taking that offer from my sister up the mainland. Her block is dry. My nets will suffer less there. I'm not staying to pay to fish."

Time has a new weight. The buoys did what they promised physically: several low blocks are not underwater. But the town's social map has been redrawn with ribbons of absence.

Inside the greenhouse you and Mateo work side by side, hands in soil—an odd, holy chore after the chaos of storms and contracts. You plant salicornia plugs along a tray, small green markers of something that might one day be tide-holding matter rather than a news headline.

Etta watches from her stool, a cup of tea steaming in her palms. Jun’s absence is a sore hollow at the edge of the conversation; you reserve a small, private grief for him and the other neighbors who left.

The town is quieter and louder—quieter because some voices are gone, louder because the ones who stayed have to be louder to be heard over the machinery and the new legal frameworks.

Months fold into a new kind of normal. The buoys hum; maintenance scholarships pay a few fishermen’s fees; the oversight board meets and grinds out regulations that are better than nothing. You walk the greenhouse path in the late light, and the town’s noise is a distant shore. Somewhere a child laughs—maybe the Torres child, perhaps a new neighbor. The sound is a thread. You follow it, knowing the fabric is not whole.
