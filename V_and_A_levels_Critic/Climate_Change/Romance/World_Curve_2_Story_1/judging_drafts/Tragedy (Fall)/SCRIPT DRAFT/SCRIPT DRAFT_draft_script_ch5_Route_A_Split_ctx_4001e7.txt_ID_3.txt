[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Dawn — hours after the storm]
[Music: Low cello tremor, irregular percussion building]
[Visual: Wide shot of the breached sill—coir logs torn like frayed rope, black water licking at newly exposed foundations]
[Sound: Wind whipping through reed beds, the distant groan of a municipal pump]

You arrive with the group before the sun clears the low cloud. The flats smell of cold peat and the metallic tang of upended earth. Your boots puddle in salt-sour slurry; each step sinks and sends up a wet, complaining hiss. The seedlings you and volunteers planted only weeks ago lie mashed, their bright flags bent like wilted fists.

Etta stands near the torn edge, hands clenched in the pockets of her apron. She is smaller than the destruction around her but her posture cuts the air like a straight line. Mateo kneels beside a collapsed coir log, gloves slick with mud. Jun's jaw is a hard line; his knuckles work at a rope as though it were a splinter that needs tearing out.

Etta: (voice like gravel wrapped in wool) "They held—until they didn't. The sills were young. They needed time. We needed more hands."

You crouch, fingers numb, and touch a blade of eelgrass pinned under a plank. It slides through your grip, then breaks. In that small give you feel the whole day's failure.

You: "How bad is it?"

Mateo: (breathing fast) "The sill tore along its midsection—wave set exceeded our buffer. If the pumps from the buoy array were live we could have held—at least buy time to reset the logs."

Jun: (spitting the words) "Could've, should've. We keep saying 'give it time' and the tide keeps taking it while someone with money buys the rest of the shore."

A distant vehicle horn—Elio's PR team rolling in, cameras shuttering—carries over the flats like another storm. You can almost taste the press of an audience behind the wind.

[Visual: Elio stepping out of a matte vehicle, coat clean though damp; his eyes narrow, measuring]
[Sound: A portable speaker from Elio’s team—calm, authoritative voice] 

Elio: (even, clinical) "This is proof that incrementalism cannot stand alone. We can reinforce the seawall—fast. We can secure the harbor's critical infrastructure."

Etta's face twists, unreadable for a beat—then she turns back to the damaged sill, the weight of more than one loss in her shoulders.

Etta: (quiet, dense) "Fast is a word that eats neighborhoods and names them 'collateral.'"

The flats feel smaller now, rimmed by the crowd and the cameras, the argument sharpening with each breath. Your throat tightens. Old guilt—about the families you watch over—shoves against your ribs like an animal that will not be tamed.

Mateo: (urgent, hopeful) "I can get the buoy prototypes out faster. If we deploy the buoy network now, patch the grid, we can run pumps at scale—temporary but immediate. It could buy us days."

Elio watches the exchange the way a surgeon watches a heartbeat monitor—calm, interpreting. When he speaks it is precise, a scalpel.

Elio: "Temporary measures delay the inevitable. Our model shows you need a continuous barrier. We can build that in weeks, not months. It will require consolidated access and funding."

The argument snaps like a wire. Around you, people start throwing names—'sellout,' 'reckless,' 'coward.' The hallmarks of a community under pressure. You feel Mateo's hand touch your arm—light, as if to anchor you. In that touch there is warmth and a question.

Mateo: (soft, pressing) "Ari—let me try. If we can power pumps with the buoys we can keep water out of those houses tonight. I can mobilize volunteers and some city students. I need your call."

Etta: (harsh with concern) "And strings? Funding with ladders attached? Remember what happened to other towns. Strings pull and pull until there's nothing left to tie with."

You swallow. The choice forks not just between tactics but between identities—autonomy and fragility, speed and self-determination. Your shoulders weigh like the harbor skyline.

[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor | Midday — the neighborhood after the surge]
[Music: Percussive rhythm upticks; violin shrieks under the wind]
[Visual: Flooded porch of a pale blue house; children's rubber duck floats against a submerged threshold]
[Sound: A child's quiet sob, muffled under rising water; boards creak]

You walk toward the blue house because Jun is already there, sleeves rolled, holding a soaked toy between two fingers. A little boy stands beside him, eyes red, lip trembling—his sneakers caked in silt. Inside, you can see a mattress partly afloat and a picture frame with a family photograph tilted submerged.

Woman's Voice: (strangled) "Lucas's room—it's gone. We packed what we could but—" (she breaks off)

Your camera hangs heavy, but you forget to lift it. You find yourself apologizing—not because it fixes anything but because the muscles need the motion of speech.

You: "I am so sorry. I—"

Woman: (sharply) "Sorry. Sorry doesn't stop it from happening. You said 'we would protect the flats.'"

Her accusation lands with the weight of every morning you spent budgeting seedlings, every night you wrote grant emails, every volunteer hour that felt like a stitch. The guilt you carry spins into a blade.

Mateo steps between you and the woman, voice low but quick.

Mateo: "We can dry the house tonight. If we deploy the buoys and run pumps, we can divert the water. But I need space—funds, hands, and a clear route to place them."

Elio: (over a loudspeaker, control unruffled) "We can guarantee zones of safety if the town cooperates with a centralized plan. It's not just about pumps—it's about infrastructure that lasts."

Jun: (teeth bared) "And who decides which houses get saved? The ones with property deeds? The ones with investors behind them? You come here with your models and your guarantees and think you can pick the town up and set it down somewhere else."

Voices swell—some pleading, some angry, some quiet with an old resignation. You try to summarize, to ask for calm, to call a vote, but the words disintegrate against the tide of emotion.

Mateo: (after either exchange, voice taut) "If we move fast we risk being vulnerable to terms—yes. But if we do nothing, people lose roofs. I keep thinking—if I can buy us a week—"

Etta: (cutting in) "A week buys breath. A week does not buy a future. If we open ourselves to outside leverage now, we may never close it."

The harbor feels like a fault line with voices as tremors. Elio's team lays out a map—zones, blueprints, 'managed relocation' marked in neat, unreadable lines. People point. People cry. Some points on the map have names; some have faces. The process of quantifying loss is a violence you feel in your gut.

You: (to Elio, voice cracked) "What does 'managed' mean for Mr. Rivera's house there? For the school? For the boy with the yellow toy?"

Elio: (measured, as if reciting data) "It means prioritization based on risk and criticality. We will consult. There will be options."

Jun: (spits) "Consultation is a meeting. A meeting is paper. Paper is what the sea eats."

The words strike true. People begin to fork off into small knots—some want to fight the tide with what they have, hands in the mud; others are drawn to the promise of quick infrastructure and a dry bed for tonight. Your skin prickles. The town's fabric is fraying in your hands.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Late afternoon — shelter and strategy collide]
[Music: Discordant piano clusters, thunder rumbling in the distance]
[Visual: Amber light through steamed glass; the scent of wet soil, crushed herbs, and rain]
[Sound: Murmured arguments, the clink of a teacup against a saucer]

Etta's greenhouse is a pocket of warmth; the steam on the panes blurs the harbor into watercolor. Inside, chairs are pulled into a circle. The volunteers who could not sleep cluster with those who need to talk. Mara's recorder ticks. Councilor Rosa waits, composed but exhausted.

You sit opposite Mateo. He stares at his hands—mud under the nails, the field notebook open but empty between his palms. Across from you, Elio leans back against a bench, the corporate badge at his chest catching a stray shaft of light like a tiny, indifferent star.

Etta: (to the room, steady) "We need triage—houses, food, fresh water, and morale. But we also need a strategy that doesn't sell our rights for a hose."

Mara: (pressing) "If Mateo can get portable pumps tonight, and they can be sourced from local funding, we can keep people in place. The question is transparency. Will Mateo lay out the plan publicly? No hidden sponsorships?"

Mateo: (looking at you) "I'll open it. All donors listed. No conditional infrastructure we can't control. I promise that."

Elio: (soft, insistent) "Promises are not infrastructure. If we engage the firm's resources, we can guarantee a plan with enforceable outcomes. It avoids the chaos of patchwork fixes."

Jun: (bitter) "Guarantee whose outcomes? Yours? Mine? Ours?"

The greenhouse hums with tension. You feel it in the damp air—the way the heat presses at the back of your neck, the way your pulse drums against your throat. This is not just technical; it is ethical, threaded with families and histories, with the weight of the boy and his floating duck.

You: (to the room, voice raw) "We cannot make a decision based on fear alone. We cannot let tonight's panic write tomorrow's terms."

Woman in the back: (shouts) "Those terms are already being written while we argue!"

Everyone pivots. The sound is a single animal that may not be trainable. You look at Mateo—his jaw clenches, eyes bright with something like panic and hope. You look at Elio—cool, focused. You look at Etta—an older map of hurt and preservation.

Your throat is a closed door. Every plan feels like a trade. Your instinct is to protect people; your guilt tells you that your protection has limits. The child at the harbor is a compass you cannot un-spin: you think of the wet toy, the small face, the way the boy looked at you as though you had the map to keep everything safe.

The greenhouse fills with people who want answers. You can feel the room lean against you—the leader everyone had, for better or worse. The moment stretches tight as a wire.

[Music: Percussion spikes, a single sustained note—then silence right before a breaking point]

You inhale once, the air thick as stew, and the decision compresses into a single need: act in a way that saves the most people without selling the town's soul—or act in a way that keeps autonomy but risks more loss.
