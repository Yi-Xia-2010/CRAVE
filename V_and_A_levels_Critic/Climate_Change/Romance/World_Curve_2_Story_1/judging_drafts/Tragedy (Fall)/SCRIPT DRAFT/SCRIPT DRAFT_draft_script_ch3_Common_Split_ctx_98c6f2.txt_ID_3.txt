[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Fall — late morning, misting drizzle]
[Visual: Wide shot of the plaza—banners, puddles, a scattering of folding chairs and a hastily built podium; the council chamber windows watch over everything]
[Music: Low, tense strings under a marching percussive rhythm]
[Sound: A megaphone coughs; pamphlets rustle; distant gulls; a steady whisper of rain]

You step into the plaza with your camera tucked into the tote at your side. The fabric presses against the long, collected weight of the morning—notes, seed lists, voices you've shepherded through rooms and hallways. People carve small islands of conversation beneath umbrellas; faces are flush from cold and from argument. The air tastes of salt and wet paper and something metallic—fear dressed as civic process.

Across the square, Etta stands near a table of seedlings, palms deep in soil even with gloves on. Her back is a small, stubborn arch against the weather, a silhouette that says repair by hand. Mateo is near the Wave Lab booth, hunched over his field notebook, sleeves rolled—hands stained with sensor grease and marsh mud, eyes only occasionally meeting yours. Elio’s representatives hover by a coral-colored pop-up whose logo looks untroubled by the weather; they pass out glossy pamphlets with figures that are crisp, certain, wrong in a way that feels like policy spoken in a different language.

Your chest tightens. The plaza is a pressure cooker and you are the metal in its center.

Mara threads through the crowd—recorder at the ready, blue coat soaked at the collar. Jun leans on a post, arms crossed, chin lifted: he looks like someone who has already counted losses and is waiting for the town to give him a reason not to add more.

The first shout goes up—someone at Etta's table has scooped a tray of seedlings to their chest like a child rescued from a swell. The crowd splits and reforms, calls and countercalls layering into a confusing tide of rhetoric. Councilor Rosa appears on the steps, shawl tight at the throat; when she raises her hand the noise sharpens into expectancy. She walks the steps with a gravity that hushes the square in fits.

Councilor Rosa: "People of Tidebridge—thank you for coming. We will begin an advisory vote to guide the council's consultations. This is symbolic but necessary; it will shape our next steps and what we ask partners to consider. Speakers, limit to two minutes. Please be direct, please be truthful."

Her voice carries, but it does not comfort. It informs. The plaza leans forward like a body bracing for impact.

Etta catches your eye, and for a moment something unspoken travels between you—age and history, dirt under nails and a lifetime of watching tides erode promises. She steps up, voice small at first and then gathering like wind.

Etta: "We know how to repair marshes. It's not glamorous. It takes hands and time and patience. Fingers in mud are not numbers on a ledger—" (she glances at the corporate tent)— "they are a leash to this place. We can keep people here with living shorelines and the work that keeps fisheries and gardens alive."

You watch her, the way her shoulders roll the words out like coir logs into place. People cheer and clap—neighbors who remember when the estuary fed them, and those who fear losing their houses more than their pride.

Mateo finds you after she steps down, voice a little too quick, notebook tucked under his arm.

Mateo: "They were handing prototypes to the kids at the market. Small buoy mock-ups with LED cores—people loved them. I thought—" (he stops, looks at you) "I thought we had momentum."

You can hear the undercurrent in his tone: hope braided with worry. There's more at stake than validation; there is funding out there, and if Tidebridge takes a vote that looks like it won't blend tech into ecology, a lot of doors will close.

You: "Momentum alone won't keep tides from taking the pier." (You feel the answer sour on your tongue because it sounds harsher than you mean.)

Mateo: "No, but neither will telling people to wait five years and hope storms don't come back sooner. Hybrid is a compromise that buys us time for marshes to recover."

Your reply is interrupted when Elio arrives—less a man than a human wind that rearranges the crowd. He stands with that professional stillness, the steel-gray eyes taking stock. He nods at Councilor Rosa in a way that acknowledges both duty and the possibility of calculus.

Elio: "Councilor, residents—time is not neutral. Every season without decisive infrastructure increases risk. Our firm proposes a centralized protection plan that stabilizes critical infrastructure quickly. It will require coordinated investment and yes, some difficult choices."

His voice is clean, sharp-edged but not cruel. There is a politeness behind it that makes the argument seem like a necessary emergency measure. You see people nodding—logical people, frightened people, those who crave certainty.

Mara sidles up to you, recorder half-hidden in her palm.

Mara: "They're printing projected loss figures—localized. The firm has a sponsor packet and a list of matching funds. They make a neat story, Ari. It's tempting to believe in a neat story when you are tired."

You: "They're also proposing rules about who gets priority for sheltered housing." (The words hang over the plaza like a shroud.)

Mara: "People will read 'stability' and not read the fine print."

Jun's laugh is a harsh thing that cuts across the polite architecture of this debate.

Jun: "Fine print doesn't feed a family when nets come back empty. I'm not asking for slogans—I built a boat for three years. I need a reason to keep using it."

Etta steps nearer, voice low and urgent.

Etta: "This isn't just numbers and houses. This is where we grew up. Negotiated walls push people out. We have techniques that strengthen what we have—living roots, coir, eelgrass. We can do this without making people strangers to their own streets."

Elio's face is composed but something smaller—something private and human—flickers when Etta says 'without making people strangers.' For a second he is not the strategist; he is a person listening to the sound of loss.

Elio: (softening) "I understand the human cost—that's how I've made the choices I make. But I've also watched places drown when decisions were delayed in the name of sentiment."

There is a clash of logics in the air: hands-on repair versus decisive structural protection, long-term community knowledge versus scalable capital. It is not a new conflict, but here it is compressed into a single pulse: the council's symbolic vote.

You feel the plaza's attention tilt—then settle—on you. No one says your name. They do not have to. The camera strap tucked into your tote is a thin line to the work you've done: photographs, mapping, voices collected and given shape. You are known for speaking truth to tides. For some, you are a beacon. For others, you are a stubborn remnant of the old fight.

The murmur folds into a physical pressure at your back. It makes your shoulders compress. Your mouth goes dry. Your instincts reel through the tactical: back Etta's grassroots plan and solidify the collective; endorse Mateo’s hybrid plan and try to fuse tech and ecology; or step into formal negotiations with Elio and accept centralized measures that promise speed at the cost of autonomy. Each choice teases another set of consequences that you can taste like brine.

Your internal monologue accelerates—brief, sharp tides of thought.

You (internal): I keep thinking of Etta's hands in the mud, Jun's boat, the sound of Mateo's laugh before it falters. I can see the graphs Elio brought—neat curves of reduced flood risk—and feel the pull of money and machines. If I lean one way, I betray someone. If I lean another, I may cost people safety in the short term. I am not ready to hold responsibility for choosing who stays and who goes—but the town looks to me as if I already have.

The plaza's noise swells—chants, a child crying, someone shouting "We won't leave!" The moment feels like an incoming swell, all of you standing on a thin line between water and concrete. Councilor Rosa calls for calm and then for order, and the makeshift podium's microphone squeals when you step toward it on reflex.

Councilor Rosa: "Ari—if you would speak. The square would value your perspective."

You hesitate with your hands on the podium. It is colder than you expect. Someone's rain drips into a puddle at the base and sends a tiny, insistent sound across the wood. You open your mouth; it closes. The decision is already compressing you like a wave against a seawall.

Mateo appears beside you, touch light as he steadies your elbow. The contact is small but it carries a hundred unsaid things: support, fear, and an awkward intimacy that has been stretched thin by public life.

Mateo: (quiet) "Whatever you say, say it with what you want Tidebridge to be. Not only with what we can sell to someone else."

You: (low) "Not only—" (the words crack) "—but also with how we keep people safe now. We can't ask people to gamble with their homes."

Mateo: "Then don't make it a half-measure that invites co-option. We can design for the people who live here. Protection can be community-led and tech-enabled. Let me show you the prototype—" (his voice hardens slightly) "—let me show them what 'hybrid' could be."

Your eyes search his. For a breath you find more than policy there: a hope that the two of you share a bridge that can carry both of you. Then a voice from the crowd cuts across both the tender and the strategic.

Voice in crowd: "Choose, Ari! Don't stand in the middle again!"

You flinch. Middle is no longer neutral—it's a place of accusation. The pressure tightens into a physical thing.

Mara moves up next to you, lowering her recorder.

Mara: "You know their numbers, you know the seed beds—and you also know Mateo's work. The town will read your choice as a signal. If you try to straddle both, people will see it as indecision."

Jun: "And indecision is what costs nets, crops, houses. We need action. Choose a side you can live with, Ari."

The crowd's hum becomes a chorus of urgent, sometimes contradictory needs. Your internal monologue turns faster, a dangerous undertow now.

You (internal): If I back Etta, I honor history and people's lives as they are. If I back Mateo, I try to fuse hope with pragmatism but risk being accused of selling out. If I back Elio, we might get hard infrastructure that means fewer immediate losses but also the slow drowning of shared access, of neighborhoods. My chest aches thinking of what I'll be responsible for.

Your pulse hammers. The rain intensifies into a curtain; speakers’ voices thin. People begin chanting—some for 'roots and hands,' some for 'safe shorelines now.' Elio moves through the crowd with smooth, practiced timing, and when he reaches the front he does something you did not expect: he makes an admission in a quiet voice that carries.

Elio: "I won't pretend my approach is painless. I would be dishonest to say there aren't sacrifices. But I don't want this to be about absolutes. I'm here to negotiate—if Tidebridge wants protections, we can make them fast and transparent. Let us help you make it right."

The plaza interprets 'negotiate' as either balm or betrayal. You can see both readings ripple across faces. The emotional charge is almost unbearable—a mixture of anger, exhaustion, fear, and the need to clutch to any anchor that promises forward motion.

Mateo grips your sleeve. His fingers are damp and warm. He speaks low, every syllable deliberate.

Mateo: "If you choose the collective, I will stand with Etta. If you choose hybrid, I will—" (he stops, swallows) "—I will work to keep it community-first. If you choose to negotiate with Elio, I won't stop you. But I need to know what you want me to risk for."

The line between 'you' and 'us' tightens. You feel the shape of a personal question lodged inside the political one: can your relationship survive a public decision that will be read as a moral stance?

The mic crackles. Councilor Rosa steps forward, paper folding in her hands like a map. Her gaze is frank and patient; it is the kind of face that remembers that governance is ugly and necessary.

Councilor Rosa: "This will be an advisory vote. The options are not the end of the conversation but they will guide our priorities. We ask one brief statement from each of the nominated speakers. Ari—if you will summarize where you stand and why for the people, then we will collect ballots."

The plaza compresses into silence that is loud. Your mouth forms words you have rehearsed and words you have sworn to avoid. There is no safe phrasing left—only the moral grammar of choice.

You inhale shallowly. Every face in the square seems to be a question: Etta’s lined patience, Mateo’s tentative hope, Jun’s blunt need, Mara’s recorder facing you like an inquisition, Elio’s measured expectancy, Councilor Rosa’s procedural calm. The rain drums a staccato on the tents as if amplifying the beat of your heart.

You open your mouth. The decision sits like a brick. The tide rises in your ears and it feels like the entire town is riding it with you.
