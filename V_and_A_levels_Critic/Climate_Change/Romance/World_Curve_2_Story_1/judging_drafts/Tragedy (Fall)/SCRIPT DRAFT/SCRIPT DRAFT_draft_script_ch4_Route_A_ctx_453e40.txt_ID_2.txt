[Scene: Saltmarsh Flats | Dawn]
[Visual: Low gray sky over a ribbon of glassy tidal pools; boards and flagging mark restoration plots]
[Music: Tense, pulsing strings with distant percussion]
[Sound: Wading boots squelch, a gull's cry cut off by wind]

You stand at the edge of the flats with the taste of the plaza still metallic on your tongue. You said Etta's name in public yesterday; you chose the slower, messier work of healing instead of the promise of a quick wall. The crowd's applause is already damp memory—this morning the world smells of wet peat and diesel from a distant tug, and the slate sky looks like something that could split open any minute.

Your camera hangs against your chest, coral strap cold under your collarbone. You're not here for pictures today, not really. You're here because the town needs arms that know how to bind coir logs and palms that can lift saplings without smashing them. You inhale. The salt in the air tastes like a promise and a threat at once.

[Visual: Close-up on Etta's hands as she ties knot after knot]
Etta: "Good. You got people out." (She rubs her palms together, a fine silt falling into the hollow of her hands.) "The Flats won't build themselves."

You: "We got more than I expected." (You try for light and catch only a tiredness that makes your voice thin.) "Fifty volunteers at the last count. Jun's bringing the boat. Mateo's—" Your sentence collapses when Mateo steps up beside you, a pack of sensor modules slung over one shoulder, his hair windblown and eyes too awake.

Mateo: "—bringing the things that beep." (He gives you a small, stubborn smile.) "I said I'd help. But I'm telling you now: sensors are a bridge, not a miracle. If you expect them to stop the sea, you'll be disappointed."

You: "I don't expect miracles. I expect information." (You glance at the flagged plots, at the neat rows of coir logs waiting like mammal ribs along the mud.) "We need to know if the sills are holding and where the currents are cutting."

Mateo: "Information's power only if you can act on it fast. And right now, we don't have fast." (He hesitates, then softer.) "I'm here because you were there for the buoy designs, Ari. I hate being on the outside of this—" He stops, fingers worrying the strap on his satchel.

You: "You're not on the outside, Mateo." (The words want to be steadier than they come.) "You can be here with hands and with what you build."

Mateo: "Then don't make me the only engineer at a shovel." (He gives you a half-laugh that isn't funny.) "I'll help. But I will keep telling people the scale of what we do matters."

Etta watches you both, a moth of light crowning the furrows of her face. She ties another knot with the economy of practiced movements and says, quietly, "Scale can't be the only deciding logic. These hands remember how to sew the land together."

[Sound: The slap of a coir log onto wet earth]
[Visual: Volunteers move into formation; boards creak as they're set into the mud]

You organize the first shift with a list in your head—who stacks the coir, who digs the trenches for transplanted eelgrass, who carries saplings from the nursery. The list is a thin scaffold over everything you feel: hope, fear, the old guilt that whispers if one person's house floods again, it will be your name they say under their breath.

You fold yourself into the rhythm—lift, set, tamp—your rubber boots sinking, the peat giving with a damp surrender. The smell of mud and salt saturates everything: it clings to the back of your mouth and to the hems of your jacket. Jun passes you a shovel; his hands are rough and quick, calluses split into pale crescents.

Jun: "You got your back set up, or you want me to take the heavy end?" (He grins, but it doesn't reach the ache in his eyes.)

You: "I'll take it." (The answer comes out before you think, because you can't not be the one to take more weight.)

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Midmorning]
[Visual: Amber light through steamed panes, rows of seedlings leaning toward the warmth]
[Music: Low, urgent cello under a percussive heartbeat]
[Sound: Water drip, the soft crinkle of plastic seed trays]

You come back to the greenhouse on rotation to grab trays of salicornia and eelgrass plugs. Inside it's warm and smells like citrus-rind compost. Etta moves between the beds like a careful memory.

Etta: "These need shallow roots. Not too deep. They panic if they're buried." (She leans over to show you the right angle.) "You have to treat them like people sometimes—give them air and let them settle."

You: "I'll remind the crew." (You lift a tray; the soil is cool against your palms.) "We've got neighborhood kids helping with the flagging later."

Etta: "Good. Teach them the care before they learn the hard work." She pinches a leaf between two knuckles, inspecting it. "And don't let those councilmen sell them illusions."

You: "Elio's people have started canvassing." (You can't entirely keep the tightness out of your voice.) "There were vans near the Harbor. Pamphlets."

Etta's face tightens. She makes a small sound—half-laugh, half-snout of smoke through clenched teeth. "Of course they do. Money smells like offer and fear both."

[Sound: Footsteps on the boardwalk outside]
[Visual: Mateo's shadow falls across the greenhouse door]

Mateo: "They brought three vans." (He says it like a person naming weather.) "And they left a packet at the center with—" He reaches into his satchel and produces a glossy flyer, edges wet from condensation. "—'compassionate buyouts,' they call it."

You take the flyer without thinking. The paper is slick and clean where your palms are filthy. On it, smiling faces stand next to line-art seawalls and the word "STABILITY" in a font made to sound like a promise.

You: "They think money will unroot people. They don't know what it means to be from here." (You swallow.) "Jun was talking to a neighbor who almost signed."

Mateo: "People are tired, Ari. They have to choose between a known slow struggle and an immediate check. Don't pretend that's an easy call." (He meets your eyes.) "I'm not trying to tell you to step away. I'm trying to tell you to be honest about the cost."

You: "I know the cost." (It isn't enough—your voice softens.) "I just don't know if giving up now is the right kind of honesty."

Mateo: "Then don't let them buy the conversation. Document, measure, show what the marsh does. Let the data be a part of the argument." (He touches the camera strap on your chest, a small, steadying gesture.) "And let people see it with their eyes. Not just the glossy flyer."

You are at the edge of saying something other than strategy—an offer, an admission—but the greenhouse's warmth presses in and your throat tightens. You fold the flyer into your pocket instead of burning it.

[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor | Early Afternoon]
[Visual: Corporate vans parked near the weathered piers; tents and volunteers line the plaza]
[Music: Strings sharpen, tempo picks up]
[Sound: Raised voices, the clack of boards, a distant engine]

By noon the Flats look like a battlefield of determination and exhaustion. Volunteers form a sweating chain that passes saplings and coir while you move along it, a conductor without a baton. The work is intimate—hands brushing, mud under nails, breath misting in the cool air. You watch Etta lean on the shovel for a moment, then straighten with a look that tells you she will not slow.

A man in a pressed jacket steps out of one of the vans with a tablet; behind him two women hand glossy packets to an elderly homeowner whose porch is bowed and deep with salt stains. The man's smile is trained; the paper is company language softened into promises.

Corporate Liaison: "We understand the hardship here. Our program offers immediate relocation assistance and a fair buyout for those who choose stability." (He gestures to a line on his tablet.) "We can move your family to higher ground with subsidies for relocation and employment programs."

A hush falls across the nearest stretch of volunteers. Faces that had been focused on the next shovel now hold other weights—mortgage calculations, schools, the rusted washer at the back of a house.

Jun crosses his arms, stare flat and hard. Jun: "You think you can buy the sea away from us with slick printouts?"

Corporate Liaison: "We're offering choices." (He opens his hands as if offering the world.) "You can stay and struggle, or you can move to a safer place with infrastructure already in place."

You move closer before you realize you've made the decision. Your voice carries in a way you didn't intend—carrying both tiredness and the flare of anger that has been kept in under your ribs for months.

You: "You can't pay for what keeps us here. You can't buy our kids' rooms back or the stories stitched into porches. The marsh isn't a problem to be boxed and shipped; it's a living thing—it's protection when it's whole." (Your words come faster now, a wave pushing off a cliff.)

Corporate Liaison: (Cooperative smile cracking) "We don't mean to erase memory. We mean to offer security. When the storms get worse, those who stay will lose everything."

Etta steps forward with the quiet gravity of someone used to standing between tides. Etta: "The people who stay also give back to this place. We know how to make it hold. We have the knowledge. We have hands. You offer an exit. That is different."

[Sound: A thud—paper pinned to the community board]
[Visual: A legal notice, stamped and official, pinned where the protest flyers were]

A volunteer reads it aloud with a voice that trembles: "Notice of Potential Injunction—Immediate Cease and Desist of Unpermitted Shoreline Alterations—Filed by Coastal Solutions Holdings." The name—Elio's firm's, the firm that pushed the seawall plan—hangs in the air like a physical thing. For a beat you feel the ground tilt.

You: "They can't—" (You start.)

Mateo: "They can file things." (His voice is a wire taut with rage.) "Filing is legal muscle. Now it's law's turn against your hands."

Jun: "So they want to stop us with papers and payoffs." His fingers carve lines into the mud without meaning to. "Then we'll have to keep going while we can."

Etta folds the notice into her palm like a small creature and says, low, "This was always coming. It will get worse before it gets better."

Your chest tightens. The sensation is not just fear—it's that old, familiar guilt again: if you couldn't hold the plaza yesterday, if you couldn't make words land firmly enough, then maybe this notice will be the first nail in a coffin you promised you'd avoid.

Mateo: "We need legal counsel. We need press. We need hard data and a narrative." (He points at the notice, then at you.) "And Ari, we need you to keep people from being bought out one porch at a time."

You: "I will—" (You bite back a softer thing, the urge to ask for help that keeps getting swallowed.) "I'll organize night trainings, shift rotations, a documentation run. Milo—" You stop because Milo is a name you don't know will land for the reader; instead you say, "I'll arrange counsel."

Etta: "Good. Start with the parents on Clay Street. They listen to you."

[Music: Percussion drives harder; the tempo ratchets upward]
[Visual: Volunteers tighten lines; the sound of boots on wet boards becomes a march]

You set out assignments with a voice that feels like it is carrying too many verbs at once. You call for a legal volunteer, for someone to photograph the pinned notice, for extra hands at the northern sill. Each instruction you make is a stitch in a net you hope will hold the town together. The tide of people responds, but there is also a current pulling at them—pamphlets folded in pockets, the invisible math of bills and children and roofs.

Your hands ache in a way you've never let them before—every tendon singing under layers of mud. You taste iron; your heart beats a drum that matches the strings in the air. The strain sets into your shoulders, into the base of your skull. You think of the gallery of faces who will sleep in houses that may flood again and it makes your muscles tense.

Mateo pulls you aside for a heartbeat, close enough that you can see the small gold flecks in his eyes are unnaturally bright.

Mateo: "This—" (He gestures to the Flats, the pinned notice, your hands.) "—this is getting bigger than any of us planned. I can push my prototypes into the water faster, but if the injunction holds—"

You: "Then we'll challenge it." (The answer is reflex, but it's also the only truthful thing you have left in your pockets.) "We document. We show the council. We make the town the argument they can't file away."

Mateo: "And if the firm moves to buy the most vulnerable while we fight in court?" (The question is smaller than it sounds—it's a shard of all the possible losses.)

You: "Then we won't let them make the choice alone." (You squeeze his forearm with a grip that says you won't let go, even though you are not sure how to keep every anchor in place.)

[Visual: The sky darkens; a low wind begins to gather]
[Music: Crescendo of strings and percussion—high, urgent]
[Sound: Distant rumble of heavy vehicle engines]

You feel the day pivot under the weight of that notice and the vans and the volunteers' tired faces. It's no longer just about planting and stacking; it's about law, narrative, and whether a community exhausted by storms can stand against organized capital.

Your throat tightens, and you make a decision like you have always made them—fast, because there is no other rhythm that fits this hour. You send a message to the volunteer channel, your fingers clumsy on the screen, and then you step out onto a plank overlooking the flats. The mud gleams below like something that knows the taste of every foot that's walked through it.

You think of Etta's knotted hands, of Jun's calluses, of Mateo's stubborn tenderness. You think of the way the marsh holds when it's held back, when hands remember how to stitch. And beneath it—beneath the strategic plans and the political fights—there is the small, relentless worry that nothing you do will be enough for everyone.

A gust lifts a pamphlet from the corporate liaison's outstretched hand. It snaps across the plaza like a white bird and lands near your boot. You kick it back toward its van without thinking.

For a second the day is a sharpened line: hands, mud, paper, law. Everything is urgent. Everything is fragile.

You breathe. You send another message: training shift tonight, legal counsel on standby, Mateo to coordinate sensors if safe. Your muscles hum with exhaustion and an ugly, necessary adrenaline.

[Visual: Wide shot of the Flats; volunteers working under a low, oppressive sky]
[Music: Strings taper to a single, held note—tense, unresolved]
[Sound: Waves lapping somewhere off-screen, steady and indifferent]

You tuck your camera into your tote, feel the weight of other people's faces in your mind, and you move to rally the next group. The day will not let up; the week will not be kind. But you have chosen this path. You have chosen to stitch what can be stitched, to fight in courts and in mud. The strain settles in like a second skin.

Page turns itself in the charged air—you can feel the next fight in your bones, the next argument forming in your mouth. You don't know if it buys enough time, or if the town will hold, or if the people you love will sleep through the next storm.
