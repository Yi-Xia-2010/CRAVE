Jun: "You're taking the long view again, Ari? Gonna make people feel guilty with pretty pictures?"

Jun: (shrugs) "Or we make the council come down here and feel the mud in their shoes."

Jun: "Etta's greenhouse sent word. Seedlings are half-ready. She asked if you could stop by—you know, with your camera and your speeches."
You: "I'll go after I finish here. The plaza will be busy; I want good shots of the canvassers. If Elio's crew is already there, better to document their pitch too."

Jun's expression collapses into a softer thing—worn, worried for the fishers and those who live with tides for a calendar.

Jun: "People are spooked. If your photos help them stick around and keep their boats, then take a dozen."

Narration: You nod because you feel responsible the way someone feels the weather: not in control, but accountable. It's an old, heavy kind of guilt—if the shore buckles again, could you have done more? The question hums beneath your ribs when you breathe.

[Visual: Transition—steam fogs the greenhouse panes; warm amber light inside]
[Music: Gentle acoustic guitar with soft ambient synth]

Inside, rows of seedlings lean toward small lamps; labels wobble on popsicle sticks. Etta is at a bench, hands dark with soil, humming under her breath as she inspects a tray.

Etta: (looking up, eyes bright) "There you are. Camera already tired of the harbor?"

Etta: (laughs) "Coffee always, child. Sit. Tell me what the council will want this time—numbers? Pictures?"

You sit on a low stool and hand her the thermos. Your jacket squeaks as you move; the greenhouse's air warms the salt out of it.

Narration: Etta's face is a map of the town—furrows worn by wind and laughter. She worries the plants like a grandmother worries grandchildren, and you watch the easy, fierce care in her hands and think how small it feels beside the scale of storms.

Etta: "Funding's tight. The nursery's managed on scraps and favors lately. The seed trays started to die after that sudden cold-snap—root rot took the weakest rows. I can nurse them, but I need help with the transplanting, and—" (she pauses) "—and someone to make the council see this isn't charity. It's infrastructure."

You: "We can show them the growth curves, the survival rates. I can present living shorelines as mitigation as much as restoration."
Etta: (tilting her head) "You always make something of the water, Ari. Just remember—they need to hear why people belong here, not just why plants belong here."

You: (softly) "I know."

Etta's hands fold over a small bundle of salicornia seedlings, and for a moment the greenhouse is only the two of you and the muffled ocean.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse → Wave Innovation Lab (brief reference) → Back to Greenhouse | Midday]
[Visual: Mateo arrives through the greenhouse door, satchel slung over his shoulder; he brushes rain from his hair]
[Music: Subtle hopeful strings]
[Sound: The soft thunk of prototype parts against canvas]

Mateo's entrance shifts the room—he brings a small, focused optimism that feels like warm light breaking through cloud. He sets a wrapped package on the bench with careful hands.

Mateo: (breathless, smiling) "Etta. Ari. Sorry—ran late calibrating a sensor. I couldn't wait until the lab briefing."
You: (reaching) "Show me."

He unwraps a compact, buoy-like sensor no larger than a grapefruit. It has a matte finish, a ring where it could be tied, and tiny LEDs that wink when he taps a panel. The device hums faintly—lively and purposeful.

Mateo: "It's low-cost, community-grade. Wave-frequency sensors and a small solar cell. If deployed near the marsh, it can give us live swell data and battery estimates. Not a magic wall—just the info we need to make better planting and placement choices."
You: "It looks…friendly."
Mateo: (grins) "That's the intent. If the tech looks like it's trying to talk to you, people will use it."

Etta leans in, fingers hovering above the device like it's a new sprout.

Etta: "And it survives salt and storms?"
Mateo: "We've stress-tested a batch. The outer casing uses recycled polymer with an anti-fouling coat. If one fails, it's cheap enough for replacement."

You: (softly) "So—this could help us time transplants, avoid losses, show the council real-time evidence."
Mateo: "Exactly. And—" (he hesitates, eyes warm) "—I built the interface so anyone at the coop can read it. No corporate login required."

You feel a small, steady pulse of relief, almost like exhaling after holding your breath. Mateo's optimism isn't naive; it has edges—practicality and late nights in fluorescent labs—but it's earnest.

You: "Have you talked to Elio's people about this? They might like a model they can fund."
Mateo: (shrugging) "I sent a short brief. But I want this to stay in residents' hands—community deployment first. If we need larger support later, we'll chose partnerships carefully."

The conversation rests between you two, heavy with unspoken offers of help and collaboration. Mateo watches your face as if cataloguing which worries to soothe first.

Mateo: (softly) "You don't have to carry the council room on your own, you know."
You: (averting your eyes) "I know. It's just—if people leave because they think it's hopeless, I won't forgive myself."

Mateo: (reaching, lightly) "Then don't forgive yourself yet. Let us keep working."

Their fingers brush briefly as he tucks the prototype back into its satchel. The contact is small and ordinary, but it settles inside you like a warm stone.

Canvasser: "Morning. Are you interested in immediate protection options? Our plan puts safety first."
You: (measured) "Safety that lets people keep their homes is what I'm after. How does your plan affect private property?"
Canvasser: (rehearsed, not unkind) "Our models show managed barriers that protect critical infrastructure. We can discuss local compensations with the council."

You glance toward the tent. A man in a charcoal trench coat stands near the banner, watching the people move. He is Elio—slick hair, a corporate badge catching a glint of light. When your gaze meets his, he inclines his head in a manner both studied and almost apologetic.

Elio: (approaching calmly) "Ari Tanaka. I've seen your restoration work—impressive care, real results. I'm Elio Sato. My team is proposing a rapid-protection plan. We think it can buy the town time."

You: (tightening) "Time at what cost, Elio? We saw managed relocation in your old town."
Elio: (hands open) "I know what that's worth and what it costs. I lost people to complacency once. I'm not here to write anyone off. I want options on the table."

You: (leaning in) "Options, yes. But the community needs a say. We can't be boxed out by speed."
Elio: (softening) "Then let's keep the community in the room. Evidence matters, Ari. Show me what your sensors and seedlings can do; show me the data. If it's persuasive, we'll incorporate it."

Mateo: (interjecting gently) "We already have a prototype. Live data—we can calibrate it to your models. If you want us to share, we'll need guarantees: accessibility, data rights, and resident control."

Elio studies Mateo for a beat, then nods.

Elio: "Fair. We can write that into a memorandum—transparency clauses. I'm pragmatic, not predatory. But the weather won't wait for consent forever."

Narration: The exchange is less a clash and more a negotiation of fates. There's a strange relief in Elio's tone—he is reasonable, which makes him more unsettling because it means persuasion might win where brute force fails. Still, the fact he stands in the plaza and offers conversation gives you something concrete to bring into the meeting.

Jun sidles up, arms draped with nets, watching you with a question in his eyes.

Jun: "You think council will listen?"
You: "They have to. We have Etta's seedlings, Mateo's sensors, and a crowd who will show up. We tell them about people, not just projections."

Etta presses a hand to your shoulder, a private ritual before the work begins.

Etta: (quietly) "Make them see the hands that plant, Ari. Make them see Jun's callused palms and the little ones who love this water."

You nod. The plaza is full of small, ordinary proofs of life—baby shoes drying on a line, a net being mended, someone's lantern still lit despite the drizzle. Those proofs are why you won't press for spectacle; they're why you'll insist on incremental wins.

Mateo: (softly) "Come by the lab later. I want your input on the deployment map."
