[Scene: Tidebridge Harbor — Activation and Backlash]
[Visual: The buoys' LEDs blink in sync. A low-frequency hum builds in your chest as the wave dampers begin working; the water's surface around the array calms into a disciplined churn. Spray arcs less, and the line of waves compresses like something learning to obey.]
[Music: Crescendo to a roaring orchestral hit; then metallic, industrial undertones]
[Sound: Water pounding, then thinning into a muffled, controlled thud; a crowd's reaction split between cheers and bitter shouts]

From the lab's open crate, you step back onto the quay just as the first correctly timed damping sequence fires. The harbor answers: the surge that would've licked at low doors presses its forehead against the barrier and loses momentum. In the front row, a child shrieks in surprised delight; behind you, an older homeowner clasps a hand to his mouth, tears and rain indistinguishable.

Relief is immediate—and it tastes like salt and copper. It is also a blade.

Jun's voice claws across the quay. He has come forward, rain plastering his hair to his skull. His face is raw, the kind of angry that comes from being wounded where you used to stand tall.

Jun: "They fenced off the north slip. Boats will pay to put nets out. Who's going to feed kids when fishermen can't fish without a ticket?"

You: "We built exemptions—"

Jun: (cutting you off) "Exemptions on paper! The first invoice goes out and you'll see how easy exemptions look on a page. They want the harbor tidy. They want profit lines, Ari. They don't want people in the way."

Etta steps between you and Jun, soil still under her nails, her voice low but steady enough to be heard.

Etta: "We saved some blocks. That is not nothing. But our bargains always have teeth."

Jun: "Teeth? They're biting our wrists off."

Mateo arrives then, breathless from the lab, cheeks wet with more than rain.

Mateo: "The array reduced the surge height by thirty percent where it mattered. The low blocks—Jun's row and the Lanes—held. We're not looking at flooded basements this time."

Jun: (bitter) "At what cost, Mateo? A lane to pass and a line to pay and the harbor renamed with their logo. That's your victory?"

Mateo: "No—it's not the victory I wanted either. But if the choice was immediate ruin for all or a partial hold—"

Jun: "You always pick a middle they can count on, not the people they can't put a quarterly on."

The quay becomes a chorus of voices—some grateful, some furious. A family by the fishhouse loads a van with photographs and a battered armchair. A council representative reads aloud the clauses that justify relocations: safer blocks, lifestyle compensation. People argue, fists and hands punctuating sentences; demands hang raw in the cold air.

[Narration]
The buoys are keeping water off thresholds, but they are also staking territorial claims. The sensors are honest; the contracts are not. The harbor has been stabilized at the cost of being parceled.

[Scene: Etta's Greenhouse (The Nursery) | Night — Aftermath]
[Visual: The greenhouse glows amber against the rain-sheen dark. Inside, steam clings to panes; seedlings tremble under the halo of grow bulbs. Shelves sag with trays; the scent of wet earth and citrus hangs heavy.]
[Music: Slow, aching cello; sparse piano—sorrowful, intimate]
[Sound: Distant generator hum, a drip of water into a tray, the small crackle of a radio in the corner]

You and Etta walk in heavy silence. The greenhouse is a relief from the harbor's roar, a small world where things still grow despite everything. Etta wipes soil from her hands with a rag and looks at you with a face that gathers all the storms she has known.

Etta: "We won a sliver of shore. We kept roofs where roofs might have been taken. That is work. That is not betrayal."

You: "Some left tonight. Jun's cousin packed his truck. He took the relocation check. He said it was a way to keep his children's future safe. He said it like it was ripping a tooth out."

Etta: (soft, unflinching) "People make choices to survive. That isn't always what we would teach them—but it is what they must do."

You: "I keep hearing Mateo in my head—'I wanted to give Jun time.' We gave time, but we gave strings too. I don't know if time with a tether is still time."

Etta's hand finds yours—callused, warm. She squeezes.

Etta: "You did what you could with the teeth in your hand. That won't stop the ache. It will, though, make a place for some to keep living. That is not nothing."

You close your eyes. The lantern light pools on your lashes; the world contracts to the sound of your own breath and the distant memory of a council chamber vote. The town is quieter now, but for what it cost.

[Visual: Close-up of your camera on the bench. You pick it up, fingers shaking slightly, and lift it to frame the seedlings illuminated against a darkened harbor through the glass.]
[Narration]
You photograph it—an amber square of life inside a frame of clinical lights and corporate buoys beyond. The picture will be proof, and accusation, and elegy. You don't know which label it will wear in the future.

Mateo sits opposite you on a bench, knees almost touching. Neither of you offers a cure for the friction that runs like a fault line between policy and people. Instead you offer small things: a thermos of tea, a tired joke; you exchange a look that hangs with memory and the knowledge that what binds you now is as complicated as any contract.

Mateo: "I kept the community clauses alive as long as I could. I argued for exemptions. I argued for local oversight. The investors... they wrote lines in ways I couldn't squeeze around. I'm sorry."

You: "Sorry doesn't undo tonight."

Mateo: (quiet, honest) "I know. I also know the kids in the low blocks are still asleep in their rooms tonight. For now. That counts."

You: "It counts. And it hurts."

Mateo: "I'll keep fighting. With the lab. With the council. With anyone who listens. I want this to be more than a corporate stamp."

You: "So do I. But fights cost people—and some of them already paid. Jun won't forgive me easily."

Mateo's fingers find yours again, tentative, fierce. The touch is a small contract between two people who chose to stay together in a town that had to sell pieces to keep standing.

[Narration — Closing]
The harbor bears its new name in small, bureaucratic ways: corridors on maps, maintenance schedules published in the paper, bollards with firm insignia. The buoys hum through the nights, a mechanical lullaby. Some houses breathe easier. Some families are gone. The market shifts; access is no longer free and the phrase "public commons" sounds like something from an older language.

You walk the pier at dawn days later. Morning light is pallid and steady; a thin sun breaks the cloud for a single golden shard and makes the wet wood glow. You press your camera to your face and take a picture of a buoy—its shadow long in the glassy water—and then of a child untying a small boat under the new registration sign. The two images will live side by side in your memory: safety and restriction, survival and cost.

Etta's seedlings are planted along a reinforced sill, roots finding new purchase. Mateo's lab posts a maintenance roster and an open meeting schedule where, in small print, "community access negotiations" are listed every Thursday. Jun's nets hang to dry on a rack that seems, for now, half-full.

You and Mateo stand at the railing, hands clasped, watching the estuary breathe. You did not expect a neat justice here. You expected mud and stubborn hands and the interminable drawing of maps until the shoreline learned to call its own lines again. Tonight, that expectation is all the truth you have.

It is a partial victory that smells like wet rope and petrol and the lingering sweetness of rescued rooms. It is a cost counted in empty chairs and new names on corporate lists. The town is not the same; some of its heart has been boxed and relabeled. You are not the same; your love is less naive and more deliberate now—tender for what it is not, fierce for what it must protect.

You lift your camera once more, not to condemn but to catalogue: this harbor marked by human compromise. The shutter clicks, a small, precise sound. It is the sound of bearing witness.
