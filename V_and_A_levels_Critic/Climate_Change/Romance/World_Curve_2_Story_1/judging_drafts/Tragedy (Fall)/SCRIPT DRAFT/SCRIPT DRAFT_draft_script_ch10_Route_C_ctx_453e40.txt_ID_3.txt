[Scene: Municipal Hall | Late Morning — Fall]
[Visual: The municipal steps glisten with drizzle; protest banners on the plaza drip faintly, colors muted by cloud. A gray sky presses low over Tidebridge.]
[Music: Pulled strings with a taut, insistent rhythm; a low, urgent pulse]
[Sound: Distant gulls, the soft shuffle of umbrellas, a municipal door sighing shut]

You walk through the double doors with the weight of the plaza still on your skin—the press of voices, the salt of other people's fear. The municipal foyer smells of damp wool and coffee gone cold, a faint antiseptic under everything. Your fingers find the coral strap of your camera out of habit; it grounds you, but only a little.

Councilor Rosa is waiting at the foot of the stairs, shawl wrapped tight against the breeze. Her face is a ledger of obligations and compromises. She offers a small nod that is more business than comfort.

Councilor Rosa: "Thank you for coming early, Ari. We need clear heads today—this room has to be neutral, procedural. Etta's already inside waiting with the collective. Elio's team will set up their presentation. Mateo... he said he'd be here, but he looks like he hasn't slept."

You fold the strap around your wrist and step toward the council chamber. The hall's wood echoes underfoot; every step sounds like a counting of consequences. Inside, the chamber is arranged in a horseshoe: polished wood, a long table with microphones, and a projector smelling faintly of hot plastic. Outside the tall windows the sea is a flat sheet of pewter.

[Visual: Wide shot of the council chamber, flags at half-mast; people cluster—Etta in an earth-stained apron, Mateo with a satchel heavy at his side, Elio precise at a laptop beside a tidy stack of reports]
[Sound: Low murmur; the whisper of pages turning]

You sit across from Elio. He is measured, his steel-gray eyes scanning the room like a livefeed. When he speaks, his words are clean and economical.

Elio: "We appreciate the municipality opening this negotiation. Time is not neutral—storms are becoming more frequent. Our models show a narrow window to implement structural protections that prevent catastrophic failure of critical infrastructure. Seawalls and surge barriers, coupled with managed relocation for the highest-risk blocks, reduce modeled fatalities by sixty to seventy percent."

His voice is steady; numbers cascade like pebble-precise facts. The room takes them in the same way a tide takes salt: inevitable, indifferent.

You: "Sixty to seventy percent is a lot of lives. But models are only as ethical as the choices that shape them. Who decides which blocks are 'highest-risk'? Who gets the buyout offers first? And how long before access clauses privatize the waterfront we all rely on?"

Your questions land like stones. Elio doesn't flinch, but Councilor Rosa's fingers tighten around a pen.

Elio: "Those are valid concerns. Decisions will be overseen by the municipality. Priority for relocation is based on repeated inundation frequency, infrastructure criticality, and population vulnerability—metrics, not preferences. Access clauses are designed to protect infrastructure investment and ensure maintenance. We can draft clauses that preserve reasonable public access."

Etta interrupts, voice thin but carried.

Etta: "Metrics look fine on paper until you see the names attached. You call them 'blocks'—we call them homes. Are you going to mark my neighbor's street as 'unsustainable' and offer them a number that doesn't feed their grandchildren? Will you protect the nursery plots? Will you let coir walls stay where they've taken root?"

Etta's hands tremble when she says "nursery." The room tilts toward a different gravity—memory and care versus spreadsheets.

[Visual: Close-up on a printed slide Elio projects: a neat map tinted in blue and gray with red highlights marking 'Acquisition Zones']
[Sound: The projector's hum; a collective inhale, as if the room is bracing]

Elio: "We can set aside parcels for ecological preservation. It makes sense—preserved marshes reduce wave energy, and they are cheaper than constant repair."

You feel the logic pulling at the corners of your conviction. You remember Etta's hands in peat, the way seedlings press against the greenhouse glass. You also see, in Elio's tone, the outline of someone who lost people to a storm and learned ruthlessness as a form of mercy.

This is the collision you agreed to step into: municipal oversight as a buffer, a hope that procedure will curb harm.

Mateo speaks up, voice low at first, then quickening.

Mateo: "Procedure isn't a guarantee. 'Set aside parcels' can be reinterpreted by a future board or a new contract with vaguer wording. If we give them access controls, investments will get prioritized over people. They'll monetize the waterfront. I've seen that in the grant language."

Elio: (calm) "The language in contracts can be surveilled and enforced. There are legal mechanisms."

Mateo: "Legal mechanisms don't change who holds the pen, Elio. You're asking us to trust that your firm's priorities will stay the same when investors change. I—" (He stops, fingers working at the edge of his satchel like a man trying to fold himself into a smaller, safer shape.) "I can't be the person who signs away my neighbors' right to come down to the docks."

A hush follows Mateo's lines. Your chest tightens—his words are an accusation aimed as much at the process as at you for agreeing to enter it.

[Visual: Papers are passed; an aide places a sealed envelope labeled 'Preliminary Acquisition List' on the table like a landmine]
[Sound: Paper sliding; a soft intake of breath]

Councilor Rosa: "These lists are preliminary. Elio, perhaps you can explain the criteria and the appeals process we've discussed."

Elio reaches for the envelope with surgical calm and spreads the pages. Names, addresses, property types, and a column labeled 'Priority: High/Med/Low' bloom across the table. There are blocks marked in the oldest part of town—streets with porch lights that used to host summer concerts; there are also parcels of abandoned warehouses.

Elio: "Our criteria weigh repeated flooding, critical infrastructure proximity, and stabilization cost. We've labeled certain blocks 'high' because short-term stabilization is infeasible without relocation. We're prepared to offer fair market buyouts plus relocation assistance."

Etta leans forward so hard her chair creaks.

Etta: "Who's defining 'fair market' in Tidebridge? The market erodes during a storm season. It erodes with losing your fishing boat. People don't buy a new life with a photocopied offer."

Elio: "Valuation models account for market disruption. There will be advisory services."

Etta: (brittle) "Advisors won't feed the kids next winter if their nets are gone."

The exchange sharpens until it scrapes. Your throat goes dry. You can imagine Jun's face—callused, resentful—if his block were on that list. You think of the nursery, of salt-marsh seedlings whose futures have been measured in months of effort.

Mateo's hands ball into fists. He looks like he wants to say more and also like he wants to leave the room entirely.

Mateo: "I built a prototype of a buoy that could run in tandem with living shorelines. It could be deployed incrementally, community-owned. You say 'not feasible' without relocation—whose feasibility are you measuring?"

Elio: "Incremental deployment won't lower near-term risk enough. My firm faced a similar choice—delay and watch people die, or act fast. We chose the latter. I don't want Tidebridge to suffer the same loss."

You feel something in Elio's voice that isn't just conviction—it's a scar that keeps re-opening. The sentiment sits uncomfortably against the cold machinery of contracts on the table.

[Music: Tension swells—staccato strings; heartbeat-like percussion speeding up]
[Sound: A distant rumble of thunder, as if the sea itself is pressing closer]

You stand to pace, needing movement the way someone at sea needs the feel of a horizon. The camera at your hip swings against your ribs like a pulse.

You: "We're all here because we want to protect people. But we aren't interchangeable with infrastructures. If the buyout list starts with the oldest streets—if access clauses allow gated maintenance paths and private contractors—then what you call protection will become exclusion. Councilor Rosa, we need binding public-access guarantees and community oversight for any acquisition."

Councilor Rosa: (measured) "Binding guarantees are possible, but enforceability is the question. There are budgetary limits, legal precedents, and emergency clauses that complicate permanency."

Etta: "So you ask ordinary people to trust the legalese as their lifeline."

Her words hit the room like cold water. You want to tell her you'll fight tooth and nail for every parcel she names, but you also heard Elio's losses, and how decisively his firm moves to prevent repeat tragedies. The tension is a rope, and every argument pulls a different person closer to the edge.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand tapping the tabletop; the wood grooves press into your skin]
[Sound: The tick of the wall clock, louder now]

At the height of the exchange, someone passes a whisper down the row—the firm's preliminary 'access clause' language is more restrictive than Elio described. It's in a footnote in the third contract appendix: "Institutional access reserved for authorized maintenance personnel; public access may be limited to defined corridors under provider discretion."

The words bloom in your head like ink in water. "Provider discretion." You taste metal—fear mingled with the metallic residue of inevitability.

Mateo stands, not a slow rise but a spring. His voice is raw.

Mateo: "You brought us here to 'negotiate' and now hand us the shape of the shore like it's already cut. 'Provider discretion'—that sounds like privatization. It sounds like selling the dock to people who will never hand us a rope again."

Elio: (restrained) "We don't intend to privatize access. We intend to ensure maintenance so infrastructure functions."

Mateo: "Infrastructure for whom, Elio? For the people who live here, or for the investors who make 'maintenance' profitable?"

The chamber is electric. People murmur. Etta covers her mouth with a knobby hand. Councilor Rosa looks like a woman asked to hold two collapsing beams at once.

You: "Mateo—" Your voice trembles. You haven't meant to take a side apart from the municipal table, but you're the one who agreed to sit here. Your internal ledger flips: your guilt, a familiar weight, presses down. 'If we don't try to steer this—' you start.

Mateo: (cutting you off) "No. You said you'd stand with us. You said you'd keep them honest. Instead you gave them the key to the courthouse doors."

He holds your gaze as if measuring how much you've been compromised. The air between you crackles. Somewhere behind him, someone scrapes a chair back; the sound is like a match struck.

Elio: (soft, almost pleading) "Ari, I know what you're feeling. My method is harsh because I saw what delay looks like. I don't want to see your town become a cautionary tale."

You look at him—Elio's eyes are not entirely unarmed. He believes he's saving lives. You feel the high arousal of the room—urgency, pressure, the snap of choices that will have long tails.

Your internal monologue runs on a loop: If I push too hard, the entire proposal collapses and the firm withdraws resources. If I accept too much, the town's access and character erode. If I try to split the difference, I may lose Mateo and Etta and the people who taught me how to plant marsh grass.

The moment sharpens. Your chest is tight, your palms sweating against the camera strap. You realize that the negotiation is not only about structures and shorelines; it's about trust.

Etta: "Ari. Whatever you decide, remember the seedlings. Remember the porches. Remember Jun's hands. Don't let paperwork be kinder than people."

Her plea is immediate, ancestral.

[Music: A single, high cello line runs down like a struck wire; the percussion cuts]
[Sound: A rain-slap on the windows, harder now; thunder low and close]

Mateo takes a step back, breath clouding in the cool, damp air that streams through a slightly cracked window. He looks like he's deciding whether to stay and counter or to walk out and leave the room to the lawyers.

He looks at you.

Everything in you wants to stay anchored to the procedural hope you chose—municipal oversight, the promise of fairness. Everything in him is pulling toward a different shore: community control, incremental solutions, refusing to cede the waterfront.

You open your mouth. The room leans in as if listening for a verdict. Your internal alarm screams: choose and you cut. Choose not to and you drown.

You feel every set of eyes like a tide, and the moment stretches, poised on the edge of a decision you don't quite know how to fix.
