[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Late Morning — Overcast, wind picking up]
[Music: Dissonant strings, percussion ratchets up]
[Visual: Banners whip in a gust; puddles ripple. A crowd clusters beneath awnings and umbrella canopies strung with hand-lettered signs.]
[Sound: Megaphone crackle, distant thunder, the metallic slap of a council banner against its pole]

Narration
You stand where the town's breath gathers—the plaza a throat of voices. The campaign you insisted on has teeth now: audits requested, legal teams looped in, transparency demanded in plain, public language. That was the point. That was the stand.

But demands make noise, and noise invites counters. Suits arrived with binders and injunctions. People who believed in urgency now argue about timing while papers pile up in the hands of lawyers who smell deadlines like lifelines. The rain tastes of iron. The plaza smells of wet fabric, wet paper, and the sharp coconut of emergency lights.

Mara (approaching, rain speckling her recorder): "They served notice this morning. Elio's legal counsel dropped a preliminary injunction at dawn. They—" (she breathes, half laughing) "—they're fast."

You: "How fast? How bad?"

Mara: "Fast enough that our temporary restraining order will hold them back another two weeks. Maybe three, if the judge grants an extension. But the firm has deep pockets. Their PR team is already spinning the delay as 'political obstruction.'"

Narration
Your chest tightens. Two, maybe three weeks. In the weather-scarred math of Tidebridge, two weeks can be an epoch. Every delayed day is an exposed porch, an unfilled sandbag. The arousal in you becomes physical: a pressure behind your eyes, your fingers itching for action rather than paperwork.

Etta (from the edge of the crowd, voice carrying): "You're doing the right thing, Ari." (She pats your shoulder with soil-stained hands, the touch as small and enormous as an oath.) "We will slow them if we must. The nursery will house seedlings; we'll plant what's left."

You (to Etta): "If the storm comes before the injunction holds—"

Etta (cutting in, steady as peat): "Then we'll lose pieces. But if their contracts go through without audit, they'll decide which pieces are worth saving and which are not. Our choices will be auctioned."

Narration
The debate fractures into a thousand little sounds—murmurs, cameras clicking, the distant bark of police advisories. Your decision to demand audits was meant to preserve the town's agency. The air tastes of salt and consequence.

[Scene: Wave Innovation Lab | Midday — Cold fluorescent light, a tension like held wire]
[Visual: Benches strewn with prototypes; a half-assembled buoy rests under a tarp. Small LEDs blink irregularly. Mateo sits at a bench, hands dirty with grease, jaw clenched.]
[Sound: The lab's ventilation hum, the muted beep of a centrifuge, a distant roll of thunder]

Narration
You come to the lab because he is there, because his hands build translation between wind and wire. You need him—need his pragmatism, his tempered optimism—to hold the hybrid idea together. But the delay has a mechanical cruelty: crowdfunding stalls, grant windows close, and the lab's ledger reads like a wound.

Mateo (without looking up): "They pulled a matching grant yesterday. The foundation said the uncertainty is too high now. They need assurances of timeline."

You: "Assurances? We asked for audits. Insisting on transparency doesn't mean we don't want protection."

Mateo (voice sharp, a thread snapped): "Assurances mean money moving. Audits mean lawyers parsing rows of data while the sea keeps rising."

You: "We can't hand them a blank check, Mateo. You know what Elio's firm does—how many times have we seen projects nudge out communities? This—this is different."

Mateo (turning to you, eyes bright with something like hurt and exhaustion): "Different how? Because the language says 'community-first' and they stamp it with a logo? Ari, I'm tired of losing time. I'm tired of harvesting good will and printing plans that never get built because we insisted on... on ritual purity."

You (voice low, pressed): "This isn't purity. It's stewardship. If we let their deadlines dictate who stays and who leaves, we won't be stewards. We'll be tenants."

Mateo (barks a laugh, then softens): "And if we delay and more houses flood before we can scale even the first buoy prototype? Then what? We write reports while people's roofs blow off. I'm watching funding evaporate, Ari. I'm watching partners get cold feet."

Narration
Words become clambering, like hands on wet wood. The lab's fluorescent light makes everyone's faces a little too honest. You want to map out a compromise—an accelerated pilot, tighter milestones—but the papers are moving on a different plane. Emotions are blunted into strategy and then back into hurt.

Mateo (steps forward, voice almost pleading): "I left the city to build things here. I still believe we can make tech that helps people without selling them out. But I can't watch our prototypes sit idle while the ocean rehearses its next move."

You: "So what's the alternative? Sign the baseline to get money and lose oversight? Let Elio run the show because he can promise speed?"

Mateo (swallows, then speaks with brittle certainty): "Maybe I need to go where the projects get built. There are teams on the outer harbors that have the funding to test scaled buoys in real conditions. If it works there, maybe we can bring it home on our terms later."

Narration
His words land like stones. You try to catch them, to smooth the ripples, but the lab's echo is a canyon. Mateo's eyes find yours, earnestness and sorrow braided together. The choice he announces is not framed as betrayal, but it is a cut.

Mateo (reaching out, hesitates): "I don't want to leave you. I don't want to leave Tidebridge. I—" (He pulls back, the sentence unfinished, an instruction manual with missing pages.) "I have to know something can be built."

You (the protest rising in your throat): "And I'm supposed to wait?"

Mateo: "No. You're supposed to fight like you always do. I'm—" (He exhales, the sound of a kite string snapping.) "I'm going to accept a position with a program up north. It's not a job with the firm. It's an independent lab. I thought you'd be—"

You: "I thought you'd say we'd keep at it together."

Narration
Between you, the lab is suddenly too small for what's happening. The LEDs blink as if counting down. The arousal spikes—anger, pleading, grief—vertically compress into a single, jagged moment. Then the world reduces to the thud of the front door as Mateo gathers a battered waterproof satchel and steps into the rain.

Narration
Etta ran the nursery like a ledger of hope. She saved cuttings and names with equivalent tenderness. News had driven her hands into action—pots stacked in extra racks, seedlings shored up with bits of driftwood, labels handwritten in a looping, stubborn script.

Etta (kneeling by a tray, her voice slightly frayed): "We can move these into the back. The coir logs will hold for a while, but not if the winds turn hard."

You (weary): "Mara just sent the judge's preliminary response. The injunction stands for now, but Elio's team is appealing. The worst-case timeline keeps getting shorter."

Etta (pausing to listen to the greenhouse's steady cough): "When I was a girl, the tides taught me a different patience than the city's counting shows. Patience that does not mean passivity. We will plant, we will teach the children to lay down mats and stones, we will document everything."

Mara (entering, recorder held like an offering): "I've got footage. The audit request—some of the firm's environmental models are mis-calibrated. If we are vindicated on paper, people will remember why we fought."

You: "If we're vindicated on paper."

Etta (looks up, eyes harboring something sharp and ancient): "Paper doesn't warm a child's bed. Paper doesn't rebuild a roof. But it can stop people from being erased by contract."

Narration
Outside, the sky unspools in a slow bruise. Inside, the greenhouse holds a fragile warmth. You and Etta work with the rhythm of urgent hands—repotting, weighting, labeling. The physicality steadies your mind for a moment, but the pressure builds in the fingers that fold peat and tie twine.

[Scene: Workshop (Community Salvage & Repairs) | Early Evening — Sirens faint, lamplight swinging]
[Visual: Tools clatter; tarps flap; neighbors move with practiced efficiency. A barricade of furniture, sandbags, and carved-out sheets of corrugated metal runs along the street edge.]
[Sound: Distant wail of an approaching storm, shouted instructions, the metallic rasp of a generator kicking on]

Narration
The first surge comes at dusk. It is a thing with teeth—an angry, efficient animal. Despite injunctions and court dates, the sea interprets no calendar. It finds low thresholds, unbolted doors, the spaces between sandbags and human hope.

Mara (yelling over the wind): "We have to move the archives! The council records might wash out if the tide hits the basement!"

You: "Get the boxes to Etta's loft. Bring the hard drives. I—" (You stop, a scream of rain cutting the sentence.)

Narration
Water finds the small unnoticed faults like hunger. Jun hollers a curse as his boat, moored as always along a chipped piling, takes on a list and thuds against the pier. A neighbor you recognize from childhood—an aunt of someone you know—stands on a step and watches as water lifts a childhood swing and sets it free down the current like a gray, spinning moon.

The legal delays, the hours spent in court filings and press conferences, the careful footnotes of audit language—none of it stops the physical. It only keeps the record of what happened.

[Visual: The surge slaps against the barricade. A section gives; disking, glass explodes like a flock of startled birds.]
[Sound: Shouts, the horrified crack of timber snapping, a child's wail muffled by wind]

Etta (calling, voice cutting through): "Get the children! Move inland!"

Narration
You run until your lungs burn. You feel your feet slip, the slick of seawater inside your boots. Your camera—faithful, coral-strapped—bobs against your chest, a counterweight of memory. You point it—because you always point it—to the ordering of catastrophe: broken boards, a door twisted on its hinges, wet mattresses where someone once slept.

Mara (beside you, recorder forgotten, tears mixing with rain): "This—this is what we tried to prevent."

You (wordless for a stretch, then:): "Did the audit say the model missed this? Did they—"

Mara (voice thin): "The initial analyses we obtained show the firm's surge models underestimated compounding tidal effects. They adjusted later. But by then—"

Narration
By then, the water has already claimed porches and the single-wide homes that lined the lower lanes. You watch as Jun drags a sodden crate from a flooded shed, his face slate-calm and furious. He looks at you, and there are no simple words left between you.

You (to Jun): "Who did this hurt? Who will be counted?"

Jun (bites his lip): "Everyone who couldn't leave. Everyone who couldn't afford to raise their floorboards. Everyone who thought an injunction would be enough."

Narration
The arousal crescendos into a brutal clarity. The legal victories—if any—will articulate wrongs and set precedents. They will not, in themselves, replace the roofs or the boat engines or the afternoons of play that were folded into damp, sour cloth.

[Scene: Municipal Plaza | Night — After the storm, the air raw and metallic]
[Visual: Streetlamps lean, their light distorted in puddles. A few tents remain; people huddle with blankets. Etta's greenhouse is streaked with mud but upright. Mateo stands at the far edge of the plaza, his satchel at his feet.]
[Sound: Low conversation like a wounded animal, the occasional clink of salvage being sorted, footsteps sullen on wet pavement]

Narration
The town's center resembles a body after fever. Some parts are intact, some reduced to bone. The injunctions are still legally in place—the audits are still pending—but the costs have been paid in timber panels and in the slow, terrible business of counting loss.

Mateo (approaching you, voice carefully small): "I called the program up north. They can take a few of our prototypes—the raw data will be better used there."

You: "You left."

Mateo (rubs his hands together as though warming them): "I didn't want to, but—" (He looks at the ruins of a child's fort across the way and the words choke.) "If I can prove something there, maybe we can come back with a stronger case. Maybe we can rebuild with the tech in a way that doesn't—"

You (interrupting, raw): "—erase people's right to stay. I know. I know. But we needed feet in the water now, Mateo. We needed piloting here."

Mateo (shakes his head): "We needed money that would move. We needed equipment that wasn't mothballed by litigation. I'm not saying I don't believe in what we're fighting for. I am saying that I couldn't stand by while the lab's lights dimmed."

Narration
You stand a hair's breadth apart, the plaza a room that contains your ruined plans and fragile integrity. The two of you are operating on different maps—both trying to reach safety, both with compasses warped by fear.

Mateo (reaches out, brushes your shoulder—an old, careful gesture): "I'm sorry. This is not how I wanted to leave."

You (voice small and brittle, like paper wet at the edges): "This is not how I wanted any of it."

Narration
This is the romance that climate makes: a ledger of decisions folded between bodies. Love does not annihilate politics; politics does not erase love. It reshapes it, sometimes into forms that ache.

Mara (coming up, camera hanging heavy): "The audit team released a preliminary summary tonight. It supports our claims—miscalibrations, omitted compound factors, optimistic erosion timelines. The firm will be embarrassed; legally, we have leverage."

You: "Leverage won't fill the holes."

Mara (eyes shining with journalistic triumph that tastes sour): "No. But it will stop similar projects from being greenlit without oversight. It will make them open their models."

Narration
Victory on paper. Loss in the body. The contrast is a cruel, jagged geometry you recognize like a scar.

Etta (coming from the greenhouse with a wet notebook clutched to her chest): "We saved what we could. Some seedlings are gone, but others—" (She breathes in, scent of peat and rain filling the space.) "—others lived. The paperwork will matter. The audits will matter."

You (looking at the signed, stamped pages Mara holds up, then at the row of ruined homes): "For who? For the future? For the ones who stayed?"

Etta (her hands still trembling with exertion): "For the ones who can still be protected. For the ones who will come back because there is a record to point to—that this was not business as usual."

Narration
The evening narrows to a set of small truths: the audits will likely change the landscape of contracts and the firm will pay reputational costs. Lawsuits will recalibrate future timelines. An official record now exists that recognizes the town's concerns.

But the price is paid in timber and sleep and relationships. Mateo's leaving is a fissure; Jun's boat is bent; a row of houses will need to be razed. People will grieve. Some will leave. Some will stay.

Narration
You take the picture because you always take pictures. To record. To remember. To accuse the future of forgetting. The image is a ledger that no boardroom can erase.

In the days that follow, the audits are published. The firm's models are annotated, corrected, and publicly criticized. Policies change—the town's victory is legislative and procedural. The community's voice is preserved in reports and mandates that will shape future projects.

But the town around you bears a quieter set of losses: the rebuilt shelf of memories, the families who left tidewise down highways, the friendships that once fit into kitchen spaces now stretched and thin. Mateo sends occasional messages from his new lab—photos of prototypes bobbing against clean, clinical test facilities, notes about data that might eventually come back to Tidebridge. You read them and sometimes reply; sometimes you do not.

Etta continues in the greenhouse, hands never idle. She teaches children how to plant with their knees in the cold, how to read salt-struck soil, how to name the plants that marry themselves to brackish water. She keeps the nursery running because living things require tending long after legal reports are archived.

Mara publishes a feature that wins accolades and reshapes public conversation about transparency in coastal projects. Her fame is a bitter-sweet bread—good for the cause, thin at feeding the immediate wounds of your neighbors.

Narration
You keep the camera at your side. You go to council meetings and read aloud the audit findings with a voice that has been both hardened and hollowed. You help rebuild porches when you can. You write op-eds that sting with data and tenderness. You sit with people who have lost bedrooms and boats and, sometimes, answers.

In quiet hours you replay the night of the surge. You replay Mateo's hand held out and then lowered. You replay Etta's stubborn face. You replay Jun's silent curse. The arousal once a crashing wave now becomes a steady, aching current in your veins.

You thought—once—that if truth were known and visible, justice would follow. It did, in part; the audits forced change. But truth does not prevent the immediate cruelty of weather, nor the sorrow of friends who take other paths to fight the same battle.

Narration
The ending is not an end but an account. It is a ledger in which principles and people are balanced against one another, and sometimes the sums do not reconcile.

You stand at the pier weeks later, the estuary a bruised mirror. A small plaque now designates a community pilot area—an outcome of the audit hearings. It's an imperfect victory: a guarded patch of shoreline and a promise in legally binding language. There will be funding for restoration and for stricter oversight. There will be slides of data in municipal presentations and the slow work of replanting marsh grass.

You run your thumb over the plaque until the metal warms to your skin. It reads in precise, antiseptic type: "For Tidebridge—community oversight and transparency required."

Narration
You press your palm flat to the cool metal and think about the people behind you. Mateo's silhouette on an email screen, Etta's soil-stained hands, Jun's boat now mended but different, Mara's byline that won't halt the ache. You think of those who left with boxes strapped to cars, of children who will one day learn different rhythms.

There is no triumphant music. There is no neat reunion. There is a slow list of work, of apologies, of reconstruction. There is the knowledge that you did not sell out, that you refused the immediate settlement in exchange for a chance at something accountable.

You breathe in the brine and the cold. The camera hangs against your ribs like a heart on a string.
