They built it faster than anyone expected. The seawall rises—clean, efficient, and alien against the weathered wood of Tidebridge. Etta Hargrove: (soft) "They told me the nursery could stay if we signed an agreement. It said we'd have access hours and that the wall made a legal buffer. But it also said there were clauses—private management. I couldn't sign."

You read the clauses anyway, because you have always been the one who reads the fine print. The fonts are small and precise, and they rearrange your neighborhood into line items.

Mateo Alvarez: "It will have oversight—technical oversight. I thought if I controlled the architecture, I could keep it community-first. I can still—"

You don't let him finish. You are drowning in a tide of compromise, each wave washing away another edge of the community you fought to keep.

You open a letter and a map together: the town's shorelines with shaded blocks where buyouts have already cleared names. There are gaps in the map where porches used to cluster. You trace a line along the estuary and your fingertip trembles. The community is a constellation and someone has already started erasing stars.

Etta sets a cup beside you—a bitter tea that tastes like late autumn.

Etta Hargrove: "You stayed, Ari. You did what you said you'd do. That counts for something. But it won't undo what people lost."

You try to name the ledger of costs aloud: friends gone, Jun's dock sold, the school garden moved inland, trade routes rerouted. The wall keeps the water out in a clinical, efficient way. It also fences grief into certain pockets and empties others.

You: "What do we have left if the shore belongs to someone else? If our access is managed and our names are redacted?"

Etta's hand finds yours—small, callused, comforting. She doesn't give you a plan. She gives you a sentence that will sit with you for a long time.

Etta Hargrove: "You did not fail because you tried. You failed because the system wanted a different kind of success."

Her words aren't absolution. They are an observation—sharp, true, hollowing.

You walk the length of the new wall, palms grazing its cold face. Each finger leaves a damp print that evaporates. Behind you, the town's life continues in smaller, quieter pieces: a makeshift market under tarps, a woman teaching children how to tie nets again, a handful of volunteers who still plant coir logs where they can.

Mateo meets you at the last pier before the wall ends. He is tired in a new way—hardened, like someone who has measured the consequences of a promise and still signed.

Mateo Alvarez: "I've accepted the lead engineer role on the project. They'll put my team in charge of deploying the buoys as part of the system. I'll have resources, Ari. We can protect zones, build resilient grids. People will be safer in places."

Your breath makes a halo you barely see.

You: "At the cost of what you said you believed in?"

Mateo leans into you, and for a second the warmth is the old warmth—before grant terms, before contracts, before the town was measured in parcels.

Mateo Alvarez: "I'm not sure what I believe anymore. I believe in people not drowning tonight, in keeping the school garden from being swept. I'm sorry I couldn't—"

You don't let him finish. There are no neat apologies big enough to unroll all the losses.

You remember Jun's face when his neighbor signed. You remember an old woman weeping into a petition. You remember your hands full of seedlings that are laughed at by some and cherished by others. The wall stands firm. The tides are partitioned. The votes have been counted.

You are left with the ledger. You stayed to keep a town from erasing itself, but in staying you watched the town remake itself in forms you do not recognize and did not approve. People will not die in the floods as a direct result of delayed walls; some lives will be steadier, safer. But the town—the town's architecture of belonging—the docks, the porches, the places where neighbor-meets-neighbor—has been rearranged by legal lines and corporate priorities.

You think, with the blunt clarity of exhaustion, of what "keeping people here" meant in practice: a promise that required money and policy that never arrived. You think of every hand that pulled and planted and how the shore still receded.

You do not have rage left; you have a raw, empty ache. You have a record of what was tried, the photographs you took, the charts you made. They will be evidence of something—maybe of an earnest attempt; maybe of a smaller moral victory. They might be used in op-eds, in academic papers, in private, plaintive memorials. They will not necessarily change the contracts signed or the fences built.

You stand beneath the long shadow of the wall and let the weight of it settle. Mateo's choices press between you like a question with no answer you can accept. The town is safer in measurable ways and poorer in ways the balance sheets cannot see.

You bend to the last seed tray and plant one more plug, pressing soil around it with a tenderness that does not promise success. The greenhouse's warm light feels like a small, stubborn lie against the cold facts outside.

You are not a martyr. You are someone who stayed. You are someone who kept doing the work that mattered to a smaller set of people and failed to bend the system that mattered to the larger one.

The season ends. The wall stands.
