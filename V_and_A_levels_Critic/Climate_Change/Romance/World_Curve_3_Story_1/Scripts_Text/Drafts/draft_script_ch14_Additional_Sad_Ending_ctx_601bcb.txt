### DRAFT
# Route: Additional (Sad Ending)


[Scene: Old Docks | Morning — Aftermath of the Exposure]
[Visual: The Hub's corrugated walls streaked with protest flyers; a legal notice taped to the main door flaps in a gust. Volunteers cluster beneath a sagging tarp. Papers litter a bench.]
[Music: Discordant strings under a snare—urgent, scraping]
[Sound: Camera shutters, murmured questions, a distant municipal truck backing up]

You wake to the sound of other people's anger like weather—sharp, indiscriminate, and impossible to ignore. Reporters' boots slap the boardwalk; a microphone's breath grazes your cheek. Someone's phone is already recording you before you can frame your own words.

Nora is the first face you find: paint on her knuckles, hair flattened from sleeping on the repair bench. Her smile is gone. She passes you a mug that still smells of stale coffee and salt.

Nora: "They stamped an injunction on the pilot last night. Court says no more work until the hearing. They—"

Nora's sentence collapses into a laughless sound. You feel the legal paper in your hand like a papered bruise.

Samir arrives with the slow certainty of someone who has watched tides swallow things before. He carries two thermoses and the look of a man who keeps a ledger in his head for every storm.

Samir: "They'll sit in their chambers and measure harm in reports. Meanwhile the water doesn't wait for pages to dry."

You swallow. Your notebook is closed in your other hand; the page you wanted to keep folded inside is now evidence, rumor, and fuel in someone else's mouth.

Ari appears at the edge of the crowd, his blazer buttoned like armor. There is a press aide two steps behind him. He doesn't step toward you at first—he watches the scene with the measured gaze of someone trained to catalogue optics.

Ari: (careful) "Maya—there's liability now. They told me to tell you we should pursue mediation. The firm advised—"

Ari's voice is an efficient machine. You can hear the policy distance in it, and beneath that a thread—something softer, unreadable—briefly loosening the edges of his script.

You: "Mediation won't stop the water. It won't stop bulldozers if they want cheaper land elsewhere. The injunction locks the pilot; it buys time for the developers to pivot."

Ari: (pause) "I'm trying to keep you out of legal jeopardy."

You: "You can keep me out of a courtroom. You can't keep the tide out of my neighbor's door."

He opens his mouth and closes it. The crowd presses in—voices folding over one another: "Why did you go public?" "You doomed us." "You did this." Someone holds up a hastily scribbled sign: TRUST US AGAIN.

You do both in the brittle span of an hour—one hand on a microphone, the other on Samir's sleeve as he points to a row of houses that sit too low. The injunction hums in the background like a countdown. Lawyers file motions; developers' lawyers file counter-letters. Anonymous threats lace into the margins of your inbox.

Luca arrives wet from the morning's fog, a rope coiled over his shoulder. He's been on the docks, on the line, and he carries the tiredness of someone who'd rather have a plan than a headline.

Luca: (quiet, strained) "You told them everything, right? You said how the contracts were shifted, how lobbying happened?"

You: "Not for show. For record."

Luca: (exhales) "Records don't keep houses from filling up. Records make people fight in court while the sea takes their children’s toys."

The words between you are small explosives. He blinks, and for a second his face is a map of betrayals—public, private, small and jagged. He does not accuse you outright; the anger sits in the space, hot as steam.

You: (internal) You keep thinking that truth is a kind of shelter. But shelter requires walls that can hold—funding, maintenance, governance. A truth that only exposes, without protection, can rip the place open.

[Scene: Tideside Boardwalk | Late Afternoon — Notices of Retaliation]
[Visual: Heavy boots leave prints in wet wood. A municipal notice tacks next to a developer's flyer promising "Rapid Protection Now". A line of city surveyors marks off land with neon tape.]
[Music: Low brass, a marching undertow]
[Sound: Distant rumble of machinery, gulls shrieking over construction lights]

By dusk, the first sign of retaliation arrives not as a threat but as inevitability: surveyors measuring nearby blocks and crews dispatched to areas deemed 'strategic.' The city's focus shifts like a lens—money and machinery corralled to high-visibility zones. Your neighborhood is not on their prioritized list.

You: "They're prioritizing high-property-value corridors. They told the press that's 'efficient allocation.'"

Nora: (bitter) "Efficient for who?"

The lights begin to buzz as contractors set up mirrored theodolites, and a rumor spreads that the developers have quietly purchased salvage rights to lots once held by elders who didn't sign paperwork in time. You watch Samir's jaw tighten at the word 'quietly' like a fist closing.

Samir: "People will be moved. Not by force at first—by the slow work of not being in the plan."

Someone in the crowd spits a phrase that lands like a stone: "You wanted accountability. Now we're the demonstration."

Ari remains oddly still. Later, when he finds you leaning against a sodden bench, he doesn't look accusatory—only strained.

Ari: (soft) "I tried to warn you about escalation. This is what happens when systems and signals get tangled."

You: "Warning and action are different things. You told me about protocols. I told you about people's houses."

Ari: (a flicker of something) "I thought we could scale what you've started. I still—" He stops. His hands are empty. "I still think the engineering can save lives. But I see now the cost to people's roots."

You watch him step back into his professional posture. The distance between wanting to heal and wanting to control yawns open and fills with practicalities—funding cycles, legal shields, press-optimized statements.

[Scene: Salt Marsh Reserve | Night — The Storm]
[Visual: Sky torn into veins of steel and fire; grasses lash like a thousand small hands; mangrove roots exposed and collapsing into roiled water]
[Music: Full orchestra, percussion like distant avalanches; an overriding, relentless tempo]
[Sound: Wind like a beast, breakers smashing, splintering wood, distant sirens]

The storm arrives on an axis of panic and inevitability. It arrives as it always does—without respect for court orders, without heed to human machinations. The injunction that froze your hands did not freeze the water. The legal motions sat in a judge's inbox as the sea found every weakness you had left exposed.

You are at the Salt Marsh Reserve when the first living banks begin to unspool. Saplings you'd planted with volunteers a season ago are torn free like promises. The engineered reed mats that were meant to deflect surge fold under brute tide. You try to lock eyes with volunteers, to give orders that the injunction forbids, but you are choking on impotence.

Nora, mud up to her knees, yells over the wind: "We can't move the heavy anchors—we're being blocked on liability!"

Someone hands you a feed from a volunteer camera; a neighbor's roof crests like a small, doomed island. Children are being pulled from second-floor windows down to trucks. The scene is a choreography of emergency procedures and improvisation—the best of humanity and the worst of planning collided.

You: (internal, frantic) Your training gives you checklist actions—shore lines, sandbags, pumps—but the law clipped your hands. The law was a net with too many holes.

The surge takes what you could not legally touch. Water finds the tender seams: low eaves, hidden foundations, the places where elders kept keepsakes. The living shoreline—the thing you'd fought for with sweat and crowdfunding and community dinners—does not hold. Machines authorized elsewhere bulldoze protective berms hours too late for this row of houses.

Samir stands on a flooded stoop, shouting a name you do not hear through the storm. The sound of his voice is a hammered bell. You see a child's toy float past like testimony.

Luca appears at your side, soaked through, eyes raw. He does not speak for a long time; when he does, his voice is small and crystalline with grief.

Luca: "We were pushed aside... because we were inconvenient."

You: "We tried to make them see the cost."

Luca: (bitter laugh) "Facts don't hold against water, Maya."

His hands are shaking. For the first time you feel not only guilt but the cold arithmetic of consequence: your exposure initiated processes you couldn't predict. The developers withdraw funding for volunteer maintenance under the pretext of 'security concerns.' The city accelerates construction in other districts with money that was once promised to community-led maintenance.

The stormscape becomes an indictment: courts taking months to docket hearings while houses sink in days. The pilot—your pilot—lies in twisted reed and torn rope. The people you were protecting are now bargaining with temporary shelters.

[Scene: Old Docks | Days Later — Fragmentation]
[Visual: Tarps replaced by moving boxes and folding cots. A 'FOR SALE' placard in the distance flutters where a porch used to be. Children play in standing water, their laughter brittle.]
[Music: Sparse piano with occasional dissonant strings; a funeral march transformed into domestic rhythm]
[Sound: Conversations in low voices, a hammer in the distance, occasional weeping]

When the wind finally slacks, what remains is not silence but the small, noisy work of loss. The court battles slog. Developers file claims for damages, then counterclaims. The media runs retrospective pieces titled "Who Failed Whom?" Your name appears with equal parts heroism and culpability depending on whose column you read.

Councilwoman Elena Reyes gives a press conference about 'responsible allocation' and the difficulty of 'balancing equity and safety.' Her language is measured; her eyes do not meet yours when a cameraman points you out in the crowd. Behind her, a map of the city glows with new seawall lines being prioritized for wealthy districts.

Maya Ortega: (from the crowd) "This neighborhood asked for help before the headlines. We asked to be part of the plan."

Councilwoman Reyes: (careful) "We must operate within legal frameworks and fiduciary responsibility."

The words are procedure armor. They do not warm the people shivering on the docks.

At community meetings that used to hum with solidarity, you find fractured conversations. Some neighbors blame you; some blame the city; some are too exhausted to finger a target. The collective story you tried to hold together reveals seams you didn't see.

Luca speaks to you privately, voice low, edges jagged from loss.

Luca: "You made a choice. Maybe you thought transparency would save us. Instead it made us visible to predators."

You: "I thought visibility would protect us."

Luca: "Protection needs more than being seen. It needs promises kept—maintenance budgets, legal safe harbors, people assigned to keep watch. You didn't have all that."

You: (internal) He is right, in the brutal way that makes your stomach fold. You did not have the institutional scaffolding to make truth a shelter. You have only yourself and a record of what happened.

Ari shows up once, for a hearing. His statements are precise, his emotion contained. You read his testimony and feel both an old ache—for what could have been—and an unbridgeable professional divide.

Ari: (in court transcript—spoken now to you) "We offered partnership. I attempted to secure bridging funds. The legal exposure after your disclosure complicated fundraising."

You: "You say 'complicated' like it's an abstract problem."

Ari: "It is a systemic problem. But I understand grief."

He says it as if reasoning can be tender. The room strips the tenderness to a thin script. The judge postpones rulings for months. The pumps fail in the meantime.

[Scene: Tideside | Sunset — The Last Watch]
[Visual: The skyline is a strip of lights where a new seawall's construction rigs throw harsh white—farther upriver, beyond the neighborhood. Your reflection in a puddle is fractured by rain and neon repair lights.]
[Music: A slow, mournful cello line; a distant crescendo that never resolves]
[Sound: Lapping water, a child's muted laughter, a hammer dropping]

You go down to the boardwalk at dusk and touch the wet rail. The marsh smells like old things: brine, algae, tobacco from a neighbor's cigarette. Your bracelet—the small woven token you always wear—feels heavy with salt. You close your eyes and try to inventory loss not as numbers but as people: Mrs. Alvarez's herbpots, Samir's carved oar, the smell of Nora's repair oil.

People ask for your account. They ask whether you would do it again. They don't ask with curiosity; they ask with the pointedness of survival—because answers may mean shelter, or blame, or eviction notices.

You stand and speak into the hollow of the evening.

You: "I went public because I thought it would stop the quiet. I thought it would make the city keep its promises. I was wrong."

Several neighbors listen, some with soft mouths, others with shawls pulled tight. The words land like small stones.

Nora slips a hand into yours and squeezes—angry, steady.

Nora: "We needed help yesterday. We needed funds and a legal framework to protect volunteers. You did what you thought was right."

You: (internal) It's not absolution. It's not condemnation, either. It's the complex human thing between them: an ember that might warm nothing.

Samir comes and places a palm on the same railing. He doesn't speak. He hums an old line about tides and memory. In his silence there is a kind of counsel: remember and tend what you can.

Luca stands a few paces away. You can see his jaw working as if chewing on a decision. He does not make it for you. He cannot. Whatever closeness you might have shared has been stressed in the press, refracted through legal lenses, and punctured by loss. His reaction is complex—both protective and distant, both loyal and infuriated.

You: (internal, closing) The neighborhood endures in pieces. Some houses will be rebuilt; some people will leave; some traditions will be carried by those who stayed. The city's new defenses will rise in places that mattered to planners. Your name will be used as an example in op-eds—hero and scapegoat in the same breath.

You bend and pick up a bit of washed-ashore reed. It cracks between your fingers like a promise gone brittle. The weight of responsibility sits in your shoulders like a coat you cannot shrug off.

You look out across the water where distant floodlights mark the new seawall's beginning. The light is clinical and beautiful in its way. It will save many. It will not save everyone.

You close your eyes and let yourself feel the full measure of what you have cost and what you have tried to preserve. The guilt is a cold, precise instrument. You do not know if you will ever be forgiven. You do not know if forgiveness is what you need. You know only that you will remember every house, every name, every ruined sapling.

[Visual: Camera pulls back from your silhouette on the boardwalk as construction lights blink in the far distance; the tide laps at the edge of your boots]
[Music: A final, low chord that hangs and does not resolve]
[Sound: The soft, persistent pulse of water]

You breathe in salt and soot and a neighborhood's memory. You bind your hands around the reed and the bracelet and make no promises you cannot keep—only this: you will carry the truth of this place forward, even if the city names you as the cause of its pain. You will hold those stories like lanterns for those who remain.
