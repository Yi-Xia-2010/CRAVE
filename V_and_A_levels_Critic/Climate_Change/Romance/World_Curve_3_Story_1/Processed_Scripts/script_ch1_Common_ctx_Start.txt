[Scene: Tideside (Seawall Promenade) | Late Afternoon]
[Background:"ch1_Start_1"]
[Sound: Distant gulls, soft creak of weathered wood, occasional clink of tools from the hub]
[Music: Sparse, warm synth underlayer — reflective, low tempo]

You come home the way you always do: slow so you can read the tide lines with your boots and feel the boardwalk settle under you. The waterproof notebook is tucked under your arm, the cover softened by salt and thumbprint oil. Your field band—solar sliver against your wrist—ticks faintly with last week's tide readings. The air tastes like iron and something green from the marshes; it carries that peculiar wet-sand smell that stitches memory to place.

Ahead, the promenade slopes toward the Old Docks. The patchwork repairs your collective left last year are visible in a tableau of mismatched wood, braided rope, and fresh sealant. Up close, the wood is still rough where the collective's hands left their marks—Nora's hammer gouges, Samir's careful knots—but there are also the faint cracks you never had the funding to treat. The renovations look lived-in, usable, like a promise made practical.

Samir Qureshi sits on his usual bench, hat low, watching the water move like a slow, patient animal. When you pass, he lifts his chin and offers a tight, almost imperceptible smile. His skin smells of sun and old rope.

Samir Qureshi: "You're back early, Maya. Tide's leaning wrong for the month."
Maya Ortega: "I noticed. Not by much, but enough to be polite." (You let the humor slide out like a small rope between you.)
Samir Qureshi: "Polite tides make for polite storms. That never helped anyone prepare."

His voice is a dry tide pool—steady, slightly amused. You hold the notebook a fraction tighter. The failed pilot is a dull weight behind your ribs; you don't speak its name. You don't have to. Samir's eyes hold the map of the neighborhood the way only someone who's fished the same inlet for fifty years can.

Samir Qureshi: "How's the collective? Heard something about a council hearing."
Maya Ortega: "Next week. Reyes will listen. We have ten minutes." (You say it plain. The time feels both generous and miserly.) "We're pitching living shorelines and expanded marsh stewardship. No one likes those ten minutes more than I do."

Samir nods once, slow as a gull's wing. He doesn't offer advice; he offers presence.

[Scene: Old Docks Hub | Early Evening]
[Background:"ch1_Start_2"]
[Sound: Laughter in the distance, the distant hiss of a kettle, the soft tapping of tools]
[Music: Acoustic guitar thread — restrained, lifting slightly into a hopeful motif]

You push open the patched door to the Hub. Warm, humid breath of the building meets you—wet canvas, herbal tea, the faint petroleum tang from well-used tools. Nora's at the workbench, grease on her knuckles, and Luca is there too, elbows on a crate, talking with that easy cadence that makes people forget they're being organized into action.

Nora Díaz: "You're carrying the notebook like it's full of bad poetry, Maya." (She grins; the paint on her overalls is still tacky in places.) "Did you bring the new marsh survey?"
Maya Ortega: "Right here." (You tap the solar band. The notebook answers with the tiny clack of a well-worn hinge.) "But first—how did Samir look?"
Nora Díaz: "Older. Same wise. He offered two old fishing tales and one stern glare." (She laughs.) "Which you deserved."
Luca Chen: "We were just sketching the grant notes. Council wants numbers and a timeframe. Ari Nakamura's team already circulated renderings—slick, animated, you know the type." (He shifts, eyes bright but cautious.)

The three of you settle around a scarred table. The Hub smells of drying seaweed and coffee; light falls in strips through a patched skylight, dust motes floating like tiny islands.

Luca Chen: "Listen—funding is funding. If we get the municipal grant, we can fortify the living shorelines properly. We can pay for seasonal maintenance. We can hire people from here."
Maya Ortega: (You feel the careful balance in his words.) "We can, yes. But we need guarantees on stewardship funds. We can't afford another pilot that collapses when the first real storm hits."
Nora Díaz: "And we need clarity on displacement. Laptop renderings don't show families moving boxes to storage. They don't show whether the fishermen keep their slips."
Luca Chen: (His voice tightens.) "I'm not saying Ari Nakamura's plan is a villain play. Walls are walls for a reason—fast, visible, fundable. But you know him. He moves markets and meetings. People follow the picture of safety."

Maya Ortega: "Ari Nakamura's seawall is a city-level solution. It scales. It looks good in a slideshow. It could pull resources in that if we align with it—"
Nora Díaz: "—and steamroll the dockside community in the process. We all know how those deals go. We should map the consequences before agreeing to sit at a table with engineers with a bulldozer of capital."

Luca leans in, palms flat on the table, the warmth of his hands grounding.

Luca Chen: "Maya, you built the first pilot. You know what went wrong. If we ask for funds but can't guarantee upkeep, it's worse than nothing. But if we say no outright to every city offer, we starve. This isn't just ideology—it's survival."

You breathe out slowly, tasting salt and the iron of responsibility. The failed pilot loops through your chest: underspent maintenance, a permit delayed, a storm that found the seams. You remember the look on a neighbor's son's face when their front step floated for a week. Guilt presses in, the kind that tightens jaw and makes your shoulders carry the neighborhood's sleepless hours.

Maya Ortega: "I won't see that happen again. If we take municipal money, we tie it to a custodial trust—staff from the neighborhood, scheduled maintenance, transparent audits. No one walks away after the ribbon is cut. Not again."

Nora meets your gaze, searching. Luca squeezes the bridge of his nose, thinking through the logistics.

Nora Díaz: "Do you think Reyes will accept those terms? She's balancing developers and votes. Sometimes 'community stewardship' is a blessing and a bargaining chip."
Luca Chen: "Reyes knows us. She knows Samir. But she's also nervous about headlines. Ari Nakamura's plan—if he frames it as 'city-proof'—could shape the narrative in a heartbeat."

Maya Ortega: "Then we shape our own frame. We bring people to the hearing. We bring history, testimonies, Samir's voice." (You let your conviction land soft.) "We walk the Council through living shorelines—what maintenance looks like, what it costs, who does the work."

Nora Díaz: "You carry that guilt like it's a tool. Use it carefully. It helps you see what could go wrong, but don't let it freeze you."
Maya Ortega: "I won't freeze. I learn. I adjust. I demand the safeguards we didn't have before."

A brief laugh slips between you—soft, bitter, hopeful. It is not victory; it is a beginning.

[Background:"ch1_Start_3"]
[Sound: The wind finds the skylight gap; a distant rumble that might be another boat or the city's low machinery]

You step back onto the promenade as the sky sorts itself into threat and gold. The boardwalk thrums under a changed light. Your notebook pulses with the tiny data tick of the solar band—another tide reading registering in the margin—and you find your jaw set.

Maya Ortega [Internal]: "Ten minutes before Reyes. Ten minutes to shape 'safety' without handing over the neighborhood. Ten days to prepare testimony. One failed pilot taught me what not to do." (The mantra is quieter than a shout. It is a line drawn in sand that you intend to keep.)

Luca falls into step beside you as you walk. His shoulder brushes yours, accidental and then not.

Luca Chen: "Whatever happens at the hearing, we'll be there. We'll make it messy and human. That's our advantage."
Maya Ortega: "Messy and human is exactly what Ari Nakamura's deck can't show." (You allow the dry humor; it tastes like salt and grit.) "Prepare the photographs. Prepare Samir. Prepare an ask that can't be footnoted away."

Nora's voice comes from the doorway, softer: "And prepare yourself to ask for help when you need it. You don't have to carry the whole tide alone."

You pause, looking out at the marsh where reeds lean into a light you can't yet name. The storm-glow catches the edges of the city and makes Ari Nakamura's renderings—slick, confident barriers projected in your mind—look flat against the real, breathing shoreline. Your commitment tightens: not to romanticize the marsh, not to deny hardship, but to hold the community's agency like a fragile shell that mustn't be traded for quick certainty.

[Background:"ch1_Start_4"]
[Music: Fades to a single sustained note that hints at motion ahead]

You take a breath that carries salt, coffee, and the low hum of civic machinery. Outside, the storm-glow moves like a rehearsal of consequence. Inside, the Hub is a scatter of plans and people and the warm, resilient kind of hope that is built from doing, not promises.

You steady the notebook in your hand, feeling the small weight of every person you hope to protect. The next week will be a corridor of hearings, rehearsals, and hard conversations. You are not naïve about the power of renderings or the allure of a single, monumental fix. But you also know the particular, stubborn competence of a community that has kept its roof patched and its memories intact.

You close the notebook for the moment, the click a punctuation. The promenade behind you breathes out, the wind lifting the smell of wet earth and distant rain.
