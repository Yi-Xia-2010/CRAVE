[Scene: Old Docks Community Hub | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The Hub's corrugated doors yaw open; strings of solar bulbs swing as a gust pushes salt-scented air inside.]
[Music: Tense BGM — quickening strings under a low electronic throb]
[Sound: Rain spatter against tin roofing; distant car horn muffled by water]

You come back from the Atrium with your shoulders raw from speaking and your notebook still warm in your palm. The Hub smells like wet rope, frying plantains, and the iron tang of rain. People fall into work around you as if the building itself breathes you back into action — Nora at the projector, Samir folding a tarpaulin, a pair of teenagers untangling nets.

The choice you made sits like a stone in your throat: the room heard you pledge to stand with the neighborhood, not to fold into the convenience of a single engineered solution. Saying it aloud did something to the air; it made promises that needed bodies to keep them.

Luca is there before you expect him, hands smelling of grease, a rope coiled at his hip. He catches your eye and lifts his chin — an easy, dangerous smile you don't have the energy to return properly.

Luca: "You okay? You sounded like you were carrying the sea in your ribs up there."
You: "I am fine." (Your voice is small; the word cracks.) "Fine isn't the whole story."

Nora cuts in from the projector, eyes bright with the afterglow of council lights. Her knuckles are still nodding a jig only her hands understand.

Nora: "We need a timeline. Samir's table can host the volunteer sign-up tomorrow. I patched the crowdfunding page—small, many donors. Micro-grants, quick approvals. We make it airtight."
Samir: "Keep it honest, girl. Folks will smell fancy words. Tell them what they'll do, not what they'll fund."

You set the notebook on the table and open to a blank page. The first lines you write tremble, then scrawl into the shape of a plan: marsh buffers, replanted cordgrass, living piles of oyster shells at stilted intervals. Your solar wristband hums against your skin — tide data still logging in the background, a metronome you can’t turn off.

Your chest pinches with the memory of the pilot that failed two years ago: seed mats washed away because no one had been paid to keep them tended. You swallowed the loss then and promised never to repeat it. That promise now hangs over every volunteer shift you plan.

Nora leans across the table, tapping a projected map with a fingertip.

Nora: "We start three pilot stretches — East Wharf, Old Boat Basin, Marsh Lane. Volunteers for planting, plus rotation for maintenance. We make stewardship part of payback—meal vouchers, stipends from the crowdfunding pool. We keep them tied to the place."
You: "And the engineering?" (You try to keep the question clinical, but your voice betrays it.) "Councilwoman Reyes asked for contingency plans. She won't accept... nothing."

Luca folds his arms, frowning at you and then at the map, as if trying to hold both in the same line of sight.

Luca: "Contingency looks like practical backups: temporary tide barriers, sandbags held by crew, mobile pumps at the basin. Not a permanent wall. Not the kind that makes memories into artifacts."
You: "We need a delivery window. And we need the community to see progress before the next council vote."

A neighbor pats his pockets and steps over, hesitant. He's heard your commitment and is still bargaining — practical fear, plain as the calluses at his thumb.

Neighbor: "If Ari's team offers cash now, can't we—"
Samir (cutting in gently): "What they give now might buy food this week, but it could take your house next month. We've seen it before."

The conversation tangles, every sentence an argument with both hope and dread. You feel the pressure rising in the room: tasks multiplying, doubts proliferating like crabgrass. Each plan breeds a dozen what-ifs.

[Scene: Salt Marsh Reserve | Early Evening]
[Visual: Long, low light; reeds cut silhouettes against a wide, trembling reflection of sky]
[Music: Percussive strings and a low drum; heartbeat-like rhythm]
[Sound: Water lapping at mudflats, a distant bird's plaintive call, the creak of boards underfoot]

By twilight you are ankle-deep in mud, boots caked and heavy. The marsh is a living map of small resistances: bags of shell and woven fascines, newly planted cordgrass trembling in the wash. You work with a small crew — hands moving with the practiced economy of people who mend things because they can't afford to wait for permission.

Luca beside you hums while he stuffs mesh with oyster shells, a tune without words. It should comfort you; instead it tightens your stomach because the music masks something else — a public relations campaign already playing, an image of Ari's seawall flashing on news feeds like a lighthouse that doesn't belong to anyone here.

Luca: "You shouldn't carry it all alone, you know."
You: "I don't feel like I'm carrying it alone," (your fingers find a stubborn root) "I feel like I'm carrying everyone who depends on a place that forgets it's alive."
Luca: "Then let us be heavy together."

You meet his hands in the wet, and for a second the world narrows to the shallow splash between your palms. Close is dangerous and necessary. You both laugh too sharply because the mud has a way of cleaning you of polite pretense.

Nora shouts coordinates; your wristband glows with a new surge alert. The sky fractures with a line of fast-moving cloud — storm-choreography. Volunteers glance at one another; the smell of ozone sharpens.

Nora: "We need to speed up planting. Tide's pushing in two hours. If we don't finish the outer stakes, the mat will slough."
You: "Do we pull people off and prep pumps? Or do we press in and risk losing work?"
Samir: "We risk everything if we stop. We risk everything if we don't. That's not helpful," he says, dry as driftwood.

Your pulse quickens. The wind picks up, pressing cold salt under your collar. Every decision now is a multiplication of stakes: lives, roofs, trust.

You choose the rhythm you will carry into the night — not now on paper, but as a pulse that sets people’s feet. The decision's weight presses at your sternum like a tide.

[Scene: Old Docks | Night — After the Marsh]
[Visual: The Hub is untidy in the dark; tarps hang like boat sails, string-lights cast small suns over a scatter of maps and seed packets.]
[Music: Low, urgent synth under brittle acoustic guitar]
[Sound: A crowd murmur outside; the distant thump of a municipal helicopter]

The crowdfunding page hums with tiny donations and a slow trickle of doubt in the comments. Someone posts a video of Ari at a podium — a shiny rendering of a seawall backed by charts and glossy endorsements. The caption reads: PROTECT THE CITY — SCALE NOW.

Councilwoman Reyes calls. You pick up on the second ring, voice steady in public but fraying privately.

Councilwoman Reyes: "Maya, we need contingency details by morning. The mayor will want a fallback if storms pick up. Can you outline what your team can deploy in forty-eight hours?"
You: "We can outline maintenance rotations, emergency pump logistics, and volunteer deployment. But a 'fallback' that removes residents isn't acceptable to us."
Councilwoman Reyes: (quiet, practiced) "I know where you stand. I also know the city needs a plan that voters can understand. The optics of an un-funded grassroots effort are—"
You: "—not enough. We're asking for visibility and modest seed funding to ensure durability. We can show you staged results, not renderings."

She hesitates. You hear the bureaucratic calculus in her pause — votes, re-election, the invisible ledger of civic survival.

Councilwoman Reyes: "Alright. I'll expect a written contingency in the morning. Keep it tight, Maya."
You: "We will."

You hang up and the Hub feels smaller, corners of it pressing inward. Nora slams a palm on a map.

Nora: "We need PR, press, a staged event that shows we can keep things running. Photos, volunteers in vests. We need numbers, too—how many meters of planting, how many people rotated weekly. Make it look like a program, not a ritual."

Samir rakes his hand through his beard.

Samir: "When people see the mud-splattered boots, they remember that hands built this place. But Council will want neat boxes and deliverables."
Luca: "We can do both. Show the wheel, and the hands turning it."

Your stomach knits with a raw, anxious thread. You think of the pilot failure—how the city used that footage to say the neighborhood couldn't manage maintenance. Fear of repeating that mistake is a constant low hum under every plan. You are certain of two things: you cannot let your people be moved aside, and you cannot watch them lose trust in you.

A volunteer approaches, hand tight around a folded flyer.

Volunteer: "Someone from Ari's group stopped by with a check. They said we'd be hired to 'assist' if we sign now. A lot of folks are tempted—food, rent."
You: (the word trusts thins) "We can't make decisions off a check if it means losing our say. Tell them we'll refuse any money that comes with relocation strings."
Volunteer: "But what if refusing means we get left without any help at all?"

The question hangs like a bell you cannot unring. The room fills with small, ragged breaths. High arousal isn't only the storms outside; it's the human furnace inside this building, all of you burning to keep something alive.

Luca moves close, voice low enough for only you to hear.

Luca: "I'm tired, Maya. I'm tired of meetings that smell like someone else's money and conferences where we stand in contrast to renderings. I don't want to lose what we are for the world's applause."
You: "We're not going to lose it. Not while we have hands willing to crook nets and plant roots. But promises must be kept."

He watches you like you're both calculating and confessing. The closeness between you has a new weight — a tether that isn't only personal but political. Loving here feels like making vows in a storm.

[Scene: Seawall Promenade (the Tideside) | Night — Late]
[Visual: The boardwalk slick with rain; neon repair beacons blink off in the murk; in the distance, the model seawall in the newsfeed glints like an impossible promise]
[Music: Climactic strings; cymbals roll under low bass]
[Sound: Emergency siren wail in the distance; waves slap louder than before]

You walk along the promenade with Luca. The city lights are smudged by salt on your glasses. The seawall renderings, projected on a temporary billboard, paint a future that seems clean and certain — a smooth barrier between people and sea. It makes your stomach ache.

Luca: "They've got the billboard lights up already. Pretty, huh?" (He tries for lightness; it doesn't land.)
You: "Pretty for a postcard. Not for the people who live with fishbone memories in their ribs."

A gust flings a glossy leaflet across your boots — a promotional piece from Ari's firm, offering 'comprehensive protection.' You pick it up and the paper flakes at the edges like ash. You want to burn it, to spit it into the tide.

Your phone vibrates: an automated alert, terse and red. The city is monitoring a fast-developing atmospheric swell; minor surge predicted, with localized flooding possible overnight. Your pulse slams against the bones of your neck.

You feel everything accelerate: Councilwoman Reyes' midnight request for contingency, volunteers wavering on conditional pay, Ari's billboard promising a one-stop answer, and the marsh you planted tonight that will either take root or wash away with the next high. The pressure crowds your thoughts until they speak in breathless fragments.

You could call the crew back to the marsh for an all-night watch. You could prioritize community outreach and public visibility in the morning. You could accept a conditional short-term fund to stave off immediate hunger. Each path shivers with consequences.

Luca grips your hand, hard enough that his knuckles blanch.

Luca: "Whatever we do, we do it together."
You: (you want to agree, but your throat tightens) "We need to choose direction now. If we lose the night, we may lose more than the plants."

The world contracts to a point where a single decision will tilt a dozen futures. Your chest is a drumbeat; your every thought is urgent and brittle.

You look at the marsh map in your notebook, then at the smear of the seawall projection cutting the skyline. You feel the old guilt — the one that makes you think every failure is your fault — press cold and inexorable against your ribs.

You inhale, tasting salt and metal and the sharp edge of impending choice.

[Visual: Close-up of your hand over the map, the inked lines trembling under the pressure of your fingers.]
[Music: Crescendo; strings and drum converge to a taut, unresolved chord]

You are about to decide where to pull the line — toward immediate, risky hands-on defense that could keep people in place, or toward a public visibility and staged progress that could buy the Council's attention but might invite the city's larger machines to rearrange lives.
