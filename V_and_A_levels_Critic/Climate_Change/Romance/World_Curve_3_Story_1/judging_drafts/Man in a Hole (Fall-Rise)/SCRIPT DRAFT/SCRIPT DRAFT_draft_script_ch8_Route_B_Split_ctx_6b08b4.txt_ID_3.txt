[Scene: Municipal Green Atrium | Late Afternoon — Stormlight]
[Visual: Wide shot of the atrium glass bowed with rain streaks; mangrove planters cast cool teal shadows]
[Music: Rapid, tense strings with a low electronic thrum]
[Sound: Hail ratting the glass; distant PA murmurs]

You step back into the atrium the way you step into every negotiation — with the weight of a dozen small lives in the pocket of your jacket and a notebook that smells faintly of wet paper. The air tastes of ionized rain and warm plant soil; the mood is filtered through panels and protocol, clinical except for the people it will decide for.

Ari stands near the projected schematic, hands measured, voice practiced and clear as a newly tuned speaker. The seawall segment he proposes is visible in the rendering — clean lines, engineered canopy attachments, a promise in steel and glass. Council aides hover like satellites; a timekeeper taps at a tablet.

Ari Nakamura: "This segment is modular. It can be deployed in stages and scaled across the waterfront. We have guarantees for two meters of surge reduction and an urban canopy that reduces heat stress."

You feel the room recalibrate around his numbers. They are bright, exact, irresistible in their certitude.

You: "And the pilot's footprint? Who decides siting and household exemptions? The neighborhood can't be an experiment that makes people leave."

Ari watches you, expression composed, a brief tilt that reads as curiosity and calculation at once — unreadable, the way someone who builds scaffolds of logic must be. He moves closer, lowering his voice but not his intent.

Ari Nakamura: "There will be a community oversight board. We can write exemptions. We want local stewards involved, Maya." (he says your given name with a calibrated warmth)

You: "Community-stewardship clauses are words without teeth if a subcontractor's contract overrides them." (your jaw tightens around the last word)

Ari Nakamura: "Subcontracting is logistical necessity. We can include explicit clauses that prohibit forced displacement." (he flicks a slide; words become a frame) "I can work with legal teams."

Nora leans in beside you, her knuckles smudged from repairs and coffee, face a map of concentration.

Nora: "He makes it sound simple. Legal teams have their ideas of 'explicit'." (she gives you a quick, hard look) "If the clause is soft, it can be folded into standard indemnities. We need language. Concrete."

You: "We need language that survives audit and investor pressure. We need it in the contract tonight so we can say yes in the morning." (your throat is dry; the strings in the room quicken)

Ari's smile narrows in a way you can't quite read. He isn't unkind; he is strategic.

Ari Nakamura: "If you bring the clause, I'll carry it through my channels. We can make this pilot happen without delay."

The timekeeper's tablet blinks: five minutes remaining. Around you, the atrium moves like a held breath.

[Visual: Cut to side table with legislative pads and a stack of crisp contract pages]
[Sound: Paper rustle, a pen cap snapped]

You gather the printed draft while hearing, through the atrium's glass, the distant chant of people on the promenade — Luca's voice rising in the rain like a second tide.

Nora: "You can trust him, or you can try to change the sails while the ship is already leaving. Both are violent in different ways." (she taps a line in the contract) "Look here — the indemnity phrasing, the subcontractor pass-through. It's ambiguous."

Your heart has gone into a kind of syncopated sprint: you are used to long projects that creep forward like tide lines; tonight, decisions must be made in minutes. Your fingers move over the paper, finding margins and weak verbs.

[Scene: Glass Council Chambers | Early Evening — Hearing in Session]
[Visual: High glass chamber; council maps glow along one wall; lights are cold white]
[Music: Percussive, urgent beats; a metronome beneath the strings]
[Sound: Microphone feedback; distant thunder]

You enter the chamber with Nora and the contract folded under your arm. Councilwoman Elena Reyes' gaze is sharp and polite; she is balancing re-election and the city's safety as if both are ceramic plates on a high pole. Ari takes the podium with the calm of someone who has argued for grant money in cities you've only studied.

Ari Nakamura: "This pilot is the city's next demonstration project. We have matching funds, international interest, and a timeline." (he gestures to the timeline projected, bright and linear)

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: "We need something that can be defended in court and presented to constituents. Maya, your endorsement matters." (her eyes flick to you, then to the legal counsel)

You: "My endorsement will be public only if we hardline the protections." (you speak slowly, each word chosen like a rivet)

Legal Counsel: "We can draft an amendment. But it will take review. There's a procurement calendar —"

Nora: "Procurement calendars are how 'soon' turns into 'never'." (she snaps)

The legal counsel's jaw tightens; he shifts through pages. The chamber's temperature feels artificially low, but your palms are warm with adrenaline. This is the very definition of a pressure-cooker: policy, memory, and love compressed into legalese.

Your wristband vibrates — a text from an unknown number, terse and technocratic: "Subcontractor pass-through in draft allows assignment to third-party firms. Watch clause 12A5." The message is the kind of whisper that unravels a plan. The sender is a tech contact you've used before — trust is partial.

You: "Nora, did you see this? It suggests the contractor could reassign obligations to a third party that doesn't honor exclusions."

Nora's eyes flash. She reads the draft again, fingers tracing a paragraph that looks like legal weather-worn print.

Nora: "It's there in legal syntax — not spelled out, but structural. You'd have to lock the chain: no assignment without community consent, binding arbitration that favors tenants, a non-waiver clause." (she lists possibilities like a mechanic naming parts)

You feel the chamber contracting around the choice. If you demand that language now, the project could stall and funding might dry. If you let it pass, you will carry the burden of what follows.

Luca Chen: "What if the council says 'pilot' and then the contractor quietly relocates tenants under 'safety' clauses? We don't have time for slow promises."

Luca Chen: "Then decide, Maya. Do you stand with us first, or with them?" (he isn't asking—he's giving you no room)

You reread the clause the tech contact hinted at — the pass-through line that could permit reassignment to a subcontractor whose priorities aren't local. Nora: "If you amend, you need legal wording that cannot be reinterpreted. If you force a public pledge, you make Ari accountable in the court of politics, but not legally. If you refuse, you preserve control but risk punitive procurement responses and lost funding."

You think of Samir's slow hands teaching you how to stitch a net — every tightening knot matters. You think of Luca's face at the rally, of Ari's unreadable expression in the atrium. You imagine the marsh in three possible futures.

Everything in you weighs the same: protection, time, dignity, control.

The strings climb. Your breath shortens. This is the escalation you knew would come — negotiations compressed into a moment where policy and people collide.

You lift the pen.
