Maya (to the gathered group): "We asked for oversight. We asked for funding for stewardship. We asked that histories not be erased for convenience."

The final lever is pulled. The floodgates close; hydraulics sing. Water that would have licked at the boardwalk is held back. For a long, suspended second, the world tilts toward a future where roofs stay dry and fishmongers don't count losses on spreadsheets.

A cheer starts small — a neighboring child's shout — and then it breaks like sunrise. People clap, some cry. You feel the gravity of the moment in every cell: the tide is kept at bay and with it immediate, measurable harm.

Narration
And yet the celebration has edges. At the fringe of the crowd, you see a small moving truck, its back open, furniture wrapped. Across the boardwalk, an old woman folds a photograph into a towel, eyes blank. A fisherman you know packs a wooden crate and looks at the seawall with a gaze you can't translate into a single language.

Luca stands off to the side, hands in his pockets, expression compressed. When his eyes find you, there is a kind of relieved rage there — glad that roofs are saved, angry at what those savings required.

Luca (quiet, when you meet later): "They'll call this progress. They'll call you the face of reasonable leadership. But what about the song we used to sing to the boats before the city started speaking in diagrams?"

Maya: "I asked for oversight. I tried to keep rituals alive. We put funding in. It's... it's not nothing."

Luca: "Not nothing is not the same as not losing."

The storm presses against the glass of the world. The wall holds. For the city, this is an epochal success. For you, it is both victory and a wound that will not close cleanly.

Maya: "You made something big, Ari. You convinced those who needed convincing. We have roofs that won't flood tonight."

You both know what else existed between those lines — the times you sat across from each other in late seminars, the terse debates that became flirtatious in their intensity, the nights your hands brushed on shared files. The partnership forming is professional, yes, but friction always carries heat.

Ari (lowering his voice): "There'll be more projects. Bigger scales. We can take what we learned here and make sure other neighborhoods don't have to make the same painful trades."

Maya (looking out at the wall, feeling the weight): "Maybe. But more projects mean more power to someone. Power doesn't always carry the songs we live by."

Ari (looks at you, unguarded for a fraction): "Then make it carry them. Stay at my side on the oversight board. Help me build the models that include ritual."

Maya: "And what if the models say relocation is cheaper, faster, more efficient?"

Maya: "You want me as a tool."

Dialogue continues — slower, more electric, each turn revealing more.

Maya: "Partners make compromises."

Ari: "Partners also guard each other's lines."

Maya: "I guard people, not projects."

Ari: "And I guard projects that guard people."

The exchange hangs like a seam pulled taut. You're exhausted; the victory is rawened by loss. Yet there's a current of possibility that feels dangerous and hopeful.

[Scene: Tideside — Dawn, Weeks Later — A Private Moment]
[Visual: The seawall is visible in the haze; the sunrise cuts through salt-smeared air. A single bench faces the water. The city is quieter here, a soft under-breath of a place that has been remade.]

Narration
You come at dawn. The city is still exhaling from the ceremony; lights wink out. The seawall is a steady, honest thing in the dour morning. You hold your battered notebook and your bracelet from the marsh, small anchors that remind you of where you came from and whom you promised.

Luca (looking at the wall): "It works. It held."

Maya (voice small): "It worked."

Luca (turning): "Are you happy?"

Maya (honest): "Relieved. Terrified. Proud. Guilty. All at the same time. The wall keeps people dry. People are alive because of it."

Luca: "Some of our people are leaving."

Luca: "Options are not always choices, Maya. Money, time, exhaustion — those are pressures too."

Luca (soft, raw): "I wanted us to fight together against displacement, not have the world rearranged around containment."

Maya (reaching, then stopping): "I won't forget them intentionally. But memories fade if you don't turn toward them sometimes."

Maya: "It is. And we are still in it. We are still together in the city that was remade."

They walk away slowly, side by side but not touching, an intimacy that is not erased.
