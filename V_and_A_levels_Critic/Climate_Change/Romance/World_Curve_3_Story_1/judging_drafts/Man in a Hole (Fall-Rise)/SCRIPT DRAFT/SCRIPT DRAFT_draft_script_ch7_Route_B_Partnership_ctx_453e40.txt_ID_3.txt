[Scene: Municipal Green Atrium | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The atrium's glass panes diffuse the stormlight into cool teal. Mangrove planters cast patterned shadows across polished floor tiles. A projection flickers with Ari’s clean renderings beside a schematic of marsh restoration pinned in chalk.]
[Music: Tense BGM—pulsing strings undercut with a tight electronic rhythm]
[Sound: Rain hammers the glass; distant gulls cry. The click of a microphone and the low murmur of umbrellas being closed]

You step into the atrium with Nora at your shoulder and Luca a half-step behind, the three of you folding into the clinical hush that hangs under the hanging ferns. Your field notebook is closed in your palm, the edges of the pages damp from the walk. You remember why you chose this path—because compromise felt like survival, not surrender—then you remember the pilot that failed and the faces that trusted you and were burned. The memory sits heavy and salted on your tongue.

Ari stands near the projection, back straight in a way that makes the lines of their blazer look like authority itself. They smile with the practiced warmth of someone used to sealing deals; the smile lands differently here, thinner against the smell of wet earth and the smell of people who have been here longer than the renderings.

Ari Nakamura: "Thank you all for coming. The firm is prepared to fund a pilot at the Tideside that combines a reinforced seawall with engineered marsh terraces. We propose a six-month deployable phase and a twenty-year maintenance plan with municipal oversight."

You hear the numbers—timeframes, funding amounts—like measurements of tide. Useful. Fast. Visible. But the things you also measure—the braided lines of community memory, the way Samir hums old boat names—aren't on Ari’s slides.

You: "The terms you describe sound...effective on paper. I need local advisory seats written into governance, binding transparency clauses for procurement, and a contingency fund controlled by residents before any ground is broken."

Ari blinks, then inclines their head the fraction of a degree that reads as calculation. Their voice smooths.

Ari Nakamura: "Binding clauses can be drafted. Transparency—yes—but remember, the city needs the project to be...legible to outside investors. Flexibility in procurement allows us to respond quickly to storm events."

Councilwoman Elena Reyes sits a few steps down, slate tablet balanced on her knee. Her face is a practiced plane of neutrality, but her hands are small and exact on the tablet’s bezel.

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: "Speed matters. We have projected storm models that show a narrowing window for implementation. The Board will want measurable reductions in inundation in the first year."

Your chest tightens. Measurable. Legible. The language is precise; it skirts the human grain.

Nora hums under her breath, the noise a small anchor.

Nora: (softly) "We can write oversight. We can insist on community procurement for upkeep. We have volunteers, we have knowledge. It just takes—"

Luca cuts in before she can finish, voice low like a rope pulled taut.

Luca Chen: "It takes you handing the neighborhood over to a private agenda if you sign those blanks. 'Transparency' is a nice word until we're the ones whose jobs are deemed 'inefficient' and outsourced."

Ari’s smile is a fraction colder now.

Ari Nakamura: "We're not seeking displacement. We're offering resilience at scale. The city cannot wait for slower green solutions to mature if the next storm is the one that erases lives."

You watch Luca's hands. They are callused, used to mending nets, not documents. The way his jaw sets is a storm in miniature.

You: "Ari, what guarantees do you offer that residents retain decision-making authority over maintenance and that contractors are locally hired? Not aspiration—hard clauses."

Ari Nakamura: (leaning forward) "We can codify local hiring targets, we can set advisory seats, and the firm is open to an independent audit committee. But we cannot put a clause that freezes technical response times—"

Luca snaps, louder:

Luca Chen: "So when the 'technical response' comes in and says 'relocate this community' because it's cheaper to move people than to maintain, what then? Who signs off on that? You think an 'audit committee' will keep developers from deciding our fate?"

The air tightens. Around you, the atrium feels smaller—glass and steel closing in like a room remembering how to hold a fight.

Ari Nakamura: (controlled) "We propose shared governance during the pilot. If the committee finds the design harmful, steps can be halted. But halting means delaying citywide protections. Do you accept the compromise of a pilot that's monitorable and fast? Or do you risk nothing happening while the coast keeps climbing?"

Your internal voice lists the calculus: faster protection equals fewer immediate homes flooded; slower, community-led measures may better preserve culture but could leave people vulnerable this season. Your responsibility presses on you like tide against a seawall—constant, insistent.

Ari's eyes narrow slightly as if recalibrating optics.

Ari Nakamura: "Naming the panel now is politically viable, but we need agreed milestones to unlock City funds. We're all accountable to constituents."

You: "Agreed milestones must include resident-controlled maintenance budgets and the right to veto contractors with conflicts of interest."

The word 'veto' lands like a small, sharp stone. For a beat, the atrium breathes with the aftershock.

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: "Ari, if we can present a document with those safeguards, will your firm commit to an open procurement process and joint stewardship clauses?"

Ari Nakamura: (a fraction warmer) "Yes. With legal oversight. With quarterly audits accessible to the public."

Nora squeezes your arm, quick and fierce. Her face is wind-burned; she looks at you like someone asking permission to believe.

Nora: "That's something. It's not everything, but it's something we can work from."

Then Luca doesn't take "something." He steps forward so that the planting behind him rustles, and when he speaks again the words are sharp enough to cut the poised cadence of the meeting.

Luca Chen: "Something won't undo the land trusts they plan to redevelop. Something won't stop families from losing rentals because a new 'resilience corridor' suddenly has a business-friendly tax break attached. You all know how this ends if we let marketable safety write the rules."

Ari's clenched composure slams into a new mode—less polished advocate, more steel under pressure.

Ari Nakamura: "I am aware of the historical inequities. My firm has mitigation clauses in place. We can structure easements so they do not become instruments of displacement."

Luca: (shouting now) "Easements become law. Law becomes leverage. You speak of 'mitigation' the same way developers speak of 'cleanup'—as a balm for what your renderings take."

The atrium erupts—voices, the rattle of a chair leg, a camera phone clicking. Councilwoman Reyes raises a hand and the room reduces to a thready silence, as if the glass itself is listening.

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: (firm) "Enough. This will not be decided by yelling. Maya—" (her eyes land on you; she uses your given name like a tether) "—the Board needs a concrete recommendation today. Public confidence hinges on a clear path."

You swallow. The blood in your ears is the sound of obligation. You are between an engineering promise that will look good on city timelines and the messy, living fidelity of your neighborhood.

Your mind scrambles to balance legal clawbacks, community labor, measurable flood reduction, and the subtle, indispensable thing that is place—the smell of the marsh at dawn, the cadence of Samir's stories. One wrong clause and you become the hand that hands them over.

Luca's choice—whether to be conduit or cannon—hangs like storm-cloud weight over the negotiation. You can feel the tempo building; each syllable now nudges the meeting toward a hinge: acceptance with clauses that might be enforceable, or rejection and the chance that the city moves ahead without you, with Ari's firm deciding how 'community' is defined.

Ari's face is hard in a way that used to be reserved for spreadsheets and boardrooms. Now it sharpens toward something personal.

Ari Nakamura: (quietly) "Maya, if we cannot come to a workable agreement now, the Board will vote on the city's proposal as-is. It won't have the community safeguards you want."

The statement is an ultimatum dressed in municipal procedure. The atrium feels suddenly like a courtroom, the plants like jurors.

Your notebook sits heavy in your hand, a small dry weight against a very wet world. You think of the pilot that failed—of a ledger of promises unpaid—and you think of the sleeping person in the room who trusted you to translate grief into policy.

Nora's fingers find yours and squeeze.

Nora: (low) "Whatever you say next, make it count."

You inhale the greenhouse air. It smells of damp leaves and disinfectant; it smells like the city trying to be kind.

[Visual: The projection switches—Ari's render blurs into a timeline with a highlighted date for immediate phase one approval.]

[Sound: The atrium's ventilation hum rises; rain becomes a drumbeat on the glass, faster, insistent.]

Councilwoman Elena Reyes: (decisive) "We will move this to the Glass Council Chambers for the formal vote. You will have three minutes to state your coalition's position. Papers will be on the dais."

The room's light narrows to a sunbeam down a corridor. The route forward is procedural, inevitable.

You stand with your people behind you, feeling the pressure like a line of water. The choice you are about to voice will ripple outward—into contracts, into living rooms, into the map of who keeps their home.

You walk toward the chambers with your team trailing, the city’s authoritative air clamping around your shoulders. Lights focus; cameras tilt. Councilwoman Reyes meets your gaze once—steady, expectant.

You stand at the dais. The clock above the chamber is a small, beating thing.

Your mouth forms words. You are about to speak.
