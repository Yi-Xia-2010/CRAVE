[Scene: Seawall Promenade | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Salt-slick boardwalk, battered benches, distant skyline blurred by spray]
[Sound: Low gull calls, water lapping against algae-darkened pilings]
[Music: Soft, low-key instrumental — a single guitar chord repeated like a tide]

You step onto the promenade with your waterproof notebook tucked under your arm, the strap of your field jacket damp with the day's humidity. The air tastes of iron and old seaweed; a warm weight presses at the base of your throat that has nothing to do with weather. Light is thinning toward that peculiar hour between gold and gray — the kind of light that makes pilings shine and hides the finer cracks.

Pieces of last year's patchwork are still visible: planks replaced in a hurry, nails pounded blunt and close together, a braided rope knotted where a board had split. Someone's paint marker has scrawled a maintenance note into the grain: "NORA + CREW — CHECK DRAIN." Your chest tightens for a second with a familiarity that is equal parts pride and gingered fear. You know where each scar came from; you can read the chronology of small, stubborn repairs across the walk like a map of the neighborhood's refusal to let itself wash away.

Samir is on the far bench, hands folded over his knee, eyes on the horizon. He looks smaller than you remember, carved like driftwood, but his presence steadies the promenade in a way that no municipal plan ever could. He nods when he sees you, slow and sure, as if time itself is approving your return.

Samir: "Maya. The tide's coming up clean today. Good day to remember what still holds."

You don't answer him out loud. You only let your fingertips drag once along your notebook's cover, feeling the raised scuffs where seawater and salt have etched your name into the leather. Fingers that have measured mud and measured loss. You don't want to make promises right now — not aloud. Not until you've counted the costs again.

[Scene: Old Docks | Dusk]
[Visual: Warehouse turned hub, strings of warm bulbs and patches of vertical garden; nets hang drying]
[Sound: Murmur of voices, a kettle's hiss, the soft clank of tools on a bench]
[Music: Quiet, domestic undertone — distant chatter threaded through the guitar]

The Old Docks Hub smells like citrus oil and wet rope. Nora is bent over a workbench, sleeves rolled, a smear of rust-colored grease across her knuckles. When she looks up and sees you, her face brightens in that quick, mischievous way that always makes it harder to stay grave.

Nora: "About time you showed up. Tide report or just nostalgia?"

You: "A little of both." (You shrug, trying to sound casual. The notebook presses against your ribs like ballast.)

Nora: "Good. Luca's outside, trying to convince half the stairwell that we're still a city and not a puddle." (She winks.) "Also — council packet copies. And Samir says hi, if you didn't scare him with your tide graphs."

You move through the hub, taking in the scatter of familiar objects: a chalkboard full of scrawled schedules, a wall of donated tools, a table where a pot of coffee cools between two mismatched mugs. The hub is noise and warmth and necessary chaos — the place where plans get knotted to reality.

Luca leans on a post outside, wind-tousled hair and a rope belt heavy with things that could fix a hull or a heart, depending on how you look at him. He grins when you approach, the kind of grin that's more a small sun than an expression. His boots leave wet impressions on the threshold.

Luca: "You're back. Notebook and all. Did the tide keep good notes for you?" (He pushes a paper across the table — a small grant outline someone from City put online.)

You: "City put out a municipal grant. Elena's looking for proposals to 'scale resilience'." (You pause, saying 'scale' like it's a word that might tip the balance of everything.) "They're scheduling a hearing next week. We have to pitch."

Luca: "Next week. Of course they did. Fast track when the lights are pretty and the banners smell of money." (He rubs his palms together.) "But it's an opportunity. We could secure steady funding, materials, a crew..."

Nora: "And inspectors." (She snorts.) "And strings attached. You know how these things go."

Luca: "We know." (He looks at you now, serious for the first time.) "We know how you feel about last year's pilot, Maya. We know what you carry."

You breathe, letting the words circle. The last pilot had been a handful of living berms and planted reeds that you all had hoped would accrete silt and soften the wave. It failed in places because repairs weren't funded, materials were cut, and a lightning reimbursement schedule turned everyone into accountants instead of stewards. You still see the erosion where mud failed to hold, where a family nearly lost a boat they had lived with for generations. You feel its weight like a stone in your chest.

You: "I still wake up with that failure under my tongue sometimes." (You close your eyes against it.) "If we make promises again, I won't let them be polite lies. No quick fixes that force people to choose between a home and a check."

Nora: "Then we say it—plain. We say stewardship, long-term maintenance, community oversight. We don't dress it up with buzzwords."

Luca: "Council likes metrics, Maya. Elena breathes data. Ari's seawall proposal looks shiny because it has nice numbers and a model people can show donors." (He mimics a polished voice.) "'We can protect the city in X years, for Y dollars.'"

You feel the name like a small, cold stone. Ari's flagship project has been in the papers: a gleaming flood barrier woven with sensors and an urban canopy on top, presented as a single elegant solution that will impress conferences and secure the city's brand as 'resilient.' The pitch would be powerful; your neighborhood could be safeguarded — at a distance. But at what cost? The seawall's footprint might require relocation along the most vulnerable stretches, and its contracts would prioritize technicians from outside the community.

Nora: "He'll put charts up and make a great speech. Elena'll probably listen politely and say it's 'evidence-based.'"

You: "We don't need platitudes." (You run a thumb along the notebook, feeling the indentations where past calculations are stored. You tape your fingernail to a corner, a tiny anchor.) "We need a plan that the people who live here can hold. Maintenance schedules that don't evaporate when budgets are tight. Labor that keeps the neighborhood working, not displaced."

Luca reaches over, and his hand rests briefly on the notebook, the calluses along his palm a reminder of practical labor. His touch is not a romantic one in the moment; it's a shared vow.

Luca: "So we build the pitch around stewardship. Show them our numbers — but show them our faces too. Samir, the fishers, the kids that keep the nets. Make the human ledger as loud as the economic one."

Nora: "And be honest about the past. Tell them what went wrong with the pilot, what we learned. People respect candor more than recovered PR."

You swallow. There is a temptation to carry the shame silently, to tuck it into the notebook and let it curl with salt. But there's also the knowledge that hiding it will let the same mistakes happen again — patched, sold as 'lessons learned' with no new funding for repair. You want, more than you want anything, to be useful without being the scapegoat.

You choose words that feel true to the room, balancing the ledger with the faces. As you speak, Luca and Nora fill the air with small clarifications and vows — who will call Council, which maps to bring, who can quantify labor hours into a budget line. Their voices are warm and practical; frictionless in the way of people who have done hard things together.

Nora: "I'll get Samir to come to the hearing. His testimony about the old marsh boundaries — it changes the narrative when it's not just numbers."

Luca: "I'll pull together footage of the living berms from last year. Show where it worked — even the small places. Show the repair logs."

You: "I'll draft the pitch. A clear ask, a maintenance fund line, a clause for local labor priorities. And —" (You hesitate, the sentence hovering like a gull in the light.) "— we'll ask for co-management, not just a contract."

Ari's name comes up again, softer this time, as if testing the air to see what it holds.

Nora: "Does Ari know we're pitching?"

Luca: "Of course." (He makes a small dismissive sound.) "He has his meetings with Elena. Press photos. But he also knows this neighborhood has a voice now. Maybe he wants to work with us, maybe he wants to steamroll us. Either way, Elena hears both."

You imagine Ari in the Municipal Green Atrium: clean lines, reflective pools, his AR cuff showing projected charts. It's a different kind of light than the one that slants across the docks. His conviction sometimes reads to you like kindness — a belief that systems and data can save people. Other times, it reads like a blink, missing the ways people measure protection in kitchens and nets rather than in models.

Luca: "We have one week. We practice. We rehearse. We show up with people, not just slides."

You nod. The hub seems to lean in with you, the warm bulb light pressing closer, the kettle hissing as if in agreement. Outside, the sky has changed; the storm-glow gathers on the horizon, not yet angry, but it colors everything with a brittle promise. It is the light you were thinking about: that mix of gold and steel that makes ways of being look at once possible and precarious.

You feel the old pilot's failure like a shadow at the base of your decision. You vow — again, quietly, to yourself — not to let your community be a footnote in someone else's headline. You also know that refusing every outside hand could strand your plans in underfunded idealism. The tension between stewardship and engineering stands in the room like an unspoken choice, tidy on paper but ragged in practice.

You pick the next step that fits your pace — measured, deliberate. The conversation dissolves into the practical: who will bring which maps, which testimonies are strongest, what language will persuade Elena without erasing the community. Multi-layered sentences and interruptions weave together — you then they, you then them — as plans gather form.

Nora: "Also — small thing. If we get funding, we set aside money just for emergency repairs, no questions. No administrative hoops. A fund the community can access for patchwork."

Luca: "And training. We hire and train local crews. No surprises."

You: "Agreed. And I'm writing the maintenance schedule into the ask. Regular audits, yes, but audited by the community."

Silence sits for a breath, and then it stretches into the sound of tools and the scrub of mugs. You find yourself smiling at a memory of the pilot — the small patch of reeds that stood stubborn against a backwash for an extra month because a neighbor went out every dawn and tended them. That stubbornness is the neighborhood's muscle; you want to protect it.

A light wind slips through the warehouse door and brings a salt tang and the distant rumble of a tide building. It is small and persistent and entirely present. The storm-glow on the horizon deepens a shade, painting the clouds with an edge that reads like an impending question.

You close your notebook and feel the weight of what you will have to say in front of Elena next week. You rehearse, in your head, the cadence of acknowledgment and insistence you need to hold — the candor about failure and the clear, uncompromising demand for stewardship.

This is the moment before the hearing; a low, steady pressure that asks you to commit to a strategy of care or to yield to the seductive efficiency of a sealed, engineered answer. Either route will ask of you different kinds of courage.

You step to the doorway, letting the hub's warmth spill around you. The promenade waits, and beyond it the city that will listen in different languages: petitions, press, contracts, private meetings.

You look once more at the horizon where the storm-glow gathers — the sea laid out like a question.

A small, inward vow settles in your chest. You will not make the same quiet mistakes again. Whatever you do next will be done with hands that know mud and with a ledger that includes the names of people who will live with the outcome.
