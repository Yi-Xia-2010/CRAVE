You remember lectures, late nights in a lab that smelled faintly of seaweed and solder, the quiet collaboration that became a clean schematic in someone else's slide deck. You feel equivalently proud and sick.

Luca Chen: "Protection that looks like a wall is protection that pushes some of us out of the picture. A seawall alone reroutes the water and the problem. It does not hold memory, it does not hold livelihoods. It makes a clean line on a map where people's lives are messier."

Councilmember (murmur): "But Luca, living shorelines take time. They're riskier during extreme events."

Luca Chen: "Risk doesn't land equally. When the grant runs out and the pilot is half-fixed, who pays? When an investment raises the value of a block and evictions follow—who pays? We are asking for stewardship, not a headline."

The exchange becomes layered, a boulder rolling into a shallow channel: Ari speaks of scale and certainty; Luca speaks of labor and heritage. The room tightens. The microphones pick up the small noises: the scrape of a chair, a child's cough in the gallery.

Nora Díaz: "Benchmarks mean money gets cut if the marsh needs time. Living systems don't run on spreadsheets."
