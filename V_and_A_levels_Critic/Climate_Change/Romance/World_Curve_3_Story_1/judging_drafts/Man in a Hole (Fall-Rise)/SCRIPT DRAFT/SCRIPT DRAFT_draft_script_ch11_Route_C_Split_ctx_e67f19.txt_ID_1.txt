[Visual: The atrium's glass panes are flecked with rain; the projection of tidal models throws pale blue schematics across the wet floor.]

You are still standing with your thumb pressed against the seam of the drive. The projection on your wrist blinks red again—an insistence you cannot ignore. The air smells of ozone and the mineral tang of wet concrete; voices in the room are close as breath and twice as crowded with meaning.

Ari Nakamura: "Let me be clear." (They step forward, calm like a blade.) "Public scandal will slow everything. Time is not neutral here; the storm schedule isn't waiting for headlines."

You hear the practiced cadence in Ari's voice—data-shaped, intended to steady the room. Their eyes flick to the projection, to the charts, then back to you. There is something open in their posture that could be an offer or a trap; you do not know which.

Luca Chen: "And secrecy won't save us if corners get cut to speed things up." (He spits the word "secrecy" like it has a taste.) "We can't let the neighborhood be collateral for a fancy ribbon-cutting."

Nora Díaz: (arms crossed, jaw working) "You should archive that drive in three places before you make any move. If anyone in the hall gets wind—" (she looks at you, thin laugh) "—we're dead in more ways than one."

Samir Qureshi: (quiet, measured) "Truth without a map brings only noise. If you shout and we can't protect the people after, the shout becomes a wound."

The room spins small and sharp around their words. You can feel the arousal in your chest—pressure building like the tide line on a window. Every plan in your head scrapes against the same hard fact: the evidence can change the balance of power in a night. It can save people. It can ruin them. It can save you. It can ruin you.

You step to the projection, the paper texture in your hand suddenly heavier than it ought to be.

Maya Ortega: "I didn't pull this drive out to play judge." (Your voice is low but steady.) "I pulled it out because I owe people the chance to choose with the whole picture visible. That's the point."

Ari Nakamura: (tilting their head) "And you think the whole picture won't be misread? Misused? Maya—" (their voice softens) "—this isn't just about optics. If you leak, you hand the city a narrative that's hard to control. You hand reporters partial diagrams and they print fear."

The word "leak" lands like an impact. Your hands remember the feel of the drive: cold plastic, a smear of salt where a seam meets your nail. Everything after that seam is potential—documented meetings, drafts of clauses, a private exchange between developers and a council aide. Proof.

Luca leans in until the scent of salted rope and diesel is close enough to sting your throat.

Luca Chen: "Or you hand us leverage. Oversight happens when people can't smooth over the mess. People who sign checks don't like their names in public. They move." (He meets your eyes.) "We can make them move where we want them to. If we don't—if we wait for negotiation—we might wake up to blue tarp and notices."

Ari's jaw tightens like a drawn wire.

Ari Nakamura: "You think public shaming is leverage? It breaks trust faster than any contract. You will fracture partnerships at a time when we can still get legally binding stewardship clauses into agreements."

Nora interrupts, sharp.

Nora Díaz: "You love clauses, Ari. You can sit in an atrium and cross Ts until the sea takes the porch. Some of us need action now."

Their voices ricochet, a volley. You feel the tempo of the room climb; each sentence accelerates the heartbeat under your ribs.

You notice Ari's hand hover near their AR cuff, an offer of a quiet channel.

Ari Nakamura: "Let's do this differently. I can open a municipal oversight window—legally binding. My firm will sign to guarantee community stewardship language. We make the inspections transparent. You let me broker the process with the council, and we avoid a public blowup."

Luca's laugh is short, almost a bark.

Luca Chen: "Broker. Right. You expect us to believe a 'broker' won't be tempted by speed and a ribbon. What's the guarantee? An honor system?"

Ari: (measured) "Not honor. Contracts. Monitored milestones. Penalties. We write it into the procurement."

You smell the coppery tang of adrenaline now. The atrium's lighting seems to harden into a spotlight on your face. You can almost taste the sea on your tongue—the metallic mix of incoming rain and salt—and it anchors you in a way that sharpens decision-making rather than softening it.

Samir clears his throat, every syllable deliberate.

Samir Qureshi: "If you go loud, make a plan to protect the people who will be hurt by the noise. If you go quiet, make a plan to make the quiet ironclad for those same people. If you walk away, do not tell me you left because it was 'too political.'"

Your chest tightens. The room narrows to the faces you see reflected in the floodlit projection: Mrs. Alvarez's little storefront, the varnisher's kids, Samir's granddaughter. Each face is a ledger entry you keep in the back of your head.

Maya Ortega: (you say, no pretense of calm) "I need leverage that doesn't endanger residents at the docks. I need ironclad protections, and I need a way to enforce them. I also need to know that truth won't just be a headline that leaves our people exposed."

Nora nods sharply.

Nora Díaz: "Then make them prove it here, in front of community. Let them write it down with us in the room. Let them put funds in an escrow that only releases after milestones. Inspections, community stewards, real penalties."

Ari folds their hands, considering.

Ari Nakamura: "That is negotiable. But we cannot do all of it in public and keep the legal teeth we need. Public pressure blunts legal negotiation. We have to choose a channel."

Luca slams a palm on the projection table; the charts jump and the tide models buzz in the silence.

Luca Chen: "You're proposing the usual backroom with prettier words. If you pitch that to the community as 'we saved you' when it's a slow bleed—" (he meets your eyes, raw) "—I won't be able to stand next to you when that happens."

The air snaps.

[Scene: Old Docks | Night — Later]
[Visual: The docks are a cluster of lamplight and tarps; the smell of fish and diesel is stronger here. Wind knifes the water; the city skyline is a distant, shimmering threat.]
[Music: Percussive beats become prickly, urgent — a heartbeat sampled and sped up]
[Sound: Boards creak; a loose tarp flaps; a radio somewhere plays an indistinct bulletin.]

You walk the docks with the group, the drive now somewhere between Nora's jacket and your internal decision. Steps are quick, shoes thrumming on wet planks. Voices drop to a different kind of intensity here—less public rhetoric, more raw calculation.

Nora Díaz: "We can set up a community escrow. We've done the math. If council matches seed funds and the legal language is explicit—" (she pulls a soaked tablet from her bag, scrolling) "—we can make the stewardship non-transferable. That means even a buyer can't cancel it."

Samir hums, a low approval.

Samir Qureshi: "And the elders will sign. If the community signs a covenant, it has weight. It ties memory to law."

Ari: (leaning against a piling, voice quiet) "Maya, I can draft clauses now. I can send them to my legal team. When the council meets tomorrow, I can table this as a motion with my signature. It will be faster if we do it without the glare."

Luca: "Faster how? Faster into someone's pocket? How do we know those clauses won't be carved with loopholes later?"

Ari: (soft) "Because we make the penalty structure non-negotiable—funds held in a restricted municipal account controlled by a community oversight board. We put in auditors. We make the release contingent on community-led milestones. I can do that."

Maya Ortega: (you say) "And if they still try to move people? If developers find another lever?"

Ari Nakamura: "Then we make those levers public. We make the procurement process transparent at every step. I will not let that happen."

Luca laughs, sharp and disbelieving.

Luca Chen: "You promise. You promise a lot, Ari. Promises don't stop bulldozers."

Ari meets Luca's gaze. Their eyes are unreadable—complex, not vacant. For a blink, something like regret crosses Ari's features, quickly masked by resolution.

Ari Nakamura: "I know what bulldozers cost. I also know how to make the cost too high to ignore. I offer you process, Maya. I offer you a way to bind them."

Nora: "And if they don't play fair? If they repackage the project and cut the community out later?"

You feel the arousal ratchet again—higher now, voices tighter, decisions sharpening into points.

[Scene: Old Docks Community Hub | Later Night]
[Visual: The Hub is a ring of warm bulbs, a scatter of maps, tide models pinned to wet plywood. The smell of strong coffee cuts through salt and oil.]
[Music: Tension peaks into a sharp, sustained synth that refuses to resolve]
[Sound: The hub's tin roof rings with a fierce, close rain; a radio counts down to the next bulletin]

You gather under the bulb-light; hands, maps, and whispered plans form the only shelter you can find. Each of them looks to you with something like expectation and accusation braided together.

Nora: "Whatever you do, Maya, make sure you protect the people who will get shouted at first. You know how mobs move—folk get targeted for being the face of a story."

Luca: "If you leak, half the city will cheer and half will want revenge. That's how it goes. People get radicalized in headlines."

Ari: (hands open, earnest) "If you negotiate, you hold the instruments. If you walk away, you keep your hands clean but you lose the leverage to shape outcomes. Each path trades one harm for another."

Samir: "We are a small boat in a big swell. You can steer, but you will not always pick the sea's direction."

You close your eyes for a second and feel the room's rhythm—fast, insistent, like a machine revving for a fight. This is the apex of the night: arguments have collided, options have been laid bare, and the pressure to act presses you from both sides.

Your internal monologue runs through a checklist—safety, enforceability, time, cultural cost. Nothing sits cleanly. Each element pulls another way.

Maya Ortega: (you speak, voice steady against the rush) "I can leak. I can negotiate. I can walk away and keep doing the work that keeps people dry today. I am not asking for permission from anyone in this room to choose. I'm telling you the choice is mine."

Nora looks at you, eyes wet and resolute.

Nora Díaz: "Whatever you pick, we'll be with you in it. But be strategic. You're the one with the drive. You're the one with the face of the argument."

Ari's expression softens for a moment—complex, unreadable.

Ari Nakamura: "Whatever you decide, make us accountable to the community. Not just to headlines, not just to contracts."

Luca steps so close the salt on his sleeve rubs your shoulder.

Luca Chen: "And if you pick 'quiet' and they betray us—then I will never forgive you for being anything but cautious."

His words are not a threat; they are a promise shaped like a hinge.

The rain outside becomes a drumroll. The tide model on your wrist blinks red at an eagerness that feels almost predatory. The room holds its breath.

You feel the arousal cresting—every sense sharpened, every option heavy with consequence—and you realize the next move will ripple outward in ways you cannot fully predict.
