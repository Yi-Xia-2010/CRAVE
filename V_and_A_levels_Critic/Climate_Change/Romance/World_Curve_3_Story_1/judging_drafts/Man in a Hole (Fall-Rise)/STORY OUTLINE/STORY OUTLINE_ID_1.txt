## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
A near-future coastal city has been remade by water. Sea levels have risen; reclaimed wetlands and retrofitted mid-century blocks form neighborhoods that cling to each other. The municipal government alternates between emergency response and grand infrastructural gestures: experimental glass atriums where planners model soft engineering, and polished council chambers where investment priorities are argued under LED timelines. Below the official projects, grassroots life persists in patched warehouses and salt-scarred docks: boatyards, communal kitchens, and volunteer planting shifts in the marshes. Culture is hybrid — fisherfolk rituals and data-driven resilience meetings sit side by side. Religion is muted to ritual and memory: memorials to lost boats and seasonal rites for safe tides. Class lines map onto the shore: longtime residents of the salt flats face displacement by capital-heavy seawalls; newer tech-funded developments promise safety but impose exclusions. The central conflict is structural and intimate — whether the neighborhood adopts nature-based, community-led resilience that preserves living culture or accepts large engineered defenses that protect more area but risk displacing neighbors. Atmosphere: humid, salt-thick air with stormlight slicing gold-and-steel sunsets; the art style is soft-realist watercolor, storm-washed, with hand-painted textures of algae and wet wood. Politics are local but stakes global — everyone can feel the tides shift.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Maya Ortega) return to the neighborhood bent on community-led, nature-based resilience. The city, pushed by investment-minded technocrats like Ari, proposes a flagship engineered seawall project promising rapid, measurable protection. My heart is with neighbors and marshes; my head is trained in environmental engineering. The Man-in-a-Hole arc begins: an early political setback and a failed pilot project push me into crisis — I lose funding, trust frays, and a storm exposes vulnerabilities (the fall). At the bottom, I must decide whether to double down on grassroots methods, accept hybrid compromises, or partner with Ari. Each romantic route (Luca — community steward; Ari — technocratic partner; staying independent) maps to a moral path for the neighborhood. Through three major decision hubs, I choose how to balance love, duty, and scale. Recovery comes from learning to combine approaches, or from hard-won integrity, or from compromise with cost. Endings vary: a happy community-hybrid with Luca; a bittersweet professional partnership with Ari; a "true" outcome of principled survival alone; or a sad result where politics and storms break what we tried to save. Each ending reflects real trade-offs between cultural continuity and technocratic security.

The choice to step away from paperwork and into the mud felt like a baptism both audacious and thin. I refused the tightening compromises and vouched, with what little we had, for continued local stewardship. We lost municipal grants and some people who could not survive without support moved away, but the people who stayed reshaped policy with collective muscle, not legal jargon. Nights were colder; supplies arrived slower; repairs took time. Yet our kitchen became louder, Samir told stories with his hands again, and kids learned to read tides by watching Luca braid lines. I did not find a tidy romance to anchor me.
