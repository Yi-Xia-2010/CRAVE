You step out and the marsh is speaking in a language you learned to listen to: water finding the channels you and the town carved into it, reed blades bending and letting surge pass instead of breaking. The sky is a leaden bowl; a last, fat cloud unspools rain along the horizon. Behind that cloud the storm that, months ago, would have cleaved this lot open is arriving like a test.

Your breath kicks higher. This is practice made manifest: months of soil work, of Evan's bolts and seals, Dr. Sora Kim's late-night spreadsheets, Tala's rosters of volunteers. Proof is not a neat line on a chart; it is this — the marsh drawing the water like a hand, the habitats bobbing and settling but not drowning.

You stand where the soil meets the boardwalk, every nerve keyed to the sound of her words. Data is beautiful when it bends toward hope, when it is not just numbers but a thing that can be held up and recognized.

Irene steps forward, unexpectedly leaving the safe perimeter of her entourage. Her voice is clear, not performative but surgical.

Irene Vale: "Impressive work. But scalable means numbers. What are the costs per unit, how do you ensure maintenance across years, and how do you guarantee this won't be overwhelmed by a larger surge?"

She is unsmiling but not hostile. Her questions are the ones that could have dismantled you in a boardroom; now they sound like someone sharpening a tool.

Maya Alvarez: "Maintenance is local. We designed the channels to accept surge and leave sediment, not to shove it into someone's porch."

Mayor Lionel steps in, visibly relieved.

Mayor Lionel: "If this means jobs, oversight, and a plan that keeps people in their homes, the town will stand together on it. We can institutionalize an oversight board with community representation."

Irene's gaze flicks to you, then to Captain Reyes, then to the Mayor. The corners of her mouth betray the smallest softening you've ever seen.

Irene Vale: "I'll propose a hybrid: structured defenses outside critical marsh corridors; larger investment in nature-based jobs; and —" (her voice drops, surprising you) "—a community oversight board with veto power over projects affecting key habitats."

A soft gasp moves through the crowd, and then — like an avalanche that has learned to be kind — applause breaks. It is not triumphant shouting. It is the sound of people realizing that what they feared would be a binary now has shading.

Irene Vale: "I didn't expect this to be conclusive," she says, folding her palms. "But data is data. My job is to secure Maris Bay's future. Maybe the future looks different than the renderings."
