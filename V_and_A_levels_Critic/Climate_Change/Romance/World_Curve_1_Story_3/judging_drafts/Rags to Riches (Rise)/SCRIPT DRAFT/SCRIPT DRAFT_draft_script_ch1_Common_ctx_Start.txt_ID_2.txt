You wake to brine in your mouth and the steady, wooden breath of the boardwalk under your boots. The sky is the color of old pewter, thin blue at the rim where the horizon tries to remember summer. You pull your field jacket tighter; the cotton is still damp with last night's mist. Your satchel bumps your hip — a quiet, familiar weight of seed packets folded in waxed paper and the copper barometer that was your mother's.

You bend to the reed beds near the dock. The temporary planters sag where the tide pushes air under them; roots lean toward the water as if listening. Fingers that know soil find the weak knots, press them back into place, check the mesh anchors. The reed tips skim your palms like cool breath. The smell here is thick with salt, seaweed, and something older — the memory of storms you have learned to name: June Surge, the Year of the Three High Tides. You trace the line where wet wood darkens and feel the slow muscle in your chest tighten.

You: "Okay. Hold. Anchor two and four, not three."

Your voice comes out small, the sound swallowed by fog. The barometer at your throat is warm from your skin; it has become a small, stubborn talisman against forgetting. You tuck a packet of cordgrass back into your satchel, thumb smoothing the printed label until the letters blur and become cadence — plant, bind, restore.

A photograph ghosts behind your eyes: your storefront, a painted sign half-submerged, the bell that used to ring when someone came in. The surge took the shop on a night that smelled like metal and thunder. The memory doesn't surge; it sits as a steady knot under your sternum. That knot is why you woke before dawn instead of sleeping in. That knot is why the rooftop nursery and the seed bank exist in the first place.

You check your phone. Mayor Lionel Park's message is pinned at the top: Preliminary briefing at ten — presence requested. Irene Vale's renderings attached.

You let your thumb swipe the attachments. Renderings bloom in harsh pixels: engineered seawalls, gleaming curves of concrete and steel, clean promenades that promise shops and jobs, but the captions are sterile — "marsh regrading," "flood-exclusion modules." The image edges look like a wound pressed to the coastline.

You fold your hands around a fistful of dirt and feel it crumble. The seed packets shift in your satchel. Dr. Sora Kim's latest email slides into your inbox with the quiet authority of someone who has watched too many tipping points approach and not enough people slow down: urgent notes, graphs, phrases you have learned to hear as alarms — "nonlinear threshold," "peat oxidation," "irreversible loss." Her signature is a neat loop that reads like a compass.

Tala: "You're at the dock? I'm five minutes out. Shoreline patrol got bumped earlier — volunteers need a quick briefing. You want to lead?"

You look down the boardwalk. The light lays stripes across the damp planks. Tala's voice messages are bright with noise and motion; she moves like wind through the town, shuttling people and hope. You could use the company. You could use every pair of hands.

Tala: "You okay? You look...flat."

You: "I'm fine. Morning habit. Check the anchors, swap seed labels. Mayor called a briefing for later."

Tala's smile loosens. Her fingers drum a rhythm against her thigh, restless. "I read. Irene Vale's team? People are split. Some say jobs, some say the marsh can't be traded. Everyone's asking me if the volunteers will protest. Honestly? They want to know if you have a plan."

You meet her gaze. You can see the weighing in her face — who to trust, who to hope in. Your throat feels tight.

You: "There isn't a solid plan yet. Dr. Sora sent data this morning that complicates the seawall idea: threshold risks that make the modeled benefits look less certain. We need the meeting to press for hybrid options, for pilot projects, not wholesale conversion."

Tala: "And evan? Is he coming? His desal kits could be a talking point — jobs and community-led tech."

You: "Evan's upgrading prototypes. He said he'd try to swing by the rooftop after dawn. But even if he comes, I can't promise engineering will sway the board without the science and our stories."

Tala: (softly) "Your stories matter, Maya. Captain Reyes will come. The older folks remember before the big developments. They remember what the marsh provides."

You imagine Captain Reyes's hands, the carved pipe in the crook of his elbow, the slow cadence of his tales. The town's memory often feels like weather: it settles over decisions unpredictably but with long-term force.

Tala watches you list priorities with your fingers: protect the marsh, keep people in their homes, scale Evan's desal kits, organize outreach. She says nothing for a beat, then nudges your shoulder with a grin that doesn't quite reach her eyes.

Tala: "You always do that—make lists out of the world. Fine. Let's make one more: keep Maya from turning into the person who sleeps in the atrium before a meeting."

You let a short, humorless laugh out. The boardwalk creaks under both of you like an old friend shifting. The ache in you is private and secret, and you tuck it under the shape of the next task.

[Scene: Rooftop Nursery | Mid-morning thin light]
[Visual: Patchwork of planters, seed tags fluttering, repurposed bottles catching dew]
[Sound: The drip of irrigation, the faint shuffle of two people passing]
[Music: Acoustic guitar, careful, patient]

You climb the narrow stairs to the rooftop nursery, soil tracking the soles of your boots. Green leans into the pale day — succulents sit like small, stubborn gardens and reed tags clack softly in the breeze. You open the cardboard crate of seedlings, inhale the slightly sour, alive-smell of new growth. This place is a ledger of small promises: each sprout a brief contract with the future.

Evan is there, sleeves rolled, soldering iron in one hand and a cup of something steaming at his side. He hasn't noticed you yet; his attention is close to the prototype beside him, hands measuring and re-measuring a microvalve. When he looks up, his face is the kind that takes in a landscape and remembers to care for the smallest part of it.

Evan: "You're up early."

You: "Couldn't sleep. You?"

Evan: "Prototype wouldn't let me. I kept dreaming about leaking seals." (He manages a crooked smile.) "I brought spare diaphragms. And coffee."

You: "Thanks. I—" The sentence washes out. Wanting him close feels like another task to steward, one with no clear steps.

Evan: "Hey. Whatever happens at that briefing, we have options. Desal kits can be run by community co-ops, training people, jobs that don't flatten the marsh. You know the specs."

You: "I know the specs and the politics." The admission tastes like rust. "But Irene's renderings look...too polished. People who need work see that polish and forget what the marsh does quietly. We need both: jobs and wetland. Not one or the other."

Evan: (hesitates) "You ever think about—" He stops and fidgets with a loose wire. He looks at you as if weighing a small, private risk. "—what it would look like if we made a pilot together. Public demo. You run the outreach, I run the tech. Side-by-side."

You fold your hands around a seedling, feeling its tiny stem. The ache in your chest pulses when his idea sketches toward something personal. The town asks for strategy, yes—but a part of you has been keeping a ledger of different debts: to the marsh, to the people, and to the small, brittle hope of having someone to rest against without the world being a ledger.

You: "A demo could help. It would also be a lot of pressure. And the briefing is in a few hours."

Evan: "We can keep it small. Keep it honest. No glossy promises."

You stare at the rooftop's edge where gulls trace the horizon. The barometer at your throat ticks faintly. You think of the store again, of boxes stacked and soaked through, and of how that night taught you to hold things together because otherwise no one would.

Evan: "And Maya—" He looks down, shy in the way inventors are when they risk feelings. "If you need me there, I'll be there."

You feel a tug. Duty on one side, a private ache on the other that looks very much like wanting — not escape, but a place to lay down the knot and be trusted with it.

[Music: A single piano chord, held]

You set the seedling down and straighten. The rooftop smells like damp cardboard and hopeful green things. Time compresses strangely; a coming meeting feels to be both far away and imminent, like the slow cresting of a tide.

Dr. Sora's email notification sounds again, insistent but quiet. You pull it open: a succinct paragraph, numbers sharpened to edges, followed by a simple directive — bring community testimony, emphasize marsh function, request hybrid modeling. Her final line is personal: "Don't let them sell certainty when the system offers only risk."

You look at the town across the bay, rooftops like small islands. Mayor Lionel Park's briefing will be the first public framing of Irene Vale's project. Irene's renderings are still warm on your phone when you slide it into the satchel. The satchel smells of waxed paper and wet cordgrass. Your hands are streaked with soil and salt, the day's tasks printed on your skin.

You lace your boots tighter. The boardwalk below looks like a path you've walked a thousand times: familiar, wooden, a little splintered at the edges. You feel the tug of duty — the town wants strategy, people want security, the marsh wants to be left alone. And there is the private ache again, a small longing that has nothing to do with policy but everything to do with what it means to carry hope with someone else.

You check your watch. The briefing is hours away. There are lists to make, volunteers to rally, Evan's demo to refine, Captain Reyes to call. The arousal in you tightens from a low hum into a focused intent; the morning is a series of small preparations that will lead to the meeting where the first public lines will be drawn.

You take one last look over the rooftop's low wall. A thin thread of light glances off the water, and for a second the sea looks like a sheet of hammered metal, both beautiful and indifferent.

You gather your satchel, tuck the seedlings, and head down. The boardwalk awaits; the town needs an argument, and you need to find the words that don't flatten the marsh into a footnote.

That is where your work grows teeth: in the telling, in the faces of the people you pull together.
