Tala appears beside you with the easy, knobby-hipped energy you've known since childhood. She smells faintly of sea-salt sunscreen and the plastic from her volunteer vest. Her clipboard is a small weapon; her eyes are lightning.

Tala: "You saw the turnout. Half the pier crew is here. Captain Reyes is—" (she taps her chin) "—he looks like he's about to speak. Are you going to—what are you doing, Maya?"

You feel the question like a current tugging at your ankles. You consider answering honestly—say you are pulled in three directions—but the practical parts of you arrange words into leverage. You let the cadence of the room decide the weight of your reply.

You: "I'm weighing what will protect the most homes and keep the marsh in the best possible state. I'm thinking about who gets to keep their memories here, Tala, and who gets left with a seawall and nothing else."

Tala's laugh is short and sharp. "So, compromise or confrontation? You always pick that middle ground."

You: (a small, private sigh) "Not always. But right now—" your hand moves to the copper at your throat, feeling the barometer's cool disk. "—right now, I need to see what will actually hold."

Across the room, a cluster of Irene's advisors gestures at the renderings with laser pointers. Irene herself stands apart—composed; her expression is small, complex, unreadable. You can feel her attention like a tide line, steady and inevitable. The mayor drifts between groups with the tired, conciliatory smile of someone carrying an impossible ledger.

Captain Reyes: "Maya, my house has held storms that would make those renderings laugh. I want jobs, damn it, I want my grandson to finish school here. But I don't want to wake up and not remember why this place mattered. You think you can keep both?"

You swallow. There is the ache of responsibility in his question—less political, more ancestral. You hold the look longer than you planned, because whatever you say next will thread through more than policy papers.

You: "I don't know how to keep everything. I want to. But I know how to test what might. If we can get pilots in place—tangible, visible—people might see an alternative to flattening marshland for profit."

Tala: (brisk) "And if we can't prove it fast enough? People will vote for the thing that looks like help now."

Evan appears at the edge of the circle, carrying a small, dented case of sample fittings and a faint smell of machine oil and lemon tea. He doesn't speak immediately; he lets the room's noise settle into his shoulders. When he meets your eyes, his face is patient but tight—like someone balancing a fragile prototype on the edge of a table.

Evan: "Those pilot rigs can be done. They can be visible and robust. They'll need funding and volunteers, and they'll need Sora's measurements verified in real tide cycles. If we hurry, we can put one at the pier's lee before the council vote bank closes."

You: (a thought moving faster than your voice) "Hurry is the problem. Evan, 'hurry' for you is different than 'hurry' for people who need rent paid this month."

Evan: (a small, rueful curl of his mouth) "I know. I'm not saying 'wait' for everyone. I'm saying 'show' them. Evidence changes minds—sometimes faster than rhetoric."

The conversation rolls: Sora's data versus Irene's capital; Captain Reyes's memories versus the mayor's ledger. Tension climbs like voltage in the atrium lights. Your pulse follows it: quickening, then steadier, then a clipped rhythm as you think through consequences.

Irene: "Maya. The town needs jobs. We can structure this to include marsh buffers. Come sit at the table; help us write the protections into the contracts."

Evan: (quiet, steady) "Or you can lead a blockade. Put people on the line. Stop the machinery before it starts. It's ugly, risky. But it's immediate."

Tala: (hands on hips) "And if the town needs jobs? We can't kneecap people who'll be fired tomorrow. Direct action could land people in jail and not put a roof over their kids' heads."

Evan's gaze is on you—soft and complex, unreadable in its own way. He doesn't plead. He lays out logistics in steady phrases, then steps back and lets the weight of the decision settle between you.

Evan: "Whatever you decide, I'm with you. I can make time and hands. But there are limits to what a prototype can do in a week."

You: "And there are limits to what negotiation can do in a vote."

Tala: (quietly) "And there are limits to what direct action can do if people can't eat."

You: (voice steady, carrying) "There are three paths I can see."

You: "I can sit at Irene's table and negotiate for marsh protections. I can organize direct action and blockade the Glass Harbor site. Or I can work with Evan to scale a visible pilot project and present data to the town."
