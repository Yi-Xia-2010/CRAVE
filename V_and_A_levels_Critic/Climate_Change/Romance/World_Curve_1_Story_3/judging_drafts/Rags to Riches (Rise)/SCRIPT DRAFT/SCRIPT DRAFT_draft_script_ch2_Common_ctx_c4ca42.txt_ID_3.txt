You feel it all at once — the private weight you've been carrying and the public expectation folded into the room.

Irene's voice cuts through the hum with the kind of calm that has been practiced into persuasion. Irene Vale: "Maris Bay deserves certainty. Our proposal guarantees jobs within months, critical infrastructure that will hold in the next storm cycle, and the private capital to lift this town out of uncertainty." (She taps the lapel pin; the renderings slide into a flythrough of engineered seawalls and retail piers.)

You watch the lapel pin flicker statistics across the projection — revenue curves, manpower estimates, timelines so tidy they could be an instruction manual. People nod. Not everyone, but enough. The room tastes suddenly like metal and possibility.

You look for contact: Evan's eyes find yours for a beat. He's quiet, but the steadiness there is a small, familiar tether. You feel the tether loosen and tighten with each of Irene's measured sentences.

Irene Vale: "This is not ideology; it's deliverable results. We can begin work this quarter. The harbor redevelopment provides immediate employment and flood-risk mitigation at scale."

Mayor Lionel Park: "Immediate work is—" (he folds his hands, voice thick with the day's exhaustion) "—it's what people need. Payrolls, repair crews, something that shows we've acted."

You hear the logic — you understand the pressure the mayor is under; you live with versions of it every morning on the boardwalk. The thing is: some 'action' can erase the very things that buffer the town in the long run. Engineered fill compresses peat, and fill here makes future storms worse. You want to say that plainly, and you can feel how saying it will land in the room.

Dr. Sora Kim rises next, moving with the unassuming steadiness of someone used to translating data into words people can carry home. Her voice is low but clear.

Dr. Sora Kim: "The models show compaction risk where fill replaces marsh. When you replace living systems with hard structures, accretion stops — the marsh can't keep pace with sea-level rise without that living process. There are microclimate feedbacks too; restoring native reeds and building living shorelines reduces near-surface temperature variance and slows salt intrusion."

You breathe in the technical language and translate it in your mind into the town: reeds that eat storm surge, mussel beds that rebuild sediment, jobs in planting and maintenance. Your role — the one you were asked to play — is to make the science live in people's hands and schedules.

You stand, clearing an ironic hitch from your throat. Tala presses her palm to your wrist briefly, a private count—two, then three — and then slips back to her seat, eyes alight with a plan. Her whisper reaches you without moving her lips: "We can stage the demo at the old pier after the meeting. People will come." Her whisper smells of citrus gum and determination.

You and Irene exchange a pragmatic look, each of you holding a different understanding of 'risk.' There's friction — civil, administrative friction — and underneath it, a deeper current: the town's fear of being left without.

The line between technical argument and personal memory tightens. Your chest feels like a ledger — numbers on one side, faces on the other.

Irene Vale: "Sequencing is a detail. What we need is certainty, not theoretical timelines."
