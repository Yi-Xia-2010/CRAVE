You wake to the smell of brine and wet wood before the light has fully decided to call the day. The boardwalk under your boots breathes and shifts — a slow, familiar language of planks swollen with last night's tide. Each step is a small accounting: where the boards rotted, where someone nailed a patch, where the tide still finds a way in.

Your satchel bumps against your hip, a steady, comforting weight. Seed packets rustle inside like a private, hopeful wind. The copper of your barometer necklace is warm against your chest; it feels heavier when you stop and listen, as if it keeps the town's pressure in its hollow.

You crouch by the temporary reed beds staked along the dock. The reeds lean together like people sharing a secret. Your fingers are cold, and when they find the damp soil at the base, they come up with a smear on your linen sleeve — salt-dark earth that sits there like memory.

You don't have to force the memory. The storefront flashes: a corroded blue awning, glass shattered into salt-slick patterns, the small wooden sign you hand-painted for the festival half-buried in mud. A storm surge took the ground floor and, with it, a year's worth of inventory and your parents' slow certainty. That loss is a knot that doesn't loosen; it is the leaning beam behind everything you try to keep standing now.

A message from Dr. Sora Kim slides into your inbox with an attached PDF. You open it with the habitual, careful reverence of someone reading test results.

Dr. Sora (email excerpt, read silently): "Maya — preliminary models show non-linear responses if more than 30% of marsh is converted. We need to stress this at the briefing. I'll be there with charts. — Sora."

You read the words twice, slower each time. "Non-linear" feels like a polite word for "collapse." Your breath goes a little shallower. Dr. Sora's data are a lamp: clear and clinical, a beam into the murk. She gives you facts you can carry into a room filled with polished renderings and promises. Facts are tools; they don't always win, but you have used them before to carve a path through rhetoric.

The dock smells of diesel and salt and the faint burnt-vegetal snap of last night's fish smoke. You tuck the phone back into your satchel and, by habit or superstition, lay your fingers over your barometer pendant. The copper is cool now. You run a pulse through it and try to translate that private metal language into something steadier: a plan. Protect the marsh. Keep people in their homes. Scale Evan's desal kits so that water insecurity doesn't make the town vulnerable to market pressure. Seed-bank, rooftop nursery, volunteer shifts. You recite the list like liturgy.

Evan's name sits at the end of that list like a comma you don't know how to close. He and you — the thought is both small and enormous: the brush of his hand when he handed you the first prototype of his desal kit, the way he waits a beat before he says things that matter. Your chest tightens with a private ache. You haven't told him you felt that way — not fully — and he hasn't left, but small things are not always proof against larger storms.

You decide to call him because you want to hear a voice that is steady and not full of municipal polish. You tap his name, and his photo opens like a familiar window.

You let out a breath that is almost a laugh.

You: "I already do."

Evan: "Good. See you at the briefing?"

You climb the narrow service stairs to the rooftop nursery. The city below is small; the marsh beyond even smaller in your head. Up here the air is greener, the light softer. The rooftop is patchwork: repurposed gutters, old tubs, a mosaic of succulents and reed starts. You move through it like someone in church, checking tags, straightening a crooked pot. Your hands smear soil on your sleeves again. There is grace in these small, repetitive ministrations — planting, naming, cataloging.

A tin watering can trembles in your fingers. Water runs slow and measured. Dr. Sora's graphs occupy one corner of your brain, Tala's volunteer list another, Irene's polished renderings another. They press together like tides. Your mind runs through logistics with the same endless, practical devotion you've always had: rally volunteers, get desal kits tested, hold an evidence table during the briefing, push for an on-site marsh survey before any final approvals. Each item is a small task that feels like holding back a wave with your hands.
