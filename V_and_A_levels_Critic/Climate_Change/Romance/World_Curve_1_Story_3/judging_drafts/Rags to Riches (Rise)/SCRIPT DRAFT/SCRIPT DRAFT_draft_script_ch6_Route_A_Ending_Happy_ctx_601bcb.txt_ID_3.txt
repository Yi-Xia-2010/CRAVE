Captain Reyes: "Names are here." (his voice is a gravel whisper) "Enough of 'em young to fill a net and kind enough to learn the tides. They'll learn marsh restoration by hand, not by contract."

You watch him hand the first envelope to a lean kid with salt-bleached hair; the boy's eyes go huge and wet, and Captain Reyes's smile splits that weathered face like sun through cloud. You feel raw and buoyant all at once.

Dr. Sora Kim steps to the microphone with her usual composed intensity, carrying a stack of printouts that smell faintly of algae and paper.

Dr. Sora Kim: "We have committed funding to long-term marsh studies — time-series data, sedimentation rates, and living shoreline trials. We will quantify what matters. Science will be a condition of any future expansion."

Her voice pins the moment: leverage wrapped in rigor. You had pushed for this. You had argued for measurable thresholds that could halt slippage into destructive engineering. It is there now, in ink and grant numbers and the crispness of her slides shown on a passing tablet.

Evan Kaito: "You did the hard part. I made a place we can try it."

[Scene: Stormcliff Lighthouse | Late Morning]
[Visual: The lighthouse looms, white against a cloud-sheared sky. Wind rips at banners; the lamp room's glass is streaked with spray.]
[Sound: The wind roars; seagulls wheel. The cliff's edge is a white spray of falling water below.]

The climb up the lighthouse stairs is faster than you remember. Your lungs catch on sea-brine; wind pins the teal streak of hair against your cheek. At the top, the lamp room opens to a panoramic rawness: the town a necklace below, the marsh a trembling edge between water and memory.

You stand close to Evan, the world small enough to feel like something you can manage with two hands.

You: "I keep imagining the marsh when it's older — when the cordgrass rides the edge, and the kids we trained are running the crews. It feels...possible, now."

Evan studies the horizon, then you, then the small barometer pendant at your throat.

Evan Kaito: "You made people listen. You did that. I—" (he sucks the wind in, then lets it out) "—I rigged a thing that can prove a point. But I wouldn't have got the funding meeting without you sparring for the clauses."

You laugh, breath scattering like sea-spray.

You: "We both bled over the clauses. Tala stitched the rhetoric into something that wouldn't tear. Captain Reyes salted every note with history. Sora threatened how many grants, exactly?"

Dr. Sora Kim's quantitative calm hovers in your memory, the sound of lab data turned into leverage. The wind wants to carry words away; you anchor them to each other.

Evan Kaito: "You carry too much alone sometimes. Promise me something stupid and small?"

You turn to him. The lighthouse light sweeps slow and sure across the surf.

You: "What's 'stupid and small' in Evan-speak?"

Evan smiles, then bares a small, vulnerable thing: he reaches for the copper barometer hanging low at your chest and touches it with two fingers, careful as someone handling a relic.

Evan Kaito: "Promise me you'll let me shoulder part of it. We'll build the habitats, and the life around them. Not some grand, fast fix — small, stubborn, and repair-minded. We'll show up for the nights no one else does. We'll take the jobs that don't come with headlines. We'll seed the town and our kitchen table with the same patience."

The wind takes his words and drops them back into your ribs. Your chest spasms with the old, familiar ache: guilt made of other people's needs and your own refusal to be unmoored from duty. But here, in the raw light, it feels different — not a chain but a shared plank.

You: "I don't know how to accept help well."

Evan's grey-blue eyes widen with a soft, startled humor.

Evan Kaito: "Then teach me how to ask better. Teach me the edges of your stubbornness. I'll tell you when to be stubborn and when to yield. We'll be a terrible and excellent team."

You both laugh at that — a crack of something bright. He steps closer until the salt air is shared between you and the sound of the lamp is the only metronome. Your hands find each other again, this time with a deliberateness that feels like a pact.

You: "I can't promise I won't feel guilty. I can promise I'll try. For the town, and for us."

Evan Kaito: "I'll try too. No running for corporate chairs, no letting go. We'll pick projects like we pick seeds — for hardiness and for flavor."

You look out over the marsh line, where the first ribbon of restoration begins to show as a darker green. It shudders in the wind, alive and wary.

You press the copper barometer to your heart. It is cool, familiar, and somehow steadier than your pulse.

You (softly): "Rain's coming."

Evan: "Good." (he grins, and it's all the warmth you need) "I'll get the tarps ready."

The two of you stand on the precipice and, for the first time in a long time, the horizon feels like a plan rather than an accusation. The town down below disentangles itself from the day's ceremony and folds back into work: apprentices walking toward marsh plots, a woman carrying seedlings, someone laughing in a way that means they could sleep.

You feel exhaustion, yes — a deep, bone-deep fatigue from negotiation and midnight clause-sessions — but it sits next to buoyancy. There is triumph in the small, stubborn things that now have funding and force: the apprenticeships in Captain Reyes's hand, Dr. Sora's studies that will hold Irene to her word, the tiny desal unit humming proof into seedlings.

You close your eyes and inhale brine, peat, and the metallic tang of ink.

You: "We bought time. Dignity, too."

Evan squeezes your fingers. The lighthouse wind claws at your jacket, but the metal against your collarbones feels like an anchor.

Evan Kaito: "And we'll use it."
