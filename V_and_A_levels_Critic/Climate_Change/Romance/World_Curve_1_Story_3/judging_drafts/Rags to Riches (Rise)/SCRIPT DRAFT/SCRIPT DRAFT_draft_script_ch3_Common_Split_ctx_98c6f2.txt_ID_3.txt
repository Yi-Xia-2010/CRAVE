Captain Reyes: "I've fished this inlet since before the boardwalk had a boardwalk. When the marsh goes, the truth of this place goes with it. You can't buy memory back with concrete."

You: "I know. I don't want to buy our past. I want to secure our future without erasing where we came from."

Captain Reyes studies you as if cataloguing tide marks on a seawall.

Captain Reyes: "Negotiate, march, or build—each one bends people in different ways. Don't let 'future' be an excuse for forgetting."

He taps an old jointed finger against the rail. The gesture lands like a small bell.

Evan: (hesitates) "Weeks to build, if we rally people. Months to collect convincing data. It's not instant, and I know that feels like stalling."

Your chest tightens. The timeline is a taut wire between urgency and prudence.

Evan: "But if Irene's metrics look good next week and ours are only a rumor, we lose the narrative. We need a thing—something tactile."

Tala: (pressing) "And if we don't stop it now, there won't be anything left to hold. Compromise here is slow death."

Captain Reyes: "Sometimes you have to stand in the water to keep the shoreline. But standing in water is dangerous. You ask mothers to risk bread."

You respond with more than policy talk; you let a trace of your private weight show.

You: "I don't want to trade our stories for scaffolding. But I also can't promise everyone a miracle tonight."

Evan: "I know. I'm not asking you to promise me a life. I'm asking you to let me help keep this town its town."

The exchange is small and significant; it feels like a hinge. The room hushes around the two of you as if recognizing that policy and intimacy are braided here.
