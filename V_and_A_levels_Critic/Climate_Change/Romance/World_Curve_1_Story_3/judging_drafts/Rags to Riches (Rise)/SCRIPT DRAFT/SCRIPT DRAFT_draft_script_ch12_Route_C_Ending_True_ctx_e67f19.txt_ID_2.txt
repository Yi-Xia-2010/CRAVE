Evan: "We designed the habitats to float and flex, not to be rigid barricades. You saw how they bobbed last night. They moved, they took the water, and they gave it somewhere to go. They didn't pretend to stop the ocean. They asked the marsh to do the work we'd forgotten to ask it to do."

An officer from a local station takes a live shot; the feed carries your patchwork plans into thousands of living rooms. People begin to clap—tentative at first, then growing. Tala calls out names, pulling neighbors into the center. You feel the momentum like wind behind a sail.

[Scene: Pilot Habitat Lot / Edge of the Saltmarsh | Mid-Dawn]
[Visual: The tide line moves; a swath of turbulent water curls toward the lot. Cameras tilt; the projector freezes a frame, then returns to live play. The moment of truth arrives raw and immediate. The surge is nearer than the simulation suggested; you feel it as a physical force at the soles of your boots. The public demonstration has become an experiment under audience and under sea at once.

Sora's laptop chirps—data blooming in rapid succession. The numbers hold. Where old models predicted breaches, the marsh takes the water, channels it, and leaves fine silt in its wake. One habitat lists and rights itself, its mooring flexing like a practiced swimmer. You laugh—an abrupt, loud thing that startled you. So do others; laughter and crying braided together.

Someone in the crowd shouts appreciation. Cameras flash. Irene's polished expression doesn't break in triumph or defeat; instead, she watches the channels as if reading a map she is learning for the first time.

Irene Vale: (approaching, voice clipped with curiosity) "Your models—Sora, these sensor arrays—this is robust, I'll give you that. The optics are…compelling. But scale is where plans fall apart. How do you propose this protects the harbor-side neighborhoods or the commerce we rely on? How do you account for a superstorm, a series of surges? How quickly can you train people at scale?"

Her questions have the force of a prosecutor but are tempered beneath with an unmistakable thread of interest. She is not here to scold—she is here to test.

You answer because you've lived with these calculations in your chest for months.

You: "We don't pretend it's a single-solution fix. This is modular: stacks of living infrastructure combined with targeted engineered defenses where absolutely necessary. Sora's data shows the marsh lowers surge energy significantly; if the harbor needs protection, those engineered defenses should be set behind critical marsh corridors, not as replacements. Training is the other half—Tala, Evan, and I have apprenticeships ready to scale. We create jobs that care for the marsh and manufacture the kits. The model is slower, but it's regenerative."

Irene: (leaning in) "Slower is an argument some won't accept when jobs are on the line."

Mayor Lionel: "And yet—the piloted jobs we've already recorded—ten apprentices, three full-time coordinators—those are jobs, Lionel gestures to Tala, who smiles, proud but guarded, and the projector flashes the employment numbers back at him."

The exchange unfolds in rapid beats, a volley of pragmatic bargaining layered with moral insistence. The crowd watches the push-and-pull like a live negotiation in miniature; some chime in with shouts of support, others voice the raw fear of lost paychecks. The atmosphere is intense—very high; every syllable matters.

Dr. Sora Kim: (quiet but firm) "We can model extreme sequences. There will be failure points—this is risk management, not fantasy. But the indicators are clear: when you pair marsh restoration with strategic, smaller engineered structures and a workforce trained in stewardship, you reduce both ecological collapse risk and long-term economic volatility."

Irene's lips press into a line. She studies the graphs, the grainy drone footage of last night's surge, and the faces in the crowd. For a beat—one small, almost private beat—something like calculation and concession moves behind her eyes.

You: (answering) "You don't have to be convinced to be responsible. That's been part of my worry: that responsibility gets folded into narratives of who wins and who loses. What we need is governance that keeps the watching at the community level."

Irene studies your face as if reading a blueprint only she can decode. For a long moment, she is unreadable, and you allow yourself the Schrödinger patience: she is complex, not a simple enemy.

Irene: "I propose this: a hybrid strategy. We build engineered harbor defenses outside the essential marsh corridors. We commit private funds into a community stewardship fund—careers, training, and scaled fabrication of your modules. And—" (she taps her cuff) "—we form an institutional oversight board with community members, scientists, and a municipal representative, so no one party can bulldoze the other."

Mayor Lionel exhales like someone who has been holding a weight in his chest for too long. The sentence hangs between you—practical, expensive, and radical in its insistence on shared power.

You: (feeling the world tilt toward a hard-won compromise) "An oversight board with veto points for ecological thresholds. Funds tied to measurable marsh metrics. Jobs guaranteed per phase. Transparency in procurement. Those are the conditions."

Irene: (a small, almost involuntary nod) "And a pilot-to-scale fund: proof points become triggers for tranche releases. You'll have to accept some engineering where the maps demand it. But never as a pretense for replacement."

The words are transactional and almost tender in their awkwardness. You see in her the same fear of being seen as ineffective that you've been circling since the atrium days—an ambition reframed as responsibility. The room tightens; the rhythm of your breath matches the desal's hum.

Mayor Lionel: "If the town accepts this—if the votes go—" (he looks at you, seeking, pleading with the worn face of someone who must reconcile neighbors' livelihoods with long-term survival) "—then we can write it into the municipal plan. We can allocate those funds, set the board, and start hiring."

Captain Reyes appears in the tent doorway as if summoned by old sea-logic, salt and timber in his posture.

Captain Reyes: "I won't call it victory yet. But I'll tell you this—I saw that water carry life back into those channels. That river knows how to be a river when you stop making it angry."

You feel the tide of relief as something you can almost touch—warm and liquid and public. Tears prick at the corners of your eyes, and you press a palm to your chest, feeling the barometer there, the pulse of a plan that could outlive you.
