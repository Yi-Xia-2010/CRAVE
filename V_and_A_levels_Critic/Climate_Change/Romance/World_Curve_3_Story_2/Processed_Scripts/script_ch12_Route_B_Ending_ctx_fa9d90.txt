[Scene: Floating Farm & Rescue — The Eye of the Storm | Night to Pre-dawn]

You are in the center of a human chain, waist-deep in icy spray. Your jacket is sodden; salt cold has crept into your bones. The Floating Farm pitches and lurches like a beast with a stern wound. For a minute, time thins to the geometry of hands and the physics of leverage — turquoise ropes, the angle of the current, people's weight.

A support beam goes under with a horrible, quick groan. Someone screams. Kaito Sakamura's voice cuts through the chaos; he is shouting above the wind, directions and encouragement fused together.

Kaito Sakamura [breathing hard]: "Hold! On the count — three — pull!"

You pull with everyone. Your muscles burn. Your mind narrows to a single liturgic motion: breathe, pull, hold. You are terrified and you are not alone.

The rescue is not cinematic. It is sweaty, ugly, brilliant. A neighbor you scolded last autumn helps you right a broken mattress into a flotation device. Maya, soaked to the bone, keeps rehearsal calm in between sobs as she photographs damaged frames for later rebuilding. Luka's voice is a lighthouse beam, making sense of the chaos.

When the last family is hauled up onto the higher rig, someone starts humming a community chant — a small, ragged song that has outlived storms. It is tinny and broken, and it keeps the edges of panic from fraying into despair.

You look at Kaito Sakamura, who is shivering and grinning with exhaustion. The charity of the moment is a wound and a balm at once.

Kaito Sakamura: "We lost some frames. We saved people. That's what matters."
He wipes his hand across his mouth and finds your eyes.

You want to argue — there are things you wish you'd secured earlier, blueprints you could rewrite in hindsight — but the immediate truth is clear and warm: people are dry, the worst of the night is passing, and the town is still a living thing.

The town assembles to assess damage. Boards are picked, soil checked, lists made and crossed. Outer losses show: garden terraces damaged, two coral frames beyond repair, a stretch of promenade scoured clean of mosaic. But inside the hall the tally is more than what was lost; it's what persisted — community structures kept, families housed, seedlings that can be replanted.

Luka stands at the map, finger tracing the line of damage and the patches that held. He looks at you like a man measuring something larger than weather.

Luka: "You and your people did good."

You feel the compound of exhaustion and relief like a new weight. It's sweet and aching. The town is bruised but not broken, improvisations sustained most of the vital systems. Your refusal to take the agency helm, and your insistence on distributed pilots, have been tested and, in many ways, vindicated.

Maya sidles up to you, hair still freckled with salt. She hands you a ragged notebook, pages full of photos, messages, and names.

Maya: "We documented everything. Grants. Memory. Letters. We can rebuild smarter and share what worked."

Kaito Sakamura is there, leaning against a pillar, sleeves rolled, the little burn scar on his thumb like a punctuation mark on his hand. He doesn't reach for your satchel; he reaches for an idea. You both know what the next step will be.

Kaito Sakamura: "I'll keep the workshop running. We'll retrofit what we can, teach what we learned, and keep rebuilding here. You... you take the pilot elsewhere. Tell other towns what worked. Seed the same network."

You feel the room narrow to the two of you in a private orbit. The town flows around your silence, respectful of the way certain conversations are meant to be private even in public rooms.

Marin Alvarez [softly]: "I'll go. I can—"

Kaito Sakamura [interrupting, quietly]: "I don't need you to stay to believe in you."
He meets your eyes, no performative flourish — only a steady, honest steadiness. "We help each other. Distance doesn't mean less. It means different work."

There is a rush behind your sternum: pride, a hollow ache, exhilaration. The plan is messy: volunteer networks, parallel pilots, shared documentation, rotating nodes of support. It will require letters and video calls, occasional boots in mud, shipments of tools, and, most of all, trust.

You remember the shell at your throat — the small family heirloom that started as a pact to the town and grew into a promise you made to yourself. You run your thumb along its edge and think of the drafts and diagrams you'll carry in a battered satchel, and of the hands that will keep Saltline's hearth alive while you teach others to kindle theirs.

Kaito Sakamura: "Promise me you'll write. And not just technical reports. Letters about the people. Send me the bad news and the good. Send sketches. Send stupid songs."
He tries to joke and fails into sincerity. His amber eyes hold something open for you.

Maya: "And bring me back something to photograph. A new trick you invent. Or a town that thinks we're mad."

You laugh, a dry release. The laughter is a bridge across the ache.

Marin Alvarez: "I promise."

The room feels bright with possibility and heavy with loss. You will leave some things behind to be rebuilt in Saltline, and you will carry something of Saltline into other places.

Loss sits beside endurance; grief sits beside music; plans sit beside improvised solutions. The shape of the future isn't a single monument; it's a lattice of people who choose to keep trying.

You take out your notebook and, with rain-flecked fingers, begin a letter. Not a technical report, not a sterile memo — but a letter that starts with weathered truths and ends with the small, stubborn lyric of hope.
