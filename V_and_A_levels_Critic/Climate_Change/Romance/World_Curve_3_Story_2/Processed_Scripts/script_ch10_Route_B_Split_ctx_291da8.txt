You step into the room with the shell at your throat like a small, steady weight. The air is warm with too much body heat and the chemical tang of sanitizer; someone has left a thermos at the edge of the staging table. Conversations collide — technical shorthand, local gossip, a single laugh that fizzes and dies. Outside, the sea hammers the cliff in a rhythm the room tries not to feel.

Elena Moro stands at the head of the table, coat buttoned against the damp. Ishaan fidgets with a tablet that projects phased diagrams in translucent layers: “Phase One: Temporary Seawall; Phase Two: Automated Retrofitting; Phase Three: Full Barrier.” The slides advance with the calm inevitability of tidal models.

Elena Moro: "Phasing gives us time. We protect critical infrastructure first and expand as funds clear. It's pragmatic."

Ishaan: "Funding timelines are fixed. The grant board requires measurable milestones — checkpoints at six- and twelve-month intervals. Delay jeopardizes the whole package."

You listen. The maps mean numbers; numbers mean deadlines. Your pulse collects into a small drum. There is no theatrics to Elena Moro's delivery; her voice is a machine that has learned to infuse certainty into a list of contingencies.

You finger the shell again. The urge to speak rises like a swell: not to deny the models or the grant, but to carve space for the people whose lives the lines on the map will redraw.

Marin Alvarez: "If I may."
(your voice — steady, practiced)

Marin Alvarez: "The barrier's phasing is clear on infrastructure, less so on ecological thresholds. There are low-cost, distributed interfaces — green berms, living breakwaters, targeted marsh buffers — that can buy time, reduce sediment disruption, and keep people connected to the shoreline."

Elena Moro tilts her head, unreadable. Ishaan's jaw tightens a fraction. The room leans in because you have a history here: you speak both the language of models and the everyday language of the harbor.

Elena Moro: "Marginal measures are fine as supplements. They don't replace the barrier's protective role."

Marin Alvarez: "They don't have to. But if we ignore the interface — how communities interact with the rising tide — we lock in ecological loss and social displacement. The pilot I'm proposing integrates community stewardship with engineered touchpoints: localized sensors, living nurseries, retrofits that residents can maintain."

A wave of questions arrives like a swell. A councilor from a neighboring district asks about cost overruns. A scientist wants sediment transport models. Someone from the back asks, loudly: "How many homes are actually saved?"

You answer a few, short and technical; you answer one with a story — of a terrace garden that held soil during a minor surge, of a child's hand that kept the clay mosaic until it couldn't. Your words hum in the room; they're small acts bent into policy language. The arousal ratchets up with each interruption: people pressing their faces into the discussion like faces into rain.

Kaito Sakamura: "You were clear."
(he leans forward when the crowd quiets)

Kaito Sakamura: "Those pilots matter. The town knows how to tend a nursery better than any automated schedule."

You can see his mouth shape the words into optimism, but his eyes are an ocean of more complex feelings. He is both your ally and a repository of the town's doubts. He holds himself like someone bracing for impact.

Maya pushes up from the front row, impatient, camera hanging at her chest like a pendulum.

Maya: "We need urgency. Talk of pilots makes funders nervous. How many seasons do you want before we build something that keeps everyone dry?"

You don't soften her edge. You meet it.

Marin Alvarez: "Urgency doesn't erase complexity. A rushed wall can create false safety and accelerate ecological collapse. Pilots can be scaled fast if we pair them with immediate, targeted reinforcement — not as a substitute, but as a parallel track."

Someone in the crowd laughs, but it is not cruel; it is frightened.

The moderator, a municipal official with a too-bright lapel mic, taps a pen. Elena Moro watches you the way someone watches a tide gauge: measuring your range.

Elena Moro: "Marin Alvarez, your work on interface models is why we're asking you to sit at this table. Ishaan, how would you imagine integrating Marin's pilot into our phased timeline?"

Ishaan: "We can slot a three-month ecology interface trial into Phase One, contingent on data meeting security parameters. It won't delay milestones materially, provided Marin's team can deliver a rapid deployment plan."

Rapid. Contingent. The words are hinges. You feel the room's breath close and quicken; the arousal climbs from conversational to kinetic.

Rin, who has been scribbling community comments into a leather notebook, catches your eye and slides a page across the table — a list of names, no demands, only presence. Her smile is small and fierce.

Rin: "If you're going to sign anything, remember the people behind the numbers."

Elena Moro inclines her head in a gesture that is almost polite.

Elena Moro: "Marin Alvarez, the agency will need leadership on the ecological interface. You'll have institutional backing, resources, and access to our modeling suite. Lead the pilot; shape the mitigations from the inside."

A silence cracks open. The room shifts; several phones rise, reflecting a scatter of tiny lights into the dim. Ishaan taps an icon; a legal draft blooms in the projection, clauses highlighted.

Ishaan: "There are conditions — reporting protocols, deliverables aligned to grant milestones, oversight from our board. You'll be embedded within our structure. We expect deliverables at three, six, and twelve months."

Your mouth goes dry in a way that has nothing to do with the room's climate. The offer is a cleaving: influence inside the machine, and the machine's timeline; or stepping away and rallying an uncompromised alternative. The arousal tightens; the percussion in the music quickens.

Kaito Sakamura stands abruptly, the sound of his chair a whip in the hush.

Kaito Sakamura: "So the choice is — be inside and steer, or stay outside and shout. Which is it, Marin?"

His voice is not accusatory, but it pulls like a tideline across your chest. Maya's camera swings to focus, a shutter like a click of judgement. Elena Moro watches you, the corner of her mouth slackly attentive. Ishaan's eyes are flat calculation. Rin's expression is mapped in ink.

You want to respond with precision; you want to place a clause where none exists. Your training recites the terms you can ask for — community governance, transparent metrics, guaranteed local hiring — but each ask might be a bargaining chip the agency refuses. The room contracts around the question as if the air itself were asking.

You inhale once, long enough that you can feel the rain against the windows like a second heartbeat. Your internal monologue runs through frames: influence versus legitimacy; the ethics of compromise; the practical mechanics of mitigation you could implement if you had the agency's data streams and materials. You imagine a pilot with living breakwaters seeded by the community, sensors that feed both the agency and public dashboards, a governance clause that guarantees community vetoes on specific displacements. You imagine the alternative: ringing a bell at the harbor, mobilizing the co-op, watching the agency move ahead without you.

Elena Moro leans forward, voice soft as a tide pulling out.

Elena Moro: "We need someone who knows both sides. If you lead, you get direct authority to set mitigation protocols. If you refuse, we proceed without that interface. We're not bluffing."

Ishaan: "The grant board will view refusal as risk. Acceptance smooths our path. The town might like that, or they might not. That's for you to weigh."

Kaito Sakamura crosses the room in three long strides and stands in front of you. Up close you can see the freckle at his temple and the small burn scar at his thumb. He does not block the room; he anchors you.

Kaito Sakamura: "Whatever you choose, don't let them make the choice for you."

Maya's camera is a low hum now; she whispers: "Whatever you do, say it clearly. People need to know which fight you're in."

The crowd is no longer a background; it is a series of faces, waiting. You feel the arousal apex pull — every look focused, the projection's light bathing your skin, the rain crescendo outside like a metronome.

Your inner voice reduces to a surgical list: authority (inside), autonomy (outside), leverage (conditional). Each path trims some kind of loss and promises another. You picture Luka's face — the slow, patient arithmetic of a life lived with weather — and you measure your capacity to bear blame if the wrong line is drawn.

Elena Moro's expression is neutral — perhaps the only truly neutral thing left in the room.

Elena Moro: "We want an answer."
