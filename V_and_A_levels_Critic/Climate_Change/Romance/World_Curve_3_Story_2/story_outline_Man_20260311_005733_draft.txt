## Visual Novel Title
Saltline: Tides of Us

---

## World View
Saltline is a near-future coastal town caught between slow cultural memory and sudden climate exigency. Once a fishing village, it now occupies a narrow crescent of land: salt-silver beaches, marsh reclamations stitched with salvaged scaffolding, terraced cliffside gardens under wind turbines, and a harbor ringed with bobbing modular farms. Political authority is split: regional agencies with capital and engineering expertise push for centralized barriers and automated retrofits; local cooperatives and craft-led collectives advocate incremental, humane adaptation and ecological stewardship. Religion and ritual are diffuse—rituals around tides, harvests, and lighthouse songs anchor community identity. Class lines are nuanced: older families with land and cultural capital (boatbuilders, lighthouse keepers) share space with technocratic actors and young activists scraping together resources in cooperative workshops. Tension centers on whether to accept large-scale, well-funded engineered protection that may displace marshes and privatize waterfront commons, or to reinforce community-scale resilience that preserves local culture at the risk of slower, riskier adaptation. Geography alternates between salt wind, reclaimed mudflats faintly glowing with biofilms, sunken promenade mosaics, and the cliffside gardens. Atmosphere is bittersweet and wind-swept: lamp-warm interiors and storm-blue exteriors, human ritual against the persistent, impersonal incoming sea.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I return to Saltline, a place I thought I understood. As Marin Alvarez—sea-glass-eyed, dark-teal bob tamed by salt and wind—I face the debris of a past project I once championed and the urgent present when storms and tides no longer forgive delay. The first half of the story is a fall: trust frays, old wounds reopen, and a high-stakes proposal from Elena Moro—an authoritative, corporate-minded engineer—threatens to redraw who owns the shore. Kaito Sakamura, my soft-amber-eyed maker and possible partner, urges a craft-led, community-first approach. The choices I make—whether to dig in with Kaito and neighbors, to align with Elena’s scaled engineering, or to broker a risky hybrid—pull the town and my relationships in different directions.

After the nadir (a public rupture and a failed simulation), the arc becomes a rise: grassroots experiments, hard conversations, and small victories rebuild trust and generate hybrid technical-cultural solutions. Branches explore different ethical compromises and romantic resolutions: cooperative hybrid victory (Happy), technical-secured survival with cultural losses (Bittersweet), centralized mitigation with cultural displacement (Sad), and an uneasy, slower federated model where community agency is restored (True). Emotional stakes intertwine with climate stakes—every intimate decision mirrors how the town chooses to keep its shore and itself.
