[Scene: Tidewise Lab & Greenhouse | Morning]
[Visual: The boathouse glows from within — warm amber against a silver sea. Fog bead on the glass panes, condensation streaks like quick-ink memories.]
[Music: Sparse cello, low and steady; each note a held breath]
[Sound: Distant gulls, soft drip of water from a gutter, faint mechanical hum of sensors]

You push the heavy door and it gives with a familiar creak, the sound of something that has been opened and closed a hundred quiet times. The pendant at your throat is cold; the shell presses into your sternum like an old question you have not yet answered.

The greenhouse air folds around you — humid, green, a close smell of wet soil and copper. Potted marsh grasses lean toward the glass. A teaching board is propped against a table; schematics, brittle at the edges, are tacked above it. Your notebooks are where you left them, pages dog-eared with half-drawn contour lines.

[Visual: Close-up on a spread of paper: an old reinvestment schematic, annotations in your handwriting blurred by a waterstain]
[Music: Cello drifts lower, a single sustained tone]

You stand at the table. The map of channels and hardened levees looks like a wound neatly stitched. Your name — a little note in the margin — sits underlined, unchanged by time. For a second you only look. The lab smells like damp cardboard and the sea; the world outside is a muted watercolor.

You touch the corner of a schematic. Your finger hesitates over a torn margin where salt once tracked. The memory of the work — the hours spent arguing models and budgets, the meeting that favored efficiency over marsh reeds — makes your chest tight. You can feel the smallness of that room and, behind it, the long list of what you couldn't protect.

Someone laughs quietly at the doorway — the sound of camera straps and restless energy. Maya folds into the doorway with her teal tips catching the amber light, cheeks flushed from the cold. She holds a small camera, but her fingers are empty of speech.

Maya: "You found them?"

You: (quiet) "They're here. They didn't disappear with the tide."

Maya steps closer, eyes skimming the schematic. She picks up a stray pencil and taps a line, as if measuring the distance again.

Maya: "People still talk about that project like it was some kind of test you failed. Like it was only you."

You: (defensive, then softer) "It wasn't only me. And I—" You stop. You don't want to say the thing that makes you sound like you are trying to absolve yourself by citing process. The lab is full of evidence: decisions stacked like plates, some cracked beyond repair.

Maya: (leaning one shoulder against the table) "I know. But knowing doesn't make it hurt less. Some of the marsh beds never came back."

You let the silence sit between your words. You know the images Maya keeps: a nursery net ripped clean, a hollow in a field where cordgrass used to be. It becomes harder to speak because each sentence might rearrange someone else's grief.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands; the skin of your knuckles bright against the paper]
[Sound: A faint static ping from a sensor, like a distant question]

Footsteps come slow, a familiar rhythm. Luka's coat smells of old wool and lighthouse oil; when he speaks the room seems to tilt toward the story he will tell.

Luka: "Maps remember what people forget. You can read years in a line."

You look up. His face is a weathered map itself — the kind that understands loss as a layer, not an end.

You: "Luka."

Luka moves to the window and sets his palm flat against the fogged glass, as if pressing the past to the present will make the two line up.

Luka: "Marin—" He pauses, choosing the name in a way that makes you soften. "You came back to do something right, not to punish yourself. That's important. But you also need to know the town remembers the cost."

You: (steadying) "I know. I do. That's why I'm here."

Luka: (soft) "Then don't let the memory become a single verdict. Let it be a listening."

Maya: "Listening's fine. But the wind's getting louder, Marin. Elena and Ishaan have been tidy about their plans: barrier, automated flats, retrofit kits. Money talks. People get paid. People move."

You: (thinking) "They talk like they can build away the sea. Sometimes engineers forget there are living things between the town and the water."

Maya: (quick, a little breathless) "So what's the move? Are we supposed to tell people to sing at the tide while the machines roll in?"

You stare back at the schematic. The hard lines used to mean certainty. Now they look brittle. Your mind flicks to possibilities you have sketched in private: living thresholds — hybrid ridges of marsh, oyster frames and engineered cores that grow with the water. The idea feels both hopeful and small, and you know Elena will call it inadequate.

Luka watches the motion of your hand as if it tells him the weather. He inhales slowly, then speaks like a tide.

Luka: "In twenty years, the things you do will be what the children learn. The marshes are not only habitat; they are history. When the town had to choose between a levee and a song, people lost both. We don't have to make that same mistake."

Maya: "But what if Elena's plan funds emergency retrofit for the low-income blocks? Ishaan says it's the only way to unlock regional support. People are hungry for assurances."

You: (low) "Assurances that come with strings."

Maya: "Exactly. Someone ends up telling folks where to live so the city saves the rest."

You close your eyes. The words land like falling rain on thin glass. You feel that old, familiar flip — guilt reordered into strategy. You should be able to hold both: the technical sense of what will keep people alive and the moral sense of what kind of town you're willing to be.

Maya steps closer; she speaks without the camera now.

Maya: "You want to propose something that isn't just a middle-ground fudge. You want a proper hybrid. But you also promised to listen. How do you keep both?"

You: (honest) "I don't know yet."

Maya: "That's not bad."

You: (bitter laugh) "It feels bad."

The three of you fall into a rhythm of small talk and careful probing. Maya offers up names of neighbors who will be angry; Luka offers a list of old tidal markers he keeps in the lighthouse; you admit the nights you've spent awake drafting containment curves and the mornings you've avoided the Harbor Market because the look in some neighbors' eyes feels like accusation.

[Visual: The greenhouse’s glass fogs from inside to out; the world blurs and the schematic blurs with it]
[Music: Cello sustains a softer chord — the day's low pulse]

A chime — subtle, unobtrusive — plays from your wristband. It is a message from the municipal feed: a courtesy note about a scheduled briefing. You glance at it because habits persist: you are an engineer who reads updates like weather.

You: (reading aloud) "'District forecast review — 48-hour model update.'"

Maya: "Sounds like Elena's playground." She half-smiles, trying to make a joke that lands thin.

Luka: "Forecast, meeting, model — all words that move power like tides. Words are used to push things where they must not go."

You fold your hands on the table, the paper crackling under your palms. You can see two roads: one of promises with corporate polish; one of slower, living fixes that feel like asking the sea to be partner rather than prisoner.

Maya: "You could go to the meeting. Or you could gather folks at the Hall and lay out a living-threshold sketch. Or you could—" Her voice tightens. "Or you could do both badly and make everyone angrier."

You: (soft) "I have to be careful. I promised to listen. But I also promised to act."

[Sound: The greenhouse vents sigh; a small, ordinary sound, like a held exhale]

You look at the doorway. Outside, the world is a watercolor of flattened light. Your chest is leadened but not immobilized — it moves like someone learning a new task with hands still shaking.

Kaito's presence arrives the way his footsteps always do: steady, the scrape of a boot that belongs to someone who has mended boats at dawn. He is at the doorway with a sketchbook under one arm, eyes holding you as if weighing what to say and how to start.

Kaito: (gentle) "Morning."

You: (half-smile) "Kaito."

Kaito steps in, the smell of salt and wood shavings clinging to him. He looks at the schematic spread and then at your face. The light makes the crow's feet at his eyes soft; he seems simultaneously tired and certain.

Kaito: "You look like someone who carries a map that keeps getting heavier."

You: "It's not the map. It's what the map remembers."

Kaito: "Then let it remember. But let it help us, too." He tilts his head, searching. "What are you thinking?"

You: (quiet, intentional) "A hybrid — living thresholds that can whisper to the sea and a couple of hardened cores where people need certainty. Not everything centralized. Not everything artisanal. Somewhere a mix."

Kaito: (slow) "That sounds like something made, not imposed. It would still be messy."

You: (bitter) "Messy and honest."

Kaito: "I can help mock up prototypes. We can use reclaimed hulls, oyster frames, and some of the spare turbines for anchoring. If you want — if you want to do this — I'll stand for it."

Your throat tightens at the offer. It's an ordinary kind of devotion — hands that will work beside yours — and it steadies you more than any formal endorsement ever could. Yet the lab is full of people who need more than steady hands. They need policies that don't bite back.

Luka: (quiet) "Promise me one thing. Promise you'll listen to the people who don't come to meetings. Their silence is often a kind of speaking."

You: "I promise. I'll listen. And I'll bring ideas, not answers."

Maya: "Then we start small. A prototype on the Floating Farm maybe? Test it, show what a living threshold can do before Elena's cylinders arrive with approval forms."

You: (thoughtful) "Yes. A demonstration. A tangible thing people can touch. We can map expected impacts without pretending it makes everything whole."

Kaito: "You'll need a story with it. Not just numbers." He taps his sketchbook. "People vote with the heart as much as they do with the ledger."

You: (smiling faint) "You always did believe in the story."

Kaito: "Someone has to."

[Visual: The three of you leaning over the table, heads close; light pooling over drawings, fingers pointing, the kind of quiet collaboration that feels like building a bridge with hands instead of approvals]
[Music: Cello falls a degree, an exhale of low strings]

A soft ping from the lab's central monitor — not an alarm, only an update. You watch the small readout blink: the regional model has a new trajectory. It is not catastrophic yet; it is a graph that shifts a little to the right. It nudges your internal weather, subtly, the way a cloud can darken an afternoon without thunder.

You: (to no one in particular) "They're updating the model."

Maya: "Which means Elena is preparing her line."

Kaito: "Or Ishaan is polishing a grant pitch."

Luka: "Preparation is different from promise. Pay attention to the promises."

The air sticks to your skin with the weight of obligation. You feel guilty for thinking of your own fear first. You feel guilty for feeling a small rush of relief that there might be a way to prove a living threshold works. Guilt folds back into resolve and then back into guilt — a tide that leaves you salt-stiff.

You stand, paper in hand, and tuck a pencil behind your ear the way you always do. You haven't resolved anything. You have small plans, a promise to listen, and a fragile coalition: a girl with a camera, an old keeper, a carpenter with sea in his palms, and you with your broken maps.

[Scene Transition: Walk toward the Sunken Promenade | Late morning]
[Visual: The boathouse door opens to a sky that has softened; the boardwalk glints where water has reclaimed old planks]
[Sound: Slow surf, the metronome of a town that has learned how to keep its bearings]

You follow the promenade where tiles glint like small, stubborn stars under shallow water. Kaito walks with you, fingers tucked into his apron pockets. Conversation here loosens and tightens in equal measure. He asks about a detail, you fumble an answer; he offers a plan, you point out a complication. The give-and-take is domestic and weighty; it presses toward a horizon that looks, to both of you, like work and home entwined.

Kaito: "If we start the prototype at the Floating Farm, we can have volunteers from the workshop and a sign-up at the Hall. People will come if they can touch the thing."

You: "And if Elena's team sends survey drones? If Ishaan's outreach promises immediate payouts for opting into the retrofit?"

Kaito: "Then we need clarity. We make the demonstration honest. We show impact and cost. We don't pretend it will fix every flood."

You: (softly) "We have to be honest about trade-offs. I owe people that."

Kaito: (after a pause) "You owe them truth, not only apologies."

You: (breathing) "I know."

[Music: A single, low piano note, the tension contained but present]

The sky to the west has a smear of denser cloud, not storm yet, but an accent: a gathering. Your wristband buzzes again — this time a slightly sharper tone. A short dispatch scrolls across its face: "Model update received. Regional briefing scheduled; advisories to follow."

You feel the room inside you narrow and lengthen like a tide channel. The emotional weight doesn't spike, but it settles — heavier and more purposeful. You are more aware of the smallness of what you can do and the stubbornness of what you must try.

Kaito watches your face, then looks out to the horizon. The sea has a thin, plain voice today; it is not shouting, only making its presence known.

Kaito: "Whatever happens, we'll do it together. Not because that will make it easy, but because it'll make it right."

You: (quietly) "I want that."

A gull cries, a solitary punctuation.

The promenade light catches on a phone screen in your pocket: a municipal notice, a name you know, and a date for the regional briefing. The notice isn't urgent. It isn't final. It is a lever.

You fold the schematic back into your bag, feeling its weight against your ribs like a small, viable claim on the future. You and Kaito walk back toward the town, the path wet underfoot, each step deliberate.

There is no resolution yet. Only the work ahead: listening, drafting, testing, and the slow politics of trust. You think of Luka's maps, Maya's restless energy, Kaito's steady hands, and the shapes of a plan that might hold both people and place.

You touch the shell at your throat again. It feels like a pact.
