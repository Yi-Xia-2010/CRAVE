[Scene: Community Hall | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: Warm wood-paneled room, string lights half-lit; maps and pinned sketches crowd the walls. A long table sits at the front with a projector casting cool, clinical blue diagrams onto a pull-down screen.]
[Music: Low, pulsing ambient tone — a restrained heartbeat beneath conversation]
[Sound: Quiet murmur of voices, the intermittent rattle of a paper cup, distant gulls caught faintly through closed windows]

You step inside on the tail of the tide — still wearing salt in the seams of your jacket, the satchel heavy at your hip. The hall breathes around you: a community that has learned to measure itself in small repairs and louder silences.

Your entrance draws a few heads. Rin nods at you from a cluster of folding chairs, fingers stained with ink from last-minute flyers. Luka sits nearer the front, hands folded into each other, eyes like a map of past storms — watchful, weathered. Kaito is halfway across the room, shoulders squared, a hand resting on the back of an empty chair as if steadying both seat and self.

You feel the schematic in your bag like a small, private ledger: your work, your notes, the thing you brought back to Saltline. It presses against your ribs and asks, quietly, what you will do next.

[Visual: Close-up of the projector as Elena steps into its light; her platinum bob catches the blue, atomic against the warmth of the room]
[Sound: Projector hums and then clicks louder, a needle of attention]
[Music: A thin string underlines her measured steps]

Elena Moro: "Thank you for coming on short notice. I'll keep this concise." (hands clasped, voice lacquer-smooth)

Her slides unfurl: clean cross-sections of the coastline, phased timelines, risk probability bands in sharp reds and greys. The images are persuasive in the way a scalpel is persuasive — precise, impossible to look away from.

Elena Moro: "Our model projects a significant chance of catastrophic overtopping within the next decade. We are proposing a staged coastal barrier: modular segments that can be deployed and reinforced as needed. Combined with targeted urban retrofits, we secure the district without waiting for slow-moving consensus."

There is a technical elegance to what she says. You hear the efficiency and feel the pressure behind it — a promise that shakes the room into listening and fearing at once.

Ishaan Rao (from the side): "The funding window is narrow. Grants and insurers are available—if we move decisively. Delays cost lives, and more importantly, they cost leverage."

Murmurs ripple. Someone says "insurance" like a benediction; someone else says "displacement" like a curse. The words stack.

Kaito Sakamura rises when the Q&A opens. He doesn't move with the urgency Elena does. His presence is quieter, but it fills the wooden space differently: from the floor up, like a repaired floorboard that bears weight without complaint.

Kaito Sakamura: "Elena, the model's impressive. But it treats Saltline like a problem to be armored. Where are the people in that cross-section? Where are the nets we already use, the gardens, the floating farms that feed kids next winter?"

Elena's jaw tightens for the briefest notch.

Elena Moro: "Kaito, with respect — community measures are noble, but they are not a substitute for infrastructure. People need certainty. The barrier is engineered certainty."

Kaito Sakamura: "Certainty is one form of safety. So is capability. So is giving people the skills and spaces to refuse total dependence on one solution. We don't have to choose only one kind of safety."

You watch the room tilt as if around a fulcrum. Faces split into factions that were only whispered about until tonight. The air tastes like copper — anxiety and salt and the metallic edge of policy.

You can feel people's memories sliding between the two presentations: Luka's old stories about the storm that almost took a neighborhood; Maya's streak of urgency last week about reef nurseries; the memory of the marsh that your earlier project once displaced. Those are not details to adjudicate in a neat diagram; they are the places people live with consequence.

Elena's eyes find you now — not accusatory, exactly, but appraising, the way an engineer reads a structure for weak points.

Elena Moro: "Marin, you've worked with both data and people. We've reviewed several of your reports. Support from someone of your standing would make this transition less… tumultuous."

Her tone is smooth but there is a blade hidden in the courtesy: an invitation wrapped in strategic pressure. Ishaan's hands fold open a contract on a tablet that sends a clean glow across the front row.

Ishaan Rao: "We can start with a memorandum of understanding. You remain a local liaison; resources flow through a managed program. We want community participation — but guided."

Around the room, "guided" settles like a shadow. You remember the marsh displacement like a bruise. Memory collides with possibility and the collision makes a cold, low ache in your chest.

Kaito's voice comes in softer, then sharper as he leans forward.

Kaito Sakamura: "Guided can become managed. Managed can become replaced. Marin, we trust each other here. You know how we work: repurposing, teaching, small nodes of resilience that stitch the town together. If Saltline becomes a set of zones behind a wall, what do we lose that isn't on any balance sheet?"

Kaito's hands — stained slightly with varnish — open in the way he always does when he's trying to include someone in what he sees. You want to slide into that openness. You want to be the person who keeps the town's rituals in the frame.

Rin calls out from the back: "We can't let outsiders write our stories!" Her voice is brittle with fear and something like righteous anger.

Luka's reply is softer, soaked in the cadence of years: Luka Sorensen: "We have to name what we save and who decides. Engineering buys time, but it does not automatically buy justice."

The conversation loops in on itself. You can feel the gathering pressure, not yet a storm, but a field of charged clouds ready to snap.

You are aware of the satchel: the sealed notes, your tide sensors, the small experimental prototypes you've been tinkering with at Tidewise Lab & Greenhouse. They are not ambitious in the way Elena's plan is ambitious, but they are real. They are yours. The weight inside you is equal parts fear for the marsh and the practical knowledge that current models predict outcomes you cannot wish away.

Elena's face registers your hesitation. She moves like someone who has rehearsed for resistance her whole life.

Elena Moro: "We can make accommodations, but the reality of funding windows and emergency thresholds is not flexible. Delays increase exposure. Marin, if your pilot does not show scale quickly, insurers withdraw. I'm offering structure. We can work in partnership — but we need public commitment."

Her words hang in the timbered air like a deadline. For the first time tonight, the room smells faintly of ozone — tension moving toward an electrical flash.

Kaito steps closer. He does not speak to Elena so much as to everyone: to you and to the hall.

Kaito Sakamura: "I won't watch our community get parceled out for expedience. Marin, if you stand with them because it's safer for you — because it's cleaner on a resume — then you know where I stand. But if you stand with us, we'll build something that keeps more of Saltline's life intact."

He looks at you with that steady amber, and all the small ways he has cared for the town — mending doors, making toys with leftover wood, saving fishers' nets — pool into a single, luminous accusation. It isn't just policy on the table; it's intimate. It's about who you are to each other when the sea pushes in.

You feel every eye in the hall find you. Elena's intent. Kaito's quiet plea. Luka's lined patience. Rin's fear. Ishaan's tablet glow like an altar.

Inside your chest, the old, private ache unfurls: survivor's guilt for the marsh you helped change; a stubborn, protective ache for the people who stayed; an urge to prove that your work can be both honest and useful. The arousal in the room mounts from low hum to a focused strain. Your pulse finds a rhythm that is both methodical and panicked.

You imagine the barrier: cold concrete tracing the shoreline, an engineered promise that solves a model but may not solve the soft human things that make a place home. You imagine the workshop's communal tables: slow repairs, daily rituals, coral frames being lifted with laughter, the smell of cedar and tar and soup.

You also imagine lives preserved by infrastructure — children not waking up to water in the streets, power systems holding when storms rip at lines. The possibility that Elena describes is not heartless; it is rigorous, hungry for solutions. The choice becomes a calculus of harms as much as a choice of loyalties.

Your mouth is dry. The room tightens like a fist. You think of the shell at your throat — your grandmother's token — and you feel the weight of something both fragile and ancient asking you which future to press it into.

Luka clears his throat. He does not tell you what to choose; he only speaks what he has always said in storms: Luka Sorensen: "Name the harms. Name the people who'll be asked to move. Name what you cannot trade for certainty. Then choose."

The hall leans in because it must. This moment is small and enormous at once: a public positioning that will rearrange trust and alliances. Your work, your conscience, and your relationships are all on the table like fragile instruments.

[Music: The ambient pulse tightens into a single long note — an inhalation held]
[Sound: Murmurs become a low, shared intake of breath; rain begins to tap a steady pattern on the windows]

You stand before the whole town and feel every choice you did or did not make in Saltline catch up with you. The arousal curve that warmed under the meeting now focuses into a single, knotted urgency: pick a side, or try — and risk failing both.

You are at the pivot. The hall waits. The sea outside presses its own slow argument against the windows.
