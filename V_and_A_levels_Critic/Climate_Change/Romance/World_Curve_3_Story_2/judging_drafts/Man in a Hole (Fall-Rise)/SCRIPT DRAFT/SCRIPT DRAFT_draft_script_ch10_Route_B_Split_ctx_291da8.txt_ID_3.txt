You step into the ring of light cast by the hologram and feel every eye sharpen like a tide cut into rock. The shell at your throat is a small, steady weight. Your breath is a rhythm that tries to be ordinary and fails.

Elena stands at the projection's edge, voice precise and sure. Ishaan flanks her with a tablet that streams numbers like falling sand.

Elena Moro: "Phase One secures the promenade and harbor access. Phase Two raises the eastern breakwater. Phase Three completes a continuous berm with adaptive sluices. We can mobilize crews within sixty days; funding is allocated contingent on a lead specialist to interface with local science teams."

Ishaan Rao: (cool, efficient) "Contracts, deployment schedules, labor contingencies — everything is staged. The region will see prioritized protection, starting with critical infrastructure and emergency routes."

The room smells of heated bodies, plastic of name badges, wet fabric. A reporter's voice rustles through the back. Someone coughs. You note the soldered sound of a chair scraping.

You know what's unspoken: quick protection carries strings. The holographic surf shimmers where the berm would sit, a pale line that will cut through marsh and promenade alike.

You slide forward, pulse a drumbeat in your ears, and find your voice even as the room tightens.

You: "Phasing is clear. But have we modeled the ecological interfaces? Where the barrier meets marsh, where migratory routes cross—if those nodes fail, the system fails in ways the model won't show."

Elena's jaw tightens almost imperceptibly. Ishaan's tablet blinks; a faint smile that is not friendly flickers and is gone.

Elena Moro: (neutral, edged) "We have ecological consultants on retainer. This is scale work. Mitigations are incorporated. The alternative is delay."

You hold the projection between your hands in the air like a map you could fold and set down. Your words feel small—marginal alternatives against the mechanical certainty in their charts.

Maya: (sharp) "Marginal alternatives will take years while the sea takes months."

Rin: (voice high with a mixture of hope and fear) "We were promised mitigation before, remember. Promises are not enough."

Kaito sits near the front, hands clasped. His face is a calm plane, but when you look closer his eyes shift in a way that reads as complex—an unreadable tide. He does not intervene yet.

The projector shifts under your fingers because someone nudged the interface; coarse-sand noise of the room swallows the end of your sentence. Elena steps forward with a longer, practiced breath.

Elena Moro: (controlled) "Marin, your work is noted. We are not blind to wetlands. But the sea does not wait for consent processes. We can include local input in a pilot interface—limited, targeted. We need someone to lead that interface, someone who understands both the science and the community."

Her eyes find you with an economy of light. The offer lands like a token and a gauntlet.

Ishaan Rao: (measured) "Lead the interface and you get resources: staffed teams, material allocation, and a seat at the decision table. You operate under agency timelines but you shape mitigation parameters."

You feel the room tilt. The speakers now carry the weight of contracts, not just plans. The air tastes like metal and rain.

Kaito stands then, voice low but carrying.

Kaito Sakamura: (interrupting) "What safeguards? How do you prevent displacement of fishing plots and the nursery frames? How do you guarantee local oversight isn't just a checkbox?"

Elena meets him mid-sentence, cool and quick.

Elena Moro: (slightly dismissive) "Safeguards are written into the deployment packages. We will audit impacts. We cannot let every local preference derail urgency."

Kaito: (faltering, then steady) "That's not an answer."

The exchange pulls the room taut. Maya breaths out a sharp sound; Rin's fingers drum a rhythm that matches your heartbeat. People shout quick questions; the moderator struggles to re-establish order.

You step back into the current of voices and feel pressure like a tide pressure gauge ticking up.

You: "If I take any role, it's because I can ensure the interface is more than a checkbox. I will need a governance clause—community veto, transparent data, real-time environmental thresholds tied to deployment pauses."

Ishaan flips a slide; legalese blooms across the model.

Ishaan Rao: "Clauses can delay procurement. They introduce liability. We can offer advisory seats, periodic reviews."

You can hear Elena calculating. Her expression flickers—fear, resolve—neither wholly hostile nor benign. It is an efficient mixture of hazard-control and opportunity-scouting.

Elena Moro: (softening) "We want your expertise to make the barrier livable. Lead the interface and you keep a hand on the real impacts. Refusal leaves mitigation to contractors who only follow the contract."

Your chest tightens. The siren outside hits a high note and fades; someone in the back begins clapping sarcastically. A camera flash blinks. The air in the room feels thinner, each inhale sharper.

Kaito approaches you between questions, his voice quieter now, but urgency bright in the set of his shoulders.

Kaito Sakamura: "If you go in, promise me you won't let them paper over the nursery beds. Promise you'll take us with you, if you can."

You: (internal) The promise tastes like salt. Promises have fissures in them. Being inside the machine lets you do damage control, but it also changes the shape of what you can refuse.

Maya pushes forward, impatience a physical shove.

Maya: "We need action that centers the people who live here, Marin. If you sign on, what will you trade?"

You: "I don't know yet." The answer is honest and hollow at once. You feel every pair of eyes leaning into that hollow.

The moderator calls the room back to order with a mechanical gavel of sound. Elena's team projects a compact, formal packet labeled: ECO-INTERFACE PILOT — LEAD ROLE DESCRIPTION. The title glows like an offered key.

You read the bullet points: authority limited; reporting structure to agency; budget allocations with strings; deliverables defined in three-month sprints; veto conditions vaguely referenced. Each phrase is both a promise and a tether.

Elena leans in, voice lowered but sharp.

Elena Moro: "Lead the pilot, Marin. You will be the liaison. You will have direct influence on mitigation elements and the authority to pause operations if ecological thresholds are breached. But you will be part of a chain. You will be accountable to the agency."

You feel the phrase "accountable to the agency" settle into your spine like cold brine.

You: "Will my decision to pause be binding?"

Elena Moro: (precise) "Binding within the pilot scope. There is an arbitration process for full deployment pauses. It's not unilateral."

You open your mouth and close it. The word 'not' has the weight of a hinge. You are aware of Luka's maps in your memory, of the mosaic, of the nursery frames on the floating farm—the things policies can quantify and the things they cannot.

The room narrows to sound: Elena's measured tones, Ishaan's quiet legalism, Kaito's breath next to you, Maya's clenched jaw, Rin's wet eyes. You are both a scientist who maps thresholds and a daughter of this town who feels every loss as if personal. The decision that will be expected of you is a fulcrum.

Your internal voice runs like a diagnostic readout and like a prayer at once: influence, legitimacy, loss, agency. The arousal in the room coalesces into a physical thing — a hot, humming pressure behind your ribs.

You sense the cameras angle toward your face. Microphones tilt. The holograph blinks awaiting input. Outside, a gust flings rain against the glass like hands insisting.

Elena waits with the patience of someone who has timed storms and bureaucracies. Ishaan's tablet shows a countdown: procurement windows closing, labor contracts pending. Kaito's hand finds yours for a moment — a small, steady anchor — then falls away, expression unreadable again.

You feel the moment compress. The choice spreads before you like a map with three paths, each with its own light and shadow, each one a different architecture of responsibility.

You inhale. The room leans forward with you.
