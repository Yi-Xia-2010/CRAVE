You arrive with sand still clinging to the soles of your boots. The tote at your shoulder has your notebooks, a tangle of rolled sketches, and the sensor wristband warm against your skin. Your palms are damp. The room smells of strong coffee, wet wool, and the salt that never quite leaves this place.

You slide into a folding chair; Kaito is two rows over, sleeves rolled, hands rubbed together over a chipped mug. He meets your eyes for a second — steady, then unreadable in a way that makes your chest contract. You tell yourself not to read too much into the way he watches; the meeting has a shape of its own and your history with him is only part of it.

[Visual: Camera pans across faces — Rin with a stack of fliers, Luka near the back with a cane, Ishaan in a pressed jacket scanning notes. Elena stands by the projector, a slim silhouette against a bright slide of a rendered seawall.]
[Sound: Low, loaded quiet. Someone coughs. A child fidgets.]

Elena Moro: (opening, measured) "Thank you all for coming. I know time is short and the models are only growing more conservative. What I'm proposing tonight is a coast-wide structural barrier, targeted retrofits for critical infrastructure, and an automated monitoring network. It's not small. It can't be small."

Her voice is precise; her presentation flicks through images — diagrams of a continuous barrier, graphs of risk reduction, a projected timeline stamped with agency logos. The light from the projector throws her features into a hard, public clarity.

Elena Moro: (softening, competent smile) "This is about preserving what we can, before the storms force harsher choices on us. Funding is possible if we act on a schedule."

A ripple of polite, wary applause. Ishaan nods in agreement from the front row, the outline of a policy pitch folded neatly into his palms.

Ishaan Rao: "The window is narrow. Regional councils prefer decisive plans. The agency wants deliverable metrics."

Kaito stands up. He doesn't go to the microphone — he doesn't need to. His voice carries in the wooden room, warm and sharper because it comes from the gathered crowd.

Kaito Sakamura: (full of quiet heat) "Decisive doesn't always mean right. Elena, a steel barrier will change tides, fishermen's routes, people's access to the beach. It will carve the town in half between the protected strip and the rest."

Elena's jaw tightens; she responds without letting the tension show as anger.

Elena Moro: (coolly) "Kaito, the proposal accounts for managed access points. We can design mitigation for livelihoods."

Kaito Sakamura: (leaning forward) "Mitigation that assumes people will move their livelihoods to bureaucratic maps misses the point. We have knowledge here. We have craft. We can reinforce docks, raise the floating farms, reorganize routes. The workshop has plans ready to prototype."

A murmur grows — some faces light up at the word "workshop"; others fold inward. Luka shifts in his seat, his voice like gravel.

Luka Sorensen: (slow, heavy) "I've watched walls go up and villages die in the shadow of them. It's not the wall that saves you; it's the people who know how to live with the sea. We need both wisdom and bones."

Rin moves to the front, cheeks flushed, clutching a clipboard.

Rin Okubo: (trying to steady) "People here are scared. The proposal looks...final. A lot of us—" (she swallows) "—a lot of us don't trust that 'managed access' will protect the marshes or the places we sing at low tide."

You feel the room tilt. The air closes in like a door being pushed the wrong way. Elena's face is professionally impassive but the thin lines at her mouth suggest strain; Kaito's hands are fisted at his sides. The stakes look suddenly smaller and unbearably personal at once.

Your notebook lies open on your lap. The memory you try to keep folded — the reinvestment project that displaced a marshbed years ago, the quiet protests you couldn't answer fast enough — presses against your ribs. You hear Luka's voice and remember the blackened reeds you helped authorize in a meeting where you believed bigger was better. Guilt tastes like iron and used paper.

Kaito Sakamura: (sharp) "Forty percent for who? The maps place the line where the harbor is busiest — where corporate shipping and the main market are. It doesn't cover the floating farms, the coral nursery, the alleys people live in."

Elena Moro: (measured, a touch brittle) "Trade-offs are inevitable. If we wait for perfect consensus, we'll have no defenses when the storm comes."

Kaito Sakamura: (voice rising) "And if we build that defense without protecting what makes Saltline Saltline, we become a different place. A safer version of something that wasn't ours to fix."

A woman at the edge of the room begins to cry quietly. A man shouts: "What about my boat?!"

Ishaan raises a hand, trying to restore order.

Ishaan Rao: (calm, efficient) "We can schedule hearings, pilots —"

Rin: (interrupting, plaintive) "Pilots feel like promises when people need rent money now, Ishaan. We need actions that honor our way of living."

You stand at the hub of these voices and feel the old model of your life — engineer, neighbor, someone who ran toward solutions — grinding against an ache in your chest. The weight of your past choices is a deliberate, continuous pressure. VeryNegative — the sea is a patient accusation.

Your options carve the air between you and the rest of the hall. Neither feels clean.

Elena meets your gaze. For the first time tonight there is a thread of something like appeal in her eyes — as if she can see you as a vector between policy and practice.

Elena Moro: (quiet, searching) "Marin — you know the models and the community. What do you think? If we combine targeted engineering with your team's localized sensors and Kaito's workshop retrofits, we could pilot a section that preserves key habitats and gives us the data to scale responsibly."

The hall hushes. Sentences hang. Elena's offer is a hand extended in a businesslike way — an invitation shorn of warmth. It is also an implicit test.

Kaito's expression hardens. He steps closer to the projector's light and speaks low, purposeful.

Kaito Sakamura: (forceful) "A pilot can be a Trojan horse. I've seen 'pilot' become default, then 'default' become permanent. We've got to make sure the governance remains local — cooperatives, not corporate oversight. People need agency, not just infrastructure."

Elena Moro: (sharp, almost tired) "You underestimate logistical realities, Kaito. Local governance without technical scaffolding can be overwhelmed. Storms don't wait for slow councils."

The room fractures along old fault lines: fear vs. control, belonging vs. preservation, immediate relief vs. long-term consequences. The chatter grows into a swell — accusations thrown like kelp, tears, practical questions, and thin slogans about 'saving the town' from both sides.

You feel every gaze as a weight: some hopeful, some accusing. VeryNegative settles into a steady ache — not a blow, but a wear that wears at the seams. Mid arousal keeps your heart moving, your thoughts sharpening rather than spiraling. You have to choose a public stance now.

Your notebook is a blank you haven't filled; your voice is the only lever you have in this room.
