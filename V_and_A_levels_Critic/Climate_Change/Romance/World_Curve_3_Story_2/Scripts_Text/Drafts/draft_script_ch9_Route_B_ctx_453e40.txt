# Route: Route B / Route A (Alternate Epilogue)


[Scene: Conference Room | Late Afternoon — Storm Approaching]
[Visual: Floor-to-ceiling window showing a gray wall of rain; tide lines smear on the lower panes]
[Sound: Rain escalating to a steady hammer on glass; distant gulls muffled by wind]
[Music: Low, urgent electronic pulse undercut with a single-string cello — tempo quickening]

You step into the conference room feeling the decision you made still like a bruise. You chose to stand with Elena's agency — to believe that scale and speed could buy Saltline breathing room. The memory of that choice hangs in your throat: a promise to protect, a promise to deliver. The room takes that promise and tests it.

The table is long and clinical, a dark grain interrupted by laptops and legal pads. Elena sits at the head with the small holographic crest of her agency fluttering above her palm; Ishaan is to her right, composed as a well-cut knot, hands folded. Luka perches near the window, wool pulled tight against the damp, an island of patience. Kaito is there, shoulders tense, face unreadable — something complex held behind a gaze that watches you like a tidewatcher watching a storm line. Maya bounces on the balls of her feet, camera forgotten, eyes sharp with impatience.

You close the door behind you. The click sounds too loud.

Elena: (smiling, precise) "Marin. Thank you for joining us. I'll be direct — time is not on our side. We have a proposal that can move from permit to concrete in under nine months. Funding is ready. Expertise is in place."

You: "Nine months," you repeat, tasting the word. The room seems to wait on it. Nine months is a calculus; for some households, it's a deadline. For the marshes, it is uncertain. You let the question sit. "What are the strings?"

Elena's smile thins. She taps her badge; a projection blooms — maps, simulations, cost curves. The visuals are clean, efficient: a ribboned barrier hugging Saltline's curve, hydro-scheduled gates, retrofitted storm drains. The room fills with schematic certainty.

Elena: "We secure the commercial harbor and main residential corridor. We stabilize the shoreline so businesses and families can rebuild without the stopgap of constant emergency repairs. In exchange, we request oversight of certain maritime commons to streamline operations — primarily the stretch by the Sunken Promenade and the floating nursery cluster. Consolidated management reduces conflict during implementation."

Ishaan: (softly) "We'd set up a local liaison board, staffed by residents, but with final sign-off under the agency for operational decisions."

You feel your mouth go dry. The phrase 'final sign-off' clicks against something inside you — a hinge closing on communal agency.

You: "Consolidated oversight means...what, exactly? Control of access, harvesting rights, the nursery leases?"

Elena: (leaning forward) "Operational control. We must be able to ensure the barrier functions as intended — that includes regulating activities that would undermine it. No one likes constraints, Marin, but we're trying to avoid greater loss."

You hear Luka clear his throat, a dry sound like driftwood rubbing. He does not leap to condemn Elena; he remembers other storms, other promises. His voice is lower than the storm.

Luka: "We've seen committees, and we've seen mandates. There's a history here — what you call constraints, others call dispossession. It's not just legal language. It's memory."

Maya: (interrupting, urgent) "People are losing homes now. We need something that moves. If Elena can get the harbor safe, that buys time for the rest of us to adapt. We can't stall."

Kaito shifts in his chair. His chin presses into his fist. He doesn't speak, but the quiet is heavy with accusation and something else — worry, maybe grief. You can't trust your read of him now, not when your own choices have put you on this side of the table.

Elena watches you with an expression like glass — reflective, hard. She does not blink fast. Her agreement or annoyance doesn't show; only a calculation.

Elena: (cool) "Both matter. We have engineers who will work with designs, but regulatory certainty is necessary. Operations require a chain of command. Marin, you have technical credibility in this community. We'd like you to chair the local steering committee we form — to guide implementation, translate community concerns into operational terms."

You: Chair. The word lands heavy. It's a position of influence, and you know influence can be both cushion and leash.

You: "Chair with what authority? Advisory? Or actual veto power?"

Ishaan: "Advisory. The agency retains final approval for operations that affect barrier integrity. Decisions that risk the project's performance must defer to engineers contracted by us. It's non-negotiable for safety."

You feel the room narrow. Safety is a rational language; memory is not. The tension ratchets up. Outside, a gust makes the window shudder. The rain hammers like a metronome counting down.

Kaito finally speaks, voice rougher than you expected.

Kaito: (quiet, trouble under his words) "So we give up the harbor to keep the harbor. We hand the keys to people who will—" He stops. His jaw works. "Will the nursery still belong to the people who tend it? Will the songs at Luka's dock get permission slips?"

Elena: (with the practiced patience of someone used to redirecting heat) "We will work to preserve cultural practices where they don't interfere with structural integrity. Traditions are important; they are not, however, foundations. Marin, think of this as triage."

The word 'triage' feels like salt in an open cut. You remember the marsh you once advocated to alter — the project that displaced habitat. The memory pricks hard under your ribs.

You: "Triage is a clinical word. For people here, it's land, livelihood, memory. There are non-engineering values you can't just 'work around' without active stewardship and restitution."

Elena's expression tightens almost imperceptibly. She flicks a glance at Ishaan.

Elena: "What we're offering is scale. We can protect the majority of the town. That has to count for something."

Maya slams a palm against the table, the sound cutting.

Maya: "Counting isn't enough when counting erases pockets of people. If the floating farm gets subsumed, who feeds the kids? If leases change overnight, who pays?"

Ishaan: (measured, persuasive) "We will provide transition funds, relocation supports, priority hiring for local cooperative workshops during retrofit. There's compensation on the table."

You sense the tension spiking — offers and counters like weather around a lighthouse: bright, blinding, and dangerous. Every concession Elena names is a gust that could topple fragile local scaffolding. Your heart hammers. You imagine the Sunken Promenade — mosaics half-lost, children's laughter echoing over water — the cultural geography the agency proposes to streamline. Your chest feels too small for all of it.

[Interactive Moment: Choice A ("Ask for legal guarantees in writing now") -> Reaction: "You lean across the table and request explicit contractual clauses ensuring community stewardship and oversight committees with binding seats. Your voice is clipped; you signal Ishaan to pull up draft language." || Choice B ("Ask for time to consult the community") -> Reaction: "You breathe, forcing the meeting's heat into a different rhythm. 'I need time,' you say. 'I need to bring this to Luka, to Kaito, to the nursery hands. I can't sign a future for people I haven't heard from.'"]

Elena's jaw tightens in a way that is almost human.

Elena: (slow, warning threaded through) "We are on a timetable. Federal grants, procurement windows — these are not indefinite. If you attempt to stall for months, the opportunity costs are significant. We can't afford to wait while another storm unthreads the whole coastline."

You can hear the agency's calendar like a threat. It is a practical knife, and it presses against urgency. The room's air shifts; the rain sounds like a drumbeat in time with the urgency. Your throat works. The obligation you feel — to the town, to remediate past errors, to prevent loss — is a pressure cooker waiting to open.

You: "If we move quickly without safeguards, history repeats. If we stall, people may lose everything now. There is no clean path."

Elena: (leaning in, tone almost gentle) "There's a smart path. You know the stakes, Marin. You know how to translate science into something people can accept. Join us formally. Take the funding, lead the mitigation, let us manage the risky operations. We'll build faster than any loose network of workshops."

Her offer is surgical: join us, accept the agency's terms, and you become the bridge between a machine that moves quickly and a people who have lived on a different clock. It is an offer of agency and constraint folded into one.

Luka's eyes find yours, and for the first time in the meeting his voice is fatigue-strangled.

Luka: "There are things money can't rebuild. Names on a dock, the taste of salt on bread made from harbor-sown grains. Decide if you are buying time or selling memory."

You feel the room constrict to the size of the table. Your hands curl. The seashell at your throat — warm against skin — feels like a relic and a question both. The storm outside hammers as if pressing an exclamation into your chest.

Your mind runs through consequences in fast cuts: children who rely on the floating farm, elders who maintain tidal songs, the coral nursery frames that need delicate tending, and the iron certainty of a concrete barrier. You count lives and marshes in the same breath and fail to make either fit.

Kaito's look is unreadable, heavy with accusation without words you can parse. Maya's impatience is a live wire. Ishaan's calmness is a sluice gate waiting to close. Elena's offer gleams like a way forward and a closing door.

Your pulse throbs in an accelerating rhythm. The room's lights seem to dim and sharpen at once. You can feel the arousal rising — the negotiating tempo turning into pressure, the room narrowing to the single axis of choice. For a breath you are a tide pulled between two banks.

You: (internal) "You promised to protect. To whom are you accountable? To the people who will lose gardens and rites? Or to the larger number who will have roofs when the worst comes?"

You put your hand to the table, feeling the grain under your palm — a human texture in an engineered plan. The shell at your throat presses into your collarbone as if asking you to play the next note of the town's song.

Elena: (soft, decisive) "Marin, we're offering you leadership with resources. We're asking for operational deference in certain zones. Say yes, and we begin construction. Say no, and we walk away to other sites. You know our reach. The window closes."

You sense the climax fold in on itself. The air is electric with consequence. Your voice comes out small.

You: "If I accept, I become the face of a choice that will take things from people in the name of protection."

Elena: "Then be the one to remake that choice so it is as equitable as possible. Your presence matters."

Your chest tightens with a pressure that is nearly pain. The storm outside crescendos — a sudden slap of wind knocks a stack of pamphlets off a side table, papers skittering like small white fish. Someone curses under their breath. The room surges with a single, shared intake of breath.

Your internal monologue runs rapid and sharp now: guilt for earlier projects, the memory of the marsh you helped redraw, Luka's maps annotated in shaky ink, Maya's pleading face. This is not just policy. This is moral scalpel work under time.

You look at the holographic map of the Promenade — a bright line where a hand-drawn mosaic once lived. The thought of those mosaics disappearing, replaced by engineered panels, is a small death.

You feel your fingers brush the seashell at your throat. Your pulse is a drum. Decision waits like a held fist.

[Visual: Close-up on your hand over the shell; the camera lingers on the wavering hologram of the promenade]
[Sound: Rain reaches a high, insistent pitch; a distant siren doppler-fades then steadies]
[Music: Electronic pulse tightens into a serrated string]

You inhale, the breath a thin wire.

You: (internal) "This is the moment I chose Elena for. This is the moment I test whether I can keep my hands from repeating the past. It is also the moment when compromise smells dangerously like surrender."

The room is a pressure chamber. Elena's offer sits incandescent and cold between you. Your options arrange themselves like a map you must cross.

You close your eyes for half a second to steady the tremor in your fingers. When you open them, the tableau is the same, but everything is louder — Luka's soft warning; Kaito's unreadable tide; Maya's impatience; Ishaan's composed gravity; Elena's quiet insistence.

You have to decide what to do with the authority they've handed you. The choice will ripple outward, reshaping more than shoreline.

[Visual: Camera pulls back slightly, the rain outside a living curtain; your reflection in the glass overlays the holographic map]
[Music: Holds a single unresolved chord — expectant, taut]

You sense the window of time Elena keeps pressing. The room waits.

You push your fingers to the shell, feeling its ridges, and the choice splits — not between right and wrong, but between kinds of wrong and different survivals. You breathe, and the chord in the room does not resolve. The next step will ask you to name which losses you will accept and which you will fight to keep.]
