## Visual Novel Title
Tidefall: Marisport Letters

---

## World View
Marisport is a near-future temperate coastal town shaped by rising seas and stubborn people. Its political life is a patchwork of neighborhood councils, an overburdened municipal office, and the occasional visiting corporate delegation. Class divides are real: small-boat fishers, market vendors, retrofit builders, and cooperative gardeners share space with investors and municipal contractors. Culture centers on reciprocity—tool-swaps, seed-lending, and memorials for storms; religion and ritual are pragmatic and local, often mediated by Reverend Sol’s community gatherings and shoreline memorials. Geography is tidal: a long bay with kelp lines, a battered promenade, raised market platforms, rooftop orchards and an old lighthouse turned Solar Spire. The corporate presence—Blackbrand Development—offers gleaming modular seawalls and mixed-use flood barriers underwritten by private capital; their arrival twists civic debate into urgent ethical conflict: prioritize rapid, engineered protection or nurture living shorelines that privilege local knowledge and long-term resilience. Atmospherically, Marisport is tactile and weathered—salt, patchwork solar film and kelp-green shadows. Visually, everything is painterly and muted: sea-grays, kelp greens, rust-orange accents; characters wear reworked field gear and salvaged tech. The stakes are both material and intimate: homes, livelihoods, memory, and love.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I am Mara Linh Alvarez, practical, loyal, and haunted by past compromises. When Evelyn Black’s polished proposal for a modular seawall arrives—promising rapid protection but risking displacement and top-down control—Marisport fractures. I return to the town I left, balancing rooftop orchards and kelp lines against the clinical certainty of Blackbrand. Kai Navarro, with sun-bleached braid and amber eyes, offers optimism and grassroots scaling; Reverend Sol and Nita hold community memory; Dr. Leila supplies crucial data. Across a single contested season, I must choose strategy: public resistance, negotiated hybridization, or a high-risk solo demonstration of living defenses. Each decision spawns consequences that spiral: legal fights, occupations, failed prototypes, media exposures, and personal betrayals. The arc is tragic—falls from hope to loss—so every branch descends: fights end in broken neighborhoods, forced relocations, or small policy wins born of greater costs. Romance complicates everything: Kai’s optimism clashes with my guarded responsibility; our love bends, strains, sometimes snaps. The endings vary—bittersweet compromises, personal exile, ambiguous policy shifts—but all bear the taste of loss. The narrative uses first-person intimacy to make systemic climate choices feel like private moral wounds.
