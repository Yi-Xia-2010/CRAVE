[Scene: Blackbrand Negotiation Room | Late Afternoon]

[Visual: A minimalist glass-and-steel conference room. Outside, the construction yard's cranes move like mechanical metronomes. The table is a long slab of cold composite, spotless except for a neatly stacked folder at Evelyn's elbow.]
[Sound: A low HVAC hum; distant clank of machinery. The room carries the faint, sharp scent of ozone and industrial coffee.]
[Music: Sparse, rhythmic piano undercurrent—measured, urgent]

You step over the threshold and the air seems to tighten. The room's sterility compresses the town's messy textures into a single proposition: a plan in clean lines, an ask written in legal cadence. Your boots make a dull sound on the polished floor; the noise feels loud in the hush.

Evelyn rises as you enter and offers a precise, measured smile. It is the same expression you remember from the public briefing—calm, like a tide pulling and pausing with intention.

Evelyn: "Mara. Thank you for coming. Please—have a seat."

You slide into the chair facing her. Across the table, Kai sits slightly forward, fingers laced; Dr. Leila is tucked to his left with a tablet balanced on her knee. Reverend Sol's cane rests against his platform; Nita's bandana is a bright smear of defiance in the neutral room. They fill the edges of your vision—the town in human form.

You fold your hands in your lap and feel the compass pendant cool against your skin, an old weight that steadies a newer one. Everything you carry into this room is memory and consequence—your father's laugh on a wet quay, late nights tending seedlings, promises whispered under salt-light. You breathe and let those images keep you steady for now.

Evelyn: "I'll be direct. Blackbrand proposes an implementation agreement: modular barrier segments—scalable, rapidly deployable—integrated with terraced greenways and public terraces. We design the segments to accept living elements: soil pockets, planted terraces, engineered reef anchors. In exchange, we ask the town to sign an agreement committing to phased installation and to authorize Blackbrand as lead implementer."

Her voice is even; her words hang in the neutral air like clinical instruments. You hear the contours of the offer—speed, visible acreage of green space, community-access language meant to sound generous. But the language you know from the margins, too: "authorize," "lead implementer," "implementation agreement."

[Visual: Evelyn slides a slim presentation across a recessed screen. A diagram blooms—concrete modules interlocked like teeth, thin strips of planting terraces rendered in green.]
[Sound: The faint click of a remote; the image's tap on-screen sounds final.]

Dr. Leila leans in, eyes flicking across the rendered cross-section. Her finger traces the stabilize-and-anchor notes; her voice is a careful balancing rail.

Dr. Leila: "Technically, integrating living terraces with modular units is feasible. The soil volumes are small, but with proper irrigation and substrate choices—we could establish salt-tolerant plant communities that attenuate wave energy and support biodiversity. The reef anchors could jump-start kelp and oyster recruitment."

You almost let yourself exhale at that; practical proof is a small, steady reassurance. But proof is not the whole ledger.

Kai: (soft, almost pleading) "And the terraces—public access? Community plots? Those were promised in your brief."

Evelyn: (inclined head) "Yes. Public terraces, promenades, designated community stewardship plots. Blackbrand will fund the initial planting and offer technical training for local stewards."

Nita's laugh is a dry knot. She leans into the table, voice cutting.

Nita: "That's generous-sounding until you read 'designated.' Who decides who's a steward when land values shift? Who pays rents for those shops you promise to 'protect'?"

Her question lands like a pebble thrown into still water. Reverend Sol's brow folds; he lifts a hand like a benediction, measured and necessary.

Reverend Sol: "We've seen this before—promises wrapped in language that feels like shelter until someone else draws the line. We need guarantees—clear governance, binding clauses that protect tenants and the market. If we do this, it must be our decision forever after, not just in press releases."

Evelyn's slate-blue eyes meet his. She doesn't flinch but her voice softens, the only concession to warmth that cuts through the room's chill.

Evelyn: "I understand the fear. We're proposing a joint governance board—community representatives, municipal appointees, and a technical oversight committee jointly staffed. The implementation agreement can include enforceable anti-displacement covenants, rent-stabilization triggers, and funding allocations to a stewardship trust."

Kai exhales, relief in the small aeration of his shoulders—relief that rides low like a tide trying to find harbor. You watch him, cataloging the way his optimism folds into worry. You can read his hope like the curve of a seedling—delicate, needing support.

You: (quietly) "Enforceable how? What legal teeth? What's the dispute resolution?"

Evelyn: (tilting her chin) "Arbitration clauses, independent trustees, and escrowed funds. Blackbrand will put capital into the stewardship trust contingent on installation milestones. We can set triggers for third-party audits and stop-work clauses if covenants are violated."

Dr. Leila: "Audits and triggers are useful. But who controls the auditors? Which standards apply? There's a difference between a public-interest audit and a firm-chosen consultancy."

Evelyn: "Standardized third-party auditors, accredited under a neutral registry we would recommend. We are open to naming auditors jointly."

Your stomach tightens. The word "open" is soft, but it does not promise insistence. Open to negotiation feels different from committing to a binding architecture that cannot be hollowed out later.

Evelyn watches the motion—whatever you choose to do. She makes no comment about the pendant, or the steadiness of your composure; she treats both as part of the negotiation tableau.

Evelyn: "I am not asking you to sign on empty trust. The implementation agreement is meant to be negotiated now. We will include the governance terms. But I will be frank: if there is no clear municipal authorization in X weeks, funding allocations will be rerouted to other regional projects. The political timeframe is limited."

The room's rhythm tightens. That deadline lands like a cliff edge. You feel the familiar burn in your chest—the thrift of time against the width of aspiration. Negative valence comes in not as rage but as the slow, gnawing knowledge that every day you wait costs someone shelter, or land, or years of rootedness.

Nita: "So—your 'hybrid' is only hybrid if we let your teams do most of the building. Where's our workmanship, Evelyn? Where's the local labor guarantee?"

Evelyn: "Local contractors will be prioritized for certain packages. Blackbrand will staff training programs. But for critical infrastructure interfaces—reef anchors, modular joints—our certified teams must be used to meet safety and warranty standards."

Dr. Leila: "Warranties are fine—until they come with proprietary repair schedules and replacement parts only available from a single supplier. That's a vulnerability for long-term stewardship."

Evelyn: (softly) "We can clause out proprietary lock-in. We will include open-source interface specifications for modules. But we cannot guarantee identical cost structures for all suppliers; economies of scale matter."

Kai's hand finds yours across the table for a brief, grounding squeeze. The touch is small and immediate; it says more than his voice could. He looks at you—asking, silently, which battle you will lead.

Kai: "Mara, if there's a way to make this actually work for people—practical protections and training—this could accelerate protections for the most vulnerable places. We can get beds in the terraces, jobs, habitats for kelp and oysters sooner rather than later."

You feel pulled inwards between two urgencies: the urgent need to protect people's homes now and the entrenched, moral need to prevent displacement and the expropriation of community power. Negative valence shades every word: there is fear that a compromise will be a slow erosion, and grief for the costs that might come with expediency.

Reverend Sol: (quietly, with gravity) "We remember who paid for protection in the past. We cannot let the story repeat with polished language."

Evelyn: (measured) "I will not pretend this is without cost. Blackbrand's team has built in options: phased payments, community stewardship endowments, and an oversight clause that requires community consent for any subsequent alteration of the barrier footprint. We are prepared to sign the governance terms we just outlined."

She slides a thin folder—matte black, corporate seal discreetly embossed—across the table. The folder is heavier than the presentation; paper has a weight that pixels do not.

[Visual: The folder lands between you like a small island. Under the lid, a stack of documents waits—draft language, tables, legalese arranged into neat columns.]
[Sound: The soft whisper of paper; a distant horn from the yard. The HVAC hum seems suddenly louder.]

Dr. Leila flips through, frowning at the clause cadence.

Dr. Leila: "These clauses are workable in spirit, but the enforcement language is vague in places that matter—definitions, triggers. For example, your definition of 'community steward' is broad enough to be interpreted either as neighborhood-appointed or as company-approved."

Evelyn: "That's why we are here—to pin down definitions. We will accept community appointments. We will accept binding trustee language."

Nita's jaw tightens; she slams one palm to the table, not loud but enough.

Nita: "Binding language that ends up in a drawer is meaningless. We need to walk away with transfer-of-ownership strings attached—funds in escrow, public records that can't be unilaterally altered, and a process for immediate legal recourse if the covenants are breeched."

Evelyn's fingers fold, sympathetic in an almost rehearsed way.

Evelyn: "Escrowed funds are possible. Legal recourse can be arbitration with a named panel. I know this is a lot to digest. My team is on standby to draft redlines. Time is the limiting factor."

You look at the clock embedded in the glass wall. The sun has dropped lower; light strips the yard in orange and flattens the cranes into silhouettes. The room's temperature feels like a measure of the town's possible futures—cool on the surface, with an undercurrent of heat in every compromise.

You: (slowly) "If Blackbrand funds the stewardship trust and we have enforceable anti-displacement covenants with named trustees and independent auditors agreed now, would that satisfy your funding conditions?"

Evelyn: (narrow smile) "Yes—if the trustees and auditors meet mutually agreed standards and the escrow schedule aligns with installation milestones, Blackbrand will execute the funding stream. We would also require the municipality to provide a resolution authorizing the implementation agreement."

The gears in your mind click—municipal authorization is political oxygen. You know the mayor's hesitance, the council members' susceptibility to promises of economic development. Every clause here is both shelter and hinge.

You hold your tongue, feeling every possible future line along the table's smooth edge. Speaking will shape outcomes; silence will leave them to imagination. The negotiation's mid-intensity continues—reasoned, anxious, deliberate. There is no dramatic shouting, only the steady compression of choice.

Evelyn folds her hands and inclines slightly—an invitation to closure disguised as courtesy.

Evelyn: "We want Marisport's partnership. We believe this hybrid approach is the only way to deliver measurable protection at scale within the timeframe regional funding allows. Our lawyers will work with yours to hash out redlines, but the key deliverable is municipal authorization and a signed implementation agreement."

Kai looks at you with earnest, amber eyes. There's love there that breaks the formality of the room into something warmer—fragile, needing tending. You feel your own feelings about Kai like a tide: a pressure of wanting to protect, to build with him in ways that do not cost what you both cherish. The romance is not a subplot—it is embedded in every negotiation, in the way you want to choose rightly for the town and for the person at your side.

Your pulse settles into a sharper rhythm. The conversation has narrowed to practicalities and legal definitions; you have the language you need to argue for the town. But the choice—how to proceed, whether to accept the architecture of oversight and escrow, whether to insist on stronger guardrails—hangs between you and Evelyn like an unlit fuse.

You sense the crescendo approaching: not a shout but a pivot—the moment where your decision will turn legal text into lived consequence.

You reach for the folder. Your fingers hover over the paper edge, the compass cool against your chest. The room leans on your next breath.

[Visual: The camera pulls back slightly, leaving the table and all the faces in a single frame—the town's future contracted into a narrow space]

You know what must come next: the lawyers, the redlines, the municipal resolution. You know time will bite. For now, all that remains is this breath—the thin hinge before action.
