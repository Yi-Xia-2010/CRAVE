You walk in with the weight of the meeting behind your ribs, the pen-suspended hesitation from the municipal chamber still raw on your skin. Reverend Sol's cane taps a precise rhythm against the metal grating as he takes the chair at the head. Lawyers fan red-lined pages like cards. Evelyn Black is already there, tablet open, calm as a tide that knows the hour it must come in.

Evelyn Black: "We don't have the luxury of open-ended experiments, Mara. People need protection this season. The indemnities protect our contractors; they let us move quickly."

You taste acrid coffee and ozone. The Spire's LED ring throws everyone in clinical light; it picks out the thin blue of Dr. Leila's glasses, the salt in Kai's braid, and the bandana-worn crease of Nita's brow. Your sketchbook is tucked away—no small talisman for now—because language matters more tonight than sketches.

You do not like the shape of that sentence. You let it settle into your chest and test it against what you know: a stewardship board with teeth, transparent procurement clauses, hard triggers tied to data. Those pieces are the only things that convince you the community won't be sidelined by polished guarantees.

Municipal Lawyer (sharp, clipped): "We can write oversight into the agreement, but binding vetoes and open procurement increase cost and timeline significantly. There's precedent—"

You interrupt the precedent with a detail that feels like splintered glass: "Precedent doesn't protect my neighbor's home."

Evelyn smiles, small and precise. It isn't cruel; it is economical.

Evelyn Black: "No, but precedent keeps projects from collapsing under litigation. We must be mindful of liability. Binding veto power can paralyze response when the water is already at the doorstep."

Kai leans forward, palms open on the table. His voice tries to be the warm rope that pulls the room together.

Kai Navarro: "We can draft escalation triggers—data tied to Dr. Leila's sensors—that allow for emergency measures without nullifying community oversight in calmer times. It's a hybrid. It buys safety and keeps—"

You cut him off, not because you want to be harsh but because the hour demands clarity.

You: "No 'keeps'. It keeps who? People with power. We need enforceable stewardship now, not hopeful language after the fact."

Dr. Leila rubs the bridge of her nose. The methodical queen of sensors speaks with the flatness of someone tuned to numbers.

Dr. Leila: "The triggers can be hard-coded. If the storm surge reaches X, procurement moves to emergency protocol, but stewardship seats are not removed. You can design thresholds tied to measurable metrics—salinity, surge height, shoreline erosion rate—"

Municipal Lawyer (interrupting, anxious): "But every hard-coded metric becomes a legal battleground. Corporations will litigate thresholds. They’ll demand flexibility clauses."

The pause hangs thin as a filament. Nita's nails drum on a folder; the sound is small and urgent. She hasn't stopped moving tonight—organizing witness lists, lining up testimony, corralling the anger that might otherwise fragment into fear.

Nita: "If we can't put teeth in this, what's the point of asking people to trust us? They're tired—people are tired of being the experiment."

Evelyn's hands are still. She tilts the tablet so the group can see the clause she prefers: a flexible veto by the developer for procurement decisions under "extraordinary circumstances," indemnities expansive enough to limit corporate exposure, a "good-faith" clause for community engagement.

Evelyn Black: "We extend community seats to the stewardship board. We write an oversight mechanism. But 'binding' has to be balanced with operational leeway. We cannot promise every contingency."

Reverend Sol leans forward, low and deliberate.

Reverend Sol: "Mara, the law reads like weather. Sometimes you can predict a storm and sometimes you cannot. The question is—do we anchor our people with wood and rope, or do we put our fate in the hands of those who—"

He doesn't finish. The implication is clear: in prior projects, promises were storm-broken.

Your palms sweat on the file. Every clause is a small moral test. You imagine Juno's spray-painted bicycle bounding across raised platforms; you imagine doors gone, families sifting through damp possessions. Those images make your breath quicken.

Municipal Lawyer: "There's another path. We draft a mediated hybrid clause—fast implementation, stewardship seats, procurement transparency language—with a sunset review. It gives you input without stopping construction."

You feel the word "sunset" like a shortcut—time-bound hope. Your stomach coils.

You: "Sunset reviews are where the commitments get diluted."

Kai interjects, softer now, trying to save both the project and the fragile thing between you.

Kai Navarro: "Mara, if we push too hard we could trigger litigation and delay the whole build. Some neighborhoods might be left exposed while lawyers argue in court."

You can hear the flash of panic beneath his optimism. It is there—he would rather risk a compromise that arrives than a perfect plan that never does.

Dr. Leila places a small stack of printouts on the table. The sensors' lines crawl like heartbeat strips.

Dr. Leila: "If you insist on binding vetoes and procurement transparency, we can use our triggers to minimize litigation risk. The clauses need specificity. Ambiguity invites challenge."

A lawyer taps a pen against his teeth. The sound becomes a metronome, speeding.

Municipal Lawyer: "Specificity makes you rigid. Rigidity equals liability. Blackbrand has lawyers. They know how to leverage narrow rules."

Evelyn lowers her voice, not unkind but sharpened.

Evelyn Black: "Mara, my team has built modular seawalls that have protected communities when time was short. We can adapt elements to include living materials; I'm willing to negotiate stewardship roles. But you must accept that some operational flexibility is non-negotiable."

You want to say the word "non-negotiable" back at her—about stewardship seats, about procurement audits—but you also know the legal machinery will chew you up if you misstep.

The conversation turns to language. Red lines bloom on the tablet, words like 'indemnity', 'good-faith', 'material adverse event', 'procurement exception'. Each phrase feels like a lever that could either lock the community out or secure its voice.

Evelyn Black: "We can include a clause for adaptation—if construction is blocked for more than ninety days due to litigation, there will be mediation with an independent arbiter. We cannot have a perpetual veto that allows inaction."

You: "We cannot trade our rights for a timetable."

Dr. Leila: "If we set the metric thresholds—"

Municipal Lawyer (cutting in): "Thresholds become targets for manipulation. If we tie procurement to a specific salinity or erosion rate, corporations can accelerate work before scrutiny."

The room's temperature seems to rise. Your pulse is a wire. Every argument ricochets. There's history here—projects that arrived fast and left splintered promises, lawsuits that fed on vagueness, communities that stitched themselves back together from loss.

Nita slams a sheaf of witness statements onto the table. The sound is loud in the cavernous lantern room.

Nita: "Testimony will show Blackbrand's past projects sometimes sidelined local oversight. People have names, Mara. People will speak."

Evelyn's hand moves a fraction. She doesn't flinch. Her voice is measured.

Evelyn Black: "Allegations require proof. If you want to raise past projects, do it with evidence. Otherwise you risk defamation claims and derailment."

A municipal lawyer leans in, his tone pragmatic, almost pleading.

Municipal Lawyer: "If you expose them publicly and it's not airtight, Blackbrand will litigate and the project stalls. This is strategic—public pressure works, but it can backfire."

You think of the town: the Floodplain Market, rooftop orchards, Reverend Sol's slow sermons—everybody's unstable daily architecture dependent on a plan that might never be. You also think of Evelyn's photograph tucked into her wallet—the shoreline she keeps like a talisman—and you imagine the fear that animates her decision-making.

You are not naive about consequences. You know that a hard refusal could stop construction and leave some neighbors exposed until you win a political fight. You know that a mediated hybrid could see parts built but with weakened community power. You know that publicly exposing Blackbrand could blow open a larger scandal and invite retaliation.

Your chest is tight. The arousal in the room ratchets; voices overlap, each claim a tide pushing toward a cliff.

Kai reaches for your hand under the table—not loud, just a physical anchor. You squeeze back, feeling both gratitude and the sharpness of the choice between you: safety now or control later; speed or sovereignty.

Reverend Sol folds his hands and speaks with the cadence of someone who has watched many councils fracture and heal.

Reverend Sol: "Mara Linh Alvarez, the town asks for a direction. Words here will be put into ink, and that ink will determine who is kept safe this season. Choose, child, but choose with the names of your neighbors in your mouth."

Silence drops like a net. Everyone looks to you—not just Evelyn, not just the lawyers, but Dr. Leila, Kai, Nita. The room shrinks to the space around your breath.

You stand, the magnetism of decision pulling you to the edge. The pen is finally in your hand again, the old hesitation replaced by an acute, driving focus. This is the high point of everything you've been building: clause vs. clause, line vs. line, people's lives waiting in the legal grammar.
