[Scene: Saltflower Greenhouse | Dusk]
[Visual: Exterior — Converted ferry hull patched with translucent solar film; warm light leaks from seams]
[Music: Soft acoustic guitar, low and steady]
[Sound: Nails of the tide tapping on pilings; distant gulls, a faint murmur of town life]

You push the slim metal latch and the greenhouse exhales—a warm, humid breath that smells of brine and compost, of wet wood and the sweet green tang of seedlings. The door sticks for a second, then gives with a sigh that sounds like the hull remembering its sea.

You step inside and the light rearranges itself around you: glass gilded by the low sun, translucence turning into a wash of gold-green. Seed trays crowd reclaimed crates—rows of salt-tolerant grasses, tiny root hairs trembling in the filtered air. A glove of fog clings to your jacket where you brushed the door. The brass of your compass rests at your throat, scuffed, familiar; you can feel its weight as an anchor when the world tilts.

[Visual: Interior — Rows of seedlings; vines climbing rusted beams; a workbench strewn with sketches]
[Sound: A soft drip from a rainwater barrel; paper rustling]

Kai is there before you notice him—kneeling at a low tank, hands moving in patient circles as he teases young kelp fronds onto starter lines. The light catches the sun-bleached tips of his braid; his face looks softer in this lowering sun. He glances up and grins, like he's caught you looking for something and found it.

Kai: "You timed the light perfectly. The kelp likes to be fussed over at dusk. It makes better babies. (He smirks.) You bring the storm stories or the schematics tonight?"

You: "Both, maybe." You set your sketchbook against the bench and let your fingers trail over the paper's edge. The first mark you make is tentative—thin pencil over thin light—until the rusted barrette at your hairline ticks against your skull and steadies the line.

You remember your father without the sharpness that used to come with it. Not the chaos of the night he was taken but the small details: the way he folded his hands when he fixed nets, the smell of diesel and lemon oil when he kept the boat's fittings from rusting through. You remember the storm only enough to know it is a contour in the map of you—an unhealed edge—but tonight the edge is smoothed by light. The compass at your throat is warmer than your skin.

You draw the first curve: a woven lattice, like the netting your father mended, scaled into a biopolymer mesh that breathes—sponge and seam. The idea tastes like salt and optimism.

Kai straightens, wiping his hands on his overalls, then waves a damp strand of kelp as if conducting an orchestra.

Kai: "Show me. I want to argue with it and then help it work."

You flip the sketchbook open. The page is a field of pencil smudges and precise arcs, crosshatching where you imagine porous cells letting water and life through. You trace the pattern with your fingertip, feeling the impression of graphite and the familiar indent where you've pressed too hard in thought.

Kai studies it, head tilted, then squints with an engineer's empathy.

Kai: "I like the way you layered the tensile fibers—like a basket that lets life slip through instead of boxing it out. If we stitch frames of driftwood at intervals, we could anchor living oysters in the spaces. They'd colonize the polymer and add mass without sealing everything off."

You: "That was my hope." Your voice is softer than you expect. "I keep thinking about repeating the same mistakes—making something that sounds like protection but kills the thing we're protecting."

Kai's smile evens into something closer to gravity. He crouches beside you, elbows on knees, and his fingers find the compass pendant where it sits against your shirt.

Kai: "You carry more weight than that little brass circle, you know."

You: "It helps." The truth: it helps because it's a piece of your father you can hold up to the light and say, This is for you. It helps because when the world hums, the compass does not pretend to answer everything. It points. You anchor.

[Sound: The greenhouse door creaks again; soft footsteps approach]

Nita bustles in, apron dusted with fish scales and soil. She carries a thermos and a cardboard tray of steamed dumplings wrapped in wax paper—her version of town comfort.

Nita: "Starter stew. Sol said he'd stop by to check the humidity, and Juno's been helping with the irrigation—figured you'd be hungry after a day of scheming." (She winks.) "Also, Mara—your mother's pickled lemons are gone. I blame everyone."

You laugh, and something in the greenhouse loosens. Laughter is small, weaving itself between kelp lines and seedling stems.

Reverend Sol arrives a heartbeat later, leaning on his carved cane, jacket smelling faintly of old oilcloth and sea-lime. He moves slower these days, but his eyes sweep the greenhouse like a captain surveying a safe harbor.

Reverend Sol: "If this is your new plan, Mara, it carries your fingerprints. I like that." He pauses near the seedlings, fingers hovering above a tray without touching. "Community makes better armor than fortress, I've always said. It bends."

You: "It shouldn't be a choice between armor and life." You look down at the sketch. "It has to be both. And honest—not something that takes from people to give to profits."

Reverend Sol studies you, a soft, steady weight of approval in his face.

Reverend Sol: "Your father would've liked that. He'd have told you to tie an extra knot." He taps his cane twice. "He would be proud of you."

The words sit warm in your chest. Guilt arrives, small and manageable—like an old bruise you can press and then let go. Tonight guilt is a candle in a lantern, not a storm.

Kai: "We could test a small panel. See what the oysters do. If it takes, we scale. If not, we learn." His optimism is practical, the kind that accepts failure as a footnote rather than a headline.

You nod, and your chest unclenches a fraction. The greenhouse feels like a net catching all the frayed ends of your day—neighbors' voices, plans in pencil, things mending in slow, stubborn rhythm. Outside, the town's patchwork repairs—a stitched boardwalk here, a tarp there—are visible through the glass as shapes of human care. They are not fixes that hide the sea; they are conversations with it.

Juno appears at the far bench, hair cropped and hoodie smeared with paint, holding a string of hand-painted wooden tags for the seedlings. They slap one onto a tray with theatrical flourish.

Juno: "For the future people. 'Do not sell—seedlings of rebellion.' Someone has to warn them."

Nita chuckles and offers you a dumpling.

Nita: "Eat. You start solving the coast's problems on an empty stomach, you'll make compromises you regret. Those were your father's words, probably after three drinks."

You take the dumpling, its steam fogging your glasses for a second. The warm, bright citrus of preserved lemon and the faint brine of fish inside is oddly like home on a plate.

Kai presses a thumb to the page, smudging a small crescent of graphite.

Kai: "You used to draw boats. Now it's structures that let the sea in and ask it to be neighborly. Funny how the ocean is always teaching us to coexist."

You breathe in the greenhouse's humid sweetness. The pencil moves on; the arcing lattices become a sequence of panels, modules that could be built by hands in town using reclaimed frames and the community's labor. You sketch a small note in the margin: 'pilot panel—promenade edge? community build day?'

Your pulse eases into the kind of rhythm that belongs to long-term plans: steady, patient, hopeful. The rising sun had once been a promise of the day's work; tonight, the descending light is a promise of what's next.

Reverend Sol clears his throat gently, a pebble dropped into still water.

Reverend Sol: "There's a meeting at the promenade tomorrow. Tideward. People will bring questions. Some will bring fears. Others will bring offers. It's time we showed up—not just with charts, but with what we can do."

The sentence sits like a bell's soft strike. You feel the ember in your chest glow a degree brighter—not a flare, but a decided warming. The sketchbook is heavy in your lap now with pencil-thick conviction.

Kai looks at you, eyes clear in the dimming light.

Kai: "We can bring the mock-up. A little model, some samples. Make it tactile so people can touch the idea and not just hear it."

You tuck the compass beneath your shirt so its cold metal presses near your sternum. The memory of your father in the boat flashes—he hums a song as he knots—and you realize that what you are building is, in small way, the same kind of careful tethering. You can be both mourning and making.

You draw one last quick annotation—'community build / oyster pilot / kids' workshop'—and the words feel true when your own hand reads them.

Evening light gilds the wet glass. The greenhouse hums softly around you: the drip, the breath of leaves, the quiet planning of people who still believe in patient labor. Hope is not something loud tonight. It's a small stubborn ember that won't blow out.

You close the sketchbook and slide it into its leather strap, fingers smudged with graphite and salt. Outside, the tide ticks against pilings as if marking the hours in its own patient hand.
