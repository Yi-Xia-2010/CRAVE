[Visual: The kelp-lines snake in the shallow chop, a single broken panel bobbing like a flaring distress buoy. The promenade behind you is a smear of wet wood and frantic figures.]

You still taste salt and metal. The storm has left grit in your teeth; your jacket clings to you with brine. The compass at your throat is a cold coin against skin—solid, familiar, useless for what comes next except as a thing to hold when the rest of you wants to break.

Around the bobbing speck the town moves like a body with a sudden wound. Voices spool into accusations and prayers. Someone curses. Someone laughs too loudly to keep from crying. The kelp platform lists where volunteers cluster—hands in gloves, ropes fraying, eyes burning with exhausted hope.

You can see Evelyn at the edge of the Blackbrand site, a silhouette where the site lights make a cold crescent. Her face reads like a ledger: precise, closed. You strain for a look she might give you—anger, pity, triumph—and find instead that same whole-image of complex unreadability. You have a dozen reasons for hate and none for satisfaction. The problem is not who is right or wrong in a headline. The problem is what you do next, and the town is looking to you with mouths open and no easy answer to feed them.

You take a breath and feel the thrum under it—Kai's bootbeats on the plank, Dr. Leila's voice clipped through a throat that will not settle, Reverend Sol's cane making a steady tap-tap that is both metronome and anchor. Nita's hands are raw; Juno's jaw is tight, furious. The crowd looks like a knot you can both untie and drown in.

You: "We stabilize the panel. Then we—" Your words snag on a dozen objections before they leave you. You feel them—each possibility a weight: immediate action, public confession, or a careful concealment that might rot the town's trust later.

Kai steps closer, breath steaming in the chill. His hair is plastered at the temple; his smile is thin. He hasn't slept properly in days and neither have you.

Kai: "We can pull that line closer, reroute the moorings. There's a chance to save the frames if we move fast. If we lose the anchor points we lose everything we tried to show—people will say the whole method is a joke."

You (sharp): "Or we go out blind and the whole platform comes apart with us on it. You saw the way that module sheared—"

Kai (interrupting, low): "I saw the way it flexed. I know what it's like to make something and watch it fail. But I also know how fast this will spiral if we don't do something now."

Dr. Leila pushes into the space between you, breath reeking of seawater and sensor gel. Her hands are still carrying a damp tablet, the readouts blinking red.

Dr. Leila: "From the transponders—structural loads spiked during the surge. If you send folks out without proper shoring, there will be injuries. Legal exposure is immediate if a camera catches a volunteer falling. You know how these feeds go—'reckless eco-activists.'"

You: (You feel the shame of being framed already, and this is before you choose anything.) "And hiding it?" you say. The word tastes like ash. "We allow a clean photo-op on the morning talk shows and repair in secret—how long until someone finds the frayed mooring and asks why we didn't say anything?"

Reverend Sol taps his cane harder, calling attention without raising his voice.

Reverend Sol: "The town needs truth. Not because it soothes journalists, but because trust is what we have left. But there's also people watching—kids, Juno—who will take actions based on what we do right now."

Juno, fists clenched at your side, spits a word that could be a curse or a promise.

Juno: "I can go out. I've taken the boat a dozen times. Let me."

Nita wipes her hands on her apron and rounds on you with the bluntness that has never failed to skewer you when you need it.

Nita: "If you lead them, Mara—if you lead anyone into the waves without a plan, the market won't forgive that. The whole damn state will sit on us. But if you own it—own the mess—people will rally. They always do when you call them honestly."

You reach for the rope with two gloved hands, feeling the fray bite into your palm. You think of the kids who skip between stalls on the raised platforms, of the house where your father kept the brass map tucked under a floorboard, of all the small scaffolds of makeshift defense that kept neighbors' roofs from washing away. Those memories make the choice less academic and more akin to a wound being pressed.

[Visual: Tight close on your fingers as they find a good knot; the camera pulls back to include Kai, Leila, Sol, Nita, Juno, a smear of the crowd behind them]
[Sound: The wind hardens into a razor hiss; a hawk—a drone—zooms a fraction too close and the crowd hushes]
[Music: Tension escalates—staccato percussion and a high-pitched string whine]

You feel pressure from everywhere—the legal threats, the optics, Evelyn's unreadable calm, Kai's imploring stare. Each pulse tells you one thing: time is moving whether you decide or not.

Kai leans in so close you can name the scent of the cloth tied to his wrist—salt and clove—and his voice drops to a ragged edge.

Kai: "If you try for the salvage, I'll go with you. I won't let this be another headline where someone else gets to decide what happened here."

You (almost whisper): "And if I decide to be honest—post the footage, ask people to come—will you stand in front of the cameras with me? Will you let the journalists cut us down while we hand them the rope to hang us with?"

Kai's hand finds the rope beside yours, fingers brushing—brief, electric. Hope flares for an instant before the wind snuffs it.

Kai: "I'll stand by you. But you know what that will mean. You're the face of this right now. The backlash could be brutal. Evelyn's people will be gunning for contracts and headlines. We could be erased from any policy table."

Dr. Leila interjects again, voice steadying with clinical clarity.

Dr. Leila: "There's also a middle ground: a controlled disclosure. We mark the site for legal safety, call in certified shoring teams under community oversight, and livestream the repair. We control the narrative and the safety protocols."

Nita scoffs. "Controlled? When have we ever controlled a live feed? They'll clip ten seconds and call it proof we're incompetent."

Reverend Sol aims a kindly, exhausted look at you.

Reverend Sol: "Whatever you choose, make it be the choice your conscience can live with. You'll have to sleep—if sleeping will be possible—and you'll be making decisions that ripple to the children. Think of them."

You hear a rising chorus: callers on a livestream, a reporter's shout from across the mudflats, a child's sharp, helpless cry. The town's faces are squeezed into inches of intensity—expectation, blame, pleading. The arousal inside you spikes—cotton in your mouth, your heartbeat thundering in your ears as if the tide itself has found a drum.

You know, with the cold, particular clarity that comes right before a storm breaks: whatever you pick now will not only affect the physical salvage of a few modular panels. It will bend trust, break or harden alliances, and write the next paragraph of your life in a public ledger.

You feel each option expanding into its consequences like tidewater. The salvage is immediate and dangerous—your hands know knots, but your hands are not invulnerable. Broadcast is humiliation now for possible trust later—a social bleeding that could scar into something lasting. Concealment is a tentatively safe shore with shark teeth under the sand; it promises calm but carries the risk of a future scandal that would devour what little legitimacy you have left.

Juno presses against your side, voice small and hard. "Don't let them make you the story," they say. "Make them tell the story of what you did."

You glance at Kai. For a sliver of time his expression fractures into something you have only seen twice before—humanness that sits under the policy and the tinkering: fear for you, fury at the injustice, but also a softness that wants to keep you whole.

Dr. Leila steps close and places a damp palm over the tablet as if the device might steady you.

Dr. Leila: "If you want to salvage, we need a manifest: who goes out, what safety gear, who records for legal protection. If you choose transparency, we need a script and community liaisons—this can't be a raw feed. If you choose concealment, you understand the legal fallout if this leaks. There are lawyers on both sides waiting."

Nita's voice cuts like shearing rope.

Nita: "You want to be visible and brave, or quiet and cowardly? Either way, people will have words. If it's the salvage, be ready to lose people you care about to the tide. If it's transparency, be ready to lose your face for a while. If it's concealment, be ready to lose your soul a bit."

You taste bile. Negative threads spool through you: fear, anxiety, the sense of inexorable loss. The wind picks up, pushing the waves against the kelp platform with a percussion that makes the whole structure shiver. Very hard now—the tempo of everything is a drumroll toward collapse or resolution.

You look at the bobbing panel again. It is small and absurd in the face of the stakes, and yet it is the fulcrum. The town's gaze slices toward you like a knife. Kai's hand brushes the rope you hold; Reverend Sol's eyes invite steadiness; Juno's jaw sets. In all of them you can read expectant hunger for leadership, for an answer that feels like a promise.

Your lungs draw in cold air. The decision crystallizes and will not be delayed.
