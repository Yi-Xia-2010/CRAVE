[Scene: Rooftop Orchards | Early Morning — Low Tide]
[Visual: Rows of raised beds backlit by a thin, cold sun; dew beaded on wind-curled leaves; a narrow plank walkway leads down toward the kelp-farm platform. Your sketchbook is strapped to your hip.]
[Sound: Low gull calls; the distant metallic rhythm of winches at the kelp farm; a rope rubbing post with a slow, nervous squeal]
[Music: Tense BGM — a single repeating motif underlaid with a rising string]
You breathe and count the seconds between heartbeats the way you used to count knots on a line. The town is still sleeping in pockets, but here, at the edge, everything is awake: the salt on your lips, the taste of compost and diesel on the wind, the cheap solar-film curtains on the rooftop whispering against the morning draft.

Kai is squinting into binoculars, hands steady while his grin tries on optimism like a work glove. Dr. Leila crouches over a cluster of sensors, fingers cold, face lit by a tablet that shows currents and a scatter of confidence dots. Your hands find familiar things—rope, a carabiner, the brass of your compass. You hold them because holding is an act that keeps the past from shoving you under.

Kai: "You madwoman. You know the tide's tricky today."
You: "Since when has that stopped us?"
Kai: (half-laugh, half-plea) "Since the last time I had bruises shaped like bad ideas."
Dr. Leila: (without looking up) "Bruises heal. Data points do not. Check the lower mooring tension before we launch the modules."

You run a palm along the mooring line, feeling fibers rubbed thin. The weather reports are a string of caveats: late-season surge probability upgraded from 'low' to 'advisory.' That word—advisory—lands in your chest like small stones. You tighten a shackle, then loosen it, then tighten again, because the right tension is less a number and more a hope.

Kai watches your face as if reading a weather map of your thoughts. He steps close enough for the warmth of him to be a thing you can rely on—literally and otherwise.

Kai: "Whatever happens today, we make it about the data. Make it about the seedlings, the tidal break I'm going to track, not the headlines they want."
You: (quiet, steadier) "We do it in front of the town. We show them the reef can hold—if we can show it. If it doesn't hold, we collect the data and we tell the truth."

Dr. Leila: (soft, urgent) "And if it fails, we learn faster. The models—if we capture the failure dynamics—are valuable. Not consolation, but leverage."

Her voice is science-sharp, not comfort-sharp; she never promised comforting platitudes. You want to believe that a failed experiment could be turned into power. You want to believe you are strong enough to take this hit if it comes. The town looks to you because you volunteered to step into the cold.

[Scene: Kelp Farm Platform | Mid Morning — Public Demonstration]
[Visual: A floating lattice of modular reef segments bobbing between kelp lines. Raised platforms ring the perimeter, packed with neighbors, Nita with a bandana tied high, Reverend Sol leaning on his carved cane, Juno fidgeting with a rope, a smattering of media from nearby towns. Beyond, the Blackbrand site is a neat geometry of concrete and cranes; a figure in a dark trench — Evelyn Black — watches from the shore through a pair of binoculars.]
[Sound: Murmured crowd, a child's laugh cut by the slap of a palm against a lifejacket; the soft pop of air in inflatable pontoons]
[Music: Tense BGM swells into a faster, insistent pulse]

You step onto the platform and the sound shifts—footsteps become a chorus, and for a second you are not just Mara but the town's chosen voice. You feel the heat behind your sternum: this is what you asked for. This is why you chose to risk the public demonstration instead of the safer meetings or the endless negotiations.

You: (into a small stage mic) "This is a pilot, not a promise. We are showing what living defenses can do when woven with local knowledge."
Nita: (calling up) "Show us they can hold, Mar!"
Reverend Sol: (steady) "Tell them about the stewardship clause."

Dr. Leila kneels by the nearest module, opening a clamp to let the kelp lines unfurl. The first fronds ripple free and the crowd exhales as if relieved. Tiny barnacles and oysters, seeded earlier by Kai and a line of volunteers, shift and settle into their new homes—an instant, fragile congregation.

Dr. Leila: (excited) "Look at the hold on that substrate. The juvenile oysters are attaching; salinity's in the sweet spot."
Kai: (loud enough for you) "That's our little army."

You watch the green unfurl and feel the thinnest thread of triumph. For a sliver of time, the demonstration sings: children pointing, elders nodding, cameras leaning in. Even Evelyn's binoculars lower for a beat; her face is unreadable.

Kai reaches for your hand and squeezes—an anchor in the small chaos. His smile is quick, and in it you hear planters and late-night meetings and hands callused from rope. For a sliver of breath, you imagine the reef holding, the gulls finding new nesting on sheltered lines, Juno growing up where your father once cast nets.

Then the first sound of wrongness arrives: a low, grinding note from under the platform. It feels like the earth has been rearranged a meter beneath the waterline.

[Sound: A sudden deep groan from moorings; a metallic snap like a snapped violin string]
[Music: Strings slash into dissonance; percussion ratchets up into an urgent, syncopated beat]

Dr. Leila: (sharp) "Current spike—unexpected. Mooring loads rising."
You: (to Kai, breathless) "Get to the aft—check the starboard cinch!"
Kai: "On it!"

Kai runs, boots slapping. You lunge toward a module as a rope snatches taut and then gives with a sound that feels like betrayal. Water hits the platform in sheets, soaking knees, cold and sharp. People stumble; someone screams—half shout, half prayer. A child's lifejacket bobs like a discarded apple.

[Sound: Rope snapping; collective shouts; the water's roar swallowing speech]
[Visual: Modules tilting, one crate of seedlings scraping free and slipping down the face of the module]

You grab a line and your fingers close around seawater and fraying synthetic. The mooring you double-checked this morning is kissing the ragged edge of failure. The module nearest you heels out, its edges scraping the platform. The seedlings—your sketches and months of careful grafting—dip under a wave and pop back up like little drowned things looking for air.

Dr. Leila: (yelling over the surge) "We need emergency ballast—now! Tie the secondary anchors!"
You: (screaming) "Where's the spare tether?!"
Kai: (voice strangely high) "It snapped—it's gone with the second module!"

Time contracts. Every instruction is a throw of hands and two-word commands. You feel the gripping need to be in two places at once—on the line and already writing the press release and making room in the shelter and being a daughter who won't repeat the compromise that took your father. The ocean, suddenly, is not merely a setting but a claim being made in loud, wet letters.

Evelyn's silhouette is sharp against the Blackbrand cranes; she is not smiling. Her binoculars slide up and she watches as if recording an argument she already won. Her stance is perfectly calm. Calm enough to be dangerous.

[Scene: Brackish Channels | Immediate Aftermath — High Tide Moment]
[Visual: A drifting modular unit, half-submerged, spinning slowly in the churn; people on platforms point, some already lowering small rescue craft; the kelp lines are tangled, some ripped free like thrown ropes.]
[Sound: Traffic of small engines; someone's radio breathing panic; the slap of retrieval nets against hulls]
[Music: Tense BGM peaks into chaotic polyrhythms, then drops to a raw, single sustained note]

You are in the water, chest burning, clinging to a piece of polymer that used to be a promise. The cold compresses thought. Your lungs trade oxygen for focused, practical calculations: can we anchor it? Can we tow it? How many people did we just put at risk?

Someone curses your name—it's Nita, and the word is not kind. Reverend Sol is shouting at volunteers to stay safe. Juno's face is white, shock and fury braided together. Kai surfaces nearby, a line tangled around his forearm, his hair a dark halo. He laughs once—sound that might have been joy turned into a nozzle of adrenaline.

Kai: (gasping) "Mar—did you see—"
You: (cutting) "We lost the forward module. The second tether—it's gone. Did Leila get the transponder?"
Dr. Leila: (already checking her tablet even as water slaps her hair) "Some packets, some telemetry—intermittent. If we get close we can still salvage the juvenile oysters' lines—if we can get enough hands."

Your chest is tight with something that isn't quite breathing. This is not a neat failure. This is messy, public, and full of witnesses. Your internal monologue is a tidal map of blame: you could have delayed, you could have negotiated, you could have stopped. You remember a phrase someone once told you about leadership: better to be the first to fall than the last to act. You feel ridiculous for the pride that made you step forward and the guilt for the people who followed.

A module drifts by, its seedlings clinging like tiny umbrellas. You reach, fingertips skimming the slimy scaffold, and fail to close on it. The ocean takes the gesture and keeps going.

[Visual: Tight shot of your hand, shaking, fingertips inches from a drifting tray of seedlings]
[Sound: The tray sloshes away; a child's sob becomes a small, sharp bell]

You haul yourself onto the nearest platform; the deck is slick with seawater and the footprints of panic. People look to you because that's the shape you made in their imagination: someone who held the lamp. Your legs feel like borrowed objects, the rest of you kept afloat by reflex and the splintered will of a community.

Dr. Leila: (soft, terrible) "We have partial data—enough to show attachment thresholds under low load, but the breach event—if the video is used against us, it will be framed as proof living defenses can't handle late-season surges."
You: (voice small) "Or it could be proof of what fails when fabrication is rushed, when equity isn't considered."
Evelyn: (from the shoreline, voice carried on a faint speaker from the Blackbrand site) "You took a gamble in public, Ms. Alvarez. The town will judge by results."

Her tone is clinical, almost pastorally blank. You want to reply—anger burning in the back of your throat that could be a match—but your words scrape like sand. The faces around you shift from watching to weighing. Some lean toward salvage; others toward spin—already counting headlines.

[Visual: The crowd parts; a dozen hands reach for tools; someone holds up a phone, recording; the sky is a watercolor of bruised gray]
[Music: The single sustained note holds, then flickers with an unresolved tremor]

You stand at the edge of the soaked platform, the salt in your mouth sour and honest. Pieces of your plan float away in the same arc as your father's memory—unpredictable, cruelly timed. Your chest is heavy with the sense that this moment will become the argument people use to either bury your work or to force the system to change.

Your thoughts narrow to one immediate axis: salvage what we can, or prepare the narrative we will need to survive the spin. Hands are already moving. Words are already forming in mouths. The town looks to you again, and the compass at your chest feels like it is being gouged by the choices you made to get here.

You could pull ropes and boats and bodies into the water and try to reclaim the modules—risking more—and maybe prove something about resilience. You could pivot to a truth-first approach: secure whatever data we have and craft the testimony that frames this as an engineered failure, not a community one. Either way, the next minutes will decide whether Blackbrand writes the headline or Marisport does.

The wind takes the banner at the platform edge and rips it free; it flutters away, a torn promise sewn back into the gulls.

You breathe. You are already making the pattern of the next move, even before your brain finishes telling your hands what to do.

[Visual: Close-up on your face — storm-gray eyes rimmed with salt; behind you, Evelyn's silhouette still stands like a punctuation mark; the modules float, smaller and smaller]
[Music: Tense BGM cuts to a single held chord that refuses to resolve]

A page-turn moment—your chest tight as a drum, your community split between action and narrative, the modules drifting like lost sentences from a story you wrote in good faith. You know the next step must be decisive, public, and dangerous; you know it will define how Marisport reads this day for months to come.
