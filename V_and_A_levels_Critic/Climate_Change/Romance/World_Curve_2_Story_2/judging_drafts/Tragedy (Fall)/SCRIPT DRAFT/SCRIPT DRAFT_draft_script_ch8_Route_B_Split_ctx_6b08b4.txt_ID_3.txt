Kai waits an arm's length from you, shoulders slightly forward, palms visible—open.

You: "We have to put stewardship on the record. Not as an addendum—binding. With named responsibilities and a community board that has real veto power over procurement."

Evelyn: "Mara, 'binding' is a legal term that carries liabilities. Blackbrand can accept stewardship language—but indemnities are standard. The company needs flexibility to respond to emergent conditions. Contracts can't be written like manifestos."

Evelyn's tone is smooth; the words are oil on cold metal. She taps her tablet once. The blue light traces a precise arc across her cheek.

You: "Flexibility becomes a loophole. 'Emergent conditions' can be defined to mean whatever keeps corporate control intact. We need thresholds and triggers—clear, measurable—and a named stewardship board that signs off on major procurement."

Municipal Lawyer 1 (Ms. Arden): (folding her hands) "Ms. Alvarez, it's not unusual for proponents to seek indemnity clauses. The city is exposed if we accept open-ended failure liabilities. We recommend language that limits municipal exposure while creating oversight mechanisms that are enforceable under state statute."

Your pulse tugs against your throat. Statutes and exposure feel like hard rocks you could drown beneath. You already imagine the headlines: 'City Exposes Itself'—and behind the words, a laundry list of houses torn from their foundations.

Evelyn: (cool) "And who verifies the data? An independent auditor? Third-party panels cost time. Blackbrand can fund rapid deployment if we allow for technical discretion—someone must be able to make on-the-spot engineering judgments."

Dr. Leila: "Independent third-party verification is standard in environmental contracts. We can specify accredited auditors and transparent reporting. It's not about cost; it's about trust."

Kai leans forward; his fingers drum where the map is wet with salt from earlier hands.

Kai: "We pick the auditors from a list vetted by the community and the municipality. We also publish rolling data—let people see the measurements as they happen. Transparency removes the 'trust us' problem."

Evelyn: (a thin smile) "Public dashboards are optics. They don't resolve liability."

The lawyers exchange a look that tastes like smoke. Reverend Sol slopes his eyes toward you—no words, just that heavy, watchful look you know too well. Nita's jaw tightens; you know she'll be ready to stand and speak when the time comes.

Dr. Leila's tablet pings; she scrolls, breath short.

Dr. Leila: "If we lock in third-party verification, specific procurement windows, and a named stewardship board with veto power over vendor selection when thresholds are met, it's enforceable. We can define the enforcement mechanism—binding arbitration under state environmental code, with expedited injunctive relief."

Evelyn: "Arbitration can be pro-Blackbrand in practice; wielded badly, it delays public remedies. We will accept arbitration only if it's balanced with emergency provisioning clauses that allow us to act in the public interest."

Nita stands before you like a lighthouse herself—small but bright. "People here don't have time for arbitration while their basements drown. We need the townsfolk on record: testimony, signatures, a public commitment. If Blackbrand wants 'public interest' as a lever, then public interest will include the town's voice."

Her voice is rough and immediate. It lands like a pebble in a still pool and sends ripples—some nod, some look away.

The room fractures into a hundred small battles: legal pragmatism against moral urgency, speed against certainty, liability against stewardship. The High Arousal of the hour presses in. You feel it like a storm rolling in fast: the rhythm of breathing quickens, your palms dampen, your jaw aches.

Evelyn's voice becomes sharper, the steel under her words surfacing.

Evelyn: "Ms. Alvarez, the compromise is this—Blackbrand will accept a stewards clause with named representatives and a community seat. Procurement will be transparent, but final procurement authority must retain technical discretion for rapid response. Indemnity language will be limited in scope. You sign this—construction begins with phased openings. You refuse—implementation stalls. You expose our past—I'll see you in court and in the headlines. The town needs protection. What is your answer?"

The room is suddenly a vacuum of sound except for the hum and the small quick breath you take that feels too loud. It takes everything in you not to remember the paper-thin sounds of the night your father left: wind, desperate calling, the shore's unanswering throat. You have the same folder; the same pen.

Kai's hand tightens on the edge of the table, knuckles white.

Kai: (softly) "We can hold them to the stewardship board if we make the board’s authority trigger-based with immediate injunction rights. Don't let 'technical discretion' be a catch-all. We can name auditors, set reporting protocols, make procurement windows public and contestable within a set period."

Evelyn: "And litigation windows? Delays? Who pays? Communities cannot be marketplaces for endless contestation."

Mr. Chen: "This is where the negotiation lives. We can write escrowed funds controlled by the stewardship board for immediate repairs tied to triggers. That balances action with accountability."

Reverend Sol: (slow, measured) "The law can hold a town. The law can also be a god that favors those with money. We must be careful with the deities we make."

Your head swims with options—each one a lever that might save houses or surrender them. The High Arousal flares; your heart drums like a hammer against a hull. You grasp at the safe things—data, clauses, charts—and none feel safe enough.

You inhale and for a beat the room's air tastes like rusted salt and old coffee. Everything has narrowed to the single line of the contract before you.

You: (to the room, voice tight) "We will not sign language that leaves 'community oversight' toothless. We will not accept indemnities that shift municipal risk onto people who live here. If Blackbrand cannot accept named auditors, named steward representatives, and clear triggers with injunctive power, then we need to pause and build public evidence, not hand them a blank cheque."

Evelyn's expression flickers—no anger, but the careful arithmetic of someone assessing assets and liabilities.

Evelyn: "Pausing risks lives, Ms. Alvarez."

You: "Continuing with a hollow promise risks the town's autonomy. It risks our history."

Silence brackets the words. The Spire's hum presses. Kai looks at you—his face full of that open, fierce hope that usually steadies you—and then looks away as if to step out from being your ballast and into being your partner in a fight.

Your internal compass spins, not with direction but with force: duty pulling you toward immediate shelter, memory pulling you away from compromises that erased people you loved. The arousal spikes—the sense that something must happen now, and that any choice will carve the town differently.

Dr. Leila clears her throat; the data stays projected like a ghost across the map. "There is a middle path," she says. "Stewardship board with named roles, named auditors—and a phased enforcement schedule: phase one, emergency protective measures on high-risk blocks triggered automatically; phase two, staged procurement subject to stewardship board veto but with escrow funds for immediate action pending review. Legal language can be drafted to allow injunctive relief on clear data triggers and fast arbitration timelines. It is messy, but it's actionable."

Mr. Chen: "Fast arbitration windows are unusual, and the state may not enforce expedited injunctive relief without clear statutory backing. We can craft it, but its robustness in court is uncertain."

Nita slams a hand palm-down on the table—an island of personal heat in the procedural chill.

Nita: "And what about the people who will stand in those houses if you sign a willow clause and they drown anyway? We need voice. We need teeth. We need a public commitment that isn't just pretty words in a brochure."

Evelyn: "We can guarantee specific funding allocations and oversight seats. We cannot accept open-ended liability."

The words ricochet. Your mouth tastes of metal. The choice hangs like a line thrown across roiling water: cross and hope, or stay and risk being cut off. A high, urgent pressure builds in your chest until it feels like if you speak wrong, everything will cave.

You look at Kai—who looks back, bearing all the weight of his own hopes and fears—and then at Reverend Sol, at Dr. Leila, at Nita, at the lawyers, at Evelyn. Each face is an argument. Each breath is a timer.

You imagine Juno—your sibling—asking, years from now, whether you did the thing you should have. You imagine the town's children on raised platforms, the salt bright in their hair. You imagine your father at the rail, laughing at something only he understood, the horizon open and indifferent.

The moment compresses. The arousal reaches its apex as you realize every clause is a moral lever—and someone must pull it.
