You walk the boards alone because that's what you have become: the steady, lonely thing that keeps walking when the town has split into its separate, private griefs. The scuffed brass of the compass bites into your palm every time you let it go; you close your fingers around it now and feel the old dent like a familiar pain.

Dawn smells of iron and old rope, of burnt coffee left too long on a neighbor’s stove. The planks are slick from last night's tide—your boots whisper across them. Each step is arithmetic: a house you helped raise, a rooftop orchard where a friend once planted figs, a childhood path Juno used to race along. The list keeps adding and subtracting itself in the bonework of you: what you saved, what you hid, what you let be rewritten.

[Visual: Close-up on the compass in your hand; the wave-clip metal barrette in your hair glints]
[Sound: Your breath, sharp and ragged against the low wind]
[Music: Strings tighten, bowing faster; the heartbeat percussion grows]

You should not have kept the ledger quiet. The lawyers said risk. The town said trust. The modules were easier to move if no one asked too many questions. You told yourself it was tactical; you told yourself it was to buy time.

But the time you bought turned to a thin, bitter currency. Kai left because, maybe, he could not live inside the rot of what we'd done; Juno left because the future here tasted like ash and compromise. Nita's shop still stands, but she looks at you now as if you owe her the next decade of mornings.

Nita appears without a formal announcement—she's a bright smear of bandana and bright apron at the market entrance, already sweeping invisible residue from last night’s tensions off the boards. She pauses when she sees you, and the broom stops mid-sweep, as if the town's breath has been held and is now very near to snapping.

Nita: "You look like you got up to meet a storm that hasn't started yet."

You: "Maybe I did."

Nita: (laughs, but there's no humor in it) "Maybe you did, Mara. Or maybe you woke up to what you helped set loose. People are talking. They say—" (she swallows, the word jagged) "—you knew more than you told."

You: "They have to understand the numbers. The evidence isn't the kind of thing you read across a table and suddenly agree to."

Nita: "No. But they understand what they lost. And that's all that matters to them this week." (she leans closer; her voice drops) "Juno's postcard came through. She didn't say much—just 'I'm gone.' Kai's letter said he's taken a job farther north. Sol says he's doing what he can, but the meetings keep getting smaller."

The wind picks up; it seems to want to carry everything away. Your tongue feels thick, like paper dampened by seawater. Rage leaks into you along with the cold.

You: "Did you think I wanted any of that? Do you think I like waking up to your faces and pretending this counts as repaired?"

Nita: (the broom taps the boards once, hard) "Don't make this only about what you want, Mara. Make it about what this town deserves. Or you can keep building little places of safety and pretend that the rest of us aren't paying in people."

You stare at her and see, in the steadiness of her jaw, the economy of anger that feeds the market. She isn't asking for an apology. She wants a ledger that shows the truth.

[Music: Cello drops to a deeper, more urgent tone; violin runs accelerate]
[Sound: A faint clanging—someone dropping a tool farther down the promenade—echoes like an accusation]

You: (voice starting to break) "I—I thought I was doing the right thing. I thought secrecy would let us get ahead of the lawyers, of the developer. I thought—"

Nita: "You thought so that you could keep your compromise for yourself." (she rounds on you sharply) "That's not leadership, Mara. That's a private saving for public ruin."

The words land like stones. For a moment the wind seems to howl approval.

You feel the impulse to explain—to list the meetings, the notes, the late phone calls. You feel the urge to name the people who pushed for engineered walls, who would have used the failure to raze entire blocks. But every explanation sounds like a new shade of excuse. The tide of small betrayals recedes and leaves a sticky film in its wake.

Reverend Sol's cane taps before he appears—soft, deliberate, carrying years and the smell of oilskin. He moves with the gravity of the town's slow reckonings. When he reaches you both, the scrape of his boots is an old metronome that used to mark better days.

Reverend Sol: "Mara." (the name is not a blessing or a judgment; it is a place-holder, as if naming could hold the moment steady) "This is not how I wanted to talk to you."

You: "There's nothing you can say that I haven't run through myself a thousand times."

Reverend Sol: "Maybe. But other people need to hear what you mean to say aloud." (he looks at Nita, then at the sea) "Hiding is a soft thing. It shields you for a while and then hardens into a wall that keeps out everything good."

Nita: "And it keeps out all the people who needed to know the truth."

You: (you laugh, a short, ugly sound) "Truth burns when you carry it. Better to build and keep people warm."

Reverend Sol: "Was it warmth, child? Or was it a blanket folded over the thing you were afraid to call—loss?"

Your chest collapses into a note of panic. The air is thin; the sound of the waves comes at you like hands. The memories are rapid-fire: your father's boat gone in the swell; the compass dented in your palm while you groped for the radio; Kai holding you once and saying the thing that would become a promise. You remember Kai's last look—the way his mouth set when he said he'd stand with you, and then, later, the box at the station labeled with a destination you don't want to open.

Nita: "You think this is still about protection? Protection from what? From truth? From your own fear?"

You: "Protection from the wrong kind of loss." (you throw the word out and it shatters) "We were trying to buy time to get the living systems planted, to anchor neighborhoods. The modules were a bridge, not the final form."

Reverend Sol: (softening, but with winter in his words) "Bridges can become prisons if the hand that holds them never opens. People need to know who built the road. They need to choose the path together."

You: "And if they choose fire? If they tear down the things that might have saved them because they were angry? You think I want that?"

Nita: "This isn't about what you want. This is about whether the town survives as a town."

The accusation cuts across something raw inside you. The panic rises—heart pounding faster, breath short—then sharp, jagged anger. You find yourself yelling, the sound brittle with too many sleepless nights.

You: "Do you think I don't already pay—" (your voice fails) "Do you have any idea what I give every day?"

Nita: (quiet now, the bandana at her temple wet with salt from her hair) "We know enough. Not everything. We need more honesty, Mara. That's the ledger you refused to open."

Reverend Sol steps between the two of you like a shore wall. He leans on his cane, and for a moment you imagine him old and unyielding as the lighthouse—past its function but still there, refusing to fall.

Reverend Sol: "You have to decide, Mara. Not tomorrow, today. Whether to stay and try to rebuild trust, or to leave and let the town untangle without you. You cannot hold both the compass and the ledger forever."

Nita: "And if you stay, you will keep losing people who don't believe in your secrecy."

The words are not new, but they press like a storm beating a pier. Your hands close around the compass until the metal bites the flesh. You taste salt and guilt and the ghost of your father's cigarettes.

You think of Juno's postcard, the jagged pen strokes; you think of Kai's letterbox at the station, the way it had felt like a hinge that would close and lock. You think of the kelp lines you planted with volunteers—some surviving, some gone—each a small proof that life could persist if tended. But the proof doesn't erase the ruin: the families who moved inland, the kid who stopped playing in the raised platforms, the market stall with a 'For Lease' card half-hidden under produce.

[Visual: The horizon is a white seam; the water eats the light]
[Sound: The wind rises, as if the sky itself has been called into bearing witness]
[Music: All instruments converge into a single, terrible chord; the heartbeat percussion becomes staccato, relentless]

You: (your voice a whisper that the wind tries to take) "I don't know if I can stay."

Nita: "Then don't stay out of guilt, Mara. Stay because there is a reason to stay."

Reverend Sol: "And if you leave, leave with the truth in your hands. Not secrets folded into your pocket."

You close your eyes. The promenade creaks. Somewhere down the way someone slams a shutter—sharp, final—and it feels like the sound of a town sealing a wound.

You think of Kai's promise—'I'll stand with you'—and how promises can be both prophecy and weight. You think of the way he said it with soft ferocity; the memory is both a warm thing and an unbearable pressure.

You open your eyes to the sea the color of old metal. The compass is hot and heavy and impossible to drop. Your breath is a ragged metronome. In your chest there is a storm you have been steering for years, and now the storm has come to claim its due.

You know the decision is a clean line on the map of your life: stay and attempt to stitch the town back while the fabric frays further—or leave and—what? Seek some redemption in distance? Find Kai? Build elsewhere? The choices are jagged and each hurts in different ways.

Your hands do not move. The wind does not tell you which way to go. The town around you is a collection of small, incandescent resentments and a few hardy lights that refuse to go out.
