[Scene: Tideward Waterline | Dusk]
[Visual: Wind-lashed horizon; kelp lines snapping against a tilted platform. A single dangling module bobs in the gray, haloed by a strip of dying light.]
[Sound: Engines idling, distant shouts, the wet slap of rope against wood. A sensor emits an intermittent, clinical beep.]
[Music: Aggressive strings undercut with a low, driving percussion—relentless.]

You are soaked through to the bone. Salt stings your eyes and mouth; your hair clings to your neck in a dozen cold strands. Rope burns map the backs of your palms—white lines that ache with each small movement. The kelp where it still holds lashes like battered flags. The one panel, the one cluster of oysters still threaded to a sliver of rope, bobs away and then back as if deciding whether to stay.

Your chest is tight with a language you learned when you were young—loss, responsibility, that particular taste of guilt that smells like diesel and wet wood. You can feel the town's faces like a tide pressing toward you: shock, accusation, stunned silence that is somehow heavier than shouting.

Kai stands a few feet away, shoulders hunched, mouth working around something he won't let himself say. His overalls are smeared with brine; the fabric clings where it shouldn't. He looks exhausted and wired at once. When your eyes catch, he forces a grin that doesn't reach them and then drops it.

Kai: "They'll run it tomorrow. Front page. 'Marisport experiment fails'—you know how the papers dress it up."

You: "Then we'll tell them what happened. We'll show them the process—not the perfect outcome, but the work. They'll see that we tried."

Kai's jaw tightens. He steps closer until the smell of herbal cloth and salt fills your nostrils.

Kai: "Tried and lost is what they're going to call it. You know Evelyn's people will make that the whole story. They'll frame it as proof that small projects are sentimental and stupid."

You: "Because she profits from proving that, yes. That doesn't change the fact that people need to know the truth." (Your voice is flat; you try to keep the edges from fraying.)

Kai's hands fidget. He has always wanted the town to see the heart of the work—data, plants, people—but he's also terrified of the headlines that will split the community's trust like a storm-saw.

Kai: "If we put it all out there, the council could use it to shut us down before we get the next grant. If we salvage now without telling—" (his gaze drops) "—we might keep the optics, but if anyone finds out later it's worse."

You feel the history of compromises press in: the bargains made to secure marginal wins, your father's absence counting like a ledger of what you could not save. The smell of wet concrete from the development site mingles with wet kelp and someone's cigarette. Every breath tastes like decision.

[Visual: A volunteer—young, hands trembling, a rope looped around one wrist—approaches you, eyes bright and raw.]
[Sound: A camera somewhere clicks like a metronome; a reporter's voice off-screen calls for a statement.]

Volunteer: "Mara—" (the name tastes wrong on your tongue but it rolls out) "What do we do? The lines are coming loose faster than we can—"

You: "We gather what we can. We secure the harbor lines. We prioritize the buoys with the oyster clusters—" (you gesture at the bobbing mass) "—and we get people off the platform if it lists more."

Nita pushes through the crowd, apron streaked with mud, voice thick with the sort of bluntness that can anchor a panic.

Nita: "You want a sermon or a chain? People are scared. Kids are crying. You're either the one that leads or you stand there and become a headline, Mara."

You: "I don't want to be a headline." (The answer tastes like iron. You almost add the memory of your father's hands, but keep it contained—this is not the time for ghosts.)

Reverend Sol steps forward, cane tapping at the boards. His face is calm but lined in a way that speaks of long decisions made, long losses held.

Reverend Sol: "Whatever we do, we owe people the truth of our intent. Not all truth is mercy; not all concealment is kindness. Remember that."

You look at him and see the town history reflected in his eyes—funerals and barn-raisings braided together. It steadies you for a second and then the world jolts again.

A reporter pushes forward with a microphone, breath visible in the chill air.

Reporter: "Ms. Alvarez—will you comment on the failed trial? Is this a result of improper testing, or design flaws?"

You turn. The microphone feels obscene in your palm, a mirror held up not to what you did but to what you are accused of being. The cameras hum. The town waits.

Kai grips your sleeve, exasperated and trembling.

Kai: "Mara—if you talk now, you take responsibility publicly. If you don't, we look like we're hiding something. There is no good optics here."

You: "No. There's choice, Kai. And one of those choices is to be true." (You let that land like a small stone.) "But true doesn't mean reckless."

Dr. Leila arrives, breathless, sensor pack slung against her chest, glasses fogged.

Dr. Leila: "Data looks muddy, but the sensors—some recorded stresses beyond predicted thresholds. We can show where the failure points are if we recover the fragments." (She flicks through a tablet with wet fingers.) "If we salvage now, some of that data will be lost. If we wait for calmer water, there's a better chance to recover clean samples. But the press will have their narrative by then."

You: "So waiting buys data and costs trust. Salvaging risks life and maybe insurance claims. Hiding risks betrayal."

Dr. Leila's eyes hold yours. For a scientist, this entire scene is a calculus; for you, it's intimate—the calculus of neighbors' futures. You feel the pulse in your throat.

Juno appears beside Nita, younger and more brittle than the kids who used to jump between platforms. They look like someone who has learned to harden quickly—angry, scared, impatient.

Juno: "Don't you dare hide it, Mara. If we let them sweep this under, what do you think they'll sweep next? My school? Our block?"

You: "I hear you." (You mean it.) "I want to protect this place. I want to protect everyone."

Your thoughts tilt toward Kai as accusation becomes accusation-into-possibility. You worry about what this could do to the fragile thing between you—a shared dream of proving a different way. The very idea of losing him over this is a cold, immediate terror, and it sharpens your next words.

Kai: "If we—if you—lead a salvage, you put people at risk. If we broadcast, we own it and invite help but invite humiliation. If we conceal, someone will know someday, and the fallout will be worse."

You: "So which is it? Which of those deaths do you want to live with?"

Kai lashes out, but it's not a punch; it's a voice that lets the fear spill free.

Kai: "I don't want any of them. But I'm not willing to watch all of us be painted as fools so some board can pat itself on the back."

Your hands find each other—his callused fingers against the rope-scarred skin of your palm. For a breath, you imagine a quieter version of this: rebuilding, a small victory. Then a gust takes that image and rips it into the open.

Dr. Leila pulls you both aside in quick, scientific movements.

Dr. Leila: "There are three actions we can take, practically. I'm laying them out as uninterested as possible: A) Attempt an immediate salvage with trained volunteers and a quick call to the harbor crew—dangerous, but salvageable materials and quick recovery of data. B) Broadcast everything—hold a press-and-community meeting, show the failure, and invite volunteers and experts for an open repair. Short-term humiliation but long-term credibility if handled well. C) Patch it quietly—rebuild what we can without announcing full scope. Less immediate risk to people and to funding, but if exposed later, trust will be gone."

Your stomach flips. It's as if the world narrows to a cone, the apex in your chest.

Reverend Sol: "Be mindful of how a choice binds you to future choices. We do not cast ballots only once."

Nita: "We need to be honest with our neighbors. But also, we need hands ready for what comes after. I can marshal people if we salvage."

Juno: "If we conceal and it's exposed, I'm out. I can't stay in a place that lies."

Kai's brow furrows—he's struggling not to plead.

Kai: "Mara, whatever you pick—promise me you remember why we started. Promise me you won't—

You cut him off with a hard look, because the truth is a jagged thing.

You: "I remember. I remember the net my father built, the nights he hitched lines in a storm and came home with one less laugh. I'm the one who swore we would do better. I'm not going to make that promise lightly."

Your internal monologue is a knot of guilt and duty and the ache for fairness. The idea of people looking at you as the architect of failure is a cold, private agony; the idea of people thinking you lied to them is worse.

[Visual: The platform creaks as another wind gust hits. The dangling module swings more violently; a reporter shouts a question you can't hear.]
[Sound: The camera's whir increases; someone yells that a rope just snapped on the outer mooring.]

The crowd fractures into movement—some volunteers racing for supplies, others dragging children to higher boards. Your heart accelerates. The moment compresses—the emergency, the moral ledger, the fragile chemistry of trust and love.

You have to choose.
