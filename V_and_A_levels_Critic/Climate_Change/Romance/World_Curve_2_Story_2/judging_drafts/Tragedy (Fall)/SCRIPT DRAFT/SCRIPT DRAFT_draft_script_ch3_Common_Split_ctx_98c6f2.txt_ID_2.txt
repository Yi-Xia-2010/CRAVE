You arrive from the boardwalk with the pressure of the meeting in your chest, the compass's weight a quiet promise at your throat. The market smells like wet wood, fried fish, and a metallic tang of nerves—sweat and salt braided into the air. Voices collide like surf against pilings: neighbors, vendors, contractors, and a scattering of Blackbrand representatives hovering near the tarped display of modular seawall segments.

Nita is already in the center, hands flour-dusted and fierce, holding the bell as if it weighs the fate of the town. Her bandana is damp at the temple; her face is all blunt light.

Nita: "They want to sell safety like a thing you can bolt on. We know what happens when 'fast' is the only word on the contract. Mara—" (She looks at you, tired and bright) "—we need you."

Your pulse is an errand. You can feel every conversation thread tugging toward you: the pilot plan you sketched last week, the sensors Dr. Leila promised, the meetings you still haven't had time to schedule. You let your fingers curl around the edge of the crate beneath you, feeling grain and salt-crust under your nails. People aren't just asking for a plan; they're asking for leadership, a voice in the moment when decisions will be written in concrete or kelp.

Reverend Sol stands a few steps away, his cane carving small, patient circles on the platform. His face is a map of the town—lines you know by heart.

Reverend Solomon 'Sol' Hart: "Quiet now. Listen." (He lifts his weathered hand and the market hushes, the hush trailing like the shadow of a gull across the boards.) "Mara—" (He says your name the way a tide names a harbor) "You know these people. They need a path, not a speech."

You inhale. You want to promise everything: kelp lines, oyster beds, bylaws, the slow knitting of communities back into the shoreline. But promises are slippery when people are hungry for certainty and when the developer's chrome cranes are two streets away.

[Visual: Dr. Leila sets up a portable tablet; a projection flares—maps, data layers, a spike of predicted surge heights]
[Sound: The tablet's soft whine, a murmur of approval, a cough of disbelief]
[Music: Tense strings pick up, tempo quickens]

Dr. Leila Osei: "These models show that a living reef will reduce wave energy in specific conditions. Not everywhere. Not instantly." (She meets your eyes.) "We can make measurable impact with time and monitoring. But we need deployment and buy-in."

Kai stands beside you, satchel of cuttings at his feet. He leans in as if to shield you from the collective gaze and offers a small, wry smile that feels like both encouragement and a question.

Kai Navarro: "Talk numbers, Mara. Talk timeline. People here need to hear what we can do in ninety days and what risks exist." (He squeezes your shoulder briefly.) "We'll be honest. They'll trust that."

You want to trust the honesty in his squeeze. His optimism acts like sunlight—warm, sometimes blinding, always insisting you look up. Behind his smile something is taut: a fear you both share of promises that unravel.

Juno folds his arms on a stall edge, eyes raw, hoodie sleeves pushed up to show callused knuckles. He looks like the town's future waiting to be asked whether to stay.

Juno Alvarez: "If we vote to stall, if we don't block them—what if they start tomorrow? What if 'hybrid' is a word that keeps us talking while they pour concrete?"

Your throat tightens. You remember your father's boat listless in a harbor after the last big storm—the small ledger of what couldn't be saved. There is a cold clarity to those memories now: the scale of loss doesn't wait for good intentions.

The bell rings again. Someone shouts: "Evelyn's team is downstairs—she's here."

A ripple of reaction moves through the crowd—some sway toward anger, others toward bargaining. Footsteps on the ramp announce her: Evelyn Black arrives with the precision of a tide wall. Her coat is immaculate against the salt-warped fabrics; she does not look like she belongs to this market, but she speaks like someone used to rooms where outcomes are decided.

[Visual: Evelyn Black under the string lights, tablet in hand; a smoothed smile]
[Sound: A brief, polite clap from a man in a branded safety vest; a distant hum of a generator]
[Music: A cold piano motif enters beneath the strings, edgier]

Evelyn Black: "Good afternoon, everyone. I want to be clear—this is about protecting lives and homes. We can move fast, secure zones, and reduce risk immediately. The modular barrier is tested. The alternative—" (She makes a small motion toward Dr. Leila's projected map) "—is longer-term. Longer isn't always better when storms aren't patient."

The phrase lands like a weight. The room splits along it—some faces folding into relief at "fast" and "tested", others darkening at the thought of private control over a public shore.

You step forward because the room waits for you to step. Words collect like stormwater in the gutter of your chest. You form the first sentence with the care of someone steering a skiff through mangled pilings.

You: "We don't—" (Your voice is steadier than you feel) "—we don't have to let 'fast' be the only option on the table. People die when decisions are made without them."

Evelyn tilts her head, the smallest of acknowledgements, then smiles like a seamstress who knows how to finish a hem.

Evelyn Black: "Community involvement is essential. Which is why our team is offering an implementation schedule that includes community liaisons and expedited neighborhoods. We're proposing a hybrid pilot as a possibility—"

Nita scoffs, voice raw: "A liaison is not community governance. A liaison is a talking point."

The market erupts; plates of argument clang as loudly as metal. Voices overlap—a cacophony that lifts your heartbeat, makes your ears ring. Reverend Sol raises his hand again, his voice trying to cut through the tide.

Reverend Solomon 'Sol' Hart: "We will not be spoken for. We will not be bought with scaffolding and PR. But we also cannot be the ones to see our folks flooded because we bicker."

The contradiction hangs between two waves—the fear of being steamrolled and the fear of inaction. In your chest, negotiation and resistance both feel necessary and insufficient. Every path carries loss; every delay invites a storm.

Dr. Leila: "The models need funding and rapid deployment to be credible quickly. Our data can support staged deployment that demonstrates efficacy, but it can't be rushed without risk of failure and public trust erosion."

Kai: "Which is why we need transparency. Which is why we need to agree on the first ninety days with legal teeth. Not slogans—actual accountability."

Evelyn: "Legal frameworks can be agreed, with escrow and oversight. My firm's willing to underwrite part of the pilot for speed."

The words sound like the clink of coins. Some in the crowd nod at the promise of money and rapid work; others close their eyes at the idea of corporate underwriting a shore.

You feel the room pivot toward you. Faces—old Mrs. Hanley's, the kids from the docks, the foreman with a sunburned neck—search you for a direction that will hold. You think of your father's ledger again, the way bureaucracy had both saved and failed people in different storms. Choices split like salt crystals: immediate protection for some, long-term stewardship for all, or a demonstration that could swing opinion overnight.

Nita cuts close to you, whisper-urgent.

Nita: "If you choose to negotiate, make them sign stewardship and community governance clauses. If you choose to fight, we'll need banners, permits, legal help. If you choose the reef—" (She looks at you like she means it) "—if you go out alone, make sure Leila can back you with data."

You: "I know." (You say it like an armor and a confession.) "I know what each of those choices risks. I know what they could cost us." (Your voice drops.) "I know what it costs not to act."

Kai's hand finds yours for a fraction of a second. It steadies you and makes the pressure personal. "Whatever you decide," he murmurs, "I'll back you. Not because I agree with everything—" (he gives you a look that holds more than the words) "—but because I'm with you."

You can feel the fracture of intimacy here: his loyalty an ember you don't want to smother, his avoidance of confrontation a small relief, and a potential fault line if your choice demands sacrifice he can't stomach. The romance of the last quiet walk feels distant beneath the roar of the market.

Evelyn watches this exchange with an unreadable expression—neither hostile nor warm; merely calculating. Her team circulates contract pages like offerings.

Evelyn Black: "We can begin construction within six weeks. We can secure five blocks—" (she gestures at the map) "—now. We can phase and reassess. We can save lives."

You: "Save some lives by condemning others?" (Your words are sharp; the crowd inhales.) "I won't be complicit in a plan that chooses winners and losers based on property values."

Evelyn: "That's not the choice I'm offering, Mara. It's triage for the town. Difficult choices, made decisively to prevent greater harm."

There is a moral logic to her brutality. You recognize the truth in it: decisive action can spare death in the short term. But you also feel the hollow in that calculus—the things that don't measure well on spreadsheets: memories, neighborhoods, the stitched-together ways of living.

Reverend Sol looks at you as if for absolution, then shakes his head slightly, choosing instead to trust you. Around you, the urgency doubles. People are no longer politely waiting; they're demanding action now. Someone begins to chant. Others lift signs. A child starts to cry. The market’s rhythm stumbles into chaos—fear and resolve knotted tightly.

You sense the arousal curve tighten: tension snapping into urgent pressure, voices becoming edges. Time feels thin.

You weigh the three paths in the span of a breath:

- Organize a public resistance and demonstration—lean into solidarity, visibility, legal risk, possibility of wider mobilization.
- Negotiate a hybrid plan with binding community governance—risk co-option, but potential to secure formal safeguards.
- Stage a bold solo demonstration of a living reef offshore—a rapid, risky test that could sway hearts or shatter credibility.

Each choice carries consequences you cannot fully predict. Each carries a version of betrayal—or salvation—depending on how storms align and who holds the next contract.

Your fingers find the compass again, thumb circling its edge. You think of your father, of ledger pages soaked by salt and a sky like a slab of pewter. The market presses around you, hungry for direction—less for the architecture of policy than for someone to claim the risk.

Nita's voice is small but fierce at your ear. "Decide."
