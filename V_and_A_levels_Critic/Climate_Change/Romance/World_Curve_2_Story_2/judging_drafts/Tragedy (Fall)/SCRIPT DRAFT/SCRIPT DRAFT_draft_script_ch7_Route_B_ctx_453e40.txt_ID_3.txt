[Scene: Blackbrand Negotiation Room | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: A glassed conference room set into a concrete shell—the city framed like a diorama beyond. Light strips in the ceiling make everything too bright and too clean. A single living terrace model sits under a glass cloche at the table’s center.]
[Sound: The soft, clinical hum of air circulation; a distant construction clang like someone testing a jaw. Footsteps on polished concrete echo too plainly.]
[Music: Low, steady cello—minor key. Tension underwrites conversation.]

You step into the room with the others and feel the air change: cooler, filtered, a scent of lemon disinfectant pressed over the salt. Your jacket feels heavier than it should; the compass at your collarbone is a small, hot weight beneath cloth.

Evelyn is already standing by the window, tablet in hand. Her posture is the same as always—precise, practiced—like an instrument tuned to persuasion. When she turns, the bright lines of the room carve her in silhouette: polished, efficient. No one smiles until she does it for them: a quick, measured lift that belongs to people who practice reassurance.

Evelyn: "Mara. Thank you for coming with your delegation. I know these conversations are hard."

You keep your hands folded where you can see them. Dr. Leila’s sensors case sits like a calm island by her knee; Kai shifts his satchel, the cloth smelling faintly of rosemary and salt. Reverend Sol leans on his cane, eyes soft but alert. Nita's jaw is set; she wears the market's stubbornness like armor.

Evelyn slides a slim packet across the table—an executive summary first, then three printed pages that gleam in the overhead light.

Evelyn: (smooth) "We've drafted a hybrid proposal. Modular barrier segments—engineered, rapid-deploy—paired with integrated green terraces and community bays. Think of it as protection that also creates public space: food gardens, stepped terraces for marsh habitat, built-in access points for mariners and markets. We can start construction within six months if Marisport signs an implementation agreement."

Your stomach drops at the word agreement, heavy as wet sand. Six months. Fast is the thing that bends every ethical question toward survival. Fast is what Evelyn is selling, wrapped in the language of care.

Dr. Leila raises a hand, fingers tapping her tablet. Her voice is tempered by data but threaded with urgency.

Dr. Leila: "Technically, the integration of living terraces is feasible at scale—if we specify species, substrates, and long-term monitoring. But the timeline Evelyn mentions compresses adaptive testing. We risk ecological mismatch unless there's a staged pilot with real-time oversight."

Evelyn: (narrowing) "We have the capacity for staged pilots. The modular approach is designed to be iterative. The terraces are engineered to accept live planting after an initial stabilization window."

You watch Kai’s hand curl under the table. He’s always been generous with optimism, but you see the way his knuckles blanch. He meets your eyes briefly—a flicker that asks a question you can both feel but not say aloud.

Kai: (quiet) "If we can guarantee community-managed spaces—actual stewardship in the contract—that could be a model. It could scale. It could—" He stops, because enthusiasm has teeth here and teeth bite.

Reverend Sol: (slow) "Guarantees mean little without enforceable governance. Contracts can promise anything on page one and be re-interpreted on page seven. What protects the fisherman's landing? What protects the market stalls from expropriation by maintenance zones?"

Nita: (flat) "They promise gardens and 'community bays'—but who gets to run them? Who gets priced out when property value climbs because the wall made the shore 'safe'?"

The room tilts into a low, uneasy murmur. Evelyn sets her tablet down with a soft exhale of composure that feels like steel.

Evelyn: "Those are fair questions. That's why we're proposing binding governance clauses—an Implementation Agreement that enshrines community seats on oversight committees, a maintenance fund seeded by Blackbrand, and dispute-resolution protocols. We want partners, Mara, not landlords."

You hear the last word catch in the syllables of the room. "Partners." It sounds like a currency that may be converted later.

Your mind races through the possible costs: a clause that reads generous in legalese but wields technicalities as a lever; a maintenance fund that is contingent on signature milestones; community seats that become advisory figures, not decision-makers. Your father's compass feels suddenly like a relic you might have to trade.

Evelyn: (leaning forward) "Sign now, and construction commences. Delay, and the permit window narrows—our legal counsel says certain zoning concessions are time-sensitive. We can offer an escalator clause for local labor and explicit protections against eminent-domain-style displacement, but only within a finalized Implementation Agreement."

Dr. Leila: "Language matters—metrics for success, independent audits, adaptive thresholds. We can write those, but we need the clause that allows us to pause if ecological parameters fail."

Evelyn: (precise) "We agree to pause clauses in principle. We can also include third-party arbitration. The problem, Mara, is that delay costs lives when the storms come. You have advocates here who want perfect solutions; I’m offering something that works at scale."

You want to say that your father died because of speedless bureaucracy, that he went out on a day when the town thought it could wait. But you don't say it. Instead, your voice comes out measured, because measured is often the only currency in negotiation.

Mara: "You say 'partners,' Evelyn. I need to know who's writing the binding clauses you describe. Who holds the arbitration power? Who appoints the oversight committee members? Words like 'pause' and 'escalator' are easy to promise and hard to enforce."

Evelyn: (calm) "We can draft the committee charter jointly—equal seats for the town, Blackbrand, and an independent technical body. Arbitration can be trilateral with a neutral chair."

Kai: (insistent) "And the maintenance fund? Upfront versus escrow matters. If Blackbrand controls release, the town could be left waiting on funds while sites degrade."

Evelyn: "Escrow with milestones. Transparent reporting. We’re open to third-party escrow management."

Nita: (brittle) "Open to. That always sounds so reassuring."

You feel a tightening behind your ribs. The room smells suddenly like paper and recycled coffee, and you are aware of a small scuff on the table where your palm rests—a white crescent scraped into the laminate, like a small scar.

This is where the negotiation sharpens: language, process, timelines. Each of you is mapping what 'community control' would legally look like and seeing different borders.

The reactions are small: a pause, a nod, the faint tightening of Evelyn's mouth. Nothing is decided, but the air has thickened with consequence. You notice Reverend Sol watching your face as if he’s reading weather.

Reverend Sol: (soft) "Mara, remember the people who will be asked to move or change everything because of this. We need guarantees they remain in place, not tokens to be reinterpreted."

You see the hospital in his eyes: decades of steeled patience, parishioners lost to progress and policy. Guilt—a familiar companion—presses at your sternum. You think of Juno—how the kid’s laughter has changed into a planning score on a notebook—and the thought tastes like ash.

Evelyn: ( suddenly direct) "None of us want displacement, Reverend. That's not our interest. But there are engineering realities. Certain low-lying blocks cannot be protected without significant elevation works—we can build terraces and ramps, but some parcels may require managed retreat. The Implementation Agreement will include relocation assistance and property protection clauses."

The phrasing is clinical; 'managed retreat' reduces houses to coordinates you move around on a map. You imagine the Floodplain Market, Nita's stall—who decides the market's new site? The word 'assistance' is a soft bandage over a deep cut.

Nita: (angry now) "Assistance doesn't keep my stall. Assistance doesn't keep my customers. It makes it easier for people with more money to buy in, to make the waterfront 'pretty' and then kick the old timers out."

Kai reaches across and lays his hand over yours beneath the table without talking. Simple, solid. He doesn't try to fix the clauses or the legalese; he just keeps you anchored with his palm. The touch is a small rebellion against the sterile edges of the room.

Kai: (low) "We have to be careful about what 'relocation assistance' looks like. It needs tenure guarantees. Right-to-return clauses. Affordability covenants tied to the project."

Evelyn: (a brief, guarded smile) "Affordability covenants are negotiable. We can set price controls on certain parcels for a defined period."

Your heart ticks faster—not in panic, but in the tightening cadence of options that all hurt in different ways. The arousal climbs steadily now—questions multiply in the room like low tide ripples.

Dr. Leila: "We need explicit language: metrics for ecological success, triggers for pause, named independent auditors, an escrow structure governed by a regional trust, tenure guarantees codified for twenty-five years, and defined roles for community stewardship—not tokenized seats."

Evelyn: (counting off) "Those are significant asks. They will alter cost and timeline. They could change the feasibility of rolling the project out quickly."

Reverend Sol: (firm) "Feasibility for whom? You speak of cost in a way that suggests people are expenses on a ledger."

Evelyn: (cool) "I understand your distrust. But I also understand the mathematics of risk and the cost of delay. My offer attempts to bridge urgency with stewardship. We can draft operational safeguards—an oversight charter, community veto power on certain actions, mandatory transparency portals."

You feel both the relief and the sting of those words. 'Veto power' gleams like a possible anchor, but you remember clauses that allow exceptions for 'national security' or 'public safety'. The exceptions are thin doors through which everything can pass.

Mara: (you speak, voice steadier than you feel) "Write the clauses transparently. Name the arbitrator. Put escrow with an independent trust and a minimum disbursement schedule. Guarantee tenure and right-to-return for market vendors. Spell out what 'pause' means; name the metrics. If you want our signature, those elements are non-negotiable on principle."

Evelyn studies you. For a heartbeat she looks almost unreadable—then, as if flipping a switch, she nods.

Evelyn: "I can take those to legal. We’ll draft them. I can't promise they'll look identical to your drafts, but I can commit that they’ll be considered and, if necessary, reviewed by a neutral panel."

A small, dangerous relief spreads through you. But it is immediately leavened by Evelyn's next sentence, precise as a scalpel.

Evelyn: "One condition: Blackbrand requires a phased sign-off schedule. Initial signature initiates procurement and permits. Final approvals—especially for the terraces—will depend on agreed milestones. Our lawyers insist on some latitude to manage logistics."

The latitude she describes is precisely where ambiguity lives. It could be a corridor for negotiation or a door that closes on local control.

Silence falls—heavy and clinical—while everyone processes the contour of a deal that could protect lives and, at the same time, rearrange them. You imagine the town map again: the Floodplain Market, the porch where your father sat, the place where someone might be told to move.

You want to push harder. You want to demand the words that make promises ironclad. You also know that negotiation is a slow, incremental thing—words amended over time can change outcomes. The mid-arousal tension sharpens into a decision edge: trust a polished offer with teeth, or demand more and risk losing the window of rapid deployment.

Dr. Leila's finger taps a rhythm on her tablet like a metronome. Nita’s hands flex; Reverend Sol breathes like someone counting prayers.

You look at the packet on the table—a glossy cover, a corporate seal—and you feel the valley between urgency and justice stretch under your feet.

You find yourself holding Kai's gaze. He doesn't say anything, but his face is a question and a plea folded into one.

You could sign the implementation agreement as Evelyn outlines and push the governance language hard in the drafting phase—push until it becomes real and enforceable. Or you could refuse to move, insist on more immediate legal guarantees before any signature, and risk the company walking away or the permit window closing.

You remember the generator thump from the market meeting—the feeling of a crowd waiting for you to pick a direction. You remember the compass, the sound of the sea against the promenade, your father's hands. The room waits.

[Visual: The camera pulls back; the city outside keeps going, indifferent]
[Music: Low chord stretches, unresolved]

You open your mouth to speak again, to tilt the negotiation toward the town's teeth. The word that comes out is small but loaded.

Mara: "We need the draft. We need time to consult—fully, with the market vendors, the elders, Juno's teachers. We need legal counsel that represents the town, not just our petitions. If you'll give us that with a binding timeline for when procurement and permits are triggered, we can consider a preliminary signature to hold the window."

Evelyn: (after a measured beat) "A preliminary signature with conditions. I can authorize that. We'll put a clause that the preliminary signature does not obligate any action until the agreed governance charters are executed."

Nita: (bitter laugh) "Preliminary signature sounds a lot like 'trust us.'"

You feel the reaction in your throat: protectiveness, fear, the itch of inevitability. The group is at a hinge. The negotiations have not collapsed, but they have not resolved. The arousal level steadies into a painful tension; your decision now will not be a clean solution but a selection among necessary compromises.

You think of the next steps—the lawyers, the town meetings, the edits that will take on a life of their own as language is parsed and money is counted. You think of Juno and the market and the ferry hull greenhouse where seedlings wait for hands. The weight of possibility presses into your ribs.

You could feel hope about what's on the table if the clauses become iron. You could feel dread that 'partners' will become administrators. Negative emotions—anxiety, suspicion, weary resignation—tumble together into a single knot.

You swallow. The decision is not fully formed here, but its outline is clear enough: the town will have to accept a staged, legalized compromise or risk losing speed. Either path demands you carry consequences.

You look down at the documents, at the line where a signature might go, and you picture the ink soaking into paper like tide into sand—impossible to completely wash away.
