Nita Ramirez: "There you are. Thought you'd turned yourself into the tide and saved us the trouble."

You lift your head. Nita stands by her stall—an island of crates and tarps—bandana tight, eyes like flint. She has always been loud with a grief that refuses to be polite; today the grief is a blade.

Your chest tightens. You can hear the market waking behind her: a boy dropping a metal lid, someone else's radio scratching with a morning show, footsteps. The town collects and leaks information like tidewater through a net.

Mara Linh Alvarez: "I made a call. I tried to keep salvage crews from endangering themselves—"

Nita Ramirez: "You tried to cover it up, Mara. You didn't call for help until the cameras were already biting. You told people to tow lines in secret while a lawyer danced with Blackbrand's counsel." Her voice breaks thin. "Juno left because of the shouting. He said he couldn't live where people lied for a living."

The word—Juno—knifes through you. Heat climbs your chest. You have rehearsed explanations in the dark: fear, the legal threat, the shards of the prototype caught like teeth in the water. None of them make the hole in Juno's face smaller.

Mara Linh Alvarez: "He left. He made his own choice."

Nita Ramirez [laughing, not kindly]: "Made his own choice because of you. You could have told him the truth. You could have let us help. Instead you hid it, like it's something you can carry alone."

The wind catches the edge of your jacket and the compass, and for a moment you feel every footstep that led you here—your father's hands on a net, the way he taught you to read a current. The compass has been your anchor since he didn't come back. You think of the dent where you once gripped it in a storm and how now that dent holds guilt.

Reverend Sol approaches, cane tapping the boards slow and steady. His coat is damp at the hem; his eyes are soft but exhausted.

Reverend Sol [gentle, but tired]: "Nita, enough. Mara—" He stops and studies you. "We all have things we thought would save us. Some of them don't."

You want him to say more, to lay the blame at his own feet for letting the town fracture; instead he searches your face for something he can't quite find.

Mara Linh Alvarez: "Sol—what did you tell them? At the hearing?"

Reverend Sol [quiet]: "I told the truth I could stand in. I told them that the town is frayed and that we need to stitch, not tear." He looks away, at the seawall segments like a row of teeth. "But the law doesn't care for stitching. We've signed things out of desperation before, Mara. People will sign anything that promises to stop water from taking their roofs."

The words land like slats snapping underfoot. You're not surprised—only hollowed. The town did what it needed to survive, and surviving meant promises that silenced other voices. You picture Blackbrand's neat men in their spotless boots, signing orders and moving concrete like a slow tide.

A young volunteer from the rooftop orchards—Elise, face blotched from windburn—approaches, phone in hand, eyes wide.

Elise: "There's a clip going around. Someone recorded the salvage crews—saying you ordered them to strip the kelp lines. People are saying you lied to keep the pilot from being headline fodder."

Your breath comes tight. The market's murmurs sharpen into accusation. You feel the crowd tilt toward you as if gravity had decided you were its fulcrum.

The conversation splinters. Voices pile on one another—Nita's anger, Sol's measured grief, Elise's frightened urgency. You are in the eye of a storm that is not weather but people.
