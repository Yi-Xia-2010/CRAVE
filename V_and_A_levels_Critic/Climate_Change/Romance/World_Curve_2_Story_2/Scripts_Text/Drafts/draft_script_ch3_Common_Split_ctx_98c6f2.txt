# Route: Additional Route (Ending)


[Scene: Floodplain Market | Late Afternoon — High Tide Approaching]
[Visual: Raised wooden platforms crowded with canvas stalls; tarps snap in a steady salt wind. The sun is low, gilding wet crates and the undersides of stilts with a bruised orange. People cluster in tight, anxious knots.]
[Sound: A constant undertow of voices—overlapping, frayed—underscored by gull cries and the distant thrum of a generator. Somewhere, a child laughs and then is shushed.]
[Music: Tense BGM — low strings and a driving percussion loop; tempo steadily increasing]

You step off the promenade and onto the market planks with your sketchbook heavy at your hip and the compass warm against your sternum. The smell hits you first—frying oil, damp kelp, hot metal from nearby gear, and the unmistakable brine that tastes like history and threat at once.

The square is packed more than usual. Stalls are shuttered; crates form a ragged ring around a makeshift stage of stacked pallets. Reverend Sol stands at the center like an old buoy: steady, carved cane in hand, his voice trying to hold a wavering sea of anger and fear. Nita is two stalls over, apron gone—her hands on her hips, eyes hard as ballast. Juno stands by her, arms folded, chin set; the kid keeps glancing at you as if trying to measure whether safety still exists within your shoulders.

You feel each face tilt toward you as you move through them. Not because you asked them to: because the town wants something from you now—direction, a plan, a name for the fear.

Reverend Sol: "Mara—" (his voice is softer than the crowd; you feel it as if he’s speaking into a fog) "—we can't let this drift. You understand the soil and the sea. Speak plainly."

Your mouth is dry. You imagine the compass pressed into your palm like the lid of a sealed promise. A hundred different outcomes crowd the hollows of your chest: compromises that saved pockets and lost neighborhoods; quick fixes that smoothed paperwork but hollowed out trust. Each possibility hums with consequence.

Nita intercepts you before you reach the pallets. Her face is a map of certainty and exhaustion.

Nita: "You heard Evelyn's plan. Concrete segments, cranes, lights. They call it progress. It will bury half of the eelgrass beds and put a toll on the fishers. Are you going to stand for that, or are we going to stand for ourselves?"

You open your mouth. You want to list the living-shoreline pilot—kelp lines, oyster clusters, porous berms. You want to cite Dr. Leila's tentative models and the idea of scaling slowly. But the crowd is a clock counting down to a public hearing, and Evelyn's company is filing permits while you two argue semantics.

You climb onto the pallets. The boards creak under your weight in the way of old wood admitting new load. Kai is beside you before you can steady your thoughts—his satchel damp with plant cuttings, a smear of mud on his sleeve. He doesn't fill silence with certainty; he offers a look you read as an anchor.

Kai Navarro: "Whatever you choose, we'll run it like the ninety-day plan—transparent, documented. I'll pull letters from the old networks; Dr. Leila's team can fast-track sensor deployment. We don't have to reinvent the wheel; we have to make our spokes visible."

You catch the tremor in his words—he is trying to make action sound practical because fear otherwise might make it sound impossible. You want to trust the gentleness in him; you want it to be enough. But you also know his tendency to avoid hard public fights, to prefer tinkering at low tide to standing in a crowd screaming. You feel protective and frustrated in the same breath.

Dr. Leila steps forward with a tablet slightly fogged at the edges. She clears her throat and the crowd yields a grudging space for science.

Dr. Leila: "The models show localized resilience from living reefs if we establish them now. They reduce erosion rates, restore nursery habitat. But—" (she taps the screen; the numbers feel smaller than the room) "—they require time, community labor, and legal protections. There’s uncertainty. I won't sugarcoat it."

Her candor is a small relief. It keeps the argument honest in the one place you trust facts to matter. The crowd murmurs. Some faces relax. Others harden, thinking of roofs and insurance notices.

You sense the market's atmosphere tilt—like a mast catching a stronger wind—before she speaks.

[Sound: A low mechanical hum rises; footsteps in wellies crunch on gravel as security personnel form a neat line at the edge of the square.]
[Visual: Evelyn Black arrives with two suited aides. Her trenchcoat absorbs the light like a void; she steps under the makeshift awning as if into a boardroom. A polished tablet is tucked against her hip; she is precise, composed—a blade in silk.]

Evelyn Black: "Thank you for convening," she says. Her voice is measured, perfectly modulated to cut through the roar. "Marisport cannot afford experiments while the water rises. Our modular seawall offers immediate protection, job contracts, and a phased integration of ecological elements where feasible."

There is an applause of security: papers rustle, a few marketplace folks clap reflexively at 'jobs'. You notice the way her words land—offering safety as a bargain, with the mercantile jingles that make the idea palatable.

Reverend Sol's hand rises, as though to steady the air.

Reverend Sol: "Evelyn, we've heard your pitch. Some here don't trust the developer's silence about the displacement risks. Where's the accountability? Who sits on the oversight—"

Evelyn interrupts gently, almost apologetically.

Evelyn Black: "Oversight is built into the project. We're entering into municipal contracts with transparent timelines. Delays cost lives."

Her assertion feels like the snap of a lifeline offered from a crane over a storm drain. It is also a yoke. The crowd shifts—some faces tighten with relief; others flare with suspicion. You notice Juno's fingers drum a nervous rhythm on a crate. The kid is young enough to disdain diplomacy and old enough to have watched neighbors leave.

Nita, voice rising, accuses: "Their 'transparency' is balance sheets and non-disclosure. We know how that ends."

The market tilts into argument, voices colliding faster, shards of accusation and promise. Your head pulses with the pressure of being the one everyone thinks will point the way. You are suddenly aware of the lineage of that expectation—of your father's absence and the unspoken calculus that made you return. Guilt tightens your throat because every choice you imagine casts shadows over someone else.

Kai squeezes your hand under the pallets. He whispers too close for anyone else to hear.

Kai Navarro: (whisper) "Don't let them box you into a binary. We can—" (he cuts himself off, as if betting on your ability to finish the sentence)

You want to trust that sentiment—three paths fanning from a single point. But Evelyn's men in the periphery, the municipal notices already in circulation, the hush when contractors spoke—these are not illusions. They are a machine that can roll regardless of how loud you shout.

A woman from the crowd shouts over the clamor: "Choose, Mara! We need action!"

Her voice cracks open the time you have been holding—every plan that takes months feels like a betrayal to someone watching a tide line climb a porch step. The market closes around you like a fist.

Reverend Sol steps forward and looks at you with a sorrowful patience, as if he knows the weight you carry is built of smaller, older sorrows.

Reverend Sol: "You have lived with this shoreline; you know the stories, Mara. Now the town asks you to lead. Speak plainly. Will you fight, negotiate, or prove another way by action?"

Your breath is a thin thread. In the crowd you see faces you've known since childhood—people who mended each other's roofs after storms, people who lent boats and listened at late-night kitchen tables. Juno's jaw is mottled with a blush of anger and fear. Nita's knuckles are white. Dr. Leila's lips are pressed together like a lab spill waiting to happen.

Evelyn watches you with an unreadable tilt—her expression calibrated to be taken seriously. You cannot tell whether there is contempt behind her civility or a contained impatience. It reads, in the only safe way you can parse it now, as complex.

You think of the ninety-day plan Kai mentioned—transparent, legally bound, with volunteers and sensors and a clutch of neighborhoods stewarding pilot reefs. You think of the immediate protection Evelyn promises: cranes, concrete, sealed contracts. You also imagine doing something impossible in an afternoon—a quick demonstration of a living reef that proves, in living color, that the sea and community can be aligned without sacrifice. It would be risky, maybe catastrophic, but it might also be the kind of stubborn proof that shifts a chair under a table of boardroom calculations.

Your internal argument shouts louder than any voice around you: protect people now or risk everything for hope; build safeguards into a system that will likely try to swallow them; or show a miracle in the water and pray the water doesn't wash it away.

Kai's voice rises then lowers, a private steadiness in the public storm.

Kai Navarro: "If you pick resistance, I'll stand with you in the front lines. If you pick negotiation, I can help draft clauses and push for community seats on oversight boards. If you pick a solo demonstration, I'll help with equipment and logistics—but Mara, if you do that, don't try to do it entirely alone."

His plea is practical, threaded with fear. He knows you and your tendency to shoulder burdens. He also knows the spectacle you could make if you let him—how your hands shape an argument into something visible and undeniable.

Evelyn slides forward a slim, glossy pamphlet—renderings of a modular wall, civic plazas crowning barrier tops, smiling families staged in promotional art.

Evelyn Black: "We can negotiate. We can form governance. But time is a contract measure. Choose a path so we can begin legally."

Her words are both an invitation and a constraint. The crowd's murmurs sharpen into a single, urgent demand. The market watches you like the tide watches an inlet: inevitable movement pressed into a narrow time.

Your chest feels raw, as if the compass inside you is being rubbed by the edges of too many competing promises. Each possible route feels like a small betrayal—of values, of people, of memory. You picture the town map in your mind, parcels outlined in the brittle script of risk and legacy. Choices made here will re-shape those parcels, rearrange futures.

You inhale—salt and diesel and the faint smell of Kai's herbal cloth. The wind catches a faded banner and snaps it, a sharp sound that seems to mark an end to deliberation.

Every eye in the market is on you. The room has narrowed to one syllable: decide.

[Sound: A collective intake of breath; strings swell to a fevered pitch; the generator thumps louder, as if counting down]

You feel the old fear—of making the wrong compromise, of becoming the person who sells out a neighbor to save a block. It is heavy and familiar. You also feel a raw, high-velocity energy—anger, urgency, the kind of pressure that either forges steel or snaps it clean.

You step forward to the pallet edge. Words gather like tidewater.

You know what you must do next. The town has asked for leadership. You will answer.
