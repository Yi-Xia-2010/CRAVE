## World View
Verdante is a mid-sized coastal city in the late 2030s, caught between memory and engineering. Seasonal King Tides have begun to rework daily rhythms; neighborhoods along the harbor—like Mariner’s Row—still carry the weathered wood and salt-streaked paint of earlier generations while glass towers and municipal screens proclaim modern resilience. Power in Verdante is shared uneasily between civic institutions that promise broad protection and private interests that sell decisive fixes. The Vale Consortium, led by the silver-black-haired Cassian Vale, peddles rapid, visible defenses: towering seawalls and redevelopment plans that secure high-value districts while displacing long-time residents. City Hall’s plaza glows with cyan renderings; the rooftop Skyfield Conservatory and Beacon Station supply data to feed public debate; the Tidal Market reminds everyone of daily livelihoods.

Culture is local, pragmatic, and sentimental—hand-lettered signs, braided hair, coral scarves, and patched expedition jackets exist alongside municipal vests and glossy proposals. Religion and formal ritual are less prominent than place-based reverence: the city mourns what it loses to the sea and argues over what to build back. Class lines follow the waterfront’s subtle slopes: a community-led Mariner’s Row versus the Vale Consortium’s polished corridors. Conflicts are civic and intimate—public hearings double as battlegrounds and romantic stages. The atmosphere is soft realism: painterly light, seafoam and slate palettes, mixed-media overlays of maps and data bleeding into watercolor backdrops as citizens choose between expediency and equity.

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
I (Mira Santos) stand between a community I love and two competing visions of adaptation. Elias Moreno, my love and an idealistic urban planner, brings municipal-scale solutions that promise safety but risk erasing neighborhood life; Cassian Vale offers a polished, rapid redevelopment that will protect wealthier blocks at the cost of displacing my neighbors. As King Tides grow bolder, my choices escalate: organize a grassroots living-shoreline plan, compromise and co-design with Elias, or follow Tamiko and Lina’s more confrontational exposes. Each route begins with hope and pragmatic energy but slides toward tragedy—failures, betrayals, and irreversible losses pile up. The narrative arc is a Fall: every decision introduces new failures or missteps that deepen the breach between personal desire and civic duty. Branching decisions lead to multiple endings—some personally devastating, others bittersweet or structurally transformative—but across every path the city faces bitter choices and I must confront what I am willing to lose for the people I love and the place I call home.
