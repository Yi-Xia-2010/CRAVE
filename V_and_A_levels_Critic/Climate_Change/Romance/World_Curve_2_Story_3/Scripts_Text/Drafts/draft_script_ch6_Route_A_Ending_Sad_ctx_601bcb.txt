### DRAFT
# Route: Route A — Ending (Sad)


[Scene: Mariner's Row | Dawn]
[Visual: Wet cobblestones and the first sickle of light cutting across boarded windows; a banner hangs in tatters where a storefront used to be]
[Sound: Distant jackhammers, the low thrum of generators; gulls argue over open water]
[Music: Percussive strings build, a rapid, anxious motif]

You wake to the sound of construction like a second tide. It moves with the same inevitability—a roar building at the edges of your hearing until it occupies everything.

You remember Dr. Arun's hand on your shoulder like a benediction or a warning. "Be careful about what you promise people tonight." His voice is a fossil in your memory; you replay it and the feeling it left—the weight of responsibility, the taste of iron on the tongue—until you can see the shape of the day before it arrives.

The street smells of diesel and wet wood. Sandbags are gone. In their place, a gleam of new concrete, a half-built promenade with temporary fencing and Vale flags snapping in the wind. Cameras perch on cranes like indifferent birds. Men in consortium jackets ferry tablets and clipboards down the row; they move precisely, as if precise motion erases history.

You move through the crowd. Faces tilt toward you—some with resignation, some with accusation, some with a hunger for explanation you cannot feed. Lina meets your eyes first; her braid is matted with salt, her palms stained with paint. Her storefront—where her mother sold pickled fish and small curios—has a Vale placard on the door and a contractor's notice tacked over the window.

Lina: "They offered us a relocation package." (Her laugh is too quick, brittle.) "They said we'd be safer. Same shop, new block. Who wants a 'same shop' when the shore is your wall?"

You step closer, the railing biting your palm through the fabric of your gloves. Words thrash in your throat. They want a single name to blame; you are painfully aware of the ledger of decisions and the way every line favors some lives over others.

[Visual: Close-up on your coral scarf; the fibers whisper with overheard promises]
[Music: The strings shift to a harsher minor interval]

Tamiko stands on a crate across the alley, live feed dangling from her phone like a flayed flag. Her face is drained but set. She shows you the feed: Tamiko's exposés—once incendiary—now run as background footage for human-interest pieces. The headlines have already been folded into the consortium's PR: "Rapid Response Saves Thousands." The nuance is gone, sanded away under the weight of tidy statistics and glossy renderings.

Tamiko: "They spin it faster than we can breathe."

You feel the air tighten. It is hotter now—pressure building—and you realize the intensity that has been coiling since the hearing is not easing; it's swelling into something nearer to violence.

[Scene: New Promenade | Midday]
[Visual: A sweep of polished concrete, brass plaques embedded with dedications, and a low, glinting seawall that slices the horizon; Cassian's consortium logo is inlaid in the handrail]
[Sound: Waves hitting a labored rhythm against engineered pavers; distant PA announcements]
[Music: A crescendo—brass and percussion—then a sudden drop into a pounding, syncopated heartbeat]

You stand at the edge of the promenade and feel like an interloper in a photograph of your own loss. The seawall is efficient and terrible. Engineers would admire the clean line; financiers would call it a monument. It has been named and ribbon-cut; a plaque lists donors and timelines. People press their faces to the fence like supplicants, and you see, reflected in the glass of the protective barrier, the outline of a place that no longer belongs to the people who raised it.

Cassian's monument gleams. He stands with his umbrella and the careful, unruffled smile of a man who has never had to choose between preserving a life and preserving profit. He spoke at the opening as if he had saved the city single-handedly. The speakers applauded. Cameras loved the spectacle.

Cassian Vale: "Emergency required emergency measures. We acted so that life could continue. This is resilience."

You want to throw something—words, an accusation, the notebook you've kept like a reliquary of promises. Instead, you walk the promenade and watch contractors hand envelopes to families, sign documents, stamp futures into boxes. The envelopes are neat. The signatures are brittle.

Lina pushes past you, the sound of her boots against the concrete loud in your ears. She looks smaller in this new light, compressed between the tower shadows and the seawall that dwarfs her neighborhood's history.

Lina: "They gave us numbers and bus vouchers. They gave the school district dates. They gave my mother a new lease somewhere else. They gave us a paper trail and called it safety."

You reach for her hand. She does not take it. There is a space between you now where insistence used to be. You taste iron.

[Visual: A contractor closes Lina's old shop door for the last time, the shutters sliding down like a curtain]
[Sound: The mechanical thunk of metal; a child's laughter muffled across the barrier]
[Music: A single violin line wails, then is cut off]

You see Tamiko filming, her shoulders shaking in a way that is unmistakably grief. Her exposés—once the prismatic light cutting through polished lies—now run as elegies: footage of families packing, elderly residents counting what matters, children leaving the tide-streaked murals behind. The narrative the consortium pitched as 'rapid salvation' reads in the raw footage as removal.

Tamiko: (voice tight) "I wanted them to see the ledger—but the ledger's been edited. The contracts move faster than oversight."

Her camera captures a man you both know—an elderly neighbor blinking as a bus idles under the Vale flag. He looks at you both as if searching for what is left. The shot lingers; the world feels tilted toward a new axis where empathy is performative and displacement is efficient.

[Scene: Promenade Overlook | Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The light goes merciless; long shadows from the seawall slice across the salvaged brick of Mariner's Row; seagulls wheel in slow, sarcastic circles]
[Sound: The murmur of speeches wraps around you like a vise; microphones hiss]
[Music: Percussion accelerates—heartbeat matches the building tension, then a choir of low strings]

He finds you before you find him. Elias stands by the inlaid plaque, hands balled inside his coat. He looks smaller here—out of the municipal office, out of the hearing rooms, lodged in a space between accusation and consequence.

Elias Moreno: (softly) "Mira."

You have a hundred responses folded like knives inside your chest. Fury blooms first—hot and immediate. It presses against your tongue. Then comes a colder thing: the ache of betrayal. Your relationship was a bridge you built with the same hands that tried to shore up this place. Bridges can be crossed or burned. In the time since the hearing, you have been both.

You: "This is what you call a compromise?"

Elias swallows. He looks like someone cataloguing injuries. You watch his hands affect the air, as if making lists could ground him.

Elias Moreno: "We were boxed in. Municipal timelines couldn't guarantee a quick response. Cassian had capital and a plan. I thought—" (his voice fractures) "I thought if I could ride this in, steer oversight, make the contracts contain community clauses, I could mitigate the worst."

You: "You thought you'd 'steer' them? You put your name next to contracts that erase people."

Elias: (defensive) "I didn't want it to go like this. But sitting on the sidelines—watching families get flooded—felt criminal. I took the position to ensure we had a seat at the table."

Your laugh is a dry, broken sound that tastes like tidewater.

You: "A seat at the table that writes the bill. Elias, oversight isn't the same as consent. You were on the inside when they circled the ledger. You had chances to fight the clauses before the seals were wet."

Elias's jaw tightens. He searches you—hungry for absolution, for a map back to the place you once were together.

Elias Moreno: "I tried to push for guarantees. I pushed for tenure protections. They said the timeline wouldn't allow for a full ethical review. They said—" (he stops, breath ragged) "They promised 'phased rehousing.' I thought I could make it real."

You: "Promises written by teams paid to keep the project moving. You should have known how they call urgency a virtue to override vigilance."

Elias takes a step toward you. For a moment, you allow yourself to remember the small, unremarkable gentleness that first drew you to him—how he would tuck a stray hair behind your ear and laugh at your stubbornness. The memory twists into a blade.

Elias Moreno: "Do you think I wanted this? Do you think I wanted to watch people shove their lives into boxes? I—"

You: (interrupting, voice high) "Then don't stand behind the monument, Elias. Don't sign away people's names and call it protection. If you stood where you said you stood, you wouldn't be inside their framed photos."

He flinches as if struck.

Elias Moreno: (quiet, breaking) "I took the oversight role. I believed I could limit harm. If I walk away now, there will be no one on the inside willing to argue for the community. Is abandoning them better?"

The world tightens into a point. Around you, the promenade hums with people who were given envelopes and a glossy plan. Beneath everything, you feel the beat of loss like a second heart.

You: "You made a choice. It's not about whether you were inside or outside; it's about what you did while you were there."

Elias: "I still care—"

You: "Do you? Or do you care more about the problem you can solve at scale than the people you can see now?"

There is no rapier-thrust victory here. Only the slow, grinding erosion of trust. Elias's face crumples in a way that makes your chest revolt with grief. He reaches for you, then drops his hand, as if your barrier is something he can't cross anymore.

Elias Moreno: (hoarse) "I thought there was another path."

You: "You chose this path while telling yourself it was the only one."

He takes in a breath as if to argue, to beg, to explain—some mechanism to reassemble what has been splintered—but you know the ledger of public moments and private promises cannot be fully recomposed.

[Music: The strings break into a single, raw chord; all peripheral sound blurs into the pounding of blood in your ears]

Cassian appears at the edge of the plaque-cut crowd like a sundering agent. He moves with the same polished composure he had in the hearings. He smiles at both of you, a smile that doesn't reach his eyes. His presence is a mechanical punctuation to the day.

Cassian Vale: (smooth) "Elias, congratulations on the oversight appointment. Mira—this development protects the city."

You: (voice low, reined in) "Protects who?"

Cassian's answer is a practiced line.

Cassian Vale: "The many, with unfortunate cost; the alternative is chaos. We did what was necessary."

You feel rage like heat. It is not rhetorical. It is animal. You step forward until the Vale logo fills your vision.

You: "You catalog human lives as externalities. You call inevitability compassion."

A cluster of consortium aides shift, uncomfortable. Cameras angle. The crowd murmurs.

Elias stands rigid. There is a fracture line between his eyes—a hard, small thing that glitters like a splinter. For a heartbeat, he looks at you not as an adversary but as a place he once knew. The look cuts deeper than any spoken accusation.

Elias Moreno: (barely audible) "I'm sorry."

You: (the word falls away) "Sorry doesn't unseal a promenade."

Your throat is thick. The scarf at your neck feels suddenly absurdly small, the coral color swallowed by the state's gray. You think of the nights spent mapping tide lines on paper tablecloths, the people who stapled sandbags into place with shaking hands, the neighbor who gave you her last blanket after the flood two years ago. Those memories are not ledger entries. They are not negotiable.

[Scene: Mariner's Row — Edge of Night]
[Visual: Cardboard boxes stacked on stoops; a single lantern sways in a window; the seawall lights cast a sterile glow over a barricaded street]
[Sound: A distant bus motor; a child's muffled sob; the creak of packing tape]
[Music: A slow, aching cello; a low wind that carries the taste of metal]

You walk back down the narrow lane, the concrete cutting you off from the sea you love and fear. The neighborhood exists now as segmented parcels—some houses boarded, some marked 'Redeveloped,' some with new tenants whose faces you don't recognize. The social fabric has been cut into tidy parcels and stitched back with corporate thread.

At your doorstep, your notebook waits. Its cover is softened by rain and hands. Inside are maps and names—lines you drew to keep people anchored. You open it; the pages resist like old wounds.

You sit on the step and begin to pack. Each entry you fold into the notebook is a chunk of a life you catalogued, fights you argued, promises you coaxed into legal language. The pen you've used for years leaves a smear. The pages smell faintly of salt and ink and the particular dust of places people called home.

Lina passes by and halts. She watches you a moment, then offers a look that holds everything: gratitude, accusation, grief, and an ember of stubbornness.

Lina: "Don't go too far, Mira. Even if the walls are new, this is still our street."

You: (voice small) "I'll keep watching."

She nods once, closes her eyes, and moves on. The sound of her footsteps mixes with the distant echo of celebratory speeches from earlier—the same speeches now hollow, like recorded greetings from a city that has learned how to speak and not to listen.

Tamiko finds you then, camera hanging loose around her neck. She sits on the stoop beside you without asking. Her hands are ink-stained; her eyes are rimmed red.

Tamiko: (soft) "I still have footage. I can keep pushing—legal, grassroots, stories. It won't rebuild what we lost. But it might make sure it doesn't happen like this again."

You fold the brittle notebook closed. It is a small, inadequate act.

You: "We made noise. We tried to make them listen. The ethics review—"

Tamiko: "It slowed them, not stopped them. Bureaucracy is an eraser when money writes the timeline."

You lean your forehead against your knees. The scarf, once a weight of memory, feels like a child's talisman now: familiar but useless against an engineered tide.

[Visual: The lantern in Lina's window flickers; the seawall lights glow like a distant, guarded city]
[Music: The cello resolves to three low notes, patient and unyielding]

Elias's choice hangs in the air like a verdict. You did not get the victory you fought for. Families relocated, neighbors scattered; some houses remain, hollowed and sutured, others emptied. The city—Verdante—continues, but its shore has been repurposed into a promise that didn't ask the people who lived there whether they wanted to keep living the same way.

You stand, the notebook under your arm feeling heavier than it should. Your fingers trace the brass carabiner at your hip—keys that now open fewer doors. You step away from the stoop, the row folding itself behind you like a book shutting.

You walk until the promenade is a cut of silver on the horizon. The wind takes the edge of your coral scarf and flutters it against your neck like a wounded flag. The taste in your mouth is iron.

[Visual: Wide shot of you leaving Mariner's Row; the seawall looms, indifferent and finished]
[Music: The percussive strings crash once, then fall into a relentless, minor wash]

You do not know if you will stay in Verdante or leave to gather evidence, allies, or the strength to try again. All you know is the shape of what has been done, the ledger of human costs, and the small, bitter clarity that some victories require losses you cannot bear.

You close one last time the notebook that contains the cartography of a neighborhood's life. The pages are brittle. You tuck it into your bag and sling it over your shoulder. The city tastes like iron. The scarf feels small.

[Visual: Fade to a close-up of your packed notebook, the coral scarf frayed at the edge]
[Music: A single piano note, sustained and then allowed to drop into silence]
