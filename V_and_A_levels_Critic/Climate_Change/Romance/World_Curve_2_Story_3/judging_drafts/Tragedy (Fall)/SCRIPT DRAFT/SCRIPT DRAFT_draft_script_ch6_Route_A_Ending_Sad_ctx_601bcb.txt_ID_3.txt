[Scene: Mariner's Row | Afternoon]
[Visual: The narrow lane of Mariner's Row, once dense with nets and hand-lettered signs, now trimmed and scrubbed; temporary fencing marks boundaries. Movers push dollies of boxed lives across sun-bleached planks.]

You want to tell her that visibility isn't the same as power. You want to tell Elias that his presence on a Vale roster is not redemption. Your handwriting scatters across them: names, coordinates, promises, neighbors' stories. They are the archive of a life spent trying to keep place and people together.

You have no neat recourse. No legal victory arrives in time. The ethics review is bureaucratic molasses; Vale's private contracts are built for speed and opacity. People who once sang on porches sign forms and collect checks. They leave with dignity bundled in boxes that no one asked permission to open.

You stand at the threshold of Mariner's Row one last time, your bag heavy, your notebook clutched beneath your arm. The wind finds your coral scarf and pulls at it until it snaps like a small, private alarm.

You think of the day you first felt the tide come up the stairs, the neighbors hauling sandbags in a circle as if their hands could weave seawalls out of willingness alone. You think of the choices you made: speaking in the hearing, pushing for accountability. The protection you demanded had a cost. Accountability had a price.

You step away from the threshold and walk. Each stride is an ending. Each step presses the new pavement that glosses over memory. The city tastes like iron.

You do not know what to call this: defeat or survival. The houses are still standing in places; the blocks still hold people in others. But the social fabric—woven of porches, storefronts, and midnight conversations—has been cut and repurposed into a tidy exhibit of resilience. It is resilient, yes, but without the people who made it human.

You fold your scarf into your coat, its color a small, stubborn ember against the industrial gray. You pack the notebook—your brittle repository of names, of histories, of promises—and you sling your bag over your shoulder. The weight of it is not heavy enough to hold down memory.

You walk away with an unignorable clarity: action creates consequences, and the moral ledger sometimes bleeds in ways that no single chosen tactic can fully reconcile. Your hands are empty of the victory you wanted, full instead of a hard, hollow knowledge.
