[Scene: Mariner's Row | Morning — Low Tide]
[Visual: Half the street cordoned with orange tape; plywood notices flutter from lamp posts. The harbor beyond is a sheet of slate, the new seawall carving a straight, alien line where the shoreline used to breathe.]
[Sound: Distant jackhammers. The steady thud of cranes. A radio voice reading bulletins through a static hum.]
[Music: A hard-edged string ostinato; a low electronic pulse that tightens with every cut.]

You wake to the sound of construction like an accusation. It enters your bones first — a percussion of machines where lullabies used to be. The coral scarf around your neck is damp with salt and an old, private shame; it feels too small against the wind that pushes off the new promenade and into the row.

Paper flutters against your door: relocation offers, eminent-domain notices, glossy pamphlets promising "resilience" and "new economic opportunities." The envelopes smell of printer ink and something metallic beneath it all — the smell of deals being closed.

Lina finds you before you finish reading. Her paint-splattered overalls are streaked with saltwater now. She stands in front of her storefront and pinches the bridge of her nose so hard the braid at her scalp trembles.

Lina: "They gutted our lease. Said it's 'parcelization'—technical term, Mira. They gave us a number and a smile like it was a math problem."

You let the line land between you. The wooden sign above her storefront — hand-lettered, worn by winter and tide — swings once and stops.

You want to fix it. You want to pull the notices down and staple your name to every post and call Cassian every terrible name you can find. Instead, you fold the paper slowly, feeling its thinness as proof of other people's choices.

Tamiko moves through the row with her camera like a priestess with incense. Her footage isn't triumphant; it is careful, ledger-like. She records boxes being loaded from doorways, faces that do not match the glossy pamphlets, the kids who set down toys and stared at men in corporate vests as if they were weather.

Tamiko: (off-camera, quietly) "They keep saying 'we're protecting you.' Lina—people are leaving with promises. They think promises can be moved in a truck."

You can hear the tremor in her voice. It makes your jaw ache.

You go to Lina's window. Inside, boxes are piled half-open; the smell of coffee that afternoons used to carry is gone, replaced by the hollow smell of packing tape and detergent.

Lina: "My mom's recipe board. The one you wrote 'best empanadas' on when you were nine. They gave us a stipend and a broker's number. A stipend. As if that can hold a family history."

You press your palm to the cold of the glass, not entirely to stop her from leaving but to feel solid for a single breath.

[Scene: Mariner's Row — Midday]
[Visual: Work crews move like a new tide, dismantling porches and uprooting stoops. Hand-painted children's drawings curl into soggy confetti at the curb.]
[Sound: A relentless drill punctuates your thoughts. The hum of a crowd gathers where the construction fencing ends.]
[Music: Percussion intensifies; brass notes cut in like an alarm.]

You go toward the sound because that is what you do. Hands find work, mouths find facts. You navigate a maze of fluorescent vests and quieted neighbors; each face you pass is a ledger of what was lost or what was being negotiated away.

Cassian's monument gleams at the far end of the new promenade — a smooth, dark stone and a polished plaque that reads something about "security" and "vision." The plaque reflects the sky and your own distorted face. Police and private guards stand like punctuation marks.

Elias stands near the monument's base. He is smaller than you remember in this light, though his presence still carries the practiced ease of someone used to addressing rooms. His hands are steady at his sides; his face is a weathered map of recent storms — fatigue carved into the corners of his eyes, regret written into the set of his mouth.

You hear the city talk in fragments — that Elias was placed on leave, that his ethics review didn't block the contracts, that he took a Vale-funded oversight role as "a compromise" after being squeezed from municipal planning. The words taste like metal when you say them aloud in your head.

You approach. The crowd folds around you like a current, every conversation a small eddy away from you and toward the monument.

Elias: (softly, before you speak) "Mira."

The name is not an address. It is a thing you both once owned together. The sound of it makes your chest rise like a bell.

Mira Santos: "You didn't have to—"

Elias: (interrupting) "I had no choice, Mira. When I was pushed out, Cassian offered a seat at the table. He sold it as oversight. As a way to steer things from inside."

You watch a man you trusted turn over a small stone and find bureaucracy underneath. The protest in your bones bangs against a new, terrible understanding: sometimes the inside position is a slow erosion, not a rescue.

Mira Santos: "A seat? You left us. You let them parcelize the Row while you sat deciding which edges to smooth. You know what those 'offers' mean. You saw Lina's storefront stamped into a packet and told her it was temporary."

Elias's jaw tightens; his sea-gray eyes flash like a shutter.

Elias Moreno: "I tried to stop them. I briefed ethics. I flagged conflicts. They used shadow contracting. They moved the private financing through loopholes so the city's hold was minimal. I—Mira, I thought working inside was the only way to keep control, to reduce harm. I thought I could mitigate the worst."

Mira Santos: "Mitigate? Their 'mitigation' is a moving van and a glossy brochure. Our people don't need mitigation; they need home."

Elias holds up a hand as if to inflict a ceasefire on both of you.

Elias Moreno: "I thought a compromise would buy time—legal reforms later, community relocation that kept social ties. I miscalculated the speed. I didn't account for how fast Cassian would break promises if no one called him out publicly."

Your voice is raw. There's no beauty to it now, only a blunt instrument.

Mira Santos: "You chose to stay. You chose the process over the people in front of you."

Elias looks like someone who has swallowed his own words. He wants to speak, to explain, to shift the blame away from the shape of his choices. He has built maps for a lifetime — lines that promise certainty. None of those lines hold now.

Elias Moreno: "I chose badly, Mira. But I don't regret trying to make the system work. If I hadn't—"

He can't finish. The cranes punctuate his silence with iron.

Cassian steps forward, his coat immaculate, his umbrella a shadow in the midday light. He smiles the carefully calibrated smile of a man who has just signed paperwork that rewrites a neighborhood.

Cassian Vale: (with staged warmth) "Elias. A shame about the headlines. We were fortunate to bring someone of your talent into oversight. Your experience will help ensure the promenade serves everyone—eventually."

There it is: "eventually." A thin, poisonous comfort.

Tamiko's camera is two steps away, pointed with the steadiness of a chronicle. She does not shout; her lens is a ledger that keeps count.

Tamiko: (low, to you) "They talk about 'service' while people absorb the cost."

Your hands clench. There is a moment — only one — where you could strike at the monument, at Cassian, at the paper thick with contract clauses. Instead, you find words you mean to cut deeper.

Mira Santos: "You call it service. You call it protection. You turn a coastline into a ledger and move the people who don't have satellites to defend them. That's not protection."

Cassian's smile cools, then reforms.

Cassian Vale: (mildly condescending) "Ms. Santos, change requires difficult choices. We elected to act with speed to prevent loss of life. Some disruption is inevitable."

Mira Santos: "Some disruption? Lina's family lost their shop. Children have fewer places to play that aren't patrolled and privatized. You call that 'inevitable,' and you get to call it progress."

Cassian: "Progress is measured in lives saved. My firm funds this project so thousands are protected."

Mira Santos: "You measure our lives on a spreadsheet and call it salvation."

The crowd shifts. A few neighbors shout. Someone throws a water bottle that misses and clatters against the stone like an accusation. Elias flinches as if hit.

Elias Moreno: (voice breaking) "Mira — don't make this only Cassian's fault. I — I signed because I thought I could make it less brutal. I believed the process could be humane."

Mira Santos: "Humane? Your compromise is a button that ejects neighbors into some sterilized promenade where their histories are footnotes."

Elias: (pained) "I'm sorry. I can try to—"

Mira Santos: "Try? Not anymore. You've already tried. The Row is packed into boxes."

He looks at the boxes in Lina's doorway across the street. His face becomes a map of wrong turns.

Elias Moreno: "I know my choices hurt people. I can—"

Mira Santos: "You can do one thing now and mean it: renounce the oversight role. Use your name to pull back their contracts. Go public with everything you can prove. Stand with us, Elias—not the monument."

You expected him to move. You expected some remnant of the man who once stood in community meetings and argued for participatory design to choose the people over the model.

Elias's shoulders fold. He shuts his eyes like a man in prayer, then reopens them with a look so small it almost disappears into his face.

Elias Moreno: (quietly) "If I resign, Cassian will replace me with someone worse. I — if I stay, I can at least try to steer the work as it moves forward. I can soften edges, insist on community clauses, push for affordable housing layers, oversee hiring from the Row."

Mira Santos: "You're telling me the only way to stop further harm is to help them 'soften' it? You want to sit at the table of the people who displace us and ask them please?"

Elias's voice is a pin-prick of light.

Elias Moreno: "I— I don't know another way. I'm trying to salvage something."

Your mouth goes dry. The world narrows to two people on a gray stone and a monument that glints more like a verdict than a solution.

Mira Santos: "Salvage? You're cleaning up the mess you helped make."

Elias blinks, hurt like a physical thing. For a long beat, you both stand in the middle of a crowd that had once been promise and now is witness.

You do neither — or perhaps you do both in quick succession. Words leave you like thrown pebbles; your hands shake. Elias's face is unreadable in the glare. People spread like waves. The construction continues as if perseverance could bury culpability.

Cassian places a hand lightly on Elias's shoulder in public display. The gesture is meant to signal alliance. To you it reads like an ownership brand.

Cassian Vale: "Elias will help guide our efforts. We all want the same thing: safety."

Mira Santos: "Safety at the price of our stories."

Cassian's retort is a refracted politeness. He will hold the clip, the soundbite. He will spin the narrative where he seems compelled to be a reluctant shepherd of the people's welfare while steering the project to his preferred outcome.

Elias opens his mouth like he's about to say something that will change everything. Instead he says:

Elias Moreno: "We'll make sure the Row has input on beautification and local hiring."

The words fall like crumbs.

You realize, with sharp, unbearable clarity, that this is the definition of compromise in a world where power already leans away from you: small concessions that paper over systemic erasure.

[Scene: New Promenade — Late Afternoon]
[Visual: The promenade glitters under a brittle sun. New benches, planters, and a line of surveillance lamps trace the new edge of the sea. Guards watch neighborhoods that once were porous with human life.]
[Sound: Waves lap against the seawall with a new, institutional cadence. A child's laugh somewhere becomes faint, like an echo from before.]
[Music: A single plaintive cello line, then silence like a wound.]

You stand looking at the polished edge of what was once public shore. People queue for the 'resilience' tours Cassian's PR team now offers. The plaque is slick. You cup your hand and touch the cold stone as if checking whether it will hold.

Your notebook is heavy in your bag — pages full of community maps, names, scraps of promises. The cover is feathered where the water has kissed it. You have written here until your fingers cramped; you have tried to fit a neighborhood into lines and lists and legal terms.

Lina crosses the promenade with a box under her arm. She doesn't meet your eyes. She moves like someone who has learned to carry her history in cardboard.

Tamiko approaches, camera slung like a cross. She walks slower now. Her footage has become a record of small defeats; it lights up the network of people who still remember. She stops beside you.

Tamiko: "People will remember. They always do. But it's not the same."

You nod. There is no arguing the fact. There is no resurrecting a storefront that has been paved into a promenade.

Mira Santos: (to Tamiko) "You filmed everything. You have the names. You have the timestamps. Use them. Please."

Tamiko: (grim) "I will. But footage won't stop the cranes. Not today."

You turn and see Elias across the promenade. He is taking notes on his tablet, the same way he always has. He looks as if he is doing this because of a duty he believes in; his jaw is set. It is the look of a man who has accepted an arrangement with a price he intends to measure carefully.

Your hands go cold. There is a domestic cruelty in the way his posture belongs to the work now more than to the people you once shared coffee with in the greenhouse.

You remember promises — not the shiny ones Cassian hands out, but older pledges, whispered plans late at night over maps and tea. You remember Elias's laugh when he said, "We can design this together." The memory now tastes like salt and old iron.

You don't say anything. There are no lines left that feel honest and unweaponized.

He catches your eye for a heartbeat, and in that heartbeat the possibility of reconciliation dies like a small animal in winter. It dies slowly and with dignity you used to admire. He looks apologetic and resolute at once: a man thinking he is being practical while the people he loves are counted as collateral.

Elias lifts his hand in what could be a gesture of peace. You let him keep the gesture. You cannot return it.

[Scene: Mariner's Row | Dusk]
[Visual: The sky goes a bruised purple. Lamplight pools on damp steps. Shadows fall long across plywood and stacked boxes.]
[Sound: The last of the construction vans pull away like departing tides. Somewhere a dog barks once, twice, then stops.]
[Music: The strings resolve into a single, low minor chord that holds like a bruise.]

You go back when the light softens because the row needs someone to carry witness into night. You go because your notebook is still there, because the coral scarf around your neck is small and stubborn, because the smell of home is lodged in your throat.

Inside your front room you place your notebook on the table. You flip through pages. Names. Maps. Notes from public hearings. Designs with sticky notes penciled in. Coffee stains. A child's drawing taped to the inside back cover — a house with salt-blue windows and a figure with a scarf.

You pack the notebook with a deliberateness that feels ceremonial. You fold pages gently, as if preservation could be an act of resistance. The binder clip you use is cold in your hand.

You fold your coral scarf and press it flat. It is smaller than memory remembers. You tuck it into the notebook like a fossil.

Your hands are steady but your heart pounds as if someone is trying to get out and hasn't found an exit.

Lina's storefront will be someone else's showroom soon. Tamiko's exposés will become case studies. Elias will walk halls that smell of varnish and municipal coffee while his name is attached to a project that hollowed out your neighborhood.

Knowledge comes with a price. You have it. You have clarity that is hollow and heavy.

You step out onto the street one last time. The promenade lights cut the dusk into hard lines. The city tastes like iron.

You close the door behind you.

[Visual: The camera lingers on the notebook and scarf in the quiet room; the watercolor background washes into a cold, desaturated gray.]
[Music: A single piano note, not a vow, but a punctuation. It lingers and then fades.]

You do not know if the chronicles you pack will change the arc of policy or memory. You only know the shape of what you've lost and what you carry. There is no triumph here. There is an exhausted, bitter compulsion to keep records, to tell the story for the people who will not be able to, to hold on to small human truths against a machinery designed to make them fungible.

You carry the notebook under your arm and the scarf against your throat. The weight of both is a small, private ledger.

You begin to walk away from the Row.

[Visual: Long shot down the empty street; your figure recedes, small against the artificial line of the new seawall. The monument glints once, then becomes only a shape.]
[Sound: The wind picks up; it carries salt and the distant clack of construction settling for the night.]

You do not know what comes next. You do not know if Elias will stay clean inside the system or become a salvage worker cloaked in the language of mitigation. You do not know if Lina's storefront will ever belong to anyone who remembers its flavor. You do not know if Tamiko's footage will bend policy or become an archival sorrow.

You do know the taste of iron in your mouth, the hollowing in your chest, the way promises can be folded into envelopes and shipped away.

You keep walking. Your steps are the record you are left with now.
