[Scene: Staging Site — Pier | Pre-dawn, damp and electric]

[Visual: Wide shot of the pier at night-blue hour; tarps and raised planters carve the space into makeshift lines. Steam rises from a kettle; a string of lanterns casts warm islands on wet planks.]
[Sound: Boards creak underfoot, hammers tap staccato, Tamiko's camera shutter whispers; a distant engine idles like a threat.]
[Music: Tense, driving rhythm with low strings building under a brittle percussion]

You step onto the pier and the soundscape closes around you: the scuff of boots on treated timber, Lina's precise calls over a din of voices, a chorus of people breathing through wool and plastic. The air tastes of salt and coffee and spent diesel. Your coral scarf is damp at the edge where the tide's spray found it; the cord of your waterproof notebook thaps softly against your hip as you move.

Planters—crate-sized, braced with rebar and filled with dunegrass and soil—line the access points. People haul sandbags, secure tarps, weave ropes between bollards. The living defenses look rough and beautiful: not a blueprint but a communal will made visible. You feel the familiar ache behind your sternum, the ledger of names and small promises your grandmother left you. This is what you swore you'd do.

Lina spots you and grins, paint-splattered and breathless in her teal boots. She mouths, "Shift change?" and points at a half-assembled barricade that needs lifting. You move to help.

You: "How many more bundles?"

Lina: "Two more here—Tamiko's on camera, so keep it photogenic." (She grins, then her face folds.) "But we need the pier clear in an hour when the tide comes up. Dr. Arun wants more monitors up."

Lina hands you a plank. Her hands are sure and warm; for a second the cold retreats. You feel the community working like a muscle—sore, steady, and tuned to a heartbeat only you all hear. You can recall the places your hands have memorized: your grandmother's knots, the way a sandbag settles. You act from memory as much as plan.

[Visual: Close-up on your hands securing a ratchet strap around a planter; water droplets bead along the metal]
[Sound: Ratchet clicks; a camera shutter snaps; the distant murmur of a bullhorn like a far-off storm]

Dr. Arun is crouched at the pier's edge, calibrating a small instrument. He speaks without looking up.

Dr. Arun: "Tide will reach the walkway in forty-two minutes. If we keep the pier above the surge line with the planters and the berm, we can hold until daybreak. Keep the sensors unobstructed."

You: "Can the monitors detect sudden surges? If Cassian's crew starts shifting equipment—"

Dr. Arun: (interjecting) "They'll be intrusive. But the data will show patterns. If we publish it early, we get the moral argument solid." (He looks at you with salt-and-pepper patience.) "Just—make sure the bundles on the north side are double-bolted."

You nod and move to the north side, fingers numb with focused work. The occupation is an organism and you are one cell keeping it alive.

Tamiko moves like a wedge through the crowd, camera bobbing. She finds the best angles—children handing sandbags to elders, an old man laughing despite the damp. Her voice, when she cuts through, is bright with purpose.

Tamiko: "This is the image that will run. Keep faces open—no shouting, just stubborn joy. The algorithm loves the quiet defiance."

You: (a wry smile) "And if the algorithm meets Cassian's PR?"

Tamiko: "Then we tilt the frame. We don't let him control the narrative."

Her confidence sparks something like warmth in you, but Elias' face appears at the periphery like a cloud. He's wearing a municipal high-visibility vest—yellow neon against the low light—and a notebook tucked under his arm. He hesitates at the row of planters, then steps forward.

Elias: (quietly) "Mira—"

You: "Elias." (You say his name like a place you know, not like a greeting.) "You're late."

Elias: (hands raised, a small, conciliatory smile) "I came as fast as I could. I thought—maybe we could pause the heavy equipment tomorrow. Municipal oversight, a temporary moratorium. I spoke with the deputy—"

You cut him off because there's no time for the polite preamble. The pier is a living thing and it needs direction now.

You: "We don't need a moratorium on our bodies. We need the equipment to stay off this pier."

Elias: (a quick intake of breath) "I know. I know. But if we can get a legal pause, if I can talk to the mayor—"

You: (voice rising with the tide of people around you) "Talking won't keep the cranes off the docks tonight. They roll in at dawn. We need flesh and planters between that equipment and our houses."

Elias looks pained. He steps closer, the smell of his linen blazer mixed with municipal-grade optimism.

Elias: "There are legal risks. Injunctions and arrests could break what we're building. I'm asking you to consider—"

You: "Consider is a luxury." (You say the words and feel them sharpen.) "Consider won't protect Lina's mother who can't lift a sandbag. Consider won't hold a child's bed dry. We are already paying every night."

The argument opens up—two people who believe in different levers pulling on the same rope. Around you, voices rise and fall: Tamiko pushing for visibility, Lina barking commands, elders discussing the old sea-stories, Dr. Arun muttering about data windows.

Elias: (softer) "I don't want to hurt you. I don't want to see arrests."

You: "Then stand with us here. Don't ask us to move so your metrics look clean." (You're aware the words cut him; you hear the micro-shift in the group's breath.)

Elias: "I thought—maybe if I bridged the city and you all, we could make this a living model." (His jaw tightens.) "But if you want my vest and my voice, ask for it. Don't ask me to choose for you."

You: (the thing you don't say swells in your chest—how many times you've carried decisions alone) "We're not asking you to choose for us. We're asking you to stand here. To risk."

Elias: "Risk what, Mira? My career? My ability to get anything done for Verdante?"

You: (you almost laugh, but it's a small, sharp sound) "We all risk. The difference is that my risk keeps people where they live."

The exchange fractures like thin ice. He reaches out as if to touch your shoulder, then pulls back. There's a hesitance in him you know intimately: he wants solutions that scale—fast, efficient, legible. You want people who can tell their grandchildren they didn't leave.

[Visual: Elias's hand hovers near your shoulder and then drops; the camera lingers on the space between you]
[Sound: The wind picks up; a distant van door slams]

Cassian's presence arrives not as a person but as an aggressive ripple in the feed. A message thread pings on your phone—diplomatic, cold—and a short while later, a cluster of private security mutters at the pier's edge. They are not in municipal vests; they are in tailored jackets and slacks, low voices like knives. One of them steps forward and raises a hand as if framing the scene for himself.

Security Lead: (via megaphone) "This is private property. You are obstructing our right to access the staging area. You'll be asked to disperse in five minutes."

Cassian's name is in the message that follows: a crisp press release forward: 'Obstructionists threaten jobs and safety. Vale Consortium committed to orderly, rapid adaptation.' His PR team frames you as the problem. You taste bile and the salt in your mouth.

Lina: (fierce) "They'll have to get through us."

Tamiko: (camera tight on faces) "Let them try. We'll make sure everyone sees it."

Dr. Arun: (low, practical) "If they force removal, we need legal counsel—now. Tamiko, get footage backed up. Lina, rotate the elderly into the shelter. Mira—"

You: (to Dr. Arun) "If they push force, how fast can we get an injunction?"

Dr. Arun: "Not fast enough." (He stares at the sensor readouts as if they could rewrite law.) "We need to buy time."

The crowd tightens. Someone hands out fresh hot soup in paper bowls; the warmth crawls into your hands but not all the way into your cold. People sit under tarps and swap scarves. An elder—hair like salt—tells a sea-story: of a night when the lights failed and neighbors lit lanterns in a chain so boats could find the channel. The story calms and sharpens at once; it's both lullaby and battle hymn. You sit for half a moment, listening, and let it anchor you.

But anchoring will not hold a bite-sized security detachment forever. Their leader starts pacing, counting the minutes like a metronome. Then his radio crackles and his voice hardens.

Security Lead: (through teeth) "Contracted by Vale Consortium. We are authorized to clear the area."

The words are small and legal but feel like weight. Cassian has the money and the lawyers to turn language into motion.

Elias moves between you and the security line, holding his hands out.

Elias: "Please—let me talk to your supervisor. I can get a municipal officer to stand between you for a temporary pause."

Security Lead: (dismissive) "We have instructions."

Elias: (pleading) "If they move now, there will be arrests. That will change everything. We can negotiate a municipal-trust pause—let the community manage the site while we formalize protections."

You: (you see the strain in his face; you feel it as if it were a bruise) "You really believe they'll listen to you, Elias?"

Elias: "I have leverage." (He looks at you, searching.) "I can pull enough strings to slow them down. But I need your buy-in. Not to step away entirely—just to reduce the physical risk."

Your gut tightens. The occupation's power is its body in the space: tarps, planters, human heat. Reduce presence and you risk losing the moral clarity that has drawn public sympathy. But full resistance could end with arrests, broken bones, or worse.

Tamiko moves in, camera tight on Elias and you.

Tamiko: "This is the moment. If you take him up on a trust, make sure it's binding on the record. Otherwise—it looks like capitulation."

Lina: (bare-knuckled) "We either make them move a van or we die trying. I didn't grow up to watch my block get bulldozed."

Your chest is hot and the air around you seems to tighten with possibility and peril. The arousal that began as busy energy hardens into a crystalline decision point: the authorities' countdown hums in your ears. People look to you because you have been the one to organize the nights, to translate grief into action. You feel the old tendency—shoulder the weight, carry the choice alone.

Night bleeds into a long, damp morning. The work doesn't stop. You help carry an elderly neighbor from a chair to a sheltered spot when the wind picks up; someone offers you tea in a chipped mug. Fatigue sits like a physical object behind your ribs: cold in the bones, numbness in the fingers, a stubborn ache that no amount of sleep will fix. Tamiko edits footage in real time; Dr. Arun rigs the last tide monitor with trembling hands. Lina organizes shifts with a voice that never leaves the present.

And then the city's legal counsel arrives—a curt letter, pushed through a doorway by a municipal aide. It's a thin thing but its language is iron: notice of potential injunction, request to vacate, terms of 'public safety'. Cassian's legal machine is already moving. The crowd hushes as if the pier itself is listening for the weight of law.

Tamiko: (voice tight) "They've issued a notice. It'll be in the feed in an hour."

Lina: (anger like heat) "We knew they'd try to scare us. They can't scare us into leaving."

Dr. Arun: (calm and blunt) "An injunction is different from a police line. It lets the courts speak. If they move to enforce it, it'll give them legal cover for force. We need counsel."

Elias: (to you, almost whispering) "We can bring in legal teams, experts. We can reduce the number of bodies on the line to protect the elderly and kids. Let the lawyers strengthen the case."

You look at the faces around you: a child with sand on her knees, Lina's hands raw from rope; Dr. Arun rubbing his knuckles blue from cold; Tamiko with her camera, facing outward like a lighthouse. The physical reality presses against you: wet clothes, sleep-starved eyes, the possibility of being dragged off a pier at dawn under the eyes of a million screens.

You feel the choice like a tide: push forward and risk bodily confrontation that could create martyrs and momentum; withdraw into legal cover and risk the occupation's moral force evaporating; or try to convert the occupation into a formally recognized community-managed site through Elias' good offices—a co-management that might buy time but demands faith in the very municipal systems and political favors you distrust.

Your pulse is a drumroll. The world narrows to the knot in your chest; your grandmother's hands are sewing a sandbag in the memory-light; you taste sea-brine and grit.

[Visual: Close-up on your face, lantern light painting lines of resolve and exhaustion]
[Sound: A distant siren wails once; the camera clicks of Tamiko quicken]
[Music: Rapid strings climb; percussion hits like a heartbeat]

The group gathers in a squeezing circle around the planters. You can feel every eye—some full of hope, some rimmed with fear. Lina leans in, elbows tucked, breath steaming.

Lina: "What do we do, Mira?"

Tamiko: "We need a headline. Whatever direction we take, make it honest."

Dr. Arun: "We need legal counsel either way. But the physical question is: can we hold them off until counsel intervenes?"

Elias: (urgent now) "If I can get a municipal-trust agreement—temporary, overseen by an independent board—that would allow us to manage the site. It keeps the equipment off and gives you legal standing without the spectacle of arrests."

You smell the morning salt and the diesel, the bread someone tore and brought around. You feel exhausted and incandescent, a vulnerability honed into resolve.

This is the hinge: push for bodies and the crowd's moral weight; call the lawyers and lose the image that keeps watchers awake at night; or trust Elias to make a political bridge that demands faith in the systems that failed your neighborhood before.

Your throat is dry. The choice sits heavy as a tide-worn stone.
