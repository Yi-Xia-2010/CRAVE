## Visual Novel Title
Tidebound Promises

---

## World View
In 2042 the coastal metropolis is a city of patched defenses and negotiated comforts: reclaimed wetlands and rooftop farms thread through skyline scars, public plazas are quasi-permanent tented assemblies, and HelioCorp’s glass towers glitter at the horizon like unattainable promises. Political power sits between municipal councils, community collectives, and private infrastructure firms; the latter wield resources and technical know-how but face growing public scrutiny. Culture is repair-forward: people upcycle, barter labor, and memorialize lost harbors through festivals. Religion is diffuse — ritualized remembrance and sea-offerings are cultural customs rather than organized doctrine. Class divides run along access to technology and stable housing: gated corporate enclaves maintain climate-controlled amenities while fringe neighborhoods rely on mutual aid. Geography compresses urgency: tidal canals, storm-battered promenades, rooftop terraces that double as food hubs. The atmosphere is melancholic yet stubbornly warm — salt-scented wind, communal kitchens, neon ads promising "safe seas." Conflict is a moral one: technocratic, centralized geoengineering vs. distributed, community-led resilience. The novel’s tactile surfaces — salt-stained boots, mossy concrete, damp field jackets, glass atriums — visually ground every decision, making policy debates feel intimately personal.

---

## General Plot Overview - Plot Outline
You are Aiko Mori, a former urban climatologist turned community coordinator. The story explores choices between Elias’s grassroots resilience and Dr. Rowan Hale’s centralized geoengineering offer. Starting with tentative cooperation and small wins (Rise), a crisis and ethical compromise shatter trust (Fall), and you must rebuild toward a lasting, principled outcome (Rise). The narrative is first-person and romance-anchored: Elias’s warmth and impulsive idealism tempt you toward community-led solutions; Rowan’s brilliant, polished assurance offers rapid risk reduction at the cost of localized autonomy. Secondary figures — Mina, Sora, Rafi — tug the plot into moral detail, while locations (Tideward Promenade, Verdant Terrace, HelioCorp Atrium, Old Beacon, Subbasement Data Lab) stage key confrontations. Multiple branching choices let you weigh love, safety, and integrity; four distinct endings reflect compromises between life-saving tech and distributed governance, culminating in either communal triumph, pragmatic sacrifice, systemic reform, or tragic martyrdom. The emotional arc follows the Cinderella template: an initial ascent to hope, a sudden collapse with high stakes, and a final rise toward resolution and meaning.
