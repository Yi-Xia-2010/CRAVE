Elias: (half-smile) "And if they spin charm? If Rowan's smile does the lobbying for them?"

Sora: (quietly) "You will go prepared. What you need from their stage is not applause — it's accountability."

Mina meets your eyes, the conflict visible in the crease between her brows. She wants the water to stay out of the gardens as much as you do, but the ledger in her mind has people to feed now.

Mina: "If they can give us space, water, guarantees... I want to welcome that. But only if we write it with our hands."

Your reply is more practiced than you mean: a list of conditions, of metrics and oversight committees. It sounds technical, but it's how you imagine trust being translated into action.

[Scene: HelioCorp Glass Atrium | Morning]
[Visual: Reflective glass, vertical gardens staged like trophies; corporate banners flutter with a practiced wind]
[Music: Subtle synth underscoring — polite, confident]
[Sound: Murmurs turning into polite applause; the click of cameras]

Rowan Hale steps into the light with the kind of calm that feels engineered. The atrium is arranged for theater: models in crystalline displays, holographic renderings that glide like scales of light across the ceiling. People lean forward; the audience is part awe, part hunger.

Rowan: (measured) "We stand, today, on the precipice not of surrender but of possibility. Modular sea-shield arrays — scalable, deployable within months — will blunt storm surges. Cloud-seeding adjuncts can reduce peak water loads in targeted corridors. Together, they buy time."

The visuals are seductive: smooth diagrams animate where a sea wall can flex, where curtain arrays shift like metallic reeds. You feel your own models in your head — smaller, rooted, grown from local materials — and yet the relief in the room is tangible. Mina's hand tightens around a program; her face trembles between awe and caution.

Rowan: (softening, effortlessly persuasive) "We propose a partnership: implementation with oversight panels, contractual guarantees for maintenance. We move quickly, we save lives."

The applause is polite, heavy with relief. You notice two things at once: the warmth in many faces that comes from imagining fewer flooded basements, and the quiet hesitation in others who read between the glossy lines.

Rafi leans close, voice low and quick as he passes you a folded printout he slipped from his bag. He always moves like an edge-of-frame camera, noticing what polished speech wants to bury.

Rafi: (whisper) "Exclusivity clauses. 'Right of first refusal' on adjacent zones. Displacement contingencies masked as 'temporary relocation.' There's fine print that funnels decision-making." He taps the paper. "This isn't just infrastructure; it's leverage."

You: (quiet) "Are you sure? This is public."

Rafi: "Public forum, public optics. Look deeper."

His fingers trace a contract clause as if reading its bones. Elias's jaw tightens; Mina's hand goes to her mouth.

Rowan: (noticing the tension, with the coolest of smiles) "Questions, please. We welcome scrutiny. Transparency is part of the package."

You step to the mic because it is what you prepared for. Your voice does not tremble; it carries the steadiness of someone who has measured tides and social trust.

You: "Dr. Hale, your models look promising. Can you commit to full, open access to the datasets that will inform the arrays' placement and maintenance — shared publicly, with community oversight on deployment decisions?"

Rowan's eyes settle on you like a light on a specimen slide. For a second his expression is unreadable — professional, attentive, faintly curious.

Rowan: (inclining) "Open data is an admirable ideal. Operational security and proprietary systems complicate unfettered access. But I'm willing to explore programmable transparency: vetted feeds for oversight bodies."

You press, because this is the hinge: "Vetted by whom? And who appoints those oversight bodies?"

Rowan: (smile precise) "We can form joint oversight councils. HelioCorp will sit with community representatives."

Elias: (from the seats, voice sharp) "And who chooses those community reps? Not HelioCorp."

The exchange pulls a small wave through the crowd. Rowan answers with a plan for nomination processes that sound reasonable, and several people nod. Mina watches you, rough certainty in her gaze — wanting guarantees, fearing the loss of agency.

Rowan listens. He does not dismiss you. He folds your language into the cadence of his talk and promises "exploration" and "joint councils" and a staged approach that will be subject to metrics. It's not a contract yet — it's a well-phrased opening.

After the forum, voices spill into the brisk air. The Atrium's polished surfaces make the city feel farther away, its edges too neat.

Elias: (urgent) "You saw how they did it. They sold 'safety' like it's a coat you can buy. We need to push back — loudly — from the street. Don't let them pick our faces for their advisory boards."

You: "And if we don't do anything? If the shields could stop the next surge?"

Elias: (looking at you with that raw thing again) "Then we do the work that saves our people without giving away our spaces."

The argument is less about ego and more about trust: whether you can reconcile the need for rapid infrastructure with the community's right to self-determination. Your internal ledger tallies lives saved against governance lost; the numbers do not resolve the ache.

You scroll through an architecture of possibilities: where arrays would sit, which blocks they would protect, and which neighborhoods become buffer zones.

You: "If 'critical infrastructure' is written broadly, they can decide who's protected."

Rafi: "Exactly. There's language that permits relocation 'for community safety' that includes temporary maintenance zones. That can become permanent."

You think of Mina's kitchen, of Sora's stories about the coastline communities carved over generations. The city's social fabric is as vulnerable as its streets.

You and Rafi run through contingencies — legal phrasing to demand, oversight language to insist upon, community governance structures that could enforce transparency. Each item narrows possibilities. Each item also risks being watered down in negotiation.

Rafi: (softly) "There's a middle road: a legal framework for data sovereignty. Obligatory public audits. A clause that requires community consent for any relocations longer than thirty days."

You map it in your head — a pilot that binds HelioCorp to community terms, verifiable, with sunlit records and rotating oversight. It feels like a lever for agency.

Elias arrives, breath fogging in the coolness.

Elias: "What did you find?"

You summarize. He reads the outlines like a man reading a map of a lost harbor. His response is immediate, the way he always wants action as soon as a risk is visible.

Elias: "We either take them down in court or we force them to sign in the open. I don't trust half-measures."

You: "Half-measures can save lives. They can also hand us the map of our own erasure."

Elias: "Then we make the measures full. We don't walk into their ready-made councils."

You look at him. The question is not only about governance. It’s threaded through with something more intimate: the way your choices will align you with different visions of the future — and with different people.

You can imagine Rowan, steady and pragmatic, offering a path that scales fast. You can imagine Elias, hands callused from planting, asking you to stand with the slow, stubborn growth of community. Both paths whisper reasons you can believe in.

Mina: "We need to decide — not out of fear, but out of care."
