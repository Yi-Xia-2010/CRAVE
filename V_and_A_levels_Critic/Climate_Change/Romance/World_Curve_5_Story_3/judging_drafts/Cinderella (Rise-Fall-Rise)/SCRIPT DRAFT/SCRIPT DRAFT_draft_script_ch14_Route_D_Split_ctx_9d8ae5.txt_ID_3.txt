[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Night — Crisis]
[Visual: Tight on the concrete grate under the Beacon, steam and saltwater dripping down the metal ribs; the lab's cool LEDs strobe in alarm]
[Music: Harsh percussion, rising dissonant strings — relentless, urgent]
[Sound: Sirens wailing above; the high hum of servers; metallic groans as water finds new channels]

You feel the subbasement as a throat closing: air cooler, heavier, tasting of iron and burnt insulation. Above, the city has turned itself into a chorus of shouts and megaphones. Down here, the world is much smaller and much louder—just you, the racks, the valves, and the time they have already stolen from you.

Rafi: "Pressure's spiking on the coastal feed—no, it's spiking where it shouldn't. Some bypass opened. Helio node's reading a reverse flow."

You: "Which valve—where?"

Rafi: (rapid, eyes flicking between monitors) "Valve fourteen's behaving like it's been compromised. There's a mis-direct into the retention manifolds that sit above the server bays. If it routes there, the scupper lines will backfeed the lab."

Mina: (over a throat-mic) "We can try manual overrides, but the access hatch is iced with pressure seals. There's talk out there—police are pushing on Tideward. We're short a crew."

The monitors paint the narrative in neon: teal models collapsing to red as a pair of lines converge where they mustn't. Water—supposed to be shunted into temporary reservoirs—has found a new plan of movement. The sound of it isn't yet a flood; it's in the clicks and in the way the valve motor stutters like a dying heartbeat.

You put a palm on the nearest console because you need something physical to hold to steady the thought. Your glove is damp. The worn leather of your notebook presses into the small of your back where you've tucked it like a talisman.

[Visual: Close on your hand on the console; tiny drops bead and tremble on the worn silver streak at your temple]
[Sound: A high-frequency whine — a valve motor straining]

Elias: (breathing hard, voice close) "We can cut the feed from the surface lines. We can isolate the node. Let me—"

You: "If you cut A or B they'll reroute and overpressurize the manifold. We need to bleed C first—slow, precise. The automated failsafes only trigger when the panel reads a secure latch. If the latch isn't engaged, it will dump to the bays."

Elias: (shakes his head) "Then we latch. I'll— I'll do it. I can reach—"

You see him flicker in the sick light, hair plastered to his forehead, paint-streaked overalls soaked where he leaned into a pump. The sight of him here—so close, every freckle sharpened by the harsh LEDs—feels like both an anchor and a risk. Your chest tightens with the knowledge that whatever happens here will bend him into some new shape.

Rafi: "Automated sequence is queued but it's waiting for a physical confirmation. It's waiting for a hand. We can trigger remotely but it won't engage unless that panel reports closed."

Sora: (a low, steady line over the comm you didn't expect to hear) "Aiko, those panels were never designed for saltwater. Think of the old beacon—when the lens cracked we had to wedge cedar to stop the leak. Find the wedge that works."

Your mind, so used to models and ledger entries, scrambles for a timber fix in a place of servers and circuits. The lab smells suddenly of ozone and wet concrete; there is a metallic tang at the back of your throat. A spark hisses a few racks over, and the LEDs flicker.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Moments later — Escalation]
[Visual: Wide shot of the lab as a thin sheet of water snakes across the floor; shadows stretch and compress]
[Music: Strings surge; a low, violent electronic throb undercuts every sound]
[Sound: Loud clang as a conduit flexes; a pop and the sharp smell of burning insulation]

You choose without choosing—the world compresses decisions into motion. The bleed valve is a cramped, cold knot of metal; your gloved fingers find the lip and slip. Water finds your wrist and drags at your sleeve. Each breath tastes like a memory of the last storm—mud up to the windows, neighbors bailing in the dark—and you are aware that memory is not guidance when machinery is failing.

Mina: "There's a short in bay three. If that arcs, the whole bank goes. You need to isolate it now."

Elias: "I'll hold the lamp. Keep pressure on the manual. Don't—" His voice cracks. "Don't let it snap back."

You grunt, matching the rhythms of the valve with thumb and forefinger. The mechanism responds with a wet, resistant clack. For a second the model on Rafi's monitor brightens—the teal returns to a listening green.

Rafi: (relief, thin) "We're in a window. If we can get the panel to report closed in twenty-two—no, fifteen seconds—automated stabilizers will take load. They can reroute the pressure away from the bays."

A timer, mental now, counting down in matchsticks.

Then something that was already wrong becomes catastrophic: a secondary seal you didn't see gives. A hot jet of seawater arcs over a bundle of cables. Sparks bloom like small, angry flowers. The air goes bitter and loud. Your boots slap the wet concrete; the soles begin to skid.

You hear Elias's step change—closer, then farther, then a clatter as he fumbles with the lamp. He is trying to string together something that isn't there: promises, a route out, a plan that saves both machines and people. Everything about him is urgent and open-handed; everything about you tightens into the know-how of this particular catastrophe.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Critical — Countdown]
[Visual: Close on a server rack as its indicator lights shudder and die; reflections of your face fragmented in an acrylic shield]
[Music: All instruments throttle, a single bowed note refusing to end]
[Sound: The lab's alarm becomes a human scream when it passes through the speakers]

The flood finds rhythm: at your knees, then mid-thigh, the water pulls at the hem of your jacket like a hand. Cables bob; one lashes out and strikes a conduit, a bright, stabbing flash. Heat and cold mingle on your skin; your silver streak—your single pale line at the temple—has a slick of salt across it that gels like quicksilver against your hair.

You think of ledger entries and long meetings and the Beacon's lamp softening to gold. You think of Sora's cedar wedges and Mina's hands kneading bread in the dark. You think of Elias's laugh, how it breaks in a particular half-note when he is trying to be brave. You think of the research you once did in sterile labs with clean walls and promises you didn't trust then. You think of all of it and how small a moment it makes you now.

Elias: (breathing fast, voice unmoving) "Aiko—this is not where you're supposed to end. You hear me? Not here."

You: "If I don't fix the latch, the safeties won't engage. They'll dump into the bay and fry the models—everything we've shown the city. The records, the proof... people will call it a failure, they'll use it to justify more secrecy."

Elias: "Then let the models go. Let the street have the story. Let us come back with witness statements and cameras. Please. Please, Aiko—"

Your hands are already moving, half of you trying to be persuaded, half of you refusing to let the ledger be erased. Time is a thin strip now. The automated sequence is waiting on one last signal: a physical read of the panel's closed state. The latch is old and swollen with salt. If it can't be depressed and held, the system will consider the node unsecured and take the only safe option it has—trip everything and dump the load into the nearest available cavity, which is the lab itself.

Rafi: "We have ten seconds to read closed or the fallback dumps. Ten. Nine—"

You look at Elias. His hands are extended toward you, water pooling at his wrists. There's pleading there so big you could live inside it.

He reaches.

His fingers find the back of your jacket. For a wild, impossible second your world compresses to the exactness of those fingers: the calluses, the rope bracelet, the way his grip is both desperate and gentle. He tries to pull you back.

You feel the tug between the person you are—rooted in community, patient resilience—and the scientist who knows the geometry of failure down to the notch. You feel how much you fear giving control over to systems that hide their contracts, but you are also not blind to the immediate calculus: if the safeties engage, many will survive. If they don't—if everything goes black—there will be no ledger left to prove a thing. There will be only ruin and a thousand small, precise acts of harm hidden beneath corporate statements.

The water reaches your waist now. The alarm changes timbre: not urgent, resigned. The panel hums; its indicator is a blink away from green.

Mina: (voice breaking) "Someone has to secure it. We can pull back and try to live—"

You: "There isn't time to do it and stay alive."

Elias: (shouting over the roar) "We can haul you out. We can wedge something—"

A server in bay four gives an explosive cough; a sheet of hot steam swells and bangs against the ceiling. Your boots slip. For an instant, the world tilts on an axis that is all water and screaming lights.

You think of Sora's words—find the wedge that works. You think of ledger entries that are pages and pages, of signatures and handprints, of Mina's soup ladled out in the rain. You think of the Beacon lit up once more because someone refused to let the arc go dark.

The panel's LED blinks green for one heartbeat and then falters back to amber. The automated sequence waits for a closed read. Your hand is already hovering above the latch, palm slick. There is, impossibly, a surety inside you that this is what must be done. There is also a sudden, hot animal fear at the thought of what holding that latch might demand.

Elias lunges. He slams a hand on your shoulder, a grip that says leave, live, love. You taste salt and his breath; the world narrows to the weight of decision and the physical sensation of being gripped.

Rafi: "Three—two—"

Your lungs clench. Somewhere above, the city's chorus sharpens into the rasp of approaching vehicles and the panic of people whose stories will be bent by whatever you do next.

[Music: All sound collapses into a single, high sustained note — an electric scream]

You can feel the pull of both futures. One is a ledger that survives, names and models and documented evidence that could change system-level outcomes later—but to get there someone must place themselves inside the mechanism and stop the dump by physical means. The other is life, the raw, violent insistence of staying, walking away, rewriting the story with what you can shout into a public square.

Your heart hammers—so hard you can hear it as if it's another alarm. You smell burning plastic and the river smell of the ocean mingled with cedar and bread and laughter from other nights, as if memory has decided to crowd the moment with everything you love.

Elias: (voice small, raw) "Please. You don't have to decide this alone."

You: (inward, everything you are condensed) You never do. You always carry them with you—the Beacon, the maps, the peoples' hands. But the choice that matters now is the one your body can make in the next breath.
