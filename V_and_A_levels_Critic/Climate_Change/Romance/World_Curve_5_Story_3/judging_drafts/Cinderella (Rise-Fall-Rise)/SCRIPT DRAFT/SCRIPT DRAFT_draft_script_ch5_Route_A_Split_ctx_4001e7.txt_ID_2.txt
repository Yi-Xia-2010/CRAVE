[Scene: Tideward Promenade | Same — The Breach]
[Visual: Ramparts of sandbags, some disintegrating; a ragged tide slices through a weak seam]
[Music: Brass stabs and rapid percussion — frantic]
[Sound: The roar of displaced water; a chorus of alarms from handheld radios]

Elias is at the front line already, sleeves rolled, shouting instructions that are part command, part pleading. His hair plastered to his forehead, his amber eyes are wide and bright with an impossible steadiness. He is pulling neighbors into a human chain to heave sandbags, to reroute a pump hose, to throw a tarpaulin like a net.

Mina's voice comes in on the radio, breathless: "The kitchen—water's coming fast. People in the back—Mina says get them out!" You think of the stack of soup cans, the long table where bread is always breaking. You picture Sora's hands—slow, knowing—anchoring someone who cannot walk. The image steadies you.

But there's another thread pulling: the Subbasement Data Lab's monitors show heat spikes, disk I/O climbing out of recommended ranges. If the servers overheat and fail while the surge of silted saltwater scours the foundation, decades of telemetry—the pilot's few honest months of open data—could be vaporized.

Rowan's voice translates through a firmer channel—corporate, controlled, the kind that calms some and strikes others like a blade.

Rowan: "Aiko. We need immediate lockdown—full remote command. Secure the servers, initiate emergency shutter protocols, and seal the lab. Civil engineers will be dispatched. We cannot have human operators in the server areas during a structural failure."

Elias hears that, and his face shifts. There's a flash—anger, disbelief—then a quick, hard look aimed squarely at you, as if the choice will anchor out of your chest.

Elias: "You want to hand them control? Now? While our neighbors are waist-deep? They can't—"

Rowan: (cutting in, voice tight) "We can secure critical assets, Elias. It's a necessary risk. If the servers fail, the pilot's integrity is gone and more neighborhoods will be exposed—data informs evacuation in real time."

Elias: "Data won't hold someone's kid while they clamp sandbags together."

Your throat tightens. The rain is a thin nail in your ears. You hear the hum of distant pumps, the wet slap of hand against sandbag, the soft, afraid chatter of people trying to be brave.

You know Rowan's models. You know Elias' muscles and lineage and the way he names the coastline like a beloved. Your mind begins to run parallel: one set of graphs that pleads for central control, another set of hands that refuses to be excluded.

[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Below Ground — Sirens and Silt]
[Visual: Narrow concrete stairwell slick with seawater; LEDs on racks strobe between teal and crimson]
[Music: Rapid electronic pulses — anxious]
[Sound: Servers cooling fans straining; a deep, thrumming vibration as water hunts]

You hit the lab's door and the air drops into cold, recycled calm—until the alarms make it all treacherous. A technician is crouched at a rack, hands smeared in lubricant and salt, face streaked with rain. She is young, mouth working around instructions she cannot get to stay calm.

Technician: "We're seeing sector latency spikes. If we shut them down gracefully, we need twenty minutes. If the breach deepens, there's structural risk—no personnel."

Rowan's presence is a tide of composure in a room that refuses to be calm. He moves like someone who's measured the length of the rope between life and liability and found it acceptable in his hand.

Rowan: "If HelioCorp initiates a hard lockdown, we preserve the node's integrity. We do not lose the pilot's continuity. Aiko, authorize remote control for stabilization."

You feel every second like tiny fractures in glass.

You think of Mina's laugh before the storm, the way she folded wings of bread for people who couldn't afford a smile. You think of Elias' wet palm on a sandbag, his promises made in the cadence of tides. You think of your ledger being written in that small, stubborn script you trust.

You also think of the data—raw, messy, honest—streams that have allowed neighborhoods to predict when the worst seam would break. Losing that would be more than equipment; it would be erasing the neighborhood's voice from the map of their own survival.

Rafi's recorder clicks into your ear, breath coming fast.

Rafi: "They're already starting the remote sequence. I can see the handshake. If you—if you don't authorize, HelioCorp will assert emergency precedence and lock our operators out."

You can feel a moral weight in your palm where the authorization tablet would sit. Whether or not the tablet is physically there does not matter; the ledger of choices is heavier than any device.

Sora, leaning on the walking stick near the stairwell, says something small and sharp, the old kind of wisdom.

Sora: "Machines are tools. Remember who wields them."

You meet Rowan's pale blue gaze. In that look you can taste the sharp cleanliness of his plans and the cost they gloss over.

Rowan: "We cannot afford sentimentality when infrastructure is failing."

Elias: "That's easy for you to say when your plan gives you an out and our people get told to step back."

Rowan: "Elias, live operations are not negotiable. Your people are courageous, but we will not risk the pilot for ad-hoc measures."

Elias: (stepping closer) "They're not 'your' people, Rowan. We built this—"

Rowan: (coolly) "We built the platform that makes it scale. The difference in our views is methodology."

Elias: "Methodology doesn't carry someone to safety."

A silence snaps between them like thin ice.

Your internal models are screaming. If you authorize lockdown, you hand control to a distant, efficient will that might protect the pilot but deny neighbors access to pumps and lines that could hold a levee. If you refuse, you keep human agency and immediate rescue in reach, but risk losing the data integrity that could protect more people later. If you try to split the difference, you risk failing both.

The storm's sound seems to swell, a single organism pressing at every fragile seam.
