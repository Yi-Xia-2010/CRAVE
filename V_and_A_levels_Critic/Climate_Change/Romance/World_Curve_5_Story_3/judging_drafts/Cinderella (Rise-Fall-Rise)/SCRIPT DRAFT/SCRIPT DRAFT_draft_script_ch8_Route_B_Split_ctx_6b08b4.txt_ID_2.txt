The terrace smells of wet soil and salt, the sweet rot of overturned compost tangling with fuel-sting from the emergency generators. Your boots slide on a plank slick with algae; your jacket is heavy with rain. Every breath tastes like iron and pressure.

You already know the math—how much water, how fast, which levee will fail first—but knowing doesn't stop your stomach hollowing when the first wave bites a notch into the outer barrier. Someone screams, then clamps a hand over their mouth as if sound could be sealed away.

Volunteers swarm like a nervous tide: hands passing sandbags, ropes hauled to anchor points, pumps thudding with desperate rhythm. The decentralized pumps you're used to—proud, patched, and resilient—stutter. One coughs and goes dead; another hiccups, and a green LED that used to glow steady flickers angry red.

You grit your teeth and move. You are supposed to be the calm node in the maelstrom: a ledger-writer who becomes an axis when things unspool. Tonight, the axis is bending.

[Sound: Radio crackle — overlapping channels, bursts of static and voice]

Rafi's voice cuts through the static, too sharp and clipped to be only about water. "—HelioCorp contingency flagged. Emergency chains. Access restricts: 'critical perimeter nodes.' Files show activation triggers tied to 'public safety' — which is vague as hell."

You remember Rowan Hale's polished smile, the HelioCorp emblem reflecting light like an eye. The memory tastes like the copper of blood. Rafi's recorder clicks in your hand, the tiny LED stuttering.

Elias leans in, rain-soaked hair plastered to his temple, eyes bright with the singular belief that motion is cure. "We hold that breach at Dockside," he says, jaw set. "Everyone. All-hands. If they think they can lock us out, we can't give them the victory."

You can see the radius in your head—if everyone charges to Dockside, evacuation corridors will be left thin. The Terrace's food stores, the children's bedding in the Beacon's lower room—everything that keeps people through weeks of blackout—would be left exposed. Your calculations click like a metronome: staggered fallback preserves exits and supplies; a mass all-hands defense risks being punched through and losing both.

Your chest tightens. Elias is not just a strategist; he's the tide-maker in people's hearts. When he calls for courage, half the Terrace answers with hands. The other half looks to you for patterns, for routes, for a way to thread more life through fewer losses.

Elias: (shouting over wind) "Aiko! This isn't the time for spreadsheets! People need hands where the water is coming in!"

Aiko Mori: "I'm not asking us to run. I'm asking us to sequence—close the immediate exits, send med teams to the shelters, route pumps to keep the corridor clear. If we split smart, we stop the cascade."

Elias: (voice breaking on the last word) "Smart? Or slow? Or cowardice dressed up as numbers? My father's boat couldn't be 'strategic' when the line broke. He grabbed, he fought. We do the same."

You feel his words like a wave. They are not arguments only; they are ritual invocation—of losses, of heritage, of fury. For a moment your professional caution feels like betrayal to the memory in his chest.

Mina appears beside you, hair plastered, apron streaked with mud. Her hands are steady; she is a moderate ship between two rocks. "Both of you," she calls, voice low and fierce. "This is where we stop telling each other what we should be, and start doing what keeps people breathing. Aiko—your plans. Elias—you—your surge. Use both. But don't make us pick sides."

Her plea blooms into action: she grabs two volunteers and begins tagging evac routes with glow-tape, voice humming directions. Sora's silhouette is a stone at the Beacon's edge in your periphery, hands raised like a weathered benediction.

Rafi: (breathing hard) "I've got the packet. They were ready to cut public access if the city response interfered with their 'stabilization protocols.' It's not hypothetical—it's in black and white. If their algorithm decides your pumps are a liability, they can deny power reroutes. They planned for this."

The phrase lands like a cold brick. The Terrace goes rigid; you can track the electrical effect across faces. Distrust is a different current than fear—it changes where people point their energy.

You step up to Elias, because he doesn't need you to argue with him across the yard. He meets your eyes; rain runs along his cheek like a thin, luminous scar. For a heartbeat, he looks like the boy who used to salvage nets with his grandfather, not the man who rallies bodies.

You want to reach for his wrist, steady him. Instead your fingers curl around the edge of your notebook as if that will anchor you more effectively.

You choose—or the scene forces you—into calculation. The radio is a humming animal. Your hands work through checklists while your mind runs a dozen models at once. The Terrace's pump network can be rerouted, but not without pulling crews from the Dockside line. If you shift two teams, you can hold the evacuation spine open for another crucial hour. That hour could move a hundred more people uphill.

Elias's voice comes in, raw and pleading. "They're taking the high ground, Aiko. If they clamp access to the main conduit, those corridors become traps. We can't let HelioCorp have the leverage."

You want to shout that you already factored that in; that is the whole point. Instead you find yourself explaining, lungs ragged, to volunteers and to Elias both. Words are thin vessels against the noise, but you press them until they carry.

Aiko Mori: "We pull the red teams—the heavy lifters—from Dockside after they shore the outer two points. We divert two pump lines to the Beacon corridors. Rafi: (grim) "On it. But if they're already gating access, there's a chance they can lock us out mid-route. We need boots on physical relays."

Elias: (exasperated) "Then we put boots there. All of them. Now."

Your throat tightens with an old, private ache—how often the world has asked you to choose between the many and the one. Elias's conviction burns like youth; your insistence is older—shaped by failed grand plans and the calculus of trade-offs.

[Scene: Verdant Terrace — The Surge Strikes]
[Visual: A black wall of water, lit from within by overturned streetlights; seawater rears like a thing alive]
[Music: Crescendo — high strings and metallic percussion, an urgent, ragged rhythm]
[Sound: A long, ravenous roar as the surge hits; wood splintering, the hollow crash of a newly-broken levee]

The first surge finds the Terrace's weakest seam. Sandbags disgorge like spilled guts; ropes snap with a sound like torn fabric. Volunteers are knocked off balance; someone is swept knee-deep and then higher. The air smells of burned plastic as a generator tips, then goes silent.

Sora is shouting old names—prayers and histories braided together—and the words anchor people for a few seconds longer than fear alone would allow. You taste salt and the metallic electricity of panic. Your hands are already moving: toggling a relay, snapping a clamp, shouting the same command until it sticks.

Rafi comes at you, eyes wide, rain on his lashes. "They're trying to trigger a perimeter lock," he says, voice raw. "Signals from HelioCorp to municipal grid—if they get through, our pumps on the lower string will be cut. They've already queued a priority wedge."

The implication is a knife. If HelioCorp can wedge the grid, your staged retreat could unravel into a rout; Elias's all-hands could be strangled mid-charge. The Terrace folds in on itself with too many variables and too few hands.

Elias grabs your shoulder—hard, not angrily but as if he is rooting himself to you. "I don't care about their emails," he says low. "I care about the person who just tumbled off the plank. I care about the kid whose blanket is in that storage box. We hold. We keep them from being swept into some corporate ledger."

Your mind flashes with faces: Mina's laugh, Sora's steady gaze, the teenager with paint on his forearms who held the lamp last week. You remember nights calculating probabilities and waking to find your neighbor's house had been taken by tide; rational plans felt petty then, like apologies to the sea.

You are not immune to the pull of heroism. The surge coaxes it out of you—visceral, pure, terrifying. But your head keeps whispering balance: a staggered fallback will save thresholds and not force a hard choice later. You can see both futures, and both are ragged.

Mina is already moving, calling volunteers: "Two to Beacon, four to corridor Alpha, the rest—shore Dockside." Her voice fractures into command and comfort. She looks at you and Elias like the space between you must be rebuilt with action.

Elias's jaw sets. He lifts his chin and, with an urgency that feels like prayer, says, "We charge Dockside. If we can hold it, we give everyone breathing room."

You count the volunteers with a quick glance; the number inches toward a dangerous deficit. Your chest tightens until talking feels like a gamble.

You are at the fulcrum. The terrace tilts. The rain hammers. The pumps cough.

Everything you have fought for feels both too fragile and too defiant for words.

The choice splits the night into three raw edges.
