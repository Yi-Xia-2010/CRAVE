[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Night]
[Visual: Narrow room of humming racks; condensation beads on concrete; monitors cast teal and red across wet metal]
[Music: Tense BGM — rapid strings under low synth]
[Sound: Rain hammering above; the lab's ventilation thrums; a distant siren folds into the wind]

You are already here because you stayed. The pilot that felt like proof yesterday is a machine of glass and code tonight, and the coils of its breath run through these servers. The air tastes of ozone and salt — a metallic tang that catches on the back of your throat. Your palms are damp, not just from the humidity of the room but from the small, steady exertion of being responsible for more than you ever signed up to hold alone.

A bank of monitors scrolls tidal projections in neon teal. One model blinks to red: a run-rate approaching limits. Red annotations crawl across the screen with the polite brutality of algorithms that don't know people. Every few seconds the system announces a new pressure point; every proclamation feels like a little nail being set.

You have Rowan on your left — composed, pinched into a charcoal coat that makes him look like a decision waiting to be made. He watches the feed the way a conductor watches an orchestra when a wrong note will cascade into a disaster. On your right, Elias leans on a maintenance rack, rain still in the seams of his jacket, his amber eyes sharp and urgent. Elias smells of wet canvas and the herbs Mina baked earlier — a human counterpoint to the sterile electricity humming around you.

Rafi's face is in a monitor above the terminal, wired into the room with that half-mock, half-earnest expression he wears when something important is happening. He looks exhausted and elated, like someone who found a truth he didn't want to find but felt he had to share anyway.

Rafi: "They hit my feed an hour ago. Leaked memos — contingency clauses. Priority override for global contracts in 'Extraordinary Hydrological Events.' That's their language."

You listen to the sentence again in your head, and the words slide under your skin. Extraordinary Hydrological Events. The phrase is both clinical and nuclear in implication: legally-written permission to centralize control when the waters get bad.

Rowan: (hands folded, voice level) "Contingency clauses are standard. HelioCorp's obligation is to preserve the integrity of the system. If we let a localized failure cascade, many more people are put at risk."

Elias: (sharp) "What about the neighborhood? About who gets cut off so a pipeline holds clean? You can't just arithmetic people's lives like that."

Rowan: "We're not arithmetic. We're triaging. The automated containment algorithm is designed to redirect surge away from critical nodes. It's tested. It protects the core infrastructure."

You can see the logic in his face. It's precise, honed. The metal gleam of efficiency. But the leaked memos add a shadow to that gleam — the shadow of contracts that could prioritize distant clients over the street-level promises you made at the Beacon.

You tap the console and the containment schematic unfurls: a lattice of sluices and redirect valves, an elegant geometry of water and force. One toggle is highlighted — "AUTOMATED CONTAINMENT: ENABLE." A legal timestamp cloud hovers nearby: EMERGENCY MANDATE — HelioCorp Provision 7.3.b.

Your chest tightens. There's a physical sensation, like cold spilling into your stomach. The room monitors exhale a fan's rasp; the rain outside sharpens into battering fists against the building overhead.

Elias steps forward. His voice is raw in a way that reaches you differently than the more formal cadences in this room.

Elias: "We call the emergency council. We don't let a corporate clause make the cut without our voices. We did this together — we promised oversight."

Rowan: (a small, practiced concession) "I respect local governance. I promised it in the agreement. But the model's error bands tighten by the minute. Delaying to convene a body that may or may not make a technical call risks overload and an uncontrolled breach."

You remember the Beacon, the ledger, the way hands laid over maps as if to steady them. You remember Sora's soft certainty about the sea listening when people speak together. Those memories are tactile — Mina's hands in flour, the rough paper of your notebook — and they insist in your chest.

Rafi: "If we expose this clause now, the political fallout will be immediate. That will delay any activation long enough for this model to run hot. If we trigger now, HelioCorp's control becomes real in the public record. Both choices burn."

Rowan: "And the system keeps running if we trigger. It buys us time to harden weak points. It's not about giving them power forever — it's triage."

Elias: "Triage that hands the scalpel to them. Aiko — you know what they did in other cities. Processes get written out of middle management and out of neighborhoods."

You: (your throat dry) "How urgent?"

Rowan: "Five minutes. Ten, if the peripheral pumps hold. After that, a cascading feedback loop could reach threshold."

The numbers land like stones. Five. Ten. Time is not a generous thing tonight. Your heartbeat escalates; the lab's cooler kicks, a fan blade thud like a metronome scoring the panic.

The storm intensifies outside, as if the weather is aware of your indecision. Rain hammers the rooftop in a sound that makes speech thin. Monitors flash, the teal curves knotting into a jagged red. You feel the group's attention narrow to you like a lens.

Rowan reaches toward the console. His finger hovers near the enable switch — an almost ceremonial motion that looks, in that light, culpable and inevitable. His face doesn't change much; the calm is not comfort but a force.

Rowan: (quiet, heavy) "If you authorize containment, the system will isolate vulnerable passageways to preserve primary flow. Access routes will be sealed until the emergency clears. It is—"

Elias cuts in, voice breaking on the last word, "—a surrender of agency."

Rowan: "A temporary, necessary surrender."

Elias: (to you, pleading) "We built something that could mean more than statistics. Aiko, you promised the Beacon oversight. Don't let it become a clause."

You watch Elias's hands, fingers splayed like a plea. His eyes are near-raw, complex; you can read longing, fear, and an ache that might be for you and might be for the neighborhood. Your own reflection in the monitor blurs with the schematic lines. You feel pulled in iron ways toward both men — toward the practical calculus of Rowan's certainty and the messy humanity of Elias's insistence on process.

Rafi's feed pings again, a terse blip that feels like a timer: more staff arriving at HelioCorp Atrium; a council member's office calling for briefing; a politician's aide asking whether to intervene. The pilot has already attracted attention — and attention moves in politics like a current that will take what it can.

Sora's voice over the Beacon channel is steady, ancient as the stones: "Speak together, and the sea listens. But speak too slowly, and the sea will answer before you finish."

The air in the subbasement is electric; your decision is no longer merely technical. It will define what the pilot means — whose lives count as infrastructure and whose lives are acceptable collateral.

Your fingers hover over the enable key. The lab seems to hold its breath with you.
