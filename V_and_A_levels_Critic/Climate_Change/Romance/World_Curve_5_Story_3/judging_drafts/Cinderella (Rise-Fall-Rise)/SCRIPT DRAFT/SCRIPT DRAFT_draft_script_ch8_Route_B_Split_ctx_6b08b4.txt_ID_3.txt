You arrive breathless, lungs burning with salt and cold. The chant that carried you at the staging gate is already fragmented into shouts: names, directions, a woman cursing at a pump. The air smells sharp with salt and diesel and the faint, bleeding sweetness of crushed greens. Rain pelts your jacket in a steady hiss; the Terrace lights bob like small, stubborn stars.

Someone screams—high and raw—and the concrete barrier nearest the inlet shudders, then collapses with a sound like a fist hitting a plate. Water lands on the beds in sheets, overturning a row of planters. The first surge finds purchase in the places you thought might hold.

You don't reason first. You move. Your fingertips find the wet rope that binds a palisade; your boots sink into cold sludge. The surge is greedy: it takes anything light and anything left unlashed. Volunteers clamp hands to rotting boards; a child who was helping is pulled back by an adult with a fierce, animal shove. You taste iron and the memory of other tides—how quickly small breaks cascade into catastrophe.

Rafi races up, raincoat slapped against him, eyes wide behind a plastic visor. He holds out his tablet like evidence.

Rafi: (panting) "Listen—this just came through a secure channel. It's not a leak; it's a contingency file. Rowan's team has clauses that—"

You: "Tell me plain. Did they plan to lock us out of pumps and gates?"

Rafi: "Not in those words. It says 'access restriction protocols' activate under 'public safety triggers.' They model abrupt closures of urban access points—transport-lanes, water-control nodes—if thresholds exceed certain risk vectors. In plain English: they can cut power or access to infrastructure and claim it's for safety."

A sick, cold slide runs under your ribs. The Terrace's pumps stutter behind Rafi—three independent units clattering as they fight intake. You imagine a dark grid where someone else, far away and polished, flicks switches while people here hold ropes.

Mina grabs your sleeve, voice pitched low and urgent.

Mina: "If they can shut the pumps, we'll drown supply lines. We can't give them a reason to call it 'safety.'"

You: (voice tight) "Then we don't give them that reason. We shore what we can and move what we must."

Whatever you choose, the Terrace demands it all. Volunteers peel off in ragged lines—some go to pumps, some to bucket brigades, others sling sandbags with hands raw and shaking. The radios are a chorus of clipped commands and bursts of white noise: "Breach two!—No, that's three—Evac route C—medic—" Lines cross; no single voice can be heard over the storm.

Elias finds you between rows of overturned beds, rain threading through his hair. His eyes are a furnace under the wet lashes—he is all forward motion, jaw set like a blade.

Elias: "We hold the breach at sector three. If we let that through, Verdant—our food stores—everything—"

You: (cutting in) "Sector three is a dead end if they hit the culvert below. The pumps are drooping at sector two; if we leave the evacuation corridors, people get trapped. My numbers—"

Elias: "Numbers won't stop that wall. People will. Hands will. We need everyone at sector three, now."

You: "If everyone floods there and the culvert fails, we lose the corridor and five blocks get cut off. We can’t gamble the entire Terrace on courage alone."

Elias: (angry, soft) "Is that all I am to you, Aiko? A gamble?"

You: (your voice flaring) "No. But 'all-hands' and 'all-at-once' are different words. I'm trying to save people, Elias. I want less loss."

He looks at you like you are a geometry problem with a heart. The rain makes his face almost luminous.

Elias: "Saving people means be where the need is. You always—" (he stops, breath ragged) "You plan retreats. Sometimes standing is the only way to keep us from running."

You: "And sometimes retreat saves more than stubbornness. We owe it to everyone to choose the smarter fight."

He snatches a length of rope from your hands, palms raw.

Elias: "Then we choose. Now."

Your chest tightens. This is not only about tactics. It's always never only about tactics. Elias's faith in direct action has pulled this neighborhood out of grief before; your calculations have kept whole blocks from needless risk. The ledger you both write is fraying under a deluge.

Sora's voice floats to you across the chaos—thin, but steady.

Sora: "There was a time the shore took everything. We learned where to stand. We learned when to move the hearth. Your hearts both remember; your heads must not forget."

Mina, kneeling by a small generator, calls out.

Mina: "Pumps at forty percent—pressure dropping. We can keep them if we split power. But that means field lights go, radios go—less coordination."

You: "We can stagger power cycles. Rotate comms through critical nodes. It's not ideal, but it's something. Rafi, can you patch a low-band channel? Route messages through handiest repeaters?"

Rafi: (fingers flying) "On it. But if HelioCorp flips an access bit, those repeaters go silent. I can't be sure we won't lose even these."

You close your eyes for half a breath and think in fragments—flow rates, volunteer stamina, the geometry of the Terrace beds, where people live above the flood plain, the sequence of gates that lead inland. Your mind maps sacrifice like a ledger: here you lose a narrow block but keep the hospital corridor open; there you hold ground and risk sealing a supply warehouse.

A shout splits the air—Mina's voice, sharp as a shell.

Mina: "There! Sector four—another wall down. Food stores flooding. Children are near that alley."

Your stomach drops like an anchor. You see a cluster of volunteers on the far boardwalk, flailing as a wave takes the outermost planks. You can run a simulation in your head and see it end two ways: with a brave, concentrated shove that may push the water back for minutes; or with a staggered pull that gets children and elders out but lets a bed of lettuces and several communal stockboxes go under, maybe irreparably.

Elias grabs your arm, urgency burning.

Elias: "We charge. We put everyone at sector four and we push. If we hold it, Verdant stays. If not—"

You: (cold) "If we do that, and the culvert below sector three fails, the evacuation corridor collapses. We might save Verdant and lose three neighborhoods. The math isn’t symmetric."

He glares at you, a flash of hurt edged in anger.

Elias: "So you choose other people's losses over my hands? Over mine?"

You: "No—" You want to say that you choose to save as many as possible, even if it means pain here. You want to say that you trust Elias's courage but cannot let sentiment write the ledger when lives are at scale. The words taste thin.

Rafi pushes through, rain streaking his face, tablet clutched like a talisman.

Rafi: (urgent) "Guys—listen. I can corroborate the HelioCorp doc. They modeled exactly this. Their contingency maps show prioritized urban arteries. If they enact the protocol, they'll isolate zones for 'stabilization'—and those zones don't include Verdant."

Your stomach flips. Every move is now doubled by the knowledge that an external actor could turn tactical choices into moral verdicts. If HelioCorp pulls remote levers and defines safety, how much of your choice even matters?

Sora: (soft but hard as rock) "Then they take; we protect. Both can be true. We act with what we have."

You inhale the cold water-scented air and feel the arousal ratchet to a white heat: the Terrace thrums with effort; someone kneels and sobs; a child screams for a lost kitten; the pumps stutter as if tired of fighting. Lightning brushes the horizon, and every sound sharpens: rope slaps, wood cracks, a portable siren stutters.

You run a last, clean permutation in your head: consolidate forces at sector four (Elias's plan), execute a staged retreat prioritizing evacuation corridors (your plan), or split teams to hold and evacuate simultaneously (a third, risk-heavy path). Each path has a simple ledger entry: lives saved, lives exposed, infrastructure kept or voided. Each path will mark who you are as a leader, and how the neighborhood will remember you and Elias.

Your hands are still wet from the rope. Mina's fingers are callused and shaking. Rafi's tablet chirps with another fragment of data; he looks at you as if asking permission to make the leak public now.

Your throat tightens; the Terrace hums and splays into fragments of decision.
