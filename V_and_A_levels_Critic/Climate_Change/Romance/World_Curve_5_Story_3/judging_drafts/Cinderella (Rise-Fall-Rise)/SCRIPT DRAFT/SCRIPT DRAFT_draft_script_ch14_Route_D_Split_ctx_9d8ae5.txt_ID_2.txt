[Visual: Tight on concrete stairs slick with condensation; LED console panels cast teal and red reflections on pooling water]

You came down for an action that was supposed to be precise: a quiet cut, a physical delay, a spectacle to buy the Beacon time. You told yourself the devil in the details would be tangential—that courage would be a set of calm hands and a plan. The plan is drowning now.

The subbasement smells of ozone and salt and something burned at the edge of memory. The servers hum like a hive. The hum fractures when the first valve shivers and a thin red banner blooms across the nearest monitor: PRESSURE ANOMALY — BACKFLOW DETECTED.

Elias's voice is a chopped radio-breath in your ear, raw and too bright. "They're turning a flush on the main. That can't— Aiko, the surface—"

Aiko Mori: (cutting in, voice low, steady) "We hold the feed. Rafi, get the camera on their crew. Mina, check med kits—"

Mina: "On it." (She sounds small in the roar, hands moving) "People—watch your ankles. The stairwell's already slick."

Rafi: (overlaid, breathless) "Helio's techs are pushing hydraulics from the promenade. They've got municipal teams with them—this is not a coincidence."

You inhale and taste salt so sharp it stings your teeth. Water ticks and grows louder, a living thing sliding over concrete. It finds the cracks you haven't thought to seal. A skitter of sparks arcs from a dying junction box; a screen flashes a cascade of error codes. The lab's lights strobe; the emergency strips at the ceiling sputter.

Sora's voice comes in thin, brittle over the comm—she's anchored at the Beacon but somehow still a presence, as if the coastline itself is offering counsel. "Remember the old beaconkeepers," she says. "You can't save everything. Choose what the shore would choose."

You hold that like a stone in your mouth, its weight heavy and unavoidable.

[Visual: Close on your hand bracing against a console as a spray of water climbs the casing; your wristband whips loose, drenched]
[Sound: The console emits a single, sustained alert tone; the building seems to inhale and then exhale water]

Rafi: "The backflow's routed into the salvage tanks—no, it's breached a bypass. We're watching pressure spike in the lower manifolds."

Elias: "If the valves seize, those control loops will polarize. They trigger emergency seals; the servers dump cooling fluids. Glass will shatter—"

Aiko Mori: (your voice edges sharper than you expect, because you can feel the ledger of the Beacon in every chest around you) "Then we go manual. We stop it at the node. We stop the valves."

Elias: (a beat, then harder) "Aiko, there's no way Helio wouldn't design for failsafes. If you go in there—"

Aiko Mori: (interrupting) "If I don't, their design will flood the data, take the models we have, and lock them away. If I do, maybe we buy more time. And maybe—" Your throat tightens—"—maybe I keep this from being another story of lost evidence."

[Visual: Wide on the lab as water begins to sluice across the floor in a low black tide; server light panels blink like wounded creatures]
[Sound: A plate of something metal clatters somewhere above; a distant megaphone muffled by concrete]

Mina moves beside you with a med kit mushrooming open, her fingers trembling. "You don't have to be the only one to risk it, Aiko. We can—"

You meet Elias's eyes across the mess of cables, and the air between you is a chaotic charge. He looks younger than he is: a bruise of fear coloring his amber gaze. He tries to smile; it rips into something that isn't a smile.

Elias: "We go together. We patch, we pull, we shove—whatever you need—"

You can see the way his hands flex, the camera strap cutting into his shoulder, the promise of blunt contact in his posture. You can see Mina poised to obey. You can see the ladder leading down into the node, slick with dark water.

You think of Sora's hands knitting rope from old nets, of Mina's soup ladles, of the Beacon's ledger—of all the quiet, patient pieces that made this moment possible. You think of Dr. Rowan Hale in a suit that never gets salt stains, of HelioCorp's polished compass, of contracts stacked like levees against the sea's truth.

Your heart speeds and your breath shortens until every intake is a hot, shallow thing. The lab's hum tilts into a high, brittle note. The emergency lighting flips to red and casts everyone in the room as a silhouette of alarm.

The micro-choice leaves a ringing in your bones. Whether you pulled them back or lunged first, the lab responds the same: pumps ping, a faraway metallic shriek sounds, and a line of steam hisses where hot coolant meets cold seawater.

Elias closes the distance in two long strides, calling out. "If you go in alone, I won't—" His voice breaks, stops. He reaches for you, fingers seeking purchase on your sleeve.

Aiko Mori: (you answer him out loud, because some things cannot be held inside) "You won't have a choice." (Then, quieter) "You and I—we both know how this ledger reads. Someone has to stay with the truth."

Elias: (both furious and pleading) "That's not how this works, Aiko. Not alone. Not like this. We planned—"

Aiko Mori: "Plans end the second something starts to drown everything we planned for."

Rafi is shouting into his recorder; Mina is barking medical triage at the edge of the rising water; Sora's voice is a thin thread that won't be cut. The alarms reach a new octave. The server lights strobe red through rising reflections. Sparks eat at a cable junction like a small, angry lightning.

You can feel the lab tilt inside your chest—gravity isn't a law in here so much as a rumor. You plant your boot toe on a raised floor tile and feel it slip. Cold water licks the hem of your trousers. Your silver hair streak—your small signature of defiance—presses wet against your temple, salt crusting under the smear.

Time fractures into a sequence of tiny, amplified moments: a wet gleam on a handrail; Elias's fingers brushing yours; the hiss of a coolant line failing; Mina shouting a name you can't hear over the noise; the console lights scrolling, red and then gone. You think of the ledger again—pages you haven't filled. You think of the neighborhood. You think of all the little ledgers stacked hopeful and trembling.

The water rises halfway up your calves. A server rack goes black with an ominous, final blink; cooling fans wind down and the air loses its hum, replaced by the roar of moving water and the sharp breathing of people pressed close.

Elias reaches you again. He grabs your forearm with both hands, knuckles whitening. "Don't," he says, almost a plea. "Don't do this alone."

You could listen. You could step back and let the lab drown and the models with it. You could herd everyone up through the stairwell into the bite of cold night and leave the servers to Helio's clean hands. You could let the Beacon burn with outrage but empty of the data you need to prove a point.

Instead, a plan no one else has time to voice unspools. You see the physical fail-safe—an exposed panel jammed with a misrouted valve, a mechanical hinge that, if held, will allow the automated failsafes to re-sequence and isolate the breaches. It needs continuous pressure. It needs someone to keep contact while scripts run, sensors re-align, a chain of coldly designed logic finishes closing. It sounds like something a single human could hold long enough for the algorithms to do the rest.

You stare at Elias. His hands tremble where they grip you. You taste iron in your mouth—blood or fear, you can't tell anymore.

Aiko Mori: (soft, certain) "If I secure the panel and hold it, the failsafes can finish the cycle. They'll have time to trigger the isolation. But it will seal me in—electric, water—" Your voice narrows into a shard. "It won't be quick."

Elias's eyes go somewhere beyond the lab, trying to find a path to the future that doesn't twist you into a story that ends in a name on a ledger. He opens his mouth, closes it. Words crowd and fall away.

Elias: (whisper, breaking) "No, Aiko—"

You can feel the room cleave between two timelines: one where you shove everyone out and live to argue tomorrow; the other where you stay and buy their lives with your body. The beacon inside you that manages ledgers and maps and marginalia doesn't calm. It shouts: protect the town, protect the truth. The part of you that is a woman who has loved and been loved throbs like a wound.

Mina presses something—no, she presses both palms flat to your shoulders. "Don't you—" She can't finish. "We need you."

Rafi's recorder is a constant rasp. "This is live. This is everything."

Sora's voice returns as if carried up from the shore: "Aiko. No one can ask you to be a lighthouse. You choose."

You know that's false. They have asked. They always ask. The sea asks. The city asks. Your ledger page flips, a gust of salt-scented wind in the dark.

Your hands find the edge of the panel. Metal bites your palms. It wants to separate. If you wedge yourself—if you wedge your body—there will be a window for the logic to run, for the processors to isolate circuits, for the network to lock the models in an immutable archive.

You look at Elias one last time, and you can see everything that will not be if you let fear win. You see him at the Beacon later, hands raw, voice a low, private roar. You see Mina in a kitchen handing out bowls to trembling people. You see Sora still stubborn in her shawls. You see the ledger, and beyond it, the coastline holding its breath.

Your chest is a tight room. The alarms crescendo. Your world narrows to the taste of salt and the friction of your sleeve under Elias's fingers.
