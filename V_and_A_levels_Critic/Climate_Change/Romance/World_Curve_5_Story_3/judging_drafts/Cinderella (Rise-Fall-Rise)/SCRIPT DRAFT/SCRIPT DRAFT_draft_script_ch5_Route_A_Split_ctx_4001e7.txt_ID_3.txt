[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Night]
[Visual: Narrow concrete room lined with racks of servers; condensation beads on fiber conduits; monitors blink teal and red]
[Music: Staccato synth pulses, a frantic rhythm undercut by distant thunder]
[Sound: A high, insistent alarm tone; wet concrete creaking; the rush of water far below]

You are at the console because that's where people come when things get thin enough that decisions need to be anchored to something physical. The pilot's green light—so steady earlier—now stutters in a way that feels like a held breath breaking.

Your fingers hover above the keys. The air tastes of metal and ozone. The room vibrates with the servers' hum; every rack throws off heat like a bad fever. On the nearest screen, the levee-monitor feed has gone from steady to jagged: a spike, then a collapsing line. A sensor pings one message, then another, faster: secondary levee—scour event—breach probable.

You can feel the ledger of the Beacon in your pocket, the memory of meetings where "conditional" and "pause authority" were written down with careful hands. That ledger feels small and inadequate now, a strip of paper against the roar.

Rafi (over the comms): "Aiko—data's spiking. Secondary breach at sector five. Salt intrusion at the ducted feed. If water hits the subbasement conduits, we lose redundancy in a heartbeat."

Aiko Mori: "Estimate timeline."

Rafi: (voice clipped) "Ten minutes, maybe less. Pumps are overworking. If those fail, servers will go thermal and—" (he swallows) "—we'll have cascading node failures citywide."

You press a hand to the console, mapping hypothetical timelines against the raw, live telemetry. Against the grain of the graphs, your intuitions—trained by years of modeling and the smell of sea storms—are humming urgent warnings. You can see two futures from here: one where HelioCorp's automated protocols snap clamps and isolate the network to save the system, another where humans on the ground shove sandbags and pray the pumps hold long enough.

[Visual: Split-screen—on one side, the HelioCorp network diagram; on the other, a live feed of Tideward Promenade with flashing lanterns and people silhouetted against rain]
[Sound: Comms chatter overlapping; a Child's shout somewhere far away]

Rowan Hale: (coming through a secure line, low and controlled) "Aiko. We have authority to execute the lockdown. It's the only way to prevent systemic collapse."

Aiko Mori: (you) "Lockdown of what, precisely? The pumps? The access protocols? If you isolate the infrastructure, volunteers can't redirect water manually. Mina's kitchen—"

Rowan Hale: (interrupting) "Humanitarian corridors can be arranged. Secure the servers first. If the data and control architecture collapse, we lose the ability to coordinate any response. A centralized short-term containment saves more lives."

Your stomach twists. The logic is surgical. The sentence is delivered like an inevitability. But your models—your gut—are screaming about immediate physical harm at the promenade, about Mina's wood stove and Mina herself outmanning a pump because someone has to keep the community fed if the kitchens flood.

Elias: (bursting into the feed, breath hot through static) "No. You don't get to 'arrange corridors' from a glass atrium while my neighbors are pulling wet sand. Aiko, we're here. We can hold the line if folks have access to the pumps."

Rowan Hale: (measured) "Elias, your courage is noted and admirable. Courage is not a substitute for systems. Authorize the lockdown and we maintain stability."

Elias: (angry, pleading) "Stability for whom, Rowan? For servers? For your models? These are people's homes."

The line snaps electric with friction. You keep listening to both sides like a seismograph. Both arguments are technically right in their way; both risks are existential now.

[Scene: Tideward Promenade | Night]
[Visual: Wooden platforms slick with rain, tarps whipping; amber lanterns bob and cast wet halos]
[Music: Rapid percussive drumming, heartbeats layered beneath the storm]
[Sound: The roar of a scouring current; calls and shouts; the slap of canvas]

You run with a tarp slung over one shoulder. The wind strips breath from your lungs. Mina's kitchen sits on a raised patch of concrete; the door is half-latched. Inside, steam and the smell of stock—rooted and human—fight the metallic bite of the sea.

Mina: (hands up, shouting) "We can't lose the cooker! The kids—"

Elias: (shoulder to shoulder with neighbors, pulling a hose) "Make space for people. We shore here and here. We need more hands on the east buttress."

Sora: (steady, voice like weathered rope) "Move as one. Not as many separate motions—one rhythm."

Volunteers pull at sandbags that are waterlogged and heavy enough to tear arms. Your sleeve is torn; salt stings a raw spot. Rain soaks the knuckles you use to shift canvas, to wedge planks. The breach is wider than you hoped. A current is already eddying, greedy.

You glance down the shoreline. The HelioCorp drones—sleek, beady—hover on the edge of visibility, their sensors blinking blue. In the atrium's light, their outlines are clinical, beautiful, and insulated. They cannot feel the weight of a child's small hand.

Elias finds you and grabs your arm, eyes bright with wet fire.

Elias: "They've locked access to the pump controls. Rowan says 'protocol' but people are on the line. Aiko—you can still authorize a manual override from the lab."

Aiko Mori: (you) "And hand over the city's controls to them? They will centralize everything under their terms."

Elias: (voice breaking, urgent) "Aiko. Right now it's sandbags and sweat. If you don't let us in, we lose houses. We lose lives. We lose Mina's kitchen. You taught me to trust evidence—right now, evidence is hands doing the work."

Your jaw tightens. It's impossible to reconcile all the values at once. The rain makes decisions feel both cruel and simple: stop the water, or save the system that coordinates salvation. You feel a fracture opening—not in the levee only, but between promises you made to your neighbors and the agreements you made in a fluorescent room.

[Scene: HelioCorp Glass Atrium | Night]
[Visual: Vast glass panels fogging from within; vertical gardens that look like curated promises]
[Music: A thin, almost surgical synth line; a low brass chord that refuses to resolve]
[Sound: The faint pop of relays; the hush of controlled climate systems]

Rowan stands alone by a window that shows the storm like a live painting. In the atrium, everything is orderly: potted ferns, immaculate walkways, a projection of the city's model in crisp cyan. He looks down at the live feeds with the calm of a person who organizes rescue by removing emotion from the equation.

Rowan Hale: (to himself, then into his earpiece) "Execute lockdown alpha. Seal nonessential access. Initiate controlled fail-safe pathways."

Assistant (filtered): "Acknowledged. HelioCorp central will assume remote control."

Rowan's hand rests on the brass compass in his pocket for a second—a small, private ritual. Then he activates the lockdown. Somewhere, an automated voice overlays the city's channels: "Authorized override engaged. Infrastructure access restricted to emergency personnel."

Rowan Hale: (over comms, to you) "Aiko. You're responsible for the pause authority language. I need your authorization to proceed."

Aiko Mori: (you) "You know my conditions. Open audits, data sovereignty clauses—"

Rowan Hale: (softly) "We will uphold transparency. For now, the system must be prioritized."

You can hear the strain in his attempt to reassure. It does not explain the way his face hardens or how the atrium's light sharpens into an accusation. There is a sense of inevitability in his voice that feels like betrayal to those hands in the rain.

Rafi: (white-faced, panning between terminals) "We're losing the feeder. Backup arrays are engaging but if that breach widens—"

Sora: (hands braced against a console, voice low) "We can't watch both. We never could. Not with this storm."

Mina: (through the doorway, shouting) "They're telling us 'authorized corridors'—but the corridors aren't here! There is water at the steps!"

Elias: (on the line, raw) "They closed the pump consoles. We tried to open them manually—locked. People are running out of strength."

Your models spread across the display like maps of grief. One line shows HelioCorp's controlled, prioritized containment. Another shows the physical reality outside your window. The two lines diverge into contradiction. In your chest, anger and terror crossbreed.

You think of all the small promises you made in the Beacon—quorum rules, audits, pause language—and how they were meant to be safeguards, not a shield to be used from above. You did not sign up to be the hinge between glass towers and wet hands. Yet here you are, and here everyone is, and the choice compresses into a single moment where your throat feels dry and loud.

The pumps stutter. A server rack flashes a cascading failure warning—heat thresholds breached, power draw erratic.

Rafi: "We can try to reroute control locally. It's risky—could corrupt the dataset. Could lose months of telemetry. But if we don't divert power to auxiliary pumps, the conduits will flood."

Rowan Hale: (over comms, voice paper-strong) "If you trigger manual access, you must authorize commands that will allow remote access to all control nodes. That will grant HelioCorp a complete admin footprint."

Elias: (voice breaking through) "Admin footprint or not, right now the water is taking someone's home."

You feel the room tilt. The storm's sound is a physical thing pressing against the building. Your hands find the ledger in your jacket—pages damp at the corner. You read the words you wrote to yourself in calmer hours: transparency, pause authority, community agency. The letters blur with rain.

Your mouth tastes of iron. You can already imagine how each decision will be narrated later—your name attached to a line on a ledger: authorized, led, split. None of the outcomes look clean. All of them will hurt.

You breathe.

Everything sharpens to a single, unbearable focus.
