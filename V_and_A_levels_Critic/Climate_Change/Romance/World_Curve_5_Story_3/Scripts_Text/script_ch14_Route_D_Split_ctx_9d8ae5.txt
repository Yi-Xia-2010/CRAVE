[Scene: Subbasement Data Lab | Night]
[Visual: Low, clinical LEDs reflected in wet concrete; panels with neon teal tidal models; a single gold light from the Beacon pendant through a grate above]
[Music: Tense BGM — restless strings, a driving high-tempo beat]
[Sound: Distant sirens; the building creaks as distant waves push at foundations; a steady, mechanical hum of servers undercut by sudden alarm chirps]

You are still riding the echo from the Beacon—its lamp a stubborn dot in your mind—but now that light is a thin filament against this room's cold, buzzing heart. The air tastes metallic and oily, like a kitchen over a storm. Condensation beads on the underside of the cable trays and drips with an insistent, ridiculous rhythm onto the concrete below.

Rafi crouches beside a rack, fingers moving with practiced impatience over a recorder and a slate of emergency logs. He spits a curse you feel more than hear: the sound bites of panic compressed into a syllable.

Rafi: "The control node's actuation feed is misaligned. It's routing to the wrong pressure manifold—look at this telemetry, Aiko, it's cascading to the lower conduit."

You lean closer to the screen. Neon models flicker: lines turn red, then blink into frantic orange. You want data to be a steady thing—numbers you can interrogate, systems you can translate into steps. The models aren't obedient tonight. They twitch and collapse.

Aiko Mori [breathing through it]: "Show me the valve map. Which manifold is being pressurized?"

Rafi: "Lower B—no wait—the bypass clamp's stuck in a half-loop. Helio's remote lock tried to reroute and it failed. Water's heading down the service shaft, not out."

The hum grows teeth. Somewhere above you, someone shouts into a comm—Elias's voice slices through with the rawness of someone on the surface being shoved by boots, but present enough to steady you.

Elias Chen [on comms]: "Aiko! The barrier's breached at Promenade West. Officers—crowd's being pushed back. We need that node off now—before they hit manual override and lock it again."

You taste salt and guilt. You hear Mina's steps, softer, clutching a med pack that suddenly seems like a child's toy. Sora's voice, moved like an old tide memory, murmurs from the doorway where she lingers with a hand on the stone.

Sora Watanabe: "Machines remember what we forget. Make your choice quick—water doesn't wait."

[Visual: A panel's red LED flashes, then a dull pop blinks somewhere deeper; a thin line of water begins to run along the baseboard]
[Sound: A hatch clangs open above; a far-off megaphone cracks]

Your hands—your hands remember ledger margins and tide maps, not soaked circuitry. Still, you move. You are the practical center your neighbors look to when the city frays.

Aiko Mori [to the team]: "Rafi, lock the network readouts to manual. Elias, can you pirate the actuator's handshake? Mina, keep the med corridor clear. Sora—watch the shaft, call any movement. I can try a manual recalibration at the panel, but I need clear access."

Elias Chen: "No—Aiko, don't—"

His protest is not a whisper; it's the sound of someone trying to pull a friend back from an edge. You know the inflection—he's already imagining the hurt in his hands. You almost answer with dry humor, but the humor drowns in the alarms.

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Focus on the servers — triage data to prevent long-term loss"
> Choice B: "Head straight for the valve controls — water first, data second"
[Outcome A: You slow your breath and push the servers' cooling priorities up one notch, fingers leaving faint steam prints on the glass. The logs stammer but hold a moment longer.]
[Outcome B: You pivot toward the valve bank, boots hitting a puddle. A metallic tang fills your mouth; the valve wheel is warmer than you expect, already slick.]

Your foot slips in shallow water and for the first time that night your body registers danger in slow, small increments. Then the increments add together and become a wall.

The bypass manifold explodes like a wounded animal—sharp, immediate sound. Water shears from a seam in the lower conduit, a high-pressure sheet that turns the dim corridor into a silver tunnel. It's not rain; it's an intent. It slams into the back of a server rack and the circuit breakers sing into life with pop and flash. Sparks spider across wet fiber.

Sound becomes a blade: someone yells, you bark back orders, and the room answers with mechanical groans. The emergency lights strobe; a thin, toxic whisper of ozone claws at your throat.

Mina [voice pitched thin, steadying]: "Get people out—get them out now. Rafi, camera—record everything—"

Rafi: "Yes—I'm on it. But if we leave the panel, the failsafes won't trigger. The node's tied to physical engagement—if the panel floods it scrubs the logs and locks the interface."

You should not be the one to make that calculation; you are not supposed to be the fulcrum of a city's safety. But there you are, ledger warm in a jacket pocket, the little solar pendant tapping against your rib like a second heartbeat.

People depend on choices that are not mine alone, and yet here—now—they fall to me. The ledger's margins are full of collective lines and signatures. Tonight the signature asks for flesh.

Elias appears in the doorway in a flash of rain-dark jacket and frantic eyes, camera hanging from one shoulder like a mute witness. He moves closer than protocol allows, close enough that you can see the salt trace at the corner of his mouth—where sea and time have marked him.

Elias Chen: "Aiko, listen—there's protocol to burn this. Pull everyone out, seal the shaft, let the municipal crews handle the node. You won't be able to hold it yourself."

Aiko Mori: "If the node floods, HelioCorp can claim sabotage and erase the telemetry. They will bury the ledger. People on the promenade will be left with nothing. I—"

Elias Chen: "You can't be everywhere. Not tonight. Not ever."

There is pleading here, undercut with a private history—his family's harbor and your maps overlapping like two weathered charts.

Mina steps between you like a small stubborn reef.

Mina: "We get one person in. Or none. No heroics that cost more than one pair of boots. If Aiko goes, we lose cred and the only mechanic who can talk tech to the contractors."

The argument fractures into shards. You answer with the thing you always keep: clarity, not grand gesture.

Aiko Mori: "No—if we evacuate now and the node resets the conduits, Helio's black box will look like the lab failed by itself. We need the physical engagement sequence to complete so the failsafes can be triggered remotely. Someone has to keep the panel stable long enough."

[INTERACTION]
> Choice A: "Override the automated seal and trust the manual failsafe"
> Choice B: "Insist on a buddy team — two people at the panel, one to watch the shaft"
[Outcome A: You flip the override switch with a fingernail that shakes. The panel clicks and hums as relays reroute; for a half-breath everything seems aligned.]
[Outcome B: You call for a partner; Elias reaches, but Mina blocks him with a hard look. Sora lifts her chin, age and experience mapped in the way she breathes.]

You move to the failing panel because that is the grammar you know—the hardware gives back to hands in a way meetings do not. Your jacket drips onto the floor. Your silver streak, usually a thin, defiant slash at your temple, is plastered with salt and sweat; it glints hot against the lab lights.

Your fingers find the main housing. The metal is slick. The manual recalibration wheel resists at first, then gives with a sound like a small, defeated animal. Sparks dance under your fingertips. The tactile reality of the machine anchors you: metal, thread, resistance. You anchor the wheel so the automatic calibration can take its micro-steps.

A shriek from a nearby rack; a vent door blows with the force of the incoming tide. Something arcs—blue-white—across a conduit and you flinch. Your boot slides. The cement is a polished mirror of shallow water. You taste iron.

Elias Chen [shouting, voice breaking]: "Aiko—get out! I'll—I'll hold the aperture—"

He lunges. His hand finds your arm, strong and warm in the cold chaos, and for a second the world is nothing but the sound of his breath and the staccato of alarms. His other hand tries to steady the wheel, fingers fumbling against your palm.

Aiko Mori [struggling to hold both the wheel and his hand]: "Elias—don't. If you pull me, the cycle breaks—"

Elias Chen [voice torn]: "Then we both stay. We both—"

Mina grabs at Elias's sleeve, desperate and practical.

Mina: "If both of you stay, both of you die. We can't afford that. We can't—"

Rafi's recorder is a tiny, whining thing in the storm; he keeps it aimed with a shaking arm, not because the file will matter to the present, but because stories need witnesses even when they cost.

Rafi: "Listen—you can trigger a manual lock if the panel stays in contact for thirty seconds. It will cut off the manifold and open the purge vents. Thirty seconds."

Thirty seconds. Time becomes a narrow, granular thing you measure by the beat of your teeth.

Your chest tightens. You have a ledger of commitments, but you also have a life threaded with people—Mina's apron, Sora's shawls, Elias's laugh. The room compresses to this small math: the panel, the wheel, the slick floor, Elias's hand.

If I let go, the node will drown and HelioCorp will own the narrative. If I hold, I sign something no ledger can undo. If both of us try, we might die together and erase even the witness. Choices have faces tonight, and every face is someone you love.

The water surges again, higher, colder. Electric arcs begin to trace the racks in irregular, violent patterns. A hot, chemical smell—thermal plastic—joins the ozone. The panel threatens to rip free, joints grinding as water wedges itself into places metal was not meant to meet it.

Your fingers slip. For a heartbeat you think of the Beacon's lamp, the way the neighborhood had leaned in that first meeting night, the small stamped confidence of people who built their own life-lines. You think of Sora's story of the old harbor, of a man who tied his boat to a stone and refused to leave even as the tide took the dock. You think of the ledger's margins where signatures blur.

Elias's hand tightens. His thumb presses into the tendon at the base of your wrist; his other hand scrabbles for leverage. Your world narrows to skin on skin, metal in palm, water in lungs—almost.

[Sound: A cavernous groan; the conduit above you explodes with the force of a small hurricane]

Everything accelerates. Time fractures into jagged, high-speed shards: the wheel tears free a hair; a server case collapses sideways; a cable snaps and lashes like a living thing; the panel begins to tilt outward under the push of water. You can almost see the moment as actors in a play do—the "before," the "now," the "after"—and you understand, with a clarity that is cruel, where you stand in the choreography.

Mina screams. Sora's hands fly to her mouth. Rafi's camera is a tiny, pitiful lighthouse in the storm.

Elias Chen [voice raw]: "Get out. Please—get out!"

Aiko Mori [answering, not fully steady]: "I can't—I won't—"

Elias Chen reaches for your face, not to pull you but to find you in the noise.

Elias Chen [whisper]: "I love you. Don't make me—"

Your boots lose traction; one slides out from under you. You sink a fraction. The panel tilts more. The failsafe requires contact, stability, a human mass to hold the circuit closed and allow the remote handlers to handshake into its safety protocol.

You understand what must be done. You understand it with the ruthless, terrible logic that you spent a life building: the ledger, the Beacon meetings, the small, accumulative work of trust. You also understand the human cost as sharp and immediate as salt.

Everything in you narrows to a decision that will break the night's arc.
