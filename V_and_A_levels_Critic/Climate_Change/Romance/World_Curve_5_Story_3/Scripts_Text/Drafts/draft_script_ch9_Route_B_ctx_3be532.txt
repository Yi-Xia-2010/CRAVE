### DRAFT
# Route: Route B


[Scene: Verdant Terrace | Night — During the storm]
[Visual: Rain sheeting across raised beds; string lights bob like slow heartbeat]
[Music: High-tempo strings with steady percussion — urgent, hopeful]
[Sound: Wind lashing, tarp flapping, distant drone whine]

The Terrace is a beast of water and movement but it is our beast, and tonight you know the rhythm of its breathing. Mud clings to your boots; a vine that held yesterday's lettuce droops heavy and sodden in your hands. Every surface smells of wet compost and salt—familiar, stubborn, alive.

Rafi’s face flickers on a patched tablet in your palm, haloed by static. His voice cuts through the wind, sharp and impossibly calm.

Rafi: "Override is live. They're starting the closure sequence now. You have ninety seconds before mains drop if they engage full lockdown."

Time wedges itself thinner, a sliver you can hold only by clenching your teeth. You count tasks against the seconds — which pumps to keep alive, which roofs need the emergency ropes, who must move now. Your ledger runs, not as numbers but as people: Mina's steadfast hands, Sora's slow clear voice, the teenager who learned to splice a hose in two nights.

Elias is already moving, all forward motion and barked orders, eyes bright with raw, frantic care. He catches your sleeve and pulls you into the center of the work like you belong there by gravity.

Elias: (breathless) "Split. You take the north ring with Mina and Lin — hold the pumps. I’ll take the terraces and the ramp. Rafi, keep that feed live. Sora, shepherd the elders."

There is no asking here. You nod, the agreement immediate and warm against the rain. The Terrace is a map you can read by feel, and your hands remember where reinforcements must go.

You shoulder a coil of worn rope, its fibers slick. Your skin prickles with the cold and with a rising, fierce electricity — not fear so much as a clean, sharp resolve.

You: (thinking) This is what we trained for. This is what we practiced with two-bucket drills and late-night mock evacuations. It is messy. It is not perfect. It is ours.

[Visual: Close on your hands knotting a bridle; water glints on the braided rope]
[Sound: A drone’s lens whirr nearby; the Terrace lights strobe as power hiccups]

Mina hustles past with a tray of bolted-down pumps, hair plastered to her forehead, and a steady stream of orders that never cracks.

Mina: "Hold that line! Aiko — (she uses your name like an anchor) — check the northern bladder. If it ruptures we lose the beds but not the path."

You move, because moving is the way of staying human tonight. Your fingers find valves, feel the reluctant warmth of a pump that has refused sleep. You tighten a clamp with a practiced snap. The sound is tiny against the storm but to you it is everything.

The choice is small, but it matters — a microfracture in the ledger. You notice, for a second, how easily trust can be given and taken when everything around you trembles. Lin nods when you delegate; her hands are sure. Mina's eyes find yours and hold, a quick, fierce thank-you.

Elias returns in under a minute, mud on his cuffs, laughing like someone who has just run a race they were terrified to lose.

Elias: "They're hauling Mrs. Duarte up now. Two more on the ramp. How's the north?"

You: "Stable. For now."

He studies your face a beat too long, rain dripping from the tip of his nose, and in that look there is exhaustion and something else — a crack wide enough to let sunlight through.

Elias: (soft) "Don't make me lose you to protocols tonight, Aiko."

You: (inside) That line — half joke, half plea — makes your chest ache the way tidewater can ache the shore. You answer with a practical breath.

You: "Not tonight. We get them all."

[Sound: A distant metallic thrum as HelioCorp's larger barrier arrays rattle on the horizon]
[Music: Percussion swells — urgency tempered by confident harmonies]

Rafi's voice cuts back through, louder now, broadcast on a community frequency. His recorder has gone live to more than the Terrace; the feed has stitched itself into the city’s nervous system.

Rafi: "People, listen in. I've got the override logs. They're trying to push full closure without community governance. Live feed up to the Beacon — Sora's going to testify and the Beacon crowd is amplifying. We are not alone out here."

You can hear it — the thousands of small voices that Rafi has corralled, the shout lines, the sound of neighbors forming human chains. HelioCorp's sterile drones hover like misplaced stars, elegant and wrong against the raggedness of tarps and knitted scarves.

Sora appears at the Ramp’s edge, shawl wrapped tight, and when she speaks it is low and steady enough for storms to listen.

Sora: (clear) "We remember the shoreline before the walls. We remember how each family threaded their nets with songs and names. You cannot lock that away."

There is rippling applause through the channels and, more importantly, through the people. Rafi keeps cutting the feed to show the company's attempt and the community's counter — a carousel of proof and witness. HelioCorp's spokes-patterns of PR paper begin to fray under public pressure.

[Scene: Tideward Promenade | Simultaneous — Media flare-up]
[Visual: Banners flapping on the Promenade; corporate shuttles reflected in puddles]
[Music: Electronic motifs — tense but building toward catharsis]
[Sound: Murmurs, camera shutters, an industrial hum]

Rafi's footage pings on the Promenade's wall screens: the override log highlighted, legal text that doesn't say what it should. People around you who had been moving sandbags stop and read. Somebody leaves a wet thumbprint on the screen. In minutes, a small knot of journalists forms; one of them points to the legal clauses as if they are suddenly physical trespasses.

Journalist: "Who authorized this override without consultation?"

HelioCorp: (pre-recorded corporate voice on a loop) "We are acting to protect metropolitan assets and ensure continuity of service."

You: (bitterly) "Assets, not people," you think, and the thought tastes like iron and rain.

Back on the Terrace, the simultaneous maneuver continues to hum with controlled chaos. The split teams are a choreography you all have sweat-patched together in weeks of drill. One group fortifies pumps and reroutes power; another guides elders, babies, and the storied cats who have lived through more tides than some city council members.

Elias appears at your side again, breath coming back now that immediate physical danger is receding.

Elias: (exhaling, laughing haltingly) "We lost half the herb beds, but damn if Mrs. Duarte didn't dance on that ramp like a queen."

You both laugh, the sound raw and unexpectedly bright against water. It carries the taste of shared danger and shared relief.

You: (thought) The laughter is a suture; it holds raw edges together. You look at Elias — the lines around his eyes deeper, his hair clinging in wet ropes — and something in you steadies, a decision that feels like coming home while standing in the teeth of a storm.

He grabs your hand and squeezes until your fingers tingle." || Choice B ("Keep it to a look — quiet, mutual reliance") -> Reaction: "You say nothing aloud. Your palm meets his; the silence speaks. He laughs into your shoulder and the laugh is an agreement." || Choice C ("Joke to cover how you feel") -> Reaction: "You jab a wet finger at his nose and say he's a ridiculous storm-chaser. He spits water and curses affectionately—a small, perfect deflection that leaves everything said without naming it."]

The emotional charge hums between you like a live wire. None of the choices undo the tide, but each one knits you together in a slightly different pattern. Whatever you do — speak it, silence it, or cloak it in humor — the bond deepens. That deepening is its own small victory.

[Scene: Old Beacon | Later — After the immediate crisis]
[Visual: Lantern room patched with tarps; neighbors gathered instead of dispersing]
[Music: Warm piano and strings — relief edged with tender resolve]
[Sound: Quiet chatter, kettle steam, footsteps on stone]

The city exhales. The Terrace is bruised but intact; elders are safe on higher ground; pumps rumble in staggered, productive intervals. Your hands are cut and raw and gloved, and you hold a paper cup of tea that someone has handed you as if it were an offering.

Sora sits on a low step, steadier than anyone you would have guessed, and the Beacon's crowd presses close, not just for shelter but for each other's warmth.

Sora: (to the crowd) "Tonight we did not wait for permission to keep each other alive. That is the law of the shoreline."

A cheer, small and genuine. Rafi appears at the Beacon's edge again, but this time his face is laughing, eyes crinkled.

Rafi: "The coverage is up across districts. HelioCorp has had to back off a full remote lockdown — for now. The logs were public; lawyers have teeth and people have mouths. Sora’s testimony is on record. They're reopening talks."

A new kind of tired settles into your bones — the warm, satisfied tired of work done well and of wounds that mean something. You are aware, with crystalline clarity, of how many hands it took: Elias's barked orders, Mina's steady packing, Lin's willing clutch at a clamp, Rafi's stubborn network of leaks and feeds, Sora's history and naming of what mattered.

Elias: (sitting beside you, thumb rubbing your knuckles) "We almost lost the northern string. You saw that, right? You held it."

You: (soft) "We held it together."

A pause stretches and then folds into something steady.

Elias: "So... what now? We keep building. Push for shared governance in every pilot, make aid pipelines open-source, make HelioCorp put its models into public scrubbers, push city policy to require community oversight panels."

You: (with quiet resolve) "Yes. And we make sure the people who live here have seats at tables they once weren't allowed to chair. We scale what worked tonight — not with top-down grants alone but through networks. We teach other terraces what we did. We demand transparency. We keep people first, always."

[Music: Builds to a gentle, triumphant swell]

Rafi: (grinning) "I already posted the how-to guides, Aiko. Viral in thirty minutes. Now every community gets a toolkit."

Mina: (teasing) "And I published the emergency stew recipe. Morale counts in the ledger, too."

Laughter rings, the kind that stitches wounds.

Outside, a waiting crowd begins to chant — not angry but resolute — and the Beacon's lamp burns like a promise. The city's skyline is a mixture of ruin and stubborn light; somewhere beyond the rain, HelioCorp's glass atrium gleams like a challenge, but it no longer feels invincible.

[Visual: Montage — Volunteers teaching other neighborhoods; shared tool pallets stamped with your Terrace's mark; Rafi on a press stage with documents; HelioCorp officials entering a public forum rather than an exclusive boardroom]
[Music: Hopeful strings and steady percussion — forward-moving]

Weeks fold into months with the steady efficiency of things that never had the luxury of being slow. Aid pipelines become standardized; open-data pilots that you helped design are adopted in three neighboring districts. HelioCorp, pressured by public testimony and leaked logs, agrees to a city oversight committee with mandatory community seats — not surrendering everything, but shifting the center of power in important ways.

Sora visits the Terrace with a basket of seedlings and a quiet smile.

Sora: "You have kept more than soil. You have kept the practice of speaking together."

You: (inside) You hold that compliment like sunlight. It warms something that had been cold for too long: the habit of trust.

Elias is there, always, with his camera still slung over his shoulder, still catching the small domestic revolutions you make: a repaired pump, a child learning to knot, a pot of stew shared between neighbors. He stands close enough that your knees brush when you sit on the low Beacon wall. He asks fewer jokes now and offers steadier hands.

Elias: "We make mistakes," he says once, voice small. "But we make them together."

You: (looking at him) "And we fix them together."

The future ahead is neither clean nor guaranteed. There will be more storms. There will be policy fights and days when systems groan and people get tired. But you have seen tonight what a community can do when it refuses to be reduced to statistics. You have seen that public pressure, when paired with practical readiness, can steer even a well-funded technocracy toward negotiation.

[Sound: Distant gulls, faint but clear; the wind has softened]
[Music: Single piano note, then a chorus of harmonies — closure]

You close your notebook, the pages full of diagrams and names and a small penciled heart next to a ledger entry that reads: "Tonight — Terrace holds. People saved. Governance demanded."

Elias reaches down and takes your hand without asking. His fingers are warm, strong, familiar.

Elias: "So — coffee tomorrow? At Mina's place. I want to show you what I shot of Mrs. Duarte dancing."

You: (smiling) "Only if you promise to stop trying to romance the ladders."

He grins, mock-offended, and then sincere.

Elias: "I promise. Mostly."

The promise lands between you like a small bridge.

You look out over the city — the patched levees, the lit windows, the small green roofs that refuse to be swallowed. You let your breath find the new, steady rhythm of someone who has fought for what she loves and found it mirrored back in another person's toil and laughter.

You: (final thought) We woke and we reckoned, and we kept more than roofs that night. We kept one another.

[Visual: Wide shot of the Beacon and Terrace glowing against a clearing sky; the city's lights steadier, the horizon softer]
[Music: Crescendo into a warm, enduring chord]
[Sound: Quiet applause, a chorus of voices in the distance]

[ENDING]
